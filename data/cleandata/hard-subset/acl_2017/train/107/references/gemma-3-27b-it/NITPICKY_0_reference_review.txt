This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a data selection heuristic and representation projection using word embeddings, further enhanced by co-decoding schemes. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of a combined system leveraging both projection methods, with a focus on practical improvements to annotation projection through data selection. A secondary, though less substantial, contribution is the application of representation projection for cross-lingual transfer, though this aspect feels relatively standard.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Improvement to Existing Techniques:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrably improves performance, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. This is a valuable, pragmatic contribution as it addresses a known weakness of projection-based methods – sensitivity to alignment quality. The reported gains, while not massive, are consistent across languages and represent a tangible benefit.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper undertakes a fairly extensive evaluation, including both in-house datasets and the standard CoNLL datasets. This breadth of experimentation lends credibility to the findings and allows for a more robust assessment of the proposed methods.  The comparison against existing cross-lingual NER baselines is crucial and positions the work within the broader field.
3. **Co-Decoding as a Useful Combination Strategy:** The investigation of co-decoding strategies, specifically the rank-based approach, offers a practical way to integrate the strengths of the different projection methods. The justification for prioritizing annotation projection’s output, based on its higher precision, is reasonable.
4. **Clear Presentation of Models:** The descriptions of the CRFs, MEMMs, and neural network models, while standard, are presented clearly and concisely, making the paper accessible. The inclusion of the neural network architecture diagrams is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Representation Projection:** The representation projection method, while functional, relies heavily on established techniques (word embeddings, linear mappings). The contribution here is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper doesn't offer any novel insights into the projection process itself, such as exploring different mapping functions or addressing the challenges of distributional differences between languages beyond dictionary weighting.
2. **Overly Detailed and Sometimes Redundant Presentation:** The paper exhibits a tendency towards excessive detail, particularly in the model descriptions. For example, reiterating the equations for CRFs and MEMMs feels unnecessary for an ACL audience and detracts from the focus on the core contributions. The text could benefit from tighter editing to remove redundancies and streamline the presentation.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper reports performance metrics, it lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by each system. Understanding *why* the systems fail, and in what specific scenarios, would be valuable for guiding future research. Specifically, an error analysis focused on the impact of alignment errors in the annotation projection stage is missing.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity and Tuning Details:** The data selection scheme introduces parameters (q and n) that require tuning. The paper mentions tuning these parameters, but lacks a detailed discussion of how this was done and the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings. Providing a more systematic analysis of parameter tuning would strengthen the validity of the findings.
5. **Formatting Inconsistencies and Typos:** Several minor formatting issues are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent spacing after punctuation, uneven table formatting). While not critical, these details detract from the overall polish and professionalism. There are also a few minor typos (e.g. “de-coding” should be “decoding”).



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors made by each system and the impact of alignment errors on the annotation projection approach?
2.  What was the specific methodology used to tune the parameters *q* and *n* in the data selection scheme?  Were sensitivity analyses performed to assess the robustness of the results to different parameter settings?
3.  In the representation projection approach, did you experiment with different methods for learning the cross-lingual embedding mappings (e.g., adversarial training, or more sophisticated mapping functions beyond linear transformations)? If so, could you share the results?
4.  The paper mentions a "smoothing layer" in NN2. Could you elaborate on the specific implementation of this layer and its impact on performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the manuscript to address the formatting inconsistencies and typos.
*   Consider reducing the level of detail in the model descriptions to focus more on the novel contributions of the paper.
