## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of cross-lingual NER with no target language annotations. It proposes two main approaches: 1) an annotation projection method with a data selection heuristic to improve quality when alignment is noisy, and 2) a representation projection method using cross-lingual word embeddings to directly transfer a source language NER model. Finally, it introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.  The paper presents extensive experiments on in-house and CoNLL datasets for several languages, demonstrating improvements over existing weakly supervised methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The paper convincingly shows that a simple yet effective data selection heuristic significantly improves the performance of annotation projection, particularly in scenarios with noisy alignment data. This is a practical contribution, as noisy alignment is common in real-world scenarios.
2. **Combination of Projection Techniques:** The exploration of co-decoding schemes to leverage the strengths of both annotation and representation projection is a valuable addition.  This is not a novel concept in machine learning generally, but its application within the specific context of cross-lingual NER with these techniques is well-motivated and produces positive results.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, including both in-house and standard CoNLL data. This allows for a robust assessment of the proposed methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of cross-lingual NER with limited resources and provides a strong motivation for the proposed approaches.
2. **Practical & Well-Justified Data Selection Heuristic:** The proposed data selection scheme is intuitive and well-motivated, leveraging frequency information to identify more reliable projected annotations. The experimental results strongly support its effectiveness, especially in low-resource scenarios. The rationale behind the quality and number thresholds is clear and logical.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements over existing baseline methods on multiple datasets. The results are well-presented and statistically significant.  The comparison against prior work on CoNLL data is particularly valuable.
4. **Thorough Ablation and Analysis:**  The paper provides a good examination of the individual components.  Specifically, the comparison of NN1 and NN2 is helpful, as is the performance analysis of different co-decoding schemes.  This allows for a better understanding of the contribution of each component.
5. **Reproducibility:** The detail provided on the models and experiments (though lengthy) lends itself to potential reproducibility, which is a positive attribute.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is a positive contribution, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection with word embeddings, co-decoding) are not fundamentally new. The core novelty lies in *how* these techniques are applied and combined within this specific cross-lingual NER context. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but it should be acknowledged.
2. **Dependency on External Resources (Alignment and Dictionaries):** The annotation projection relies heavily on the quality of the parallel corpora and alignment models. The representation projection relies on a high-quality dictionary for mapping embeddings. The paper acknowledges this, but a more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the methods to variations in alignment/dictionary quality would be beneficial. Specifically, how do the methods degrade as alignment quality *decreases*?
3. **Complexity of the Presentation:** The paper is very dense and could benefit from more concise writing. Some sections (e.g., NER Models) are overly detailed and could be summarized or moved to the appendix. The length makes it harder to quickly grasp the key contributions.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding *what kinds* of errors the systems still make would provide valuable insights for future research. For example, are there specific entity types that are particularly challenging? Are errors due to ambiguity in the source language or poor alignment?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of your methods to the quality of the alignment data (for annotation projection) and the dictionary (for representation projection)? Specifically, how do the performance metrics change as alignment accuracy/dictionary size decreases?
2.  What types of errors are most common in your combined system?  Would a detailed error analysis reveal specific areas for improvement?
3.  The paper is quite long.  Could you identify sections that could be condensed or moved to an appendix without losing essential information?
4. Could you comment on the computational cost of the representation projection method, particularly the linear mapping stage? Does this scale well to a large number of target languages?




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. This is a solid and well-executed work that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER.  While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination of these techniques, along with the thorough experimental evaluation and practical data selection heuristic, results in a significant improvement over existing methods. Addressing the weaknesses identified above (particularly the error analysis and clarity of presentation) would further strengthen the paper.
