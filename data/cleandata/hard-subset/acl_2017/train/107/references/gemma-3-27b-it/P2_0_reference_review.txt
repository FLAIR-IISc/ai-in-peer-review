## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of cross-lingual NER with no target language annotations. It proposes two main approaches: 1) an improved annotation projection method utilizing a data selection heuristic to mitigate noise from comparable corpora, and 2) a representation projection method leveraging cross-lingual word embeddings to transfer a model trained on English. Finally, it combines these approaches via co-decoding schemes. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing weakly supervised methods and achieve competitive performance, approaching supervised results in some cases.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic for selecting high-quality projection data from comparable corpora is a practical and valuable contribution, particularly given the often-noisy nature of such data. This directly addresses a key limitation of previous annotation projection techniques.
2. **Combined Approach via Co-Decoding:**  The exploration of co-decoding schemes to integrate the strengths of both projection-based methods shows a good understanding of the individual methods’ weaknesses and how to mitigate them.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive experimentation across multiple languages and datasets (both in-house and CoNLL) provides a strong empirical validation of the proposed approaches.



**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Relevance:** The problem of low-resource cross-lingual NER is a significant one, and the weakly supervised approach presented is highly relevant to real-world scenarios where obtaining labeled data in multiple languages is expensive and time-consuming.
2. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and extensive, involving multiple languages and datasets. The inclusion of both in-house and standard datasets (CoNLL) adds robustness to the evaluation.  The ablation studies (e.g., comparing NN1 and NN2, different co-decoding schemes) help to understand the contribution of each component.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:**  The paper is clearly written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the descriptions of the methods are detailed and easy to follow.  The inclusion of tables summarizing the experimental results facilitates understanding.
4. **Addressing a Known Weakness:** The paper directly addresses the issue of alignment accuracy in the annotation projection approach, a common pitfall in previous work, with a practical data selection strategy.
5. **Competitive Performance:** The reported results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing weakly supervised methods and approach supervised performance, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components – annotation projection and representation projection – are not entirely novel concepts.  The primary innovation lies in *how* these techniques are adapted and combined.  The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty within the context of the existing literature.
2. **Dependency on English:** The reliance on English as the source language introduces a potential bias. The performance might be significantly different if a different source language were used. The paper does not discuss this limitation or explore potential solutions (e.g., pivoting through a more central language).
3. **Feature Engineering (Representation Projection):**  The representation projection method relies on pre-trained word embeddings.  The quality of these embeddings is crucial, and the paper doesn't fully discuss the impact of different embedding initialization strategies or hyperparameter tuning for the embedding mapping.  While the use of CBOW is mentioned, more detail on the training parameters (window size, negative sampling, etc.) would be helpful.
4. **Co-decoding Simplification:** The co-decoding schemes, while effective, feel somewhat simplistic.  More sophisticated combination strategies (e.g., using a learned weighting scheme or a more complex fusion model) might yield further improvements.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Source Language Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of English as the source language? Have you considered or experimented with other potential source languages, and if not, why?
2. **Embedding Quality:**  How was the quality of the word embeddings assessed for each target language? Did you experiment with different embedding initialization strategies, and if so, how did they affect the results?
3. **Data Selection Parameters:** What is the sensitivity of the results to the parameters *q* and *n* in the data selection scheme?  How were these parameters tuned, and can you provide more insight into the optimal values for different languages?
4. **Co-Decoding Alternatives:** Did you explore more sophisticated co-decoding strategies beyond the confidence-based and rank-based schemes?  If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper would benefit from a discussion of error analysis – what types of errors do the systems still make, and what are the potential causes?
* The figures (e.g., Figure 1) are referenced but not included in the submitted version.  Please add them for a complete review.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in cross-lingual NER. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, the combination of techniques, the practical data selection heuristic, and the thorough evaluation make a significant contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above and answer the questions posed.
