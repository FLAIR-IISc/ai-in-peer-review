## Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to transfer knowledge from English to other languages without requiring human annotation in the target languages. The authors explore two main strategies: (1) annotation projection with a data selection heuristic to improve noisy projections, and (2) representation projection using cross-lingual word embeddings.  They further combine these approaches through co-decoding schemes. The paper demonstrates performance gains on both in-house and CoNLL datasets, outperforming existing cross-lingual NER methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Practical Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed data selection heuristic offers a tangible improvement to annotation projection, a previously established technique. By focusing on selecting higher-quality projected data, the method mitigates the impact of noisy alignment common in comparable corpora. This is a valuable contribution for real-world scenarios where perfect parallel data is unavailable.
2. **Combined System Demonstrates Effectiveness:**  The co-decoding schemes show the benefit of combining different approaches to cross-lingual NER. Successfully integrating the annotation projection and representation projection methods leads to superior performance.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a robust evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), strengthening the validity of the proposed methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant and Challenging Problem:** Cross-lingual NER with limited resources is a significant challenge in NLP.  This paper directly tackles this problem with practical solutions.
2. **Well-Defined Methodology:** The authors clearly explain the annotation projection and representation projection techniques, including the specific algorithms and implementation details (e.g., data selection criteria, embedding training).  This clarity is crucial for reproducibility.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements over baseline methods and existing cross-lingual NER systems, especially with the combined co-decoding schemes. The tables are well-organized and clearly present the performance gains.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted on diverse datasets and languages, making the findings more generalizable. The inclusion of both in-house and standard CoNLL datasets adds credibility.
5. **Realistic Scenario:**  The focus on comparable corpora for annotation projection is pragmatic.  Parallel corpora are often unavailable, making this a more applicable solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination is effective, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection, CRFs, and neural networks) are established techniques in the field.  The paper's primary contribution lies in the *integration* and *adaptation* of these existing methods rather than groundbreaking new algorithms.
2. **Dependence on English as Pivot Language:** The reliance on English as the sole pivot language introduces a potential bottleneck. Performance may vary significantly depending on the linguistic similarity between the target language and English. Exploring multi-pivot approaches or direct transfer between language pairs could be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results. A more detailed error analysis of the systems' failures would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approaches and potential directions for future research.  Understanding *what types* of errors the systems make would be highly informative.
4. **Details on Alignment Quality:**  While the authors mention alignment accuracy as a factor, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the alignment quality’s impact on projection performance.  How do results vary with different alignment scores or tools?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Pivot Language Sensitivity:**  Have you considered the potential limitations of using English as the sole pivot language?  Do you anticipate that the performance would be significantly different for languages very distant from English?  Could you comment on how linguistic similarity impacts the effectiveness of the representation projection approach?
2. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a brief error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by each system (annotation projection, representation projection, and the combined system)?  This would help to understand the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. **Alignment Impact:**  Could you elaborate on the impact of alignment quality on the annotation projection performance?  Do you have any data on how different alignment tools or alignment scores correlate with the performance of your system?
4. **Computational Cost:**  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of each approach, particularly the representation projection which involves learning cross-lingual embeddings?  Is it feasible to scale this to a large number of target languages?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive.
* Minor editing for clarity and conciseness would be helpful. For example, some sections could be streamlined to avoid repetition.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.  While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the combination of techniques, the practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection, and the comprehensive evaluation make a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER.  Addressing the weaknesses raised in my review during the author response period would further strengthen the paper.
