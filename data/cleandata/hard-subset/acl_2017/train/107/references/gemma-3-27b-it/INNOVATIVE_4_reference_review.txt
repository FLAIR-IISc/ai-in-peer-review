This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER with no target language annotations, exploring both annotation projection and representation projection techniques. The primary contribution is the combination of these two approaches via co-decoding, demonstrably outperforming existing weakly supervised methods and approaching supervised performance in some cases. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, improving performance when alignment quality is low. Finally, the thorough empirical evaluation across multiple languages and datasets is commendable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Combination of Approaches:** The most compelling aspect is the integration of annotation projection *and* representation projection.  While both techniques are individually established, combining them and intelligently co-decoding their outputs is a genuinely novel idea. The results consistently show a benefit from this combination, suggesting a synergistic effect. This is not merely incremental work.
2. **Practical Data Selection Heuristic:**  The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a pragmatic and effective improvement. Recognizing the impact of alignment noise and actively mitigating it via a quality metric is a smart and well-justified approach. The experimental results clearly demonstrate its utility.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate on a diverse set of languages (including Japanese and Korean which are typologically distant from English) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), lending strong credibility to their findings. This robust evaluation is crucial for demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed methods.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the individual components and the integration strategy. The details provided regarding model architectures and training procedures (even if not exhaustive) increase confidence in the reproducibility of the results.
5. **Competitive Results:** The achieved performance, particularly with the co-decoding scheme, is impressive for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, rivaling supervised approaches and significantly surpassing existing baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Representation Projection:** While representation projection is a core component, the analysis of *why* it works (or doesn’t work as well as annotation projection in some cases) is somewhat shallow.  A deeper investigation into the quality of the learned embedding mappings and their impact on NER performance would be beneficial. Are certain languages more amenable to this approach than others, and if so, why?
2. **Co-Decoding Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions “tuning” the co-decoding parameters but lacks detail on the tuning process itself. How sensitive are the results to the specific thresholds used in the rank-based co-decoding scheme? A more rigorous sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims about its robustness.
3. **Feature Engineering in CRFs/MEMMs:** The feature engineering in the CRF/MEMM models feels somewhat dated. While the focus is on transfer learning, exploring more modern feature representations (e.g., character-level embeddings) in the source language model could potentially improve the quality of the projected annotations and further boost performance.
4. **Alignment Quality as a Black Box:** The alignment quality is mentioned as a factor, but only reported as a single F1 score per language pair.  The *types* of alignment errors (e.g., word reordering, missing words) and their specific impact on NER performance are not discussed. Understanding these error patterns could inform further improvements to the data selection scheme.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the tuning process for the co-decoding scheme, including the search space and evaluation metric used to select the optimal parameters?
2.  How does the performance of the representation projection approach vary with the size of the training dictionary used to learn the embedding mappings?  Is there a saturation point beyond which adding more word pairs does not improve performance?
3.  Would incorporating character-level embeddings into the source language CRF/MEMM model significantly impact the performance of the annotation projection approach?
4. Can you provide examples of alignment errors that most frequently lead to incorrect NER projections and how the data selection scheme mitigates these errors?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The use of IOB tagging is standard, but consider whether BIOES might provide finer-grained information and potentially improve performance.
* The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on parallel or comparable corpora and the potential for domain mismatch.
