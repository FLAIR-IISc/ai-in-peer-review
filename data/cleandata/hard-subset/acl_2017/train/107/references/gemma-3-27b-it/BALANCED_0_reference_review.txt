This paper presents a weakly supervised cross-lingual NER system combining annotation projection and representation projection, with a focus on data selection and co-decoding. The contributions are a heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, a representation projection approach using word embedding mappings, and co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of both approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem – cross-lingual NER with zero-resource target languages – and proposes a multifaceted solution. Combining annotation projection and representation projection is a logical and potentially effective strategy, allowing the system to leverage different strengths of each approach.
2. **Effective Data Selection:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection is a significant contribution. Recognizing the impact of noisy projection data and actively mitigating it through a quality-based selection process is crucial for the success of this approach. The results demonstrating improvements in F1 score, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), strongly support the effectiveness of this scheme.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on both in-house and publicly available (CoNLL) datasets. This broad evaluation across multiple languages and datasets lends credibility to the findings and demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed methods. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is valuable and shows competitive performance.
4. **Detailed Implementation and Analysis:** The paper provides sufficient details regarding the implementation of the NER models, embedding mappings, and co-decoding schemes. The discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of each component, as well as the rationale behind design choices (e.g., the rank-based co-decoding scheme), is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination and refinement of existing techniques are valuable, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection using word embeddings, CRFs/MEMMs, neural networks) are not novel in themselves. The primary novelty lies in the integration and specific adaptations for this cross-lingual NER task. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but it diminishes the overall impact.
2. **Dependency on High-Quality Alignment:**  The annotation projection approach, despite the data selection scheme, remains heavily reliant on the quality of the parallel corpora and alignment information. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the correlation between alignment accuracy and the performance of the projection-based system. Specifically, how does performance degrade as alignment quality decreases, even *with* the data selection scheme applied?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The data selection scheme introduces parameters (q and n) that require careful tuning. The paper reports the best results found, but a more systematic exploration of the parameter space and a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial.  Was a development set used for tuning, and if so, is it clearly stated?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the system (e.g., false positives, false negatives, incorrect entity type assignments) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Knowing *why* the system fails in certain cases can guide targeted refinements to the models or data selection strategies.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the correlation between alignment accuracy (measured objectively) and the performance improvements achieved by the data selection scheme?  Are there threshold alignment accuracies below which the data selection scheme becomes ineffective?
2.  What development set was used to tune the parameters 'q' and 'n' for the data selection scheme?  Could you provide the results of a sensitivity analysis, showing how performance varies with different values of 'q' and 'n'?
3.  Could you provide examples of errors made by the system, categorized by error type (e.g., incorrect entity boundaries, incorrect entity type)? This would help understand the limitations of the current approach and suggest avenues for improvement.
4.  You mention the annotation projection system having high precision but low recall. Is this consistent across all languages, or does it vary? If it varies, what factors contribute to this variation?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a visual illustration of the annotation projection and representation projection processes.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the different approaches.
*   The formatting could be improved (e.g., consistent use of boldface for key terms).



Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper that presents a practical and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the combination and refinement, particularly the data selection scheme, represent a valuable contribution. The thorough evaluation and detailed implementation discussion further strengthen the paper. Addressing the identified weaknesses – especially providing a more thorough error analysis and exploring hyperparameter sensitivity – would significantly enhance the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
