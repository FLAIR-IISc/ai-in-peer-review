This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER with no target language annotations, exploring both annotation projection and representation projection. The primary contribution, and the most compelling aspect of this work, is the *combination* of these two distinct approaches via co-decoding. While neither projection method is particularly novel on its own, the systematic exploration of combining their outputs, and the empirical demonstration of consistent gains, represent a worthwhile contribution to the field. A secondary contribution is the heuristic data selection scheme for improving annotation projection, addressing a known weakness of this approach. Finally, the extensive experimentation across multiple languages and datasets provides a solid empirical foundation for the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Combination of Approaches:** The core strength lies in the co-decoding framework. Many cross-lingual transfer methods focus on a single strategy. This work intelligently leverages the complementary strengths of annotation projection (high precision when projections are accurate) and representation projection (better recall, even with noisy projections).  The empirical results consistently show that combining these approaches yields superior performance. This is a genuinely insightful approach.
2. **Practical Focus & Data Selection:** The paper directly addresses a key practical issue with annotation projection – noisy projected data. The proposed data selection scheme, while relatively simple, is effective in filtering out low-quality projections and significantly improving performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios with poor alignment. This demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges in real-world application.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct experiments across a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), providing a strong and convincing demonstration of the generalizability of their approach. This extensive evaluation sets this work apart from many others in the field.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup. The organization is logical, and the motivation for each step is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The individual components – annotation projection and representation projection – are well-established techniques. The paper doesn’t present fundamentally new algorithms for either of these. The significant contribution is genuinely the combination, and the paper would be significantly weakened if that wasn’t the case.
2. **Supervised Baseline Imbalance:** The comparison to supervised baselines feels somewhat imbalanced. While the authors acknowledge that supervised performance is superior, the size of the supervised datasets used for comparison isn't consistently reported or controlled.  It would be more convincing to have a more direct comparison with supervised models trained on *similar* amounts of data as the weakly supervised systems utilize.  Specifically, reporting the training size for the supervised baseline alongside the weakly supervised system’s training data (projection labeled data) would be beneficial.
3. **Co-Decoding Scheme Detail:** While the co-decoding schemes are described, the rationale behind choosing “exclude-O” and the “rank-based” approaches isn’t thoroughly justified.  More analysis of *why* these schemes work better than simple confidence averaging would strengthen the paper.  Is there an analysis of error types made by each system that justifies prioritizing annotation projection?
4. **Alignment Quality Impact:** The paper acknowledges alignment quality as crucial for projection, but doesn’t deeply explore its impact. A more systematic analysis of how performance degrades with varying alignment error rates would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for choosing the “exclude-O” and “rank-based” co-decoding schemes? What specific error patterns observed in each projection method led to these choices?
2.  Could you clarify the size of the human-annotated datasets used for the supervised baselines *specifically* for each language?  Providing a side-by-side comparison of training data size (weakly supervised vs. supervised) would greatly strengthen the evaluation.
3. Have you considered other approaches to combining the outputs of the two projection methods, such as more sophisticated weighting schemes or learned combination functions? If so, why were the chosen schemes preferred?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the cross-lingual embedding mapping process, particularly as it scales to a larger number of languages?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Minor: Throughout the paper, the terminology "MEMM" and "CRF" are used frequently. It would be beneficial to provide brief explanations of these acronyms at their first occurrences in each section, to improve readability for a wider audience.
*   The reference formatting is a little inconsistent.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The novel combination of annotation projection and representation projection, coupled with comprehensive evaluation, makes it a strong submission. The work showcases a practical and effective approach to tackling a challenging problem. I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The key strength—the co-decoding framework—is substantial enough to outweigh the lack of groundbreaking novelty in the individual components.
