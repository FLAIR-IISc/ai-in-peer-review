This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic and representation projection using word embeddings. The primary contribution is a pragmatic improvement to annotation projection through targeted data selection, demonstrably improving performance when alignment quality is low. A secondary contribution is the exploration of representation projection for cross-lingual transfer, though its performance is less consistently strong. Finally, the investigation of co-decoding schemes adds a minor incremental benefit.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Improvement to Annotation Projection:** The data selection scheme is a sensible and well-motivated approach. The authors correctly identify that noisy projection data degrades performance, and their heuristic to filter low-quality projections based on frequency analysis is effective, as shown by the experimental results, particularly for Japanese and Korean. This is a concrete, practical improvement to an established technique.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and the standard CoNLL datasets. This breadth of experimentation strengthens the conclusions and demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed methods.
3. **Clear Presentation and Established Baselines:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodologies. Importantly, it compares against established cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and provides a useful baseline for future research. The inclusion of supervised results provides context for the achievable performance.
4. **Sound Methodology in Representation Projection:** The process of learning cross-lingual embeddings using a weighted least squares approach is logically sound and consistent with established methods in the field. The authors appropriately acknowledge the importance of dictionary size for mapping quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Representation Projection:** While the application to NER is reasonable, the representation projection method itself – using pre-trained word embeddings and linear mapping – is not a novel contribution. This feels more like an application of existing techniques than a significant advance in cross-lingual representation learning. The performance isn’t consistently superior to the annotation projection, reducing its impact.
2. **Overly Complex Co-Decoding:** The co-decoding schemes, while aiming to combine the strengths of both approaches, introduce complexity without demonstrably significant gains. The descriptions of "exclude-O confidence-based" and "rank-based" schemes are somewhat convoluted, and the performance improvements are modest. It’s not clear the added complexity is justified.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the paper provides quantitative results, it lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the systems fail. Understanding the types of errors made by each approach (annotation projection vs. representation projection) and where they overlap would provide valuable insights for future work. For instance, are there specific entity types or linguistic structures that consistently cause problems?
4. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** While the data selection scheme mitigates the issue, the annotation projection approach remains fundamentally limited by the quality of the alignment between source and target languages. The paper doesn’t fully address the challenge of applying this method to language pairs with poor alignment resources.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically outlining the types of errors made by the annotation projection and representation projection approaches?
2. Regarding the co-decoding schemes, could you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of each component (e.g., the "exclude-O" aspect of the confidence-based scheme)?
3. How sensitive is the data selection scheme to the parameters *q* and *n*? Have you explored methods for automatically tuning these parameters?
4. For the representation projection approach, have you experimented with non-linear mapping functions beyond the simple linear transformation?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper’s reliance on pre-trained word embeddings is somewhat dated given the prevalence of contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT, XLM-RoBERTa). Investigating the use of these more powerful representations could potentially improve the performance of the representation projection approach. The paper would benefit from briefly discussing this limitation.
