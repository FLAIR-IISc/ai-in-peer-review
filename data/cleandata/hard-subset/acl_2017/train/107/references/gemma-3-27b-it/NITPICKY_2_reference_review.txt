The paper proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods leveraging both annotation projection with a data selection heuristic and representation projection via embedding mapping. It further introduces co-decoding schemes to combine these two approaches. The primary contribution, as I see it, is a relatively thorough empirical evaluation of several weakly supervised cross-lingual NER techniques across multiple languages, including both in-house and standard datasets. While the individual components aren’t groundbreaking, the systematic comparison and demonstration of improvements via the proposed data selection and co-decoding are valuable. A secondary contribution lies in the data selection heuristic for annotation projection, which shows some effectiveness in noisy data scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a remarkably extensive set of experiments across four in-house languages and three CoNLL languages. This broad empirical coverage is a significant strength, allowing for a robust assessment of the proposed methods and a fairer comparison against baselines. The inclusion of both in-house and standard datasets increases the reliability and generalizability of the findings.
2. **Practical Improvements via Data Selection:** The proposed heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrably improves performance, particularly in low-resource or noisy scenarios (as shown for Japanese and Korean). This is a practical contribution, offering a method to refine weakly labeled data before training. The rationale behind the metric is sound—prioritizing consistently labeled entities.
3. **Co-Decoding Effectiveness:** The co-decoding schemes, specifically the rank-based approach, consistently improve performance compared to individual systems. This shows the benefit of combining different approaches, exploiting their complementary strengths. The justification for prioritizing annotation projection results in the rank-based scheme aligns with the observed performance characteristics of each system.
4. **Clear Presentation of Models:** The descriptions of the CRFs, MEMMs and Neural Network models are reasonably clear and provide sufficient detail for understanding the implementation.  The discussion of the embedding mapping process is also well explained, outlining the weighting scheme used.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methods:** Neither the annotation projection nor the representation projection techniques are novel in themselves.  The paper largely builds upon established approaches. The improvements are incremental rather than fundamentally new. The authors need to more clearly delineate *where* the novelty resides (it seems to be in the combination and the detailed empirical study) and temper claims of significant contributions.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Embedding Mapping:** The embedding mapping approach is treated somewhat superficially. While functional, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* this approach works (or doesn’t) for different languages.  What characteristics of a language make it more amenable to successful representation projection? There’s little discussion of potential failure cases or limitations beyond the dictionary size. The choice of 800 dimensions for target language embeddings is arbitrary; justification is needed.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:** The paper mentions parameter tuning for the data selection scheme (q and n thresholds), but doesn't provide details on the tuning process.  How were the optimal values chosen for each language? More details on hyperparameter sensitivity and the impact of different settings would strengthen the results. The selection of the best co-decoding scheme is also presented as a “result”, rather than a tuned parameter.
4. **Minor Formatting and Writing Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies and stylistic issues. For instance, equations are not always clearly referenced in the text. There are also instances of awkward phrasing and redundant statements (e.g., repeating the benefits of CRFs vs. MEMMs). While not major, these issues detract from the overall polish of the submission. The equation numbering also seems inconsistent.
5. **Unclear Motivation for Fine-Grained Entity Types:** The paper mentions a motivation for 50+ entity types related to question answering but doesn’t elaborate on how this benefits the cross-lingual transfer aspect. If this is central to the in-house dataset, it should be better explained.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process for the data selection scheme (q and n)? Specifically, what search strategy was employed, and how did you avoid overfitting to the development data?
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing 800 dimensions for the target language embeddings in the representation projection approach? Did you experiment with other dimensionality values?
3.  Can you elaborate on potential failure cases for the representation projection approach? Are there specific linguistic features or language families where this approach consistently underperforms?
4. Could you clarify how the rank-based co-decoding scheme's prioritization of annotation projection results was determined? Was this a fixed rule, or was it tuned based on development data performance?
5. Please provide more justification for the statement that your work outperforms state-of-the-art approaches. The numbers presented are close, but a more thorough statistical significance analysis would be appreciated.




**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the paper for minor formatting and grammatical errors.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of the datasets used (e.g., size, number of entity types, domain).
*   The citation style is inconsistent in places (e.g., using “et al.” vs. full author lists).



Based on these points, particularly the lack of substantial novelty and the presence of several weaknesses, I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a solid empirical study and some practical improvements, it falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly by providing a more thorough analysis of the representation projection approach and clarifying the novelty of the work, might warrant reconsideration.
