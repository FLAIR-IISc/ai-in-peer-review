## Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:** This paper tackles the problem of cross-lingual NER with zero-shot transfer from English to other languages. It proposes two main approaches: 1) an annotation projection method incorporating a data selection heuristic to mitigate noise from comparable corpora; and 2) a representation projection method leveraging cross-lingual word embeddings.  Finally, it explores co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The paper demonstrates improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The paper effectively shows the benefit of a simple, yet effective, data selection heuristic for improving the quality of annotation projection in low-resource scenarios where alignment accuracy is imperfect. This is a practical contribution for anyone working with noisy comparable corpora.
2. **Combination of Diverse Projection Methods:** The exploration of co-decoding schemes to combine the strengths of annotation projection (precision) and representation projection (recall) is a sensible approach, and the results suggest a tangible improvement in overall performance.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation across multiple languages and datasets (both in-house and standard CoNLL) provides a reasonably robust assessment of the proposed methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of building multilingual NER systems, particularly the cost of annotation, and sets a strong motivation for exploring weakly supervised approaches.
2. **Sound Methodology:**  Both the data selection heuristic and the representation projection approach are based on well-established principles and techniques (CRFs, neural networks, word embeddings, alignment models). The combination of these techniques in a novel way is a strength.
3. **Positive Empirical Results:**  The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline systems, particularly with the co-decoding schemes. The gains on the CoNLL datasets, compared to existing work (Täckström et al. & Tsai et al.) are particularly noteworthy.
4. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup, including the datasets, models, and parameters used, allowing for reproducibility.  The ablation studies comparing NN1 and NN2 are helpful.
5. **Practical Insights:** The observation that annotation projection favors precision while representation projection favors recall provides valuable insight into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, and informs the design of the co-decoding schemes.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** The individual components – annotation projection, representation projection, CRFs, and neural networks – are not novel contributions in themselves. The primary novelty lies in *how* these techniques are combined and refined (especially the data selection heuristic). The paper should more explicitly acknowledge this and focus on the synergistic effect of the approach.
2. **Reliance on Strong English NER System:**  The performance of both approaches heavily depends on the quality of the English NER system.  While the authors report a reasonable F1 score for the English NER system, a weakness in its performance could significantly impact the cross-lingual transfer. This dependency isn't thoroughly discussed. The robustness of the approach to variations in source language NER quality needs further investigation.
3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the proposed systems. Identifying common error patterns (e.g., misclassifying certain entity types, difficulty with ambiguous names) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
4. **Co-decoding Schemes are Relatively Simple:** While effective, the co-decoding schemes presented (confidence-based and rank-based) are relatively straightforward.  Exploring more sophisticated combination techniques, such as weighted averaging or more complex ensemble methods, could potentially yield further gains.  The rationale behind choosing these particular schemes isn’t thoroughly explained.
5. **Scalability Concerns:** The reliance on parallel corpora (even comparable ones) for annotation projection limits the scalability of this approach to languages for which such resources are available.  The paper doesn’t adequately address this limitation.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the representation projection approach to the quality and size of the cross-lingual dictionary used to learn the embedding mapping?  Could you provide results showing the performance with varying dictionary sizes and qualities?
2.  The paper mentions the high precision/low recall trade-off observed in annotation projection and representation projection. Have you experimented with different thresholds for the data selection heuristic (beyond the parameters tuned in your experiments) to specifically address this trade-off?
3.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the errors made by the systems?  Specifically, what types of entities are most often misclassified or missed?
4.  The co-decoding schemes seem to primarily leverage the confidence scores of the models. Have you considered incorporating other features, such as the length of the entity or the surrounding context, into the co-decoding process?
5.  The scalability of the annotation projection approach is limited by the availability of comparable corpora.  Are there any plans to explore alternative approaches that could reduce this dependency, such as leveraging machine translation or distant supervision techniques?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* Consider adding a table comparing the computational cost of each approach.
* The reference list is slightly outdated; including more recent work on cross-lingual embeddings and NER would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**  This is a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. While the individual techniques are not groundbreaking, the combination of annotation projection, representation projection, and the carefully designed co-decoding schemes, along with the comprehensive evaluation, makes this paper worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
