## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER with zero annotation in the target language. It explores two main approaches: (1) annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme to mitigate noise from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection leveraging cross-lingual word embeddings to directly transfer an English NER model. Finally, it proposes co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of both approaches.  The authors evaluate their methods on in-house and CoNLL datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing cross-lingual NER techniques.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Practical Improvement to Annotation Projection:** The proposed data selection heuristic for annotation projection offers a tangible improvement in performance, particularly when alignment quality is subpar, which is a common real-world scenario. This practical consideration is valuable.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a relatively thorough evaluation across multiple languages (both in-house and standard datasets) and multiple models (CRF, MEMM, neural networks), allowing for a robust comparison of the proposed methods.
3. **Combined System and Analysis:** The combination of annotation projection and representation projection via co-decoding, alongside an analysis of each component's strengths and weaknesses, provides valuable insights into leveraging different approaches for cross-lingual NER.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** The paper directly addresses a significant bottleneck in multilingual NLP – the scarcity of labeled data.  Weakly supervised methods are crucial for scaling NER to low-resource languages.
2. **Data Selection Scheme is Effective:**  The data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrably improves performance, as shown by the consistent gains across the tested languages (Table 2). The rationale behind selecting higher-frequency projected tags is sound and intuitive.  The fact that it shows the largest improvement in languages with poorer alignment quality reinforces its practical value.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The paper employs a diverse set of experimental setups, using both in-house datasets with a large number of entity types and the standard CoNLL datasets. This showcases the generalizability of the proposed approaches.
4. **Co-decoding Improves Performance:** The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, offer consistent improvements by intelligently combining the complementary strengths of annotation projection (high precision) and representation projection (better recall).
5. **Clear Presentation of Methods:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly describes the proposed methods, including the NER models used and the details of the representation projection process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the *combination* and *application* are valuable, the individual techniques (annotation projection, representation projection via word embeddings) are not novel. The core contribution lies in adapting and combining these existing techniques and demonstrating their efficacy within this specific cross-lingual NER context. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but the paper should acknowledge this more explicitly.
2. **Sensitivity to Dictionary Quality (Representation Projection):** The representation projection relies heavily on the quality and size of the initial translation dictionary.  The paper mentions this but doesn’t deeply analyze the impact of dictionary quality variations on performance.  A more detailed ablation study examining this dependency would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While the paper presents quantitative results, it lacks a qualitative error analysis.  Understanding *what types* of errors each approach makes, and *why*, would provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific entity types that are consistently misidentified?  Are the errors related to ambiguity in the alignment process?
4. **Feature Engineering & Model Choices:** The paper mentions feature engineering choices being limited to word embeddings for the representation projection method to ensure transferability.  However, the motivation for the specific features used in the CRF/MEMM models isn't strongly justified. Is there a systematic exploration of feature combinations that could further improve performance?
5. **Co-Decoding Simplification:** The co-decoding scheme is relatively simple.  Exploring more sophisticated integration techniques (e.g., weighted averaging of probabilities, joint inference) could potentially yield further gains.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the representation projection approach, how sensitive is the performance to the size and quality of the initial translation dictionary? Can you provide some analysis or ablation studies exploring this dependency?
2.  Could you provide a qualitative error analysis of the proposed methods?  What types of errors are most frequent, and which approach tends to make those errors?
3.  For the annotation projection scheme, what is the impact of the `q` and `n` parameters on performance across different languages, and how do these optimal parameter values correlate with the alignment accuracy of the comparable corpora?
4.  The co-decoding scheme appears relatively simple.  Have you experimented with more sophisticated integration methods (e.g., weighted averaging of probabilities, joint inference) to combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems?
5.  The paper states the source English NER system is trained on 320K tokens of human annotated data.  How was this dataset curated, and what is the distribution of entity types within it?  Could the distribution of entity types in this training data influence the performance of cross-lingual transfer?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the limitations of the proposed approaches.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key parameters used for each experiment (e.g., embedding dimensionality, dictionary size, data selection thresholds).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. While the individual techniques are not entirely novel, the combination of annotation projection with a carefully designed data selection scheme, representation projection, and intelligent co-decoding demonstrates a practical and effective approach for building NER systems in low-resource languages.  The thorough evaluation and clear presentation make this a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and answering the questions outlined above.
