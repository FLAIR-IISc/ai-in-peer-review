## Review for "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid and well-executed investigation into weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The combination of a thoughtful data selection strategy for annotation projection with a representation projection approach, and a subsequent co-decoding scheme, leads to demonstrable improvements over existing methods. While not groundbreaking in terms of entirely novel concepts, the paper offers a valuable contribution through its practical implementation, thorough experimentation, and clear presentation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The paper addresses the challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages by leveraging resources from a high-resource language (English). The authors propose and evaluate a combination of two main approaches: 1) annotation projection with a data selection heuristic to mitigate noise from imperfect alignment, and 2) representation projection using cross-lingual word embeddings.  A key contribution is the co-decoding scheme that intelligently combines the outputs of these two systems.  The strongest contribution, in my view, is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of the data selection heuristic in improving annotation projection performance, particularly when alignment quality is low.  A secondary contribution is the practical application and evaluation of representation projection for cross-lingual NER within this combined framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over baseline methods across multiple languages and datasets (both in-house and CoNLL). The results are presented clearly in tables and support the claims made by the authors. The ablation studies demonstrating the benefit of the data selection strategy are particularly convincing.
2. **Practical and Well-Implemented Approach:** The proposed methods are grounded in practical considerations – dealing with noisy data, efficient use of limited resources, and combining complementary techniques.  The implementation details, while not overly complex, are clearly explained, making the work reproducible.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets, which strengthens the generalizability of their findings.  The inclusion of both in-house and standard datasets (CoNLL) is a significant positive.
4. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The sections are well-defined, and the technical details are presented in a clear and concise manner.
5. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:**  Cross-lingual NER, especially in low-resource settings, remains a crucial research area.  The work directly addresses this challenge with a pragmatic and effective solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** The individual components – annotation projection and representation projection – are not novel ideas.  The main contribution lies in *how* they are combined and, crucially, the data selection heuristic. While effective, a stronger statement of the novelty within the broader context of cross-lingual NER would be beneficial.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** While the data selection strategy aims to mitigate the impact of poor alignment, the overall performance is still heavily influenced by the initial alignment quality.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more in-depth analysis of the correlation between alignment quality and performance gains from the data selection strategy would be valuable.  Could the data selection be *too* reliant on already good alignments?
3. **Feature Engineering Choices:** The paper briefly mentions feature selection for the CRFs/MEMMs. A more detailed explanation of the feature set used, and perhaps an ablation study investigating the contribution of specific features, would strengthen the analysis.  Why were gazetteers and character-level representations omitted from the neural network models?
4. **Co-Decoding Scheme Complexity:**  While the co-decoding schemes improve performance, the rationale behind the “rank-based” approach isn’t fully clear. A more detailed explanation of why prioritizing annotation projection leads to better results would enhance understanding.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between alignment accuracy and the effectiveness of the data selection strategy?  For example, do you observe diminishing returns in performance gains as alignment accuracy increases?
2.  What was the rationale for excluding features like gazetteers and character-level representations from the neural network models?  Would their inclusion potentially impact the performance of the representation projection approach?
3.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind prioritizing the annotation projection system in the rank-based co-decoding scheme?  What specific characteristics of the annotation projection output make it more reliable in your observation?
4. The paper mentions the complexity of CRFs. Could you discuss the computational cost associated with the training and inference stages of the CRFs and MEMMs, especially when dealing with a large number of entity types?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The phrase "state-of-the-art" when comparing to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) might be a slight overstatement. These are relatively older works.
*  Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as the dependence on a high-resource source language and potential biases introduced during representation projection.
