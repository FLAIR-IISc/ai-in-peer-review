- Summary: Tackles zero-target-language cross-lingual NER using annotation projection with data selection, representation projection via cross-lingual embeddings, and co-decoding to combine them; evaluated on in-house and CoNLL datasets.

- Contribution 1: Practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection that mitigates noisy alignments and improves performance, especially with poor alignment quality.

- Contribution 2: Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages, datasets, and model types (CRF, MEMM, neural) for robust comparison.

- Contribution 3: Co-decoding schemes combining annotation and representation projection, with analysis of their complementary strengths.

- Strength 1: Addresses the real-world problem of labeled-data scarcity for multilingual NER through weak supervision.

- Strength 2: Data selection scheme consistently improves results and is particularly helpful for languages with low-quality alignments.

- Strength 3: Rigorous experimental setup demonstrating generalizability across datasets and entity-type inventories.

- Strength 4: Co-decoding (especially rank-based) yields consistent gains by leveraging annotation projection’s precision and representation projection’s recall.

- Strength 5: Clear presentation and detailed methodological descriptions.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty in core techniques; primary value is adaptation and combination rather than fundamentally new methods.

- Weakness 2: Representation projection’s dependence on translation-dictionary quality is noted but not thoroughly analyzed.

- Weakness 3: Lacks qualitative error analysis to explain common failure modes and per-entity-type errors.

- Weakness 4: Feature choices for CRF/MEMM lack strong justification and no systematic feature-ablation study is provided.

- Weakness 5: Co-decoding is relatively simple; more sophisticated integration (weighted averaging, joint inference) isn’t explored.

- Question 1: How sensitive is representation projection to the size and quality of the initial translation dictionary (requesting ablation)?

- Question 2: Can the authors provide qualitative error analysis detailing frequent error types and which approach causes them?

- Question 3: How do annotation-projection parameters q and n affect performance across languages and correlate with alignment accuracy?

- Question 4: Have more advanced combination methods (e.g., weighted probability fusion or joint inference) been tried for co-decoding?

- Question 5: What is the provenance and entity-type distribution of the 320K-token English training set, and could this distribution bias transfer?

- Additional comment 1: Paper should discuss limitations more clearly.

- Additional comment 2: Add a table summarizing experimental hyperparameters (embedding dims, dictionary size, thresholds).

- Overall assessment: Solid, practical contribution with thorough evaluation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing analysis, error analysis, and parameter/dictionary sensitivity.
