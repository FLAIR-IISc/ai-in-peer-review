This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme and representation projection. The primary contribution lies in the practical demonstration of combining these two distinct approaches via co-decoding, and showing performance gains over existing methods on several datasets. A secondary, though less compelling, contribution is the proposed data selection heuristic for improving annotation projection quality.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), providing a reasonable basis for assessing the effectiveness of their methods. The comparison against established baselines (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) and supervised learning provides necessary context.
2. **Practical Combination of Approaches:** The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, demonstrate a clear benefit from combining the strengths of annotation projection (precision) and representation projection (recall). This suggests a sensible direction for future research, acknowledging the limitations of individual techniques.
3. **Clear Presentation of Established Methods:** The paper provides a concise and understandable overview of established NER models (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) and related work in cross-lingual transfer. This helps situate the proposed contributions within the existing literature.
4. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The problem of low-resource NER remains significant, and exploring methods for leveraging resources from high-resource languages is valuable. Weakly supervised approaches represent a pragmatic solution in many real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methods:** While the combination is sensible, neither the annotation projection with data selection nor the representation projection using pre-trained embeddings are fundamentally novel ideas. The data selection heuristic is relatively simple and lacks theoretical justification beyond its empirical effectiveness. The representation projection relies heavily on the quality of pre-trained embeddings and the linear mapping, without exploring more sophisticated projection techniques.
2. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* the proposed methods succeed or fail. Examining specific error cases, particularly those where the co-decoding schemes struggle, would provide valuable insights for improvement.  What types of entities are misidentified?  Where does the alignment fail to project accurately?
3. **Dependence on Data Quality:** The performance of annotation projection is heavily reliant on the quality of the parallel corpora and alignment accuracy. While the data selection scheme mitigates this, it doesn’t eliminate the issue. The paper does acknowledge this, but doesn’t sufficiently discuss limitations in scenarios with particularly poor alignment quality. The sensitivity to the choice of dictionary size for representation projection isn’t fully explored.
4. **Overly Complex System:** The overall system, involving multiple models (CRF/MEMM, NN1/NN2) and co-decoding schemes, feels somewhat convoluted. Simpler, more streamlined approaches might achieve comparable performance with less engineering effort.  The benefit of having multiple NN architectures (NN1 vs NN2) is not entirely clear.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors made by each component of the system (annotation projection, representation projection, co-decoding)?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the representation projection approach to the size and quality of the training dictionary used for learning the cross-lingual embedding mapping?
3. What is the computational cost associated with training and deploying the proposed system, particularly the representation projection component? How does this compare to supervised approaches?
4. Is there a clear rationale for using both NN1 and NN2, beyond empirical observation that NN2 performs better? Could a single, more carefully tuned NN architecture achieve comparable results?



**Additional Comments:**

The formatting of the tables is a bit inconsistent (e.g. bolding of F1 scores). Minor changes to improve readability would be appreciated. The reference to "cognitive question answering applications" feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't directly relate to the technical contributions of the paper.
