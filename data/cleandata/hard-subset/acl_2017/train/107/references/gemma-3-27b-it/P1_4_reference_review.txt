## Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to transfer knowledge from English to other languages without requiring annotated data in those target languages. The authors explore two main strategies: 1) annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic to improve quality from noisy comparable corpora, and 2) direct model transfer via representation projection using cross-lingual word embeddings.  Finally, they propose co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The work is evaluated on in-house and CoNLL datasets across multiple languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed heuristic data selection scheme demonstrably improves the accuracy of NER systems trained via annotation projection, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. This addresses a critical weakness of prior work in this area.
2. **Combined Approach via Co-Decoding:** The exploration of combining the outputs of annotation projection and representation projection offers a practical improvement over relying on a single approach. The rank-based co-decoding scheme shows consistent gains.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a relatively thorough evaluation across a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), providing a reasonable assessment of the proposed methods' effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:**  Weakly supervised cross-lingual NER is a vital area of research, given the high cost of annotating data for multiple languages. This paper directly tackles this challenge.
2. **Practical Improvement in Annotation Projection:** The data selection heuristic is a valuable contribution.  The authors acknowledge the impact of alignment quality and provide a concrete method to mitigate its effects, which is well-justified through experimentation. The results clearly show significant improvements, especially in languages where alignment quality is poor.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both in-house and CoNLL datasets demonstrate that the proposed approaches (particularly the combined systems) outperform strong baselines, including previous state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016). The consistent performance across different languages strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, logically presented, and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup. The inclusion of detailed parameter settings and results tables is commendable.
5. **Reproducibility Potential:** While not explicitly stated, the paper provides enough detail regarding the models, data, and procedures that it appears reasonably possible to reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components – annotation projection and representation projection – are not entirely novel.  Both approaches have been explored in previous work. The primary novelty lies in the data selection heuristic and the co-decoding scheme, and to a lesser extent, the weighting in embedding mapping.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection approach remains heavily reliant on the quality of the parallel or comparable corpora and the alignment accuracy. While the data selection heuristic improves performance, the method is fundamentally limited when alignment is extremely poor. This limitation should be more explicitly discussed.
3. **Embedding Mapping Dictionary Size:** The authors mention tuning the dictionary size for embedding mapping, but the justification for the chosen size (5-6k pairs) is somewhat weak. A more detailed ablation study or analysis of the impact of dictionary size on performance would be beneficial.
4. **Feature Engineering in MEMM:** The use of a 2nd-order MEMM model seems somewhat outdated given the prominence of neural network approaches.  While justified by computational constraints, it feels like a potential bottleneck. Exploring a neural network-based model for the annotation projection side might yield further improvements.
5. **Error Analysis Limited:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by each approach and the combined system would provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the data selection scheme: Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the parameters `q` and `n` were tuned?  Did you use a grid search or another optimization method?
2.  For the representation projection approach, what impact did different initialization methods for the linear mapping `M_f` have on performance?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required for training the different models and performing the experiments? This would help assess the practicality of the approach.
4.  What types of errors are most common when using the annotation projection method, even *after* applying the data selection heuristic? Are there specific entity types or linguistic phenomena that are particularly challenging?
5.  Is there a planned release of the code and data used in this study to encourage reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to “Figure 1” in section 2.2 should be verified; the paper does not contain a Figure 1.
*   The phrase "co-decoding" is used frequently. While understandable, clarifying this term as "output combination" early on might improve clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. While not revolutionary, the work offers practical improvements to existing techniques and provides a comprehensive evaluation. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through author responses and potential future work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses raised above.
