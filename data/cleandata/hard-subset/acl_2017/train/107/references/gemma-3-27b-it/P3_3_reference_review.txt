## Review of “Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection”

**Summary:** This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic and representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings. A co-decoding scheme is proposed to integrate the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their system on in-house and CoNLL datasets, demonstrating performance improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Practical Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed heuristic for selecting high-quality projected data significantly improves performance in low-resource scenarios, addressing a critical limitation of existing annotation projection approaches. This is a solid engineering contribution.
2. **Combined System & Co-Decoding:** The integration of annotation projection and representation projection, along with the proposed co-decoding scheme, demonstrates a practical pathway to building more robust cross-lingual NER systems. The rank-based co-decoding seems particularly effective.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across multiple languages and datasets (including both in-house and established benchmarks) provides a solid empirical grounding for the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Challenge:** The problem of building NER systems for low-resource languages is extremely important. This work directly tackles that challenge with a practical, albeit not entirely novel, combination of existing techniques.
2. **Data Selection is Effective:** The data selection heuristic is a strong point. The authors demonstrate convincingly that selectively using projected data improves performance, which is crucial for real-world application where parallel data and alignment accuracy are often imperfect.  The experimental results clearly show the benefits, particularly when alignment quality is low.
3. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple datasets, including both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), provides a thorough evaluation. The comparison to existing methods like Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) allows for a reasonable assessment of the proposed approach’s benefits.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodologies. The organization is logical, and the experiments are well-described.
5. **Practical Focus:** The focus on using readily available resources (word embeddings, parallel corpora) and a modular system design makes the approach readily implementable and adaptable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:**  While the combination and data selection heuristic are valuable, the core techniques (annotation projection, representation projection with word embeddings) are not fundamentally new. The paper primarily focuses on *improving* existing approaches rather than introducing a breakthrough innovation.  The paper would benefit from more explicit acknowledgement of this, framing the contribution as a synergistic integration and optimization rather than a completely new method.
2. **Reliance on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection component is fundamentally limited by the quality of the parallel corpora alignment. While the data selection heuristic mitigates this, the authors don’t fully explore or discuss strategies for handling particularly poor alignment scenarios.  What happens when alignment is *extremely* noisy?
3. **Co-Decoding Complexity:** The rank-based co-decoding strategy is effective, but the reasoning behind prioritizing the annotation projection system is somewhat superficial (“annotation projection system tends to have a high precision”).  A more detailed analysis of the error profiles of each system would justify this prioritization more strongly.  Also, the co-decoding schemes could be described with more clarity; the explanation feels a bit hand-wavy.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, a more detailed error analysis would be highly beneficial. What types of errors are the systems still making? Are there specific entity types that are particularly challenging? This would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper could benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the system.  For example, how much does the data selection heuristic contribute compared to using all projected data?  What's the impact of the smoothing layer in NN2?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Alignment Robustness:**  Could you elaborate on strategies for handling scenarios with very low alignment quality? Have you considered techniques beyond data selection, such as incorporating alignment confidence scores directly into the training process?
2. **Co-Decoding Rationale:**  Could you provide a more detailed justification for prioritizing the annotation projection system in the rank-based co-decoding scheme?  Specifically, what are the characteristics of the errors made by each system that support this prioritization?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a detailed error analysis, identifying the most common types of errors made by the system and suggesting potential avenues for improvement?
4. **Ablation Studies:** Can you present ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component of your system, such as the data selection heuristic and the smoothing layer in NN2?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of the representation projection step, particularly for languages with large vocabularies? Is this a practical bottleneck for scaling the approach to many languages?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper uses consistent notation and terminology.
* The references are relevant and up-to-date.
* The English language is generally very good.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses a practical and important problem in cross-lingual NER. While the core techniques are not revolutionary, the data selection heuristic, the combined system design, and the comprehensive evaluation make a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the weaknesses raised above, particularly providing more detailed justifications for design choices and conducting a more thorough error analysis.
