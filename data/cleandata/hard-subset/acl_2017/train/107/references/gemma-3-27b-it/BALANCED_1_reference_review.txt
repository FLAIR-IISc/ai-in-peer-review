This paper addresses the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The authors propose a combination of techniques – a data selection scheme for annotation projection and representation projection for direct model transfer – alongside co-decoding strategies to integrate the outputs of these approaches.  The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a comprehensive evaluation.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme, leveraging frequency analysis of projected entity labels, is a significant contribution. The results demonstrate that selectively using projection-labeled data, particularly when alignment accuracy is low, substantially improves performance. This addresses a critical weakness of traditional annotation projection methods.
2. **Representation Projection with Word Embeddings:** The application of representation projection, utilizing cross-lingual word embeddings, offers a viable method for direct model transfer.  While not entirely novel (cross-lingual embeddings have been explored), its integration into a cross-lingual NER pipeline and comparison to other methods is valuable.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes:** The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, effectively combine the strengths of both projection-based systems, demonstrating improved performance over individual methods and state-of-the-art baselines. The insight that the annotation projection system excels at precision while representation projection offers better recall is well-leveraged.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes extensive experiments on both in-house and publicly available (CoNLL) datasets, covering multiple target languages. This robust evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The inclusion of supervised baselines provides valuable context.
2. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed methods. The motivation behind each technique is well-articulated, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.
3. **Practical Improvements:** The data selection scheme is a practical and immediately applicable improvement to annotation projection. The fact that it improves performance significantly, especially when alignment quality is poor, is compelling.
4. **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) establishes the contribution of this work and demonstrates its superiority.
5. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The rationale behind design decisions, such as using a smoothing layer in NN2 and prioritizing annotation projection in co-decoding, is clearly explained and supported by experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Representation Projection:** While the application to NER is solid, the core idea of using cross-lingual embeddings for transfer learning isn't groundbreaking. The paper doesn’t deeply explore the nuances of embedding space alignment or discuss more recent advances in this field (e.g., adversarial learning for embedding alignment).
2. **Dependency on Seed Dictionary:** The performance of the representation projection approach is heavily reliant on the quality and size of the seed dictionary. The paper mentions a fixed size (5K-6K word pairs) without a more rigorous exploration of its impact.  A sensitivity analysis of the dictionary size and quality would strengthen the findings.
3. **Alignment Quality as a Bottleneck:** The paper acknowledges that alignment accuracy is a crucial factor, but it doesn't propose any methods to *improve* alignment quality. Exploring techniques to filter or correct alignment errors could further enhance the annotation projection approach.
4. **Feature Engineering in MEMM/CRF:** The paper states that the MEMM/CRF models use a template of features but does not detail *which* features are used.  Providing more details about the feature set would allow for better reproducibility and understanding of the results.
5. **Granularity of Evaluation:**  While reporting F1 scores, the paper doesn't provide a detailed error analysis.  A deeper understanding of the types of errors made by each system (e.g., false positives, false negatives, entity boundary errors) could inform future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the specific features used in the MEMM/CRF models?
2.  Have you experimented with different methods for creating the seed dictionary for representation projection (e.g., using different alignment resources or bootstrapping techniques)?  If so, how did those impact performance?
3.  Did you explore any techniques for improving alignment quality, even as a pre-processing step?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by each system?
5.  Regarding the co-decoding schemes, what criteria were used to determine the thresholds or weights used in the rank-based approach?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be slightly improved for consistency (e.g., using consistent subscripting).
*   Consider briefly discussing the computational cost of each approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed piece of research. While the representation projection component isn’t highly novel, the contribution of the data selection scheme for annotation projection and the effective co-decoding strategy are significant. The thorough evaluation and clear presentation make this paper a valuable addition to the literature on cross-lingual NER.  I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above in the author response. The authors should particularly focus on providing more details regarding feature engineering and the seed dictionary creation process.
