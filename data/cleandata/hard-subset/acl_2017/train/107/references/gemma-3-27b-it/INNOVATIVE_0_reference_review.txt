## Review

This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER with no target language annotations, exploring both annotation projection and representation projection techniques. The core contribution lies in the *combination* of these approaches via co-decoding, specifically the rank-based scheme which demonstrably improves performance. While individual components aren't groundbreaking, the empirical demonstration that intelligently combining relatively straightforward techniques yields state-of-the-art results is a noteworthy, and pragmatic, achievement. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection – a simple yet effective way to mitigate the noise inherent in this approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Pragmatic & Effective Combination:** The paper’s strongest point is the successful integration of annotation projection and representation projection. The co-decoding scheme, particularly the rank-based version, consistently outperforms individual methods. This demonstrates the power of leveraging complementary strengths of different approaches, a conceptually sound and practically valuable insight.
2. **Empirical Results & Thorough Evaluation:** The authors present a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (both in-house and CoNLL datasets) which convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach. The comparison against existing state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) provides strong evidence of improvement.
3. **Simple & Effective Data Selection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection addresses a critical weakness of this approach – noisy projected labels. Its simplicity makes it easily deployable and the reported performance gains are significant, especially in low-resource scenarios.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the methods and experimental setup. The details provided, though not exhaustive, facilitate understanding and potential reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** Both annotation projection and representation projection are well-established techniques. The individual contributions – the data selection heuristic and the representation projection approach itself – are incremental improvements rather than fundamentally new ideas. The primary novelty resides in the synergistic combination.
2. **Supervised Baseline Variance:** The performance gap between the best weakly supervised system and the supervised baseline varies significantly across languages. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis of *why* the gap is larger for some languages than others (e.g., linguistic differences, data quality) would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning & Ablation:** While the authors mention parameter tuning, more details on the specific values used and the sensitivity of the results to different settings would strengthen the analysis. Similarly, a more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of each component of the co-decoding scheme (e.g., excluding the rank-based prioritization) would provide valuable insights.
4. **Alignment Quality Dependency:** The performance of annotation projection is inherently tied to alignment quality. The paper briefly acknowledges this but doesn’t fully explore the correlation between alignment accuracy and the effectiveness of the data selection scheme. Demonstrating this relationship would provide stronger justification for the scheme’s usefulness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study for the co-decoding schemes, specifically analyzing the contribution of the rank-based prioritization over a simple confidence-based combination?
2. What is the correlation between alignment accuracy (on your comparable corpora) and the performance gain achieved by the data selection scheme? Can you provide specific examples illustrating this relationship?
3. The paper mentions using a linear mapping for representation projection. Have you explored non-linear mappings (e.g., neural networks) and, if so, what were the results? If not, why?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of parameters (q and n) for the data selection scheme, and how you determined optimal values for each language?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly in scenarios with very low alignment quality or significantly different linguistic structures.
*   It would be helpful to include the computational cost (training time, inference time) of each component and the combined system.



**Overall:**

Despite the incremental nature of some individual components, this paper presents a solid and valuable contribution to cross-lingual NER. The successful combination of annotation projection and representation projection, along with the effective data selection scheme, results in state-of-the-art performance. I recommend **acceptance**, as the pragmatic approach and strong empirical validation outweigh the limitations. The focus on combining existing techniques to achieve substantial gains is a highly commendable approach.
