## Review for ACL: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER without relying on human annotation in the target languages. It proposes two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method improved by a data selection heuristic to mitigate noise from imperfect alignment, and (2) a representation projection technique based on mapping word embeddings from the target language to the source language. Finally, it explores co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of the benefit of data selection in annotation projection:** While annotation projection isn't novel, the authors convincingly show that a simple, yet effective, heuristic for selecting higher-quality projected data significantly improves performance, particularly when alignment quality is low.
2. **Practical and competitive representation projection approach:**  The word embedding projection method provides a reasonably effective baseline for zero-shot cross-lingual NER, achieving competitive results with relatively straightforward implementation. 
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation across multiple languages and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), including comparisons to established baselines.


**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of building multilingual NER systems with limited resources and motivates the need for weakly supervised approaches. The introduction is well-written and provides necessary background.
2. **Effective Data Selection Heuristic:**  The proposed data selection scheme is a significant practical improvement to annotation projection. The rationale behind using frequency counts to assess annotation quality is sound, and the experimental results strongly support its effectiveness, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The analysis regarding alignment accuracy is helpful.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance gains across multiple languages and datasets. The improvement over previous state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL datasets is notable. The inclusion of in-house data offers a broader evaluation.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors provide sufficient detail regarding the models, training procedures, and evaluation metrics, allowing for reproducibility.  The comparison to strong baselines (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) strengthens the claims.
5. **Co-decoding strategy improves performance:** The co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based approach, effectively leverages the complementary strengths of the two projection methods, leading to further performance gains. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Techniques:**  Neither annotation projection nor representation projection are novel concepts. The primary contribution lies in *applying* and *combining* these existing techniques in a specific way and demonstrating their effectiveness in the context of NER. While valuable, this doesn’t represent a breakthrough in algorithmic innovation.
2. **Dependence on High-Quality English NER System:** The performance of both approaches is heavily reliant on the accuracy of the source (English) NER system.  The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t thoroughly investigate the impact of different English NER systems or error analysis focusing on errors originating from the source system.  A more robust analysis of this dependency would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Analysis of Embedding Mapping Quality:** The paper focuses on the downstream NER performance after embedding projection but provides limited analysis of the quality of the learned embedding mappings themselves.  Metrics to assess the semantic similarity between translated words (beyond simply NER performance) would provide valuable insight.  For example, are certain types of words consistently poorly mapped?
4. **Co-decoding Complexity:** The rank-based co-decoding scheme, while effective, appears somewhat ad-hoc. The rationale for prioritizing the annotation projection system isn't deeply explained beyond observation of precision/recall trade-offs. A more theoretically grounded justification would be stronger.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a detailed error analysis of the representation projection approach, specifically identifying common types of errors (e.g., entity boundary errors, incorrect entity types) and potential causes (e.g., poor embedding mapping for specific words)?
2. **English NER System Sensitivity:** How sensitive are your results to the performance of the source (English) NER system? Have you experimented with different English NER systems and/or error-injection techniques to assess this dependency?
3. **Embedding Mapping Evaluation:** What metrics did you use (if any) to evaluate the quality of the learned cross-lingual embedding mappings independently of the downstream NER task?
4. **Co-decoding Rationale:** Could you elaborate on the rationale behind prioritizing the annotation projection system in the rank-based co-decoding scheme? Is there a theoretical basis for this decision, or is it purely empirical?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but some tables could benefit from clearer labeling and potentially highlighting the best results more prominently.
*   The related work section is adequate, but could be more comprehensive.  Specifically, it might benefit from discussing recent advances in cross-lingual embeddings beyond those already mentioned.
* While the experimental setup is detailed, it would be useful to report confidence intervals for the F1 scores to better assess statistical significance.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. While the individual techniques aren't entirely novel, the combination of effective data selection, representation projection, and co-decoding, coupled with a thorough experimental evaluation, makes this work a significant step forward. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
