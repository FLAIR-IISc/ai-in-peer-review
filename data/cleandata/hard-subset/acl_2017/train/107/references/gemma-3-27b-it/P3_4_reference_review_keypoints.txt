- Tackles zero-shot cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation using projection methods and co-decoding.
- Proposes annotation projection improved by a simple data selection heuristic to reduce alignment noise.
- Proposes representation projection by mapping target-word embeddings to the source language as a practical baseline.
- Explores co-decoding schemes (notably a rank-based method) to combine annotation- and representation-projection outputs.
- Shows consistent empirical improvements over prior methods on in-house and CoNLL datasets.
- Demonstrates that frequency-based data selection significantly boosts annotation-projection performance, especially with poor alignments.
- Presents a competitive, easy-to-implement embedding projection approach for zero-shot NER.
- Provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple languages and datasets with comparisons to strong baselines.
- Clearly motivates the problem and gives a well-written introduction and background.
- Supplies sufficient experimental detail for reproducibility, strengthening the empirical claims.
- Co-decoding (rank-based) yields further gains by leveraging complementary strengths of the two methods.
- Core techniques lack novelty—main contribution is their application and combination rather than new algorithms.
- Heavy reliance on the quality of the English NER system, with limited analysis of sensitivity to source-system errors.
- Limited analysis of the quality of learned cross-lingual embedding mappings beyond downstream NER performance.
- Rank-based co-decoding appears ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification for prioritizing annotation projection.
- Requests for additional analyses: detailed error breakdown for representation projection, sensitivity to different English NER systems, independent embedding-mapping evaluation metrics, and rationale for co-decoding choices.
- Minor presentation issues: clearer table labeling, more comprehensive related work on cross-lingual embeddings, and reporting confidence intervals for F1 scores.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
