Summary
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation by proposing two projection-based approaches and smart combination: (1) an annotation projection pipeline with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections from comparable corpora; (2) a representation projection approach that linearly maps target-language word embeddings into English space and applies a single English NER model directly, with no retraining; and (3) two co-decoding strategies that exploit complementary precision/recall profiles to combine outputs. Experiments across 7 languages and two settings (fine-grained in-house labels and CoNLL 4-type labels) show consistent gains, substantial improvements from data selection under noisy alignments, and combined systems that outperform prior cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Universal NER via representation projection: a conceptually bold, practically appealing approach that maps target embeddings into English and reuses a single English NER model for all languages, requiring only a small bilingual dictionary and monolingual embeddings. No retraining per target language.
- Effective data selection for annotation projection from comparable corpora: a simple, frequency-based quality score over entities aggregated to the sentence level, with dual thresholds (q, n), significantly improving results under low-quality alignments (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese; +20.0 F1 Korean).
- Co-decoding schemes leveraging complementary strengths: a rank-based method (prioritizing high-precision annotation projection) and an exclude-O confidence-based method, consistently improving over either system alone (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev).

Strengths
- Novel and impactful direction: The representation projection idea—training one English NER and transferring by embedding mappings—is elegant, scalable, and reduces engineering cost. It cleanly separates representation alignment from task modeling, enabling “plug-and-play” transfer to new languages.
- Strong empirical coverage and consistent gains: Extensive experiments on Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese (fine-grained) and Spanish, Dutch, German (CoNLL) show (i) large selection gains when alignments are noisy; (ii) consistent co-decoding improvements; and (iii) outperformance of prior cross-lingual NER systems, especially on German.
- Practicality and modest resource needs: The mapping uses ~5–6k dictionary pairs, and the selection scheme uses statistics obtainable from comparable corpora with alignments. This makes the approach viable where full target supervision is unavailable.
- Clear precision/recall understanding and design: The authors diagnose AP as high-precision/low-recall and representation projection as more balanced; the rank-based co-decoding directly operationalizes this insight and empirically delivers the best weakly supervised results on multiple languages (e.g., JP 59.3, KO 51.8, DE 69.3, PT 69.9 F1).
- Broad applicability: The techniques are simple, modular, and potentially transferable to other sequence labeling tasks where representations can be shared across languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty beyond adaptation: Linear mapping for cross-lingual embeddings (Mikolov et al.) and projection-based annotation are known; the novelty is in applying them to NER and combining them. While I find the universal NER angle compelling, some readers may view the technical additions (weighted LS mapping, prototype smoothing, rank-based merge) as incremental.
- Dependence on MT resources and alignments: Despite “no human annotation” in the target language, the method relies on phrase tables/word alignments (for the mapping dictionary) and comparable corpora with alignments for AP. The feasibility in truly low-resource settings without MT infrastructure is unclear.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analysis: No study of dictionary size/quality (beyond a heuristic cap), mapping choice (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes vs. general linear), weighting scheme, embedding dimensions, or thresholds (q, n) and their cross-language robustness. Similarly, limited OOV/subword handling analysis.
- Modeling baselines may under-represent current practice: Using MEMMs for AP and feedforward NNs without character/subword features likely underperforms modern BiLSTM/CRF or transformer-based baselines; the paper emphasizes transfer, but stronger monolingual/source models could change the comparative picture.
- Heuristic co-decoding and calibration: The exclude-O rule and rank-based priority are effective but ad hoc. There is no investigation of score calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) to enable principled confidence-based merging, or structured joint decoding with constraints.

Questions to authors (optional)
- What minimal resources are required to build the mapping dictionary in truly low-resource scenarios without phrase tables? Can unsupervised bilingual lexicon induction be substituted, and with what impact?
- Why use a general linear mapping rather than an orthogonal Procrustes solution (which preserves geometry and often improves transfer)? Did you compare the two?
- How sensitive is representation projection to dictionary size/coverage and frequency thresholds? Please provide learning curves and error analysis for rare entities.
- How robust are the (q, n) thresholds across languages/domains? Can they be selected automatically (e.g., via small held-out projected data) without language-specific tuning?
- What is the OOV handling beyond an “unk” vector? Would subword or character-level representations improve transfer, especially for morphologically rich languages?
- Please report per-entity-type metrics and error categories to substantiate claims about precision/recall profiles and to show which types benefit most from co-decoding.
- Did you attempt score calibration to enable purely confidence-based co-decoding without the exclude-O heuristic?

Overall, I value the universal NER via representation projection and the pragmatic, effective co-decoding. Despite some heuristic choices and missing ablations, the paper advances a bold, practical direction with convincing multi-language evidence. I lean toward acceptance.
