Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation. It proposes: (1) an annotation projection method with a heuristic data selection scheme that filters noisy projected labels using an entity–tag frequency-based quality score; (2) a representation projection method that linearly maps target-language word embeddings to English embeddings using a weighted bilingual dictionary, enabling direct application of an English NER model; (3) two simple co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the two systems’ outputs.
- Empirically, the methods are evaluated on four in-house languages with fine-grained (>50) types and on CoNLL (ES, NL, DE), and the combined systems outperform two prior cross-lingual NER baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection that substantially improves NER under noisy alignments by scoring sentences via entity–tag reliability aggregated over projected corpus.
2) A simple, universal representation projection pipeline for zero-training transfer: learn monolingual embeddings, fit a weighted linear mapping via a small dictionary, and run a single English NN-based tagger on mapped vectors.
3) A lightweight ensemble/co-decoding strategy that leverages complementary precision/recall profiles of the two projection views to yield consistent gains across languages.

Strengths
- Clear, modular methodology with realistic resource assumptions: The paper avoids target-language supervision, uses small bilingual dictionaries and comparable/parallel resources obtainable in many settings, and isolates where each component contributes (AP with selection; RP with NN1/NN2; co-decoding).
- Effective, robust gains in low-alignment settings: The sentence selection scheme yields large improvements when projection noise is high (JP +13.9 F1, KO +20.0 F1). This shows the selection metric captures signal beyond raw projected volume, which is a recurring practical bottleneck.
- Sensible and complementary modeling choices: AP (MEMM) provides high precision/low recall; RP yields more balanced recall. The rank-based co-decoding that prioritizes AP entities then augments with non-conflicting RP entities is intuitive and empirically strong, improving the best single system by 1.3–6.2 F1 across datasets.
- Broad evaluation across type inventories and languages: Results on both fine-grained in-house data and CoNLL (4 coarse types) demonstrate the approach is not overly tailored to a particular label space. The method remains competitive with or better than prior cross-lingual NER work on CoNLL.
- Engineering practicality: The representation projection uses a single English model with no retraining per target language; the dictionary size is modest (~5–6k pairs), and the mapping is linear and fast. This is attractive for deployment.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior projection selection and cross-lingual mappings: The sentence selection based on entity–tag frequency is reasonable but conceptually close to prior noise-aware projection/self-training ideas; the embedding mapping is a straightforward weighted least squares without constraints. The paper does not compare against stronger alternatives (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes, CCA, CSLS/hubness mitigation, joint training) or show that the weighting is crucial via ablation.
- Resource assumptions and “comparable corpora” clarity: The AP method depends on sentence and word alignments for millions of tokens and a phrase table to extract the dictionary. It remains unclear what supervision is needed to train these aligners/phrase tables and whether truly comparable (not parallel) corpora suffice in practice. The claim that prior work “did not consider alignment accuracy when selecting sentences” also needs better positioning with respect to related noise-aware projection literature.
- Potential tuning on test for in-house results: For in-house languages, thresholds (q, n) are “reported best numbers,” but no development set is mentioned. If tuned on test, this inflates reported gains. Clear separation of dev/test and selection of hyperparameters is needed to validate improvements.
- Under-specified details that affect reproducibility: Tokenization/segmentation for JP/KO, entity surface normalization for the frequency table (casing, morphology, compounding), dictionary extraction details, monolingual data sizes, and how OOV handling works in RP (the statement about mapping OOV target words to English embeddings “if found” is unclear) are not sufficiently described.
- Modeling choices likely suboptimal by current standards: The English NER uses locally normalized feedforward networks without character/subword features and with label bias; AP uses MEMMs due to perceived CRF scalability issues, though modern sequence models (BiLSTM-CRF, subword/char, multilingual pretraining) alleviate this. While the paper predates some advances, the absence of comparisons to stronger multilingual baselines (e.g., joint cross-lingual embeddings, multilingual encoders) limits the persuasiveness of the claims.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How were q and n tuned for in-house experiments? Was there a separate dev set per language, or were thresholds adjusted on test? Please clarify the selection protocol and provide sensitivity analyses for q and n (e.g., performance curves vs. number of selected sentences).
- Frequency table construction: How are entity surface forms canonicalized across mentions (casefolding, punctuation, morphology/inflection, segmentation in JP/KO)? How do you handle rare entities (appearing once) whose T(e, l) may be unreliable? Any smoothing?
- Alignments and dictionaries: What corpora and supervision were used to obtain sentence/word alignments and phrase tables? If corpora are comparable rather than parallel, how was word alignment achieved? Please quantify alignment confidence used (if any) in selection or training, and detail dictionary frequency thresholds and their impact (ablation with different sizes).
- Representation projection: Why choose an unconstrained linear map over an orthogonal mapping? Did you try hubness mitigation or CSLS at inference? Provide ablations on dictionary weighting and dimensionality (800→300 vs matched dims).
- OOV handling: The text says target OOV words are “projected into their English embeddings if found in the English vocabulary.” How can a target token be found in English vocab? Do you rely on string overlap or transliteration? If not found, how is the UNK vector trained?
- Co-decoding: For the exclude-O scheme, how are token-level confidences calibrated across models? Did you evaluate more principled combination (e.g., stacking with a meta-classifier, constrained ILP/CRF union) or analyze error types introduced by conflicts?

Additional comments/suggestions
- Include per-entity-type performance and error analysis, especially to assess whether the selection scheme biases toward frequent entities and harms tail types.
- Report the amount and domain of monolingual data per language, OOV rates on test, and projected embedding coverage.
- Consider adding language-agnostic subword features (character CNN/LSTM or byte/byte-pair) to improve morphology and OOV handling without sacrificing transferability.
- If possible, compare against a stronger cross-lingual baseline (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mappings, joint multilingual embeddings) and a modern multilingual encoder to contextualize gains.

Overall, the paper presents a coherent, practical set of techniques with solid empirical gains, but the methodological novelty is moderate, several details need clarification, and stronger baselines/ablations would substantially strengthen the case.
