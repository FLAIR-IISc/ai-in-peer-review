Summary
This paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations. It develops: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projected labels from comparable corpora; (2) a direct model transfer approach via representation projection that maps target-language word embeddings into English space using a weighted linear mapping, enabling reuse of an English-only neural NER; and (3) two co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine both systems. Experiments on in-house data (50+ types) and CoNLL 02/03 show the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and approach supervised performance in some settings.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, effective data selection criterion for annotation projection using an entity–tag frequency table to score sentence-level annotation quality, improving robustness under noisy alignments.
2) Direct model transfer for NER via weighted least-squares cross-lingual embedding mappings into English, using only word embeddings in the English NER.
3) Simple but effective co-decoding heuristics that exploit complementary precision–recall profiles to yield consistent gains over either projection-based approach alone.

Strengths
- Clear, practical framework with complementary components. The paper identifies precision–recall asymmetries of annotation projection vs representation projection and leverages them via straightforward co-decoding. The rank-based scheme notably improves F1 by 2.9–6.2 on in-house and 1.3–3.6 on CoNLL dev.
- Solid multilingual evaluation with both fine-grained and CoNLL tagsets. Results span 7 languages, include in-house data with >50 types (relevant for downstream IE), and CoNLL 02/03. This breadth supports claims of robustness and generality.
- Target-language-annotation free with scalable supervision. Both components avoid target human labels. The selection scheme demonstrably helps when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean), validating the quality metric under adverse conditions.
- Methodological simplicity and reusability. The weighted linear mapping with a small seed dictionary (5–6k pairs) and an English-only NER keeps training and deployment simple, facilitates adding new languages, and avoids retraining target models.
- Empirical analysis of behavior. The paper reports precision/recall trade-offs across methods, ablates NN1 vs NN2, and connects improvements to alignment quality and selection thresholds.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to modern cross-lingual NER baselines. The paper does not compare against multilingual pretrained transformers (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) in zero-shot or lightly supervised settings, which are widely used for cross-lingual NER and often strong even without target labels. Without these, it is hard to position contributions relative to current state of the art.
- Limited technical novelty in core components. Entity–tag frequency-based selection, linear embedding mappings (without orthogonality constraints) from small dictionaries, and heuristic decoding combinations are incremental relative to existing literature on annotation projection and cross-lingual embeddings; the main novelty is the particular combination and application to NER.
- Reproducibility and missing details. Key implementation specifics are underspecified: data sources/sizes for comparable corpora, alignment training and hyperparameters, dictionary extraction thresholds per language, normalization/orthogonality of mappings, how unknown/UNK embeddings are trained/applied, and whether confidence calibration was attempted. No code/data release is mentioned.
- Dependence on bilingual resources limits “low-resource” applicability. The approach requires sentence alignments (or at least word/phrase tables) to build projected data and bilingual dictionaries. For truly low-resource languages without reliable aligners or parallel data, these assumptions may be unrealistic.
- Limited analysis/ablations. The selection scheme’s sensitivity to q and n, contribution of alignment coverage/quality signals, effect of dictionary size/quality and mapping choices (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes vs unconstrained, normalization), and error analyses are minimal. Co-decoding is ad hoc; more principled combination (e.g., calibrated confidence, stacking, constrained decoding) is not explored.

Questions to Authors
- How does your method compare to zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuned on English only, and to simple self-training on target unlabeled data using those models? Please report on CoNLL and (if possible) in-house data.
- Did you try orthogonal Procrustes mappings (with/without CSLS) and vector normalization? If so, how do they affect NER transfer vs your weighted least squares?
- How is the UNK embedding trained, and how often is it used at test time across languages? What is the OOV rate per language, and how does it impact performance?
- Can you include ablations on the data selection scheme: (a) using alignment coverage/consistency features; (b) different aggregation (min/median) vs mean; (c) robustness to surface-form ambiguities and morphology?
- Are the confidence scores calibrated (e.g., temperature scaling, Platt scaling)? If calibrated, does the exclude-O rule still help? If not, could calibration remove the need for ad hoc tie-breaking?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong modern baselines (mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot, language-adaptive fine-tuning, unsupervised MT-based projection) and, if feasible, combine your selection/co-decoding with them.
- Explore more principled combination: confidence calibration across systems, ensemble decoding with constraints, or agreement-based training.
- Strengthen the representation projection: orthogonal mapping with normalization, larger but pruned dictionaries (with CSLS retrieval to mitigate hubness), and subword/character features to better handle morphology and OOVs.
- Provide thorough ablations and error analyses: sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, alignment noise, selection thresholds, NN1 vs NN2 contributions; include statistical significance.
- Clarify and release resources: alignment/data sources; code for selection, mappings, and co-decoding; and, if possible, projection-labeled subsets to facilitate reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper is well-motivated, empirically solid, and practically useful, but the technical novelty is moderate and the lack of comparison to current multilingual pretrained baselines makes it difficult to judge significance in 2025. With stronger baselines, deeper analysis, and modest technical refinements, this could become a compelling contribution.
