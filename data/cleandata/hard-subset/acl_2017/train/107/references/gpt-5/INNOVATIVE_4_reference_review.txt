Summary
The paper tackles zero-target-annotation cross-lingual NER by combining two projection-based strategies: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme that filters noisy comparable-corpus projections, and (2) representation projection that linearly maps target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space and reuses a single English NER. Two simple co-decoding schemes further improve results by exploiting complementary precision/recall profiles. Experiments across multiple languages (in-house with fine-grained types and CoNLL with 4 types) show consistent gains over prior cross-lingual NER systems.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Direct model transfer via representation projection: train one English NER on embeddings only, learn a small weighted linear mapping from target to English embeddings, and decode target text with zero retraining. Conceptually bold and practically attractive.
- Co-decoding strategies that yield consistent, often sizable gains by combining annotation and representation projection, improving over both individual systems and prior SOTA on CoNLL.
- A simple but effective data selection scheme for annotation projection from noisy comparable corpora, based on entity-tag frequency consistency and entity count thresholds, delivering large improvements when alignments are noisy.

Strengths
- Novel direction with high practical impact: The “universal NER via embedding projection” is a clean idea that eliminates per-language retraining. Despite its simplicity (one linear map + one English model), it delivers competitive performance and scales to new languages, aligning well with weak-supervision goals.
- Strong empirical coverage and gains: Results across 7+ language settings, two label inventories (4 types and >50), and both in-house and standard CoNLL benchmarks. The combined systems outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), with especially notable improvements for German and substantial boosts in low-alignment scenarios.
- Exploiting complementary error profiles: The paper identifies a clear precision/recall contrast between projection approaches and operationalizes it via two simple co-decoders. The rank-based scheme (prioritizing annotation projection) consistently helps, and the exclude-O confidence variant is sensible and effective when both are high-precision.
- Data selection that matters in practice: The entity-tag frequency reliability and entity-count thresholds are straightforward yet produce dramatic F1 gains (+13.9 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean) when alignment noise is high—precisely where annotation projection often fails.
- Resource efficiency: The representation projection uses small seed dictionaries (~5–6k pairs) and only monolingual embeddings otherwise; the annotation projection requires no target-language labels. This matches the stated weak-supervision setup and lowers the barrier to multilingual deployment.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on bilingual resources and alignment quality: Both pillars require supervision beyond monolingual text: phrase-table-derived dictionaries for mapping and sentence/word alignments for projection. This weakens the “weakly supervised for truly low-resource” claim, especially when phrase tables are unavailable. The paper should clarify resource requirements and robustness when only minimal or noisy bilingual signals exist.
- Heuristic methods without learned combination: The data selection and co-decoding are rule-based. While effective, the paper lacks exploration of learned combiners (e.g., stacking, calibration) or confidence calibration across models, which might further improve consistency and reduce ad-hoc preferences (e.g., exclude-O rule).
- Limited ablations on mapping design: The linear mapping is unconstrained; no comparison with orthogonal Procrustes, hubness-mitigation (e.g., CSLS) or dictionary-size sensitivity is provided. Given that mapping quality is central, a deeper analysis (dictionary size, weighting, dimensionality choices) would strengthen the case and illuminate failure modes.
- Feature sparsity and morphology: The English NER uses only word embeddings (no char/subword/casing features). While this maximizes portability, it likely hurts languages with rich morphology or no capitalization. The paper would benefit from a discussion or experiment showing whether simple, language-agnostic character features could be integrated without compromising transferability.
- Reproducibility details: Several implementation specifics are underspecified (e.g., context window sizes, prototype learning in NN2, exact training data sources/sizes for aligners and phrase tables, hyperparameters, compute). Given the engineering-heavy nature, more detail (or code) would facilitate adoption.

Questions to authors
- What are the precise sources and sizes of the bilingual resources? How are phrase tables constructed (bitext domains, sentence counts), and how sensitive is representation projection to dictionary size/quality? Any results with smaller dictionaries (e.g., 1–2k pairs) or purely comparable corpora?
- Is the mapping constrained (e.g., orthogonal)? If not, did you try orthogonal Procrustes, and what was the effect? Any analysis of mapping-induced hubness and whether retrieval/cosine scaling affects NER?
- For the data selection frequency table T: is it computed only on the training pool prior to selection (no leakage from dev/test)? How many selection iterations or re-estimation passes did you try?
- How are NN2’s prototype vectors initialized/learned? What is the number of prototypes, and how sensitive are results to that choice?
- Could you report ablations on the co-decoding rules (e.g., calibrating confidences, learned meta-decoder) and provide per-entity-type gains to validate the precision/recall complementarity claim?
- What is the end-to-end resource/time footprint to add a new language (embedding training, dictionary extraction, mapping fitting), and how does it compare to re-training a target NER with projection-labeled data?

Overall, I find the representation-projection idea compelling and the combined system’s gains meaningful across multiple settings. Despite heuristic components and reliance on bilingual resources, the work opens a clear, novel direction for zero-target-annotation NER with strong practical appeal. I lean positive.
