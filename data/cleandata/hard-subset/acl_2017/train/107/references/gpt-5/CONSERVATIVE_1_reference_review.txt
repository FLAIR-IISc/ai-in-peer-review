Summary and claimed contributions
- A weakly supervised cross-lingual NER framework combining two projection-based methods: (i) annotation projection from English with a heuristic data selection scheme based on entity label frequency consistency; and (ii) direct model transfer via representation projection, mapping target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space through a weighted linear dictionary-based mapping, enabling use of an English-only NN NER.
- Two simple co-decoding strategies to merge outputs from the two systems: an exclude-O confidence-based scheme and a rank-based scheme prioritizing the annotation-projection output.
- Empirical evaluation on in-house fine-grained NER (50+ types) for four languages and CoNLL 02/03 for three languages, showing that combined systems outperform two prior cross-lingual baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

As I see it, the primary contribution is the empirical finding that combining two relatively simple projection-based systems with a straightforward co-decoding heuristic outperforms prior cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL. Secondary contributions are the frequency-based data selection for annotation projection and the application of a weighted linear embedding mapping for NER transfer.

Strengths
- Solid empirical improvements over prior weakly supervised cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL. The paper reports consistent gains across Spanish, Dutch, and German, with the combined system narrowing the gap to supervised models. This directly addresses a practical cross-lingual setting without target-language annotation.
- Sensible ablations and analysis across two architectures (NN1 vs NN2), two combination schemes (confidence vs rank), and with/without data selection in the annotation projection pipeline. This makes the empirical story clearer and supports the design choices (e.g., rank-based co-decoding often better when AP is high-precision/low-recall).
- Practicality and scalability. Both components are computationally lightweight: MEMMs instead of CRFs for large type sets; a simple linear mapping for representation projection that can be learned from modest bilingual resources; no need to retrain the NER when adding languages.
- Clear articulation of precision/recall trade-offs across components and how co-decoding exploits their complementary behaviors. The observation that AP is high-precision and RP more balanced is well leveraged.

Weaknesses
- Methodological concerns in tuning and selection: for the in-house experiments (Table 2), thresholds (q, n) for data selection are “tried” and the best numbers are reported, but no development set or cross-validation is described. This implies tuning on test, which invalidates those improvements as a fair estimate of generalization. A proper held-out development set or nested tuning is needed.
- Heuristic nature and limited justification of key components. The frequency-based quality score uses weak labels to select training data, which risks reinforcing systematic noises (e.g., alignment biases or source NER errors on frequent entities). There is no statistical analysis or robustness study (e.g., sensitivity to threshold q/n, unseen entities, or skewed frequency distributions), and no comparison with alternative selection criteria (confidence-based selection, agreement across multiple projections, alignment quality thresholds).
- Representation projection lacks rigor and modern baselines. The linear mapping is a straightforward weighted least squares fit with no orthogonality constraint, Procrustes refinement, or neighborhood preservation; there is no comparison against other cross-lingual embedding methods known to work well (e.g., orthogonal mappings, CCA, joint training, CSLS retrieval), nor analysis of dictionary size/quality beyond a single heuristic. Claims that larger dictionaries “might harm” are not substantiated with controlled experiments.
- Resource assumptions undermine the “weakly supervised” narrative. Both AP and RP rely on alignment models/phrase tables and comparable or parallel corpora; for many genuine low-resource languages these prerequisites are unavailable or weak. The paper does not quantify the data/quality requirements (e.g., how many aligned pairs, how alignment accuracy affects each component) nor demonstrate viability in truly low-resource conditions (no phrase tables, noisy monolingual corpora).
- Limited reproducibility and missing details. Critical components are under-specified: NN hyperparameters (hidden size, prototype count in NN2, regularization), dictionary extraction procedure and thresholds, alignment model training/data, and mapping regularization. No code or data release is mentioned. No statistical significance testing is reported. Error analysis is minimal, and there is no breakdown by entity type or length, nor domain mismatch assessment (English newswire vs diverse target data).
- Comparisons are dated and somewhat narrow. Especially for CoNLL, stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., cross-lingual word representation CRF models, multilingual contextual encoders when available) are not considered. Even within non-contextual settings, comparing against more robust bilingual embedding transfer pipelines would strengthen the claims.

Questions to authors
- How were q and n tuned for the in-house experiments? Were test sets used directly for selecting thresholds? If so, can you rerun with a proper dev split and report results?
- Did you try adding an orthogonality constraint (Procrustes) or CSLS-based refinement to the mapping? How do these affect NER transfer performance?
- What is the size and composition of the training dictionaries per language, and how sensitive are results to dictionary size/quality? Please provide an ablation varying dictionary size and frequency threshold.
- Can you quantify the dependence on alignment quality and phrase tables? For languages with no parallel data, can you use weak dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary) or induce lexicons, and how does performance change?
- NN2 “prototype vectors”: how many prototypes, how are they initialized/trained, and how sensitive are results to this choice? What regularization/dropout/optimization settings were used?
- Are the reported gains statistically significant? Please provide tests on the CoNLL benchmarks.

Additional comments
- A small error analysis (e.g., boundary conflicts, type confusions) and per-entity-type breakdown would make the co-decoding benefits more convincing.
- Please clarify whether English and target label inventories fully match in the in-house 50+ label setting, and whether any mapping or collapsing was required.
