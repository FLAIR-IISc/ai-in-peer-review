- Paper addresses cross-lingual NER without target annotations using annotation projection with data selection, representation projection via embedding mapping, and their co-decoding.
- Annotation projection: project English NER labels via alignments and select high-quality projected sentences using entity reliability and sentence thresholds.
- Representation projection: train English-only NER on word embeddings and map target embeddings into English space via a weighted linear dictionary for zero-retraining transfer.
- Co-decoding: combine systems with exclude-O confidence or rank-based fusion to leverage high precision from projection and recall from representation.

- Contribution: practical co-decoding strategy yielding consistent F1 gains across languages (notable improvements on in-house and CoNLL dev sets).
- Contribution: effective entity-frequency reliability and sentence-level selection heuristic that substantially improves noisy projection results.
- Contribution: simple, generalizable representation-projection approach needing only monolingual embeddings and a small weighted dictionary for multi-target decoding without retraining.

- Strength: Clear empirical demonstration that data selection provides large gains, especially when alignments are noisy.
- Strength: Co-decoding effectively exploits complementary precision/recall profiles and gives consistent improvements across multiple languages.
- Strength: Resource-efficient representation projection allowing rapid addition of languages via mapping without retraining.
- Strength: Broad evaluation on both fine-grained in-house (>50 types) and standard CoNLL (4 types) datasets with discussion of alignment effects.
- Strength: Modeling choices favor cross-lingual portability by using only word embeddings and avoiding language-specific features.

- Weakness: Tuning protocol on in-house data is unclear; selecting best thresholds without a held-out dev set may inflate reported gains.
- Weakness: Core techniques (annotation projection, dictionary-based mapping) are incremental; main novelty is engineering/integration rather than new algorithms.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to stronger neural and multilingual transformer baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, mBERT/XLM-R).
- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns due to reliance on in-house resources and insufficient details on dictionary construction, tokenization, and mapping sensitivity.
- Weakness: Lack of statistical significance testing, limited qualitative/error analysis, and shallow analysis of co-decoder design choices.

- Question: How were selection thresholds q and n chosen for in-house data—was there a held-out dev set or cross-validation?
- Question: Can you report sensitivity to dictionary size/composition (e.g., 2K/5K/10K entries) and explain why larger dictionaries may harm performance?
- Question: How are tokenization and sentence alignment handled for Japanese/Korean, and how sensitive is the reliability table to segmentation or inflectional variation?
- Question: Can you quantify inference-time efficiency and added latency from co-decoding versus per-language retraining?
- Question: For CoNLL experiments, what exact external resources (phrase tables/dictionaries) were used and are they reproducible?

- Overall originality: Moderate—combines known methods with useful heuristics; main novelty in co-decoding and selection.
- Overall soundness: Mostly sound but weakened by unclear tuning procedures and missing significance tests.
- Overall comparisons: Good relative to earlier cross-lingual NER work but missing stronger modern baselines.
- Overall clarity: Generally clear and well structured; methodology is easy to follow.
- Overall impact: Practical and useful for weakly supervised settings, but limited by proprietary resources and lack of comparisons to current strong baselines.
