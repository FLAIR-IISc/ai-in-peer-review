Summary and main contributions
- The paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations, building on two established paradigms: annotation projection and direct model transfer.
- Contributions, as I see them:
  1) A simple data selection heuristic for annotation projection: compute entity-level tag frequency from projected data to estimate sentence-level quality q(y), and select sentences with high quality and sufficient entity count. This improves robustness when alignments are noisy.
  2) A representation-projection transfer method: learn a linear mapping from target to English word-embedding space using a small weighted bilingual dictionary, then apply a single English-only NN NER to target text without retraining.
  3) Two heuristic co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the two systems, exploiting different precision/recall profiles and yielding consistent gains.

Strengths
- Practical, broadly applicable setup with no target labels: The methods are lightweight and require resources many groups already have (monolingual embeddings, bilingual lexicon/phrase tables), making them attractive baselines for low-resource transfer. The “no retraining” property for representation projection is efficient and operationally appealing.
- Empirical breadth and clarity: Results on both in-house fine-grained NER (4 languages) and CoNLL (3 languages) are reported, with ablations: with/without data selection; two NN architectures; and two fusion strategies. The paper also details alignment accuracies and shows that the selection heuristic helps most where alignment is weakest (Japanese/Korean), supporting the intended effect.
- Co-decoding is simple and effective: The rank-based scheme that prioritizes high-precision AP and augments with non-conflicting RP entities delivers consistent, non-trivial gains (up to ~6 F1 on in-house; 1–3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev), and the paper explains the precision/recall profiles that motivate it.
- Comparisons to prior cross-lingual NER work: On CoNLL test, the combined systems claim to outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), especially for German, indicating competitiveness with established baselines.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty; largely heuristic: Each component uses known ideas with minimal methodological innovation. The annotation selection relies on frequency-based heuristics; the representation mapping is standard linear regression à la Mikolov (2013b); co-decoding uses ad hoc rules (exclude-O, rank). The paper would be stronger with principled formulations (e.g., calibrated joint inference or sequence-level combination), or theoretically grounded criteria for data selection.
- Reproducibility and resource ambiguity: A substantial portion of the evidence relies on in-house datasets (50+ types), proprietary aligners, and unspecified comparable corpora. The source of phrase tables/bilingual dictionaries is unclear: if they come from parallel data, that weakens the “no parallel data” premise. Key details are missing (tokenization, tagset mapping, training corpus descriptions, dictionary extraction thresholds), and no code/data are promised, which limits impact and verifiability.
- Baselines and ablations are incomplete: 
  - For representation projection, no comparison to stronger cross-lingual embedding methods (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes/CCA-based mappings, joint training, or CSLS neighborhood correction), nor to character-aware or CRF-based sequence decoders that are now standard in NER. 
  - No analysis of calibration or sequence consistency; the decoders are locally normalized (MEMM/NN) and co-decoding may violate BIO constraints if not carefully repaired. 
  - The claim that larger dictionaries harm performance is asserted without a systematic study; similarly, the effect of embedding dimensions or orthogonality constraints is not examined.
- Potential bias in data selection: The frequency-based selection likely favors very frequent, unambiguous entities (countries, famous names), possibly skewing training toward lexical memorization and harming coverage of rare entities/types. There is no error analysis by entity frequency or type, nor examination of domain shift effects.
- Statistical rigor: No significance testing or variance across random seeds is reported. Choice between confidence-based vs rank-based fusion is tuned per language, raising concerns about stability and generality.

Questions to authors
- What is the exact source and nature of the “phrase tables” used to create the bilingual dictionaries? If derived from parallel corpora, please specify sizes/domains and whether this contradicts the “no parallel data” objective. If from comparable corpora, how are alignments obtained?
- How is BIO/IOB consistency enforced after co-decoding, particularly in the presence of conflicts? Is there any post-processing to ensure valid sequences?
- Can you report sensitivity analyses: 
  - dictionary size vs F1 (beyond the anecdotal “5–6K works best”), 
  - effect of orthogonality constraints in M (e.g., Procrustes), vector normalization, and embedding dimensionality, 
  - thresholds (q, n) vs F1 curves?
- How are confidence scores computed and calibrated across AP and RP systems? Did you attempt temperature scaling or Platt scaling to make the confidence-based fusion more principled?
- What is the OOV handling in practice for representation projection (coverage rates, behavior on morphologically rich languages), and how much of the error is attributable to OOV vs mapping noise?
- For CoNLL, are improvements on test statistically significant over the strongest baseline, and how many runs were averaged?

Overall, the paper assembles simple, established techniques into a practical pipeline and demonstrates competitive results across several languages. However, the methodological novelty is modest, some resource assumptions are unclear, and reproducibility is limited. A stronger empirical and analytical treatment (clearer resource description, stronger baselines, significance tests, and deeper ablations) would substantially improve the case for publication.
