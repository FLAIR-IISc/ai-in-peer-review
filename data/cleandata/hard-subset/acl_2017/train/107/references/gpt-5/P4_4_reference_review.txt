Summary
The paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) without target-language annotation. It studies two projection-based approaches and their combination: (1) annotation projection from English to the target language with a heuristic data selection mechanism to filter noisy projected labels; (2) direct model transfer via representation projection, which learns a linear mapping from target-language word embeddings to English and applies an English NER model without retraining; (3) two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine outputs. Experiments on in-house datasets (50+ entity types) for Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese and on CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German show that data selection improves annotation projection markedly when alignments are noisy, representation projection is competitive, and co-decoding yields further gains, outperforming prior cross-lingual NER baselines.

Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1) A simple, effective data selection criterion for annotation projection that aggregates entity-level labeling frequencies to score sentences, leading to substantial gains under low alignment accuracy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
2) A direct model transfer pipeline using weighted linear mapping of target embeddings into English space and an English-only NER model, enabling zero-shot application without target-side training.
3) Empirical demonstration that combining annotation and representation projection via co-decoding yields consistent improvements and surpasses prior cross-lingual NER methods on CoNLL.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results across languages and settings. The paper evaluates on both in-house (with fine-grained types) and CoNLL datasets, showing that: (a) the data selection scheme turns very noisy projection into usable training data (Table 2; e.g., Japanese 41.1→55.0 F1, Korean 18.7→38.7); (b) representation projection is competitive; and (c) co-decoding improves over either alone (e.g., in-house German AP+NN2 rank-based 69.3 vs best single 66.4/64.9, Table 3; CoNLL Spanish AP+NN1 confidence-based 63.4 vs best single 59.8/57.4, Table 4). Top systems outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL test (Table 5).
2) Clear articulation of complementary error profiles and principled combination. The observation that annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall while representation projection is more balanced is supported by precision–recall figures, and motivates the rank-based co-decoding that prioritizes high-precision entities while augmenting recall without conflicts.
3) Methodological simplicity and practicality. Both the sentence selection metric (frequency-based reliability averaged per sentence with an entity-count threshold) and the weighted least squares mapping (Equation 7) are straightforward to implement and require modest resources (monolingual embeddings, a small bilingual dictionary from a phrase table). The approach avoids target-language annotation and retraining for representation projection.
4) Sensible modeling choices and documentation. The paper motivates MEMMs for large label sets and provides model and training details (embedding dimensions, dictionary size ~5–6K, mapping objective with weights P(x|y), two NN architectures with a smoothing layer). It also reports alignment accuracies per language and relates them to gains from selection.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty beyond well-known paradigms. Annotation projection and cross-lingual embedding mapping are established; the proposed advances are mainly heuristic refinements (frequency-based selection; weighted linear mapping; simple co-decoding). The paper’s primary contribution is empirical rather than conceptual; no theoretical analysis supports the selection or combination strategies.
2) Incomplete ablations and baseline comparisons for data selection. The selection scheme is only compared to using all projected data. There is no evaluation against alternative selectors (e.g., alignment confidence thresholds, English NER confidence, entity length/type distributions), nor an ablation disentangling the roles of q (quality) and n (entity count). For in-house data, the best (q, n) values are “reported” without clarity on tuning splits, raising possible overfitting if tuned on test.
3) Representation mapping choices underexplored. The mapping is unconstrained linear regression; the paper does not compare against orthogonal Procrustes mappings commonly used to preserve geometry, or more recent normalization/centering schemes. The observation that larger dictionaries “might harm” is not analyzed; no sensitivity study on dictionary size/quality or weighting is provided. OOV handling is reduced to an unk vector; subword morphology is not considered.
4) Reproducibility details are sparse. Key resources (monolingual corpora sources/sizes, comparable/parallel corpora and phrase table creation, dictionary composition per language) are insufficiently specified to reproduce results. For CoNLL comparisons to prior work, details of baseline training and resource parity are not fully described.
5) Limited analysis of results. Statistical significance is not reported; improvements on CoNLL dev are sometimes small (≈1–3 F1). There is no per-entity-type breakdown, error analysis, or correlation analysis beyond a brief alignment-accuracy observation, limiting insights into when each component helps most.

Questions to Authors (optional)
1) For in-house experiments, how were the thresholds q and n selected? Was a held-out development set used, or were they tuned on test? Can you report performance as a function of q and n and ablate the contributions of each term?
2) How does the proposed sentence selection compare to simpler baselines: (a) filtering by alignment confidence; (b) filtering by English NER confidence; (c) entity-count threshold only?
3) Is the mapping Mfe constrained to be orthogonal? If not, did you try orthogonal Procrustes and/or normalization steps, and with what effect? Please include sensitivity to dictionary size and weighting.
4) How do you enforce IOB/sequence consistency in co-decoding after merging spans, especially in the presence of conflicts? What fraction of conflicts arise and how are ties handled?
5) What are the exact monolingual corpora and alignment resources used for each language (sizes, domains, tokenization)? Can you share more details to facilitate reproduction?
6) Are the improvements over Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL statistically significant? If re-implemented, were identical resources used?
