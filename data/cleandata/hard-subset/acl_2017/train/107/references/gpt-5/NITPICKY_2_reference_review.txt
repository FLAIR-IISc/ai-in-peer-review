Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles zero-resource cross-lingual NER by (i) selecting better projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora via an entity-tag frequency heuristic; (ii) directly transferring an English NER model to target languages by linearly projecting target word embeddings into English space using a weighted dictionary-based mapping; and (iii) combining the two systems with simple co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to improve F1. Experiments on in-house datasets (50+ types; JA/KO/DE/PT) and CoNLL (ES/NL/DE) report consistent gains over individual systems and prior cross-lingual NER approaches.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple yet effective selection criterion for choosing projection-labeled sentences from noisy alignments using entity-level tag frequency statistics.
2) A direct model-transfer approach via weighted linear embedding projection to English without retraining per target language.
3) Two lightweight co-decoding heuristics that leverage complementary precision/recall of the two projections.

Strengths
- Practical and resource-lean pipeline: Both components (annotation projection with selection; representation projection) are simple, language-agnostic, and easy to add for new languages once embeddings and a small dictionary are available. Co-decoding incurs trivial overhead and yields consistent gains (often +2–6 F1).
- Broad multilingual evaluation: Results on 7 languages across two settings (fine-grained in-house vs CoNLL 4-type) demonstrate robustness. The observation that AP has high precision/low recall while NN2 is more balanced is consistent across languages and motivates co-decoding.
- Sensible engineering choices: Weighted least squares for mapping uses P(x|y) from a phrase table, which plausibly improves over unweighted Procrustes when dictionary pairs are noisy/imbalanced. The NN2 “smoothing layer” to mitigate extreme precision/recall imbalance is a reasonable architectural tweak.
- Empirical improvements: The data selection significantly boosts performance under noisier alignments (e.g., +13.9 JA, +20.0 KO F1). Combined systems beat Täckström (2012) and Tsai (2016) on CoNLL test, especially for DE.
- Clarity of the high-level idea: The paper clearly separates the two projections and explains how they are combined; the overall goal of a universal English-space NER is well-motivated.

Weaknesses
- Potential test-set tuning and lack of dev splits for in-house: For in-house experiments, selection thresholds (q, n) are “tried” and best numbers reported (Table 2). It is unclear whether a held-out dev set was used. If tuned on test, results are optimistic. Similarly, co-decoding choice (rank vs confidence) seems selected based on dev only for CoNLL; for in-house this is not explicitly stated.
- Novelty is limited and key technical choices are under-specified: Annotation projection with heuristic filtering is long-standing; selecting by entity-level agreement/frequency is intuitive but incremental. Embedding mapping follows Mikolov et al. with a weighting tweak; no orthogonality/Procrustes constraint, hubness mitigation (e.g., CSLS), or ablation vs unweighted mapping is reported. Co-decoding heuristics are simple rules without calibration or principled training.
- Reproducibility gaps and missing ablations: Many critical details are absent: tokenization/segmentation for JA/KO; vocabulary sizes; NN hyperparameters (window, lr, optimizer, batch size, epochs, dropout/regularization, early stopping); exact feature templates for CRF/MEMM; phrase table source/training; dictionary extraction thresholding; unknown (“NER-trained unk”) training procedure; BIO decoding constraints post-merge; time/memory costs. No ablations for dictionary size, mapping dimensionality (why 800-d for targets), weighting effect, NN1 vs NN2 under identical conditions, or sensitivity to context window.
- Possible biases in data selection and entity memorization: The frequency table T(e, l) favors frequent entity strings (e.g., Estados Unidos), potentially biasing the training toward high-frequency/“celebrity” entities and news-domain distributions. The method does not address string normalization (casing, morphology, multiword variation) across languages; how are entity surface variants aggregated? If not, the frequency statistic may be sparse and unreliable for long-tail entities, undermining recall.
- Fairness/consistency of comparisons: Source English models differ across pipelines (CRF vs NN), and the CRF feature set for AP is not specified (are gazetteers used?). For in-house, supervised baselines’ training sizes vary widely, complicating interpretation. No statistical significance tests are provided. For CoNLL, resources used for phrase tables/comparable corpora may differ from prior work, but comparability is not discussed.

Questions to authors
- Data selection:
  - How were q and n tuned for in-house languages? Was there a held-out dev set per language? If not, please re-run with a clean dev/test split.
  - How are entities canonicalized when building T(e, l)? Do you normalize case, punctuation, inflection, or multiword variants? How do you handle nested or overlapping entities?
- Representation projection:
  - Is M learned with an orthogonality constraint? If not, did you compare to orthogonal Procrustes? Any evaluation of the weighting term’s impact vs unweighted?
  - Why 800-d for target embeddings; is this critical? Please provide an ablation on dimensionality and dictionary size (you mention >6k harms performance—show the curve).
  - What is the source of the phrase table and alignment? Are these resources available for the CoNLL languages, and are they comparable to those used by prior baselines?
  - How is the “NER-trained unk” vector obtained?
- Co-decoding:
  - How do you ensure valid BIO sequences after merging (especially when resolving conflicts)? Is there a final CRF/Viterbi pass to repair sequences?
  - For exclude-O confidence, how are confidences calibrated across systems? Did you try temperature scaling or classwise normalization?
- Preprocessing and features:
  - How are JA/KO tokenized/segmented for embeddings and NER? What is the impact of segmentation errors?
  - What features are used in the English CRF for AP? Are there any language-specific resources (gazetteers, clusters)?
- Evaluation:
  - Please report statistical significance and provide per-entity-type breakdowns. Also clarify whether the same English label set is used across in-house vs CoNLL (you imply separate English models, but this should be explicit).

Given the cumulative methodological and reproducibility issues (especially potential tuning on test, missing details/ablations, and limited novelty), I lean toward rejection despite the promising empirical gains. I would encourage a revision addressing the above.
