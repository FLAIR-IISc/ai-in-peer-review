Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation, proposing two projection-based approaches and a simple ensemble:
- Representation projection for direct model transfer: learn a small weighted linear mapping from target monolingual embeddings to English, then run a single English NER NN on projected target text, requiring no retraining per target language.
- Effective annotation projection with data selection: build projection-labeled data from comparable corpora and alignment, and filter sentences using an entity-frequency reliability score and entity-count threshold.
- Co-decoding schemes that leverage complementary precision/recall profiles: an exclude-O confidence-based method and a rank-based method prioritizing high-precision AP spans.

Main contributions as I see them (in order):
1) A practically compelling representation-projection paradigm for NER that yields true zero-retraining transfer across languages using only a small seed dictionary and monolingual embeddings.
2) A simple, effective heuristic for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data that delivers large gains when alignments are noisy.
3) Co-decoding that smartly combines two weak signals and consistently improves over either component, yielding SOTA cross-lingual NER on CoNLL dev/test versus prior work.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and practicality of universal decoding via representation projection: Mapping target embeddings into English and reusing a single NER model is bold and appealing. It removes per-language training and offers instant support for new languages. The weighted dictionary-based mapping is simple, scalable (5–6K pairs), and doesn’t require parallel sentences for each new language once embeddings are available.
- Strong empirical gains and breadth of evaluation: Results span in-house datasets with >50 entity types and CoNLL with 4 types, across 6+ languages. The approach outperforms Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL, and the combined model approaches supervised performance in some settings. The data selection heuristic yields dramatic improvements when alignment is poor (+13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean), demonstrating robustness in challenging conditions.
- Complementarity exploited effectively: The paper characterizes precision/recall tradeoffs (AP high-precision/low-recall; representation projection more balanced) and designs two co-decoding strategies accordingly. The rank-based scheme is simple yet effective and consistently boosts F1 over the best component by 2.9–6.2 in-house and 1.3–3.6 on CoNLL dev.
- Methodological frugality: The NER model intentionally avoids language-specific features (gazetteers, char-level), making the transfer cleaner and highlighting the contribution of representation projection itself. The choice to use MEMM for many-label training is pragmatic and well-motivated.
- Clear problem framing and reproducible ideas: The data selection metric (entity-tag frequency reliability and minimum-entity threshold) is intuitive and easy to implement; the linear mapping objective (weighted least squares) is standard and well-specified.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of embedding mapping design choices: The mapping is unconstrained least squares; no discussion of orthogonality constraints, normalization, hubness mitigation, or retrieval criteria (e.g., CSLS) that often matter in cross-lingual alignment. Ablations on dictionary size/weighting and embedding dimensionality would clarify sensitivity and robustness.
- Resource assumptions and “no supervision” claim: While there is no target-language annotation, the approach relies on phrase tables and alignment models to build seed dictionaries and projection labels. For truly low-resource settings lacking alignments/phrase tables, feasibility is unclear. A discussion or experiment with induced lexicons or weaker bilingual signals would strengthen the story.
- Calibration and decoding theory: The exclude-O confidence scheme and rank-based ensemble are heuristic. There is no calibration analysis or constraints to enforce BIO consistency across systems beyond per-system decoding. A learned meta-decoder or constrained merging could improve reliability and is not explored.
- Incomplete error and fairness analysis: Little qualitative error analysis by entity type, OOV, or language morphology (e.g., Japanese/Korean segmentation). Comparisons to alternative cross-lingual embedding methods are sparse; the baselines are relatively dated by embedding standards (though strong in NER). It’s hard to disentangle effects of embeddings vs. mapping vs. model choice (NN1 vs NN2) due to limited ablations.
- Reproducibility concerns: Several components (alignment systems, comparable corpora, in-house datasets with >50 types) are proprietary or insufficiently described for replication. Clarity on data sources and release plans would improve impact.

Questions to Authors
- Mapping details: Is M constrained (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) or regularized? Did you normalize embeddings (mean-centering, length normalization) before mapping? Any experiments with orthogonality or CSLS to mitigate hubness?
- Dictionary sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to dictionary size and weighting by P(x|y)? Please provide an ablation varying dictionary sizes (1K–20K) and with/without weights.
- Embedding choices: Why 800d for target and 300d for English? Did you try matched dimensions or different training epochs? Any robustness to domain mismatch in monolingual corpora?
- Co-decoding: Do the merged outputs always satisfy BIO constraints? If conflicts arise, how resolved? Have you tried a learned meta-classifier to arbitrate spans/tags?
- Low-resource feasibility: If phrase tables/alignments are unavailable, can you induce the dictionary from monolingual corpora (e.g., identical strings, numerals, or unsupervised lexicon induction)? Any preliminary results?
- Data selection: How are q and n tuned? Can you report sensitivity curves and how performance varies with alignment quality?
- Resources: Will you release code, mapping scripts, and the frequency-based selection tool? Any plan to release the in-house >50-type evaluation or a mapping to public schemas?

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The representation projection idea for zero-retraining cross-lingual NER, paired with a simple yet effective data selection and co-decoding framework, is a clear, practical contribution with strong empirical evidence. Despite missing ablations and some heuristic choices, the work pushes a useful direction and provides a strong, deployable baseline for low/no-annotation NER. Further analysis of mapping design and resource-light lexicon induction would strengthen the final version.
