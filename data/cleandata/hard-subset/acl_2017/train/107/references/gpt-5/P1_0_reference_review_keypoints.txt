- Paper combines annotation projection (AP), representation projection (RP), and co-decoding to do cross-lingual NER without target annotations.
- AP: filters noisy projected labels using an entity-tag frequency reliability score and entity-count threshold to improve training data quality.
- RP: builds a small bilingual dictionary from a phrase table, learns a weighted linear mapping to project target embeddings into English, and uses a single English NER model at inference.
- Co-decoding: proposes two fusion schemes — exclude-O confidence-based and a rank-based strategy that prioritizes high-precision AP outputs.
- Large empirical gains from data selection on noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese, +20.0 Korean), showing importance of quality-aware filtering.
- Co-decoding consistently improves over individual systems (e.g., rank-based AP+NN2 gains 2.9–6.2 F1 in-house; confidence-based AP+NN1 up to 3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev).
- Best combined systems beat prior cross-lingual NER methods on CoNLL and sometimes approach supervised baselines for some languages.
- Complementarity is well-characterized: AP yields high precision/low recall while RP is more balanced; rank-based fusion effectively leverages this.
- RP is practical and scalable: avoids per-target retraining, uses lightweight weighted least-squares mapping from a small dictionary.
- MEMM for AP training improves tractability with many labels and millions of weakly labeled tokens.
- Experiments include ablations isolating data selection thresholds and comparing NN variants, clarifying their roles.
- Broad evaluation across seven typologically diverse languages, two label granularities (CoNLL vs 50+ in-house), and multiple domains.
- Dependence on bilingual resources (sentence alignments, phrase tables) weakens the “weak supervision” claim and is unclear for low-resource settings.
- Limited novelty: core components (annotation projection, linear embedding mapping) are established; main contributions are heuristic selection and fusion.
- Missing comparisons to other cross-lingual embedding methods (CCA/Procrustes, joint/adversarial embeddings) and recent multilingual sequence models.
- No statistical significance testing reported, leaving variability of reported gains uncertain.
- Lack of error/diagnostic analysis (boundary errors, type confusions, OOV handling, dictionary size effects).
- Reproducibility concerns: sparse details on phrase-table/corpus construction, alignment training, threshold selection, and in-house label schema; code/data release not specified.
- Questions for authors: how phrase tables/alignments were obtained and resource costs per language?
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to dictionary size/quality and whether orthogonality/regularization on mapping was tried?
- Questions for authors: are model confidences calibrated or normalized for fusion?
- Questions for authors: how is the NER UNK vector learned and were character/subword features explored for non-Latin scripts?
- Questions for authors: can code, mappings, and in-house 50+ type schema/training statistics be released?
- Overall judgment: solid empirical work with practical value; leans toward acceptance pending clearer resource details and stronger methodological/comparative analyses.
