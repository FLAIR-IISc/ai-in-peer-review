- Paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via annotation projection, representation projection, and two co-decoding schemes.
- Annotation projection: English→target pipeline with heuristic sentence selection using entity-level tag consistency q(y) and filters for quality and entity count.
- Representation projection: learn linear mappings from target embeddings into English space (weighted least squares from a phrase table) and decode with a single English NN NER model.
- Co-decoding: exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based scheme that prioritizes high-precision annotation projection then adds non-conflicting representation-projection entities.
- Empirical evaluation on 7 language–dataset pairs (in-house: Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese with >50 types; CoNLL: Spanish/Dutch/German with 4 types).
- Key empirical findings: data selection greatly improves AP when alignments are noisy; representation projection yields more balanced precision/recall; co-decoding outperforms individual systems and prior methods.
- Contribution: simple, effective entity-frequency-based data selection for annotation projection yielding clear gains, especially with poor alignments.
- Contribution: practical universal representation-projection recipe using linear mappings into English and a single English NN tagger, avoiding retraining per target language.
- Contribution: lightweight co-decoding exploiting complementary error profiles, consistently improving F1 and achieving SOTA on CoNLL at the time.
- Strength: addresses high-impact no-target-annotation setting and scales to multiple languages and large unlabeled corpora; operationally attractive unified English model.
- Strength: strong empirical breadth across typologically diverse languages and entity granularities, with plausible causal link between selection gains and alignment quality.
- Strength: demonstrated complementarity between AP (high-precision) and RP (balanced), with rank-based co-decoder delivering consistent F1 improvements.
- Strength: methodological simplicity and transparency—components are easy to reproduce and extend, making a useful baseline.
- Strength: fair historical baselines and clear relative gains, notably on German, narrowing gap to supervised models.
- Weakness: relies on external parallel data/alignments and phrase tables despite claiming no target annotation, limiting applicability in truly low-resource settings.
- Weakness: selection heuristic may amplify systematic errors from English NER or aligner because frequency table is built from projections.
- Weakness: limited ablation and sensitivity analyses on hyperparameters, mapping constraints (e.g., orthogonality), embedding settings, and corpus size.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns due to omitted details (corpus sizes, alignment/phrase-table specifics, NN hyperparameters, tokenization) and unavailable in-house data.
- Weakness: evaluation choices risk overfitting on in-house data (best thresholds reported without clear dev sets) and lack of significance testing and diagnostic breakdowns.
- Question: what are sizes/sources of comparable corpora and phrase tables, and how do results degrade with only small seed dictionaries or noisy/absent alignments?
- Question: were constrained mappings (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) or embedding normalization tried, and how did they compare to weighted least squares?
- Question: how are confidence scores computed and calibrated across models for co-decoding, and was calibration like temperature scaling evaluated?
- Question: can you provide sensitivity curves for q and n, and heuristics for choosing them without test leakage?
- Question: were character/subword or byte-level features explored for languages without capitalization/whitespace, and would they help representation projection?
- Question: will code and resources (selection script, mapping training code, learned mappings) be released to aid reproduction?
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — practical, well-motivated combination of simple methods with convincing multi-language results, but needs stronger discussion of resource dependence, deeper ablations, and reproducibility details.
