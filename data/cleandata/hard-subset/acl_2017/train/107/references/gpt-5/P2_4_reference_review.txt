Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles cross-lingual NER without any human annotation in the target language via two projection-based approaches and their combination. (1) An annotation projection pipeline from English to target languages with a heuristic sentence selection scheme that scores projected sentences by entity-level tag consistency (q(y)) and filters by both quality and number of entities. (2) A direct model transfer method based on representation projection: learn linear cross-lingual mappings from target word embeddings into English embedding space (weighted least squares on a small bilingual lexicon from a phrase table) and decode with a single English NN NER model. (3) Two co-decoding schemes to merge outputs: exclude-O confidence-based and a rank-based scheme prioritizing high-precision annotation projection outputs, then adding non-conflicting entities from representation projection.
- Empirical evaluation across four in-house languages with >50 types (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German (4 types) shows: (i) data selection greatly improves AP when alignments are noisy; (ii) representation projection with NN2 yields more balanced P/R; (iii) co-decoding outperforms either system alone and beats prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Primary contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective data selection method for annotation projection that uses entity-level frequency consistency to improve label quality, with clear gains especially when alignments are poor.
2) A practical universal representation-projection transfer recipe for NER using linear mappings into English and a single English NN tagger, removing retraining needs for new target languages.
3) A lightweight co-decoding strategy that exploits complementary error profiles (AP high precision; RP more balanced), yielding consistent improvements and SOTA results on CoNLL at the time.

Strengths
- Practical significance and scope: The paper addresses a high-impact setting (no target-language annotation) and proposes methods that scale to multiple languages and large unlabeled corpora. The unified English NER model via representation projection is operationally attractive for deployment.
- Strong empirical evidence and breadth: Results across 7 language–dataset pairs, including typologically different languages (Japanese/Korean) and two entity-type granularities, support the claims. The improvements for AP with selection correlate with measured alignment quality, providing a plausible causal story.
- Complementarity analysis and effective combination: The observation that AP is high-precision/low-recall while RP is more balanced is borne out in tables, and the rank-based co-decoder delivers consistent F1 gains (2.9–6.2 on in-house, up to 3.6 on CoNLL dev). This shows the approaches are genuinely complementary.
- Methodological simplicity and transparency: Each component (frequency-based selection; linear mapping with weighted dictionary; simple NN taggers; straightforward co-decoding rules) is easy to reproduce and extend, which increases the work’s utility as a baseline.
- Fair historical baselines and relative gains: The paper compares to prior cross-lingual NER methods of the time and shows clear improvements, especially on German, narrowing the gap to supervised models.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external parallel/alignments while claiming “no human annotation”: Although there is no target-language NER annotation, both AP and RP depend on aligned sentence pairs (or phrase tables) and alignment models. The strength of results depends on the availability and quality of these resources (e.g., Ittycheriah and Roukos aligner). This limits applicability to truly low-resource scenarios without parallel data; the paper should discuss this constraint more explicitly.
- Heuristic selection could reinforce systematic errors: The frequency table T(e,l) is computed from projections themselves. If the English NER or aligner makes consistent, systematic mistakes on high-frequency entities, the selection criterion will amplify these errors. The independence assumption between NER and alignment errors is debatable. An ablation showing error patterns and robustness to noisy high-frequency entities would strengthen the claim.
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analyses: Key hyperparameters (q, n thresholds; dictionary size; mapping form) are tuned, but sensitivity plots or principled selection criteria are missing. For RP, the mapping is a general linear regression; orthogonality constraints (Procrustes) are known to improve cross-lingual mappings, but are not evaluated. Similarly, the role of monolingual corpus size/epochs and embedding dimension choices is underexplored.
- Reproducibility and resource transparency: Important details are omitted or only briefly stated: sizes/nature of comparable corpora, specifics of the alignment/phrase-table extraction, NN hyperparameters (hidden size, context window, training regimen), and tokenization/segmentation for non-segmenting languages. Much of the in-house data is unavailable, hindering reproducibility.
- Evaluation choices and potential overfitting on in-house: Table 2 reports “best” thresholds without a clear development set for in-house languages, raising the risk of tuning on test. Significance testing is not reported. More diagnostic analyses (entity-type-wise performance, OOV breakdowns, capitalization effects) would clarify where each approach helps or fails.

Questions to authors
- Data and resources: What are the sizes and sources of the comparable corpora and phrase tables per language? Can you quantify how results degrade when only a small seed dictionary (e.g., 1–2K pairs) is available, or when alignments are noisy or absent?
- Mapping constraints: Did you try orthogonal Procrustes or other constrained mappings? If so, how did they compare to weighted least squares? Any effect from normalizing embeddings before mapping?
- Calibration for co-decoding: How are token-level or span-level confidence scores computed and calibrated across the two models? Did you try simple temperature scaling or class-wise calibration to improve the exclude-O scheme?
- Sensitivity to q and n: Can you provide development-set curves showing F1 as a function of q and n, and a heuristic for choosing them without test leakage? How many sentences are retained at different settings?
- Character/subword features: Given the absence of capitalization or whitespace in some languages, did you explore language-agnostic character or subword features that could still transfer (e.g., byte-level CNNs)? If not, do you expect them to help RP especially on Japanese/Korean?
- Release plan: Will you release code and any portion of the resources (e.g., frequency-based selection script, mapping training code, learned mappings) to facilitate reproduction?

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper combines simple, well-motivated ideas into a practical toolkit for cross-lingual NER without target annotation, with convincing multi-language evidence and clear gains over contemporaneous baselines. Addressing the limitations above (resource dependence, deeper ablations, and reproducibility details) would further strengthen the contribution.
