Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotations. The key ideas are:
1) A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection from English to target languages using comparable corpora with alignments. It estimates per-entity tag reliability from projected data and retains sentences exceeding quality and entity-count thresholds.
2) A representation-projection (direct model transfer) approach: train an English NER (feed-forward NN over embeddings), learn a weighted linear mapping from target to English embedding space via a small bilingual dictionary, and decode target text with the English model without retraining.
3) Two simple co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine outputs from annotation projection and representation projection.

Experiments on four in-house languages with >50 types and CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German (4 types) show the combined systems outperform each individual component and older cross-lingual baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Strengths
- Practical, modular pipeline with clear engineering value. The annotation selection heuristic is simple, requires no additional supervision, and yields large gains when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese, +20.0 F1 Korean). This is a pragmatic improvement to an established annotation-projection paradigm.
- Direct model transfer via linear embedding mapping is lightweight and scalable. Mapping target embeddings into English and decoding with a single English model is computationally efficient and easy to extend to new languages; no retraining per language is required.
- Co-decoding consistently helps. The rank-based and exclude-O schemes are straightforward and produce consistent improvements across languages and settings, reflecting complementary precision/recall behaviors of the two systems.
- Broad empirical coverage. The paper evaluates across typologically diverse languages, two entity inventories (coarse and fine-grained), and both in-house and standard public datasets, providing a fairly thorough empirical picture.
- Sensible ablations/tradeoffs. There is some analysis around alignment quality, the effect of selection thresholds (albeit limited), and the contrast between NN1/NN2 architectures, giving readers a sense of when each component is beneficial.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established techniques. The core components (annotation projection with selection, linear cross-lingual mapping à la Mikolov et al., and heuristic output fusion) are incremental. The sentence/entity-level selection by frequency of projected tags is intuitive but not theoretically grounded, and similar noise-aware selection ideas exist in the projection literature; the paper does not position its selection method against them.
- Missing strong contemporary baselines. There is no comparison to multilingual pre-trained transformer baselines (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R English-only fine-tuning, adapters, or pseudo-labeling) that are now standard for cross-lingual NER. These often outperform projection-based and embedding-mapping approaches without parallel resources, so the empirical claims are hard to interpret in 2025 without such baselines.
- Unclear tuning protocol and potential overfitting in data selection. For the (q, n) thresholds the paper “tries different” values and reports the best per language. It is unclear whether a target-language dev set (human-annotated) was used or whether selection was tuned on test data. Without a clearly defined, target-agnostic procedure (e.g., using only source/unsupervised signals), the improvements risk being optimistic.
- Dependency on parallel resources and proprietary infrastructure. The approach requires sentence- and word-level alignments and phrase tables to obtain bilingual dictionaries, as well as large monolingual corpora. Many experiments are on in-house data (including >50 types), with no release of corpora, aligners, or code, making reproducibility and adoption difficult. The “comparable corpora with alignment” setting is underspecified: are the corpora truly comparable or de facto parallel?
- Methodological gaps and missing analyses. 
  - No statistical significance testing.
  - No sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, mapping choice (orthogonal vs unconstrained), or embedding dimensionality. The claim that larger dictionaries “might harm” is not quantified.
  - Minimal detail for NN2’s smoothing layer (number of prototypes, training dynamics) and its effect beyond reporting scores.
  - Co-decoding may create IOB inconsistencies; the paper does not state how sequence-level constraints are enforced post-merge.

Questions to authors
1) Data selection tuning: How are (q, n) chosen without target-language labels? Is there a target-language dev set? If not, what unsupervised criterion was used, and can you report performance with a fixed, language-agnostic selection policy?
2) Comparable vs parallel: You state “comparable corpora with alignment.” How are sentence pairs obtained for each language—truly comparable (document-level) or sentence-aligned parallel data? Please clarify the source and nature of alignments and whether similar resources are publicly available.
3) Modern baselines: How does your best system compare to zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R (fine-tuned on English only), and to multilingual fine-tuning with projected pseudo-labels? If not available, can you provide at least zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R baselines on CoNLL?
4) Dictionary/mapping sensitivity: Please provide ablations on dictionary size/weighting, mapping constraints (orthogonal vs general linear), and the effect on NER. How robust is performance when the seed lexicon is reduced to 1–2k entries?
5) Co-decoding and constraints: Do you enforce BIO/IOBES consistency after merging? If so, how? If not, how often do inconsistencies occur and how are they handled during scoring?
6) Reproducibility: Will you release code, selection procedure, and training dictionaries/embeddings for the public CoNLL experiments?

Overall, the paper offers practical improvements to established projection-based methods, but the methodological novelty is limited and the lack of comparison to contemporary multilingual transformer baselines weakens the empirical claims. Clarifying tuning without target supervision and adding strong modern baselines would substantially strengthen the work.
