Summary and contributions
This paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation by combining two projection-based approaches plus a co-decoding strategy:
- Annotation projection with data selection: Labels are projected from English to target sentences via word alignments in comparable corpora. A simple sentence-level selection metric averages entity-level tag-consistency statistics computed from the whole projected corpus, favoring sentences with both high estimated annotation quality and enough entities.
- Representation projection for direct model transfer: Train an English-only NN NER that consumes word embeddings; learn a weighted linear mapping from target to English embedding space via a small bilingual dictionary (from phrase tables), then decode target text with the English model without retraining.
- Co-decoding: Two schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) combine outputs, exploiting complementary precision/recall profiles.

Empirical results on in-house data (4 languages, 50+ entity types) and CoNLL (Spanish/Dutch/German) show substantial gains from data selection in noisy alignment settings, consistent improvements from co-decoding, and competitive or superior results to prior cross-lingual NER methods.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A practical, effective data selection criterion for annotation projection from noisy comparable corpora, yielding very large gains when alignments are poor.
2) A simple yet strong representation projection approach enabling direct zero-shot NER transfer via bilingual embedding mapping and English-only decoding.
3) Lightweight co-decoding heuristics that reliably improve F1 by leveraging the complementary strengths of the two views.

Strengths
- Strong practical impact with simple methods: The tag-consistency-based sentence selection is easy to compute, requires no labeled target data, and produces sizable boosts where needed most (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean). The approach is robust and actionable for low-resource pipelines with noisy alignments.
- Complementary, modular design: The representation projection pathway is elegant—single English NN, per-language linear mapping, no retraining. This decoupling makes adding languages easy and isolates engineering complexity. The annotation and representation projections capture different error/coverage profiles.
- Consistent gains from co-decoding: Both exclude-O confidence and rank-based schemes improve over individual systems across languages and domains. Rank-based co-decoding (prioritizing annotation projection entities) yields +2.9–6.2 F1 on in-house and 1.3–3.6 on CoNLL dev, showing reliable complementarity exploitation with minimal overhead.
- Broad evaluation: Results on four in-house languages with 50+ entity types and three CoNLL languages (standard PER/ORG/LOC/MISC) demonstrate scalability across type inventories and domains. The paper benchmarks against two influential cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), and reports improvements, especially for German.
- Sensible engineering trade-offs: Use of MEMMs to handle many entity types and large weakly labeled corpora is justified; the two NN variants (with/without prototype smoothing) are well-motivated and their precision/recall profiles are clearly analyzed.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in components: Annotation projection and bilingual embedding mapping are well-established. The data selection (frequency-based tag consistency) and co-decoding heuristics, while effective, are relatively straightforward, and the representation projection follows Mikolov-style linear mapping. The contribution is more in integration and empirical validation than in methodological innovation.
- Unclear tuning protocol and potential test-set tuning: For in-house data, thresholds (q, n) are “tried” and best numbers reported (Table 2), but no development set is described for these languages. If test data were used to select q/n, results overestimate performance. Please clarify how selection thresholds and co-decoding choices were tuned without target labels.
- Resource assumptions and “no supervision” claim: The approach requires (i) comparable or parallel corpora with word alignments for annotation projection, and (ii) phrase tables or alignment models to extract bilingual dictionaries for mapping. While no human labels are needed, these resources can be scarce for truly low-resource languages; the paper should discuss feasibility and performance when only small or noisy dictionaries are available.
- Limited analysis of biases and failure modes: The frequency-based selection may bias toward frequent entities (e.g., country names) and penalize rare/novel entities, potentially harming recall and diversity. There is little error analysis (entity-type breakdowns, boundary vs. label errors), or ablations (e.g., removing weights wi in Eq. 7, varying dictionary sizes) to expose where each component helps or fails.
- Missing stronger modern baselines and calibration: The confidence-based co-decoding presumes poorly calibrated probabilities; yet no calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) is attempted, which might further help. The paper also omits comparison to multilingual contextual models (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R) or multilingual subword embedding transfer, which are now common baselines for zero-shot NER; even if not strictly “weakly supervised,” a discussion would contextualize the contribution.

Questions to authors
- How were (q, n) thresholds (and co-decoding scheme choice) tuned for the in-house languages? Was there an unlabeled proxy, or did you reserve a labeled dev set? If tuned on test, can you re-run with a proper dev split?
- How sensitive is the representation projection to dictionary size and quality? You mention 5–6K pairs work best; can you provide a curve (e.g., 1K/3K/10K) and show the impact of weighting by wi?
- What resources are needed to build phrase tables and alignments for low-resource languages? Can you quantify performance when only small comparable corpora or induced lexicons are available?
- Did you try probability calibration for co-decoding? Would calibrated confidences narrow the gap between exclude-O and rank-based strategies?
- Any error/type-wise analysis (e.g., PER vs. ORG vs. LOC; boundary vs. label errors) to understand where data selection or projection helps most?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider noise-aware training (e.g., instance weighting by q(y), or label denoising) instead of hard thresholds to exploit more data while mitigating noise.
- Explore subword/character features to help with OOV and morphologically rich languages without language-specific resources.
- Provide run-time and memory comparisons across MEMM, CRF, and NN to substantiate scalability claims with 50+ entity types.
- If possible, release code/scripts for data selection, mapping training, and co-decoding to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall, this is a well-executed, pragmatic study that advances the practice of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER through simple, effective components and thorough evaluation. Clarifying tuning protocols and resource requirements, and adding more analysis, would strengthen the paper.
