Summary and key contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation via two projection-based approaches and their combination:
1) A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection from noisy comparable corpora. It builds an entity-tag frequency table from projected labels to estimate per-entity reliability and computes per-sentence quality q(y); sentences are selected by thresholds on quality and number of entities. This substantially boosts performance when alignments are noisy.
2) A direct model transfer method via representation projection. It learns a weighted linear mapping from target to English embedding space using a small bilingual dictionary from a phrase table, then applies an English NN-based NER (using only word embeddings) directly to the projected target embeddings. No retraining per new language is required.
3) Two co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) that prioritize high-precision entities from annotation projection and augment with non-conflicting entities from representation projection to improve recall.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains across languages and settings, with careful evaluation. The data selection for annotation projection delivers large improvements where alignment is weak (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean). Co-decoding consistently improves over either system alone on both in-house (>50 types) and CoNLL (4 types) datasets, outperforming prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), notably for German.
- Practical, scalable methods that minimize target-language supervision. The representation projection approach avoids retraining per language and uses only monolingual embeddings and a small bilingual dictionary; the annotation projection selection is simple and efficient. These are easy to deploy and extend to new languages.
- Sensible modeling choices and analysis of precision/recall trade-offs. The paper identifies that annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall, while representation projection is more balanced, and designs co-decoding to exploit this. The two NN variants (with and without smoothing) provide useful insight into calibration and recall.
- Broad evaluation with both fine-grained and standard tag sets. Results on an in-house fine-grained label set (>50 types) demonstrate robustness beyond the standard CoNLL 4-class setting, and comparisons to supervised baselines give context on the gap remaining.
- Clear formulations and actionable heuristics. The frequency-based sentence quality metric and conflict-aware rank-based merging are intuitive, reproducible ideas that others can adopt.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in core technical components. The linear bilingual mapping (Mikolov et al., 2013b) and projection-based annotation are established; the main novelty lies in a heuristic selection metric and simple co-decoding rules. While the empirical gains are notable, the methodological contribution is incremental.
- Reliance on parallel/comparable data and alignment tools conflicts with the “no supervision” narrative. Annotation projection needs aligned sentence pairs and alignment models; representation projection requires a phrase-table-derived dictionary. The feasibility for truly low-resource languages without reliable alignments or phrase tables is unclear.
- Incomplete analysis of key design choices and sensitivity. The thresholds (q, n) are tuned but there is no sensitivity or ablation showing trade-offs between quality and coverage, or impact on rare entities. For the mapping, the claim that larger dictionaries harm performance is interesting but unexplained; no exploration of constraints (e.g., orthogonality), normalization, or alternative mapping objectives is provided.
- Calibration and conflict resolution are heuristic without principled grounding. The exclude-O confidence rule and rank-based merging work empirically, but there is no calibration analysis (e.g., reliability diagrams) nor learning-to-rank/stacking baseline; it’s unclear how robust these schemes are across label sets or domains.
- Reproducibility details are sparse. Key hyperparameters (embedding training corpora sizes, NN architecture specifics, context window, hidden sizes, optimization details), dictionary construction thresholds, and alignment/comparable corpora sources are not fully specified. No code/data release plan is mentioned, which limits verification.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How are entity surface forms canonicalized in the frequency table across tokenization and casing (especially across scripts)? Does the scheme disproportionately filter rare or morphologically variant entities, and what is the impact on long-tail recall?
- Threshold tuning: How are q and n selected without target-language labeled data? Do you use a small dev set, proxy metrics, or cross-validation on projected labels? Please include sensitivity plots for q and n.
- Embedding mapping: What are the sizes and sources of the monolingual corpora per language? Why do larger dictionaries hurt mapping quality? Did you test orthogonal Procrustes, mean-centering/whitening, or vocabulary normalization? Any comparison to joint cross-lingual embedding training?
- Dictionary/bitext requirements: For languages without phrase tables, can you build the dictionary from weak signals (e.g., seed lexicons, identical strings) and how does performance degrade? What is the minimal dictionary size to retain most of the gains?
- Label set transfer: For the in-house >50 types, are tag sets identical across languages? If English lacks a category commonly expressed in the target, how is this handled?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting error analyses (boundary vs type errors; per-entity-type F1; rare vs frequent entities) to better understand where each component helps.
- A learned fusion (e.g., stacking with simple features: span length, source agreement, model confidence) might outperform the heuristic co-decoding while remaining lightweight.
- Training a character-level component that transfers (e.g., Unicode categories, script features) could improve OOV handling in representation projection without language-specific resources.
