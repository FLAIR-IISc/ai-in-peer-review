Summary
The paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations via two projection-based methods and their combination. (1) Annotation projection: apply an English NER model to English side of comparable sentence pairs and project tags to the target using word alignments. A heuristic data selection strategy ranks target-language sentences by a quality score derived from consistency of entity-tag assignments across the corpus and selects sentences with high average quality and sufficient entity coverage. (2) Representation projection: learn a weighted linear mapping from target-language embeddings to English using a small bilingual dictionary extracted from phrase tables; run a single English NER neural model on projected target embeddings. Two co-decoding schemes combine both systems’ predictions: an exclude-O confidence-based rule and a rank-based rule that prioritizes the (high-precision) annotation-projection outputs and augments with non-conflicting entities from representation projection. Experiments on in-house datasets (4 languages, >50 types) and CoNLL (ES/NL/DE, 4 types) show gains from data selection and from co-decoding; the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Contributions (as assessed)
1) An effective, simple data selection criterion for annotation projection from noisy comparable corpora, based on corpus-level consistency of entity-tag assignments, yielding large gains when alignments are noisy.
2) A direct model transfer method for NER via linear cross-lingual embedding mapping and an English-only NER with only word-embedding features, eliminating target-side training.
3) Two lightweight co-decoding strategies that exploit complementary precision/recall profiles to improve over either projection method.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: Both projection approaches use readily available resources (monolingual corpora, small bilingual dictionaries/phrase tables, sentence alignments) and avoid target-side annotations or retraining. The methods are computationally light (linear mapping; MEMM/FFNN architectures).
- Effective data selection: The corpus-consistency quality score and entity-count filter substantially improve annotation projection when alignment F1 is low (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 on JA/KO). This addresses a common pain point of noisy projection from comparable corpora.
- Complementary systems and co-decoding: The analysis that AP is high-precision/low-recall while representation projection is more balanced is supported by results, and the rank-based co-decoding reliably improves F1 by 2.9–6.2 on in-house and up to 3.6 on CoNLL development.
- Broad evaluation: Results are reported on multiple languages and two settings (fine-grained in-house, coarse-grained CoNLL), with consistent gains from both the selection heuristic and co-decoding. The combined systems outperform two established cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL test and approach supervised performance in some settings.
- Clear problem setup and method descriptions: The formulation of MEMM/CRF/NN models, the mapping objective (weighted least squares), and the co-decoding rules are clearly specified and easy to reproduce conceptually.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to current literature: Linear cross-lingual embedding mappings using small dictionaries and direct model transfer are established; the proposed mapping adds weights but otherwise follows prior work. The data selection and co-decoding strategies are heuristic. The main novelty lies in applying these elements to NER and combining them, which is incremental by today’s standards.
- Missing comparisons to stronger modern baselines: The paper does not compare to multilingual contextual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) for zero-shot NER, or to CRF/BiLSTM-CRF transfers with cross-lingual subword features. Without such baselines, it is difficult to assess competitiveness in a contemporary setting.
- Reproducibility and detail gaps: Critical details are under-specified: size/source/domain of monolingual and comparable corpora; construction of sentence alignments on comparable (not parallel) data; exact alignment resources; how multiword/one-to-many alignments are handled in projection; tokenization and entity canonicalization for the frequency table; smoothing or backoff for rare entities; threshold selection protocol for q and n (whether tuned on target dev sets); hyperparameters for NN models; whether mapping was constrained (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes). No statistical significance testing is reported.
- Analysis and ablations: There is no ablation on dictionary size and weighting, effect of orthogonality constraint, number of epochs, or sensitivity to the quality thresholds. The claim that 5–6k dictionary pairs are optimal is not substantiated by curves. Error analyses (e.g., boundary vs type errors, language/script effects) are limited.
- Resource assumptions: The approach requires phrase tables or alignment models to build dictionaries and align comparable corpora, which may not be available for truly low-resource languages. The dependency and cost of obtaining these resources are not quantified.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Data selection: How are q and n chosen per language (tuned on target dev sets or fixed heuristics)? Is there any smoothing of T(e,l) for rare entities, and how are entities normalized (casing, morphology)? What happens for sentences with no entities?
- Alignments and corpora: What are the sources, sizes, and domains of the comparable corpora and alignment models? How are alignments produced for comparable (non-parallel) pairs, and how are many-to-many alignments handled during projection?
- Embedding mapping: Did you try orthogonal Procrustes or other constraints/regularization on M to preserve geometry? Please provide an ablation on dictionary size and on the weighting scheme (wi). How sensitive is performance to monolingual corpus size and embedding dimensionality?
- Models and features: Why restrict to word embeddings only? Did you experiment with character/subword features or cross-lingual subword embeddings to mitigate OOV/morphology issues?
- Co-decoding: How are conflicts resolved when both systems predict different types with the same span? Have you considered learning a meta-decoder or calibration to make confidences comparable?
- Evaluation: Are gains statistically significant? Can you provide detailed dataset statistics and code/resources to facilitate reproducibility? How do results compare against multilingual transformer zero-shot baselines on CoNLL?
