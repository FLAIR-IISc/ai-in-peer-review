Summary
The paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without any labeled data in target languages. It studies two projection-based pathways and their combination:
- Annotation projection: apply an English NER model to aligned English sentences and project entity spans/tags to target sentences; propose a heuristic data selection method that scores sentence quality via entity–tag frequency consistency and enforces minimum entity count per sentence.
- Representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target to English embedding space using a small bilingual dictionary (weighted least squares), then run a single English NER neural model on mapped target embeddings.
- Co-decoding: combine outputs from the two systems via (i) an exclude-O confidence comparison and (ii) a rank-based heuristic that prioritizes annotation-projection detections and augments with non-conflicting entities from representation projection.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective data selection criterion for annotation projection that substantially improves performance under low alignment quality by leveraging global entity–tag frequency reliability scores.
2) A practical representation-projection pipeline for direct model transfer that uses only word embeddings and a small weighted bilingual lexicon to map target words into the English space.
3) Co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based priority) that reliably improve over either projection method alone across several languages and datasets.

Strengths
- Solid empirical improvements from data selection under noisy alignments. On in-house Japanese and Korean, selecting sentences with high entity–tag reliability and minimum entities yields large gains (+13.9 and +20.0 F1 over using all projected data), clearly supporting the utility of the selection heuristic when projection noise is high.
- Complementary transfer strategies with consistent, combined gains. The paper demonstrates that annotation projection tends to be high-precision/low-recall, while representation projection is more balanced. The rank-based co-decoding leverages this complementarity, producing the best weakly supervised results in most in-house settings and meaningful gains (≈2–6 F1) over single systems.
- Careful multilingual evaluation on both fine-grained (50+ types) in-house data and CoNLL 02/03 (4 types), across typologically diverse languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese; Spanish, Dutch, German). This provides a reasonably broad picture of applicability.
- Practicality and scalability. The representation projection approach avoids retraining per target language; mapping requires only inexpensive bilingual lexica from phrase tables and monolingual embeddings. This is attractive for quick deployment to new languages.
- Comparisons against prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL indicate the combined system is competitive or better, especially for German.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong modern baselines and architectures. The study omits comparisons to transformer-based multilingual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) used in zero-shot or translate-train settings, which are now standard for cross-lingual NER. The main NER architectures (MEMM/FFNN) are relatively dated, and it is unclear whether conclusions hold versus contemporary BiLSTM-CRF or transformer encoders with CRF decoding.
- Potential tuning on test data / lack of clear validation protocol for in-house experiments. For data selection thresholds (q, n), the paper states “we have tried different threshold parameters and reported the best numbers” for Table 2. There is no mention of an in-house development set, raising concerns about test set tuning and overestimation of gains. A principled selection via dev sets or cross-validation is necessary.
- Insufficient methodological detail and ablations for representation mapping. The linear mapping lacks standard constraints (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) or regularization; no analysis on whether centering/normalization/hubness mitigation was used; no ablation on dictionary size/quality or weighting strategy. Given the sensitivity of cross-lingual mapping quality, these choices could materially affect NER performance.
- Reproducibility concerns and reliance on in-house resources. Comparable corpora, alignment models, phrase tables, and the 50+ type tagset are not described sufficiently or released. Without public data/resources, results on in-house sets are hard to verify; only CoNLL experiments are broadly reproducible.
- Limited analysis of the data selection heuristic and its risks. The reliability table can bias toward very frequent entities and majority-type labels, potentially suppressing rare/ambiguous entities (e.g., Apple ORG vs PRODUCT), and it does not explicitly model sentence-level alignment quality or English NER uncertainty. Error analyses probing entity frequency/ambiguity effects are missing.
- Evaluation/reporting gaps. No statistical significance tests; no calibration analysis for co-decoding (confidence comparability is a known issue the paper acknowledges); tokenization/segmentation details (especially for Japanese/Korean) are not described; and it’s unclear how “UNK” vectors are trained/used.

Questions to Authors
- In-house tuning: How were (q, n) thresholds selected for data selection on in-house languages? Was there a separate dev set, or were thresholds tuned on test? If no dev set, can you provide cross-validation or a held-out split to avoid test leakage?
- Mapping details: Did you center and length-normalize embeddings before learning Mfe? Did you try orthogonality constraints (e.g., Procrustes)? Any regularization? Please report ablations on dictionary size (1k–50k), weighting, and constraints, and their effect on NER.
- Lexicon/alignments: What corpora and toolchains produced the phrase tables/dictionaries and comparable-corpus alignments? What are their sizes/domains, and how do these domains match the NER test sets?
- Tokenization/segmentation: How are Japanese/Korean tokenized? Are subword features used? Do segmentation errors impact annotation projection boundaries, and if so, how are conflicts resolved?
- Unknown handling: You mention projecting words not in target vocabulary “into English embeddings if found in the English vocabulary, or into an NER-trained unk vector otherwise.” Please clarify this mechanism and how the UNK vector is trained.
- Co-decoding calibration: Did you attempt any calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) to improve comparability of probabilities across systems? How sensitive are the co-decoding results to confidence thresholding?
- Comparisons to modern baselines: Can you add zero-shot baselines with multilingual transformers (e.g., fine-tune XLM-R/mBERT on English, evaluate on target), and possibly translate-train/translate-test setups, to contextualize your approach against current practice?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add strong baselines (mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot; translate-train; BiLSTM-CRF) and report significance tests.
- Establish a clean validation protocol for threshold tuning on in-house data; avoid using test sets for model selection.
- Strengthen the mapping with well-studied practices (normalization; orthogonal constraints) and provide detailed ablations.
- Enhance the data selection with sentence-level alignment quality, English NER confidence, and entity ambiguity modeling; include analyses of frequency/ambiguity effects.
- Improve reproducibility: release code, dictionaries, and as much data/preprocessing detail as possible; at minimum, provide scripts to reconstruct CoNLL experiments.
