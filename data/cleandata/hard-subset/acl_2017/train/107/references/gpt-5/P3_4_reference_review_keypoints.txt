- Proposed two complementary projection-based methods (annotation projection with data selection; representation projection via linear embedding mapping) plus two simple co-decoding ensemble schemes.  
- Annotation projection: heuristic per-entity tag-frequency consistency scoring + minimum-entities filter to select higher-quality projected training sentences.  
- Representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target to English embeddings using a weighted bilingual dictionary and apply an English-only NER model without per-language retraining.  
- Co-decoding: exclude-O confidence-based selection and a rank-based scheme that favors high-precision AP and augments with non-conflicting RP entities.  
- Practical for low-resource targets: data selection mitigates noisy projection (large F1 gains for Japanese/Korean) and RP removes retraining costs.  
- Complementary error profiles: AP is high-precision/low-recall while RP is more balanced; their combination gives consistent F1 improvements.  
- Clear, reproducible methodology using standard models (MEMM/CRF for AP, feedforward NN for RP) and explicit hyperparameters.  
- Broad evaluation across multiple languages and label granularities, with combined weakly supervised systems approaching supervised performance in some cases.  
- Missing comparisons to modern multilingual pretrained transformers (mBERT/XLM-R) and contextual embedding baselines, weakening claims of competitiveness.  
- Limited novelty: methods are largely known (annotation projection, Mikolov-style mapping); data selection and co-decoding are simple heuristics.  
- Possible tuning/evaluation leakage: unclear whether in-house thresholds (q, n) were chosen on held-out dev sets or on test data.  
- Resource assumptions limit applicability: approach still requires parallel/comparable corpora, aligners, phrase tables, and robust tokenization for languages like Japanese/Korean.  
- RP mapping choices concerning non-orthogonal WLS and 800→300 dimensional mismatch may distort geometry; no comparisons to orthogonal Procrustes or other mappings.  
- Insufficient ablations and analyses: no study of dictionary size/quality, domain mismatch, OOV handling beyond a single unk vector, or effect of segmentation/subword representations.  
- Heuristic conflict resolution and lack of BIO/legal-span enforcement or global decoding in co-decoder; no calibration of confidence scores across systems.  
- Question on tuning protocol: were q and n selected on held-out development sets for in-house languages?  
- Question on resources: what exact data/tools produced weighted bilingual dictionaries and aligners (MT phrase tables vs. extracted parallel corpora)?  
- Question on mapping: why non-orthogonal mapping and mismatched dims; were orthogonal/matching-dim or normalization/CSLS tried?  
- Question on tokenization: how were Japanese/Korean tokenized and were subword/character representations evaluated?  
- Question on calibration/legal spans: do you enforce BIO constraints or calibrate AP/RP probabilities during co-decoding?  
- Question on modern baselines: how does the combined system compare to zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R, and can AP/RP signals integrate with such models?  
- Suggestion: provide detailed error analysis (boundary vs type errors, frequent vs rare entities) to quantify selection bias.  
- Suggestion: include stronger RP baselines and mapping ablations (orthogonal mappings, joint multilingual embeddings, CSLS) and vary dictionary size/quality.  
- Suggestion: explore principled ensembling (learning-to-rank/stacking, joint decoding with BIO constraints, CRF lattice combination) and clarify data/normalization procedures to avoid leakage.
