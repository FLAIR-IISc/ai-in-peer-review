Summary
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations via two complementary projection strategies and a simple combination scheme. (1) It proposes an annotation-projection pipeline from English to a target language with a heuristic data selection method that scores target sentences using tag consistency across occurrences of the same surface entity, selecting sentences with both high “quality” and sufficient number of entities. (2) It proposes a representation-projection (direct model transfer) approach: learn monolingual embeddings, estimate a weighted linear mapping from target to English using a small bilingual lexicon extracted from a phrase table, project target text into the English embedding space, and apply an English NN-based NER. (3) It introduces two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and a rank-based scheme that prioritizes annotation-projection spans) to combine outputs. Experiments on in-house (fine-grained, >50 types) and CoNLL (4 types) datasets show that co-decoding improves over each component and outperforms prior cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective data selection criterion for annotation projection that leverages cross-sentence entity-tag frequency consistency to mitigate alignment noise in comparable/parallel corpora.
2) A practical representation-projection transfer setup for NER using weighted linear bilingual mappings plus a minimal English NN NER that uses only word embeddings.
3) A lightweight, effective co-decoding procedure that systematically leverages complementary precision/recall profiles of the two projection approaches.

Strengths
- Clear complementary design and solid empirical gains: The two projection approaches have distinct error profiles (AP high precision/low recall; representation projection more balanced), and the proposed rank-based co-decoding consistently improves F1 (e.g., +2.9–6.2 F1 on in-house; +1.3–3.6 on CoNLL dev over best component). The combined method yields competitive performance against stronger supervised targets (notably close on Korean/German in-house).
- Pragmatic weak supervision with few assumptions: The representation-projection approach requires only monolingual corpora and a small bilingual lexicon (~5–6k pairs), and no target annotations or retraining per language. The annotation-projection selection method is simple, interpretable, and demonstrably beneficial especially under noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
- Sensible engineering choices and analyses: The use of MEMMs for speed with many labels, linear mapping with weighted least squares (leveraging P(x|y)), exploration of two NN architectures with/without a prototype smoothing layer, and practical guardrails (exclude-O preference) are well-motivated and reflected in results.
- Breadth of evaluation: Both in-house fine-grained and standard CoNLL settings; comparisons to relevant cross-lingual NER baselines; per-language discussion tying improvements to alignment quality.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and underdeveloped learning components: The core techniques (annotation projection; linear bilingual mapping; simple feedforward NNs) are well-established. The new elements (sentence selection by majority-consistency and co-decoding heuristics) are conceptually straightforward and could be strengthened by principled learning (e.g., learned confidence calibration, meta-decoder/stacking, or discriminative selection models).
- Resource assumptions and “low-resource” claim: The approach relies on an alignment model and phrase tables to extract bilingual dictionaries and sentence/word alignments. For truly low-resource languages, these may be unavailable or very noisy. The paper mentions “comparable corpora” yet evaluates with alignment setups typical of parallel corpora; details about building alignments for comparable data are underspecified, and the generality to truly non-parallel settings is unclear.
- Potential selection bias and missing ablations: In Table 2, thresholds (q, n) are “tried” and the best reported; it is unclear what development data guided this choice for in-house languages (risk of tuning on test). There is no statistical significance testing. Important ablations are missing: effect of dictionary size and weighting; mapping dimensionalities; impact of the prototype smoothing layer; sensitivity to alignment quality; analysis by entity frequency/length; robustness to tokenization/morphology.
- Context-insensitive representations for a context-sensitive task: Mapping static word embeddings cross-lingually and using a locally normalized NN ignores context-specific senses and subword signals crucial for NER, especially in morphologically rich languages. While the authors deliberately avoid char/gazetteers to keep language independence, even language-agnostic subword features would be applicable. This likely caps performance and limits generalization beyond the evaluated languages.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps: Key training details are missing or too high-level (monolingual corpus sizes; word2vec hyperparameters; selection of “comparable” vs parallel corpora and their alignment pipelines; NN training details/regularization; decoding thresholds). The co-decoding “confidence” relies on heterogeneous scores from MEMM vs NN; no calibration method is described.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How were (q, n) chosen for in-house experiments—was there a target-language development split, or were thresholds selected post hoc on test? Can you report performance curves vs q and n?
- Comparable vs parallel: What are the sources of your “comparable corpora,” and how are sentence/word alignments obtained for non-parallel data? Do results materially change with truly comparable (non-aligned) inputs?
- Dictionary creation: How sensitive is representation transfer to dictionary size/quality and the weighting by P(x|y)? Can you provide ablations (e.g., 1k/3k/10k pairs; unweighted vs weighted)?
- Calibration/combination: Did you attempt simple calibration (e.g., Platt/temperature scaling) or a learned meta-decoder to fuse the two systems, and how does that compare to your heuristics?
- Morphology and rare entities: How does the selection scheme handle inflected or multiword variants (string matching of entities across sentences)? Any lemmatization or normalization? Analysis of performance by entity frequency/length?

Overall assessment
The paper offers a well-executed, practical combination of two classic projection paradigms with a simple yet effective selection and fusion strategy, backed by solid empirical improvements across multiple languages and datasets. While methodological novelty is modest and some evaluation/design choices warrant stronger justification and ablation, the contributions are useful and likely to be impactful for practitioners needing target-annotation-free NER. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors address tuning/protocol clarity, add key ablations, and better substantiate claims about applicability to comparable corpora and low-resource settings.
