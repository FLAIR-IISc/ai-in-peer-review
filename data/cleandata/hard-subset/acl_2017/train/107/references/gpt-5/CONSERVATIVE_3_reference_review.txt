Summary and contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two weakly supervised strategies and their combination. The three main contributions, in order of strength as I view them, are:
1) A simple yet effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection from English to target languages using comparable corpora, where per-entity majority statistics induce a sentence-level quality score; this reduces noise when alignment accuracy is low and yields sizable F1 gains.
2) A direct model transfer method based on linear cross-lingual embedding mapping (target embeddings projected into English space) that enables reuse of a single English NER model without retraining per language.
3) Two lightweight co-decoding heuristics (exclude-O confidence and a rank-based merger prioritizing high-precision AP spans) that consistently improve over either component alone across multiple languages and datasets.

Strengths
- Practical problem setting and resource footprint: The focus on zero target-language annotation is important and pragmatic. Both projection-based approaches use broadly available resources (monolingual embeddings, small bilingual dictionaries/phrase tables, comparable corpora) and modest engineering, which is attractive for bootstrapping NER in new languages.
- Broad multilingual evaluation: Experiments on four in-house languages with >50 entity types and on CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German with standard four types show consistent improvements. The rank-based co-decoding boosts F1 by 2.9–6.2 on in-house data and the confidence-based scheme adds 1.3–3.6 on CoNLL dev, with favorable test results vs two prior cross-lingual baselines.
- Clear precision/recall trade-off analysis: The paper documents that annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall while representation projection is more balanced; the co-decoding exploits this complementarity sensibly. The data-selection heuristic provides clear gains when alignments are noisier (JP/KR).
- Method simplicity and portability: The linear mapping (weighted least squares) and majority-based sentence scoring are straightforward, interpretable, and easy to implement. Not needing retraining for new targets is appealing operationally.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and ad hoc design: All three components are largely straightforward adaptations of known ideas: majority-vote filtering for projection noise, Mikolov-style linear mapping for cross-lingual embeddings, and heuristic co-decoding. There is little methodological innovation beyond practical packaging, and the co-decoding lacks principled probabilistic calibration or global inference guarantees.
- Missing comparisons to strong modern baselines: The paper does not compare against multilingual contextual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) in zero-shot or translate-train settings, nor BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual/static embeddings, which are standard baselines for cross-lingual NER today. Without these, it is difficult to assess the competitiveness of the approach. The claim of outperforming prior work is limited to older non-contextual baselines (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.).
- Incomplete experimental detail and limited reproducibility: Critical details are missing or under-specified (size/domain of monolingual/comparable corpora, embedding training hyperparameters, dictionary extraction criteria, tokenization, number of prototypes and training for NN2, learning rates/regularization, exact feature templates). In-house datasets are not described sufficiently and appear unreleased. No statistical significance testing is reported.
- Potential bias in data selection heuristic: The entity-frequency table aggregates surface forms; handling of morphology, compounding, transliteration, and homonymy is unclear. This may bias selection toward frequent, unambiguous entities and against long-tail or morphologically complex mentions. There is no analysis of coverage changes (recall by length/type/frequency) or error typology to justify the heuristic beyond headline F1.
- Dependence on parallel resources and sensitivity not quantified: Although framed as weakly supervised, the representation projection relies on a phrase-table-derived bilingual dictionary. Availability and quality for low-resource languages are not discussed. The claim that 5–6K pairs “works best” lacks sensitivity analyses (dictionary size/quality, weighting in Eq. 7, dimensional mismatches like 800→300, or alternative mapping objectives/constraints).

Questions to authors
- Entity aggregation: How are entities e normalized for the frequency table (casing, punctuation, lemmatization, transliteration)? How do you handle multiword tokenization differences and compounding (e.g., German) to avoid fragmentary counts?
- Co-decoding consistency: After merging spans, do you enforce BIO/IOB2 consistency with a sequence-level pass? How are conflicts resolved at the token level to ensure valid sequences?
- Dictionary and mapping sensitivity: Please report performance vs dictionary size/thresholds, with/without weights wi, and vs alternative mappings (orthogonal Procrustes, CSLS, shared normalization). Why choose 800-d target and 300-d English with a non-square mapping?
- Resource requirements: What are the sizes and domains of monolingual and comparable corpora per language? How much parallel/comparable data is needed to reach the reported performance?
- NN2 details: How many prototype vectors are used, how are they initialized/updated, and how sensitive are results to this design? What is the computational overhead vs NN1?
- Modern baselines: Please compare against zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R (fine-tuned on English only), and a BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual/static embeddings, and translate-train or translate-test pipelines. If not feasible, justify why and discuss expected relative performance.
- Alignment confidence: Did you try sentence-level alignment confidence scores directly for selection, or combining them with q(y)? How does this compare to the frequency-based heuristic?

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses an important problem with simple, practical methods and demonstrates consistent gains across several languages. However, the methodological novelty is limited, the design relies on heuristics without deeper analysis, key experimental details are missing, and the absence of comparisons to strong modern multilingual baselines significantly weakens the empirical case. Strengthening the evaluation with contemporary baselines, fuller ablations/sensitivity analyses, and clearer reproducibility details could make this a stronger submission.
