Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language, proposing two complementary projection-based methods and simple co-decoding to combine them. The work is pragmatic and empirically driven.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Effective data selection for annotation projection: A simple but useful entity-reliability score (from frequency of projected tag assignments per entity form) plus an entity-count threshold is used to filter projection-labeled sentences from noisy comparable corpora. This substantially improves AP-based NER when alignments are noisy (e.g., Japanese/Korean).
2) Representation projection for direct model transfer: Learn a linear mapping from target to English embedding spaces using a small weighted bilingual dictionary, then run a single English NN-based NER on mapped target words. This enables “train once, apply to many languages” without retraining.
3) Co-decoding schemes: Two lightweight strategies—exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based (prefer AP entities, add non-conflicting RP entities)—that consistently improve over either system alone and outperform prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL.

Strengths
- Practical and simple methods with clear empirical gains: The AP data selection heuristic is straightforward, cheap, and yields large F1 improvements when alignment noise is high (+13.9 Japanese, +20.0 Korean). The co-decoding rules are easy to implement and reliably boost F1 by 1–6 points across languages.
- Broad and meaningful evaluation: Results on both in-house data with >50 fine-grained types and public CoNLL with 4 types across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese; Spanish, Dutch, German) demonstrate robustness. The paper also reports precision/recall profiles and identifies complementary error tendencies (AP high precision/low recall; RP more balanced).
- Sensible system design choices: The representation projection approach uses only word embeddings as features, making transfer straightforward and avoiding language-specific resources such as gazetteers or character models that may not transfer. Mapping many languages into English enables a single universal model.
- Comparison to strong prior art: On CoNLL test the combined systems outperform the prominent cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), especially for German, and approach supervised performance, lending significance to the contribution.
- Resource-aware: The authors acknowledge CRF training complexity with many labels and motivate using MEMMs and lightweight NNs, which is a practical consideration for large type inventories and weak supervision.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Annotation projection with data selection and linear embedding mappings are known ideas; the novelty is mainly in the particular reliability-based selection heuristic and simple co-decoding rules. While effective, the conceptual contribution is incremental.
- Potential tuning on test data and lack of significance testing: For in-house AP selection (Table 2), the best (q, n) thresholds are “tried” and “reported,” but no dev set is described, suggesting thresholds may be chosen on test—risking optimistic estimates. No statistical significance tests are reported for improvements.
- Dependence on non-trivial resources contradicts the “no target annotation” premise for truly low-resource settings: AP requires comparable corpora with alignment models; RP needs phrase tables (or equivalent) to extract a 5–6k weighted dictionary and sizable monolingual corpora for embeddings. The feasibility for very low-resource languages (without aligners/phrase tables) is unclear.
- Limited analysis/ablations: Several design choices are not probed: effect of dictionary size/quality and weighting on mapping; sensitivity to embedding dimensionality and training epochs; coverage/OOV rates and their impact; comparisons of NN1 vs NN2 beyond anecdotal precision/recall; effect of the quality threshold n (entity count) vs q (reliability) individually; alternative co-decoding strategies. An error analysis (e.g., boundary conflicts, type confusions) would strengthen insights.
- Baselines could be broader and more modern: Given the task’s timeline, a multilingual neural baseline (e.g., sharing subword/character features or multilingual word embeddings trained jointly) would be informative. The AP system uses MEMMs; comparing to CRF with approximate training or neural sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF) under weak supervision would better contextualize gains. Also, it is unclear whether representation projection was compared to other cross-lingual embedding methods (e.g., CCA/procrustes with orthogonality constraints) beyond Mikolov-style mapping.

Questions to authors
- How were the AP selection thresholds (q, n) chosen for in-house experiments—was a dev set used, or were they tuned on test? If the latter, can you provide results with a held-out dev set or fixed thresholds?
- What are the sizes/sources of the monolingual corpora used to train embeddings for each language? What is the OOV rate on test and how often was the “UNK” embedding used?
- How are the phrase tables/dictionaries obtained for RP? Do they require parallel corpora? If so, how much data was needed? Could you quantify the impact of dictionary size/quality on NER performance?
- Did you try imposing an orthogonality constraint on the linear mapping (Procrustes) and/or different weighting schemes? Any ablation comparing weighted vs unweighted mapping?
- Can you report statistical significance for the main improvements (co-decoding vs best single system) on CoNLL test?
- Will you release code, selection scripts, and trained mappings/models to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important practical problem with simple, well-motivated techniques and demonstrates consistent empirical gains across languages and settings. While the methodological novelty is moderate and some evaluation details (tuning, significance, ablations) are missing, the work is solid, clear, and likely to be useful to practitioners. Strengths in empirical validation and practicality outweigh the weaknesses. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying tuning practices and adding significance/ablation analyses.
