Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations by combining two projection-based approaches and a co-decoding strategy:
1) A heuristic data selection method for annotation projection that filters noisy projected labels from comparable (aligned) corpora using an entity-tag frequency reliability score and an entity-count threshold. This substantially improves training data quality when alignments are noisy.
2) A representation projection approach for direct model transfer: learn monolingual embeddings, build a small bilingual dictionary from a phrase table, estimate a weighted linear mapping to project target-language word embeddings into the English space, and run a single English NER model at inference time.
3) Two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and a rank-based strategy prioritizing high-precision AP outputs) that exploit the complementary precision/recall profiles of the two systems and consistently improve over either alone.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains across diverse languages and settings:
  - Data selection for AP yields large gains when alignments are poor: +13.9 F1 (Japanese) and +20.0 (Korean), validating the importance of quality-aware filtering of noisy projected labels.
  - Co-decoding consistently improves over both individual systems: e.g., rank-based AP+NN2 gains 2.9–6.2 F1 on in-house data; confidence-based AP+NN1 gains up to 3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev.
  - The best combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test, notably for German, and approach supervised baselines in some cases (e.g., Korean/German in-house).
- Complementarity is well-characterized and operationalized:
  - AP yields high precision/low recall; representation projection is more balanced (NN2 especially). The rank-based scheme that first accepts AP entities, then adds non-conflicting representation-projected entities, is a simple and effective way to leverage complementary strengths.
- Practicality and scalability:
  - Representation projection avoids retraining per target language: a single English NN (NN1/NN2) plus a small dictionary-based linear mapping suffices. The approach is computationally light (weighted least squares) and easy to extend to new languages.
  - MEMM choice for AP training improves tractability with many labels and millions of weakly labeled words, reflecting engineering awareness of scalability constraints.
- Clear experimental setup with ablations:
  - Table 2 isolates the effect of data selection (q, n thresholds) and correlates improvements with alignment quality; comparison of NN1 vs NN2 clarifies the role of the smoothing layer in precision/recall trade-offs.
- Breadth of evaluation:
  - Seven languages spanning typological diversity (e.g., Japanese/Korean vs Indo-European), two label granularities (4 CoNLL types vs 50+ in-house), and multiple domains/resources, indicating robustness.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on bilingual resources undermines the “weak supervision” narrative:
  - AP requires sentence alignments over large comparable corpora; representation projection requires a phrase table to build a 5–6K pair dictionary with P(x|y) weights. The paper does not clarify how these are obtained without substantial parallel data or a trained MT system. This is important for low-resource settings where such resources are unavailable. The resource cost and data requirements should be made explicit and contrasted with prior work.
- Limited novelty in core components:
  - Annotation projection and embedding-space linear mapping (à la Mikolov) are established; the main novelty is the specific sentence selection heuristic and the co-decoding schemes. While effective, these are relatively simple heuristics. The paper would benefit from stronger methodological contributions (e.g., principled confidence calibration, orthogonality-constrained mappings, or uncertainty-aware fusion).
- Missing or limited comparisons and analyses:
  - No comparison to alternative cross-lingual embedding approaches (e.g., CCA/Procrustes with orthogonality constraints, joint bilingual embeddings, adversarial alignment) or to more recent cross-lingual sequence models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings). This makes it hard to position gains due to the proposed mapping vs better-known mapping techniques.
  - Lack of statistical significance testing across systems/languages; effect sizes are promising but variability is unknown.
- Insufficient error and diagnostic analysis:
  - The paper reports P/R/F but offers little qualitative analysis (boundary errors, type confusions, language-specific failure modes, OOV handling efficacy, rare entities) or ablations on dictionary size/quality, mapping constraints, and calibration of probabilities for co-decoding.
- Reproducibility concerns:
  - Details about building the phrase tables, the comparable corpora, alignment training, and release of code/data are sparse. For the in-house 50+ type setting, label definitions and mapping to English model outputs are not described, making replication difficult. Threshold selection (q, n) is reported as “best numbers” for in-house tests, raising possible overfitting concerns.

Questions to authors
- Resources: How are the phrase tables and alignments obtained for each language pair? What is the amount of parallel/comparable data used, and is any human supervision involved? Could you quantify the total resource cost (tokens, bilingual pairs) per language?
- Dictionary sensitivity: How sensitive is representation projection to dictionary size/quality? Did you try orthogonality constraints or other regularization on the mapping, and with what effect?
- Calibration and fusion: Are the model confidences calibrated (e.g., temperature scaling)? Did you try score normalization across systems to strengthen the confidence-based co-decoding?
- OOV and subword: How is the NER-trained unk vector learned, and did you explore character/subword features to mitigate OOV in non-Latin scripts?
- Reproducibility: Can you release code and at least the mapping/dictionaries for CoNLL languages? For the in-house setup, can you share more details on the 50+ type schema and training data statistics?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper demonstrating that simple, well-engineered projection and fusion tactics can deliver competitive cross-lingual NER without target annotations. Clarifying resource requirements and strengthening methodological and comparative baselines would further improve its impact. I lean toward acceptance for its strong empirical results and practical value.
