Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation via two projection paradigms and their combination. The primary contributions as I see them are:
  1) A practical data selection method for annotation projection from comparable corpora, using an entity-tag frequency table to score sentence-level annotation quality and thresholding by quality and entity count. This substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy.
  2) A direct model transfer approach via representation projection: learn monolingual embeddings per language and a weighted linear mapping from target to English space using a small bilingual dictionary; then apply an English NER model to projected target embeddings.
  3) Simple but effective co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence and a rank-based method prioritizing high-precision AP outputs) that consistently outperform each individual system across several languages and datasets.

Strengths
- Clear framing and thorough empirical evaluation across diverse settings. The paper evaluates on both in-house data with >50 entity types (JP/KO/DE/PT) and CoNLL (ES/NL/DE), reporting P/R/F1 and analyzing precision-recall tradeoffs. The inclusion of alignment-quality statistics and ablations (AP with vs. without selection; NN1 vs. NN2; two co-decoders) supports the claims.
- Data selection substantially mitigates noise in annotation projection. The quality metric based on entity-level tag consistency is intuitive and language-agnostic. The gains are large when alignment is weak (e.g., +13.9 F1 JP, +20.0 KO), validating the selection mechanism’s benefit in realistic noisy settings.
- Representation projection offers a simple, resource-light path to universal zero-shot NER. Using small bilingual dictionaries (~5–6K pairs) and a linear map to a single English model is appealing for scalability to multiple languages without retraining. The NN2 smoothing layer that trades precision for recall is a thoughtful design to complement AP.
- Co-decoding is well-motivated and effective. The observed precision-recall complementarity between AP (high precision, low recall) and representation projection (more balanced) is exploited by rank-based co-decoding, yielding consistent improvements (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev). The simplicity of the combination is a plus for deployment.
- Claims are grounded in prior work and positioned appropriately. The paper contrasts with alignment-based annotation projection and feature-based direct transfer (cross-lingual clusters/wikifier), and situates representation projection within cross-lingual embedding literature.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in the representation projection component. The linear mapping approach (à la Mikolov et al.) is well known; the novelty here is largely its application to NER and the co-decoding. The paper would benefit from stronger methodological advances (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mapping, mean-centering/length normalization, or non-linear maps), as well as comparisons to alternative cross-lingual embedding methods (e.g., joint bilingual training, CSLS, adversarial alignment, or multilingual subword models).
- Missing strong modern baselines. Zero-shot transfer with multilingual pretrained encoders (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuned on English and applied to target) has become a standard baseline for cross-lingual NER and often outperforms earlier projection methods. The absence of such comparisons weakens the empirical claims, especially on CoNLL where those baselines are widely available.
- Potential bias and instability in the data selection metric. The entity-tag frequency table T is computed from the same noisy projections used for selection. This can: (i) bias selection toward high-frequency entities and common types, potentially hurting recall for rare names; (ii) propagate systematic errors from the English NER (e.g., consistent GPE/ORG confusions) rather than filtering them; and (iii) inflate quality scores if self-counting is not controlled (no leave-one-out). Ablations with leave-one-out scoring or smoothing, and sensitivity to thresholds (q, n), are not reported.
- Incomplete methodological details and reproducibility concerns. Important choices are not specified or explored: how phrase tables/dictionaries are built from comparable corpora; whether the mapping M is constrained (e.g., orthogonal) and how regularized; embedding training hyperparameters, tokenization for non-Latin scripts, and BIO-consistency enforcement in co-decoding. The in-house datasets and aligners are proprietary, complicating reproducibility; even for CoNLL, statistical significance and per-type breakdowns are missing.
- Scope and resource assumptions. Despite the “no target annotation” claim, the approach requires: (i) comparable corpora with word/sentence alignments; (ii) phrase tables for dictionary extraction; and (iii) sizable monolingual corpora to learn embeddings. These resources may be unavailable or low-quality for genuinely low-resource languages. The paper does not explore very low-resource regimes or sensitivity to dictionary size/quality beyond a brief note.

Questions to authors
- How is the bilingual phrase table constructed from comparable corpora? Do you require any parallel data or supervised alignments? What is the coverage and accuracy of the extracted dictionary, and how sensitive are results to its size (beyond the 5–6K heuristic)?
- Is the mapping M constrained (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes)? Did you experiment with mean-centering/length-normalization or CSLS to mitigate hubness? How do these choices affect NER accuracy?
- For the frequency-based selection, do you exclude the sentence under evaluation when computing q(y) (leave-one-out) and do you smooth T(e, l)? Could you report sensitivity curves over q and n and per-entity-type effects (rare vs. frequent entities)?
- How do you enforce BIO constraints in co-decoding when entities overlap/conflict? Is there a post-processing step to ensure valid sequences?
- Can you compare against multilingual pretrained encoders (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) in a zero-shot setting on CoNLL? If not feasible, please discuss how your approach would complement or compete with such models.
- What is the impact of adding lightweight character/subword features that are language-agnostic (e.g., Unicode script, word shape) to either system?

Additional comments
- Consider calibrating confidences (e.g., temperature scaling on unlabeled target data) for better confidence-based co-decoding.
- Incorporating alignment link confidences into q(y) or learning a small meta-combiner on source or unlabeled target data could further improve fusion.
- Please include statistical significance tests on CoNLL, and per-type F1 to illuminate where each method excels/fails.
