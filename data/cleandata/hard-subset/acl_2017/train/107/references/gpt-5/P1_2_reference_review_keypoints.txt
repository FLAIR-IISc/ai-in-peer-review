- Paper addresses zero-shot cross-lingual NER by combining annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding.  
- Annotation projection projects English labels via word alignments and uses a sentence-level tag-consistency selection metric to filter noisy projections.  
- Representation projection trains an English NN NER and maps target embeddings to English space via a weighted linear mapping learned from a bilingual dictionary, enabling direct decoding without retraining.  
- Co-decoding combines the two systems with two heuristics (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to exploit complementary precision/recall profiles.  
- Main contributions: practical data selection for noisy projection, simple strong representation projection for zero-shot transfer, and lightweight co-decoding heuristics.  
- Empirical results: substantial gains from data selection in noisy alignment settings and consistent improvements from co-decoding on in-house (4 languages, 50+ types) and CoNLL datasets.  
- Strength: tag-consistency sentence selection is simple, requires no labeled target data, and yields large F1 improvements for noisy languages (e.g., Japanese/Korean).  
- Strength: modular representation projection (single English model + per-language linear maps) simplifies adding languages and avoids retraining.  
- Strength: co-decoding reliably improves F1 across languages and domains, with rank-based method favoring annotation projection performing well.  
- Strength: broad evaluation across many entity types and comparisons to established baselines show competitive or superior performance, notably for German.  
- Engineering choices (MEMMs, NN variants) are well-justified for handling many entity types and large weakly labeled corpora.  
- Weakness: core components (annotation projection, linear bilingual mapping) are not novel; primary contribution is integration and empirical validation.  
- Weakness: unclear tuning protocol for thresholds (q, n) and potential risk of test-set tuning for in-house languages; need clarification on dev/test splits.  
- Weakness: resource assumptions for phrase tables, alignments, and dictionaries contradict “no supervision” claims for truly low-resource languages; feasibility should be discussed.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of biases/failure modes—selection may favor frequent entities and hurt rare ones; lacks type-wise and boundary/label error breakdowns and ablations.  
- Weakness: no probability calibration attempted for co-decoding and missing comparisons to multilingual contextual models (mBERT/XLM-R) as modern baselines.  
- Questions for authors: how were thresholds/co-decoding tuned for in-house data, sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, resource requirements for phrase tables/alignments, effect of calibration, and error-type analyses?  
- Suggestions: consider noise-aware training or instance weighting, subword/character features for OOVs, runtime/memory comparisons for scalability, and releasing code for reproducibility.
