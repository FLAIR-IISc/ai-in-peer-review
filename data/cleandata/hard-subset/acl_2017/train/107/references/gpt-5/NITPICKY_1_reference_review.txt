Summary and main contributions
- Proposes two weakly supervised, projection-based approaches for cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation: (i) annotation projection from English to target sentences with a heuristic data selection based on per-entity label frequency; (ii) representation projection via linear cross-lingual embedding mapping enabling direct application of an English NER.
- Introduces two simple co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based, rank-based) to combine the two projections, yielding consistent gains.
- Provides empirical evaluation on multiple languages (in-house with 50+ types; CoNLL with 4 types), showing that data selection substantially improves noisy annotation projection, and that co-decoding outperforms prior transfer methods (Tä cköström et al., Tsai et al.).

Strengths
- Broad empirical scope and practical baseline value: The paper evaluates across seven languages (in-house + CoNLL) and two granularities of type inventories. It demonstrates that combining two weak views yields improvements across settings, establishing a useful baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.
- Data selection for projection is effective when alignment is poor: The per-entity frequency heuristic yields very large gains on Japanese/Korean (Table 2, +13.9/+20.0 F1), which is operationally important when alignments are noisy.
- Representation projection is simple, flexible, and resource-light at inference: A single English NN with word embeddings can be reused for new languages via a linear map, avoiding re-training per language.
- Co-decoding improves over both components: The rank-based combination (AP prioritized) consistently yields 2.9–6.2 F1 gains on in-house and competitive gains on CoNLL dev/test.
- Comparisons to prior cross-lingual NER: On CoNLL test, the best combined system beats Tä cköström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), especially for German.

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is limited and lacks ablations: The two core ideas—label projection with quality filtering and linear embedding mapping—are known. The data selection criterion (per-entity label frequency) is a straightforward heuristic. There are no ablations isolating: (i) impact of q vs. n thresholds; (ii) effect of weighting wi in the mapping; (iii) effect of the “smoothing layer” (NN2) beyond reporting numbers; (iv) choice of embedding dimensions (300 vs. 800) or training epochs. Without ablations, the paper’s scientific contribution is unclear beyond engineering.
- Potential test-set tuning/data leakage in Table 2: The paper states “we have tried different threshold parameters q and n and reported the best numbers in Table 2,” but for in-house datasets only test sets are available (no dev mentioned). This implies tuning on test, inflating gains. Clarify whether a held-out dev split was used; otherwise results are not valid.
- Resource and reproducibility gaps: Key training data and preprocessing are insufficiently specified: sources and sizes of monolingual corpora; how “comparable corpora with alignment information” were obtained for non-parallel pairs; how alignment F1 was computed and on what gold; how phrase tables/dictionaries were produced (from what bitext?); tokenization/segmentation for Japanese/Korean; NN hyperparameters (hidden size, optimizer, learning rate, regularization, early stopping). No code or data release; the approach is not reproducible as written.
- Technical inaccuracies and ambiguities: 
  - Complexity claims: CRF training “grows exponentially O(M^(o+1))” and MEMM training “linear O(M) independent of o.” For linear-chain CRFs, time is typically O(n M^2) (o=1), and while higher order increases cost, “exponential” is too strong; MEMM training/decoding cost also grows with the size of the label history (beam/Viterbi over o-order transitions). The discussion oversimplifies.
  - Equation (5) uses T(e,l) called “frequency,” but Table 1 shows normalized probabilities; clarify whether T stores counts or normalized frequencies. The averaging by n(y) biases toward sentences with many mentions; no normalization by mention frequency distribution.
  - The statement “Words not in the target vocabulary are projected into their English embeddings if found in the English vocabulary” is unclear—how can a target OOV be “found” in English by string identity? Is transliteration used?
  - Co-decoding does not discuss BIO constraint enforcement post-merge; combining spans may yield invalid sequences.
- Fairness of comparisons and evaluation setups:
  - For CoNLL, the compared methods (Tä cköström, Tsai) rely on different resources (e.g., Wikipedia, cross-lingual clusters). This paper also uses nontrivial resources (phrase tables/alignments/monolingual corpora). A resource table is needed to ensure fairness.
  - The in-house “supervised” baselines (90–272K) are opaque: domains, annotation guidelines, and training details not described; hence “close to supervised” is hard to interpret.
  - No statistical significance testing; many gains are small (e.g., +1–2 F1), particularly on CoNLL German/NL.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How were q and n chosen for in-house experiments? Was a dev split used, or were thresholds tuned on test? Please provide a dev/test protocol and results with fixed thresholds.
- Resources: Precisely what corpora were used for (i) monolingual embeddings per language (tokens, domains); (ii) comparable corpora and the alignment pipeline; (iii) phrase tables/dictionaries (bitext source)? Are these the same across in-house and CoNLL experiments?
- Alignment: How is “alignment F1” defined and what is the gold standard? How are sentence pairs obtained in “comparable” (non-parallel) corpora with alignments?
- Embedding mapping: Why 300 dims for English and 800 for targets? Did you try symmetric dimensions or orthogonal Procrustes constraints? Provide ablations and the effect of dictionary size/weighting wi.
- Neural models: Provide full hyperparameters for NN1/NN2 (hidden size, optimizer, LR schedule, epochs, batch size, dropout, prototype count in NN2). Were character or subword features attempted, especially for morphologically rich languages?
- Co-decoding: How do you ensure valid BIO sequences after merging? What is the proportion of conflicts and how sensitive are results to the merge heuristic? Did you try calibration or temperature scaling to make confidences comparable?
- Evaluation: For in-house 50+ types, are the label sets and guidelines exactly shared across English and targets? Any language-specific type mapping applied? Please report per-type or macro results and significance.

Overall, while the empirical gains are promising and the combined system is practically useful, the paper’s novelty is modest and there are serious concerns about experimental rigor, missing details, and potential tuning on test, which should be addressed to meet ACL standards.
