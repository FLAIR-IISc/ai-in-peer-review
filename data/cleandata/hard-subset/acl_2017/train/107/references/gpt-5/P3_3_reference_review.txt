Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based routes and their combination. (1) Annotation projection from English to target sentences with a heuristic data selection method that ranks projected sentences using entity–tag frequency reliability and entity count. (2) Representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target monolingual embeddings to English embeddings using a small weighted bilingual dictionary, then run a single English NN-only NER model directly on projected target embeddings. (3) Co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine outputs from (1) and (2).
- Empirically, the data selection substantially helps when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9/+20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean). Co-decoding improves over either component alone on in-house (50+ labels) and CoNLL (4 labels), and beats prior cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.).

Strengths
- Clear, pragmatic framework with complementary methods and a well-motivated combination. The observation that AP is high-precision/low-recall and representation projection is more balanced underpins a simple, effective rank-based co-decoder that consistently improves F1 (e.g., AP+NN2 rank-based reaches 69.9/69.3/51.8/59.3 F1 on PT/DE/KO/JA).
- Effective and simple data selection for projection-labeled training. The per-entity tag frequency reliability aggregated at sentence-level is an intuitive signal that delivers large gains under low-quality alignment (e.g., +13.9 F1 JA, +20.0 KO), while yielding modest gains where alignment is stronger (DE/PT). This addresses a known pain point in projection methods.
- A strong empirical study spanning two settings: challenging in-house data with fine-grained 50+ types and public CoNLL with 4 types, across six languages including non-Latin scripts (JA/KO). The study analyzes precision/recall trade-offs, compares two NN architectures (NN1/NN2), and shows consistent benefit from co-decoding on dev/test.
- Representation projection is a lightweight direct-transfer path: once the English model and cross-lingual mapping are trained, no target-side model training is required. This is appealing for deployment to new languages and for scenarios with only monolingual unlabeled data plus a small bilingual dictionary.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. Both components build on well-known ideas: sentence-level selection heuristics for projection noise reduction and linear cross-lingual embedding mapping (Mikolov et al.) for transfer. The main technical additions (weighted least squares using phrase-table probabilities; sentence-level frequency-based filtering; ad-hoc co-decoding) are reasonable but not conceptually deep.
- Resource assumptions and clarity. The approach relies on (a) sentence/word-level alignments for AP over “comparable corpora” and (b) phrase tables for bilingual dictionaries in representation projection. It is unclear whether these originate from parallel corpora and full SMT pipelines, which weakens the “no target supervision” claim in low-resource settings where alignments/phrase tables may be unavailable. More detail on sources, sizes, and comparability vs parallelism is needed.
- Tuning and evaluation rigor. For the in-house AP data selection, thresholds (q, n) are “tried” and best numbers reported, but there is no explicit development set; this risks tuning on test. Significance testing is absent. For co-decoding, the dev-based choice between confidence vs rank-based is stated for CoNLL, but not for in-house. Reporting exact selection protocol and statistical tests would strengthen claims.
- Missing modern baselines and ablations. Strong contemporary cross-lingual NER baselines based on multilingual contextual encoders (mBERT/XLM-R, LASER/LaBSE + CRF) are not considered. Given the paper’s goal (no target annotation), zero-shot mBERT fine-tuned on English is a natural baseline. Ablations on dictionary size/quality, mapping constraints (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes vs unconstrained), regularization, and embedding dimensionality (800→300 mapping) are missing; these choices could substantially affect transfer.
- Methodological limitations. The AP frequency table treats surface forms as types, which may be brittle for morphologically rich languages and tokenization differences; smoothing/backoff is not described. The representation projection model uses locally normalized feed-forward NNs without character/subword features or CRF layers, which likely hurts robustness to OOV/inflection. Co-decoding is heuristic and may violate global BIO constraints; ensuring consistency is not discussed.

Questions to authors
- Data selection tuning: How were (q, n) chosen for in-house experiments? Was a held-out dev set used per language, or were test sets used to pick best thresholds (which would be problematic)? Can you report performance with a fixed selection protocol determined on a small dev split?
- Alignment and dictionary resources: What corpora and tools produced the sentence/word alignments and phrase tables? Are they truly “comparable,” or parallel? How much parallel data was needed to obtain the phrase tables used for dictionaries? How would the method perform with only induced lexicons or unsupervised bilingual lexicon induction?
- Mapping details: Did you try orthogonal constraints (Procrustes) or regularization when learning Mfe? Any comparison between weighted and unweighted mappings? Given the dimensionality mismatch (800→300), did you test projecting English to the target space or using a shared space learned jointly?
- Robustness to morphology/OOV: How are entity forms normalized in the frequency table (e.g., case-folding, lemmatization)? Is any smoothing applied to T(e, l)? For representation projection, did you try subword/character representations to mitigate OOV and improve transfer in JA/KO?
- Co-decoding consistency: How do you enforce BIO/IOB2 constraints when merging spans, especially under conflicts? Is there a global reconciliation step or re-decoding with constraints?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed engineering paper showing that two simple projection paradigms can be made more effective and complementary with careful selection and combination. Clarifying resource assumptions, tightening the tuning protocol, and adding stronger baselines/ablations would significantly strengthen the work.
