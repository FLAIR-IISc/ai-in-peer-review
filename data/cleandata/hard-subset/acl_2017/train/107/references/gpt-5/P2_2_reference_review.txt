Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without any target-language annotation by proposing two projection-based approaches plus co-decoding:
  1) A data selection scheme for annotation projection from English to target languages using comparable corpora with alignments. It scores sentence-level label quality via entity-level agreement frequencies and filters sentences by quality and entity-count thresholds.
  2) A direct model transfer method via representation projection: learn bilingual linear mappings from target to English embedding space (weighted least squares on a small dictionary), then decode target sentences with an English-only NN NER model.
  3) Two co-decoding heuristics to merge outputs: exclude-O confidence-based and a rank-based scheme prioritizing high-precision annotation-projection predictions.
- Empirically, the combined systems outperform two prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL 02/03, and achieve meaningful performance on in-house datasets with >50 types.

Strengths
- Clear, practical problem framing with two complementary transfer routes. The paper identifies and exploits complementary error profiles: annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall, representation projection is more balanced. This guides the design of the rank-based co-decoding and is borne out by results.
- Effective, simple data selection for noisy projections. The entity-frequency–based quality score substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 for Korean vs. using all projected data). The method is easy to implement and does not require extra supervision.
- Representation projection enables zero retraining in the target language. The English-only NN model with bilingual linear mappings is simple, flexible (one model for many languages), and yields competitive results given low-resources. The weighting in the dictionary regression is a small but sensible improvement.
- Broad evaluation across multiple languages and settings. The paper reports both in-house fine-grained NER (4 languages, 50+ types) and CoNLL (3 languages, 4 types), includes comparisons to prior cross-lingual methods, and reports supervised “upper bounds,” helping readers gauge the gap.
- Co-decoding gives consistent gains over either component alone. On in-house data, rank-based AP+NN2 improves over the best single system by 2.9–6.2 F1; on CoNLL dev, AP+NN1 confidence-based improves 1.3–3.6 F1. This validates the complementarity claim.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to contemporary cross-lingual representation literature. Using linear mapping to a shared embedding space for transfer is well known, and the data selection idea resembles denoising strategies in distant supervision. The weighted regression and specific co-decoding heuristics are incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Missing strong modern baselines and ablations. Current standard baselines for cross-lingual NER include multilingual contextual encoders (mBERT, XLM-R, LaBSE) with zero-shot transfer or lightweight adaptation. The paper does not compare against these, nor to more recent CLWE methods (e.g., orthogonal/Procrustes mappings, self-learning). This weakens claims of state of the art by 2025 standards.
- Reproducibility and resource clarity. Key ingredients are in-house or not fully specified: comparable/parallel corpora sources, alignment models and training data, phrase table creation, monolingual corpus sizes, hyperparameters (e.g., window, negative samples), NN architecture details (prototype count in NN2), and dictionary selection criteria. No code or resources are promised. This makes replication difficult.
- Potential selection bias/tuning on test. For the data selection thresholds (q, n), Table 2 reports “best numbers” without a separate development split for in-house languages; it appears thresholds may have been tuned on test, inflating reported gains. Similarly, significance testing is not reported anywhere.
- Scope limitations not fully discussed. The representation projection requires a translation lexicon/phrase table and monolingual embeddings; in the truly low-resource setting where these are unavailable, feasibility is unclear. The method also avoids character and subword features, which are known to be critical for morphologically rich languages; the trade-off between portability and accuracy is underexplored.

Questions to authors
- How were the (q, n) thresholds selected for in-house languages? Was there a held-out dev set, or were thresholds optimized on test? Please clarify the validation protocol and, if needed, re-run with a proper dev split.
- What are the exact NN hyperparameters for NN1/NN2 (context window, hidden size, prototype count and training for NN2, dropout, optimizer, learning rate)? How sensitive are results to these?
- For the bilingual mappings: did you enforce an orthogonality constraint or use Procrustes? Did you compare weighted vs. unweighted regression, and vary dictionary sizes (beyond 5–6k) to quantify sensitivity? Any results for fully unsupervised CLWEs (e.g., VecMap/MUSE)?
- What are the sources and sizes of monolingual corpora per language, and the precise alignment/phrase-table training pipelines (tokenization, preprocessing)? Can you release the seed dictionaries and mappings?
- Are the co-decoding gains statistically significant? Could you report bootstrap resampling or similar tests?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider evaluating against multilingual contextual baselines (mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot, possibly with adapters) to situate your contribution in today’s landscape; also try a hybrid with your co-decoding.
- Provide ablations: effect of data selection alone, mapping weighting, orthogonal constraint, dictionary frequency threshold, and exclude-O vs. pure confidence co-decoding. Include error analyses by entity type/length.
- Discuss long-tail entities: the frequency-based selection likely favors frequent entities and may filter rare but correct mentions. A calibration or diversity-aware sampling strategy could mitigate this.

Overall evaluation
- The paper is clear, well-motivated, and shows solid empirical gains for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using simple, practical techniques. However, the methodological novelty is moderate and comparisons miss strong modern baselines; reproducibility details are sparse, and some validation choices are unclear. With stronger baselines, clearer tuning protocol, and released resources, this would be a solid contribution. As is, I view it as borderline, leaning accept if the authors can address the evaluation and reproducibility concerns.
