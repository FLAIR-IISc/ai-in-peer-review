Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language, proposing two complementary projection-based methods and simple ensemble schemes:
1) Annotation projection with data selection: A heuristic scoring of projected sentences based on per-entity tag frequency consistency across the weakly labeled corpus, combined with a minimum-entities filter, to select higher-quality training sentences from noisy comparable/parallel data.
2) Representation projection (direct model transfer): Learn a linear mapping from target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space using a small weighted bilingual dictionary; then apply an English-only NER neural model (embedding-only features) to mapped target tokens, requiring no retraining per target language.
3) Co-decoding schemes: Two simple ways to merge the outputs—exclude-O confidence-based selection and a rank-based scheme that prioritizes the high-precision AP system and augments with non-conflicting RP entities.

Empirically, the methods yield solid gains across four in-house languages (>50 types) and CoNLL languages (4 types), with the combined systems outperforming prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Strengths
- Practical weak supervision for low-resource targets: The paper demonstrates two low-touch transfer routes, both feasible in settings without target-side labeled data. The data-selection heuristic substantially mitigates noisy projection when word alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 to +20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean), and the embedding projection eliminates retraining costs for new languages.
- Complementary error profiles and effective combination: The observation that AP is high-precision/low-recall while RP is more balanced is borne out across languages; the rank-based co-decoding achieves consistent improvements over each component (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house data), showing meaningful complementarity.
- Clear, reproducible high-level methodology: The modeling choices are straightforward (MEMM/CRF for AP; simple feedforward NNs for RP; linear WLS mapping for alignment), with explicit hyperparameters (embedding dims, dictionary sizes), making the approach understandable and potentially extensible.
- Broad evaluation: Results span multiple language families and labeling schemes (coarse CoNLL vs. fine-grained in-house). The inclusion of supervised baselines contextualizes the gap and shows that the best weakly supervised systems can approach supervised performance in some cases (e.g., Korean/German in-house).

Weaknesses
- Missing strong contemporary baselines: The paper lacks comparisons to multilingual pretrained transformer transfer (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) and multilingual contextual embedding-based NER, which are standard zero-shot/weakly supervised baselines today and typically outperform static-embedding methods. Without these, it’s hard to judge the competitiveness of the proposed approach in 2025.
- Limited novelty in core techniques: Both components are largely known ideas—annotation projection and Mikolov-style linear embedding mapping—applied to NER. The data selection (frequency-based confidence aggregated per surface form) and the co-decoding schemes are simple heuristics; more principled alternatives (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mappings; calibration/stacking; joint decoding under BIO constraints) are not explored.
- Potential tuning and evaluation leakage: For in-house languages, thresholds (q, n) are “reported as best numbers,” but it is unclear whether a development set was used. Tuning on test data would invalidate the gains. Similarly, the co-decoding choice (confidence vs. rank) is driven by development data for CoNLL but not clearly for in-house.
- Assumptions on resources and segmentation: “No human annotation” still presumes access to parallel/comparable corpora, aligners, and phrase tables to derive weighted dictionaries. These may be scarce for truly low-resource languages. Furthermore, languages with complex morphology or no whitespace tokenization (Japanese/Korean) require robust segmentation; the paper omits details about tokenization and its impact on alignment and embedding quality.
- Methodological gaps and ablations: The RP mapping uses non-orthogonal WLS between different dimensionalities (800→300), which can distort geometry; no comparison to orthogonal Procrustes, dimension-matched mappings, or multilingual joint embedding methods (e.g., joint training, CSLS, anchor-based) is provided. There is also no analysis of dictionary size/quality, effect of domain mismatch, or OOV handling beyond a single “unk” vector. For the co-decoder, BIO legality and conflict resolution are treated heuristically; no global decoding or calibration is attempted.

Questions to authors
- Tuning protocol: For in-house experiments, how were q and n selected? Was there a held-out development set distinct from the test set? Similarly, how was the co-decoding variant chosen for in-house languages?
- Resource requirements: What data and tools are needed to build the weighted bilingual dictionaries and aligners? Are you relying on existing MT phrase tables or extracting them from parallel corpora? How do results change if only a small bilingual lexicon (e.g., 1–2k pairs) is available?
- Mapping details: Why use a non-orthogonal linear mapping and different source/target dimensions (800→300)? Did you try orthogonal Procrustes with dimension matching (which preserves distances), or normalization (mean centering, length normalization) and CSLS at inference? Any ablations?
- Tokenization and morphology: How are Japanese and Korean tokenized for embeddings and alignment? Did you evaluate the effect of subword-level representations (character/byte/wordpiece) on RP and AP, particularly for morphologically rich languages?
- Calibration and legality: Do you enforce BIO constraints when co-decoding or post-process to ensure valid spans? Did you attempt probability calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) to make AP and RP confidences more comparable?
- Comparisons to modern baselines: How does your best combined system compare to zero-shot transfer from multilingual transformers (mBERT/XLM-R) trained only on English? Could you combine your AP/RP signals with such models?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide error analysis (e.g., boundary vs. type errors; frequent vs. rare entities; cross-domain robustness). The sentence-level selection likely favors frequent entities; quantify its impact on recall for rare types.
- Consider stronger RP baselines: orthogonal mappings, joint multilingual embeddings, and contextual embeddings. Include ablations on dictionary size, domain, and mapping regularization.
- Explore more principled ensembling: learning-to-rank or stacking on a dev set; joint decoding with constraints; or CRF lattice combination.
- Clarify whether frequency tables are built only on training (projection-labeled) data to avoid leakage, and whether entity normalization (e.g., case-folding, punctuation) is applied.

Overall, the paper offers a practical and well-executed combination of two classical transfer ideas with a simple, effective data-selection heuristic and ensemble. Its empirical gains are meaningful. However, the technical novelty is limited, and the lack of comparison with contemporary multilingual baselines weakens the case for acceptance without further experimentation and clarity on tuning.
