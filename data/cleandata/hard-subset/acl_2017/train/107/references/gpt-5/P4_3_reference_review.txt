Summary and contributions
This paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation. It presents: (1) an annotation projection (AP) method with a heuristic data selection mechanism to filter noisy projections from comparable/parallel corpora; (2) a representation projection (RP) approach that linearly maps target-language word embeddings into English, enabling direct application of an English NER model; and (3) two co-decoding schemes that combine AP and RP outputs. Experiments on in-house data (50+ types) and CoNLL 02/03 (4 types) across several languages show that the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A practical co-decoding framework that systematically combines AP and RP outputs (rank-based and exclude-O confidence-based), yielding consistent gains over individual systems across multiple languages and datasets.
2) A simple, effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection based on entity-wise tag frequency reliability and per-sentence thresholds, which substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy.
3) A direct model transfer method via weighted linear embedding mapping from target to English, enabling reuse of a single English NER model without retraining.

Strengths
- Co-decoding improves robustness and accuracy: The rank-based and exclude-O confidence combinations consistently outperform standalone AP/RP. For in-house datasets, AP+NN2 (rank) yields +2.9 to +6.2 F1 improvements over the best single system and approaches supervised performance in Korean/German despite no target annotations. On CoNLL dev/test, the chosen co-decoding variant improves over AP/RP by 1.3–3.6 F1 and surpasses prior cross-lingual baselines.
- Effective data selection for AP in noisy settings: The entity-frequency-based sentence quality score q(y) and entity-count threshold n(y) produce large gains when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean). The method is simple to implement, unsupervised with respect to target labels, and directly linked to the noise characteristics of projection.
- Clear resource efficiency via RP: The RP approach enables zero-retraining transfer by mapping target embeddings to English. This yields a reusable universal NER model and demonstrates competitive performance given the minimal assumptions (monolingual corpora + a small bilingual dictionary/phrase table).
- Broad evaluation across languages and label inventories: The study includes both fine-grained (50+ types) in-house datasets and standard CoNLL (4 types), and reports precision/recall/F1, highlighting complementary error profiles (AP high-precision/low-recall; RP more balanced), which motivates and supports the co-decoding design.
- Technical soundness of core components: The AP pipeline is standard with a principled filtering heuristic; the RP mapping uses a weighted least squares dictionary fit, a well-studied and effective method for cross-lingual embedding alignment; the NER models are standard CRF/MEMM/NN variants suited to the data scale and feature design.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in RP and modest innovation in AP selection: The RP mapping and direct transfer largely follow established cross-lingual embedding literature, with weighting in the least squares objective as a minor extension. The AP selection heuristic, while effective, is simple and could bias toward frequent entities and high-surface-form overlap, potentially harming rare or morphologically variable entities.
- Resource assumptions and clarity: The approach depends on (a) sentence-aligned comparable/parallel corpora with alignment models for AP, and (b) phrase tables or bilingual dictionaries for RP. The paper claims “no human annotation in the target language” but does not fully clarify the availability/creation of these resources for all languages, nor the degree of parallelism of “comparable” data. This affects reproducibility and applicability to genuinely low-resource scenarios with limited parallel/comparable data.
- Hyperparameter selection and potential leakage: For AP selection thresholds (q, n), the paper reports “best numbers” without clearly stating the tuning procedure or validation data. If thresholds are chosen against test performance, results may be optimistic. Similarly, co-decoding choice (confidence vs rank) is decided by dev data on CoNLL but is not clearly specified for in-house sets.
- Missing implementation and ablation details: Key NN hyperparameters (context window, hidden size, number of prototypes in NN2, training regimes), embedding training corpora sizes/sources, dictionary size sensitivity, and the impact of the weighted dictionary vs unweighted mapping are not ablated. There is no analysis of OOV handling beyond a brief mention of an “NER-trained unk vector,” whose training is unspecified.
- Inference validity and calibration: Co-decoding hinges on confidence comparability across models. No calibration procedure is discussed, and it is unclear whether the combined outputs enforce valid IOB constraints. The potential for conflicting spans is acknowledged, but constraints and tie-breaking beyond two stated heuristics are not fully formalized or analyzed.
- Limited error analysis and significance testing: The paper reports macro results but provides minimal breakdown by entity type, frequency, or error category. No statistical significance testing is provided, making it difficult to assess robustness of observed gains.

Questions to authors (optional)
- What data and tools were used to obtain sentence alignments for the “comparable” corpora? Are they truly comparable or parallel? How are alignment models trained per language, and are these resources publicly available?
- How are entity surface forms normalized for the frequency table (case folding, diacritics, tokenization, lemmatization)? How does the selection affect inflected or multiword variants?
- How were q and n selected for AP, especially for in-house experiments where only test sets are mentioned? Was a held-out dev set used, or were thresholds tuned on test?
- For RP, what are the monolingual corpora sizes and sources per language? How many dictionary pairs per language after filtering, and how does performance vary with dictionary size and the weighting scheme?
- How is the “NER-trained unk vector” obtained? Is it a learned parameter in the English NER model, and how is it used during transfer?
- During co-decoding, are BIO/IOB constraints enforced post hoc (e.g., via constrained decoding) to ensure valid sequences?
- Did you evaluate calibration (e.g., ECE) or apply temperature scaling before confidence-based combination?
- Can the methods operate when only a small seed lexicon is available (no phrase table)? If so, how does performance change?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound in its core components, with strong empirical results. The main limitations are modest novelty in RP, limited detail on resources/hyperparameters, and potential tuning ambiguities.
