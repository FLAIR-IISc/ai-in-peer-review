Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations by combining two projection paradigms and a simple ensembling strategy. The authors:
- Introduce a practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection from English to a target language using comparable corpora: sentences are scored by entity-level agreement statistics derived from projected labels, balancing label quality q(y) and information content n(y).
- Propose a representation projection approach: learn a linear mapping from target to English monolingual embedding spaces using a small bilingual dictionary, and run an English-only NER model on mapped target embeddings, avoiding retraining per language.
- Design co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the two systems, yielding consistent gains and SOTA performance on CoNLL test sets among prior cross-lingual NER methods without target labels.

As I see it, the primary contribution is the empirical finding that a simple, rank-based fusion of two weakly supervised projections reliably improves over each component and prior art; second is the effective, low-overhead data selection for annotation projection; third is the lightweight direct model transfer via linear embedding mapping.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements and broad coverage. Across four in-house languages and CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German, the combined systems consistently outperform individual projection systems, often by 2.9–6.2 F1 (in-house) and up to 3.6 F1 (CoNLL dev). On CoNLL test, the top co-decoding systems beat Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), notably on German, narrowing the gap to supervised models.
- Practical and resource-efficient methods. The annotation projection selection requires only alignment information and projected labels; the representation projection uses a small (~5–6k pairs) bilingual lexicon and pre-trained monolingual embeddings. No target-side annotations or language-specific gazetteers are needed, and the English NER is reused across languages.
- Sensible analysis of model behaviors and design of fusion. The paper observes complementary precision/recall profiles (AP high precision/low recall; RP more balanced), and exploits this with a rank-based co-decoder that prioritizes high-precision spans and fills recall with non-conflicting spans—simple yet effective.
- Clear reporting of resources and alignment quality. The paper quantifies alignment accuracies and shows that the data selection heuristic is especially beneficial under low alignment accuracy (e.g., +20.0 F1 for Korean), providing intuition about when AP selection helps most.
- Method simplicity and extensibility. Linear mapping and MEMM/FFNN models are easy to implement and adapt. The approach could be readily tried by practitioners on new languages with modest resources.

Weaknesses
- Questionable tuning protocol and potential reliance on target test data. For in-house languages, the paper reports “best” thresholds (q, n) and co-decoding choices but does not specify a target-language development set. If tuned on test, results overestimate real-world performance where no target labels exist. For CoNLL, development data are used appropriately; for in-house, the tuning methodology needs clarification.
- Resource assumptions undermine the “no-parallel/weakly supervised” claim. AP requires sentence/word alignments on (possibly comparable) corpora; RP requires a phrase table or bilingual dictionary extracted from parallel data. In many low-resource settings, such alignments/dictionaries are unavailable or noisy; the paper does not quantify the cost or availability of these resources or explore unsupervised lexicon induction alternatives.
- Limited novelty in core techniques. The RP approach is a weighted linear mapping (akin to Mikolov et al., 2013b), and the co-decoding heuristics are straightforward. The main technical novelty lies in the AP data selection heuristic, which itself relies on frequency counts from noisy projections and lacks deeper justification or analysis (e.g., calibration, robustness to systematic bias).
- Missing ablations, significance tests, and deeper error analysis. There is no statistical significance testing on CoNLL; ablations on dictionary size/quality, choice of mapping (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes), or neural architecture (NN1 vs NN2 components) are minimal. The effect of q and n, and sensitivity curves for both AP and RP, are not reported. Error analysis by entity type/OOV and conflict-resolution errors in co-decoding would strengthen insights.
- Comparisons to stronger baselines are absent. The English NER is a locally-normalized FFNN without character/subword features or CRF decoding. While the choice is motivated by cross-lingual simplicity, comparisons to stronger transfer baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with char features, or multilingual contextual embeddings) are missing, limiting claims of state-of-the-art beyond the two cited cross-lingual methods.

Questions to authors
- How were (q, n) thresholds and co-decoding choices tuned for in-house languages without target annotations? Was any target-labeled development data used, or were thresholds selected via a proxy (e.g., alignment scores, held-out projection quality)? Please detail the protocol to ensure no test leakage.
- What are the sources and costs of the alignments and phrase tables/dictionaries? For “comparable” corpora, how are sentence/word alignments produced? How much parallel signal is required to reach the reported performance?
- Sensitivity to dictionary size/quality: can you provide performance as a function of dictionary size and frequency threshold? What happens with automatically induced dictionaries (unsupervised cross-lingual embeddings)?
- Did you try orthogonal mapping with normalization/CSLS or other constraints shown to improve cross-lingual retrieval? Any effect on RP performance?
- Are the CoNLL test gains statistically significant? Please report significance testing.
- How are tokenization and segmentation handled for Japanese/Korean, and how do OOV rates and subword modeling affect results? Did character/subword features help or hurt cross-lingual transfer?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider calibrating model confidences (e.g., temperature scaling) to improve confidence-based co-decoding.
- Explore iterative self-training: use high-quality AP spans to fine-tune RP, or vice versa, which may further close the gap to supervised NER.
- Analyze errors by entity type and length, and conflicts resolved by the rank-based scheme.
- For broader applicability, evaluate with unsupervised bilingual lexicon induction or multilingual contextual models to reduce reliance on alignments/phrase tables.
- Provide more implementation details (embedding training variant, prototype vectors in NN2, hyperparameters) and consider releasing code to enhance reproducibility.

Overall, this is a solid and practical paper with consistent empirical gains. Addressing the tuning protocol, resource assumptions, and providing stronger analysis would substantially strengthen the submission.
