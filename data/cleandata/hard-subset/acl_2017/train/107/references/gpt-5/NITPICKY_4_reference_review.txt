Summary and main contributions
- The paper studies cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based methods and a combination scheme. Contributions as I see them:
  1) A heuristic data selection strategy for annotation projection on noisy comparable-aligned corpora, using an entity-tag frequency reliability score to select pseudo-labeled target sentences.
  2) A direct model transfer method via representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target-language embeddings to English embeddings using a small weighted bilingual dictionary; then decode with an English-only NN tagger.
  3) Two simple co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based priority to AP) to merge outputs and improve over either system alone.
- The work is evaluated on in-house datasets (50+ types) for 4 languages and CoNLL 02/03 (4 types) for 3 languages, reporting that co-decoding beats both components and prior cross-lingual baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Strengths
- Practical, resource-lean design: Both projection methods avoid target-language annotation and use readily accessible signals (weak alignments, small dictionaries). The representation mapping is simple and fast to learn, and the AP data selection uses a single pass frequency table.
- Strong empirical coverage: Results on 7 languages across two settings (fine-grained and CoNLL) with detailed P/R/F1, and consistent gains from co-decoding. The AP data selection yields large improvements when alignment quality is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese, +20.0 Korean), which is practically meaningful.
- Complementary error profiles exploited: The paper identifies that AP is high-precision/low-recall while RP is more balanced, and designs a straightforward but effective rank-based merger. The reported gains of 2.9–6.2 F1 (in-house) and 1.3–3.6 (CoNLL dev) over the best single-view system show the combination is not trivial averaging.
- Clear articulation of modeling choices: MEMM vs CRF tradeoffs for scalability, NN architectures (NN1 vs NN2 including a smoothing layer), and the use of weighted least squares for dictionary-based mapping are explained.

Weaknesses
- Questionable novelty and limited comparisons: The representation projection approach largely restates Mikolov et al. (2013b)-style mapping; no comparison to stronger, widely-used variants (orthogonal Procrustes, CSLS, unsupervised/supervised bilingual mapping like Artetxe et al., MUSE) or multilingual contextual models. For AP, the selection heuristic is essentially frequency-based self-consistency; similar ideas (confidence/consensus filtering, tri-training) are not discussed/compared. Without these, it is hard to assess true novelty/advantage.
- Potential tuning on test data and unclear validation: For in-house AP data selection, you “tried different q,n and reported the best numbers” but there is no mention of held-out development sets. This risks test set overfitting and inflates reported gains. Likewise, for co-decoding on in-house data, selection of scheme (rank vs confidence) appears to be based on test outcomes.
- Reproducibility gaps and missing details: Crucial implementation specifics are absent: tokenization/segmentation for Japanese/Korean; BIO/IOB2 handling and constraint enforcement; decoding details for NN models (beam? teacher-forcing?); precise hyperparameters (context window, hidden sizes, number of prototypes in NN2, dropout, regularization, early stopping, learning rates); dictionary extraction thresholds; phrase table source and size; alignment training data and domain; vocabulary cutoffs; OOV handling (the description “project into English embeddings if found in English vocabulary” is unclear for target OOVs). No code/data release; no random seed or variance reporting; no significance testing.
- Co-decoding may violate sequence constraints: The merge procedure defines conflict resolution but does not guarantee valid BIO sequences or global consistency. There is no description of post-processing (e.g., constrained Viterbi) to enforce tagging constraints after merging. This could artificially inflate span counts or produce ill-formed sequences.
- Claims and baselines: The argument that CRF training is infeasible with 50+ labels and a few million tokens is unconvincing; modern linear-chain CRFs handle M≈100 with O(TM^2) decoding, and there exist scalable toolkits. The embedding mapping is not constrained (non-orthogonal) and is known to distort geometry; no ablation compares orthogonal vs unconstrained, or dictionary sizes (beyond a brief claim that larger dictionaries can hurt). There is no error analysis or per-entity-type breakdown, hindering understanding of where methods help/hurt. Finally, “comparable corpora” yet with sentence-level alignments and phrase tables implies significant parallel resources; the resource requirements and how they differ from prior AP work are under-specified.

Questions to Authors
- Validation/tuning: How were q and n chosen for the in-house AP selection? Was any development set used, or were thresholds tuned on the test sets? Similarly, how was the co-decoding scheme selected for in-house experiments?
- BIO constraints: After co-decoding, do you run any constraint enforcement (e.g., invalid I- tags, overlapping spans)? If not, what fraction of outputs are ill-formed? If yes, please describe the algorithm.
- Mapping details: Did you try orthogonal Procrustes constraints for M or post-hoc normalization (mean-centering, whitening)? Any comparison to CSLS or other cross-lingual mapping methods? Please provide ablations on dictionary size, weighting, and mapping regularization.
- Resources: What are the sources and sizes of the “comparable corpora” and phrase tables? How were sentence alignments obtained for comparable data? What is the coverage and noise level of the bilingual dictionaries per language?
- Implementation specifics: Provide tokenization/segmentation tools per language; treatment of casing; POS usage (if any); NN1/NN2 hyperparameters (embedding window, hidden size, prototypes, optimizer, LR, epochs, batch sizes, dropout); feature templates for MEMM; decoding beam widths. How are target OOVs represented before mapping?
- Significance/variance: Are improvements over baselines and between co-decoding schemes statistically significant? Report mean±std over multiple runs.
- Domain/type mismatch: For in-house data with 50+ types, how are types aligned/compatible across AP and RP? Any analysis on which types benefit most? How does domain mismatch between English training and target test affect performance?

Overall recommendation
- While the paper is practical and shows promising gains from simple ideas, the methodological novelty is limited, key comparisons and ablations are missing, and there are serious concerns about tuning and reproducibility. Given these issues, I lean toward rejection unless the authors can address validation leakage, add stronger baselines/ablations, and substantially improve experimental transparency.
