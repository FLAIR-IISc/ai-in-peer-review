Summary and main contributions
- A simple, effective data-selection scheme for annotation projection from comparable corpora. The method builds an entity–tag frequency table over projected annotations and assigns each target sentence a quality score q(y) (mean frequency of its projected entity labels) and an information score n(y) (entity count). Selecting sentences with q(y) ≥ q and n(y) ≥ n substantially improves AP-based NER when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean; Table 2).
- A direct model transfer method via representation projection: learn monolingual embeddings and a small weighted bilingual dictionary to fit a linear mapping from target language to English, then apply an English-only NN NER (NN1/NN2) to the mapped embeddings without any target-language retraining (Section 4).
- Two co-decoding schemes to combine AP and representation-projection outputs: an exclude-O confidence-based method and a rank-based method prioritizing AP spans, yielding consistent gains and SOTA cross-lingual NER on CoNLL test data compared to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) (Tables 4–5), and strong results on in-house 50+ type datasets (Table 3).

Strengths
- Practical, low-resource effectiveness. The sentence-level data selection is simple, requires no extra supervision, and delivers large gains precisely where annotation projection is most brittle (low alignment quality). The improvements are substantial (Japanese: 41.1→55.0; Korean: 18.7→38.7 F1, Table 2), demonstrating real utility in weak settings.
- Universal, training-free transfer at test time. The representation projection approach allows a single English NN NER to be reused across languages by linear mapping with only a 5–6k word dictionary. This is attractive for scalability and deployment: no per-language retraining, no language-specific features, and minimal bilingual resources (Section 4.2).
- Complementary error profiles and effective combination. The paper documents that AP has high precision/low recall while projection-based NN2 is more balanced. The rank-based co-decoding that first includes AP spans and then adds non-conflicting NN spans improves F1 across languages (e.g., German in-house: 66.4→69.3 with AP+NN2; Portuguese: 62.9→69.9; Table 3). On CoNLL dev, AP+NN1 via confidence-based co-decoding is best for Spanish and Dutch (e.g., 59.8→63.4; 65.4→66.7, Table 4).
- Broad empirical evaluation. Results span four in-house languages with >50 entity types and three CoNLL languages with standard 4 types. The paper reports precision/recall/F1, shows comparisons to supervised upper bounds, and demonstrates improvements over two strong cross-lingual baselines (Table 5), indicating robustness across settings.
- Methodological clarity and resource frugality. The mapping uses a weighted least squares objective on a small dictionary (Eq. 7), and the AP selection uses only redundancy in the projected data (no reliance on alignment confidences). Both are easy to reproduce and extend.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in representation learning and combination. The embedding mapping follows Mikolov et al. (2013b) with minor weighting; no exploration of more recent or stronger cross-lingual embedding techniques (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes constraints, CSLS, joint multilingual training, or unsupervised alignment). The co-decoding strategies are heuristic; no principled calibration or joint decoding is attempted.
- Incomplete reproducibility and data transparency. The in-house datasets (50+ types), the comparable corpora, and the alignment/phrase-table sources are not described in sufficient detail (sizes, domains, license) and are not available. It is unclear how phrase tables/dictionaries were obtained for all language pairs, which affects the “weakly supervised” claim.
- Missing analytical ablations. Key sensitivities are not quantified: dictionary size/quality and weighting, choice of embedding dimensions (300 vs. 800), orthogonality constraints, alignment quality vs. AP gains, and the impact of tuning thresholds q and n on the coverage–quality trade-off. An analysis per entity type and per language family would strengthen the claims.
- Fairness and comparability. AP trains target-language MEMMs on up to millions of projected tokens, whereas the representation-projection method uses no target training. This makes direct comparisons challenging; a discussion of compute and data budgets would help. Moreover, comparisons to contemporary cross-lingual NER methods using multilingual contextual encoders (or character-aware transfer) are absent.
- Statistical rigor. No significance testing is reported. Given some gains are modest on CoNLL (e.g., 1–3 F1), confidence intervals or tests would increase confidence in claims.

Questions to authors
- Alignment and dictionary resources: What corpora and tools produced the alignments and phrase tables for each language pair? Are they truly comparable (not parallel), and what is the coverage? How many sentence pairs and tokens per language? Could you quantify the overlap between dictionary entries and test vocabularies?
- Mapping design: Did you experiment with orthogonality constraints or CSLS-normalized nearest neighbors? How sensitive are results to dictionary size and weighting scheme wi = P(x|y)? Any results with unsupervised or induced bilingual lexicons?
- Data selection: How were q and n tuned (dev set vs. held-out)? How sensitive is performance to these thresholds? Did you try incorporating alignment confidence or span-consistency scores directly into q(y)?
- Calibration and co-decoding: How are per-token probabilities produced and calibrated across MEMM and NN? Did you try simple temperature scaling or Platt scaling to improve confidence-based fusion? What fraction of conflicts arise from boundary vs. label disagreements?
- Error analysis: Which entity types benefit most/least from AP selection and from representation projection? Are improvements larger for head entities (PER/LOC) versus fine-grained types in the 50+ type schema?

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper combines two straightforward but effective ideas and demonstrates consistent, practical gains across multiple languages, with particularly compelling improvements under noisy projection. The methodological novelty is modest, and more analysis and transparency would strengthen the work, but the empirical utility and simplicity make it a valuable contribution.
