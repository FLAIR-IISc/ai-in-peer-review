- Paper proposes two complementary projection strategies for cross-lingual NER: annotation-projection with heuristic sentence selection, and representation-projection via weighted linear bilingual mapping.
- Introduces two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based prioritizing annotation-projection) to combine outputs.
- Main contribution 1: simple, effective sentence selection for annotation projection using entity tag-consistency across occurrences.
- Main contribution 2: practical representation-projection setup using weighted linear mapping from target to English embeddings and a minimal English NN NER.
- Main contribution 3: lightweight co-decoding that leverages complementary precision/recall profiles of the two methods.
- Empirical results: co-decoding consistently improves F1 over individual components and outperforms prior cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL and in-house datasets.
- Strength: complementary design with solid empirical gains (notable F1 improvements across languages, sometimes close to supervised targets).
- Strength: pragmatic weak supervision — requires only monolingual corpora and a small bilingual lexicon, no target annotations or per-language retraining.
- Strength: sensible engineering choices (MEMMs for speed, weighted least squares mapping, NN architectures explored) and per-language analyses tying gains to alignment quality.
- Strength: broad evaluation on both fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL datasets with relevant baselines.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty — builds on well-known techniques with straightforward new heuristics.
- Weakness: resource assumptions conflict with “low-resource” claim — relies on alignments and phrase tables that may be unavailable or noisy for true low-resource languages.
- Weakness: potential selection bias and missing ablations — threshold tuning procedure unclear, no significance testing, and key ablations (dictionary size, mapping dims, prototype layer, alignment sensitivity) are missing.
- Weakness: context-insensitive representations — static embeddings and local NN ignore sense/context and subword signals important for NER, limiting generalization.
- Weakness: reproducibility and clarity gaps — many training and data-prep details (corpus sizes, word2vec settings, alignment pipeline, NN regularization, confidence calibration) are missing.
- Question: How were (q, n) selection thresholds chosen for in-house experiments and can performance curves vs q and n be reported?
- Question: What are the sources of “comparable corpora,” how are alignments obtained for non-parallel data, and do results hold with truly comparable inputs?
- Question: How sensitive is representation transfer to dictionary size/quality and the weighting by P(x|y)? Provide ablations (e.g., 1k/3k/10k pairs; unweighted vs weighted).
- Question: Did the authors try calibration (Platt/temperature) or a learned meta-decoder to fuse systems, and how do those compare to heuristics?
- Question: How does selection handle inflected/multiword variants — any lemmatization/normalization — and what is performance by entity frequency/length?
- Overall: Well-executed, practical combination with useful empirical gains; acceptance recommended if authors address tuning/protocol clarity, add key ablations, and better justify applicability to comparable/low-resource settings.
