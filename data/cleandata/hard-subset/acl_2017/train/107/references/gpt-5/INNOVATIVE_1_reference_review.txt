Summary and main contributions
- Universal direct-transfer NER via representation projection: Train a single English NER (NN using only word embeddings), learn a small, weighted linear mapping from target-language embeddings into English space, and decode target text with the English model without retraining. This offers a simple, scalable route to multilingual NER with zero target-language supervision.
- Effective annotation projection from comparable corpora: A frequency-based quality metric over projected entity–tag pairs and a (quality, entity-count) thresholding scheme to select high-quality projected sentences when alignments are noisy.
- Simple, complementary co-decoding: Two schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) that combine the high-precision annotation-projection system with the more balanced representation-projection system to outperform each individually.

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness and practicality of universal direct transfer: Mapping target embeddings into English and decoding with one model is elegant and removes the need to retrain per language. Despite its simplicity, it works across typologically diverse languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch), and even with a fine-grained label set (>50 types) on in-house data. This is a strong, broadly applicable idea likely to influence follow-on work in multilingual IE.
- Strong empirical gains from principled data selection: The proposed frequency-based sentence quality metric and (q, n) selection substantially improve annotation projection when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean). Using comparable corpora rather than strictly parallel data increases practicality and scalability.
- Complementarity captured via co-decoding: The paper characterizes precision/recall profiles of the two projections (AP: high precision/low recall; RP: balanced), and shows that simple co-decoding yields consistent and sizable gains on both in-house and CoNLL sets (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev), outperforming prior SOTA transfer methods (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) on test.
- Efficiency and scope: MEMM choice for many labels is justified; the linear mapping uses small dictionaries (≈5–6K pairs), training is lightweight, and the approach avoids gazetteers and language-specific features—sensible for low-resource settings.
- Clear, multi-setting evaluation: Results across multiple languages and two label granularities (fine-grained in-house vs. CoNLL 4-class) help establish robustness, and supervised upper bounds contextualize the weakly supervised performance.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Representation projection relies on standard bilingual mapping (Mikolov et al.), the sentence selection is heuristic, and co-decoding is rule-based. The paper’s novelty is primarily in the combination and application to NER rather than new algorithms or theory.
- Reproducibility and missing details: Important implementation choices are under-specified: sources/sizes of comparable corpora; phrase-table construction; tokenization/segmentation (critical for Japanese/Korean); hyperparameters for NN1/NN2; whether mappings are constrained (orthogonal Procrustes), centered/normalized embeddings; how unk vectors are trained; how confidence scores are computed/calibrated. No code/resources are announced.
- Tuning with “no target supervision” unclear: The data selection thresholds (q, n) are “tried” and best numbers reported. Without target dev labels, how were these set? If a target dev set was used, that weakens the zero-supervision claim; if not, an unsupervised tuning procedure should be described and evaluated.
- Evaluation breadth and analysis: Baselines focus on two prior works; comparisons to alternative cross-lingual embedding methods (e.g., joint bilingual/multilingual embeddings, document-level objectives) and feature-rich transfer (character/subword) are missing. There is no statistical significance testing, limited ablation (weighted vs. unweighted mapping, dims 300 vs. 800, dictionary size), and little error analysis (e.g., entity boundary vs. type errors, OOV/morphology effects).
- Heuristics need justification: The observation that larger dictionaries hurt mapping is intriguing but unexplained; exclude-O and rank-based decoding are plausible but ad hoc; calibration issues are acknowledged but not addressed with principled methods (e.g., temperature scaling, constrained decoding, stacking).

Questions to authors
- How were the (q, n) thresholds chosen without using target-language annotations? Can you provide an unsupervised selection/tuning protocol and its performance?
- Details of the embedding mapping: Did you center/length-normalize embeddings? Use orthogonality constraints (Procrustes)? Any regularization? Can you report ablations on weighted vs. unweighted L2, dictionary size, and embedding dimensionality (300 vs. 800)?
- Why do larger dictionaries degrade performance? Is it due to noise in low-frequency pairs, domain mismatch, or hubness? Would frequency cutoffs plus orthogonality alleviate this?
- Tokenization/segmentation: What segmenters were used for Japanese/Korean/German/Portuguese? How sensitive are results to segmentation quality?
- Confidence calibration and co-decoding: How are confidences computed for MEMM vs. NN, and are they calibrated (e.g., temperature scaling, Platt)? Did you try simple calibration before exclude-O? Could constrained decoding (e.g., using AP spans as must-keep constraints) further improve results?
- Resources: Are the comparable corpora, alignment models, dictionaries, and trained mappings available to facilitate replication?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, practical, and broadly applicable direction—universal NER via representation projection—plus effective heuristics for data selection and co-decoding. While the methods are not deeply novel, the idea, scope, and empirical gains make this a valuable contribution for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. I encourage acceptance, contingent on clarifying tuning without target supervision and adding key ablations.
