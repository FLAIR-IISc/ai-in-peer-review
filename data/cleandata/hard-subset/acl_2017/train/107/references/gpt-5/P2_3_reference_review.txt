Summary and contributions
This paper tackles zero-shot cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based approaches and their combination:
- A heuristic data selection method for annotation projection from comparable corpora. It builds an entity–label frequency table over projected data to estimate per-sentence annotation quality q(y) and selects sentences by thresholds on quality and number of entities. This substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy.
- A representation-projection approach that learns a linear mapping from target-language word embeddings to English and directly applies an English-only NN NER model, avoiding retraining for new languages.
- Two simple co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and a rank-based scheme that prioritizes high-precision AP outputs and augments with non-conflicting RP detections), which consistently improve F1 over either subsystem.

Strengths
- Practical, effective data selection for noisy projection. The frequency-based sentence quality metric is simple, language-agnostic, and yields large gains where alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean). This addresses a known but under-explored bottleneck in projection methods: selecting reliable supervision.
- Clean direct-transfer recipe via representation projection. The linear mapping with a small dictionary enables zero-shot NER with no target-side training. The approach is computationally light, plug-and-play (map to English space and decode), and uses only word embeddings, making it easy to deploy across many languages and domains.
- Complementarity and effective fusion. The paper documents that AP is high-precision/low-recall while RP is more balanced. The rank-based co-decoding leverages this by preserving AP entities and adding non-conflicting RP entities, improving F1 by 2.9–6.2 on in-house data and 1.3–3.6 on CoNLL dev.
- Broad evaluation with clear ablations. Results span seven languages across both in-house (50+ types) and CoNLL (4 types). The AP module is evaluated with/without data selection; RP includes two NN variants (NN1 vs NN2). Comparisons to prior cross-lingual NER (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) on CoNLL show consistent gains.
- Clarity and soundness. Methods are well-described (weighted least-squares mapping, selection metrics, conflict definition for co-decoding). The modeling choices are coherent with the stated constraints (no target annotation, minimal language-specific features).

Weaknesses
- Potential test-set tuning and missing dev protocol for data selection. Table 2 reports “best” thresholds (q, n) per language but does not specify a development set for tuning; if optimized on test data, the reported gains are overestimated. This is a significant methodological concern and should be rectified with a proper dev split or cross-validation.
- Lack of comparison to modern multilingual transfer baselines. No experiments against multilingual pretrained transformers (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) or more advanced cross-lingual embedding alignment methods (orthogonal Procrustes, VecMap, joint multilingual embeddings). Given current standards, this omission weakens claims of state of the art and limits the paper’s significance.
- Limited analysis of representation projection design choices. The linear mapping is unconstrained and maps 800-d to 300-d; no analysis of normalization, orthogonality constraints, or the impact of dictionary size/quality beyond a brief note. No diagnostics on mapping quality or error types. This makes it hard to generalize or optimize the approach.
- Reproducibility/resource concerns. Core resources (comparable corpora, alignment systems, phrase tables, monolingual data) appear proprietary and are not slated for release; many implementation details (embedding training params, dictionary creation specifics, thresholds tuning protocol, NN hyperparameters) are under-specified. This limits practical reproducibility.
- Modest novelty of co-decoding. The fusion strategies are heuristic and not learned; while effective, the conceptual novelty is limited. A learned meta-decoder or calibration analysis could strengthen this component.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How were the selection thresholds (q, n) chosen? Was a held-out dev set used for each language, or were thresholds tuned on test? Please clarify and, if necessary, re-run with a proper dev protocol.
- How does the proposed RP and co-decoding compare to zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R and to orthogonal Procrustes/VecMap-style mappings under the same data constraints?
- What are the precise sources and sizes of the comparable corpora and phrase tables? Can you release the frequency-based selection tool, dictionaries, and trained mappings to aid reproducibility?
- How sensitive is RP to dictionary size/coverage and to embedding dimensionalities? Did you try constraining M to be orthogonal or normalizing vectors?
- Are the improvements statistically significant across multiple runs? Please report variance or confidence intervals.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem with practical, scalable methods and provides solid empirical evidence across multiple languages. The data selection contribution is impactful, and the two views are complementary with effective fusion. However, the lack of a clear dev-set tuning protocol for selection, missing comparisons to current multilingual baselines, and limited reproducibility reduce confidence in the reported gains and the broader significance. If the authors can (1) clarify and fix the tuning protocol, (2) add strong modern baselines, and (3) improve reproducibility, the work would be substantially strengthened. As it stands, I would lean toward a weak reject in a contemporary ACL venue, but see promise if these issues are addressed.
