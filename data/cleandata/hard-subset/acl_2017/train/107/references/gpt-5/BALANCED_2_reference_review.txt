Summary
This paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotations. It presents: (a) an annotation-projection pipeline with a heuristic data selection scheme that filters noisy projection-labeled sentences from comparable corpora using an entity/tag frequency-based reliability score; (b) a direct model transfer approach that projects target-language word embeddings into English via a weighted linear mapping and applies an English-only NN NER; and (c) two co-decoding strategies that combine the two systems (confidence-based with exclude-O, and a rank-based scheme prioritizing the high-precision projection system). Experiments on in-house datasets (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, >50 types) and CoNLL (Spanish, Dutch, German, 4 types) show gains for data selection, complementary precision/recall profiles for the two projections, and consistent improvements from co-decoding, outperforming earlier cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective data selection criterion for annotation projection that leverages entity/tag frequency consistency to filter noisy comparable-corpus alignments.
2) A practical representation projection method for direct model transfer that uses a small, weighted bilingual dictionary to linearly map embeddings to English, enabling a single universal NER model.
3) Empirical demonstration that a rank-based co-decoding scheme can exploit complementary strengths (precision vs recall) of the two projections to deliver consistent gains across several languages.

Strengths
- Practical, resource-lean cross-lingual transfer: The approach requires no target labeled data and relies on lightweight resources (monolingual corpora, a small bilingual dictionary; comparable corpora with alignments for projection). The embedding mapping is simple, fast, and language-agnostic, facilitating scalability to new languages.
- Effective noise mitigation for projection: The frequency-based sentence-level quality score and entity-count filter substantially improve performance when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean). This addresses a known vulnerability of projection methods and is supported by clear experimental evidence.
- Complementarity and combination: The paper identifies and exploits complementary error profiles (high-precision/low-recall for annotation projection vs more balanced for representation projection). The rank-based co-decoding improves the best individual system by 2.9–6.2 F1 on several in-house languages and yields consistent gains on CoNLL dev/test.
- Broad multilingual evaluation: Results span typologically diverse languages (Latin and non-Latin scripts) and two type inventories (>50 and 4). The methods beat strong classical cross-lingual baselines (Täckström; Tsai) and approach supervised performance in some setups, suggesting robustness.
- Clarity and simplicity: The techniques (weighted least squares mapping, frequency-based selection, simple conflict rules) are transparent and easy to reproduce conceptually, lowering barriers to practical adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to current literature: Annotation projection and selection via frequency consistency are heuristic and incremental; representation projection is a straightforward application of Mikolov-style linear mapping with a minor weighting tweak; co-decoding is rule-based. The work would benefit from stronger conceptual advances or principled learning-based combination.
- Missing comparisons to modern baselines: No evaluation against multilingual pretrained transformers (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) in zero-shot or translate-train settings, or stronger cross-lingual embeddings/mappings (e.g., VecMap/RSIM, MUSE, joint training with parallel data, multilingual sentence encoders). Given the current state of the field, this omission makes it hard to assess competitiveness.
- Reproducibility and resource transparency: The approach depends on proprietary comparable corpora, alignment systems, and in-house test sets (>50 types), with limited detail on availability. The dictionary extraction relies on phrase tables whose source/size/quality are not fully specified. Without released code/data, it is difficult to replicate results.
- Evaluation methodology and tuning clarity: For in-house experiments, the choice of thresholds (q, n) is “tried and best reported,” but it is unclear whether a held-out dev set was used or if test data informed tuning. Significance testing is absent. Architectural choices (MEMM vs CRF, local normalization) and omission of standard features (char/subword, gazetteers) may disadvantage baselines and confound comparisons.
- Potential bias in frequency-table selection: Building reliability scores from noisy projections risks reinforcing systematic errors (e.g., common mis-tags getting high frequency). The method lacks smoothing/regularization analyses, and there is limited ablation on dictionary size/weights, alignment quality, or language-specific effects.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: Can you add comparisons to mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot (English fine-tune, target inference), translate-train or translate-test, and stronger cross-lingual embedding methods (e.g., VecMap/MUSE), especially on CoNLL? How does your best co-decoding compare to these?
- Tuning protocol: How were q and n selected for in-house languages? Was there a separate dev set, or cross-validation? Please clarify to rule out test-set tuning.
- Resources: What are the sources/sizes/domains of the comparable corpora and alignment systems? How are alignment F1 scores computed (what gold, how large)? Will you release dictionaries, mappings, and selection code?
- Co-decoding details: How do you enforce BIO constraints when merging sequences and resolving conflicts? Do you calibrate confidences (e.g., temperature scaling) to make cross-system scores more comparable?
- Mapping ablations: Please report performance vs dictionary size, weighting scheme, dimensionality, and alternative mapping objectives (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes). How sensitive is the method to domain mismatch between monolingual corpora and NER data?
- Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative/quantitative error analyses (boundary vs type errors, language-specific phenomena like compounding or tokenization), and the impact of alignment noise vs English NER errors?
- Generalization: Have you tried adding character/subword features or multilingual subword embeddings in the representation-projection model? Do they help or hurt transfer?

Overall, the paper presents simple and effective engineering for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, with convincing gains over older baselines. To meet current ACL standards, it needs stronger comparisons to modern multilingual models, clearer tuning/reproducibility, and deeper analyses/ablations.
