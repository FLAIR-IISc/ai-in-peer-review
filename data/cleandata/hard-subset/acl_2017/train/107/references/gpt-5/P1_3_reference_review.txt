Summary and contributions
This paper tackles zero-resource cross-lingual NER by combining two projection-based paradigms and a co-decoding strategy:
1) A denoised annotation projection method from English to a target language using comparable (not necessarily parallel) corpora and word alignments. The key contribution is a simple, effective sentence-level data selection criterion based on entity–tag consistency statistics across the projected corpus.
2) A direct model transfer method via representation projection: learn monolingual embeddings, learn a weighted linear mapping from target to English embedding space using a small dictionary, and run a single English NER model (NN1/NN2) on mapped target embeddings.
3) Two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based, and a rank-based scheme prioritizing the high-precision annotation-projection outputs) that consistently outperform each component.

Strengths
- Clear, practical advances in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER
  - The proposed entity–tag consistency-based selection substantially improves annotation projection when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese, +20.0 F1 Korean). The heuristic is simple, computationally light, and effective, addressing a known pain point in projection approaches (noise from alignment and source tagger errors).
- Universal, retraining-free transfer via representation projection
  - Mapping target embeddings into English and decoding with a single English NER is attractive operationally: no per-language training, no reliance on target-language resources beyond monolingual text and a small dictionary. Results are competitive and, when combined with projection, push SOTA on CoNLL (versus Täckström et al. 2012; Tsai et al. 2016).
- Thoughtful combination of complementary views
  - The annotation projection system is high-precision/low-recall; the representation projection system is more balanced. The rank-based co-decoder that first accepts AP entities then adds non-conflicting RP entities is well-motivated and consistently improves F1 (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev).
- Broad evaluation across languages and tag sets
  - Experiments on four in-house languages with >50 types and CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German with 4 types demonstrate robustness. Including precision/recall breakdowns and comparisons to supervised upper bounds helps contextualize the gap.
- Sensible engineering choices and ablations
  - Two NN architectures (NN1/NN2), empirical tuning of dictionary size (~5–6K pairs), discussion of dimensionality choices, and two co-decoding strategies provide useful practical insights.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established ingredients; missing stronger baselines
  - Annotation projection with denoising via consistency heuristics resembles prior noise-robust projection/distant supervision ideas; weighted linear mapping for cross-lingual embeddings is standard. The novelty lies largely in the specific selection heuristic and the co-decoding recipe. Stronger embedding-mapping baselines (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes, CSLS, seed-dictionary bootstrapping) are not compared; this leaves open whether the reported gains are due to method choices or engineering.
- Resource assumptions and comparability
  - The approach relies on word alignments/phrase tables to build the dictionary and sentence alignments for annotation projection. It is unclear whether these are truly “comparable” corpora or weakly parallel; details on source, size, domain, and how alignments are obtained are sparse. Comparisons to Täckström/Tsai may not be apples-to-apples if resource types/sizes differ.
- Reproducibility and missing details
  - The in-house datasets, alignment systems, and selection thresholds are not publicly available; hyperparameters for NNs (context window, hidden sizes, learning rates, training regimes), data preprocessing (tokenization, casing, normalization), and exact dictionary construction thresholds are underspecified. No code or data release plans are stated.
- Analysis depth
  - There is minimal error analysis: no breakdown by entity type/length/frequency, language scripts (Latin vs. CJK), OOV handling, or sensitivity to dictionary size/quality and alignment quality. The claim that NN2 “balances precision/recall” could be supported with more systematic analysis or calibration diagnostics.
- Architectural constraints and omitted comparisons
  - The choice to avoid CRFs due to label set size is understandable, but many recent NER architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with char/affix features) are strong even under resource constraints. A comparison to multilingual subword/character models or multilingual pretraining (even lightweight) would situate the gains better. Also, an ablation on the weighted vs. unweighted dictionary regression and on the exclude-O heuristic would clarify where improvements originate.

Questions to authors
- Data and resources:
  - What is the exact nature of the “comparable corpora”? Are they sentence-aligned? How are sentence and word alignments produced for non-parallel data? Please detail sizes, domains, and overlap with test sets.
  - How is the bilingual dictionary derived from phrase tables if there is no parallel data? Do you use external parallel corpora to train the alignment/phrase tables?
- Selection heuristic:
  - How are entity strings normalized across sentences/languages (casing, punctuation, morphology)? How do you handle multiword variants and transliterations? Does the heuristic overfit to very frequent entities?
  - Please provide a sensitivity analysis for q and n and the trade-off between selected size and F1.
- Representation projection:
  - Why not use an orthogonal (Procrustes) mapping or CSLS retrieval, which are known to improve cross-lingual alignment? Did you try constraining Mfe or comparing to unweighted least squares?
  - How sensitive is performance to dictionary size and noise? What happens below 1K pairs?
- Co-decoding:
  - How are confidence scores calibrated across systems? Did you consider simple temperature scaling or Platt scaling to improve the confidence-based fusion?
  - In the rank-based scheme, how do you resolve tag disagreements on identical spans? Does always prioritizing AP hurt recall for rare types?

Additional comments
- Please report entity-type-wise F1 and OOV/entity-length breakdowns.
- Clarify training corpora and hyperparameters for word2vec and the NNs.
- Consider releasing the selection code and a small benchmark to foster reproducibility.

Overall, this submission offers a solid, pragmatic package for zero-resource cross-lingual NER with clear empirical gains, though methodological novelty is moderate and some experimental details and baselines are missing.
