Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotations via two projection-based approaches and their combination:
- Annotation projection with data selection: Project English NER labels to target sentences via word/sentence alignments from comparable corpora, then select “high-quality” projected sentences using an entity-level reliability table and sentence-level quality/coverage thresholds.
- Representation projection (direct model transfer): Train an English-only NER model using only word embeddings and project target-language word embeddings into English space via a weighted linear mapping learned from a small bilingual dictionary, enabling zero-retraining transfer.
- Co-decoding: Combine outputs from the two systems using either an exclude-O confidence-based scheme or a rank-based scheme that prioritizes high-precision entities from annotation projection and augments recall with non-conflicting entities from representation projection.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical co-decoding strategy that exploits complementary precision/recall profiles of two weakly supervised systems and yields consistent F1 gains across languages (notably 2.9–6.2 F1 on in-house data; 1.3–3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev).
2) An effective heuristic for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data based on entity-tag frequency reliability and sentence-level thresholds, with large improvements when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 JP, +20.0 F1 KO).
3) A straightforward but generalizable representation-projection transfer for NER that requires only monolingual embeddings and a small weighted dictionary/mapping, enabling single-source training and multi-target decoding without retraining.

Strengths
- Clear empirical value of data selection: The ablation in Table 2 isolates the benefit of the selection heuristic, and the improvements correlate convincingly with alignment quality (largest gains for JP/KO with lower alignment F1). This supports the method’s utility in realistic noisy-projection settings.
- Complementarity exploited by co-decoding: The paper demonstrates that annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall and representation projection is more balanced; the rank-based co-decoder is a simple, transparent scheme that yields consistent gains across four in-house languages and three CoNLL languages. This is a practically useful recipe for weakly supervised NER.
- Resource efficiency and extensibility: The representation-projection approach requires only monolingual embeddings, a 5–6K word dictionary, and a linear map. After training an English model, new languages are “plugged in” via mapping without retraining. This is attractive for rapid multilingual coverage.
- Broad evaluation: Results on both fine-grained in-house data (>50 types) and standard CoNLL (4 types) across several typologically diverse languages. The paper reports precision/recall and exact-match F1, and discusses how alignment quality affects outcomes, lending insight rather than just scores.
- Sensible modeling choices for cross-lingual portability: English NER uses only word embeddings (no language-specific gazetteers/char features), supporting the claim of easy transfer. The neural models and MEMMs are described clearly with motivations (e.g., avoiding CRF training costs with many labels).

Weaknesses
- Questionable tuning protocol on in-house data: For Table 2, the paper states it “tried different threshold parameters q and n and reported the best numbers,” but does not mention a held-out development set. If best thresholds were chosen on the test set, this inflates reported gains and undermines methodological soundness. A clear dev/test split or cross-validation is needed.
- Limited novelty of core techniques: Annotation projection and dictionary-based linear cross-lingual mapping are established; the entity-frequency reliability table and co-decoding heuristics are incremental. The smoothing layer (NN2) is a modest architectural tweak. The paper’s contribution is more in engineering and integration than in new algorithms.
- Comparisons to stronger baselines are missing: Even at the time, stronger neural sequence tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character/subword features) and cross-lingual embedding transfer for NER existed; currently, multilingual transformers (mBERT/XLM-R) dramatically raise the bar for zero-shot transfer. The absence of comparisons to such baselines weakens claims of state-of-the-art performance.
- Reproducibility and resource clarity: Key resources appear in-house (alignment models, comparable corpora, in-house labeled test sets), with no indication of release. Details on dictionary construction (source, size per language, coverage, OOV handling), segmentation/tokenization for non-Latin scripts, and sensitivity to mapping size (the claim that larger dictionaries harm performance is not substantiated with experiments) are insufficient for replication.
- Statistical rigor and analysis depth: No significance testing is reported. Qualitative/error analyses are minimal. The co-decoder design is motivated by observed P/R profiles but lacks deeper analysis (e.g., calibration mismatch, confidence normalization, or learning-to-rank fusion). It’s unclear when rank- vs confidence-based co-decoding should be preferred beyond the development set criterion.

Questions to authors
- How were q and n selected for Table 2 on in-house data? Was there a held-out dev set? If not, can you provide results with a proper dev/test split or cross-validation to validate the selection scheme’s gains?
- Can you report sensitivity to dictionary size and composition for the mapping (e.g., 2K/5K/10K pairs, with/without weights)? You state larger dictionaries can harm; why?
- How are tokenization and sentence alignment handled in non-segmented scripts (Japanese/Korean), and how sensitive is the entity-frequency reliability table to segmentation inconsistencies or inflectional variation?
- Can you quantify the inference-time efficiency gains from the representation-projection approach (no retraining per language) and the added latency from co-decoding?
- For CoNLL, what exact external resources were used to obtain the phrase tables/dictionaries for mapping? Are these standardized and reproducible?

Overall assessment relative to ACL criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The work combines known ideas with useful heuristics; main novelty is in the co-decoding and quality-aware selection.
- Soundness/correctness: Mostly sound, but the tuning on in-house test sets is a concern; lack of significance testing and limited ablations leave some claims less substantiated.
- Meaningful comparison: Good against earlier cross-lingual NER (Täckström, Tsai), but missing stronger neural/multilingual baselines.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well structured; methodology is easy to follow.
- Impact: Practical, especially the co-decoding recipe and data selection heuristic for weakly supervised settings; broader impact is limited by reliance on proprietary resources and by missing comparisons to modern baselines.
