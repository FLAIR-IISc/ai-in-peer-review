Summary and main contributions
- A universal, training-once direct model transfer for cross-lingual NER via representation projection: learn monolingual embeddings, fit a weighted linear mapping from a target language to English using a small translation dictionary, and apply a single English NN-based NER model to mapped target embeddings without retraining. This “map-then-decode” design is simple, scalable, and bold.
- A quality-aware data selection scheme for annotation projection on comparable corpora: define per-entity reliability from projected tag frequencies and select target sentences with high average reliability and sufficient entity count, substantially improving weakly supervised NER when alignments are noisy.
- Two complementary co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) to fuse outputs from annotation and representation projection systems, yielding consistent gains and new SOTA on CoNLL cross-lingual NER against strong prior work.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and practicality of universal model transfer: The representation projection approach trains a single English NER and reuses it for any language via a lightweight mapping. This is a bold, elegant idea that removes per-language retraining and most feature engineering. It directly targets the core bottleneck of cross-lingual NER—annotation scarcity—by shifting effort to cheap monolingual data plus a small dictionary.
- Strong empirical results across diverse languages and settings: The paper reports gains on four in-house languages (JP/KR/DE/PT) and three CoNLL languages (ES/NL/DE), often approaching supervised systems trained on tens to hundreds of thousands of tokens (e.g., KR/DE). The combined systems consistently outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL, with notably large improvements for German.
- Effective, low-cost data selection for annotation projection: The per-entity frequency-derived reliability signal is simple yet impactful. It particularly shines where alignment is weak (JP/KR), delivering +14 to +20 F1 over using all projected data. The scheme makes projection feasible on comparable (not strictly parallel) corpora, improving practicality.
- Insightful complementarity and robust fusion: The paper identifies precision-recall profiles (annotation projection high-precision/low-recall vs representation projection more balanced) and designs two co-decoding strategies. The rank-based scheme that privileges high-precision entities while augmenting recall is empirically justified and yields the best overall F1 in most settings.
- Sensible engineering choices and clear ablations: Weighted least-squares mapping that emphasizes frequent dictionary pairs, tuning dictionary size (~5–6k pairs), comparison of NN1 vs NN2 architectures, and analysis of co-decoding (exclude-O vs rank-based) collectively demonstrate careful, reproducible design decisions.

Weaknesses
- Incremental elements and limited methodological novelty in components: The core techniques (annotation projection, linear cross-lingual mapping per Mikolov, locally normalized NNs) are known; the novel parts are mainly the specific selection heuristic, weighting, and the “decode-only” transfer setup for NER. While I value the conceptual unification and practicality, some readers may see parts as engineering rather than methodological leaps.
- Dependence on external bilingual resources and alignments: The approach still requires a phrase-table-derived dictionary and comparable corpora with alignments for annotation projection. The paper does not quantify how performance degrades with weaker dictionaries, no phrase tables, or noisier/nonexistent alignments—important for low-resource scenarios.
- Limited analysis on rare entities and type coverage: The data selection favors frequent entities (by design); the effect on tail entities/types is unclear. There is no per-entity-type breakdown or qualitative error analysis to understand which categories benefit or regress, nor how the 50+ type setup interacts with the mapping and co-decoding.
- Mapping choices and ablations could be deeper: The mapping is unconstrained linear regression; orthogonality constraints (Procrustes) often stabilize mappings. The observation that larger dictionaries hurt is intriguing but unexplained; an ablation on dictionary curation, weighting schemes, and embedding dimensions would strengthen the case.
- Feature limitations and morphology/OOV handling: The models purposely avoid char/subword features to keep language independence, but this may hurt morphologically rich languages. The “unk vector” strategy is under-specified, and there’s limited analysis of OOV rates and their impact.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Dictionary/mapping details: Did you try orthogonal (Procrustes) constraints or normalization of embeddings before mapping? How sensitive are results to dictionary size/quality and to weighting schemes? Why do larger dictionaries hurt?
- Selection thresholds: How are q and n tuned—do you require any target-language dev labels? If not, what criterion is used to pick them without peeking? How robust are results to these thresholds?
- Resource scenarios: Can representation projection work without phrase-table dictionaries (e.g., using identical strings or unsupervised bilingual lexicon induction)? Any results for truly low-resource settings?
- Error/type analysis: Which entity types gain most from each component and from co-decoding? How does performance vary by entity length/frequency and across languages with richer morphology?
- Calibration: For co-decoding, did you attempt probability calibration or temperature scaling to make confidences more comparable across systems?

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper proposes a clean, bold framing—universal NER via representation projection without retraining—and backs it with strong empirical evidence across multiple languages, plus a practical data selection method and effective fusion. While several components are incremental, the overall direction is novel and impactful for low/zero-shot multilingual NER. With more analysis on mapping choices and low-resource robustness, this could be a strong reference for cross-lingual transfer without target supervision.
