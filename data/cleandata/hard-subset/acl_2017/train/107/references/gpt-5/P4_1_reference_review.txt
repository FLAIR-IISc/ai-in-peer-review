Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations. It studies two projection-based approaches—annotation projection from English to target languages using aligned comparable corpora, and representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings—and proposes simple co-decoding schemes to combine them. Experiments across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese; plus CoNLL Spanish, Dutch, German) show that (i) careful selection of projected training data substantially improves annotation-projection NER when alignments are noisy, (ii) a direct model transfer using linear embedding mappings is viable, and (iii) co-decoding yields consistent gains and outperforms prior cross-lingual NER baselines.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) A heuristic but effective data selection method for annotation projection that scores sentences by entity-tag frequency consistency and entity count, yielding large F1 gains when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
2) A representation projection approach for direct model transfer: a linear mapping from target to English embedding space, enabling a single English NER (NN1/NN2) to run zero-shot on target languages.
3) Co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) that reliably improve over either projection approach alone and surpass prior cross-lingual NER systems on CoNLL test sets.

Strengths
- Clear empirical benefit of data selection for annotation projection in low-alignment settings. The paper quantifies alignment F1 for each language and reports substantial improvements when using the proposed quality thresholds (Table 2), demonstrating the method’s utility under realistic noise. This addresses a common weakness of annotation projection pipelines.
- Simple, resource-light direct model transfer. The embedding projection method uses a small bilingual dictionary (~5–6K pairs) and a linear mapping to reuse an English NER model without retraining. Despite its simplicity, it delivers competitive F1 and balanced precision/recall (NN2), broadening applicability when parallel corpora are limited.
- Complementary behaviors and successful combination. Annotation projection tends toward high precision/low recall; representation projection is more balanced. The co-decoding schemes exploit this complementarity: rank-based AP+NN2 yields the best weakly supervised results on several in-house languages; confidence-based AP+NN1 is best on CoNLL development. Gains over the better individual system are consistent (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 in Table 3).
- Broad multilingual evaluation and comparison to prior work. Results across seven target languages and two datasets (fine-grained and CoNLL) indicate robustness. The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) and reports improvements, especially in German, situating the contribution relative to recognized baselines.
- Computationally pragmatic modeling choices. Using MEMMs instead of CRFs for large label sets and millions of tokens is justified by training complexity. The neural NER uses only word embeddings, facilitating cross-lingual transfer without language-specific features.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in core techniques. The data selection heuristic (frequency-based consistency), linear embedding mapping (weighted least squares), and heuristic co-decoding are largely known ideas; the contribution is primarily in combining and applying them to NER. The paper would benefit from deeper methodological innovation (e.g., calibrated confidence fusion, constrained mappings).
- Insufficient analysis of the data selection scheme. The sentence quality score q(y) is derived from noisy projections, risking self-reinforcement of systematic errors. Only best-threshold results are reported; there is no sensitivity analysis across q and n, no ablation on the frequency table construction (e.g., entity normalization), and no study of failure modes (e.g., rare entities, morphology).
- Incomplete specification of resources for representation projection. The bilingual dictionary is extracted from a phrase table, but the origin, size, and domain of the parallel data used to build that phrase table are unspecified. This affects replicability and the true resource footprint; “no target annotation” still appears to rely on some parallel signal. Moreover, the chosen mapping does not use orthogonality constraints or hubness mitigation, and there is no comparison to alternative cross-lingual embedding methods.
- Co-decoding lacks principled calibration and evaluation details. Confidence comparability issues are acknowledged but not addressed beyond exclude-O or rank heuristics. There is no statistical significance testing of improvements, no per-entity-type analysis, and limited discussion of conflicts beyond a simple rule. A learned meta-decoder or calibration could be more robust.
- Reproducibility and missing hyperparameter detail. Key training details are sparse: sizes of monolingual corpora for embeddings, context windowing specifics, NN hyperparameters (e.g., hidden size, regularization, optimization), dictionary frequency thresholds, and mapping training settings. Code and data availability are not mentioned, limiting reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
1) What data were used to build the phrase tables for dictionary extraction (size, domains, availability)? Can your method work with lexicons mined from comparable corpora or unsupervised dictionaries, and how does performance change?
2) How sensitive are results to the q and n thresholds in the data selection? Is there a language-agnostic setting that performs robustly, and can you report performance curves rather than best-case points?
3) How are entities normalized when constructing the frequency table (e.g., casing, diacritics, morphology/segmentation)? How does the method handle inflectional variants and multiword entities across languages?
4) Did you try orthogonal Procrustes or other constraints for the embedding mapping? If so, how did they compare to the weighted least squares you use?
5) How large are the monolingual corpora used for training embeddings per language, and what preprocessing/tokenization was applied (especially for Japanese/Korean)?
6) Can you report statistical significance of the co-decoding gains and provide an error analysis (e.g., boundary vs. type errors; entities benefited/hurt by each component)?
