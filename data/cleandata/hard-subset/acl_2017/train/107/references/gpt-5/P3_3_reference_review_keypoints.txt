- Paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target annotations via annotation projection (AP), representation projection (RP), and co-decoding to combine them.
- AP uses a sentence-ranking data selection heuristic based on per-entity tag frequency reliability and entity count.
- RP learns a linear mapping from target embeddings to English using a small weighted bilingual dictionary and runs an English NN NER on projected target embeddings.
- Two co-decoding schemes proposed: exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based, to merge AP and RP outputs.
- Empirically, data selection yields large F1 gains under noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9/+20.0 F1 for JA/KO).
- Co-decoding consistently improves over either component alone and outperforms prior projection baselines on in-house and CoNLL data.
- Strength: complementary, pragmatic methods with AP being high-precision/low-recall and RP more balanced, enabling an effective rank-based combiner.
- Strength: simple, effective sentence-level data selection that substantially mitigates noisy alignment problems.
- Strength: thorough empirical study across fine-grained in-house (50+ types) and CoNLL (4 types) datasets and six languages including non-Latin scripts.
- Strength: RP is lightweight for deployment — no target-side model training required beyond monolingual data and a small dictionary.
- Weakness: Novelty is incremental; builds on known ideas (projection selection heuristics, linear embedding mapping) with mostly engineering contributions.
- Weakness: Resource assumptions unclear — reliance on sentence/word alignments and phrase tables may imply parallel data/SMT pipelines, weakening the “no target supervision” claim.
- Weakness: Tuning and evaluation concerns — unclear threshold selection protocol for in-house data, possible tuning on test, and missing significance testing.
- Weakness: Missing modern zero-shot baselines (mBERT/XLM-R, LASER/LaBSE) and ablations on dictionary size/quality, mapping constraints, regularization, and embedding dimensionality.
- Weakness: Methodological limits — AP treats surface forms as types (brittle for morphology/tokenization), RP lacks subword/char/CRF modeling, and co-decoding is heuristic and may violate BIO constraints.
- Question: How were AP thresholds (q, n) chosen for in-house experiments — held-out dev or test tuning?
- Question: What corpora/tools produced alignments and phrase tables — truly comparable or parallel, and how much parallel data was needed?
- Question: Were orthogonal/regularized mappings or weighted vs unweighted mappings compared, and was projection direction or shared-space mapping tried?
- Question: How are entity forms normalized in the frequency table (case/lemmatization/smoothing), and were subword/char features tested for RP?
- Question: How are BIO/IOB2 constraints enforced when merging spans in co-decoding — any global reconciliation or re-decoding step?
- Overall: Solid engineering paper showing complementary projection approaches; clarifying resource assumptions, tightening tuning, and adding stronger baselines/ablations would strengthen the work.
