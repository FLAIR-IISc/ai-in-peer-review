Summary and contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based approaches and simple combination heuristics. The contributions, in my view, are:
1) A pragmatic annotation-projection data selection heuristic: use entity-level tag frequency across projected data to score sentence quality and filter training instances, yielding sizable gains when alignments are noisy.
2) A representation-projection baseline: learn a linear mapping from target-space monolingual embeddings to English space using a small bilingual dictionary, then directly apply an English NN NER model to the mapped target inputs.
3) Simple co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) to combine the two systems, with consistent improvements over each individually on several languages and CoNLL benchmarks.

Strengths
- Practicality and breadth of evaluation: The paper presents a simple toolkit of weakly supervised techniques that are easy to adopt (sentence-level filtering, linear embedding mapping, heuristic combination). It evaluates across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese; plus CoNLL Spanish, Dutch, German), and considers both in-house (>50 entity types) and standard (CoNLL) settings. The clear precision/recall profiles and the consistent gains from co-decoding provide useful takeaways for practitioners.
- Empirical gains in low-alignment settings: The data selection heuristic delivers large improvements where alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean), which is exactly the challenging scenario for projection. This demonstrates that simple reliability scoring can materially mitigate noise.
- Solid cross-lingual baseline via representation projection: The direct transfer approach uses only word embeddings and a small bilingual dictionary (~5–6k pairs) and avoids retraining per target language. Despite its simplicity, it yields competitive F1 and complements the annotation projection system well.
- Comparative results on CoNLL: The combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test, notably for German, narrowing the gap to supervised systems.

Weaknesses
- Methodological tuning on test data: For the data selection thresholds (q, n), the paper states “we have tried different threshold parameters … and reported the best numbers” (Table 2). It is unclear whether a held-out development set in the target language (without labels) was used; as written, this suggests tuning on test, which invalidates those gains. This must be clarified and, if necessary, corrected using a development protocol that does not use test labels.
- Limited novelty and weak baselines: The core techniques are well-known or straightforward variants: (i) entity-frequency-based filtering over projected labels; (ii) linear bilingual mapping à la Mikolov et al. (2013b) without orthogonality constraints; (iii) heuristic output merging. Stronger baselines are missing: e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with cross-lingual embeddings and character/subword features (which often transfer well), or more robust bilingual mappings (orthogonal Procrustes, CSLS). The use of MEMMs rather than CRFs for target models is justified only by training time; however, first-order CRFs or neural CRF decoders are standard and could materially impact results.
- Data and supervision ambiguities: The representation-projection approach relies on a phrase table to extract bilingual dictionaries. This implies access to parallel data or a machine translation system, which undercuts the “no constraints on the type of data” claim. Similarly, the “comparable corpora with alignment information” for annotation projection are not sufficiently described (source, size, sentence alignment method, and supervision needed). The overall supervision footprint and resource requirements should be made explicit and fairly compared to baselines that also exploit bilingual signals.
- Reporting and analysis gaps: No statistical significance testing is reported. Important implementation details are underspecified (e.g., how entities are canonicalized for the frequency table across tokenization/morphology; BIO constraint handling in co-decoding; calibration of confidences; OOV treatment beyond a single unk vector). The choice of 800d target vs. 300d English embeddings and varying epochs per language introduces confounds without ablation. Error analysis is limited; it would help to see performance by entity type, frequency, and OOV rate, and to quantify the impact of mapping dictionary size/quality.
- Reproducibility: Key resources (in-house datasets with >50 types, alignment systems, phrase tables) are not available, which makes it hard to verify the claims. Without code or released mappings, community impact is reduced.

Questions to authors
1) How were q and n selected for Table 2? Was a target-language development set with gold labels used? If not, how did you avoid tuning on the test set? Please provide a protocol that does not use test labels and report results under that protocol.
2) What supervision/resources are required to build the phrase tables used for dictionary extraction? If parallel corpora or an MT system are needed, please quantify their size and compare resource requirements to prior baselines (e.g., Täckström, Tsai).
3) Why not enforce an orthogonality constraint on M (Procrustes)? Did you compare with orthogonal mapping and/or CSLS retrieval to mitigate hubness? Please add ablations.
4) For co-decoding, how do you ensure valid BIO sequences after merging? Do you run a constrained decoder post-merge? Please clarify and report BIO-violation rates if any.
5) Can you provide significance tests on CoNLL (e.g., paired bootstrap over sentences)? Also, report per-entity-type scores and the effect of dictionary size on representation projection.
6) Why were MEMMs chosen over CRFs or neural CRF decoders for the projection-labeled target training, especially on CoNLL (4 labels)? A CRF baseline seems feasible and more standard.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The work is practical and shows promising gains from simple heuristics, and the CoNLL comparisons are encouraging. However, the potential tuning on test data, reliance on unspecified bilingual resources, limited novelty relative to established techniques, and missing stronger baselines and significance testing undermine the rigor required for acceptance. Addressing the methodological concerns (especially proper tuning and clearer resource accounting) and strengthening baselines could make this a solid contribution.
