Summary
This paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation. It studies two projection-based approaches and their combination: (i) annotation projection from English to target sentences in comparable corpora, augmented with a heuristic data selection scheme that filters noisy projected labels; (ii) representation projection that learns a linear mapping from target to English word embedding space, enabling direct application of an English NER; and (iii) two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to merge the outputs. Experiments across multiple languages (in-house data with 50+ types and CoNLL with 4 types) show substantial gains from data selection when alignments are noisy, complementary precision/recall profiles of the two approaches, and consistent improvements from co-decoding, outperforming prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A simple, effective co-decoding framework that exploits complementary precision/recall traits of annotation and representation projection, yielding consistent gains over individual systems and prior state-of-the-art on CoNLL.
2) A heuristic quality- and density-based data selection method for annotation projection from comparable corpora that markedly improves performance when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 JP, +20.0 F1 KR).
3) A lightweight representation projection approach for direct NER transfer via weighted linear embedding mapping trained on small bilingual dictionaries.

Strengths
- Strong empirical complementarity and practical gains from co-decoding: Across in-house and CoNLL settings, combining systems consistently improves F1 (e.g., +2.9–6.2 F1 in-house; +1.3–3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev), validating the core idea that two weakly supervised views can be fused effectively. The rank-based scheme is especially sound given AP’s high-precision/low-recall profile.
- Effective data selection for projection-labeled training: The sentence-level quality score averaging entity-level frequency-based reliability is simple, requires no extra supervision, and yields large boosts precisely where needed (low alignment accuracy languages).
- Resource-light, scalable design: Representation projection uses small weighted dictionaries (~5–6K pairs) and monolingual embeddings, enabling quick onboarding of new languages without target-side training. The approach avoids gazetteers/features tied to specific languages.
- Breadth of evaluation: Multiple target languages, two families of models (MEMM vs. NN), two co-decoding schemes, and comparisons against prior cross-lingual NER. Reporting precision/recall clarifies complementary error modes.
- Clear articulation of engineering choices and constraints: Rationale for MEMM vs. CRF under many labels; avoiding language-specific resources; consistent setup across languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The main technical pieces (annotation projection, linear embedding mapping, heuristic ensembling) are adaptations of established ideas. The weighted dictionary regression is a modest tweak over Mikolov et al. (2013b). The co-decoding rules are heuristic rather than learned/calibrated.
- Missing or underpowered baselines and ablations: The representation approach is compared only to older cross-lingual feature methods; stronger modern baselines (e.g., multilingual contextual encoders, bilingual lexicon induction with orthogonal Procrustes/CSLS, or multilingual sequence models with character-level features) are absent. Ablations on dictionary size/quality, mapping constraints (orthogonality), and alternative selection criteria (e.g., confidence calibration, agreement-based filtering) would strengthen claims.
- Incomplete experimental details and fairness concerns: 
  • “Comparable” corpora still rely on alignments/phrase tables; how these are obtained without parallel data is unclear. 
  • The small “supervised” targets for in-house (e.g., 90–173K tokens) may not reflect best achievable supervised performance, complicating the “close to supervised” narrative (e.g., JP gap remains large: 59.6 vs 82.7 F1). 
  • No statistical significance testing is reported for key gains.
- Potential biases in the selection metric: The frequency-based reliability table may over-favor frequent entities and conflate homonyms/morphological variants, risking systematic bias against rare or polysemous entities. The effect on long-tail entities and error trade-offs is not analyzed.
- Modeling limitations: 
  • The NN models rely only on word embeddings with local context and no character/subword morphology or structured decoding, which are known to matter for NER. 
  • Score calibration across models is not addressed; the exclude-O confidence heuristic sidesteps but does not solve non-comparability of posteriors.

Questions to Authors
1) Comparable corpora and alignments: How are alignments/phrase tables obtained for comparable (non-parallel) data? Do you rely on external MT resources or induced alignments from comparable data? Please detail the alignment training data and pipeline.
2) Dictionary and mapping: Why do larger dictionaries harm mapping? Did you try orthogonality constraints and/or CSLS retrieval, which often improve cross-lingual mapping robustness? How sensitive are results to dictionary composition and weighting?
3) Selection bias: How do you disambiguate entity strings that are homonymous/morphologically variant in the frequency table? Any smoothing or canonicalization? Can you report performance by entity frequency buckets to assess bias?
4) Calibration and combination: Did you explore temperature scaling or Platt scaling to calibrate model confidences prior to co-decoding? Would a simple learned meta-classifier over span candidates outperform the fixed rules?
5) Error analysis and per-type breakdown: Can you provide per-entity-type F1 and typical error categories (boundary vs. label errors; transliteration/translation mismatches) to understand where each method helps most?
6) Modern baselines: How would your methods compare to multilingual contextual encoders and modern cross-lingual embedding induction? Could representation projection be layered on top of contextual subword models?
7) Reproducibility: Please specify monolingual corpus sizes, preprocessing (tokenization/casing), embedding training hyperparameters, and release code/data for the selection and co-decoding components.

Overall, the paper presents a solid, practical study demonstrating that two weakly supervised projections can be made complementary via simple selection and combination strategies. The empirical results are compelling, but the paper would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer data/aligner descriptions, calibration or learned ensembling, and deeper analysis of the selection mechanism’s biases and error profiles.
