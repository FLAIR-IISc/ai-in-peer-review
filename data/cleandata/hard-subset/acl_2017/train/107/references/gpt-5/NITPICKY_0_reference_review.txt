Summary and contributions
This paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation. It presents:
1) A heuristic data selection strategy for annotation projection that scores target sentences by aggregating per-entity tag-frequency reliability computed from projected labels; thresholding by quality and number of entities yields cleaner training data.
2) A direct model transfer method via representation projection: learn a weighted linear mapping from target to English embedding space using a small weighted bilingual dictionary, then run an English NER NN on mapped target tokens.
3) Two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) that combine the complementary outputs of the two systems.

Strengths
- Strong empirical coverage and consistent gains across settings. The paper evaluates on four in-house languages (>50 types) and three CoNLL languages (4 types), showing sizable improvements from data selection when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean) and outperforming prior cross-lingual NER (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test. The complementarity between AP (high precision, low recall) and representation projection (more balanced) is clearly demonstrated; co-decoding reliably improves over single views.
- Practical, simple, and efficient. The selection metric is easy to compute and language-agnostic; the representation projection uses a single English model for all targets, avoiding retraining. The approach is appealing for low-resource and engineering contexts.
- Sensible engineering of cross-lingual mapping. Using a weighted least-squares mapping with probabilities from a phrase table and constraining dictionary size acknowledges frequency/quality variation in seed pairs. The design choice of training only on embeddings makes the English model portable.
- Transparent reporting of alignment quality and its impact. The paper reports alignment F1 scores and qualitatively relates the effectiveness of selection to alignment noise. This helps diagnose where the approach helps most.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage/tuning concerns for data selection. For Table 2, the paper states “we have tried different threshold parameters q and n and reported the best numbers” without specifying a development set. This strongly suggests thresholds were chosen on the test set for in-house languages, invalidating the evaluation. A proper dev split or cross-validation is required. Additionally, since the quality table T is computed from all projection-labeled data, it can bias selection toward frequent entities and domains; no analysis is provided on long-tail entities or domain drift.
- Reproducibility gaps and insufficient experimental detail. Critical components are under-specified: sources and sizes of monolingual corpora per language; tokenization and segmentation (especially for Japanese/Korean); how “comparable corpora” are sentence-aligned and evaluated; phrase-table construction (parallel data used, aligner, pruning); dictionary extraction details and thresholds; the exact decoding procedure for the NN model (greedy vs Viterbi, beam, handling of IOB legality); handling of OOVs in representation projection (the paper claims “projected into their English embeddings if found in the English vocabulary,” which is unclear for OOV target tokens). Without these, results are hard to reproduce.
- Limited novelty. The representation projection largely follows established bilingual mapping (Mikolov et al., 2013b); the novelty seems to be weighted pairs and using it for NER. The annotation selection is a heuristic frequency-based denoising; similar reliability aggregation has been explored in projection literature. Co-decoding is heuristic (exclude-O preference; rank-based priority) without principled calibration or learning-to-rank. The contribution is more in system engineering than in methodological innovation.
- Missing ablations and analysis. Key design choices lack justification: 300-d English vs 800-d target embeddings; 1 epoch for EN/ES vs 5 for others; dictionary size “5–6K” and claim that larger dictionaries “might harm” (no plots/ablation); effect of enforcing orthogonality in mapping (known to help geometry preservation); sensitivity of selection to q and n; impact of NN1 vs NN2 with and without the smoothing layer; per-entity performance (e.g., rare types) and error analyses. No statistical significance testing is reported.
- Inconsistent or questionable technical claims. The statement that “CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words” because of ~50 types is too strong and dated; modern toolkits and techniques (stochastic/approximate training, regularization) handle this scale. Complexity discussion for MEMM/CRF is simplified and may mislead (order-o MEMM training is not strictly O(M) independent of o when using dynamic programming/beam). The merging procedure for co-decoding does not state how IOB well-formedness is guaranteed after combining spans, nor how nested/overlapping entities (if any) are handled.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How were q and n tuned for in-house datasets? Was there a dev split distinct from the reported test sets? Please provide results with a proper dev/tune protocol and report robustness across a grid of (q, n). How is T computed—over all projected data or only the candidate pool? Are counts case-normalized or lemmatized, and how do you handle segmentation (e.g., Japanese/Korean)?
- Representation projection details: What monolingual corpora and sizes per language were used to train embeddings? Why 300-d for English and 800-d for targets? Please provide ablations for dimensionality, epochs, and dictionary size. Did you test orthogonal Procrustes mappings vs unconstrained least squares? Any regularization?
- Dictionary/phrase table: What parallel data and aligners were used? How were P(x|y) estimated and smoothed? What pruning was applied? How sensitive are results to dictionary noise or domain mismatch?
- NN decoding and OOVs: For your locally normalized NN that conditions on previous tags, do you decode greedily or with Viterbi/beam? How do you enforce legal IOB sequences? For target OOV tokens lacking embeddings, how can you “project into their English embeddings if found in the English vocabulary”? Please clarify the exact fallback mechanism.
- Co-decoding: How do you resolve conflicts while maintaining IOB validity and consistent entity types across label sets? Can you report per-entity precision/recall changes after co-decoding and significance tests?
- Evaluation protocol: For in-house datasets, please specify test set composition, domains, and whether English training data/domain match target domains. Can you release code, selection scripts, and at least the bilingual dictionaries to enable reproduction?
