Summary
The paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without human annotation in target languages. It proposes: (i) an annotation projection approach that selects higher-quality projected training instances from noisy aligned comparable corpora using an entity–tag frequency reliability score; (ii) a direct model transfer approach via representation projection, where target-language word embeddings are linearly mapped to English, enabling an English-only neural NER to decode target text; and (iii) two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine outputs from the two systems. Experiments on in-house datasets (50+ entity types for Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and CoNLL (Spanish, Dutch, German; 4 types) show consistent gains from data selection and co-decoding, and improvements over prior cross-lingual methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A practical co-decoding framework that combines annotation projection and representation projection, yielding consistent improvements and state-of-the-art weakly supervised results on multiple languages and datasets.

2) An effective, simple data selection criterion for annotation projection based on per-entity tag frequency reliability and entity count per sentence; it significantly improves performance under low alignment quality.

3) Application of linear embedding-space projection for direct cross-lingual NER model transfer with no target-language training, along with a weighted dictionary-based mapping that emphasizes frequent translation pairs.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains from data selection under noisy alignment conditions: On in-house datasets, selecting sentences with high reliability (q) and sufficient entities (n) yields large improvements when alignments are weak (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 for Korean), while retaining or modestly improving performance for better-aligned languages (German, Portuguese). This supports the effectiveness of the quality metric and thresholds.

- Complementary systems and effective combination: Clear precision–recall trade-offs are documented (AP high precision/low recall; representation projection more balanced). The rank-based co-decoding that prioritizes AP mentions and augments with non-conflicting mentions from representation projection consistently improves F1 (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 over the best single system on in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev), demonstrating the utility of combining independent weak models.

- Direct model transfer via representation projection is simple and deployable: Mapping 5–6K dictionary entries with a weighted least squares linear transform and then decoding with a single English NN avoids retraining per target language. The approach is language-agnostic and only requires monolingual embeddings plus a small bilingual dictionary from a phrase table.

- Competitive and in several cases superior to prior cross-lingual NER approaches: On CoNLL test, the best co-decoding systems outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), especially for German, while narrowing the gap to supervised baselines. This indicates practical advances over contemporaneous cross-lingual transfer methods.

- Clarity and technical soundness of formulations: The models (CRF, MEMM, NN variants) and mapping objective are precisely defined. The rationale for MEMM vs CRF in large label settings and the architecture differences between NN1 and NN2 are clearly explained. The co-decoding conflict resolution is explicitly specified.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental across components: Annotation projection and cross-lingual embedding mappings are well-studied. The data selection heuristic (entity-level tag frequency reliability and sentence-level thresholds) is intuitive but relatively simple; related selection/quality estimation (e.g., alignment confidence, agreement-based filtering) is not compared. The representation projection uses a standard linear mapping; no comparison to orthogonal/Procrustes constraints, hubness mitigation, or joint multilingual embedding methods is provided.

- Dependency on parallel/aligned resources and alignment quality: While no target-language NER labels are used, both the AP approach and the dictionary for representation projection rely on aligned corpora/phrase tables. The paper does not quantify how much parallel or comparable data is required to build the phrase tables and the alignment models, nor does it analyze sensitivity to dictionary size more thoroughly beyond the 5–6K guidance.

- Limited analysis of calibration and error modes: The co-decoding strategies are motivated by qualitative precision/recall observations, but there is no quantitative calibration assessment (e.g., reliability diagrams) or statistical significance tests of improvements. Error analysis is minimal: boundary errors, type confusions, and language-specific challenges (script, morphology) are not dissected, particularly for agglutinative languages (Korean, Japanese).

- Reproducibility and fairness of comparisons: Important details are missing or limited, such as monolingual corpus sizes, training hyperparameters for embeddings and NER models, and the construction of phrase tables for each language pair. The in-house datasets use >50 types, while CoNLL uses 4; differences in type sets and domains complicate cross-study comparisons. It is unclear whether thresholds (q, n) were tuned strictly on development data; for in-house datasets, only test sets are mentioned.

- Baseline scope: The paper does not compare against stronger modern neural sequence tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character/subword features) adapted to cross-lingual settings, which would contextualize the benefit of the proposed methods vs more recent architectures. The choice of MEMM for AP training may underperform CRF or neural CRF in some regimes.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How are entity strings normalized when constructing the frequency table across projection-labeled sentences (e.g., casing, diacritics, tokenization, inflectional variants)? How are nested entities handled, if present?

- Threshold tuning: On what splits were q and n tuned for in-house datasets? If only test sets exist, how was overfitting avoided? Can you report sensitivity curves showing F1 vs q and n?

- Phrase tables/dictionaries: What corpora and sizes were used to induce the phrase tables? How much parallel/comparable data is required to obtain the 5–6K reliable pairs? Did you test orthogonal constraints (Procrustes) for the mapping or other regularization? Any results on sensitivity to dictionary size beyond the stated observation?

- Confidence calibration: How are confidence scores computed and calibrated across AP (MEMM) and NN decoders? Did you attempt temperature scaling or Platt scaling to improve the exclude-O and confidence-based fusion?

- OOV handling in representation projection: Section 4.3 mentions projecting words not in the target vocabulary “into their English embeddings if found.” Please clarify this mechanism and its frequency in practice. How are subword signals or character features considered for languages with rich morphology?

- Prior work comparisons: For CoNLL results, did you control for external resources used by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) (e.g., Wikipedia, cross-lingual entity links)? Please elaborate on resource parity.
