Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based approaches and their combination:
1) Annotation projection with heuristic data selection: Build weakly labeled target data by projecting English NER tags through sentence/word alignments, then select “high-quality” sentences using a sentence-level quality score q(y) computed from per-entity tag frequency statistics T(e,l), plus a minimum-entity threshold n(y) ≥ n.
2) Representation projection for direct model transfer: Train an English-only NN-based NER that uses word embeddings; learn a weighted linear mapping from target embeddings to the English embedding space using a small bilingual dictionary; decode target sentences with the English model.
3) Co-decoding: Combine the outputs of (1) and (2) using two heuristics: an exclude-O confidence scheme and a rank-based scheme that prioritizes AP spans and adds non-conflicting RP spans.

The methods are evaluated on in-house datasets (50+ types) for Japanese/Korean/German/Portuguese and on CoNLL 02/03 (4 types) for Spanish/Dutch/German. The combined systems outperform each individual component and reported baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Strengths
- Practical, simple, and broadly applicable pipeline: The projection/data selection and the linear embedding mapping are resource-lean and easy to port. The two-view combination is sensible and yields consistent gains across languages and type inventories.
- Clear empirical benefit from data selection: When alignments are poor (Japanese/Korean), the q(y), n(y) selection produces large F1 boosts (+13.9, +20.0). This is a useful takeaway for noisy projection settings.
- Co-decoding synergy: The rank-based scheme effectively exploits the high-precision/low-recall profile of AP and the more balanced RP, leading to sizable improvements (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev).
- Sensible weighted dictionary mapping: Weighting training pairs by P(x|y) is a modest but reasonable enhancement over unweighted least squares.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines and outdated comparisons: No comparison against modern multilingual transfer methods (mBERT/XLM-R, multilingual adapters, LaBSE) or contemporary cross-lingual NER with contextualized representations. This omission makes it hard to judge SOTA relevance in 2025. The main external baselines are from 2012/2016.
- Reproducibility gaps and incomplete specification: Key details are missing or ambiguous: corpora sources and domains; sentence alignment method for “comparable corpora”; definition and test sets for the reported alignment “F1”; tokenization/segmentation for Japanese/Korean; full MEMM feature templates; NN hyperparameters (context window, negatives, prototypes count for NN2, embedding training settings beyond epochs); dictionary extraction pipeline (parallel data? phrase table construction); calibration of confidences; decoding constraints (BIO/BILOU enforcement during co-decoding). Table 5 (test results vs baselines) is referenced but not included.
- Potential tuning on test and selection bias: For in-house experiments, q and n are “tried” and the best numbers reported (Table 2), but no held-out dev set is mentioned; this risks test-set peeking. Similarly, dictionary size is chosen because “larger dictionaries might harm” without principled selection or dev tuning protocol.
- Methodological oversights/claims: 
  • CRF scalability: The claim that CRFs “cannot handle training data with a few millions of words” with ~50+ types is disputable given modern toolchains (and first-order CRF complexity O(nM^2) is manageable). Using MEMM to avoid CRF cost may hurt performance (label bias), yet there is no direct CRF vs MEMM comparison under equal conditions.
  • “Comparable” vs “parallel” confusion: The approach presumes sentence-aligned pairs with word alignments; how are alignments obtained for comparable corpora? The pipeline practically requires parallel or at least high-quality alignments, which undermines the “comparable corpora” claim.
  • Representation mapping choice: The linear mapping is not constrained (no orthogonality), and stronger, standard alternatives (orthogonal Procrustes, CSLS, iterative refinement, MUSE/VecMap) are neither used nor compared. Also, asymmetric embedding dimensions (800→300) are arbitrary and unexplained.
  • Confidence handling: The paper itself notes non-comparable confidence scales, but proposes only exclude-O and a rank heuristic; no calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) or principled fusion is attempted.
- Evaluation/reporting issues: 
  • No statistical significance tests; no per-entity-type or error analysis; no robustness to domain shift; limited discussion of failure cases.
  • OOV handling is unclear (“project into English embeddings if found in the English vocabulary” contradicts being OOV on the target side); the “NER-trained unk vector” is mentioned but not described.
  • The CBOW “concatenates the context words … using a weight 1/dist” is contradictory (concatenate vs weighted sum) and under-specified.

Questions to authors
- Data/alignments: What are the sources/domains of the “comparable corpora”? How are sentence and word alignments produced for non-parallel data? What exactly is the alignment “F1” metric and the test sets used?
- Tuning protocol: How were q and n selected for in-house experiments? Was any dev set used? Similarly, how was the dictionary size threshold chosen?
- NN details: Context window, negatives, subsampling, learning rate/schedule, vocab cutoffs, parameter counts. For NN2, how many prototypes, how are they initialized/learned? Do you enforce BIO/BILOU constraints in decoding?
- Embeddings/mapping: Why 800d for targets and 300d for English? Did you try orthogonal Procrustes and/or CSLS? How sensitive are results to dictionary size/quality?
- Baselines: Can you compare against zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R and/or multilingual adapters on CoNLL and in-house? Also include a first-order CRF trained on projected data to test the MEMM choice.
- Missing results: Please provide the full Table 5 (test comparisons), scripts or sufficient details to reproduce, and significance testing.

Recommendation
While the paper presents practical heuristics with non-trivial gains and a useful combination strategy, the lack of modern baselines, reproducibility gaps, ambiguous alignment/embedding details, and possible tuning on test data outweigh the contributions. I recommend rejection in its current form; addressing the above and adding strong contemporary comparisons could make it competitive.
