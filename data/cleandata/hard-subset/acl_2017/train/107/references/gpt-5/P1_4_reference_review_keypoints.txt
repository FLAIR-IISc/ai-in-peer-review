- Paper proposes cross-lingual NER without target annotations using: (1) heuristic data selection for annotation projection, (2) representation projection via a linear embedding mapping, and (3) two co-decoding strategies to merge outputs.

- Heuristic data selection builds an entity-tag frequency table to score sentence quality and filters projected sentences by quality and entity count, improving performance with noisy alignments.

- Representation projection learns a weighted linear mapping from target to English embeddings using a small bilingual dictionary and applies an English NN NER directly to projected embeddings without per-language retraining.

- Co-decoding combines annotation projection (high precision) and representation projection (better recall) using exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based conflict-aware merging to boost recall.

- Strong empirical gains across languages and settings, notably large F1 improvements for languages with weak alignments (e.g., Japanese, Korean) and overall outperforming prior cross-lingual NER methods.

- Practical and scalable: methods minimize target-language supervision, avoid per-language retraining, and rely mainly on monolingual embeddings plus a small dictionary.

- Sensible analysis of precision/recall trade-offs and useful NN variants (with/without smoothing) that shed light on calibration and recall behavior.

- Broad evaluation on both fine-grained (>50 types) and standard CoNLL (4 types) tag sets, with supervised baselines for context.

- Clear, actionable heuristics (frequency-based sentence quality and conflict-aware rank merging) that are intuitive and reproducible.

- Incremental novelty: core components like linear mapping and annotation projection are established; main contributions are heuristic selection and co-decoding rules.

- Dependence on parallel/comparable data, alignment tools, and phrase-table-derived dictionaries undermines the “no supervision” claim and may limit applicability to truly low-resource languages.

- Incomplete sensitivity analysis and ablations: thresholds (q, n), impact on rare entities, reasons why larger dictionaries hurt, and alternatives (orthogonality, normalization) are not explored.

- Calibration and conflict resolution are heuristic with no principled grounding or calibration analysis and no learned fusion baselines presented.

- Reproducibility concerns: missing hyperparameters, embedding/data sources, alignment/comparable corpora details, and no code/data release plan.

- Question: How are entity surface forms canonicalized across tokenization/casing/scripts, and does filtering hurt long-tail recall or morphologically variant entities?

- Question: How are q and n thresholds selected without target labeled data—using dev sets, proxy metrics, or cross-validation on projected labels—and what are the sensitivity plots?

- Question: What are the sizes/sources of monolingual corpora per language, why do larger dictionaries degrade mapping, and were orthogonal Procrustes, mean-centering/whitening, or joint embeddings tested?

- Question: For languages without phrase tables, can dictionaries be built from weak signals (seed lexicons, identical strings), how does performance degrade, and what is the minimal dictionary size needed?

- Question: For the in-house >50 types, are tag sets identical across languages and how are categories absent in English handled during transfer?

- Suggestion: Report error analyses (boundary vs type errors; per-type F1; rare vs frequent entities) to clarify component-specific benefits.

- Suggestion: Consider a learned fusion/stacking approach with simple features (span length, source agreement, confidence) instead of heuristics.

- Suggestion: Add a character-level transferable component (Unicode categories, script features) to improve OOV handling in representation projection.
