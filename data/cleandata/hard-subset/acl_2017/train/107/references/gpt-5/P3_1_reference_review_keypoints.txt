- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER paradigms: annotation projection with data selection and representation projection via linear mapping to English embeddings, plus their combination.  
- Data selection: entity-tag frequency table scores sentence-level projection quality and thresholds by quality/entity count to filter noisy alignments.  
- Representation projection: learn monolingual embeddings per language and a weighted linear map from target to English using a small bilingual dictionary, then apply an English NER model to projected embeddings.  
- Co-decoding: two simple fusion schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based prioritization of high-precision AP outputs) that improve over individual systems.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation across in-house (>50 entity types) and CoNLL datasets with P/R/F1 and precision-recall analyses.  
- Ablations and alignment-quality statistics support claims about the effectiveness of selection, mapping variants (NN1 vs NN2), and co-decoders.  
- Data selection yields large gains under noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9 F1 JP, +20.0 KO), validating the selection metric’s utility.  
- Representation projection is resource-light and scalable: uses small bilingual dictionaries (~5–6K pairs) and a single English model for zero-shot transfer.  
- NN2 smoothing trades precision for recall intentionally to complement AP, showing design thoughtfulness.  
- Co-decoding exploits complementarity (AP high precision/low recall; rep-proj more balanced) to obtain consistent F1 improvements (in-house +2.9–+6.2; CoNLL dev +1.3–+3.6).  
- Paper situates work within prior alignment/projection and cross-lingual embedding literature appropriately.  
- Limited novelty in representation projection: linear mapping is standard; stronger methods (orthogonal Procrustes, CSLS, non-linear maps) and comparisons are missing.  
- Missing strong modern baselines: no zero-shot comparisons to multilingual pretrained encoders (mBERT/XLM-R), weakening empirical claims.  
- Potential bias/instability in selection metric: T computed from noisy projections may favor frequent entities, propagate systematic errors, and lack leave-one-out control.  
- Reproducibility concerns: missing details on phrase-table/dictionary construction, mapping constraints/regularization, embedding hyperparameters, tokenization, and BIO enforcement; in-house data/aligners are proprietary.  
- Resource assumptions limit applicability: requires comparable corpora, phrase tables, alignments, and sizable monolingual corpora—unclear performance in very low-resource settings or sensitivity to dictionary size.  
- Asks authors details on phrase table construction, coverage and sensitivity to dictionary size, and whether supervised alignments are required.  
- Requests clarification on mapping M constraints (orthogonality), experiments with mean-centering/length-normalization or CSLS, and their effect on NER.  
- Seeks whether frequency-based selection uses leave-one-out or smoothing, and requests sensitivity analyses over thresholds q and n and per-entity-type effects.  
- Asks how BIO constraints are enforced in co-decoding and how overlapping/conflicting entities are resolved.  
- Requests comparison to multilingual pretrained encoders (mBERT, XLM-R) in zero-shot CoNLL, or discussion of complementarity/competition.  
- Asks about impact of adding lightweight character/subword/language-agnostic features (script, word shape) to systems.  
- Suggests calibrating confidences (e.g., temperature scaling), integrating alignment link confidences into q(y), or learning a meta-combiner for better fusion.  
- Recommends reporting statistical significance on CoNLL and per-type F1 to show where methods excel or fail.
