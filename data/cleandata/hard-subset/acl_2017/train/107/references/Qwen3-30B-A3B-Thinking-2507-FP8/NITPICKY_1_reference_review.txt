

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve the quality of projected labels, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to the source language space. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The paper evaluates these approaches on in-house multilingual NER data and CoNLL data, showing improvements over previous cross-lingual NER methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The data selection scheme for annotation projection (modest contribution, with clear empirical validation)
2. The representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer (modest contribution, extending existing cross-lingual embedding work)
3. The co-decoding schemes to combine the two approaches (minor contribution, with limited novelty)

## Strengths
1. **Well-motivated data selection scheme**: The heuristic for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data is well-motivated and shows significant improvements (up to +20 F1 for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low. The frequency-based metric is intuitive and well-justified.

2. **Practical evaluation on multiple languages**: The paper evaluates on four languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) with varying alignment accuracy, providing a comprehensive view of the approach's effectiveness across different language pairs.

3. **Clear comparison to prior work**: The paper provides a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple languages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Fundamental technical error about CRF training time**: The paper states "CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words, since our NER system has over 50 entity types, and the training time of CRFs grows at least quadratically in the number of entity types." This is factually incorrect. CRF training time grows exponentially with the order of the model (e.g., first-order, second-order), not with the number of entity types. The number of entity types affects the size of the transition matrix (which grows quadratically with the number of entity types), but this is a constant factor for a given model order, not a growth rate. For a first-order CRF with M entity types, the transition matrix size is M x M (O(M²)), but M=50 is manageable (2500 elements), not prohibitive. This error undermines the paper's technical credibility.

2. **Misunderstanding of CBOW word2vec**: The paper claims to have "a variant of the CBOW word2vec model" that "concatenates the context words surrounding a target word x using a weight (1/dist(x,xc)) that decays with the distance of a context word xc to x." This is not standard CBOW. In standard CBOW, context words are averaged (or summed) with equal weights, not weighted by distance. The paper doesn't cite any prior work that uses this approach, making it unclear whether this is a novel variant or a misunderstanding of CBOW. This is a significant technical flaw.

3. **Ambiguous frequency table in Table 1**: The frequency values for "Estados Unidos" sum to exactly 1.000 (0.853 + 0.143 + 0.001 + 0.001 + 0.001 + 0.001 = 1.000). The paper doesn't clarify whether these are normalized probabilities or counts. If they're normalized probabilities, the sum should be 1, but the paper doesn't explain why the sum equals 1.000. If they're counts, the sum should exceed 1. This ambiguity makes it difficult to understand how the data selection scheme actually works.

4. **Lack of justification for different thresholds across languages**: The paper uses different threshold parameters (q, n) for different languages (Japanese: (0.8, 4), Korean: (0.9, 3), German: (0.5, 3), Portuguese: (0.5, 4)). It doesn't explain why these thresholds were chosen or whether they were tuned on development data. This lack of transparency undermines the validity of the results and makes it difficult to reproduce the experiments.

5. **Misleading claim about performance close to supervised learning**: For Korean, the best combined system (Rank: AP+NN2) achieves 51.8 F1, while supervised learning with 90K tokens achieves 54.8 F1. The paper claims "the best combined system achieves an F1 score close to the supervised Korean system." A 3-point difference in F1 score is significant in the NER domain and cannot reasonably be called "close." This misleading claim undermines the paper's significance.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that CRFs cannot handle training data with a few million words due to quadratic growth in the number of entity types. Can you provide evidence for this claim? How does this compare to standard CRF implementations that handle large datasets (e.g., in the CoNLL 2003 dataset)?

2. The paper describes a "variant of CBOW" that uses weighted context words based on distance. Can you provide references to prior work that uses this approach, or explain why this is a valid variant of CBOW?

3. In Table 1, the frequency values for "Estados Unidos" sum to 1.000. Are these normalized probabilities or counts? If they are normalized probabilities, why does the sum equal exactly 1.000? If they are counts, why does the sum equal exactly 1.000?

4. Why were different threshold parameters (q, n) used for different languages in the data selection scheme? Were these thresholds tuned on development data, and if so, what were the development results?

5. The paper claims the best combined system is "close" to supervised learning for Korean (51.8 vs 54.8 F1). How do you define "close" in this context? What is the typical F1 difference that would be considered "close" in the NER community?

## Additional Comments
- The paper has inconsistent formatting (e.g., "F1 Score" vs "F1" in tables).
- "GeoPolitical" should be "Geopolitical" in the Introduction.
- The reference to "Täckström et al., 2012" is incomplete in the References section.
- The paper claims "CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words," but the authors used CRFs for the source English NER system (trained with 320K tokens) and for the target-language NER system (trained with up to 3.37M tokens), contradicting their own claim.

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents a well-structured approach to cross-lingual NER with some empirical improvements, the fundamental technical errors (about CRF training time and CBOW variant), ambiguous presentation of key components (frequency table), and misleading claims (about performance being "close" to supervised learning) significantly undermine the paper's credibility. These are not minor issues but rather fundamental flaws that suggest a lack of understanding of basic NLP concepts. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with such significant technical inaccuracies.
