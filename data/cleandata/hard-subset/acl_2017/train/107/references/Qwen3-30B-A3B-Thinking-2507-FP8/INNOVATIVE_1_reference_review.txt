

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projected labels, and (2) representation projection that maps target language word embeddings to English vector space for direct NER model transfer. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine outputs from these approaches. The paper evaluates these methods on multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength)
1. **Co-decoding schemes combining two projection-based systems**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme that prioritizes the annotation projection system's output (which has higher precision) while adding entities from representation projection that don't conflict with the first system.
2. **Data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The frequency-based metric to measure annotation quality and select high-quality projection-labeled sentences.
3. **Representation projection approach**: Mapping target language word embeddings to English using linear mappings learned from a small bilingual dictionary.

## Strengths
1. **Practical application with clear benefits**: The paper addresses a genuine practical challenge in NER - building systems for low-resource languages without human annotation. The data selection scheme provides a concrete solution when alignment accuracy is low (evidenced by +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean), which is a valuable practical contribution.

2. **Combination approach shows promise**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme that combines the two projection-based systems (AP+NN2) achieves the highest F1 scores among all weakly supervised approaches for Korean, German, and Portuguese, improving over the best single system by 2.9-6.2 points. This demonstrates that combining complementary approaches can yield meaningful improvements.

3. **Clear methodology with thorough evaluation**: The paper provides detailed experimental results across multiple languages and evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F1), comparing against previous work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and supervised baselines. The evaluation design is sound and covers multiple languages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of significant novelty**: The core approaches (representation projection using cross-lingual embeddings, data selection based on label frequency) are not novel. These are standard techniques in the field that have been applied to NLP tasks before. The paper does not introduce a fundamentally new concept or direction that would justify acceptance under the reviewer's criteria of prioritizing "significant new concept or direction."

2. **Incremental contribution**: The improvements over previous work are modest (2.9-6.2 F1 points) and do not represent a substantial leap forward in the field. The paper is an incremental extension of existing techniques rather than a bold new approach. This is exactly the type of paper the reviewer would be inclined to reject.

3. **Limited theoretical insight**: The paper lacks any theoretical contribution or new understanding of cross-lingual transfer. The representation projection approach is simply a standard application of cross-lingual embeddings to NER, with no new insights about why or how it works better than other methods.

4. **No clear new direction for the field**: The paper does not propose a new research direction or paradigm for cross-lingual NER. It simply combines existing techniques in a practical way, which is valuable for practitioners but not for advancing the field.

5. **Comparison with recent work**: The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but does not compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., recent transformer-based cross-lingual approaches), making the claimed "outperforming state-of-the-art" claim questionable.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" approaches, but the comparison is only against older methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016). How do your results compare with more recent cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., those using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based approaches)?

2. The representation projection approach uses linear mappings between word embeddings. How does this compare with more sophisticated methods for cross-lingual embedding alignment (e.g., adversarial training, iterative projection), which might yield better results?

3. The data selection scheme relies on frequency tables of projected entities. What would happen if the English NER system made consistent errors on certain entities (e.g., always labeling "United States" as ORGANIZATION instead of GPE)? Would the frequency-based scheme reinforce these errors rather than correct them?

## Recommendation
Reject

This paper presents a methodologically sound but incremental contribution that fails to meet the bar for novelty and boldness that the reviewer prioritizes. While the data selection scheme and co-decoding approaches are practically useful, they are not sufficiently novel or transformative to justify acceptance. The paper is an incremental improvement over existing techniques rather than a significant new concept or direction. The modest improvements over previous work (2.9-6.2 F1 points) do not constitute the kind of "significant new concept" that would warrant acceptance under the reviewer's criteria.

The paper would be more appropriate for a more application-focused venue rather than ACL, which prioritizes theoretical contributions and novel directions. The authors should consider reframing their work as a practical engineering contribution rather than a novel research contribution.
