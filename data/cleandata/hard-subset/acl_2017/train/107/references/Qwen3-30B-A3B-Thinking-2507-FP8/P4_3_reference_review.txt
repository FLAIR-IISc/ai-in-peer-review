

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of cross-lingual NER (Named Entity Recognition) without requiring human annotation in the target language. It proposes two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve the quality of projected labels from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target language to English, allowing direct application of an English NER system. The paper also proposes two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves NER performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer that leverages cross-lingual word embeddings.
3. A rank-based co-decoding scheme that combines the outputs of the two projection-based systems to achieve better performance than either system alone.

## Strengths

1. **Significant practical contribution of the data selection scheme**: The paper identifies a critical limitation in previous annotation projection approaches (ignoring alignment accuracy) and proposes a simple yet effective data selection scheme. The experimental results clearly demonstrate that this scheme substantially improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean), with improvements diminishing as alignment accuracy increases. This addresses a real problem in the field and provides a valuable technique for practitioners.

2. **Thorough evaluation across multiple languages and datasets**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation on both in-house data (with fine-grained entity types across Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese) and standard CoNLL data (Spanish, Dutch, German). The results consistently show that the proposed approaches outperform previous state-of-the-art methods, with the combined system achieving F1 scores close to supervised learning (e.g., 69.3 F1 for German vs. 72.6 F1 for supervised). The evaluation design is robust, using multiple metrics (precision, recall, F1) across different languages and entity types.

3. **Well-motivated co-decoding scheme**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme is effectively motivated by the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (annotation projection has high precision, representation projection has better recall). The experimental results demonstrate that this scheme consistently outperforms both individual systems and the confidence-based co-decoding scheme, with improvements of 2.9-6.2 F1 points across languages. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the representation projection approach**: The core idea of projecting word embeddings across languages is not novel, as the paper acknowledges that similar approaches have been applied to other NLP tasks (document classification, parsing, POS tagging). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its specific application to NER from existing work in cross-lingual embeddings. The approach is technically sound but lacks significant methodological innovation.

2. **Ad-hoc nature of the data selection scheme**: The data selection scheme relies on frequency tables of entity labels to measure annotation quality, but the paper provides limited theoretical justification for why this metric correlates with true annotation quality. The threshold parameters (q, n) are tuned empirically without thorough analysis of their impact, which may limit generalizability to different datasets or languages.

3. **Lack of detail on comparable corpora**: The paper mentions "comparable corpora" but doesn't specify what these are, how they were collected, or how they differ from parallel corpora. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach and whether the results would hold with different comparable corpora.

4. **Insufficient comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with more recent cross-lingual NER approaches. For example, it doesn't compare with transformer-based methods (e.g., multilingual BERT) that have shown strong performance in cross-lingual tasks and might outperform the proposed methods.

5. **Simplistic co-decoding schemes**: While the co-decoding schemes are effective, they are relatively simple and don't represent significant methodological innovation. The paper could have explored more sophisticated combination methods beyond the rank-based and confidence-based schemes.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the authors justify the choice of English as the source language for all projections? Would the approach work better if a different source language was used for different target languages?

2. The paper mentions "comparable corpora" but doesn't provide details on how these were collected. Could the authors clarify what specific comparable corpora were used for each target language (e.g., domain, size, source)?

3. How does the representation projection approach handle out-of-vocabulary words that don't have good translations in the training dictionary? The paper states that such words are projected into "NER-trained unk vector," but it's unclear how this vector was trained.

4. The authors claim their approach outperforms previous work, but don't provide a comparison with recent cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., using multilingual BERT). Could they add such a comparison to strengthen their claims?
