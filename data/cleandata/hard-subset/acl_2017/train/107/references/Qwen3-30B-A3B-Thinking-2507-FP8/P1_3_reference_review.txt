

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme, and (2) a representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The main contributions are:

1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
2. A representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target language to English space, allowing a single English NER model to be applied to multiple languages without retraining.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches to achieve better performance than individual systems.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and Effective Data Selection Scheme**: The paper's most significant contribution is the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection. This addresses a critical limitation in previous work that didn't consider alignment quality when selecting sentence pairs for projection. The experimental results clearly demonstrate that this scheme significantly improves performance for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean), which is a major practical contribution to the field. The authors provide a well-motivated metric (frequency-based reliability score) that effectively filters noisy data.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Multiple Languages**: The paper evaluates both approaches across four different languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets. This extensive evaluation provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed methods beyond a single language pair. The results consistently show that the combined systems outperform individual approaches and previous state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Well-Designed Co-Decoding Schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme is particularly effective, combining the strengths of both projection approaches (high precision of annotation projection and better recall of representation projection). The paper demonstrates that this combined approach achieves F1 scores that are 2.9-6.2 points higher than the best individual system, which is a significant improvement. The empirical justification for the rank-based approach (based on observed precision/recall characteristics) is well-supported.

4. **Clear Implementation Details**: The paper provides sufficient technical details about the implementation of both approaches, including the specific parameters used for the neural networks and the data selection thresholds. This level of detail makes the work reproducible and increases confidence in the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Representation Projection Approach**: While the representation projection approach is well-executed, it's largely based on existing work (Mikolov et al., 2013b) and similar approaches used for other NLP tasks. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate how this approach specifically addresses NER challenges better than previous work. The novelty here is incremental rather than transformative.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent approaches that might use multilingual BERT or other transformer-based methods. For example, the paper doesn't mention or compare with work like the recent cross-lingual BERT-based NER systems that have achieved strong results. This weakens the claim of outperforming "state-of-the-art" approaches.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Data Selection Thresholds**: The paper uses empirically tuned thresholds (q and n) for data selection but doesn't provide a principled way to determine these thresholds without human-labeled data. The results show that different languages require different thresholds, but the paper doesn't explain why these thresholds vary or how they could be determined automatically for new languages without test data.

4. **Limited Analysis of Language-Specific Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze the limitations of their approach for languages with very different linguistic structures from English. For example, the approach might struggle with languages that have different word order, morphological complexity, or entity naming conventions. The evaluation is limited to languages that share some structural similarities with English (German, Portuguese) and East Asian languages (Japanese, Korean).

5. **Underestimated Impact of Alignment Quality**: While the paper acknowledges alignment accuracy as a factor, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the data selection scheme specifically handles different alignment quality levels. A more detailed analysis of the relationship between alignment accuracy and the effectiveness of the data selection scheme would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with recent cross-lingual NER approaches that use multilingual transformers (e.g., XLM-R) or other state-of-the-art methods beyond Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016)?

2. How would your data selection scheme perform if the alignment accuracy was extremely low (e.g., below 50%)? Is there a minimum alignment quality threshold below which the scheme becomes ineffective?

3. The paper mentions that the representation projection approach uses English as the universal language. How would the approach change if you wanted to use a different language as the universal language (e.g., for languages where English is not the most widely spoken)?

4. Could you elaborate on the limitations of your approach for languages with very different writing systems (e.g., Arabic or Chinese) compared to the languages evaluated in your experiments?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a practical and effective solution to the cross-lingual NER problem with significant improvements in weakly supervised settings. The data selection scheme is particularly strong and addresses a real limitation in previous work. However, the novelty of the representation projection approach is limited, and the comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods is insufficient. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding the comparison with recent methods and deeper analysis of language-specific limitations, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
