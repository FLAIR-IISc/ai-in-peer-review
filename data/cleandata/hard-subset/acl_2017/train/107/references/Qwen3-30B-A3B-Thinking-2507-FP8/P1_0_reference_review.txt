

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER (Named Entity Recognition) without human annotation in target languages. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to a source language (English) and uses a single English NER system for all languages. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches to achieve better performance.

## Main Contributions

1. **Heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The authors propose a metric based on entity frequency analysis to select high-quality projection-labeled data, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).

2. **Representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer**: This approach projects word embeddings from target languages to English using linear mappings, enabling the use of a single English NER system for multiple languages without retraining.

3. **Co-decoding schemes**: The authors design two smart schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, leveraging their complementary strengths (annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall).

## Strengths

1. **Practical and effective data selection scheme**: The paper identifies a critical limitation in previous annotation projection work (not considering alignment accuracy in data selection) and proposes a simple yet effective heuristic that significantly improves performance when alignment is poor. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the value of this approach, with substantial improvements (13.9-20.0 F1 points) for low-alignment languages like Japanese and Korean. This is a genuinely useful contribution for practitioners working with weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The authors provide comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house data with fine-grained entity types and standard CoNLL data. The results consistently show that their combined approaches outperform previous weakly supervised methods, with the best co-decoding systems achieving F1 scores close to supervised learning (e.g., 69.9% vs 75.6% for Portuguese). This thorough evaluation makes the claims compelling.

3. **Novel combination of complementary approaches**: The co-decoding schemes effectively leverage the complementary strengths of the two projection-based approaches. The rank-based scheme, which gives priority to the annotation projection system (high precision) and then adds entities from the representation projection system (more balanced precision/recall), demonstrates a smart way to combine models. The 2.9-6.2 F1 point improvements over individual systems are significant and well-documented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper only compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are now dated (2012 and 2016). The field has advanced significantly since then, with recent methods using multilingual BERT and other transformer-based models for cross-lingual NER. The authors should compare with more recent work (e.g., from 2020-2023) to properly position their contribution in the current landscape. Without this comparison, it's hard to assess how much progress this work represents.

2. **Insufficient analysis of the data selection scheme**: While the paper presents the data selection scheme and shows its effectiveness, it lacks a deeper analysis of why it works. For instance, the authors don't explore how the thresholds (q and n) should be set based on alignment accuracy, or what the relationship is between alignment accuracy and optimal threshold values. A more thorough analysis would strengthen the contribution and provide practical guidance for future work.

3. **Questionable claim about "no human annotation"**: The paper claims "no human annotation in the target languages," but the representation projection approach requires a small training dictionary (5K-6K unique word pairs) for cross-lingual mapping. This dictionary is typically extracted from phrase tables, which may require some human oversight or alignment effort. The authors should clarify whether this counts as "human annotation" and how this might limit the approach's applicability to languages with no available parallel data.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides strong quantitative results but doesn't include an error analysis to understand where the weakly supervised systems fail. This would help identify limitations of the approach and guide future improvements. For example, it would be valuable to know if the systems have particular difficulty with certain entity types or language structures.

5. **Limited discussion of scalability and computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed methods scale with the size of the comparable corpora or the computational resources required. Given that the data selection scheme processes large corpora (2-5.5M tokens), understanding the computational complexity would be important for practical adoption.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your approaches compare with recent transformer-based cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., using multilingual BERT or mBERT) that have achieved state-of-the-art results in recent years?

2. The paper states that the alignment accuracy varies across languages (Japanese: 69.4%, Korean: 62.0%, German: 76.1%, Portuguese: 88.0%). How does this variation affect the performance of your data selection scheme, and can you provide more insight into why alignment accuracy differs across languages?

3. In the representation projection approach, you require a small training dictionary (5K-6K unique word pairs). How would your approach perform if this dictionary was not available or was very small (e.g., for a very low-resource language)?

4. The paper uses English as the source language for all approaches. How would the performance change if a different source language was used, and what factors would influence the choice of source language?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the weakly supervised cross-lingual NER field with a practical and effective data selection scheme, a novel representation projection approach, and smart co-decoding schemes. The empirical results are strong and well-documented. However, the authors need to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by comparing with more recent methods and clarifying the "no human annotation" claim. These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and relevance to current research.
