

# ACL Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that requires no human annotation in the target language. The authors present two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method enhanced by a heuristic data selection scheme that selects high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to English, allowing direct application of an English NER model. They also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as CoNLL datasets for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection** that significantly improves cross-lingual NER performance when alignment accuracy is low. This addresses a critical limitation in existing annotation projection approaches that has been overlooked in prior work.

2. **A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer** that maps word embeddings from target languages to English, enabling the use of a single English NER model across multiple languages without retraining.

3. **Co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches**, with the rank-based scheme showing particularly strong results by leveraging the complementary strengths of the two methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and practical data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The authors identify a key limitation in existing annotation projection approaches (dependence on alignment accuracy) and propose a simple yet effective solution using frequency tables of projected entities. The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements (up to +20 F1 points for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low, which is a substantial contribution to the field. This is particularly valuable as alignment accuracy is often low in real-world comparable corpora.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets**: The paper evaluates the proposed methods on both in-house NER data (for Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and standard CoNLL datasets (for Spanish, Dutch, German). This broad evaluation across different languages and data characteristics strengthens the authors' claims about the effectiveness of their approach.

3. **Effective co-decoding schemes that demonstrate complementary strengths**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme, which gives priority to the annotation projection system (which has higher precision), significantly outperforms both individual systems. This shows a practical way to combine complementary approaches to achieve better results, which is an important contribution to weakly supervised learning.

4. **Strong empirical results that demonstrate clear improvements over prior work**: The best combined system outperforms previous weakly supervised approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) by 2.9-6.2 F1 points across multiple languages, and achieves F1 scores close to supervised learning baselines (trained with human-annotated data) for some languages. This is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with more recent cross-lingual NER methods**: The paper only compares with methods from 2012 and 2016, which are outdated in the current landscape of cross-lingual NER. The field has advanced significantly with transformer-based approaches like XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020) and multilingual BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), which have achieved state-of-the-art results. Without comparison to these recent methods, the claim that the approach "outperforms the state-of-the-art" is not well-supported.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why the methods work**: While the paper provides empirical results, it lacks deeper analysis of the mechanisms behind the success of the data selection scheme and representation projection. For instance, it's unclear how the frequency table specifically helps distinguish between correct and incorrect annotations, or what types of errors the data selection scheme corrects. This limits the theoretical contribution of the work.

3. **Inconsistent comparison with supervised baselines**: The paper compares weakly supervised approaches to supervised learning, but the amount of human-annotated training data for the supervised baselines varies significantly across languages (e.g., 90K tokens for Korean vs. 125K for German). This makes it difficult to assess whether the weakly supervised approach is truly competitive with supervised learning, as the supervised baselines have different amounts of training data.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of their approach (e.g., the data selection scheme vs. the co-decoding scheme). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of their method are most important for the observed performance gains.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't address how the method would perform for languages with very different syntax or morphology from English, or for languages with no comparable corpora available. The computational cost of the approach compared to other methods is also not discussed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform "the state-of-the-art" cross-lingual NER approaches, but only compares with methods from 2012 and 2016. Could you add a comparison with more recent methods (e.g., XLM-R, multilingual BERT) to better establish the novelty and significance of your approach?

2. In Table 3, the supervised baseline for Korean uses 90K tokens while for German it uses 125K tokens. Could you clarify whether the weakly supervised approach would be competitive with supervised learning if the supervised baseline had the same amount of training data for all languages?

3. Could you provide more details about the comparable corpora used for the annotation projection approach? Specifically, what is the source of the comparable corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, news articles), and how does the quality of these corpora affect the performance of your method?

4. The paper mentions that the representation projection approach uses a mapping from target language embeddings to English embeddings. How does this approach handle languages that don't have a direct mapping (e.g., due to missing words in the training dictionary)? Could you provide examples where this might fail?

5. Could you provide more detailed analysis of why the data selection scheme works particularly well when alignment accuracy is low? Specifically, how does the frequency table help distinguish between correct and incorrect annotations?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a practical and effective approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with clear empirical results. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is particularly valuable as it addresses a key limitation in existing methods. However, the lack of comparison with more recent cross-lingual NER methods and insufficient analysis of why the methods work well are significant weaknesses that limit the paper's contribution.

I recommend **rejection** due to the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, which is a critical requirement for ACL. However, with the addition of comparisons to more recent methods (particularly transformer-based approaches), a deeper analysis of the mechanisms behind the success of the proposed approaches, and ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each component, this paper could be significantly strengthened.

The authors should also address the inconsistent comparison with supervised baselines and provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach. With these improvements, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER.
