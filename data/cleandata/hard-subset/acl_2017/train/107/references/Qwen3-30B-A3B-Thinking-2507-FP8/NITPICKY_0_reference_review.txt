

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection for direct NER model transfer using cross-lingual word embedding mappings. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The experimental results demonstrate that the combined systems outperform individual approaches and previous state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

## Main Contributions
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer using cross-lingual word embeddings
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a relevant problem in cross-lingual NER, where acquiring human-annotated data for multiple languages is expensive. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is well-motivated and demonstrates significant improvements (up to +20.0 F1 score) for languages with low alignment accuracy.

2. The representation projection approach is a reasonable extension of existing work on cross-lingual embeddings and provides a practical solution for direct NER model transfer without retraining for new languages.

3. The co-decoding schemes are practical and well-motivated, with the rank-based approach showing particularly strong performance across multiple languages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Technical inaccuracy in Section 2.1**: The paper states "CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words, since our NER system has over 50 entity types, and the training time of CRFs grows at least quadratically in the number of entity types." This is misleading. The training time of CRFs grows with the number of entity types (O(M^2) for first-order CRFs), but it's not about the number of words. The issue is not the number of words but the number of entity types (M), which affects the complexity. This inaccuracy undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Unsupported claim in Section 4.2**: The paper states "we set the threshold to obtain approximately 5K to 6K unique word pairs for a target language, as experiments show that larger dictionaries might harm the performance of representation projection for direct NER model transfer." This claim lacks experimental evidence. The paper does not provide specific numbers or analysis to support this assertion, making it a significant gap in the methodology.

3. **Inconsistent results in Table 3**: For Korean, the best weakly supervised system (51.8 F1) has a lower F1 score than the supervised system (54.8 F1), but the paper claims "the best combined system achieves an F1 score close to the supervised Korean/German system." This is factually incorrect and misleading. The paper should clarify this discrepancy or provide a more accurate comparison.

4. **Incomplete methodology for conflict resolution**: The paper defines conflict between the two systems (e.g., "different boundaries but overlap" or "same boundary but different NER tags") but doesn't explain how conflicts are resolved in practice. For example, when the annotation projection system and representation projection system disagree on the same entity, what is the final decision? This is a critical gap in the methodology.

5. **Multiple formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting issues throughout, including inconsistent punctuation (e.g., "PERSON, ORGANIZATION, GPE (GeoPolitical Entities), EVENT, LOCATION, DATE, etc." with an extra comma), inconsistent terminology (e.g., "NER tags" vs. "NER tag"), and spacing issues. These minor inconsistencies reduce the professionalism of the paper.

## Questions to Authors
1. In Section 2.1, please clarify the technical relationship between the number of entity types and CRF training time. Is it accurate to say CRFs "cannot handle training data with a few millions of words" due to the number of entity types?

2. In Section 4.2, please provide specific experimental evidence supporting the claim that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance of representation projection for direct NER model transfer."

3. In Table 3, for Korean, the weakly supervised system (51.8 F1) has a lower F1 score than the supervised system (54.8 F1). Please clarify the claim that "the best combined system achieves an F1 score close to the supervised Korean/German system."

4. Please provide a clear explanation of how conflicts between the two systems are resolved in the co-decoding schemes, particularly when the annotation projection system and representation projection system disagree on the same entity.

## Additional Comments
The paper has a strong overall structure and presents a relevant approach to cross-lingual NER. However, the technical inaccuracies, unsupported claims, and inconsistencies in results undermine the paper's credibility. The formatting inconsistencies, while minor, further reduce the paper's professionalism. These issues collectively make it difficult to fully trust the paper's claims and methodology.

The paper makes a contribution to the field, but the technical shortcomings are significant enough to warrant rejection. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication.
