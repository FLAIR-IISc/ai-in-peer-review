

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotation in target languages. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method enhanced by a novel data selection scheme that selects high-quality projection-labeled data based on frequency analysis of entity tags, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to English, allowing a single English NER model to be applied across languages. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The paper evaluates these methods on in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as on CoNLL 02/03 data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Data Selection Scheme for Annotation Projection**: The paper's strongest contribution is the frequency-based data selection scheme that effectively identifies high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora. This addresses a critical gap in previous work where alignment quality was not considered when selecting data for projection.

2. **Practical Implementation of Representation Projection for NER**: While cross-lingual embeddings have been used in other NLP tasks, the paper provides a clean and effective implementation specifically for NER, demonstrating that representation projection can be a viable approach for cross-lingual transfer without retraining.

3. **Co-Decision Schemes that Leverage Complementary Strengths**: The authors' insight that annotation projection has high precision but low recall while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall leads to effective co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Contribution: Data Selection Scheme**  
   The data selection scheme is the paper's most valuable contribution. Previous work on annotation projection assumed all projected data was equally useful, ignoring the impact of alignment quality. The authors' frequency-based approach to identify high-quality data is simple yet highly effective, as demonstrated by the substantial F1 score improvements (13.9 for Japanese, 20.0 for Korean) when alignment quality is poor. This is a practical solution that could be adopted by practitioners.

2. **Strong, Comprehensive Evaluation**  
   The evaluation is thorough and well-designed, covering multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch), two different data sets (in-house and CoNLL), and multiple metrics (precision, recall, F1). The comparison with two strong baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) provides a clear demonstration of the proposed methods' superiority. The results consistently show that the combined approaches outperform individual systems.

3. **Insightful Analysis of System Characteristics**  
   The paper provides valuable insights into the complementary strengths of the two approaches: annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall. This analysis directly informs the design of the co-decoding schemes, making the combination more effective than simply averaging results.

4. **Clear, Well-Structured Presentation**  
   The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the methodology, solid experimental design, and appropriate figures (Figure 1). The writing is precise and accessible, making it easy for readers to understand the contributions and evaluate the results.

5. **Practical Implementation Details**  
   The paper provides sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, including model architectures, training data sizes, and specific parameter settings. This is particularly valuable for a method that relies on specific engineering choices.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with More Recent Work**  
   The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are quite dated (2012 and 2016). The field has advanced significantly since then, with recent work using multilingual BERT, XLM-R, and other transformer-based approaches. The authors should compare with more recent weakly supervised or semi-supervised methods to properly position their contribution. For example, how do their results compare with methods like those using multilingual BERT with minimal supervision?

2. **Limited Analysis of Data Selection Scheme**  
   While the data selection scheme is presented as a key contribution, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of why it works. It doesn't explore how the quality threshold (q) and entity number threshold (n) should be set in practice, or how the scheme behaves across different alignment accuracies. A deeper analysis of the relationship between alignment accuracy and the effectiveness of the data selection would strengthen this contribution.

3. **No Discussion of Limitations**  
   The paper doesn't discuss important limitations of their approach. For instance:
   - The data selection scheme requires sufficient comparable data to build the frequency table (Table 1), which may not be feasible for very low-resource languages.
   - The representation projection approach depends on the quality of the translation dictionary, which may not exist for some languages.
   - The paper doesn't address how the methods would perform on languages with different writing systems (e.g., Arabic, Chinese).

4. **Questionable Claim of "State-of-the-Art"**  
   The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" cross-lingual NER approaches, but the baselines they compare against are not state-of-the-art in 2023. This is misleading given the rapid advances in the field. The authors should clarify whether they're comparing with older methods relevant to weakly supervised settings, or provide more recent comparisons.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**  
   The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, how much of the improvement comes from the data selection scheme versus the co-decoding scheme? Ablation studies would help readers understand the relative importance of each contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" cross-lingual NER approaches, but the baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) are quite old. How do your results compare with more recent methods that use multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., XLM-R) in weakly supervised settings?

2. In Table 2, for German, the data selection scheme actually slightly decreases the F1 score (65.7 vs. 66.4). Why does this happen, and how do you explain the difference in behavior compared to other languages? Is there a particular characteristic of German that makes the data selection scheme less effective?

3. The paper mentions that the source English NER system has over 50 entity types, but doesn't specify how many entity types the target language NER systems have. How does the size of the label set affect the performance of the models, especially for languages with different entity type distributions?

4. The representation projection approach requires training a new cross-lingual embedding mapping for each target language. How does the computational cost of this mapping compare to the cost of training a new NER model for the target language? What is the practical impact on scalability?

5. The paper states that the data selection scheme "significantly improves the accuracy" of the target-language NER system. What is the minimum amount of comparable data needed for this scheme to be effective? How would the scheme perform with only 100K tokens of comparable data versus 5M tokens?

## Additional Comments

- The paper doesn't provide enough detail about the alignment systems used (Section 3.2). How was the alignment system trained, and what was its performance on the test sets? More details would help readers evaluate the quality of the comparable corpora.

- The paper claims that the representation projection approach "does not require re-training" when a new language is added, but to add a new language, you need to train a new cross-lingual embedding mapping (Section 4.2). The authors should clarify this point to avoid misleading readers.

- The paper doesn't discuss how their approach would handle languages with different writing systems (e.g., Arabic, Chinese) that might not be well-supported by the word2vec embeddings. This is an important limitation for real-world applications.

## Recommendation
I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant practical contribution with the data selection scheme for annotation projection, which addresses a critical gap in previous work. The evaluation is thorough and well-executed, and the co-decoding schemes provide a valuable way to leverage the complementary strengths of the two approaches. While there are some weaknesses (particularly the lack of comparison with more recent work), the core contribution is strong and well-supported by the experiments. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses (particularly the comparison with more recent work), this paper would make a valuable contribution to the ACL community.
