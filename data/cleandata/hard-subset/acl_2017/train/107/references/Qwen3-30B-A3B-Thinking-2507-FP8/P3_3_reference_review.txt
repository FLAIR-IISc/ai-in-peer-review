

# Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language, along with co-decoding schemes to combine them. The first approach improves annotation projection through a heuristic data selection scheme that selects high-quality projection-labeled data based on entity frequency statistics. The second approach uses representation projection to map target language word embeddings to the source language space, enabling direct application of a source-language NER model. The authors then combine these two systems using confidence-based and rank-based co-decoding schemes.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment quality is low (demonstrated with +13.9 to +20.0 F1 improvement for Japanese and Korean)
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer that uses cross-lingual embeddings to map target language words into source language space
3. Co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems, achieving better performance than either system alone

## Strengths

1. **Practical and effective data selection scheme**: The paper's frequency-based metric for selecting high-quality projection-labeled sentences is simple yet highly effective. The intuition that consistent tagging across multiple instances is more likely to be correct is well-founded and addresses a critical limitation of prior annotation projection work. The experimental results (Table 2) clearly demonstrate the value of this scheme, particularly for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean), showing substantial improvements over using all projected data.

2. **Clear demonstration of complementary strengths**: The paper insightfully identifies that annotation projection (AP) tends to have high precision but low recall, while representation projection (RP) has more balanced precision/recall. This observation directly motivates the rank-based co-decoding scheme, which prioritizes AP outputs and then adds RP outputs that don't conflict with AP detections. The results (Table 3) show this approach consistently outperforms individual systems by 2.9-6.2 F1 points.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on four in-house languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) with fine-grained entity types and three CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) with standard entity types. This broad evaluation demonstrates the generalizability of their approach and provides strong evidence for their claims. The comparison with prior work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is thorough and shows consistent improvements across most languages.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper only compares with approaches from 2012 and 2016, missing the opportunity to compare with more recent cross-lingual NER methods. Modern approaches like XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020) or multilingual BERT fine-tuning have shown strong performance on cross-lingual tasks, and the authors should demonstrate how their method compares to these. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether their approach remains competitive with current techniques.

2. **Insufficient discussion of failure modes and limitations**: The paper lacks qualitative analysis of errors and doesn't discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform on languages with very different grammatical structures or writing systems? What happens when the target language has no comparable corpus? Understanding these boundaries is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the method.

3. **Inadequate methodological details**: Several important implementation details are missing. The paper mentions using "a smoothing layer" in NN2 but doesn't explain how it's implemented or trained. The exact parameters for the data selection scheme (q and n thresholds) are not thoroughly justified beyond being tuned for best F1. The paper also doesn't explain how they handle OOV words during representation projection.

4. **Overstated novelty**: The representation projection approach builds directly on Mikolov et al. (2013b) for cross-lingual embeddings, and the data selection scheme shares similarities with approaches used in unsupervised word alignment. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contributions from prior work in these areas.

5. **Unjustified model choices**: The paper uses MEMMs instead of CRFs for annotation projection due to "training time growing exponentially with number of output labels," but doesn't provide evidence that this was actually a problem in their experiments. They also don't justify why they chose to use neural networks for representation projection rather than simpler methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare with recent multilingual transformer-based methods (e.g., XLM-R) for cross-lingual NER, which have shown strong performance on standard benchmarks? Have you attempted to compare with these approaches?

2. Could you provide more details about how you handle out-of-vocabulary words in the representation projection approach? What happens when a target language word doesn't have a corresponding English translation in your dictionary?

3. The paper mentions that CRFs "cannot handle training data with a few millions of words" due to "training time growing at least quadratically in the number of entity types." Could you provide evidence for this claim, such as training times for CRFs with different numbers of entity types on similar data sizes?

4. Why did you choose to use a rank-based co-decoding scheme over confidence-based, especially since the paper states that confidence scores may not be directly comparable between systems? Could you elaborate on the empirical evidence supporting this choice?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is particularly strong and addresses a real limitation in prior work. The representation projection approach is a practical and effective method for model transfer. The co-decoding schemes effectively leverage the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches. However, the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods and insufficient discussion of limitations significantly weaken the paper's impact. With these issues addressed, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
