- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approaches: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection for direct model transfer, plus two co-decoding schemes.  
- Heuristic data selection for annotation projection significantly improves performance when alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese, +20.0 Korean).  
- Representation projection maps target embeddings into English space so one English NER model can be reused across languages.  
- Two co-decoding schemes (including rank-based) effectively combine systems, outperforming individual approaches by 2.9–6.2 F1.  
- Data selection scheme is practical, well-motivated (frequency-based reliability score), and clearly improves noisy projection data.  
- Evaluation is comprehensive across four languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and both in-house and CoNLL datasets, showing generalizability.  
- Rank-based co-decoding balances high precision from annotation projection with better recall from representation projection, yielding strong empirical gains.  
- Provides clear implementation details and parameters, supporting reproducibility.  
- Representation projection approach is incremental, largely building on prior embedding-mapping work (limited novelty).  
- Lacks comparison with recent transformer-based cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., multilingual BERT/XLM-R), weakening state-of-the-art claims.  
- Data selection thresholds (q and n) are empirically tuned without principled or automatic determination for new languages.  
- Limited analysis of performance on languages with very different linguistic structures or writing systems; evaluation set may be narrow.  
- Insufficient in-depth analysis of how varying alignment quality affects the data selection scheme’s effectiveness.  
- Asks for comparisons with multilingual transformer NER systems (e.g., XLM-R) and other recent methods.  
- Questions robustness of data selection when alignment accuracy is extremely low (e.g., <50%) and whether a minimum alignment threshold exists.  
- Asks how the representation projection would change using a non-English universal language.  
- Requests elaboration on limitations for languages with very different writing systems (e.g., Arabic, Chinese).  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—strong practical contribution (especially data selection) but needs more comparisons and deeper language-specific analysis.
