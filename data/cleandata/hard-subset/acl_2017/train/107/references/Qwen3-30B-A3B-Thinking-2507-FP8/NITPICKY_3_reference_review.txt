

# Review for "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme, and (2) representation projection for direct NER model transfer. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The paper evaluates these methods on in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as on CoNLL NER data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A heuristic data selection scheme that significantly improves annotation projection by selecting high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer using cross-lingual embeddings
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches

## Strengths
1. **Novel data selection scheme**: The authors propose a clever heuristic for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data based on frequency tables of entity tags. This approach significantly improves performance (up to +20.0 F1 score) for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), addressing a real weakness in previous annotation projection methods.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates the proposed methods across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and provides thorough comparisons with baseline approaches. The results demonstrate that the combined systems outperform previous state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.

3. **Clear presentation of intuition**: The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their methods, particularly regarding the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision and recall).

## Weaknesses
1. **Technical inaccuracy**: The paper states "the training time of order-o CRFs grows exponentially (O(M^o+1)) with the number of output labels M, which is typically slow even for moderate-size training data if M is large." This is factually incorrect. The training time of order-o CRFs grows polynomially (O(M^o+1)), not exponentially, with respect to M. This is a serious technical error that undermines the authors' credibility and should be corrected.

2. **Unjustified fine-grained entity types**: The paper uses over 50 entity types for in-house NER data, which is unusual (most NER datasets have 4-10 entity types). This makes it difficult to compare results with standard NER datasets and previous work. The authors do not justify why they chose such a fine-grained entity type set, which significantly limits the paper's impact and reproducibility.

3. **Lack of comparison to recent work**: The paper only compares to approaches from 2012 and 2016 (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), but does not compare to more recent cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., those based on multilingual BERT or other transformer-based models). This makes the contribution seem less significant in the current landscape of cross-lingual NER.

4. **Inconsistent notation and terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent notation (e.g., "5.10M" vs. "5.10 million" for training sizes) and terminology (e.g., "projection-labeled" vs. "weakly labeled" used interchangeably). These inconsistencies make the paper harder to follow and appear unprofessional.

5. **Missing figure**: The paper mentions "Figure 1" in Section 2.2 but does not include it, making it impossible to understand the neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) that are central to the paper.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you choose to use over 50 entity types for your in-house NER data? How does this affect the comparability of your results with standard NER datasets (which typically have 4-10 entity types)?

2. Can you clarify the technical statement about CRF training time growth? Is the growth really exponential with respect to M, or is it polynomial? Please provide the correct complexity analysis.

3. Why didn't you compare your approach to more recent cross-lingual NER methods, such as those using multilingual BERT (e.g., Devlin et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020)? How would your approach compare to these state-of-the-art methods?

4. Can you provide the missing Figure 1 that describes the neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) mentioned in Section 2.2?

5. How did you choose the thresholds q and n for the data selection scheme? Is there a principled way to choose these thresholds, or did you simply try different values and pick the best ones?

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonable approach to cross-lingual NER with some practical contributions, the technical inaccuracies (particularly the incorrect statement about CRF training time complexity), the unjustified use of over 50 entity types, and the lack of comparison to recent work are serious flaws. The missing figure and inconsistent notation further undermine the paper's quality. These issues collectively make the paper difficult to evaluate and limit its contribution to the field. The paper's significance is overshadowed by these technical and presentation flaws, despite the potentially useful data selection scheme.
