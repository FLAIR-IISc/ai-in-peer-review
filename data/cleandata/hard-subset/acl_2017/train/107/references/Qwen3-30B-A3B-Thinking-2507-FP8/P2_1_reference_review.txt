

# ACL Review: "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotations in the target language. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to improve the quality of projected labels from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to English space for direct application of an English NER system. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low and projected data is noisy (up to +20 F1 points for Korean).
2. A representation projection approach that enables direct NER model transfer by mapping word embeddings from target languages to English space, eliminating the need for retraining.
3. Two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems, achieving better performance than either system alone.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and Well-Motivated Solution to a Real Problem**: The paper addresses a genuine challenge in NLP - building NER systems for low-resource languages without human annotations. The authors correctly identify that previous work on cross-lingual NER either requires parallel data (which is expensive to obtain) or relies on assumptions about alignment quality that aren't addressed. Their heuristic data selection scheme directly tackles this limitation, making the approach more practical for real-world applications. The significant improvements demonstrated (up to +20 F1 points for Korean) show the approach has tangible value.

2. **Effective and Well-Justified Data Selection Scheme**: The authors' insight that high-frequency tags across multiple projections are more likely to be correct is well-motivated and empirically validated. Their data selection scheme (q(y) ≥ q; n(y) ≥ n) is simple yet effective, and the paper demonstrates how it significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low. This is a strong contribution that addresses a critical limitation in previous annotation projection work, where alignment quality was not considered when selecting sentence pairs for projection.

3. **Comprehensive and Thorough Evaluation**: The paper evaluates on six languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets, with careful comparison to relevant baselines. The results consistently show that their combined approaches outperform previous work by a significant margin (2.9-6.2 F1 points). The evaluation demonstrates the robustness of their methods across different languages and datasets, which strengthens the paper's claims.

4. **Novel Co-Decision Schemes with Clear Rationale**: The authors' observation that the annotation projection system has high precision but low recall, while the representation projection system has more balanced precision/recall, leads to a well-motivated rank-based co-decoding scheme. This scheme prioritizes the high-precision system while incorporating the higher-recall system, resulting in improved overall performance. The empirical demonstration of this approach's effectiveness is a strong point of the paper.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but fails to compare to more recent cross-lingual NER methods. For example, the paper does not compare to approaches using multilingual BERT (e.g., Devlin et al., 2019) or other transformer-based cross-lingual transfer learning methods that have become dominant in NLP. Without this comparison, it's unclear how the proposed method stands against current state-of-the-art approaches, which is a significant limitation for an ACL submission.

2. **Limited Analysis of Why the Methods Work**: While the paper demonstrates that their methods work well, it lacks deep analysis of *why* they work. For instance, the paper shows that the data selection scheme works better for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the underlying factors. Similarly, the paper doesn't explore why the rank-based co-decoding outperforms the confidence-based approach. More analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution by providing deeper insights into the mechanisms at work.

3. **Implementation Details and Reproducibility Concerns**: The paper lacks sufficient implementation details for reproducibility. For example, the training of monolingual word embeddings is described as a "variant of CBOW," but without specific details about the training procedure, vocabulary size, or other hyperparameters. The paper also doesn't provide details about the exact architecture of the neural networks (beyond mentioning "one hidden layer"), which would be important for others to replicate the results.

4. **Questionable Novelty of Representation Projection**: The core idea of mapping embeddings to a common space is not novel (as acknowledged in the paper, citing Mikolov et al., 2013b). While the specific application to NER is novel, the paper doesn't clearly differentiate this from previous work on cross-lingual embeddings. The authors should more explicitly position their work within the broader context of cross-lingual transfer learning to establish the novelty of their contribution.

5. **Overstated Claims About "No Human Annotation"**: The paper claims "no human annotation in the target languages," but the approach actually requires human-annotated data in the source language (English) for training the NER system. The paper should clarify this to avoid misleading readers about the true requirements of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your methods compare to recent transformer-based cross-lingual approaches like multilingual BERT? Have you attempted to compare with these more recent state-of-the-art methods?

2. The paper mentions that the representation projection approach uses English as the common language. How would the approach scale to languages without a strong English resource base? Could the approach be adapted to use a different pivot language?

3. The paper shows significant improvements for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), but the improvements for German and Portuguese are more modest. Could you provide more analysis about why the data selection scheme performs differently across languages?

4. The paper states that the rank-based co-decoding scheme outperforms the confidence-based approach. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, particularly regarding the "exclude-O" aspect of the confidence-based approach?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a practical and effective approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with a well-motivated data selection scheme and a novel representation projection approach. The evaluation is thorough and demonstrates significant improvements over previous work. However, the lack of comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods (particularly transformer-based approaches) is a significant weakness for an ACL submission, as it limits the paper's impact in the current NLP landscape. The paper would be substantially stronger with additional comparison to recent work, deeper analysis of why the methods work, and more complete implementation details.

**Overall Rating**: Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses, particularly regarding comparison to recent work and implementation details)
