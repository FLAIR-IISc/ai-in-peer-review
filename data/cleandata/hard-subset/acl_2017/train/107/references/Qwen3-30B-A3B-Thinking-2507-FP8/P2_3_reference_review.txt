

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the challenge of cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language. The authors propose three main contributions: (1) a heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that filters noisy projection-labeled data based on entity frequency statistics; (2) a novel representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer by mapping target language word embeddings to the source language space; and (3) two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, and on CoNLL data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Strengths

1. **Practical solution for cross-lingual NER with limited resources**: The paper tackles a significant real-world problem - building NER systems for low-resource languages without human annotation. The proposed methods are practical and could be immediately useful for applications requiring multilingual NER systems. The data selection scheme is particularly valuable when alignment accuracy is low (as demonstrated for Japanese and Korean), where previous approaches would fail.

2. **Novel representation projection approach**: The idea of mapping target language word embeddings to the source language space for direct NER model transfer is a meaningful contribution to cross-lingual NER. Unlike previous approaches that require retraining for each language, this method allows using a single source-language NER system for multiple target languages with minimal adaptation. The authors provide a clear technical description of this approach and its implementation.

3. **Strong empirical results with consistent improvements**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines across multiple languages. For example, the data selection scheme improves F1 scores by 13.9 for Japanese and 20.0 for Korean compared to no data selection. The rank-based co-decoding scheme consistently outperforms individual approaches, with improvements of 2.9-6.2 F1 points across languages. These results are well-documented with detailed tables and analysis.

4. **Effective combination of complementary approaches**: The authors recognize that different projection approaches have different strengths (annotation projection has high precision, representation projection has balanced precision/recall) and develop co-decoding schemes that leverage these complementary properties. The rank-based scheme, which prioritizes the higher-precision annotation projection system, proves particularly effective.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper only compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are relatively outdated approaches (2012 and 2016). The authors should compare with more recent cross-lingual NER methods, particularly those based on multilingual transformer models (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT), which have become the new state-of-the-art in cross-lingual NER. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed method.

2. **Insufficient analysis of language resource levels**: The paper evaluates on languages including German, Dutch, and Portuguese, which are not truly low-resource languages. The authors should clarify whether their methods would work equally well for languages with significantly fewer resources (e.g., languages with no parallel corpora at all). The evaluation would be more convincing if they included a comparison between languages with varying resource availability.

3. **Lack of ablation studies and failure analysis**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (data selection, representation projection, co-decoding). Without these, it's unclear which parts of the system provide the most value. Additionally, the paper lacks qualitative analysis of failure cases, which would help understand the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.

4. **Implementation details for reproducibility**: The paper lacks sufficient details for reproducibility, particularly regarding the cross-lingual embedding mappings (e.g., how the training dictionary was constructed, exact parameters for the word2vec models). The authors mention "a small training dictionary" but don't specify how it was created or what the threshold was for including word pairs. More implementation details would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison with recent cross-lingual NER methods based on multilingual transformers (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT) to better position your work in the current state-of-the-art?

2. The paper evaluates on German, Dutch, and Portuguese, which are not low-resource languages. Could you clarify which languages in your evaluation are considered low-resource, and how your method would perform on truly low-resource languages with no parallel data?

3. The paper claims that the rank-based co-decoding scheme outperforms the confidence-based scheme. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of cases where the rank-based scheme makes different decisions than the confidence-based scheme, and why the rank-based approach is superior in those cases?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical and well-motivated approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The data selection scheme and representation projection approach are novel and address real limitations in existing methods. The empirical results are strong and consistent across multiple languages. However, the lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods and insufficient analysis of language resource levels are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication. With these concerns addressed, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper has strong potential but requires substantial revision to properly position itself within the current research landscape. I recommend a major revision to address the weaknesses identified above.
