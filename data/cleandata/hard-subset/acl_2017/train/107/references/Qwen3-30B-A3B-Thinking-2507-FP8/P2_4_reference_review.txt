

# Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language. The authors present two main contributions: (1) a data selection scheme for annotation projection that selects high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to English space to enable direct application of a single English NER model. They also introduce two co-decoding schemes that intelligently combine the outputs of these two approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection**: The paper identifies a critical gap in existing annotation projection approaches - the lack of consideration for alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs for projection. The proposed data selection scheme, which uses frequency tables to measure entity label reliability, significantly improves performance (13.9 F1 for Japanese, 20.0 F1 for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low. This is a substantial practical contribution that addresses a real challenge in cross-lingual NER.

2. **Novel Representation Projection Approach**: The representation projection method, which maps target language word embeddings to English space using linear mappings, is elegant and simple to implement. The paper demonstrates this approach works well across multiple languages (German, Portuguese, etc.) and provides a compelling alternative to previous direct model transfer methods. The approach's flexibility (adding new languages without retraining) is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

3. **Effective Co-Decoder Schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme that prioritizes the annotation projection system (which has higher precision) while incorporating entities from the representation projection system (which has better recall) is well-motivated and effective. The paper shows this approach outperforms individual systems by 2.9-6.2 F1 points across multiple languages, demonstrating significant value in combining complementary approaches.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across six languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and standard CoNLL datasets. The results clearly demonstrate that the combined approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), with the best combined system approaching supervised learning performance (e.g., 69.9 F1 for Portuguese vs. 75.6 F1 for supervised).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper only compares with methods from 2012 and 2016, but there has been significant progress in cross-lingual NER since then. Recent methods like multilingual BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) or XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020) have achieved state-of-the-art results. Without comparison to these, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed methods. The paper's claim of outperforming "state-of-the-art" is questionable without this context.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide clear ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, how much of the improvement comes from the data selection scheme versus the co-decoding? How does the representation projection approach compare to using standard word embeddings without projection? Without these details, it's hard to assess the relative importance of each contribution.

3. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss important limitations of their approach. For instance, how does the method perform with very low-resource languages? What is the impact of different alignment accuracies on the data selection scheme? The paper only evaluates on languages with moderate alignment accuracy (62-88%), leaving open questions about performance on languages with poor alignment.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Threshold Selection**: The paper uses different threshold parameters (q, n) for different languages (e.g., (0.8, 4) for Japanese, (0.9, 3) for Korean), but doesn't explain how these thresholds were chosen or whether they generalize across languages. This makes it difficult to replicate or adapt the method to new languages.

5. **Inconsistent Performance Comparisons**: The paper shows that for German, the representation projection approach (NN2) has slightly better F1 than annotation projection (64.9 vs. 66.4), but for Portuguese, annotation projection is better (62.9 vs. 64.7). However, the paper doesn't explain why one approach works better for some languages than others, which would be valuable for understanding the method's strengths and weaknesses.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your methods compare with more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (e.g., using multilingual BERT or XLM-R)? Would your co-decoding approach be applicable to these neural network-based methods?

2. Can you provide more details about how the threshold parameters (q, n) for data selection were determined? Are these thresholds language-specific, or can a single set of thresholds be used across languages?

3. The paper mentions that the representation projection approach works well for Portuguese but not as well for German. What factors might explain these differences, and how can these differences be addressed in future work?

4. How does your approach scale to languages with significantly lower alignment accuracy (e.g., below 50%)? Would the data selection scheme still be effective in such cases?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with strong empirical results that addresses a practical challenge in cross-lingual NER. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is particularly valuable, and the representation projection approach is elegant and effective. The co-decoding schemes provide a meaningful way to combine complementary approaches.

However, the lack of comparison with recent methods (especially neural approaches that have dominated NER in recent years) is a significant weakness that undermines the paper's claim of outperforming "state-of-the-art" methods. The paper would be much stronger if it included these comparisons and provided more detailed analysis of why their approach works better than recent alternatives.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to address the comparison with recent methods and provide more detailed ablation studies. The paper has significant practical value for cross-lingual NER, and with these additions, it would make a valuable contribution to the field.
