

# Review for ACL: "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotation in the target language. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projected annotations from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to English space, allowing direct application of an English NER model. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on both in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as standard CoNLL datasets for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (evidenced by +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).
2. A representation projection approach that allows direct transfer of NER models across languages without retraining.
3. Co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems to achieve better performance than either approach alone.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Data Selection Scheme**: The paper identifies a critical limitation in previous annotation projection approaches (not considering alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs for projection) and proposes a well-motivated solution. The data selection scheme based on frequency tables of projected entities is simple yet effective, with significant improvements when alignment accuracy is low. The experimental results (e.g., +20 F1 for Korean) clearly demonstrate the value of this contribution.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and data sets (in-house and CoNLL), demonstrating the approach's robustness. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test data is particularly convincing, showing that the combined system outperforms previous approaches.

3. **Effective Co-Decision Schemes**: The authors observe that annotation projection tends to have high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall. This leads to a principled rank-based co-decoding scheme that effectively combines the strengths of both approaches, improving over the best single approach by 2.9-6.2 F1 points.

4. **Practical Approach**: The representation projection method is straightforward and practical, allowing a single English NER model to be applied to new languages without retraining. This has significant potential for real-world applications where human annotation in target languages is unavailable.

5. **Clear Presentation of Key Insights**: The paper clearly articulates the key insight that alignment accuracy critically affects the quality of projected annotations, and that a data selection scheme is necessary when alignment accuracy is low. This is a valuable contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Novelty in Representation Projection**: The representation projection approach is largely based on prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b) without clear justification for why this specific approach is particularly well-suited for NER. The paper states "We opt for the first solution in this paper because of its flexibility," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this approach is better than alternatives for NER specifically.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods that use multilingual BERT or other transfer learning approaches. This makes the claim of "state-of-the-art" less convincing, as the paper doesn't establish how it compares with the most recent methods.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: There are no ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component. For example, how much does the data selection scheme contribute versus the representation projection? How much does co-decoding add? Without these, it's difficult to assess the relative importance of each contribution.

4. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't provide any qualitative analysis of where the approaches fail, which would be valuable for understanding the limitations of the method. For instance, it doesn't discuss specific error cases where the data selection scheme failed or where the representation projection produced incorrect tags.

5. **Unclear Justification for Model Choices**: The paper uses a variety of models (CRFs, MEMMs, neural networks) without clear justification for why these particular models were chosen. For example, it's not clear why they chose to use a 2nd-order MEMM for the target language NER system rather than a more modern neural model.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your approach compare with recent cross-lingual NER methods that use multilingual BERT or other pretrained multilingual language models? These have become the state-of-the-art in many cross-lingual tasks, and it would be valuable to see how your method compares with these approaches.

2. The paper states that the data selection scheme is particularly effective when alignment accuracy is low. Can you provide more details on how the alignment accuracy affects the performance, and what the minimum alignment accuracy is for the scheme to be effective?

3. For the co-decoding schemes, why does the rank-based approach work better for in-house data but the confidence-based approach works better for CoNLL data? Is this due to differences in the data or model characteristics?

4. The paper mentions that the representation projection approach uses a linear mapping. What would be the impact of using a more complex mapping (e.g., nonlinear) to better capture cross-lingual relationships, and why did you choose a linear mapping?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a well-motivated and practically useful approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, with the data selection scheme being particularly strong. However, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and the absence of ablation studies significantly weaken the paper's contribution claims. The representation projection approach also lacks sufficient novelty justification.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The paper has several strong contributions, particularly the data selection scheme for annotation projection, which addresses a real limitation in the field. However, the paper would need to address the weaknesses noted above (especially the comparison with recent work and the lack of ablation studies) to be fully compelling. The authors should also provide more detailed justification for their model choices and consider a more thorough analysis of failure cases.

## Additional Comments
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their approach compares to recent neural cross-lingual approaches.
- The paper could provide more information about the computational cost of the data selection scheme and representation projection, which would be valuable for practical applications.
- The authors should clarify whether the data selection scheme is language-dependent or language-agnostic, which would impact the generalizability of their approach.
