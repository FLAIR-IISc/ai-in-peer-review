

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that requires no human annotation in the target language. The authors introduce two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a novel data selection scheme that identifies high-quality projection-labeled data from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to the source language (English) to directly apply a pre-trained English NER system. They also propose two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches. The method is evaluated on in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as on CoNLL 2002/2003 data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions

1. **A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low** - This is the most novel and impactful contribution, as it directly addresses a key limitation of previous annotation projection methods that didn't consider alignment quality when selecting data for projection.

2. **A well-executed representation projection approach that leverages cross-lingual embeddings for direct NER model transfer** - While not entirely novel, the application to NER is well-executed and provides a complementary approach to annotation projection.

3. **A co-decoding scheme that effectively combines the outputs of both projection-based approaches** - This is a practical contribution that demonstrates how to integrate multiple weakly supervised methods for improved performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The paper's most significant contribution is the frequency-based quality metric for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data. This is a clever solution to the problem of noisy data from alignment errors. The experimental results are compelling, showing substantial improvements (+13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean) compared to using all projection-labeled data. This addresses a critical limitation in previous work that simply projected all aligned sentences without considering alignment quality.

2. **Comprehensive and well-structured empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and standard CoNLL datasets. The results are presented clearly with precision, recall, and F1 scores. The comparison to previous state-of-the-art approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is well-executed and demonstrates clear improvements, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy.

3. **Practical significance for low-resource language NER**: The work addresses a critical real-world problem: building NER systems for languages where human annotation is prohibitively expensive. The demonstrated ability to achieve F1 scores close to supervised learning (e.g., 69.9% vs. 75.6% for Portuguese) with no human annotation in the target language has significant practical value for multilingual information extraction applications.

4. **Clear presentation and well-executed experiments**: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The figures and tables are well-constructed and effectively support the claims made in the paper. The experimental setup is thorough, with appropriate baselines and ablation studies.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art approaches**: The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but these are relatively old approaches (2012 and 2016). The field has advanced significantly since then, with recent works like multilingual BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020) setting new benchmarks for cross-lingual transfer. The paper does not compare with these more recent methods, which undermines the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance. For example, XLM-R achieves over 80% F1 on CoNLL 2003, which is significantly higher than the 69.9% achieved by the proposed method.

2. **Limited discussion of the representation projection approach's novelty**: The representation projection approach is presented as novel, but it's essentially a straightforward application of cross-lingual embeddings to NER. The paper cites Mikolov et al. (2013b) for the embedding mapping technique, but doesn't clearly differentiate this approach from prior work. In fact, similar embedding-based approaches have been used for cross-lingual NER before (e.g., in "Cross-lingual NER with Multilingual Word Embeddings" by Søgaard et al., 2014). The paper doesn't demonstrate how this approach differs from or improves upon existing embedding-based methods.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases and limitations**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when the methods fail or why they work better for some languages than others. For example, German shows modest improvements (66.4% vs. 65.7% F1) despite high alignment accuracy (76.1%), while Korean shows large improvements (38.7% vs. 18.7% F1) with lower alignment accuracy (62.0%). The paper doesn't discuss these discrepancies or provide insights into the underlying causes.

4. **Lack of ablation studies for components**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component. For example, it doesn't show how much of the improvement comes from the data selection scheme versus the co-decoding schemes. The paper also doesn't compare the impact of different parameters for the data selection scheme (q and n thresholds) in detail.

5. **Questionable assumptions in the data selection scheme**: The data selection scheme assumes that the most frequent tag for an entity is the correct one, which may not hold in cases where the English NER system has systematic biases or when entities have multiple valid interpretations in the target language. The paper doesn't provide examples where this assumption fails or discuss how to handle such cases.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your data selection scheme handle cases where the same entity name has multiple valid NER types (e.g., "Apple" as a company vs. "Apple" as a fruit)? Could this lead to incorrect frequency scores and poor data selection?

2. Why does the representation projection approach work better for some languages (e.g., German) than others (e.g., Korean), and how could this be improved? Is there a correlation between language distance from English and performance?

3. Could you provide a comparison with more recent state-of-the-art approaches, such as XLM-R or multilingual BERT, to better position your work in the current landscape of cross-lingual NER?

4. The paper mentions that the representation projection approach uses 800-dimensional embeddings for target languages while English uses 300-dimensional embeddings. Why were different dimensions chosen, and how did this affect performance?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed and practical approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with a novel data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates clear improvements over previous work. However, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art approaches and the limited discussion of the representation projection approach's novelty are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

**Recommendation: Minor Revision** - The paper has strong practical value and a solid technical contribution (the data selection scheme), but it needs to be strengthened by comparing with more recent approaches and providing a more thorough analysis of the limitations and failure cases. The authors should also clarify the novelty of their representation projection approach relative to existing work. With these revisions, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the field.
