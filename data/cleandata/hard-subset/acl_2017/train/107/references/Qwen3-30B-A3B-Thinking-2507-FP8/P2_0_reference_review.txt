

# ACL Review: "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two novel approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language: (1) an annotation projection method with a heuristic data selection scheme that filters high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to English space for direct NER model transfer. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. **Effective data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The paper introduces a frequency-based metric to evaluate annotation quality of projection-labeled data and uses it to select high-quality sentences, significantly improving performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).

2. **Representation projection approach for direct model transfer**: The authors propose a method to project word embeddings from target languages to English space using a linear mapping learned from a small bilingual dictionary, enabling direct application of the English NER system without retraining.

3. **Co-decoding schemes that combine the two approaches**: The paper presents two schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) that effectively combine the outputs of the annotation and representation projection systems, achieving higher F1 scores than either approach alone.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results with clear practical impact**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements over previous weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods, particularly for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean). The rank-based co-decoding scheme achieves the highest F1 scores among all weakly supervised approaches for Korean, German, and Portuguese, improving over the best single approach by 2.9-6.2 F1 points. This practical impact is well-supported by comprehensive evaluation on both in-house and CoNLL datasets across six languages.

2. **Clear methodology with appropriate evaluation**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the two main approaches (annotation projection with data selection and representation projection) and their combination through co-decoding. The evaluation methodology is sound, with proper ablation studies (e.g., comparing NN1 vs. NN2, different data selection thresholds) and appropriate baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016). The use of both fine-grained in-house data and standard CoNLL data provides a balanced assessment of the approach.

3. **Novel data selection scheme addressing a key limitation**: While annotation projection has been previously used, the paper identifies a critical gap in previous work (not considering alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs) and proposes a novel solution. The frequency-based quality metric for evaluating projected entities is both intuitive and effective, as demonstrated by the substantial performance improvements, particularly for low-alignment-accuracy languages.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with more recent cross-lingual NER methods**: The paper compares only with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are from 2012 and 2016, respectively. The field has advanced significantly since then, with recent methods using multilingual transformers (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) and more sophisticated transfer learning techniques. The paper fails to demonstrate how their approach compares to these more recent state-of-the-art methods, which is a major weakness given the current landscape of cross-lingual NER research.

2. **Insufficient analysis of limitations and failure cases**: The paper provides strong results but lacks discussion of when the approach fails or its limitations. For example, it doesn't analyze performance on languages with very different morphological structures from English (e.g., agglutinative languages), or how the method performs with minimal comparable corpora. The paper also doesn't discuss computational complexity or resource requirements for the data selection and representation projection steps, which are important for practical deployment.

3. **Lack of guidance for selecting threshold parameters**: The paper uses different thresholds (q, n) for different languages but doesn't provide a principled way to determine these thresholds for new languages without access to gold-standard data. The paper states "we have tried different threshold parameters and reported the best numbers," but doesn't explain how to choose these parameters in practice for new languages or languages with different characteristics.

4. **Limited analysis of the representation projection approach**: The paper presents representation projection as a novel contribution, but the comparison between NN1 and NN2 (the two neural architectures) is somewhat superficial. The paper states NN2 "tends to have a more balanced precision and recall" but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this is the case or how the smoothing layer affects performance. Additionally, the paper doesn't explore whether the representation projection approach could be combined with the data selection scheme.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach compare to more recent cross-lingual NER methods that use multilingual transformers (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT)? Have you considered adapting your method to work with these modern architectures, which have shown strong performance in cross-lingual transfer tasks?

2. The paper uses a small bilingual dictionary for the cross-lingual mapping. How would you handle cases where the bilingual dictionary is very small (e.g., only a few hundred word pairs) or nonexistent for a new language? Would your approach still be effective in such scenarios?

3. For the data selection scheme, the paper uses different quality thresholds (q) for different languages. How would you recommend selecting these thresholds for a new language without access to gold-standard data? Is there a way to automatically determine these thresholds based on the alignment accuracy or other characteristics of the comparable corpora?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with strong empirical results. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is particularly novel and well-motivated. However, the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods and insufficient analysis of the approach's limitations are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the authors should address these weaknesses in their revision, particularly by comparing with more recent methods and providing more thorough analysis of the approach's limitations.

The paper has good potential for impact in practical multilingual information extraction systems, but it needs to better position itself within the current landscape of cross-lingual NER research.
