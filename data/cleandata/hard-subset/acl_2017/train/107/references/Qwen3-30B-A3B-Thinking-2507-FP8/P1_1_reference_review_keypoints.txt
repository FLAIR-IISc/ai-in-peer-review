- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection via linear embedding mapping, plus two co-decoding schemes.  
- Annotation projection innovation: selects high-quality projection-labeled data using entity frequency analysis to mitigate noisy alignments.  
- Representation projection: maps target-language embeddings to English so a single English NER model can be applied directly to target languages.  
- Co-decoding: two schemes designed to combine annotation- and representation-projection outputs, with rank-based scheme performing well.  
- Contribution: effective data-selection heuristic that substantially improves performance when alignment quality is low.  
- Contribution: practical representation-projection transfer method enabling multilingual NER without retraining per language.  
- Contribution: co-decoding strategies that improve results over individual approaches.  
- Strength: data-selection mechanism yields large F1 gains for low-alignment languages (e.g., +13.9 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation across six languages and two dataset types, showing competitiveness with supervised baselines.  
- Strength: methods require no target-language human annotation beyond a small dictionary, making them practically useful.  
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation and experimental setup.  
- Strength: combining approaches produces better performance than either alone, especially with rank-based co-decoding.  
- Weakness: representation projection builds on prior cross-lingual embedding work and novelty over prior NER uses is unclear.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to recent transformer-based cross-lingual methods (e.g., multilingual BERT), which are now strong baselines.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of failure cases and limitations (entity types, difficult language pairs).  
- Weakness: claim of “no human annotation” is overstated because embedding mapping uses a small (5–6K) training dictionary.  
- Weakness: missing ablation studies for other components (embedding dimensions, NN smoothing layer, etc.).  
- Question: How does representation projection compare to transformer-based cross-lingual transfer (performance and resources)?  
- Question: What were the specific sources and construction methods for the “comparable corpora” per language pair?  
- Question: How was the 5–6K word-pair dictionary created—automatic extraction or manual effort?  
- Question: How sensitive is data selection to thresholds q and n, and were they tuned per language or shared?  
- Question: Why use MEMMs for large projected target training data instead of CRFs, and how does CRF training time scale in practice?  
- Additional comment: Paper is well-written with strong empirical results but should address comparisons to recent methods and clarify the “no human annotation” claim while adding more failure analysis.
