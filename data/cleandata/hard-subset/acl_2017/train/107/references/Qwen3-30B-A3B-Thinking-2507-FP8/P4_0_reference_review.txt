

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper addresses cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in target languages. It proposes two main approaches: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection that maps target language word embeddings to the source language space, enabling direct application of a source language NER system to target languages. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

## Main Contributions
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low by selecting high-frequency entity tag projections.
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer that maps target language embeddings to the source language space, allowing a single NER system to handle multiple languages without retraining.
3. Two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems, with the rank-based scheme consistently outperforming individual systems.

## Strengths

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection**: The paper introduces a practical and effective data selection scheme that addresses a critical weakness in existing annotation projection approaches. The authors demonstrate significant improvements (13.9-20.0 F1 points) when alignment accuracy is low (Japanese and Korean), showing the scheme's value in real-world scenarios where alignment quality varies. The approach is well-motivated by the observation that high-frequency tags in projection-labeled data are more likely to be correct due to the independence of the source NER system and alignment system. This contribution has clear practical value for cross-lingual NER.

2. **Representation Projection Approach**: The representation projection method is technically sound and well-executed. By mapping target language word embeddings to the source language space, the authors create a universal NER system that doesn't require retraining for new languages. The method builds on established cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b) but applies them in a clean, principled way to NER. The paper provides thorough experimental validation across multiple languages, showing consistent performance improvements over baseline approaches.

3. **Effective Co-Decision Schemes**: The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, are well-motivated by the complementary strengths of the two projection methods (annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall). The rank-based co-decoding consistently outperforms individual systems across languages (2.9-6.2 F1 points improvement), demonstrating a meaningful practical contribution that enhances the overall system.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Representation Projection**: While the application to NER is novel, the core representation projection technique is based on established cross-lingual embedding methods (Mikolov et al., 2013b). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing cross-lingual embedding techniques or justify why their specific implementation is superior for NER. The authors mention they "opt for the first solution" (building monolingual representations separately), but don't thoroughly justify why this is better than inter-lingual representation approaches for NER specifically.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares only to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are older works (2012 and 2016). The state-of-the-art for cross-lingual NER has advanced significantly since then, with multilingual BERT and other pre-trained models now being the standard. The paper doesn't compare to these more recent approaches, making it difficult to assess how competitive their method is with current techniques. This is a significant omission for an ACL submission.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Data Selection Scheme**: While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the data selection scheme empirically, it lacks deeper analysis of why it works. The authors don't explore how the scheme performs with varying quality thresholds beyond the optimal values reported, nor do they provide theoretical justification for why high-frequency tags are more likely to be correct. A more thorough analysis would strengthen this contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the proposed representation projection approach compare to more recent multilingual BERT-based approaches for cross-lingual NER? Since the paper doesn't compare to these, it would be valuable to understand if the representation projection method remains competitive with current state-of-the-art techniques.

2. Could you elaborate on why the frequency-based metric for data selection works well? Is there a theoretical justification for why high-frequency tags in projection-labeled data are more likely to be correct, or is this purely empirical?

3. The paper uses a 2nd-order MEMM for the target language NER system, but the source English NER system is a CRF. Why was MEMM chosen for the target language instead of CRF? How would the results change if the target language NER system also used CRF, especially given that CRFs are generally preferred for NER?

4. The paper states that the quality of projection-labeled data depends on both the source NER system accuracy and alignment accuracy. Could you provide more details on how these factors interact, and whether the data selection scheme helps mitigate the impact of a lower-quality source NER system?
