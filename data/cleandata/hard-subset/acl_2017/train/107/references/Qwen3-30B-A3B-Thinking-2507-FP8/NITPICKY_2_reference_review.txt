

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approach using two main techniques: annotation projection with a data selection scheme, and representation projection via word embeddings. The authors claim to improve upon existing weakly supervised methods by introducing a heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection and a representation projection approach for direct model transfer. They also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that filters low-quality projection-labeled data
2. A representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to source language space for direct NER model transfer
3. Two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in cross-lingual NER, which is important for multilingual information extraction applications.
2. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and both in-house and CoNLL datasets.
3. The representation projection approach is well-motivated and could be useful for practical applications where retraining models for new languages is costly.
4. The paper provides a clear comparison with previous state-of-the-art approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

## Weaknesses

### 1. Fundamental flaw in the data selection scheme (Critical)

The paper's core contribution, the data selection scheme for annotation projection, contains a fundamental methodological flaw. The scheme relies on a frequency table T(e, l) built from the projection-labeled data itself to determine which tags are "more likely correct." This creates a circular dependency: the scheme assumes that the most frequent tag for an entity is the correct one, but the frequency is determined by the same projection process that may be biased.

For example, if the English NER system consistently mislabels "United States" as ORGANIZATION instead of GPE, then when this is projected to Portuguese as "Estados Unidos," the frequency table would show ORGANIZATION as the most frequent tag (0.143 in Table 1), and the data selection scheme would incorrectly select this as high-quality data. This flaw undermines the entire data selection approach, as it could actually propagate errors rather than filter them out.

The authors should have evaluated the quality of the English NER system's predictions on source language data before using it for projection, rather than relying on the projection-labeled data to determine quality.

### 2. Inconsistent reporting and evaluation methodology (Critical)

The paper demonstrates inconsistent reporting of results across different languages and tables:

- In Table 2, they report "Training Size" as the number of tokens for projection-labeled data, but don't clarify whether these are tokens or sentences (the comparable corpora range from 2.0M to 5.5M tokens, so it's likely tokens, but this should be specified).
- For German and Portuguese (Table 3), they report NN2 as having better F1 scores than NN1 (64.9 vs 57.2 for German, 64.7 vs 56.8 for Portuguese), but for Spanish and Dutch (Table 4), they report NN1 as better than NN2. The paper doesn't explain why NN1 performs better for some languages while NN2 performs better for others, nor why they would report only one architecture for certain languages.
- The abstract claims their combined systems outperform both Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL test data, but Table 5 only compares with Tsai et al. (2016) and doesn't mention Täckström et al. (2012) in the results. This is a significant discrepancy.

This inconsistency undermines the credibility of the evaluation and makes it difficult to replicate the results.

### 3. Insufficient detail on co-decoding schemes (Critical)

The paper describes two co-decoding schemes ("exclude-O confidence-based" and "rank-based") but provides insufficient detail to understand or replicate them:

- For the "exclude-O confidence-based" scheme, the authors state "it prefers a non-O tag to an O tag independent of the confidence scores" but don't explain how this preference is implemented algorithmically (e.g., what threshold is used for preferring non-O tags).
- For the "rank-based" scheme, they state "the scheme will first include all the entities detected by the annotation projection system. Then, it will add all the entities detected by the representation projection system that do not conflict with entities detected by the annotation projection system." However, they don't provide a clear algorithm for resolving conflicts when multiple entities might be detected by both systems. The definition of "conflict" (overlap with different boundaries or same boundaries with different tags) is given, but no resolution strategy is provided.

This lack of detail prevents readers from understanding the exact contribution of these schemes or replicating the experiments.

### 4. Technical inaccuracies in methodology (Significant)

The paper contains several technical inaccuracies that undermine the credibility of the work:

- In Section 2.1, the authors claim "CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words, since our NER system has over 50 entity types, and the training time of CRFs grows at least quadratically in the number of entity types." This is incorrect. The training time of CRFs grows linearly with the number of training examples, not quadratically with the number of entity types. The number of entity types affects the number of parameters, but the growth is linear in the number of labels, not quadratic. This misunderstanding suggests a lack of technical rigor in the paper.

- The description of NN2 in Section 2.2 is too vague: "a smoothing layer that computes the cosine similarity between a word embedding and a fixed set of prototype vectors (learned during training) and returns a weighted average of these prototype vectors as the input." The paper doesn't specify the size of the prototype vector set, how they're learned, or how the weights are determined. Without these details, the NN2 architecture is not properly defined.

### 5. Lack of ablation studies (Significant)

The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the individual contribution of each component. For example:
- They don't show how much of the improvement comes from the data selection scheme versus the representation projection approach.
- They don't show how much of the improvement comes from the co-decoding schemes versus using just one of the projection-based approaches.

Without these ablation studies, it's impossible to determine the true contribution of each component to the overall performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide evidence that the English NER system's errors are distributed randomly rather than systematically? How would you address the fundamental flaw in the data selection scheme where errors could be perpetuated rather than filtered out?

2. Why does NN1 perform better than NN2 for some languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) while NN2 performs better for others (Japanese, Korean, Portuguese)? What factors determine which architecture is better for a given language?

3. Can you provide the exact parameters (q, n) used for the data selection scheme for each language in Tables 3 and 4? This is necessary for reproducibility.

4. For the co-decoding schemes, can you provide the exact algorithmic implementation for resolving conflicts when entities detected by both systems have the same boundary but different NER tags?

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper has several fundamental methodological flaws and inconsistencies that undermine its contributions. The most critical issue is the fundamental flaw in the data selection scheme, which could actually propagate errors rather than filter them out. Combined with the inconsistent reporting of results, insufficient detail on co-decoding schemes, and technical inaccuracies, these issues make the paper's claims unreliable and its results difficult to reproduce. While the problem addressed is important, the technical shortcomings are too severe to warrant acceptance in a top-tier ACL venue.
