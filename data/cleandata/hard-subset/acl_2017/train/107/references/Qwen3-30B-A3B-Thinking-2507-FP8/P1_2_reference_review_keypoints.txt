- Paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target annotations, proposing annotation projection with heuristic data selection, representation projection via embedding mapping, and two co-decoding schemes; evaluated on multiple languages/datasets.

- Contribution: a heuristic data selection scheme that filters noisy projected labels and yields large F1 gains when alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 JP, +20.0 KR).

- Contribution: a representation-projection approach mapping target language embeddings into English so a single English NER model can be applied to multiple languages without retraining.

- Contribution: two co-decoding schemes to combine systems, with a rank-based scheme improving results up to +6.2 F1 over the best single system.

- Strength: practical, impactful data selection addressing alignment-quality oversight in prior projection work, with clear empirical gains on low-alignment languages.

- Strength: comprehensive, multi-language evaluation showing consistent improvements and strong comparisons to prior baselines.

- Strength: representation projection is simple, easy to implement, and flexible for adding languages without retraining.

- Weakness: representation projection novelty is limited relative to prior cross-lingual embedding work and lacks clear differentiation from existing methods.

- Weakness: insufficient ablation study on data selection hyperparameters (quality threshold q, entity count n) and sensitivity across languages.

- Weakness: limited comparison to more recent cross-lingual transfer methods (e.g., multilingual BERT), and lack of discussion on relevance versus these advances.

- Weakness: co-decoding superiority (rank-based vs confidence-based) is asserted but not thoroughly analyzed or evidenced.

- Weakness: absence of error analysis to reveal failure modes across entity types or languages.

- Question: how would linear mapping compare to more recent cross-lingual embedding techniques (adversarial methods, multilingual BERT), and could they improve results?

- Question: how sensitive are results to the data selection thresholds q and n across languages; please provide detailed analysis.

- Question: why does rank-based co-decoding perform well even when annotation projection does not always have higher precision (e.g., German)?

- Question: how does the approach compare empirically to multilingual BERT-based cross-lingual NER, and why are weakly supervised methods still relevant?

- Question: how would using larger bilingual dictionaries affect embedding mapping performance, and what evidence supports the claim that larger dictionaries might harm performance?

- Overall recommendation: accept — practical data selection contribution and strong empirical results outweigh limitations in novelty and ablation analysis.
