

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER (Named Entity Recognition) that require no human annotation in the target language. The first approach, annotation projection, projects NER tags from a source language (English) to a target language using comparable corpora. The key innovation is a heuristic data selection scheme that identifies high-quality projection-labeled data based on entity frequency analysis. The second approach, representation projection, maps word embeddings from the target language to the source language using linear mappings, allowing a single English NER model to be directly applied to target languages. The paper also proposes two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection**: The paper introduces a novel heuristic for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low. This addresses a critical limitation in previous annotation projection approaches.

2. **Representation Projection Approach**: The paper proposes a direct NER model transfer method using cross-lingual word embeddings, which is a practical and effective way to build multilingual NER systems without retraining.

3. **Co-Decoding Schemes**: The paper designs two smart ways to combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems, with the rank-based scheme showing particularly strong results.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Practical Data Selection Mechanism**: The paper's data selection scheme based on entity frequency analysis is a clever solution to a real problem in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The empirical results are compelling - for Japanese and Korean (with lower alignment accuracy), the data selection scheme improves F1 scores by 13.9 and 20.0 points, respectively. This is a significant contribution that directly addresses a key limitation in prior work.

2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across six languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets. The results consistently show that the proposed methods outperform existing approaches, with the combined systems achieving F1 scores close to supervised learning baselines (within 5-10 points). The evaluation is thorough and well-structured.

3. **Practical Relevance and Impact**: The methods require no human annotation in the target language (beyond the small dictionary for embedding mapping), which is a major practical advantage for building multilingual NER systems. The paper demonstrates the approach's effectiveness across multiple language pairs with varying alignment qualities, making it highly relevant for real-world multilingual information extraction applications.

4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, clearly explaining the methods, results, and evaluation. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented in a way that makes it easy to understand the contributions and their significance.

5. **Effective Combination of Approaches**: The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based one, demonstrate that combining different projection approaches can yield better results than using either approach alone. This insight is valuable for future work in cross-lingual NLP.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Representation Projection**: The representation projection approach largely builds on prior work on cross-lingual embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013b). While the application to NER is novel, the core technique is not. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this approach differs from or improves upon prior work on cross-lingual embeddings for NER, making it difficult to assess the novelty of this contribution.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent methods in cross-lingual NER, such as those using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based approaches. This is a significant omission, as transformer-based approaches have become the state-of-the-art in many NLP tasks, and the paper's claims about outperforming "state-of-the-art" approaches would be strengthened by comparison with these newer methods.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't provide detailed analysis of cases where the proposed methods fail. Understanding the limitations of the approach (e.g., which entity types are most difficult to transfer, which language pairs are most challenging) would help readers assess the approach's practical utility and guide future improvements.

4. **Overstated Claims about "No Human Annotation"**: The paper claims to require "no human annotation in the target language," but the cross-lingual embedding mapping requires a small training dictionary (5-6K word pairs). This is a minor human annotation requirement that the paper doesn't adequately acknowledge, potentially overstating the "weakly supervised" nature of the approach.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: While the paper evaluates the impact of data selection, it doesn't provide ablation studies for other components of the system (e.g., the impact of different embedding dimensions, the effect of the smoothing layer in NN2, etc.). This makes it difficult to assess the relative importance of different design choices.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your representation projection approach compare to recent transformer-based cross-lingual transfer methods (e.g., multilingual BERT) in terms of performance and resource requirements? Given the recent advances in this area, it would be helpful to understand where your approach stands in the current landscape.

2. Could you provide more details about the nature of the "comparable corpora" used in your experiments? What specific sources were used for each language pair, and how were they obtained?

3. The paper mentions a "small training dictionary" for cross-lingual embedding mapping (5-6K word pairs). How was this dictionary created? Was it automatically extracted from parallel corpora, or did it require manual effort? The paper's claim of "no human annotation in the target language" might be misleading if this dictionary required human intervention.

4. For the data selection scheme, how sensitive is the performance to the thresholds q and n? Could you provide more details on how these thresholds were tuned, and whether the same thresholds work across different languages?

5. The paper states that "CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words" due to the quadratic growth in training time. However, the paper uses CRFs for the source language NER system (trained on 320K tokens) but switches to MEMMs for the target language (with training data up to 5.5M tokens). Could you clarify why the authors chose to use MEMMs for the target language instead of CRFs, and how the training time of CRFs scales with the size of the training data in practice?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and presents a compelling case for the proposed methods. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over existing approaches. However, to fully appreciate the contribution, the authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding comparison with recent methods and clarification of the "no human annotation" claim. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the limitations of the approach and how it might be extended to address these limitations.
