

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without human annotation in target languages: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to a source language space. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on in-house data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as CoNLL data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Data selection scheme for annotation projection**: A heuristic method to select high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora using frequency tables of entity tags.
2. **Representation projection approach**: A method to transfer NER models directly by projecting word embeddings from target languages to source language space.
3. **Co-decoding schemes**: Two methods to combine outputs from the two projection-based approaches, with a rank-based scheme that prioritizes the annotation projection system.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear descriptions of the proposed methods and thorough experimental evaluation across multiple languages. The authors provide detailed explanations of their data selection scheme and representation projection approach, making it easy to understand their methodology.

2. **Empirical improvements over prior work**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over previous cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL test data, particularly for German (with a 2.5-3.6 F1 score improvement). This shows that the proposed techniques have practical value for cross-lingual NER tasks.

3. **Practical utility of the data selection scheme**: The heuristic data selection method is simple yet effective, especially for languages with low alignment accuracy (e.g., Japanese and Korean). The observation that this approach significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese) is a valuable practical contribution for practitioners working with weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The core contributions of this paper are not truly novel. The "representation projection" approach is a direct application of standard cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b), and the data selection scheme is a straightforward heuristic that could be considered an incremental improvement rather than a bold new idea. The paper does not introduce a fundamentally new concept or direction for cross-lingual NER.

2. **Incremental contribution**: The paper combines existing techniques rather than proposing a significant new approach. The co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) are simple ensemble methods that don't represent a substantial conceptual advance. This is a classic case of "incremental improvement" rather than a novel contribution, which is the type of paper I'm less enthusiastic about as a reviewer.

3. **Limited comparison with recent work**: The paper only compares with two specific prior works (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), which are not the most recent state-of-the-art methods. The paper does not compare with more recent approaches in cross-lingual NER (e.g., using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based methods), which would better establish the significance of their contribution.

4. **No theoretical justification**: The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why the proposed approach should work better than existing methods. The improvements are demonstrated empirically, but the authors don't provide a convincing explanation for why their data selection scheme or representation projection approach should yield better results.

5. **Marginal improvements**: While the paper reports improvements over previous work, the gains are relatively modest (typically 2-6 F1 points), and the best combined system still lags significantly behind supervised learning (e.g., 69.9 F1 vs. 75.6 for supervised German NER). The paper doesn't sufficiently argue why these marginal improvements are significant enough to warrant publication.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your representation projection approach compare with more recent cross-lingual transfer methods that use multilingual language models (e.g., multilingual BERT)? Would your approach be competitive with these state-of-the-art methods?

2. The paper mentions that the "representation projection" approach is based on "mapping all languages to English rather than requiring separate embeddings for each language pair." This is a standard approach (as cited in Mikolov et al., 2013b), but could you clarify what makes your specific implementation novel compared to previous work in this area?

3. The paper reports that the annotation projection approach has high precision and low recall, while the representation projection approach has more balanced precision and recall. Could you provide more detailed analysis of why this difference occurs, and how it informs your co-decoding scheme?

## Recommendation

**Reject**

While this paper is well-written and presents a clear evaluation of its methods, the contributions are primarily incremental rather than novel. The core ideas are applications of existing techniques (cross-lingual embeddings, heuristic data selection, simple ensemble methods) rather than a bold new concept or direction. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be an incremental improvement rather than a significant contribution to the field. The paper would be better suited for a more application-focused venue rather than ACL, where the emphasis is on theoretical and methodological innovation.

The paper does not meet the bar for novelty that I would expect from an ACL submission, and the improvements over previous work, while consistent, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance under my review criteria.
