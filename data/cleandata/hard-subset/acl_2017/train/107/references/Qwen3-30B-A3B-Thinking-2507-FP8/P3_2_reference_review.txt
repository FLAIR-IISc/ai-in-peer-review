

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual NER that doesn't require human annotation in the target language. The authors present two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve quality of projected labels, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to the source language space for direct model transfer. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on several languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: This is the strongest contribution, addressing a critical issue in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER - the quality of projected labels is highly dependent on alignment accuracy, and the authors propose a simple but effective way to filter out low-quality projections. This is particularly valuable as previous work often assumed high-quality alignment data, which is rarely the case in practice.

2. **Representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer**: This is a solid but less novel contribution, as it builds on existing work on cross-lingual embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013b) and applies it to NER. The novelty is in the application to NER, not the core technique.

3. **Co-decoding schemes**: These are practical additions but represent a minor contribution rather than a significant methodological advance.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and Effective Data Selection Scheme**: The authors propose a simple yet effective quality metric for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data. The frequency-based approach (Table 1) is intuitive and well-justified, as it leverages the independence assumption between the English NER system and alignment system. The experimental results (Table 2) demonstrate significant improvements (up to +20.0 F1 for Korean) when using their data selection scheme compared to using all projected data, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy. This addresses a real-world problem that has been overlooked in previous cross-lingual NER work.

2. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough experimental results across multiple languages and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), with detailed precision, recall, and F1 scores. The evaluation is well-designed, showing both the strengths and limitations of each approach. The comparison with previous state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is convincing, with the authors' methods consistently outperforming these baselines, especially for German.

3. **Clear Methodological Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed methods. The data selection scheme is particularly well-explained with concrete examples (Table 1) and a logical rationale for the approach. The representation projection approach is also clearly described, building on solid foundations from existing work on cross-lingual embeddings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Representation Projection**: The representation projection approach is essentially applying a well-known technique (cross-lingual word embeddings) to the NER task. While the application to NER is novel, the core technique isn't. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work on cross-lingual embeddings, and the results (Table 3) show that the annotation projection approach often outperforms the representation projection approach, raising questions about the necessity of the latter.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent work. For example, there has been significant progress in cross-lingual NER using multilingual BERT (e.g., Xie et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020). The paper's claim that their method "outperforms the state-of-the-art" may not be accurate if they're not comparing with these more recent approaches, which have become the new standard in the field.

3. **Lack of Analysis on Why Approaches Work**: The paper provides strong empirical results but lacks deeper analysis of why their approaches work. For instance, they show that their data selection scheme improves performance, but they don't analyze what specific types of errors are being reduced. Similarly, they don't provide any analysis of the quality of the projected embeddings or how the representation projection approach handles morphologically complex languages.

4. **Questionable Necessity of Co-Decoding**: The co-decoding schemes are presented as a significant contribution, but the improvement over the best single system is only modest (2.9-6.2 points). The paper could have done a better job of justifying why combining the two systems is better than simply using the better one. In Table 3, for Portuguese, the best single system (AP) already achieves 62.9 F1, while the best combination (AP+NN2 with rank-based co-decoding) only achieves 69.9 F1.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't address how their method would perform on languages with very different grammatical structures from English (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish) or on languages with very different writing systems (e.g., Chinese). The focus on European languages and Japanese/Korean limits the generalizability of the findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., multilingual BERT-based approaches). Why was this comparison omitted, and how would your approach compare with these more recent methods?

2. The representation projection approach uses word embeddings and projects them to English space. However, NER is not just about word-level semantics but also about morphological and syntactic structures. How does your approach handle morphologically rich languages or languages with very different syntax from English? For example, how does it handle agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish, which are not covered in your experiments?

3. The data selection scheme is based on frequency of tags in the projected data. However, this assumes that the English NER system and the alignment system are independent. Have you analyzed the correlation between the errors of the English NER system and the alignment system? How does this affect the quality of the projected labels?

4. The paper mentions that "the alignment systems between English and the target languages are maximum entropy models" but doesn't provide details about the training data or the quality of the alignment systems. How were the alignment models trained, and what is the actual alignment accuracy on the test sets used for evaluation?

5. The co-decoding schemes are presented as a significant contribution, but the improvement over the best single system is only modest (2.9-6.2 points). Could you provide more justification for why this combination is necessary, and whether there are cases where the combination actually hurts performance?

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper presents a practical and effective solution to a real problem in cross-lingual NER, particularly with the data selection scheme for annotation projection. However, the representation projection approach lacks novelty, and the paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and deeper analysis of why their approaches work. If the authors can address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly the limited comparison with recent work and the lack of analysis of their approach's limitations, this paper would be significantly stronger.

The strengths of the paper (particularly the data selection scheme) make it a valuable contribution to the field, but the weaknesses prevent it from being a strong acceptance. The paper is well-written, well-evaluated, and addresses an important problem, but it doesn't push the state-of-the-art significantly beyond what's already been done in the field.
