

# ACL Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme, and (2) representation projection for direct NER model transfer. The authors also design co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Primary Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **Representation projection approach** - The novel idea of projecting word embeddings from target language to source language (English) and applying the source language NER system directly without retraining. This is distinct from previous work that used language-independent features or labels.

2. **Heuristic data selection scheme** - A practical solution for filtering noisy annotation-projection data by selecting sentences based on label frequency consistency, which significantly improves performance for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean).

3. **Rank-based co-decoding scheme** - A smart way to combine outputs from the two projection-based systems that prioritizes the annotation projection system (which has high precision) while adding entities from the representation projection system (which has better recall).

## Strengths

1. **Novel representation projection approach** - This is the paper's strongest contribution. Previous cross-lingual NER work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) focused on creating language-independent features or labels. The authors' approach of directly projecting word embeddings to a common space (English) and using the source language NER system is a fresh perspective that avoids the need for language-specific feature engineering. The method is straightforward but effective, and it represents a meaningful conceptual shift in how we think about cross-lingual transfer.

2. **Significant improvements for low-alignment languages** - For Japanese and Korean (with lower alignment accuracy), the data selection scheme provides dramatic improvements (+13.9 and +20.0 F1 points, respectively). This is not just an incremental improvement but a substantial practical advance for languages where alignment accuracy is problematic. The paper effectively demonstrates that the quality of projection-labeled data depends on alignment accuracy, and their selection scheme addresses this critical limitation.

3. **Thorough evaluation across multiple languages** - The paper evaluates on four in-house languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and CoNLL data (Spanish, Dutch, German), providing comprehensive evidence for the approach's effectiveness. The results consistently show that the combined systems outperform the individual projection-based approaches and previous state-of-the-art methods for the languages where alignment accuracy is low.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods** - The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" approaches but fails to compare with the most recent and strong baselines, such as Lample et al. (2019) on cross-lingual transfer learning for NER. This is a significant omission that weakens the paper's claims about its contribution to the field. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the improvements are truly significant or just incremental.

2. **Overstated novelty of the representation projection approach** - While the representation projection approach is novel in its specific application to NER, it's essentially a straightforward application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this from other embedding-based transfer learning approaches, which makes the novelty seem overstated. The authors should clarify how their specific implementation differs from and improves upon existing embedding projection methods.

3. **Limited analysis of why the approaches work** - The paper provides empirical results but lacks deeper analysis of why the data selection scheme works (beyond frequency-based intuition) or why the rank-based co-decoding is superior. For example, it would be valuable to see which specific entity types or sentence structures benefit most from the data selection, or to analyze error patterns between the two systems to better understand why the rank-based co-decoding works.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your representation projection approach specifically differ from the multilingual embedding approaches used in recent work (e.g., Lample et al., 2019)? Why is direct embedding projection better than fine-tuning on multilingual data?

2. Can you provide a comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., Lample et al., 2019) to strengthen your claim that your approach outperforms "state-of-the-art" methods?

3. The data selection scheme relies on label frequency as a proxy for quality. Can you provide evidence that this frequency-based approach is better than alternative quality estimation methods (e.g., using the alignment model's confidence scores)?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the paper has some weaknesses (particularly the lack of comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art), its core contribution—the representation projection approach—is novel and significant. The paper makes a meaningful conceptual shift in how we think about cross-lingual NER, moving from language-independent features to direct embedding projection. This is the kind of bold, novel approach that aligns with ACL's preference for innovative ideas over methodological perfection.

The improvements for low-alignment languages (Japanese, Korean) are substantial and demonstrate real practical value. The heuristic data selection scheme is a useful practical contribution that addresses a genuine problem in the field. While the paper doesn't make a revolutionary contribution, it does introduce a significant new direction that is both novel and effective, which is exactly what this reviewer values most.

The paper would be even stronger with the suggested comparisons to recent work and more analysis of why the methods work, but these are improvements rather than deal-breakers for acceptance. The novelty of the representation projection approach is sufficient to warrant acceptance, especially given the clear empirical validation across multiple languages.
