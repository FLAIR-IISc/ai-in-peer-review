

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual NER that combines two methods: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme, and (2) representation projection for direct NER model transfer. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The main novelty claims are the data selection scheme for annotation projection, the representation projection approach for direct model transfer, and the co-decoding schemes.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The paper proposes a frequency-based metric to select high-quality projected NER data from noisy comparable corpora, which significantly improves performance for languages with low alignment accuracy.

2. **Representation projection approach for NER model transfer**: The paper proposes projecting word embeddings from target language to source language to enable direct application of the source language NER model to target languages without retraining.

3. **Co-decoding schemes**: The paper introduces two schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Practical solution to a real problem**: The paper addresses a significant challenge in NLP - building NER systems for low-resource languages without human annotation. The weakly supervised approach is practical and could be immediately useful for many applications requiring multilingual information extraction.

2. **Significant empirical improvements**: The data selection scheme provides substantial improvements (13.9-20.0 F1 points) for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean), which is a meaningful contribution to the field. The results demonstrate that the approach can achieve performance close to supervised learning for some languages (e.g., Korean with 90K tokens of human annotation).

3. **Effective combination of approaches**: The co-decoding schemes successfully leverage the complementary strengths of the two projection-based approaches (annotation projection has high precision, representation projection has more balanced precision/recall), resulting in consistent improvements over individual systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty for the main contribution**: The representation projection approach is essentially based on existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Faruqui and Dyer, 2014) applied to NER. The paper does not clearly establish how this differs from or improves upon these prior methods specifically for NER. The claim of novelty is not sufficiently supported, and the authors fail to cite key works in the cross-lingual embeddings space (e.g., Artetxe et al., 2017; Conneau et al., 2018), which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Incremental contribution**: The main contribution (data selection scheme) is a practical improvement to existing annotation projection methods, but it's not a bold new concept. The paper builds on well-established approaches (Zitouni and Florian, 2008; Ehrmann et al., 2011) without introducing a fundamentally new direction or insight.

3. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but fails to compare with more recent work on cross-lingual NER, such as approaches using multilingual BERT or other pretrained language models. This makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed methods.

4. **Methodological details lacking**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the training of word embeddings, the exact process for learning the cross-lingual mappings, or how the threshold parameters for data selection were determined. The claim that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance" is not supported with evidence.

5. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is limited to only 7 languages (4 in-house, 3 from CoNLL), which is a small subset of the languages where cross-lingual NER is needed. More extensive evaluation would strengthen the claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your representation projection approach specifically differ from or improve upon existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (e.g., Artetxe et al., 2017; Conneau et al., 2018) that have been applied to NLP tasks? Please provide a more detailed comparison.

2. Why did you choose to compare against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) specifically, rather than more recent state-of-the-art methods for cross-lingual NER?

3. How did you determine the optimal threshold parameters (q and n) for the data selection scheme? Were these parameters tuned on the development set, and if so, how did you avoid overfitting?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a practical and effective approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with significant empirical improvements, it fails to establish a truly novel contribution or bold new direction. The main contributions are incremental improvements to existing methods rather than a significant conceptual advance. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from the state of the art, particularly in the cross-lingual embedding space, and the novelty claims are not well-supported.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be a solid engineering contribution but lacking the conceptual innovation required for acceptance at ACL. The paper offers a useful pipeline for cross-lingual NER, but it doesn't push the field forward in a meaningful way. I would encourage the authors to either identify a more novel angle or provide stronger evidence of how their approach fundamentally advances the field beyond incremental improvements.
