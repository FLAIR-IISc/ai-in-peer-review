- Paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation using annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding.  
- Contribution: heuristic entity-frequency-based data selection for annotation projection that boosts performance under low alignment (e.g., +13.9 F1 JP, +20.0 F1 KO).  
- Contribution: representation projection mapping target word embeddings to English to apply a single English NER model across languages.  
- Contribution: two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) that combine systems and improve F1 by 2.9–6.2 points.  
- Strength: practical and effective data selection addressing alignment-quality issues, with large empirical gains for low-alignment languages.  
- Strength: strong empirical evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, showing combined methods approach supervised performance in some cases.  
- Strength: novel, sensible combination of complementary approaches (high-precision annotation projection + balanced representation projection).  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to recent transformer-based cross-lingual NER (e.g., mBERT-era methods), limiting assessment of current-state performance.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of the data selection scheme (threshold choices, relation to alignment accuracy) and guidance for practitioners.  
- Weakness: claim of “no human annotation” is questionable because representation projection requires a 5K–6K word-pair dictionary often derived from parallel resources.  
- Weakness: no error analysis to show where systems fail (by entity type or language phenomena).  
- Weakness: limited discussion of scalability and computational cost when processing large comparable corpora.  
- Question: how do the methods compare to recent transformer-based cross-lingual NER (e.g., multilingual BERT)?  
- Question: how does variation in alignment accuracy affect data selection performance and why does alignment vary across languages?  
- Question: how would representation projection perform with little or no training dictionary for very low-resource languages?  
- Question: what is the effect of using a different source language instead of English, and what factors influence that choice?  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—address comparisons to recent work, clarify the “no human annotation” claim, and add analyses (data-selection, errors, scalability).
