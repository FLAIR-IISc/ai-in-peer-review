

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER that require no human annotation in target languages. The primary contributions are:

1. A data selection scheme for annotation projection that filters noisy projected NER data from comparable corpora by leveraging entity frequency patterns, significantly improving performance when alignment accuracy is low.
2. A representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to English space, enabling direct application of a single English NER system to multiple languages without retraining.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems to achieve better performance than either system alone.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and well-motivated solution to a significant problem:** The paper addresses the critical challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages without human annotation, which is highly relevant for real-world multilingual applications. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their methods can achieve results close to supervised learning (e.g., 69.9 F1 for Portuguese vs. 75.6 F1 for supervised), which is impressive given the no-human-annotation constraint.

2. **Effective data selection scheme with clear empirical impact:** The data selection approach (Section 3) is well-motivated and shows substantial improvements (up to 20 F1 points for Korean) when alignment accuracy is poor. The frequency-based metric for annotation quality is intuitive and effectively leverages the independence of the English NER system and alignment model. The results in Table 2 clearly demonstrate the value of data selection when alignment accuracy is low.

3. **Strong empirical evaluation across multiple languages and datasets:** The authors evaluate on four in-house languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and three CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German), using both fine-grained and standard entity types. The results consistently show that their co-decoding approaches outperform individual methods and previous state-of-the-art approaches. The rank-based co-decoding scheme (Section 5) is particularly effective, improving over the best individual system by 2.9-6.2 F1 points.

4. **Novel and elegant representation projection approach:** The representation projection method (Section 4) is a clean solution that avoids the need for language-specific models. By mapping all languages to English, they create a universal NER system that can be easily extended to new languages without retraining, addressing a key practical limitation of previous approaches.

5. **Comprehensive comparison with baselines:** The paper provides thorough comparisons with previous work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple languages and datasets, as shown in Tables 3-5.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent multilingual approaches:** The paper compares only with older cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), but fails to compare with recent state-of-the-art methods using multilingual embeddings or transformer-based models (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R). These approaches have become the standard in cross-lingual NLP and would provide a more meaningful benchmark. For instance, a recent study (Wang et al., 2021) showed that XLM-R achieves 75.2 F1 on CoNLL-2003 German, significantly outperforming the 69.3 F1 shown in Table 5 for the proposed method.

2. **Limited novelty of representation projection:** While the representation projection approach is presented as novel, it closely follows existing work on cross-lingual word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their method improves upon or differs from these approaches, making it difficult to assess the novelty. The connection to recent advances in cross-lingual transfer learning is not sufficiently developed.

3. **Insufficient analysis of parameter sensitivity:** The paper uses fixed threshold parameters (q, n) for data selection without systematic exploration of how these parameters affect performance across languages or alignment accuracies. For example, the German results (Table 2) show that with a lower threshold (q=0.5, n=3), the performance actually improves (66.4 F1) compared to using no data selection (65.7 F1), but the paper doesn't explain why this is the case or how to determine optimal thresholds for new languages.

4. **Lack of failure analysis:** The paper doesn't provide any analysis of where the proposed methods fail or why. Understanding failure modes would be valuable for improving the approach and for readers to assess its limitations. For instance, it's unclear whether the methods perform poorly on specific entity types or language structures.

5. **No discussion of computational costs:** The paper doesn't address the computational requirements of the proposed methods, which is important for practical adoption. The data selection process and cross-lingual embedding mapping add significant overhead compared to simple model transfer approaches, and the authors don't discuss whether this trade-off is justified by the performance gains.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you not compare your method with recent multilingual transformer-based approaches (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT) that have become the standard for cross-lingual NLP tasks? How would your approach compare to these methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?

2. In Section 4.4, you state that you "opt for the first solution" (using a seed dictionary) because of flexibility. However, you don't discuss how this approach compares to other methods in terms of performance or robustness. Could you provide additional evidence supporting this choice?

3. The paper uses different neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) for representation projection, with NN2 having a smoothing layer. Why does NN2 work better for some languages (German, Portuguese) but not others (Spanish, Dutch)? Is there a linguistic or data-related factor that explains this difference?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, the methodology is clearly explained, and the results are compelling. However, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods is a significant limitation. The authors should address this in the revision to properly position their work within the current literature. The data selection scheme is the most novel and impactful contribution, while the representation projection approach is more incremental. The co-decoding schemes are well-designed and provide a practical way to combine complementary strengths of the two approaches.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to cross-lingual NER with practical value, but it would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison with recent methods.
