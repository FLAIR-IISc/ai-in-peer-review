

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme, and (2) a representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine these approaches. As a skeptical reviewer, I assess these contributions based on methodological soundness and rigorous validation.

From my perspective, the most significant contribution is the data selection scheme for annotation projection, which addresses a real problem in cross-lingual NER by selecting high-quality projected data. The representation projection approach is the most novel but lacks sufficient novelty and validation. The co-decoding schemes are minor improvements that don't represent a substantial contribution.

## Strengths

1. **The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a practical, well-validated improvement to an existing approach**: The authors provide a clear metric for annotation quality based on entity frequency (Table 1), and they demonstrate significant improvements (up to +20.0 F1 score) for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean). This is a substantial practical contribution that addresses a genuine limitation in previous work. The experimental validation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) is thorough and convincing.

2. **The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets**: The authors evaluate their approaches on both in-house data (four languages) and CoNLL data (three languages), showing consistent improvements over baselines. This demonstrates the robustness of their data selection scheme and the viability of their overall approach. The comparison with supervised learning (Table 3) shows that their best combined system achieves F1 scores close to supervised systems trained with 90K-125K tokens of human-annotated data.

3. **The paper addresses a practical problem with real-world significance**: Building NER systems for multiple languages without human annotation in the target language is an important practical problem. The authors' approach provides a viable solution that could be deployed in multilingual information extraction systems, which is valuable for industry applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison with established baselines**: The authors claim their approach outperforms "state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches proposed in Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016)" but fail to provide a direct comparison with these baselines. Table 5 shows their results on CoNLL test data but does not include the exact results from these previous works. This is a significant weakness as the paper's primary claim is that their approach outperforms existing methods.

2. **The representation projection approach lacks novelty and sufficient validation**: The authors propose a "new" approach for direct NER model transfer based on word embedding projection, but this is very similar to existing work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). The authors don't clearly explain how their approach differs from or improves upon existing methods. Additionally, the results for this approach (NN1 and NN2) are not as strong as the annotation projection approach, and the improvement over the best single approach is marginal (only 2.9-6.2 F1 points). The paper would be significantly stronger if they demonstrated a clear advantage over existing cross-lingual embedding methods.

3. **Lack of clear justification for neural network architectures**: The authors introduce two neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) but don't provide a compelling justification for why they chose these specific architectures. They state "we do not employ other common features such as gazetteers or character-level representations as such features might not be readily available or might not transfer to other languages," but this is a weak justification. They don't compare their neural network approaches to more standard architectures or provide ablation studies to justify the specific design choices.

4. **The co-decoding schemes are not substantially novel**: The confidence-based and rank-based co-decoding schemes are relatively simple combinations of existing approaches. The authors don't provide strong evidence that these schemes are significantly better than simpler alternatives, and the improvements they report are marginal (only 2.9-6.2 F1 points). This represents a minor contribution at best.

5. **Lack of discussion of limitations**: The paper fails to adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, they don't address how their approach would perform on languages with very different grammatical structures or on low-resource languages with minimal comparable data. They also don't discuss the computational requirements of their approach or how it scales with language pairs.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their approach outperforms Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL test data, but they don't provide the exact results from these previous works. Can they provide a direct comparison with these baselines?

2. The representation projection approach appears very similar to existing work on cross-lingual embeddings. Can the authors clarify how their approach differs from or improves upon existing methods like Mikolov et al. (2013b)?

3. The authors use a heuristic data selection scheme that requires a frequency table of entities. How would this approach scale to languages with very large vocabulary sizes or to languages with many rare entities?

4. The paper claims that the representation projection approach "does not need to re-train when a new language is added," but they don't provide evidence of how this would work in practice. Can they provide details on the computational cost of adding a new language?

5. The authors state that "the quality of the projection-labeled data heavily depends on the alignment accuracy of the parallel sentences," but they don't discuss how their approach would handle cases where alignment accuracy is extremely low (e.g., below 50%).

## Overall Recommendation

I have reservations about this paper due to the lack of sufficient novelty in the representation projection approach and the insufficient comparison with established baselines. While the data selection scheme for annotation projection is a valuable contribution, the paper's primary claim of outperforming state-of-the-art approaches is not adequately supported. The paper would be significantly stronger with more rigorous validation against established baselines and a clearer demonstration of the novelty of the representation projection approach.

I recommend rejection, but would consider a major revision if the authors can provide a direct comparison with the claimed state-of-the-art approaches and better justify the novelty of their representation projection method.
