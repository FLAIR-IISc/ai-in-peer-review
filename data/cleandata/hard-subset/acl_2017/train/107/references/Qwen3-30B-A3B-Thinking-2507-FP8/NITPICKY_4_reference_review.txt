

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme, and (2) representation projection based on cross-lingual word embedding mappings. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on in-house NER data for four languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and CoNLL data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves accuracy when alignment accuracy is low (evidenced by +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean).
2. A novel application of cross-lingual embedding mappings to NER, enabling direct model transfer without retraining for new languages.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, with the rank-based scheme providing the most significant improvements.

## Strengths
1. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is well-motivated and addresses a genuine problem in cross-lingual NER. The authors provide clear evidence that this scheme significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., Japanese and Korean), which is a substantial contribution to the field.

2. The representation projection approach is a novel application of cross-lingual embeddings to NER. While the core techniques (word embeddings and cross-lingual mapping) are not new, their application to NER in a weakly supervised setting is innovative and could be useful for other NLP tasks.

3. The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based one, provide a simple yet effective way to combine the outputs of two different approaches. The paper demonstrates that combining the two projection-based systems yields better results than either approach alone across multiple languages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Technical errors in neural network architecture description**: The paper states "both networks have one hidden layer, with sigmoid and softmax activation functions on the hidden and output layers respectively." Sigmoid activation for hidden layers is highly unusual in modern neural networks (typically ReLU or tanh is used) and can lead to vanishing gradients. This is a fundamental technical error that undermines the credibility of the neural network experiments.

2. **Overstated performance claims**: The paper claims that "the best combined system achieves an F1 score close to the supervised Korean/German system (trained with 90K/125K tokens of human-annotated data)." For Korean, the supervised system has 54.8 F1 and the best weakly supervised system has 51.8 F1 (a 3-point difference), and for German, the supervised system has 72.6 F1 and the weakly supervised best system has 69.3 F1 (a 3.3-point difference). In NLP evaluation, a 3-point difference is significant and cannot be considered "close." This overstatement misrepresents the results.

3. **Missing technical details**: The paper fails to explain several key technical aspects:
   - The "smoothing layer" in NN2 is not described (what does it do? how is it implemented?).
   - The "NER-trained unk vector" is not defined (what is it? how was it trained?).
   - The exact implementation of the co-decoding schemes is vague (e.g., how is "priority" implemented beyond just including all entities from AP first?).
   These omissions make it difficult to reproduce the results.

4. **Inconsistent evaluation methodology**: The paper states "for each target language, we also have a set of human-annotated NER data (ranging from 30K to 45K tokens) which are served as the test data," but also states "The development data are used for tuning the parameters of the models." This is confusing and suggests the human-annotated data might be used for tuning rather than as true test data, which would invalidate the evaluation. The paper should clarify whether these are development or test data.

5. **Formatting and technical inconsistencies**: 
   - In Section 2.1, the equation for pλ(l|x) uses "e" instead of "exp" to indicate the exponential function.
   - In Table 2, for German, the training size with data selection (3.37M) is larger than without data selection (5.47M), which is impossible since data selection should reduce the dataset size. This is likely a typo.
   - The paper states "We train word2vec for 1 epoch for English/Spanish and 5 epochs for the rest of the languages for which we have less data." But it's unclear why they would train for fewer epochs on English (which has more data) than on target languages.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that the hidden layer activation function is sigmoid. Why was this choice made, and how does it compare to using ReLU or tanh? Is there evidence that sigmoid is better for this specific task?

2. In Table 2, the training size for German with data selection (3.37M) is larger than without data selection (5.47M). This appears to be a typo. Please clarify the correct numbers.

3. The paper claims that the weakly supervised systems achieve "F1 scores close to the supervised systems" for Korean and German. For Korean, the difference is 3.0 points (54.8 vs. 51.8), and for German, it's 3.3 points (72.6 vs. 69.3). In NLP evaluation, these differences are significant. Can you explain why you consider these differences "close"?

4. The paper mentions a "smoothing layer" for NN2 but provides no details. Please describe how this layer works and what it contributes to the model's performance.

5. The "NER-trained unk vector" is mentioned but not defined. What is this vector, how was it trained, and how does it differ from a standard unknown token embedding?

## Additional Comments
- The paper claims that "CRFs cannot handle training data with a few millions of words, since our NER system has over 50 entity types, and the training time of CRFs grows at least quadratically in the number of entity types." This is an overstatement. While CRF training time does grow with the number of labels, it's not quadratic in the number of labels (it's linear in the number of labels for first-order CRFs). The paper should clarify this.

- The paper states "We have implemented a linear-chain CRF model and a general-order MEMM model" in Section 2.1, but in the experiments, they use 2nd-order MEMM models for all experiments. They should clarify why they chose 2nd-order MEMMs rather than using the general-order MEMM they implemented.

- The paper does not provide statistical significance tests for the improvements. In NLP, it's standard practice to provide statistical significance tests (e.g., using the t-test) to show that improvements are not due to chance.

Given these significant technical issues and overstatements, I recommend rejection of this paper. While the core ideas have merit, the technical errors and lack of detail undermine the paper's credibility. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication.
