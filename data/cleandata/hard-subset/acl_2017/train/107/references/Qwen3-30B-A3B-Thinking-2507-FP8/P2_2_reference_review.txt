

# ACL Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without requiring human annotations in the target languages. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve the quality of projected labels from comparable (non-parallel) corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to English for direct NER model transfer. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves performance (by 13-20 F1 points) for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean).
2. The representation projection approach that enables direct NER model transfer without retraining for new languages.
3. The rank-based co-decoding scheme that effectively combines the complementary strengths of the two projection-based approaches.

## Strengths

### 1. Well-motivated data selection scheme for annotation projection
The paper identifies a critical gap in previous work: the quality of projection-labeled data heavily depends on alignment accuracy, yet previous approaches didn't consider this when selecting sentence pairs for projection. The authors' heuristic data selection scheme, which uses frequency tables to measure annotation quality, is well-motivated and addresses a real problem in the field. The empirical results are compelling, showing significant improvements (13.9 F1 for Japanese, 20.0 F1 for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low. This is a practical contribution that could benefit many researchers working on cross-lingual transfer.

### 2. Clear presentation of a simple yet effective representation projection approach
The representation projection approach is elegantly simple: map target language word embeddings to English space using a linear transformation, then apply the English NER system directly. This avoids the need for retraining for new languages, which is a major practical advantage. The paper provides a thorough technical explanation of how the cross-lingual embeddings are built and applied, making the approach easy to understand and implement.

### 3. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets
The authors evaluate their approaches on four in-house languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and three CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German), with both in-house and open datasets. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is thorough, showing consistent improvements across languages. The results demonstrate that the combined approaches achieve F1 scores close to supervised learning (e.g., 69.9% for Portuguese vs. 75.6% supervised).

### 4. Effective co-decoding schemes that leverage complementary strengths
The rank-based co-decoding scheme is particularly well-designed, recognizing that annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall. The scheme first includes all entities detected by annotation projection, then adds non-conflicting entities from representation projection. This is a smart, practical approach that effectively combines the strengths of both methods.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited novelty of the representation projection approach
While the implementation is clean and well-executed, the core idea of using cross-lingual embeddings for NER transfer has been explored in prior work (e.g., Gouws et al. 2015, Guo et al. 2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach from these prior works or explain why their specific implementation provides meaningful improvements over existing methods. The authors should clarify the novelty of their representation projection approach compared to prior work.

### 2. Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods
The paper compares only with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are older approaches. The field has advanced significantly since then, with recent methods using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based models achieving much better results. The authors should compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches to properly position their contribution.

### 3. Limited analysis of why data selection works better for some languages
The paper shows that the data selection scheme provides the most improvement for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), but doesn't adequately analyze why this is the case. For example, German has high alignment accuracy (76.1 F1) yet still shows some improvement (from 65.7 to 66.4 F1), while Portuguese (88.0 F1 alignment accuracy) shows minimal improvement. The paper doesn't provide a deeper analysis of the relationship between alignment accuracy, data selection effectiveness, and final NER performance.

### 4. Unaddressed limitations for non-European languages
The experiments are limited to European languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch), which have relatively similar linguistic structures to English. The paper doesn't discuss how well the approach would work for languages with very different writing systems or morphological structures (e.g., Arabic, Chinese, or agglutinative languages). This limits the generalizability of the claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your representation projection approach compare to recent multilingual transformer-based approaches (e.g., multilingual BERT) for cross-lingual NER? Have you considered incorporating such models?

2. The paper shows that data selection works best for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case. Could you elaborate on the relationship between alignment accuracy, data quality, and the effectiveness of your selection scheme?

3. The paper claims "no human annotation in the target languages," but relies on comparable corpora with alignment information, which may require some human effort (e.g., to build the alignment model). Could you clarify the human effort required for the alignment step and how it compares to the human effort required for NER annotation?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a well-executed and practical approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with clear empirical results. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is particularly strong and addresses a real problem in the field. However, the novelty of the representation projection approach is limited, and the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods is a significant weakness. The paper would be strengthened by addressing these limitations and providing a clearer position in the current literature.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above)
