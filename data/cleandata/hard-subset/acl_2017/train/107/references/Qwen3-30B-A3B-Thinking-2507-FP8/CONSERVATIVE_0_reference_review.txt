

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve the quality of projection-labeled data, and (2) a representation projection approach based on cross-lingual word embeddings. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. They claim their methods outperform existing approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on both in-house and CoNLL NER datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A data selection scheme for annotation projection (which refines existing methods rather than introducing a fundamentally new technique)
2. Application of cross-lingual embedding techniques to NER (representation projection approach)
3. Co-decoding schemes that combine the two projection-based systems

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear and well-structured comparison with the two baseline approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), which is good practice for evaluation.

2. The data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrates practical utility, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean), where it provides significant improvements (+13.9 and +20.0 F1 score respectively).

3. The paper evaluates the approaches on multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch), which demonstrates reasonable generalizability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core approaches**: The representation projection approach is essentially an application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b) to NER, which is not novel. The annotation projection with data selection is a refinement of existing work (Zitouni and Florian, 2008; Ehrmann et al., 2011), not a fundamentally new approach. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how these approaches represent a significant conceptual advancement over established methods.

2. **Comparison with outdated state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" approaches, but the baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) are over 6-7 years old. The field has advanced significantly since then, with more recent works (e.g., using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based models) that would provide a more meaningful comparison. This undermines the significance of the claimed improvements.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies and validation**: The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to justify the specific components of their approach. For example, it doesn't demonstrate why their data selection scheme (with its specific metrics) is better than simpler alternatives like thresholding on confidence scores. The paper also doesn't provide evidence that the representation projection approach is fundamentally better than other embedding-based transfer methods.

4. **Unfair comparison with supervised learning**: The paper compares weakly supervised approaches to supervised learning on the target language, but the supervised baselines use much larger amounts of data (e.g., 90K tokens for Korean vs. 246K tokens for the weakly supervised approach). This comparison is misleading, as the supervised approach has more data to work with. A fairer comparison would be to compare against weakly supervised approaches that use similar amounts of data.

5. **Questionable claims about "no human annotation"**: The paper claims "no human annotation in the target languages," but it relies on human-annotated English data for training the source model. This is a standard weakly supervised approach, but the framing might mislead readers into thinking it's a more radical approach than it actually is.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you choose to compare against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) rather than more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (e.g., those using multilingual transformers)?

2. Could you provide ablation studies demonstrating the necessity of your specific data selection metrics (q(y) and n(y)) compared to simpler alternatives like thresholding on confidence scores?

3. How do you account for the fact that the supervised baselines use more data than the weakly supervised approaches? Would your results hold if you compared against weakly supervised baselines with similar data constraints?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a useful refinement of existing weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approaches, particularly for the data selection scheme. However, the core contributions lack sufficient novelty to justify publication in ACL. The comparison with state-of-the-art is outdated, the experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous, and the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that these approaches represent a significant advancement over established methods. I would recommend rejection, but the authors could potentially revise and resubmit with a more thorough comparison to recent work, stronger validation, and clearer articulation of the novelty.

**Rating: Reject**
