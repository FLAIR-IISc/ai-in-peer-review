

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotation in target languages. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projection-labeled data, and (2) representation projection for direct NER model transfer by mapping target language word embeddings to source language space. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches.

My assessment of the paper's main contributions (in order of strength):

1. **Heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The paper's key contribution is a practical method to select high-quality projection-labeled data based on entity frequency analysis in the target language. This addresses a critical limitation in existing annotation projection approaches where alignment accuracy affects data quality.

2. **Co-decoding schemes combining projection-based approaches**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme, which prioritizes outputs from the annotation projection system (which has higher precision), consistently outperforms individual systems across multiple languages by 2.9-6.2 F1 points.

3. **Representation projection approach for direct NER transfer**: While the core technique builds on prior work, the application to NER is well-executed and demonstrates practical utility for cross-lingual transfer without retraining.

## Strengths

1. **Effective data selection scheme**: The paper's heuristic data selection method significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean), demonstrating clear value for real-world scenarios where alignment quality varies. This addresses a specific, unmet need in the literature that previous work overlooked.

2. **Practical co-decoding approach**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme effectively combines the strengths of the two projection-based approaches (annotation projection's high precision and representation projection's better recall), consistently outperforming individual systems. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as it shows how to intelligently combine complementary approaches.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages**: The paper evaluates on both in-house data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese (with 50+ entity types) and standard CoNLL data for Spanish, Dutch, and German. This broad evaluation across languages and data sets strengthens the paper's claims.

4. **Clear comparison to prior work**: The paper provides a thorough comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), demonstrating that the proposed combined systems outperform these previous approaches, especially for German. This establishes a strong baseline for future work.

5. **Technical soundness of representation projection**: The implementation of the representation projection approach is well-executed, with careful attention to word embedding mapping and handling of out-of-vocabulary words. The approach enables direct application of a single English NER system to multiple languages without retraining, which is a significant practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of representation projection**: While the application to NER is valuable, the core technique (cross-lingual word embedding mapping) is largely based on Mikolov et al. (2013b) and similar prior work. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing methods or explain why it's particularly well-suited for NER.

2. **Lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: The paper only compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't include comparisons to more recent approaches using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based models. This makes it difficult to assess the current relevance of the paper's contribution.

3. **Inconsistent performance of neural network architectures**: The paper shows that NN2 (with smoothing layer) performs better than NN1 for representation projection in some cases (in-house data) but worse in others (CoNLL data). The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for this inconsistency, which weakens the confidence in the representation projection approach.

4. **Insufficient analysis of data selection scheme**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why the data selection scheme works or provide a systematic way to determine the thresholds (q, n). The thresholds were chosen empirically for each language, but the paper doesn't explain the general principles behind these choices.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the requirement for comparable corpora with alignment information. It doesn't compare the approach to methods that use parallel corpora (which might be more accurate but harder to obtain).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods using multilingual BERT or similar transformer-based models. Could the authors provide a comparison with these methods to better position their contribution?

2. For the data selection scheme, how were the thresholds (q, n) determined for different languages? Was this done through systematic analysis or trial-and-error? Is there a principled way to determine these thresholds without relying on held-out data?

3. The paper shows that NN2 (with smoothing layer) works better than NN1 for some languages but worse for others. What specific characteristics of the languages or data might explain this difference in performance?

4. How does the approach handle languages with very different grammatical structures or word order from English? The paper focuses on German, Japanese, Korean, and Portuguese, but doesn't discuss how the method would perform with more divergent languages.

5. The paper mentions that the training dictionary for cross-lingual embedding mappings should be limited to approximately 5K-6K word pairs. What happens if the dictionary is smaller or larger? Is there an optimal size for this dictionary, and how might it vary across languages?
