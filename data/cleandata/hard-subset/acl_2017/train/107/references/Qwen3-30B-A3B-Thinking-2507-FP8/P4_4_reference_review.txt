

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual NER (Named Entity Recognition) with no human annotation in the target language. The authors present two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to English vector space for direct model transfer. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches to achieve better performance.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)

1. **The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection** - This is the most significant contribution, as it directly addresses a critical limitation in previous annotation projection approaches by introducing a quality metric based on entity frequency tables and a threshold-based selection mechanism.

2. **The representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer** - This is a solid technical contribution that applies existing cross-lingual embedding techniques to NER in a principled way, though it builds on established methods rather than introducing fundamentally new ideas.

3. **The co-decoding schemes that combine the two projection-based approaches** - The rank-based co-decoding scheme is well-motivated by the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (high precision/low recall vs. more balanced precision/recall) and demonstrates practical value.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and effective data selection scheme**: The authors introduce a well-motivated and effective method for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora. The frequency table approach to measure annotation quality is simple yet effective, and the experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements (up to +20.0 F1 score for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low. This directly addresses a key limitation in previous annotation projection work, where alignment accuracy was not properly accounted for in data selection.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across six languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and two distinct NER datasets (in-house and CoNLL). The results consistently show that the proposed methods outperform previous cross-lingual NER approaches, with the combined co-decoding schemes achieving the best results. The comparison with supervised learning (using human-annotated data) provides a meaningful benchmark for the weakly supervised approach.

3. **Clear technical contribution of the representation projection approach**: The paper clearly explains how to project word embeddings from target languages to English vector space, enabling direct application of an English NER system to target languages without retraining. This approach is well-justified by the related work section and builds on established cross-lingual embedding techniques while applying them to NER in a novel way.

4. **Practical co-decoding schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme is particularly well-motivated by the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (annotation projection tends to have high precision/low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall). The empirical results show that this scheme consistently outperforms the individual projection-based approaches, with improvements of 2.9-6.2 F1 score for in-house data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the representation projection approach**: The representation projection method builds directly on existing work in cross-lingual embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Faruqui and Dyer, 2014) and does not significantly advance the state of the art in this area. The paper does not clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon existing cross-lingual NER methods, which weakens the novelty claim for this contribution.

2. **Lack of analysis on the data selection scheme**: While the data selection scheme shows strong empirical results, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of why it works. For instance, it would be valuable to understand which specific types of sentences are being selected (e.g., sentences with high alignment confidence, specific entity types, etc.) and how the quality threshold parameters (q and n) should be set for different languages or alignment accuracies. The paper states that "the differences in the improvements are mainly due to the alignment accuracy of the comparable corpora" but doesn't provide deeper insights into this relationship.

3. **Comparison with more recent cross-lingual NER work**: The paper claims to outperform the state-of-the-art approaches of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but does not compare with more recent cross-lingual NER methods that have emerged since these papers (e.g., multilingual BERT-based approaches). This is a significant omission given the rapid progress in cross-lingual transfer learning in recent years.

4. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper does not provide any analysis of when the proposed methods fail or under what conditions they perform poorly. Understanding the limitations of the approach would be valuable for future work and would strengthen the paper's contribution by providing a more complete picture of the method's applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but does not compare with more recent cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., multilingual BERT-based approaches). How would your approach compare with these more recent methods, and what are the advantages/disadvantages of your weakly supervised approach compared to fully supervised methods that use multilingual pretraining?

2. The paper states that the annotation projection approach uses a linear-chain CRF model while representation projection uses neural networks. Why were different model architectures chosen for the two approaches? Would using the same architecture across both approaches provide more comparable results, and how might this affect the performance of the co-decoding schemes?

3. The data selection scheme uses thresholds (q and n) that are tuned per language. Could you provide more insight into how these thresholds relate to alignment accuracy, and whether there are principled ways to determine these thresholds without relying on held-out development data?
