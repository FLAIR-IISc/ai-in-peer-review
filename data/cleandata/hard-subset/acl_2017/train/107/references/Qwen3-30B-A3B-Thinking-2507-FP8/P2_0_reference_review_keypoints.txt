- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection via embedding mapping, plus two co-decoding schemes.  
- Data selection frequency-based metric substantially improves annotation projection, especially when alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).  
- Representation projection maps target-language word embeddings to English with a linear mapping learned from a small bilingual dictionary to enable direct NER model transfer.  
- Two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) effectively combine annotation and representation projection outputs and outperform individual methods.  
- Strong empirical results across in-house and CoNLL datasets, showing practical impact and top weakly supervised performance for several languages.  
- Clear methodology and solid evaluation with ablation studies and appropriate baselines.  
- Novel data selection scheme addresses alignment-quality issues in annotation projection and yields large gains for low-alignment languages.  
- Lacks comparison to recent multilingual transformer-based cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), limiting claims of state-of-the-art relevance.  
- Insufficient analysis of limitations and failure cases (e.g., performance on typologically different or low-resource languages, computational costs).  
- No principled guidance for choosing threshold parameters (q, n) for new languages without gold data.  
- Limited analysis of the representation projection model variants (NN1 vs. NN2) and unexplored combinations with data selection.  
- Question: How does the approach compare or adapt to multilingual transformers like XLM-R/mBERT?  
- Question: How robust is the method when the bilingual dictionary is very small or absent?  
- Question: How should one select data-quality thresholds for new languages without gold-standard data?  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on comparing to recent methods and addressing limitations and analysis gaps.
