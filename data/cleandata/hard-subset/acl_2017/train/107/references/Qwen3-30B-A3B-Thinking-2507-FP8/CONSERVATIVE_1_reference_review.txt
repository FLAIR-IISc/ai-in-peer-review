

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps word embeddings from target languages to English to enable direct use of an English NER system. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of these two approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on in-house NER data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as on CoNLL NER data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: This is the most methodologically sound contribution, addressing a real limitation in annotation projection approaches by developing a frequency-based metric to select high-quality projection-labeled data.

2. **Evaluation of cross-lingual NER approaches on multiple languages**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, which is valuable for understanding the practical applicability of weakly supervised approaches.

3. **Co-decoding schemes for combining projection-based systems**: While not particularly novel, the co-decoding schemes provide a practical way to combine multiple weakly supervised approaches to improve performance.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation of the data selection scheme**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the heuristic data selection scheme significantly improves performance, especially when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean). The evaluation across multiple languages with different alignment accuracies demonstrates the robustness of this approach.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation on multiple datasets and languages**: The paper evaluates on both in-house data (for Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and standard CoNLL data (for Spanish, Dutch, German), providing a thorough assessment of the methods' performance across different languages and entity types.

3. **Clear presentation of the limitations of weakly supervised approaches**: The paper acknowledges that weakly supervised approaches don't reach the performance of supervised learning, but are much better than previous weakly supervised methods. This realistic assessment is valuable for the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" methods, but only compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are relatively old approaches. The paper does not compare with more recent methods like multilingual BERT, XLM-R, or other transformer-based approaches that have become the new state-of-the-art for cross-lingual tasks. This is a significant oversight, as the paper's claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" is questionable without such comparisons.

2. **Insufficient novelty in the representation projection approach**: The representation projection approach is essentially a straightforward application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b) to the NER task. The paper doesn't provide strong evidence that this approach is significantly better than existing methods for cross-lingual NER, nor does it explain why this specific approach would be better than alternatives.

3. **Lack of ablation studies and deeper analysis**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to understand the impact of different parameters in the data selection scheme. It also lacks detailed error analysis to understand why the co-decoding schemes work better than individual approaches. Without this, it's difficult to assess the true value of the proposed methods.

4. **Unjustified assumptions in the data selection scheme**: The paper assumes that high-frequency labeling is more likely to be correct, but doesn't provide evidence that this assumption holds in practice. It also doesn't discuss cases where this assumption might fail (e.g., if the source NER system has a strong bias toward a particular entity type).

5. **Insufficient justification for the co-decoding schemes**: The co-decoding schemes are presented as novel, but they are relatively simple extensions of standard model combination techniques. The paper doesn't provide strong evidence that these specific schemes are significantly better than other possible combination approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" methods, but only compares with approaches from 2012 and 2016. Can you provide a comparison with more recent methods like XLM-R, mBERT, or other transformer-based approaches that have become the new state-of-the-art for cross-lingual NER?

2. The representation projection approach seems to be a direct application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques. Can you provide more evidence that this approach is significantly better than existing methods for cross-lingual NER, beyond what's shown in Table 3?

3. The data selection scheme assumes that high-frequency labeling is more likely to be correct. Can you provide evidence that this assumption holds in practice, and discuss cases where this assumption might fail (e.g., if the source NER system has a strong bias toward a particular entity type)?

4. The paper mentions that "the combined systems outperform the state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches proposed in Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016)." However, these are relatively old papers. Can you provide a comparison with more recent work that has been published since then?

5. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies showing how the quality threshold (q) and entity number threshold (n) affect performance. Can you provide such an analysis to demonstrate the sensitivity of the data selection scheme to these parameters?

## Overall Recommendation

While the data selection scheme for annotation projection is methodologically sound and provides a valuable contribution to the field, the paper's claims of outperforming "state-of-the-art" methods are undermined by the lack of comparison with more recent approaches. The representation projection approach lacks novelty, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify the claimed improvements. The paper would benefit significantly from comparing with more recent state-of-the-art methods and providing deeper analysis of the proposed approaches.

Given my preference for established methods and skepticism toward unproven approaches without extensive validation, I am unable to recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. The paper would need to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by comparing with more recent state-of-the-art methods and providing stronger evidence for the claimed improvements.
