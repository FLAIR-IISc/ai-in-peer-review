

# ACL Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme for selecting high-quality projected NER data from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to a source language (English) vector space. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**

1. **The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: This is the most significant practical contribution, as it addresses a critical limitation in previous annotation projection approaches (that quality depends heavily on alignment accuracy). The scheme uses frequency tables to measure annotation quality and select high-quality projection-labeled data.

2. **The representation projection approach**: This offers a simpler, more direct method for cross-lingual NER by mapping word embeddings from target to source language, enabling the use of a single English NER system across languages.

3. **The co-decoding schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme that prioritizes the high-precision annotation projection system demonstrates a clever way to combine the complementary strengths of the two approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and effective data selection scheme**: The authors address a real limitation in annotation projection approaches that has been overlooked in previous work. Their data selection scheme, which uses frequency tables to measure annotation quality and select high-quality projected data, is simple but highly effective. The results for Japanese and Korean (where alignment accuracy is low) are particularly impressive, with F1 improvements of 13.9 and 20.0 points respectively. This is a significant practical contribution that could be widely adopted in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER systems.

2. **Clear demonstration of complementary strengths of two approaches**: The authors correctly observe that the annotation projection approach has high precision but low recall, while the representation projection approach has more balanced precision and recall. Their rank-based co-decoding scheme that prioritizes the high-precision annotation projection system is a smart solution that effectively leverages these complementary strengths, as evidenced by the consistent F1 improvements (2.9-6.2 points) over individual systems.

3. **Good evaluation design with multiple languages**: The evaluation covers multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) across both in-house and CoNLL data sets, providing a reasonable demonstration of the approach's applicability. The comparison with supervised learning (using human-annotated data in the target language) is also valuable, showing that the weakly supervised approach can achieve performance close to supervised methods (e.g., 69.9 F1 for Portuguese vs. 75.6 for supervised).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" approaches, but only compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are quite dated (2012 and 2016). The field has advanced significantly since then, with recent works using multilingual BERT, XLM-R, and other neural transfer learning approaches. Without comparison to these more recent methods, the claim of "outperforming the state-of-the-art" is not well-supported. For example, XLM-R has achieved strong results in cross-lingual NER, and the authors should compare with this.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide enough ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component. For instance, how much of the improvement comes from the data selection scheme versus the representation projection approach? How much does the co-decoding scheme contribute beyond the individual approaches? Without these, it's difficult to assess the relative importance of each contribution.

3. **Limited evaluation scope and language coverage**: The evaluation is limited to six languages total (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch), which is insufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. The paper doesn't evaluate on low-resource languages where cross-lingual transfer would be most beneficial, nor does it test on a wider range of languages to show scalability.

4. **Questionable novelty of the representation projection approach**: While the authors present this as a novel contribution, the core idea of projecting word embeddings between languages for NER is not entirely new. Previous work (e.g., by Mikolov et al. 2013b) has explored similar approaches. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing work in this area, making the novelty claim somewhat weak.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches, but only compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). Could the authors provide a comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods, such as XLM-R, mBERT, or other neural transfer learning approaches that have been shown to achieve strong results in cross-lingual NER?

2. The paper states that "the quality of the projection-labeled data heavily depends on the alignment accuracy of the parallel sentences, while this factor was not considered in the previous work." However, the paper uses "comparable corpora" rather than parallel corpora. Could the authors clarify how the comparable corpora were obtained and how they differ from the parallel corpora used in previous work?

3. The representation projection approach uses English as the common vector space. The paper mentions using 800-dimensional embeddings for target languages but doesn't specify if these were pre-trained or trained from scratch. Could the authors clarify this and discuss how the dimensionality choice affects performance?

4. The authors state that "the rank-based co-decoding scheme that combines AP and NN2 achieves the highest F1 score among all the weakly supervised approaches for Korean, German and Portuguese." However, for Japanese, the AP system alone achieves the highest F1. Could the authors explain why the co-decoding scheme doesn't improve performance for Japanese (where alignment accuracy is lowest)?

## Overall Recommendation

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper makes some valuable practical contributions, particularly the data selection scheme for annotation projection, it has several significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting ACL's high standards. The lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods (particularly neural approaches like XLM-R) is a major flaw, as it undermines the novelty claim. The limited evaluation scope and insufficient ablation studies also weaken the paper's contribution.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Update the state-of-the-art comparison**: Extend the evaluation to include recent neural cross-lingual NER approaches (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT) to properly establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.

2. **Conduct more thorough ablation studies**: Provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (data selection, representation projection, co-decoding) to better understand the relative importance of each contribution.

3. **Expand the evaluation scope**: Test the approach on a wider range of languages, including low-resource languages where cross-lingual transfer would be most impactful, to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. **Clarify novelty claims**: Better differentiate the representation projection approach from existing work in cross-lingual embeddings, emphasizing what makes their specific application to NER novel.

5. **Consider using more modern NER systems**: Instead of using a CRF as the source NER system, consider using a neural NER model (like the ones they've developed) as the source system for annotation projection, which might yield better projected data quality.

The paper addresses a real problem in cross-lingual NER and offers some practical solutions, but to be acceptable for ACL, it needs to better position itself against recent work and provide more comprehensive evidence of its contribution.
