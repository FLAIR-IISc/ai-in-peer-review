

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme based on frequency tables of NER tags, and (2) a representation projection approach using cross-lingual word embeddings. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The paper claims that the combined system outperforms previous work by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL NER test data.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that improves performance when alignment accuracy is low
2. A straightforward application of cross-lingual word embeddings to NER model transfer (representation projection)
3. Co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the data selection scheme, demonstrating significant improvements (+13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low. This is a concrete contribution that addresses a real problem in cross-lingual NER where alignment quality is often poor.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and both in-house and CoNLL datasets. The authors provide detailed breakdowns of precision, recall, and F1 scores across these languages, which is valuable for understanding the approach's strengths and weaknesses.

3. The paper shows that the combined system (using co-decoding) achieves F1 scores close to supervised learning baselines (e.g., 69.9% vs. 75.6% for Portuguese), demonstrating that weakly supervised approaches can be quite effective for certain languages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison against recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper only compares against work from 2012 and 2016, while there has been significant progress in cross-lingual NER since then. Recent approaches based on multilingual BERT and other transformer models (e.g., Devlin et al., 2019; Pfeiffer et al., 2020) would be a much more relevant baseline. Without comparison against these, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed methods are truly competitive with current best practices.

2. **Unjustified novelty claims**: The "representation projection" approach is essentially a straightforward application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b) to NER. The paper doesn't demonstrate how this approach is better than simply using pre-trained multilingual embeddings directly (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) without any additional projection. The claim of "novel" representation projection lacks sufficient justification.

3. **Heuristic data selection lacks theoretical grounding**: The data selection scheme is described as "heuristic" and based on a frequency-based metric. The paper doesn't provide any theoretical justification for why this metric should work, nor does it compare it to other potential metrics (e.g., confidence scores from the source NER system). The claim that "it is less likely that both systems make consistent errors simultaneously" is a heuristic assumption that isn't rigorously validated.

4. **Insufficient analysis of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the proposed approaches. For example, how well do these methods work for languages with very different grammatical structures from English? What happens when the target language has no comparable corpus (e.g., low-resource languages)? The evaluation is limited to languages with relatively good comparable corpora.

5. **Overstated claims about performance**: The paper claims the approach "outperforms the state-of-the-art," but only compares against two specific older methods. The results (e.g., 69.9% F1 for Portuguese) are still significantly below supervised baselines (75.6% F1), suggesting that the weakly supervised approach still has substantial room for improvement.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you choose to compare only against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) rather than more recent state-of-the-art methods, such as those using multilingual BERT or XLM-R? How would your approach compare to these more recent methods?

2. The representation projection approach seems to be a direct application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques to NER. Can you provide evidence that this approach is better than simply using pre-trained multilingual embeddings directly (without any additional projection)?

3. The data selection scheme is described as "heuristic" with no theoretical grounding. Could you provide more justification for why the frequency-based metric is effective, and how it compares to other potential metrics (e.g., confidence scores from the source NER system)?

4. The paper shows improvements for some languages (e.g., Japanese, Korean) but not for others (e.g., German). Could you provide more analysis of why the approach works better for some languages than others?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly regarding their applicability to low-resource languages with poor comparable corpora. The evaluation should also include error analysis to understand where the approaches succeed and fail, rather than just reporting aggregate F1 scores. The authors should also clarify how their approach differs from simply using pre-trained multilingual embeddings directly, as this would strengthen their claims about novelty.
