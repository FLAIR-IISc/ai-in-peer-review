

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotation in the target language. It proposes two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection method with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve quality of projected labels, and (2) a representation projection method that maps target language word embeddings to the source language space. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two methods. The authors evaluate their approaches on in-house data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as on CoNLL 02/03 data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions

1. **Effective data selection scheme for annotation projection**: A heuristic method that selects high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean compared to no data selection).

2. **Representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer**: A method that maps target language word embeddings to the source language space, allowing a single English NER system to be directly applied to target languages without retraining.

3. **Co-decoding schemes**: Two methods to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, with the rank-based scheme achieving the best results (up to 6.2 F1 points improvement over the best individual system).

## Strengths

1. **Practical and impactful data selection scheme**: The authors identify a critical limitation in previous annotation projection work (ignoring alignment quality when selecting sentences for projection) and propose a simple yet effective heuristic based on entity tag frequency. The results clearly demonstrate the value of this approach, especially when alignment accuracy is low (Japanese and Korean). This is a significant practical contribution that directly addresses a real limitation in the field.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation and strong results**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL). The results consistently show that the proposed methods outperform previous state-of-the-art approaches, with the combined systems achieving F1 scores that are very close to supervised systems (e.g., 69.9 F1 for Portuguese vs. 75.6 for supervised). The comparison with prior work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is particularly convincing.

3. **Clear implementation and practical benefits**: The representation projection approach is simple to implement and offers significant flexibility in adding new languages without retraining. The paper explains the approach clearly, and the results show that it's effective. The ability to map all languages to English (a common language) is a practical advantage over approaches requiring separate embeddings for each language pair.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of representation projection approach**: The core technique of representation projection is largely based on prior work by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and others. While the application to NER is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their specific implementation from prior work on cross-lingual embeddings. The authors should better explain how their approach differs from or improves upon existing methods.

2. **Lack of thorough ablation study**: The paper shows that the data selection scheme improves results but doesn't provide a comprehensive ablation study. For example, it doesn't explore how the quality threshold (q) and entity number threshold (n) affect performance across different languages. The paper mentions they "tried different threshold parameters" but doesn't show how they determined the optimal values or how sensitive the results are to these choices.

3. **Comparison with more recent work**: The paper compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare to more recent cross-lingual NER work. There's been significant progress in cross-lingual transfer learning with BERT and other transformer models. The paper should at least acknowledge these approaches and explain why their weakly supervised approach remains relevant.

4. **Insufficient explanation of co-decoding scheme superiority**: The paper states that the rank-based co-decoding scheme outperforms the confidence-based one "based on this observation" (that AP has high precision and low recall while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall), but doesn't provide evidence or analysis to support this explanation. A deeper analysis of why one co-decoding scheme works better would strengthen the paper.

5. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any error analysis to understand where the systems fail. For example, it would be useful to know if the systems have particular difficulties with certain entity types or languages. An error analysis would provide more insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The representation projection approach uses a linear mapping from target language embeddings to English. How does this compare to more recent approaches to cross-lingual word embeddings (e.g., those based on adversarial training or using multilingual BERT)? Could these more recent techniques further improve the results?

2. The data selection scheme uses a frequency-based metric to select sentences. How sensitive are the results to the choice of threshold parameters (q and n)? Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how these thresholds affect performance across different languages?

3. The paper states that the rank-based co-decoding scheme "gives higher priority to the annotation projection system" because it "tends to have a high precision and low recall." However, the results show that for some languages (like German), the representation projection system (NN2) has better precision than the annotation projection system. Could you explain why the rank-based scheme still works well for these languages?

4. The paper compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but not to more recent work on cross-lingual NER. How does your approach compare to recent methods that use multilingual BERT or similar models for cross-lingual transfer? Is there a reason why weakly supervised approaches are still relevant given the success of these more recent supervised approaches?

5. The paper uses a small training dictionary (5K-6K word pairs) for cross-lingual embeddings. How would the results change if a larger dictionary were used? The paper states that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance," but doesn't provide evidence for this claim.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution with its data selection scheme for annotation projection, which addresses a critical limitation in previous work. The results are strong and well-evaluated across multiple languages and datasets. While there are some weaknesses in the novelty claim for the representation projection approach and the lack of thorough ablation studies, these are outweighed by the practical significance of the data selection scheme and the strong empirical results. The paper provides a strong baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER that is both effective and practical to implement.
