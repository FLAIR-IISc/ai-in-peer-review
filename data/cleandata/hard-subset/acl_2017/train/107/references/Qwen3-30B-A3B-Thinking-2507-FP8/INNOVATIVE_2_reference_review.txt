

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projection-labeled data, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to a source language (English) to enable direct model transfer. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems. The approach is evaluated across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (evidenced by +20.0 F1 score for Korean).
2. The representation projection approach that applies cross-lingual embeddings to NER, enabling direct model transfer without retraining.
3. The co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems.

## Strengths
1. **Practical innovation for low-resource scenarios**: The data selection scheme is a meaningful practical improvement to annotation projection, particularly valuable for languages with low alignment accuracy. The paper demonstrates significant gains (up to +20.0 F1 for Korean) where previous approaches would fail, addressing a real pain point in cross-lingual NER.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages with both in-house and CoNLL datasets. The results consistently show improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), with the combined systems achieving F1 scores close to supervised learning (especially for Korean and German).

3. **Clear articulation of system behaviors**: The authors insightfully observe that the annotation projection system has high precision/low recall while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall. This understanding directly informs their rank-based co-decoding scheme, which is a clever and effective way to combine the two systems' strengths.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of fundamental novelty**: While the paper presents a solid application of existing techniques (data selection, cross-lingual embeddings), the core contributions are not fundamentally novel. Cross-lingual embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013b) and data selection based on label consistency have been used in other NLP tasks before. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how the approach is conceptually different from prior work.

2. **Underdeveloped theoretical justification**: The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why the data selection scheme works better than random selection or why representation projection would be effective for NER. The heuristic approach is practical but doesn't advance the theoretical understanding of cross-lingual transfer.

3. **Incremental nature of contributions**: The paper builds incrementally on existing approaches without introducing a truly bold new paradigm. While the combination of these techniques is effective, it's more of a "systems" contribution than a "methods" contribution that would warrant high novelty scores. The co-decoding schemes, for example, are standard ensemble methods.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your data selection scheme compare to other filtering methods (e.g., using alignment confidence scores directly)? Could the frequency-based approach be generalized to other NLP tasks?

2. The paper claims your representation projection approach is "new," but it's essentially applying cross-lingual embeddings to NER. How is this fundamentally different from the approach used by Tsai et al. (2016) who also used language-independent features?

3. The paper shows significant improvements for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), but the gains are smaller for high-alignment languages (German, Portuguese). Can you provide a deeper analysis of why the data selection scheme is more beneficial for some languages than others?

## Recommendation
**Weak Accept**

The paper presents a well-executed, practical contribution to cross-lingual NER that addresses a real-world problem with significant empirical gains. While the core techniques aren't fundamentally novel, the systematic application and thorough evaluation across multiple languages with substantial improvements (especially for low-alignment languages) make this a valuable contribution to the field. The paper's strength lies in its practical approach and clear demonstration of how to effectively apply existing techniques to achieve better cross-lingual NER performance.

I would recommend acceptance because the paper successfully demonstrates a practical, effective solution to a challenging problem, and the data selection scheme represents a meaningful improvement over previous annotation projection approaches. While the novelty isn't groundbreaking, the paper's strong empirical results and clear practical contribution outweigh the incremental nature of the methodological improvements. The authors have provided a strong baseline for future work in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.
