- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approaches requiring no target-language annotation: annotation projection with data selection and representation projection of embeddings, plus co-decoding schemes.  
- Data selection scheme filters noisy projected NER data using entity frequency patterns, improving performance when alignment is poor.  
- Representation projection maps target word embeddings into English space so a single English NER model can be applied to multiple languages without retraining.  
- Two co-decoding schemes combine outputs from both projection methods to outperform individual systems.  
- Addresses a practical, important problem and achieves strong results close to supervised baselines for some languages (e.g., Portuguese).  
- Data selection shows clear empirical gains (up to ~20 F1 for Korean) and effectively leverages frequency-based annotation quality.  
- Strong multi-language empirical evaluation across in-house and CoNLL datasets, with rank-based co-decoding improving 2.9–6.2 F1 over best individual system.  
- Representation projection is elegant and enables a universal English-centric NER system extendable to new languages without retraining.  
- Comprehensive comparisons with older baselines demonstrate consistent improvements over prior non-transformer methods.  
- Fails to compare with recent multilingual transformer-based methods (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), which are now standard and likely stronger baselines.  
- Representation projection’s novelty is limited given prior cross-lingual embedding work and lacks clear differentiation from existing methods.  
- No systematic analysis of sensitivity to data-selection parameters (q, n) or guidance for choosing them across languages.  
- Lacks failure analysis detailing where and why methods fail (e.g., specific entity types or language structures).  
- No discussion of computational costs or trade-offs for data selection and embedding mapping versus simpler transfer approaches.  
- Asks why recent transformer baselines were not compared and how the proposed methods fare in performance and efficiency.  
- Requests more evidence comparing the chosen seed-dictionary mapping approach to alternative mapping methods in terms of robustness and performance.  
- Seeks explanation for why NN2 architecture helps some languages but not others—whether linguistic or data factors explain this.  
- Overall: well-written, compelling results with the data selection scheme as the most novel contribution, but needs comparisons to recent SOTA and more analysis to strengthen the paper.
