

# ACL Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotation in the target language. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) an annotation projection approach with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection approach that maps target language word embeddings to English vector space, allowing direct application of an English NER system to target languages. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength):**

1. **Effective Data Selection Scheme for Annotation Projection:** The authors identify a critical but overlooked issue in traditional annotation projection approaches (that alignment accuracy heavily influences projection quality but was not considered in data selection) and propose a simple yet effective heuristic to select high-quality projection-labeled data based on frequency tables of entity tags. This approach significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (up to +20 F1 points for Korean).

2. **Representation Projection Approach for Direct NER Model Transfer:** The paper introduces a practical method to project word embeddings from target languages to English vector space, enabling direct application of an English NER system to target languages without retraining. This approach is conceptually simple and provides a "one-size-fits-all" solution for cross-lingual NER.

3. **Co-Decoding Schemes to Combine Projection-Based Systems:** The authors design two smart co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) that effectively combine the outputs of the annotation projection (high precision, low recall) and representation projection (more balanced precision/recall) approaches, achieving better performance than either individual approach.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Practical Data Selection Scheme:** The authors correctly identify a significant gap in previous work on annotation projection - that alignment accuracy was not considered when selecting sentence pairs for projection. Their data selection scheme based on frequency tables of entity tags is simple yet highly effective, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy (e.g., Japanese and Korean). The experimental results clearly demonstrate the value of this approach, with substantial improvements (+13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean) when compared to no data selection. This is a significant contribution to the field as it addresses a practical challenge in cross-lingual NER that previous work overlooked.

2. **Practical Representation Projection Approach:** The representation projection approach is conceptually simple and highly practical. By mapping target language embeddings to English vector space, the authors enable direct application of an English NER system without retraining, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications where adding new languages should require minimal effort. The paper provides clear explanations of how to learn cross-lingual embeddings and apply them for NER, making the approach easy to implement and understand. The use of a small training dictionary (5K-6K word pairs) for mapping is also a practical consideration that addresses a common challenge in cross-lingual embedding learning.

3. **Effective Co-Decoding Schemes with Complementary Strengths:** The authors demonstrate a thoughtful understanding of the complementary nature of their two approaches (annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall). Their rank-based co-decoding scheme, which gives priority to the annotation projection system, is particularly effective and shows significant improvements (2.9-6.2 F1 points) over the best individual approach for Korean, German, and Portuguese. This shows good insight into how to combine different NER approaches effectively.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art Methods:** The paper claims to outperform "the state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches proposed in Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016)," but fails to compare with more recent methods that could provide stronger baselines. For example, recent work using multilingual models (like mBERT, XLM-R, or XLM) has shown strong performance on cross-lingual NER tasks. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to establish the true novelty and impact of the proposed methods. The authors should compare with at least 2-3 recent state-of-the-art approaches to properly contextualize their contributions.

2. **Unclear Justification for "Low-Resource" Languages:** The paper refers to Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, and Dutch as "low-resource" for NER, but for some of these languages (e.g., German, Dutch, Spanish), there are publicly available NER datasets. For example, German has the GermEval dataset, and Spanish has the CONLL-2002 dataset. The authors should clarify why these languages are considered low-resource in their context and how their approach would perform on truly low-resource languages with minimal or no available data. Without this clarification, the motivation for the weakly supervised approach is weakened.

3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks qualitative analysis of where the proposed methods fail. For instance, what types of entities or language constructions are particularly challenging for the proposed approaches? Without this analysis, it's difficult to understand the limitations of the method and where future work should focus. A few examples of failure cases would greatly strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but does not compare with more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods. Could you provide a comparison with recent methods using multilingual transformers (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT) that have shown strong performance on cross-lingual NER tasks? This would help establish the true novelty and impact of your approach.

2. The paper claims that Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, and Dutch are "low-resource" languages for NER. However, German, Dutch, and Spanish have well-established NER datasets (e.g., GermEval, Dutch NER, CONLL-2002). Could you clarify why these languages are considered low-resource in your context, and how your approach would perform on truly low-resource languages with minimal or no available data?

3. The representation projection approach uses 800-dimensional embeddings for target languages while English uses 300-dimensional embeddings. Could you explain the rationale behind using different dimensions for different languages, and how this choice affects the performance of the model? Would using the same dimension for all languages yield similar results?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes valuable contributions to the field of cross-lingual NER, particularly with its effective data selection scheme for annotation projection and the practical representation projection approach. The co-decoding schemes are well-motivated and demonstrate significant improvements over individual approaches.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with recent state-of-the-art methods (particularly those using multilingual transformers) and by providing a clearer justification for why the target languages are considered "low-resource." The lack of qualitative analysis of failure cases is also a notable limitation.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The paper presents a solid contribution with practical and effective methods that address real challenges in cross-lingual NER. While the weaknesses noted above need to be addressed, the core contributions are strong and the experimental results are promising. With the suggested revisions (particularly adding comparisons with recent methods and clarifying the low-resource justification), this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL.

**Recommendation for Authors:** To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT) for cross-lingual NER
2. Clarifying why the target languages are considered "low-resource" in your context
3. Including qualitative analysis of failure cases to better understand the limitations of the approach
4. Potentially adding a discussion of how the approach might scale to truly low-resource languages with minimal data
