

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: (1) an annotation projection method with a new heuristic data selection scheme to improve quality of projected labels, and (2) a representation projection method that maps word embeddings from target languages to source language space. The paper also introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine these approaches. The authors evaluate their methods on multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and CoNLL datasets, claiming improvements over previous work.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A practical heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that improves quality of projected labels from noisy comparable corpora (this is the most substantial contribution)
2. A straightforward application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques to NER via representation projection (this is not a fundamentally novel contribution)
3. Simple co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches (this is a minor contribution)

## Strengths
1. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The paper provides extensive experiments across multiple languages and datasets, including both in-house and CoNLL data. The evaluation covers different aspects of the problem (precision, recall, F1) and includes comparisons with multiple baselines.

2. **Practical data selection scheme**: The heuristic data selection based on frequency tables of projected entities is a reasonable and practical approach to handling noisy projected labels, which addresses a genuine challenge in this area.

3. **Methodological soundness**: The authors build on established techniques (CRFs, neural networks, word embeddings) rather than introducing entirely new models or architectures, which aligns with my preference for methodological soundness.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty in core approach**: The representation projection approach is simply a direct application of existing cross-lingual embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b) to the NER task. The paper doesn't introduce any novel method for cross-lingual representation learning, but rather applies existing techniques. This is a significant weakness for a paper claiming to make new contributions.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but these are quite old works (2012 and 2016). The paper fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods, such as those using multilingual BERT or other contemporary neural approaches. This omission significantly undermines the claimed significance of the contribution.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate how much each component contributes to the overall performance. For example, it's unclear how much the data selection scheme contributes versus the representation projection approach.

4. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims "the combined systems outperform the state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches," but doesn't properly define what "state-of-the-art" means in the current context. The comparison is limited to two older methods, making the claimed improvements appear less significant than presented.

5. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides no strong theoretical justification for why representation projection should work well for NER. The justification ("NER is semantic in nature") is vague and not sufficiently supported by evidence or analysis.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why didn't you compare with more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., those using multilingual BERT or other recent neural approaches) instead of just Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016)?

2. Can you provide more detailed ablation studies to show how much each component (data selection, representation projection, co-decoding) contributes to the overall performance?

3. What is your theoretical justification for why representation projection (word embeddings from target to source language) would work well for NER, beyond the vague statement that "NER is semantic in nature"?

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper makes a modest practical improvement to an existing approach (the data selection scheme) but overstates the novelty of its core contribution (representation projection). The lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods and insufficient ablation studies make it difficult to assess the true significance of the work. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims to be overstated and insufficiently validated against current standards.
