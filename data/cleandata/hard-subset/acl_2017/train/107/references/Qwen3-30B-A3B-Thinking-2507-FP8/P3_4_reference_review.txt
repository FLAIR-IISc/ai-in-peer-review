

# Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotations in the target language. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) Annotation Projection with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projected labels, and (2) Representation Projection that maps target language word embeddings to a source language vector space for direct NER model application. They also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The work is evaluated on in-house data for Japanese, Korean, German, and Portuguese, as well as CoNLL 02/03 data for Spanish, Dutch, and German.

## Main Contributions
1. A practical heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves accuracy when alignment is noisy.
2. A representation projection approach that enables direct NER model transfer without retraining.
3. Effective co-decoding schemes that combine the two projection-based approaches to achieve better results than individual methods.

## Strengths

**1. Practical data selection scheme:** The paper identifies a critical gap in previous annotation projection work (ignoring alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs for projection) and proposes a simple yet effective heuristic. The frequency-based metric (Table 1) and quality thresholding (Equation 6) are well-motivated by the intuition that consistent labeling across multiple instances is more likely to be correct. The empirical results (Table 2) are compelling, showing +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean when alignment accuracy is low (69.4% and 62.0% respectively), demonstrating the real-world impact of this contribution.

**2. Efficient representation projection approach:** The authors present a novel way to enable direct model transfer without the need for language-specific resources or retraining. By projecting word embeddings to a common English vector space (Section 4.2-4.3), they create a single universal NER system. This is more efficient than previous approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) that required language-specific resource creation. The approach also allows for easy addition of new languages without constraints on data type.

**3. Effective co-decoding schemes:** The rank-based co-decoding scheme (Section 5) is particularly well-motivated by the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (annotation projection has high precision/low recall, representation projection has more balanced precision/recall). The results (Table 3) show that combining approaches via rank-based co-decoding improves over the best individual method by 2.9-6.2 F1 points across languages, which is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

**1. Lack of comparison with recent SOTA methods:** The paper claims to outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches. Multilingual transformer models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) have achieved strong results in cross-lingual NER and would provide a much more meaningful benchmark. The paper's approach should be compared to these methods to demonstrate its relevance in current NLP research.

**2. Limited evaluation scope:** The evaluation is restricted to CoNLL 02/03 data and in-house datasets. The paper doesn't evaluate on more challenging, recent cross-lingual benchmarks like XTREME or XNLI, which would better demonstrate the real-world applicability of the approach. The in-house datasets are not publicly available, limiting reproducibility.

**3. Questionable novelty of representation projection:** While the authors present representation projection as novel, the core idea is not substantially different from existing cross-lingual embedding work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work in this space. The comparison with related work (Section 4.4) is superficial and fails to highlight key distinctions.

**4. Lack of ablation studies and implementation details:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (data selection vs. representation projection). The training details for the word embeddings and cross-lingual mappings are insufficiently described (e.g., what specific phrase table was used to create the training dictionary?). These omissions make it difficult to assess the relative importance of the proposed methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why didn't the authors compare with recent multilingual transformer models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) that have shown strong cross-lingual transfer capabilities in NER tasks?

2. How does the data selection scheme perform on languages with high alignment accuracy (like Portuguese in Table 2)? Would the benefits be less pronounced in such cases?

3. The paper claims the representation projection approach "does not need to re-train when a new language is added," but how does the computational cost of projection compare to the cost of training a new NER model for a language?

4. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component. Could the authors share results showing the individual impact of the data selection scheme versus the representation projection approach?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable practical contribution to cross-lingual NER by addressing a key bottleneck in annotation projection through a simple yet effective data selection scheme. The representation projection approach is also a useful addition to the toolkit. However, the lack of comparison with recent SOTA methods and limited evaluation scope significantly weaken the paper's impact. The authors should address these limitations in the revision to strengthen their contribution to the field. The paper is technically sound but needs to better position itself within current research trends.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above)
