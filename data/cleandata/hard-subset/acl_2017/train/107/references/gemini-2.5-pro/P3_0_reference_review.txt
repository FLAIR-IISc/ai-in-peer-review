### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any annotated data. The authors propose and evaluate two primary, and largely orthogonal, approaches: an improved Annotation Projection (AP) method and a Representation Projection (RP) method. They then demonstrate that combining these two methods via co-decoding schemes leads to state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A highly effective combination of two distinct cross-lingual transfer paradigms.** The core strength of this work lies in successfully combining an annotation projection approach (which relies on parallel corpora and alignment quality) with a representation projection approach (which relies on monolingual corpora and embedding space alignment). The proposed co-decoding schemes demonstrate that these methods are complementary, leading to significant performance gains over either individual system and establishing a new state-of-the-art on the CoNLL benchmarks.

2.  **A simple yet powerful data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** The authors introduce a method to filter noisy projected annotations based on entity-label frequency and the number of entities per sentence. This heuristic provides substantial improvements, especially for language pairs with low-quality word alignments (e.g., +20 F1 for Korean), addressing a critical weakness of traditional annotation projection.

3.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse settings.** The paper's claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on both in-house data with a large, fine-grained tagset (50+ types) and standard CoNLL benchmark data (4 types). This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the proposed methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper's primary strength is its impressive empirical results. The final combined systems outperform strong, established baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL test data. The gains, particularly for German, are significant and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed combination.

2.  **Novelty in the Combination Strategy:** While annotation projection and representation projection are not new concepts in themselves, the insight to treat them as two independent "views" of the data and combine them is powerful. The paper shows that these methods have different precision/recall characteristics (AP is often high-precision, RP is more balanced), making them ideal candidates for an ensemble. This system-building contribution is valuable for the community.

3.  **Practical and Effective Heuristic for Annotation Projection:** The data selection scheme proposed in Section 3.1 is intuitive, easy to implement, and demonstrably effective. By filtering the training data for the target language model, it directly tackles the "garbage-in, garbage-out" problem inherent in projection-based methods that rely on noisy automatic alignments. The dramatic F1 score improvements shown in Table 2 for Japanese and Korean validate this contribution's practical utility.

4.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, explain each component of their system (AP, RP, Co-Decoding), and present their results in a structured and compelling manner. The methodology is described with sufficient detail to understand the key ideas.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Representation Projection Component:** The paper presents representation projection as a "new approach for direct NER model transfer" (Section 1). However, the core technique of learning a linear transformation between pre-trained monolingual word embedding spaces using a seed dictionary was introduced by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been widely applied to other cross-lingual tasks like parsing and POS tagging, as the authors acknowledge in Section 4.4. The contribution here is more the successful application and integration of this existing technique into an NER system, rather than a novel method for representation projection itself. The framing could be more precise about this.

2.  **Insufficient Detail on the Neural Network Models:** The description of the neural network architectures, particularly NN2, is vague. The "smoothing layer" is described as computing cosine similarity with "a fixed set of prototype vectors (learned during training)" and returning a "weighted average." This description lacks critical details for reproducibility: What is the nature of these prototype vectors? How many are there? How are they initialized and learned (e.g., are they part of the backpropagation)? Is this a form of attention or memory network? This lack of clarity makes a key component of the representation projection system opaque.

3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Co-Decoding Results:** The paper observes that different co-decoding schemes (rank-based vs. confidence-based) work better on different datasets but does not provide a deep analysis as to why. The authors note that the rank-based method is suited for when one system (AP) has high precision, but the results in Table 4 (CoNLL) show AP having high precision, yet the confidence-based co-decoding performs better. Is the difference due to the tagset size (50+ vs. 4)? Or the specific P/R trade-offs for those languages? A more thorough error analysis or discussion of this discrepancy would strengthen the paper's conclusions about when to apply each co-decoding strategy.

4.  **Reliance on Heuristics and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The data selection method relies on two manually tuned thresholds (q, n), and the rank-based co-decoding is a hard-coded heuristic. While empirically effective, the work could be strengthened by discussing the sensitivity to these parameters or exploring more principled, learnable combination methods instead of fixed rules. For instance, the thresholds for data selection were chosen to report the best numbers, but it's unclear how one would set these for a new language without a development set.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed architectural description of the "smoothing layer" in the NN2 model? Specifically, how are the prototype vectors parameterized and learned during training, and what is their dimensionality?

2.  You observe that the rank-based co-decoding excels on your in-house data, while the confidence-based scheme is superior for the CoNLL data. What is your hypothesis for this difference? Could it be related to the drastically different number of entity types, which might affect the reliability of confidence scores from the softmax output?

3.  The linear mapping for representation projection is a simple and effective choice. Did you experiment with or consider more advanced mapping techniques (e.g., using orthogonal constraints, or non-linear mappings) that have been shown to improve cross-lingual embedding alignment in other work?
