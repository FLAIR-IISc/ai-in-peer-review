### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where no labeled data is available in the target language. The authors propose two complementary approaches: (1) an improved annotation projection method that uses a novel heuristic to filter noisy training instances generated from aligned corpora, and (2) a representation projection method that transfers an NER model directly by learning a linear mapping from target-language word embeddings to source-language embeddings. The key insight is that these two methods have different error profiles, and the paper demonstrates that combining their outputs via a co-decoding scheme leads to significant performance gains.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A simple and highly effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** This method directly tackles the critical problem of noise from imperfect alignments, showing dramatic improvements (+14-20 F1 points) in low-alignment-quality scenarios. Its simplicity and effectiveness make it a valuable and practical contribution.
2.  **A strong, new state-of-the-art system for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.** By systematically developing two diverse projection methods and intelligently combining them, the authors establish a new SOTA on the standard CoNLL benchmark datasets. This provides a robust and high-performing baseline for future work in this area.
3.  **A thorough empirical investigation of different projection strategies.** The paper provides a valuable comparison between projecting at the label level (annotation projection) and at the feature level (representation projection), analyzing their respective strengths and weaknesses (e.g., precision/recall trade-offs) across seven languages and two distinct NER tasks (fine-grained and coarse-grained).

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed combined system consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets. The gains are particularly notable for German (+8.8 F1 over the best previous result). Achieving a new SOTA on a well-established benchmark is a major strength.

2.  **Novelty and Practicality of the Annotation Filtering Method:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a standout contribution. It is based on a clear and sensible intuition: annotations that appear frequently and consistently across a corpus are more likely to be correct. The method is simple to implement and shown to be extremely effective, especially when alignment quality is low. This is a practical technique that can be readily adopted by others working on annotation projection for any task, not just NER.

3.  **Insightful Combination of Complementary Models:** The paper doesn't just present two separate methods but shows that their true power lies in their combination. The analysis of the different characteristics of the annotation projection (high-precision) and representation projection (more balanced) systems is insightful. The design of the rank-based co-decoding scheme, which leverages this observation, is clever and empirically justified by the consistent improvements shown across almost all experiments.

4.  **Thorough and Extensive Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering seven target languages from different families. The use of both a fine-grained (>50 entity types) in-house dataset and the standard 4-type CoNLL benchmark demonstrates the robustness and versatility of the proposed methods. Including supervised baselines provides a clear context for the performance of the weakly supervised systems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility:** The paper suffers from a lack of key details that would be necessary to reproduce the results.
    *   The "smoothing layer" in the NN2 model is poorly explained. The description is opaque, and critical details about how the "prototype vectors" are learned and how many are used are missing. As this is a novel architectural component, it requires a much clearer explanation.
    *   Crucial hyperparameters for the neural models are omitted (e.g., hidden layer size, context window size).
    *   The nature of the "comparable corpora" used for annotation projection is not specified (domain, size, source), nor is the process for creating the bilingual dictionary for representation projection (e.g., data/tools used for the phrase table).

2.  **Limited Novelty of the Representation Projection Component:** The core technique for representation projection—learning a linear transformation between pre-trained monolingual embedding spaces—is a direct application of the method from Mikolov et al. (2013b). While its successful application to NER is a useful data point, this part of the work is more of an empirical contribution than a methodological one. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the unique challenges or adaptations needed for NER compared to other tasks where this technique has been applied.

3.  **Key Ablation Studies are on In-House Data Only:** The most compelling evidence for the data selection heuristic's effectiveness (Table 2) is presented exclusively on the private, in-house dataset. To strengthen the paper's claims and demonstrate the method's general utility, it would be highly beneficial to include an ablation study showing the impact of data selection on one of the public CoNLL datasets (e.g., German). As it stands, it is difficult for the community to verify or build upon this key result.

4.  **Inconsistent Performance of NN Architectures:** The NN1 and NN2 models show inconsistent relative performance: NN2 is superior on the fine-grained in-house data, while NN1 is better on the coarse-grained CoNLL data. The paper notes this but provides no analysis or hypothesis for why this might be the case. Is it due to the number of entity types, the domain, or something else? This lack of analysis leaves an important empirical result unexplained.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the architecture and training of the "smoothing layer" in NN2? Specifically, how are the prototype vectors initialized and updated during training, and how many prototypes are used? What is the intuition for why this layer would lead to more balanced precision and recall?
2.  In your CoNLL experiments, did you apply the data selection heuristic for the annotation projection (AP) system? Could you provide the performance of the AP system on the CoNLL development sets both with and without data selection? This would help confirm the general applicability of your heuristic on a standard benchmark.
3.  You observe that NN2 performs better on your in-house data while NN1 is better on CoNLL. Do you have a hypothesis for this discrepancy? Could it be related to the large difference in the number of entity types between the two datasets (>50 vs. 4)?
