### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) and two schemes for combining them. The first method, Annotation Projection (AP), projects NER labels from a source language (English) to a target language via word-aligned corpora. Its main novelty is a heuristic for selecting high-quality projected sentences based on entity-tag consistency and entity density. The second method, Representation Projection (RP), trains a neural NER model on source-language word embeddings and applies it directly to the target language by learning a linear mapping from the target to the source embedding space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems. The authors evaluate their methods on in-house datasets for four languages with a large tagset and on standard CoNLL datasets, showing that their combined system outperforms previous state-of-the-art approaches.

### **Contributions**

1.  **Effective System Combination:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that combining two methodologically distinct weakly supervised systems—one based on annotation projection and the other on representation projection—via simple co-decoding rules yields state-of-the-art performance in cross-lingual NER. The two systems provide complementary views (high precision from AP, better recall from RP) that are effectively leveraged.
2.  **Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** A secondary contribution is the simple yet effective heuristic for filtering noisy data in annotation projection. The paper shows this method provides substantial improvements, especially for language pairs with low-quality alignments, making the AP approach more robust.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The combined system achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard CoNLL benchmark, outperforming well-regarded prior work by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). This demonstrates a clear and significant advance in the field.
2.  **Comprehensive and Diverse Evaluation:** The methods are validated on two distinct types of datasets: in-house data with a fine-grained set of over 50 entity types and the standard CoNLL data with 4 types. The consistent effectiveness across these different settings suggests the approach is robust and broadly applicable.
3.  **Practical and Intuitive Methods:** The core ideas—filtering noisy projections and combining complementary systems—are intuitive and relatively simple to implement. The proposed data selection heuristic for AP, in particular, is a practical technique that could be readily adopted by others working with projected data.

### **Weaknesses**

My main concerns with this paper lie in the lack of methodological clarity, technical inaccuracies, and missed opportunities for deeper analysis. While the results are strong, the paper's description of its methods is often imprecise, making reproducibility difficult and undermining the scientific contribution.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail:** Several key components of the methodology are inadequately described.
    *   **Annotation Quality Score (Eq. 5):** The frequency table `T` and the calculation of the score `T(e, l)` are not formally defined. The text describes it as "the frequency T(e, l) that entity e is labeled with tag l," and Table 1 shows values that look like probabilities. Is `T(e, l)` simply the count of entity `e` being labeled as `l` divided by the total count for entity `e`? This must be explicitly stated.
    *   **Neural Network Architectures:** The descriptions of the NN1 and NN2 models are vague. For NN1, what is the context window size, and how are the context word embeddings combined (e.g., concatenation, averaging)? The "smoothing layer" in NN2 is a novel component that is explained opaquely as returning a "weighted average of these prototype vectors." How are these prototypes initialized and learned? How many are there? Without these crucial details, the model is not reproducible.
    *   **Embedding and Dictionary Hyperparameters:** The choice of 800 dimensions for target language embeddings (vs. 300 for English) is presented without justification. Was this value tuned? Similarly, the paper notes that dictionary sizes larger than 6K pairs "might harm the performance," a counter-intuitive and important claim that is presented without any empirical evidence or analysis.

2.  **Technical Inaccuracies and Inconsistencies:**
    *   **CRF Complexity:** The paper incorrectly characterizes the training complexity of CRFs. Section 2.1 states the training time of order-o CRFs grows exponentially (`O(M^{o+1})`) with the number of labels `M`. This is only true for fully connected, higher-order models. For a standard linear-chain CRF (first-order, `o=1`), which is what the authors use, the complexity per iteration of training is polynomial, typically scaling with `O(M^2)`. Footnote 2 repeats this inaccuracy, claiming training time "grows at least quadratically," which is closer but still imprecise. The justification for using MEMMs over CRFs is therefore based on a flawed premise.
    *   **Citation Style:** The citation style is inconsistent throughout the paper. For example, the introduction uses both `(Zitouni and Florian, 2008)` and `Täckström et al. (2012)`, corresponding to `\citep` and `\citet` respectively. This should be made uniform.
    *   **Footnote Clarity:** Footnote 1 provides a confusing description of IOB tagging, first using an undefined notation `l1` and then giving the standard BIO format. The first part is redundant and unclear.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis:** The paper reports many interesting results but fails to provide analysis or insight into *why* they occur.
    *   **Model Performance Differences:** NN2 outperforms NN1 on the in-house data, while the reverse is true for CoNLL data. Why? Is it related to the size of the tagset, the domain of the data, or some other factor? The authors do not offer any hypothesis.
    *   **Co-Decoding Scheme Differences:** Similarly, the rank-based co-decoding is best for the in-house data, while the confidence-based scheme is best for CoNLL. This is a key result of the paper, but no explanation is attempted. An analysis of the precision/recall trade-offs of the base models on each dataset would likely shed light on this and strengthen the paper's conclusions.
    *   **Data Selection Parameters:** The parameters `q` and `n` for the data selection heuristic are tuned, but only the best-performing combination is shown. A sensitivity analysis or ablation study showing how F1 score varies with these parameters would provide a much better understanding of their impact and the trade-offs involved (e.g., quality vs. quantity of training data).

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a formal definition for the frequency score `T(e, l)` used to calculate the annotation quality `q(y)` in Equation 5?
2.  Could you please provide a more detailed description of your NN2 architecture's "smoothing layer," including how the prototype vectors are trained, their dimensionality, and their total number?
3.  Could you justify the choice of 800 dimensions for target-language word embeddings? Was this a tuned hyperparameter, and if so, what was the tuning process?
4.  You claim that larger dictionaries (>6K pairs) can harm performance. Could you provide evidence or a more detailed explanation for this finding, as it runs contrary to common intuition?
