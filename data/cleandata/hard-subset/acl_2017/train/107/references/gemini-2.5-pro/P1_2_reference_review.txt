### Summary of the Paper

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate two complementary approaches. The first is an **Annotation Projection** (AP) method, which projects NER tags from a source language (English) to a target language via word alignments in a comparable corpus. The key novelty here is a heuristic data selection scheme that filters the projected data based on annotation consistency, significantly improving performance when alignments are noisy. The second approach is **Representation Projection** (RP), a direct model transfer method. An English NER model is trained on word embeddings, and for a target language, word embeddings are mapped into the English vector space using a learned linear transformation, allowing the English model to be applied directly. Finally, the paper introduces two **Co-Decoding** schemes (one rank-based, one confidence-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems. Experiments on both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL datasets show that the combined system consistently outperforms the individual components and achieves new state-of-the-art results compared to previous work.

### Main Contributions

In my view, the main contributions of this work, in order of importance, are:

1.  **A highly effective framework for combining complementary cross-lingual NER systems.** The paper demonstrates that by intelligently merging the outputs of a high-precision annotation projection system and a more balanced representation projection system, it's possible to achieve significant performance gains. The final combined system establishes a new and strong state-of-the-art on standard CoNLL benchmarks.
2.  **A simple and practical data selection method for annotation projection.** The proposed heuristic for filtering noisy projected annotations is intuitive and shown to be highly effective, especially for language pairs with poor word alignment quality. This technique makes the classic annotation projection approach much more robust and usable in real-world scenarios where perfectly parallel corpora are unavailable.
3.  **A thorough empirical study of projection-based methods for cross-lingual NER.** The paper provides a solid implementation and evaluation of two major paradigms for cross-lingual transfer (annotation vs. representation projection) across multiple languages and two very different NER tasks (fine-grained vs. coarse-grained), offering valuable insights into their respective strengths and weaknesses.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper's strongest point is its empirical results. The proposed co-decoding system convincingly outperforms prior work by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL test data (Table 5). This is a significant achievement and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
2.  **Synergistic Combination of Complementary Methods:** The authors correctly identify that their two base systems (AP and RP) have different performance profiles—AP tends towards high precision, while RP is more balanced. The motivation for combining them is clear, and the proposed co-decoding schemes are well-designed to exploit this. The consistent improvements from co-decoding across all languages and datasets highlight the strength of this combination strategy.
3.  **High-Impact Contribution to Annotation Projection:** The data selection scheme presented in Section 3.1 is a valuable contribution. Annotation projection is a well-known technique, but its performance is notoriously brittle and dependent on alignment quality. The proposed filtering method directly addresses this core weakness. The dramatic F1-score improvements shown in Table 2 for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), where alignment quality is low, effectively demonstrate the practical utility and impact of this contribution.
4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and on two distinct types of datasets: a proprietary, fine-grained dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard, coarse-grained CoNLL benchmark. This breadth adds significant weight to the paper's conclusions and shows the general applicability of the methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty in Representation Projection:** The representation projection approach is a straightforward application of the cross-lingual embedding mapping technique from Mikolov et al. (2013b). While its application to NER is useful, the method itself is not novel. The paper could be strengthened by providing more analysis on *why* this simple linear mapping is sufficient for a structured prediction task like NER, or by comparing it to more sophisticated non-linear mapping techniques. The NN2 architecture with its "smoothing layer" is also introduced without much justification or analysis.
2.  **Dependence on Substantial Language-Specific Resources:** While the method requires no *annotated NER data* in the target language, it still has significant resource requirements: large monolingual corpora for training embeddings, and comparable corpora with word alignments for both annotation projection and for creating the seed dictionary used in representation projection. The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the methods to the size or quality of these underlying resources, which limits the understanding of its applicability in truly low-resource scenarios.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis on Model and Co-decoding Behavior:** The experiments show that different model variants (NN1 vs. NN2) and different co-decoding schemes (confidence vs. rank-based) perform best on different datasets. However, the paper offers little insight into *why* this is the case. For instance, why does the NN2 architecture help on the fine-grained task but not on CoNLL? Why is rank-based co-decoding better for the in-house data? A deeper analysis would elevate the paper from a report of strong empirical results to a source of more generalizable knowledge.
4.  **Ambiguity in Hyperparameter Tuning for Data Selection:** The data selection scheme relies on two thresholds, `q` and `n`, which are tuned per language (Table 2). The paper does not specify how this tuning was performed. If a labeled development set was used for each target language, this would contradict the "no human annotation" premise of the work. Clarification on this process is essential for reproducibility and for assessing the practical deployment of the method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The data selection scheme for annotation projection requires tuning the thresholds `q` (quality) and `n` (entity number). Could you please clarify how these parameters were selected for each language in your experiments? Specifically, was any labeled data from the target language used for this tuning, and if so, how does that align with the weakly supervised setting?
2.  Your two neural network architectures, NN1 and NN2, exhibit different performance characteristics across the datasets (NN2 is superior on the in-house data, while NN1 is better on CoNLL). Could you offer more intuition or analysis as to why the smoothing layer in NN2 is beneficial for the fine-grained in-house task but appears to hinder performance on the coarse-grained CoNLL task?
3.  The optimal co-decoding scheme also changes with the dataset (rank-based for in-house, confidence-based for CoNLL). Do you have a hypothesis for this difference? Is it related to the different precision/recall profiles of the base models on these datasets, or perhaps to the fine-grained nature of the entity types in the in-house data?
