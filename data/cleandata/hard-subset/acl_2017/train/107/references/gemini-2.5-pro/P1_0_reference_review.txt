### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate two primary approaches. The first, **Annotation Projection (AP)**, leverages a source-language NER system and word alignments to create silver-standard training data in the target language. The key novelty here is a heuristic data selection scheme to filter out noisy projections based on entity-tag frequency statistics, which proves highly effective. The second approach, **Representation Projection (RP)**, is a direct model transfer method. It learns a linear mapping from a target language's word embedding space to a source language's (English) space, allowing a single English NER model to be applied directly to the projected target-language representations. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the complementary outputs of the AP and RP systems. Experiments on seven target languages across both fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL datasets show that the combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and highly effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** This method provides a principled way to handle the noise inherent in projection-based approaches, yielding substantial performance gains, particularly for language pairs with low-quality alignments. This is a significant practical contribution for anyone using this paradigm.
2.  **A strong and well-motivated combined system built on two distinct transfer paradigms.** The paper systematically investigates both data-level (AP) and model-level (RP) transfer, analyzes their complementary strengths (e.g., high precision vs. balanced P/R), and demonstrates that a simple co-decoding scheme can effectively merge them to create a new state-of-the-art system.
3.  **A thorough and convincing empirical evaluation across multiple languages and task settings.** By testing on seven languages, a fine-grained 50+ type NER task, and the standard CoNLL task, the authors demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of their methods, setting a strong new baseline for the field.

### Strengths

1.  **The data selection scheme is a clear highlight.** Annotation projection is a standard technique, but its performance is often crippled by noise from the source NER model and, more critically, from word alignments. The proposed heuristic directly addresses this weakness. The results in Table 2 are compelling evidence of its effectiveness: the F1 score for Korean more than doubles (18.7 to 38.7), and for Japanese, it improves by nearly 14 F1 points. This simple idea significantly increases the viability of annotation projection.
2.  **The dual-methodology approach is sound and leads to strong results.** The paper intelligently combines two orthogonal approaches: creating a new target-language model from projected data (AP) versus directly applying a source-language model to projected representations (RP). The analysis showing that AP trends towards high precision and RP towards better recall provides a strong justification for combining them. The proposed co-decoding schemes consistently outperform the individual components, demonstrating the value of this combined view.
3.  **The experimental setup is comprehensive and rigorous.** The authors evaluate their methods on a wide range of languages (Germanic, Romance, East Asian) and on two very different NER tasks. The use of a fine-grained, in-house dataset with over 50 entity types shows the methods are applicable beyond the standard 4-type CoNLL setting. Crucially, by comparing against strong, published SOTA systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on a standard benchmark and showing superior performance, the paper firmly establishes its contribution.
4.  **The paper is well-written and easy to follow.** The methods are described clearly, the motivation for each component is well-articulated, and the experimental results are presented in a way that supports the claims effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of guidance on hyperparameter selection in a true zero-shot setting.** The data selection scheme relies on thresholds `(q, n)`, and the overall system requires choosing between NN architectures (NN1/NN2) and co-decoding schemes (confidence/rank). The paper reports results with the best-performing settings, which appear to have been selected using target-language development data. This is problematic for a truly "no human annotation" setting. The paper would be significantly stronger if it discussed how these crucial hyperparameters and model choices could be made without access to labeled target-language data (e.g., via unsupervised metrics or heuristics).
2.  **The novelty of the representation projection component is limited.** The core idea of learning a linear map between monolingual word embedding spaces is a direct application of prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b), which has also been used for other cross-lingual transfer tasks. The neural models employed are standard feedforward networks. While its application to NER and combination with AP is effective, the contribution here is more empirical than methodological.
3.  **The analysis could be deeper.** The paper notes that different system variants (NN1 vs. NN2, confidence vs. rank co-decoding) perform best on different datasets but offers little insight into why. For instance, why does the NN2 smoothing layer help on the in-house data but hurt on CoNLL? Is this related to the number of entity types, data domains, or something else? A more in-depth analysis of these differences would provide more valuable takeaways for future work.
4.  **A potentially important baseline for data selection is missing.** The paper presents the data selection scheme as a hard filtering step. An alternative approach would be to use all the projected data but to weight each training instance by its quality score `q(y)`. Comparing the proposed filtering method against such a soft-weighting baseline would provide a more complete assessment of the best way to leverage the quality heuristic.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how the data selection thresholds `(q, n)` and the choices for the best co-decoding scheme were determined? If a target-language development set was used, how do you envision applying your method in a practical scenario where no such data exists?
2.  Do you have any hypotheses as to why the NN2 architecture with the smoothing layer improves performance on your in-house dataset but degrades it on the CoNLL dataset? Similarly, why does the rank-based co-decoding excel on the in-house data while the confidence-based one is generally better for CoNLL?
3.  Regarding the representation projection, have you considered the impact of the dictionary size on NER performance? You mention setting a threshold to get 5-6K word pairs because larger dictionaries "might harm the performance," which is an interesting and perhaps counter-intuitive finding. Could you elaborate on this?
