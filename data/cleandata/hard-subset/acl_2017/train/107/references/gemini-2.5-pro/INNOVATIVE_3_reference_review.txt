### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct approaches based on projection from a resource-rich source language (English). The first, an enhancement of the classic **annotation projection** method, introduces a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projected labels generated from comparable corpora. The second is a bold **representation projection** approach, where word embeddings from a target language are mapped into the source language's embedding space, allowing a single, source-language NER model to be applied directly to the target language without retraining. Finally, the paper demonstrates that these two methods provide complementary views and can be combined effectively through co-decoding, outperforming prior state-of-the-art systems on standard CoNLL benchmarks.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and elegant direct model transfer framework for NER via representation projection.** The idea of learning a simple mapping between embedding spaces and then applying a single, unchanged source-language model to the projected target language data is the most significant contribution. It presents a clean, conceptually powerful, and surprisingly effective paradigm for cross-lingual transfer that avoids the complexities of creating and re-training on noisy projected datasets.
2.  **A practical and effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** While annotation projection is a known technique, the proposed method for filtering the resulting noisy data is a valuable practical contribution. By scoring projected sentences based on entity label consistency, the method provides a simple way to dramatically improve performance, especially in low-resource or low-quality alignment scenarios.
3.  **A convincing demonstration that annotation and representation projection are complementary.** The paper successfully shows that combining a label-space projection method (AP) with a feature-space projection method (RP) yields substantial gains over either approach alone. This highlights a valuable insight for future work in cross-lingual transfer.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Boldness:** The standout strength of this paper is the representation projection approach. In a field where cross-lingual transfer often involves complex pipelines of annotation projection, retraining, or joint modeling, the proposal of a "train-once, project-and-apply" model is refreshingly bold. This direct model transfer is not just an incremental tweak but a different way of thinking about the problem. Its effectiveness suggests that semantic similarity captured in embedding spaces can be robustly transferred for structured prediction tasks like NER, which is a significant finding. I highly value this kind of conceptual leap.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Broad Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation across six different target languages and two very different NER schemas (fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL). The fact that their combined approach consistently outperforms strong, published baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) is compelling evidence of the methods' efficacy. This is not a paper with a cool idea that barely works; it's a paper with a cool idea that sets a new state-of-the-art.
3.  **Pragmatic and Impactful Engineering:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a perfect example of a simple heuristic that solves a critical problem. The massive F1 score improvements for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), where alignment quality was noted to be low, demonstrate the real-world utility of this technique. While not as theoretically deep as the representation projection idea, this contribution makes an existing technique far more viable and robust.
4.  **Excellent Synergy in Co-Decoding:** The authors correctly identify that their two proposed methods operate on fundamentally different principles—one manipulating the label space, the other the feature space. The observation that one (AP) tends to be high-precision and the other (RP) more balanced is astute, and the design of a rank-based co-decoding scheme that leverages this observation is clever. The resulting performance gains from the combination strongly validate the central thesis that these are complementary approaches.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be stronger. I view these as minor limitations that do not detract from the overall contribution, but they are worth noting.

1.  **Dated Base NER Architectures:** The paper utilizes relatively simple NER models (MEMMs and basic feedforward NNs) for its experiments. At the time of this work, more powerful architectures like BiLSTM-CRFs were already established as the state-of-the-art for monolingual NER. While the focus here is on the cross-lingual transfer mechanism, it leaves open the question of how these projection techniques would interact with stronger, more context-aware encoders. It's possible that the representation projection approach would be even more effective with a more powerful base model, but it's also possible that such models are more sensitive to projection errors. This is more a question of unexplored potential than a flaw in the current work.
2.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** The paper presents strong quantitative results but offers little in the way of qualitative error analysis. What kinds of errors does representation projection make? For instance, does the linear mapping struggle with syntactic divergences between languages, or with words that have no direct semantic equivalent? Understanding the failure modes of such a bold approach would be highly valuable for the community and for inspiring future work to address these specific shortcomings.
3.  **Reliance on Tuned Heuristics:** Both the data selection scheme and the choice of co-decoding method rely on thresholds and choices tuned on a development set. This is standard practice, but the mechanisms themselves (e.g., the quality score `q(y)`, the rank-based combination) are presented as effective heuristics rather than being derived from a more formal objective. This is a minor point, as the empirical success is clear, but it makes the framework feel slightly less integrated than it could be.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The choice to use a simpler feedforward neural network instead of a more contemporary BiLSTM-CRF is interesting. Was this primarily for efficiency, or did you find in preliminary experiments that the simpler architecture was more robust to the noise introduced by embedding projection?
2.  Regarding the data selection for annotation projection: the results show it's most effective for low-quality alignments (Japanese/Korean). Is this purely a de-noising tool, or do you hypothesize it could also be beneficial in high-quality alignment scenarios by, for example, filtering out sentences that are grammatical but non-literal translations, which might confuse a target-language model?
3.  For the representation projection, you note that out-of-vocabulary target words are mapped to English embeddings if the surface form exists in the English vocabulary. Could you provide a sense of how often this happens for the languages tested? This seems like a crucial backoff for handling code-switching and untranslated proper nouns, and understanding its impact would be insightful.
