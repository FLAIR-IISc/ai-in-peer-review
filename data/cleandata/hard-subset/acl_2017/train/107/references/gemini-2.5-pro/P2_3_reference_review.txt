### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose a comprehensive framework that leverages two distinct and complementary projection-based methods.

The primary contributions of this work, in my view, are:
1.  **A powerful combination framework:** The key strength is not just the individual methods but their effective combination. The paper presents two distinct views for cross-lingual NER: a data-level approach (annotation projection) and a model-level approach (representation projection). It demonstrates that these methods have different precision/recall characteristics and that combining them via a simple but effective co-decoding scheme leads to state-of-the-art results.
2.  **An effective heuristic for Annotation Projection:** The paper introduces a simple and intuitive data selection scheme to filter noisy data generated by annotation projection. This scheme, based on entity-label frequency and entity count, is shown to provide massive improvements (+14-20 F1 points) in low-quality alignment scenarios, making annotation projection a much more viable technique.
3.  **A thorough empirical evaluation:** The authors validate their methods extensively across seven language-dataset pairs, including four in-house languages with a fine-grained tagset (>50 types) and three standard CoNLL languages with a coarse-grained tagset. This robust evaluation demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed framework and establishes a new state-of-the-art on the CoNLL benchmark.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper's most significant strength is its impressive results. The combined system consistently outperforms the individual components across all tested languages. Crucially, it surpasses previously published state-of-the-art results from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the standard CoNLL test sets. The improvements are particularly notable for German, where their system is much closer to the supervised baseline. This provides a strong argument for acceptance.

2.  **Complementary and Well-Motivated Approaches:** The paper cleverly frames the problem around two distinct paradigms: projecting annotations versus projecting representations. The analysis that Annotation Projection (AP) tends to yield high precision and low recall, while Representation Projection (RP) is more balanced, is insightful. This observation provides a strong motivation for the co-decoding schemes, which are designed to leverage the strengths of both systems—using AP for high-confidence entities and RP to improve recall.

3.  **Practical and Impactful Data Selection Method:** The proposed heuristic for filtering projected annotations is simple, elegant, and highly effective. The intuition that consistent (high-frequency) entity-label pairings are more reliable is sound. The experimental results in Table 2 are compelling, showing that this simple filtering can turn a mediocre system (e.g., Korean F1 of 18.7) into a much more respectable one (F1 of 38.7). This is a valuable contribution for anyone working with noisy, projected data.

4.  **Robust and Generalizable Framework:** The proposed methods are evaluated on a wide variety of languages (Germanic, Romance, East Asian) and NER tagsets (fine-grained in-house, coarse-grained CoNLL). The consistent gains from co-decoding across these diverse settings suggest that the framework is robust and not overly tuned to a specific language family or task definition.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of Individual Components:** While the overall framework and its combination are novel and effective, the individual components are based on well-established ideas. Annotation projection is a classic technique. Representation projection via linear mapping of embedding spaces was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been applied to other cross-lingual tasks. The paper applies these known techniques to NER effectively but does not introduce fundamentally new modeling architectures or projection algorithms. The novelty lies more in the engineering and combination of these parts.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper presents strong results but would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* certain phenomena occur. For instance:
    *   Why does the NN2 architecture (with the smoothing layer) perform better on the in-house data, while NN1 is superior on the CoNLL data? Is this related to the fine-grained vs. coarse-grained nature of the tagsets? The description of the smoothing layer is also quite high-level, making it difficult to understand its precise function.
    *   The paper states that larger dictionaries (for training the embedding mapping) "might harm the performance," leading to the selection of 5-6K word pairs. This is a critical hyperparameter, and the lack of an ablation study or further explanation makes this choice seem arbitrary. Showing this effect empirically would strengthen the paper.
    *   The best co-decoding scheme also differs between datasets (rank-based for in-house, confidence-based for CoNLL). An analysis of why this might be the case would provide valuable insights for future work.

3.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** Some details necessary for full reproduction are missing or unclear. The in-house dataset is not public, which is understandable but a limitation. More importantly, the NN2 architecture's "smoothing layer" involving "prototype vectors" is not sufficiently described. How are these prototypes initialized and learned? Without this detail, a key component of the representation projection system is not reproducible.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more details on the NN2 architecture's "smoothing layer"? Specifically, what are the "prototype vectors," how many are used, and how are they learned during training? A clearer explanation would significantly improve the paper's reproducibility.

2.  You mention that dictionaries larger than 5-6K pairs hurt the performance of representation projection. This is a counter-intuitive finding. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Did you observe overfitting, or perhaps the inclusion of noisier, lower-frequency translations from the phrase table degraded the quality of the linear mapping?

3.  The optimal co-decoding scheme seems to depend on the dataset (rank-based for in-house, confidence-based for CoNLL). Do you have any hypotheses as to why? Could it be related to the number of entity types (over 50 vs. 4), which might make confidence scores less reliable and favor a simpler rank-based combination?
