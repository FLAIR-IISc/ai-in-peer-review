### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any annotated data. The authors propose and combine two distinct projection-based approaches: Annotation Projection (AP) and Representation Projection (RP). The AP method leverages aligned corpora to project NER tags from a high-resource source language (English) to a target language, introducing a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections. The RP method involves direct model transfer, where monolingual word embeddings from a target language are mapped into the source language's embedding space, allowing a single source-language NER model to be applied directly. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these complementary systems, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL benchmark datasets.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective combination of two complementary cross-lingual transfer paradigms (AP and RP).** The paper's strongest contribution is the insight that these two methods have different error profiles (AP being high-precision, RP more balanced) and that a simple, well-motivated combination (co-decoding) can significantly outperform either individual system and prior state-of-the-art methods.
2.  **A simple and highly effective data selection heuristic for Annotation Projection.** The proposed quality scoring mechanism, based on the consistency of projected labels for a given entity string, is an intuitive and practical method for mitigating noise from imperfect source NER and cross-lingual alignments. The dramatic improvements shown for Japanese (+13.9 F1) and Korean (+20.0 F1) highlight its real-world value in low-quality alignment scenarios.
3.  **A solid application and evaluation of representation projection for NER.** While the core technique of learning linear mappings between embedding spaces is not new, its successful application to the NER task and its role as a complementary "view" in the co-decoding framework is a valuable contribution to the field.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed combined system demonstrates compelling performance, outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the standard CoNLL test sets for Spanish, Dutch, and German. This is a clear and significant result that pushes the boundaries of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.
2.  **Excellent System Design and Synergy:** The paper successfully identifies and exploits the complementary nature of the AP and RP approaches. The observation that AP yields high precision but low recall, while RP is more balanced, provides a strong motivation for their combination. The rank-based co-decoding scheme, in particular, is a clever and effective way to leverage this observation by prioritizing the high-confidence predictions of the AP system while using the RP system to fill in the gaps.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation across Diverse Datasets:** The authors evaluate their methods on two very different types of data: a fine-grained in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard 4-type CoNLL benchmark. This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of their framework across different label schemas and domains, strengthening the paper's conclusions.
4.  **Practical and Intuitive Heuristics:** The data selection scheme for AP is a standout example of a simple, elegant idea with a significant practical impact. It requires no complex modeling and is based on a sound intuition—that consistent projections across a corpus are more reliable. This makes the method easy to understand, implement, and likely adopt by other researchers and practitioners.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty in Core Components:** The primary weakness is that the core techniques for both AP and RP are adaptations of existing work. Annotation projection itself is a classic approach, and representation projection via linear mapping of word embeddings was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been applied to other NLP tasks. The novelty lies in the specific data selection heuristic for AP and the effective combination of the two methods for NER, making this more of a strong systems/engineering paper than one introducing a fundamentally new algorithm.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Ablation:** The paper presents strong results but would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* certain choices work. For instance:
    *   The NN2 architecture with its "smoothing layer" outperforms NN1 on the in-house data but underperforms on CoNLL. The mechanism and intuition behind this layer are not fully explained, nor is the reason for its differential performance.
    *   The choice between confidence-based and rank-based co-decoding is made based on dev set performance. A deeper analysis correlating this choice with the precision/recall characteristics of the base models for each language/dataset would provide more generalizable insights.
    *   The justification for using MEMMs for AP (training time) and NNs for RP is practical but creates an architectural inconsistency. A comparison using the same base model (e.g., NN) for the target-language AP system would have made the comparison between AP and RP more direct.
3.  **Reproducibility Challenges:** While the use of CoNLL data is commendable, key resources for reproducing the results are not fully specified or available. The in-house dataset is private, the comparable corpora used for AP are not detailed, and the source of the phrase table for extracting the RP dictionary is not mentioned. This makes it difficult for others to fully replicate the experiments or build directly upon this work.
4.  **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a qualitative error analysis. What kinds of errors do the AP and RP systems make, respectively? Which errors does the co-decoding successfully correct, and which ones persist? Such an analysis would provide valuable insights into the remaining challenges for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The finding that RP dictionaries of 5K-6K pairs are optimal and that larger dictionaries can degrade performance is counter-intuitive. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible that larger dictionaries extracted from phrase tables introduce noisier or more ambiguous 1-to-many translations that perturb the linear mapping? Have you analyzed the quality of the dictionary pairs as its size increases?
2.  Could you provide more intuition behind the NN2 "smoothing layer"? It is described as computing a weighted average of prototype vectors based on cosine similarity. How are these prototypes learned, and what do they represent? Why would this mechanism be more effective for a fine-grained 50-type classification problem than for a 4-type one?
3.  Your AP data selection scheme is highly effective for languages with low-quality alignment (Japanese, Korean) but provides marginal gains for those with high-quality alignment (German, Portuguese). This suggests the alignment quality is a dominant factor. Did you consider incorporating alignment confidence scores directly into your sentence quality metric `q(y)` as an additional signal?
