### Paper Summary

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without annotated data. The authors propose two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, **Annotation Projection (AP)**, is a classic method that projects labels from a source language (English) to a target language via aligned corpora. The main contribution here is a novel and effective heuristic data selection scheme to filter the noisy projected annotations based on entity-label frequency counts, which yields significant improvements, especially when alignment quality is low.

The second and more novel approach is **Representation Projection (RP)**. Instead of projecting annotations, this method projects the word representations themselves. An NER model is trained once on source language word embeddings. At inference time, target language word embeddings are projected into the source language's vector space via a learned linear mapping. This allows the single source-language model to be directly applied to any target language without retraining, creating a universal NER system.

Finally, the paper observes that these two approaches have complementary error profiles (AP is high-precision, RP is more balanced) and proposes two co-decoding schemes to combine their outputs. The combined system is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods on CoNLL benchmark datasets.

### Contributions

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The proposal of Representation Projection for direct NER model transfer.** This is a conceptually elegant and bold approach that reframes the cross-lingual transfer problem from one of training new models on projected data to one of mapping new languages into a universal semantic space. The idea of a single, fixed NER model that can process multiple languages by simply projecting their inputs is a significant new direction for this task.
2.  **A simple yet remarkably effective data selection heuristic for Annotation Projection.** While the filtering method itself is a heuristic, its huge empirical impact (e.g., +20 F1 for Korean) demonstrates a crucial insight into managing noise in projection-based methods and turns a classic but often brittle approach into a strong baseline.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical study** demonstrating that these two distinct projection methods are complementary and that their intelligent combination leads to new state-of-the-art results in weakly-supervised cross-lingual NER.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance of Representation Projection:** The core strength of this paper is the Representation Projection idea. It is a bold departure from the standard "project-then-retrain" pipeline of annotation projection. The approach is simple, elegant, and efficient, as it requires only one NER model to be trained. This is exactly the kind of high-level conceptual shift that can open up new research directions, focusing future work on learning better universal representation spaces rather than just better target-language models from noisy data.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed methods, particularly the final combined system, achieve significant improvements over established state-of-the-art baselines on the standard CoNLL datasets. The gains are not minor or incremental; for German, the F1 score jumps from 57.5 (previous SOTA) to 64.2. This provides strong evidence that the ideas presented are not just intellectually interesting but also practically highly effective. The experiments are also thorough, covering multiple language pairs and two different types of datasets (fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL).
3.  **Intelligent Combination of Complementary Methods:** The paper does not simply present two independent methods. It analyzes their distinct characteristics (AP's high precision, RP's better recall) and uses this insight to design a motivated co-decoding scheme. The rank-based combination, which prioritizes high-confidence AP outputs and uses RP to fill in the gaps, is a clever way to leverage the best of both worlds. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and the proposed solutions.

### Weaknesses

While I am very positive about this paper, there are areas that could be stronger. I view these as minor flaws that do not detract from the significance of the core contributions.

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Representation Projection Method:** The paper introduces the exciting RP approach but stops short of providing a deeper analysis. For instance, what is the impact of the dictionary size and quality on the learned projection matrix and, consequently, on NER performance? What kinds of entities or linguistic structures transfer well versus poorly? A more in-depth error analysis or a qualitative investigation of the projected space could have provided valuable insights into the mechanisms and limitations of this new approach.
2.  **Heuristic Nature of Key Components:** Both the data selection scheme for AP and the rank-based co-decoding are effective but highly heuristic. The thresholds `(q, n)` for data selection are tuned on a per-language basis, and the ranking logic for co-decoding is based on an empirical observation. While I value ideas that work over those that are merely theoretically sound, the paper would be stronger if it provided more justification or sensitivity analysis for these design choices, moving them from "effective tricks" towards more generalizable principles.
3.  **Novelty of Individual Components is Debatable:** While the *application* to NER is novel and impactful, the foundational ideas are not entirely new. Annotation projection is a classic technique, and learning linear maps between word embedding spaces has been proposed before (as the authors cite). The paper's primary novelty lies in the synthesis of these ideas and the specific application to NER. A critic could argue the work is more of an extremely well-executed application of existing techniques rather than a fundamental breakthrough. However, I believe the direct transfer approach via RP is a significant enough conceptual leap for the NER task to be considered a major contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The finding that dictionaries larger than 5-6K word pairs harmed the performance of representation projection is quite counter-intuitive. Do you have a hypothesis for why this occurred? Could it be that larger dictionaries, extracted automatically from phrase tables, begin to include noisier or lower-quality translation pairs that corrupt the learned linear mapping?
2.  Could you provide any qualitative examples for the representation projection method? For example, it would be insightful to see a target-language sentence and visualize where its entities' projected embeddings land in the source (English) embedding space, perhaps relative to their English counterparts. This could help build intuition for how the direct transfer is succeeding (or failing).
