### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), designed for scenarios where no annotated data exists in the target language. The first approach, based on **Annotation Projection (AP)**, projects NER labels from a source language (English) to a target language using aligned comparable corpora. The key novelty here is a heuristic data selection scheme that filters the projected data based on a quality score, aiming to mitigate noise from imperfect alignments. The second approach, **Representation Projection (RP)**, is a direct model transfer method. It trains a neural NER model on the source language using only word embeddings as features. This model is then directly applied to the target language by first projecting the target language's word embeddings into the source language's embedding space via a learned linear transformation. Finally, the paper proposes two **co-decoding** schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, leveraging their complementary strengths. The methods are evaluated on both in-house datasets with fine-grained entity types (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and standard CoNLL benchmark datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German), demonstrating that the combined system outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods.

### Contributions
1.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Projection Methods:** The primary contribution is the successful combination of two distinct paradigms for cross-lingual transfer—data-level annotation projection and model-level representation projection. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that these two approaches produce complementary outputs (e.g., one being high-precision, the other more balanced) and that a simple yet effective co-decoding scheme can leverage this to achieve significant performance gains over either individual system and prior state-of-the-art baselines.
2.  **A Simple and Effective Data Selection Heuristic for Annotation Projection:** The paper introduces a practical data selection method to improve the quality of training data generated via annotation projection from noisy comparable corpora. By scoring and filtering projected sentences based on entity-label consistency and entity density, the method yields substantial improvements, particularly for language pairs with low-quality word alignments.
3.  **Systematic Application and Evaluation of Representation Projection for NER:** The paper presents a direct model transfer approach for NER based on projecting word embeddings. While cross-lingual embedding projection is an existing technique, this work provides a thorough implementation and evaluation specifically for the NER task across a diverse set of languages, establishing it as a strong standalone baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Results on Standard Benchmarks:** The proposed combined system achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets. As shown in Table 5, the system outperforms the strong baselines of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on all three reported languages (Spanish, Dutch, and German), with a particularly large margin on German. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
2.  **Comprehensive and Diverse Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, covering seven target languages from different language families and two distinct evaluation settings: an in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types and the public CoNLL datasets with 4 coarse types. This extensive evaluation demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the proposed framework across different domains and annotation granularities.
3.  **Well-Motivated Combination of Two Different Views:** The paper effectively frames the AP and RP methods as providing two independent "views" of the data. The analysis of their distinct precision/recall profiles is insightful and provides a clear motivation for the co-decoding schemes. The rank-based co-decoding method, which prioritizes the high-precision output of the AP system, is a simple but well-reasoned strategy that proves effective, especially on the in-house data (Table 3).
4.  **Demonstrated Utility of the Data Selection Scheme:** The proposed heuristic for filtering projected annotations is shown to be highly effective. In Table 2, the F1 score for Korean improves from 18.7 to 38.7 (+20.0 points) and for Japanese from 41.1 to 55.0 (+13.9 points). The paper correctly correlates this improvement with the lower alignment quality for these languages, demonstrating that the method successfully addresses a key challenge in annotation projection.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination and empirical results are strong, the core technical components are adaptations of existing work. Annotation projection is a standard technique; the data selection heuristic, while effective, is a straightforward frequency-based filter. Similarly, representation projection via a linear mapping on pre-trained embeddings was introduced by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been applied to other cross-lingual tasks. The paper's novelty lies more in the careful engineering and combination of these parts for NER rather than in foundational methodological advances.
2.  **Confounding Model Architectures:** The Annotation Projection (AP) approach uses a 2nd-order MEMM, while the Representation Projection (RP) approach uses a feedforward neural network. This choice is justified by the practical concern of CRF/MEMM training time on large projected datasets. However, it introduces a confounding variable, making it difficult to determine whether the observed differences in performance and precision/recall profiles between the two systems are due to the projection method (AP vs. RP) or the underlying sequence model (MEMM vs. NN). A cleaner comparison would have used the same model architecture for both approaches.
3.  **Insufficient Details on the Representation Projection Model:** The source NER model for the RP approach is trained using only word embeddings. The paper states this is to ensure features are transferable, but this likely makes for a weaker source model compared to state-of-the-art English NER systems that use character embeddings and other features. The performance of this source model on a standard English benchmark is not reported, making it difficult to assess how much of the performance gap between the weakly supervised and fully supervised systems is due to the transfer process versus the limitations of the source model itself. Additionally, the description of the NN2 architecture's "smoothing layer" and its "prototype vectors" is brief and could benefit from more detail.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The comparison between the Annotation Projection (AP) and Representation Projection (RP) systems is confounded by the use of different underlying models (MEMM vs. NN). Could you comment on how you think this choice might have influenced the distinct precision/recall profiles observed for each system? Have you considered an experiment, perhaps on a smaller dataset, where the AP system also uses the same NN architecture to provide a more direct comparison of the projection methodologies?
2.  The performance of the Representation Projection approach is fundamentally capped by the quality of the source English NER model. To better understand the limitations of the transfer, could you report the performance of your English neural network models (NN1 and NN2, which use only word embeddings) on the standard CoNLL-2003 English test set?
3.  The data selection heuristic for AP is very effective. Can you provide more qualitative analysis of the data it filters out? For instance, does the filter primarily discard sentences with clear word alignment errors, or is it more effective at identifying and removing cases where the source English NER model made an error that was then correctly projected?
