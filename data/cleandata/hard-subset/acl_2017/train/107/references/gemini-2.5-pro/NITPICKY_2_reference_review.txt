### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) where no annotated data is available in the target language. The first method, based on annotation projection (AP), introduces a heuristic data selection scheme to filter high-quality training instances from noisy labels projected across a comparable corpus. The second method, based on representation projection (RP), learns a linear mapping from a target language's word embedding space to the source language's space, allowing a source NER model to be applied directly to the projected target language representations. Finally, the authors propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, leveraging their complementary strengths. The combined approach is evaluated on seven languages across in-house and CoNLL datasets, outperforming previous state-of-the-art systems.

**Contributions**

1.  **Empirical Demonstration of Heuristic Filtering:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that a simple, heuristic-based data selection scheme can substantially improve the performance of annotation projection, particularly in settings with low-quality word alignments.
2.  **Effective Combination of Projection Methods:** The work systematically evaluates the combination of two orthogonal cross-lingual transfer paradigms (annotation projection and representation projection). It shows that their outputs can be combined via simple co-decoding schemes to achieve results superior to either individual system.
3.  **New State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed combined system establishes a new state-of-the-art for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER on the CoNLL 2002/2003 benchmarks, providing a strong new baseline for future work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors evaluate their methods on seven target languages, using both a proprietary dataset with a large, fine-grained tagset (>50 types) and the standard CoNLL benchmark datasets. This extensive evaluation provides convincing evidence for the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed techniques.
2.  **Significant Performance Gains from Data Selection:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection, while simple, yields impressive results. The reported +20 F1 point improvement for Korean demonstrates that filtering noisy projected data is a critical step, a detail that is often overlooked.
3.  **Well-Motivated System Combination:** The motivation for combining the two projection systems is sound. The paper correctly identifies that the systems have different characteristics (AP being high-precision, RP being more balanced) and leverages this observation in the design of the rank-based co-decoding scheme. The consistently positive results from co-decoding across all languages validate this approach.

**Weaknesses**

My recommendation is borderline, leaning towards rejection. While the paper presents strong empirical results, it is marred by a substantial number of methodological gaps, under-justified heuristics, and a lack of technical depth that undermine its contributions and reproducibility.

1.  **Over-reliance on Heuristics and Lack of Principled Justification:** The core methodological contributions are presented as simple heuristics with minimal theoretical or empirical justification beyond the final results.
    *   **Data Selection:** The data selection scheme `q(y) ≥ q; n(y) ≥ n` is entirely heuristic. The paper provides no details on how the optimal thresholds `q` and `n` were determined. Table 2 presents only the single best configuration for each language, which suggests post-hoc selection rather than a reproducible tuning methodology.
    *   **Representation Projection:** The choice of embedding dimensions (300 for English, 800 for target languages) is adopted from prior work without justification for its suitability to NER. The claim that dictionaries larger than 6K pairs "might harm the performance" is a significant and counter-intuitive finding that is stated without any supporting evidence or analysis.
    *   **Co-Decoding:** The co-decoding schemes are simplistic. The rank-based method is brittle, as it relies on a specific precision/recall profile that may not hold in other domains or language pairs. The paper fails to explore or compare against more principled ensemble methods from the literature.

2.  **Superficial Description of Models and Missing Experimental Details:** The paper lacks crucial details required for reproducibility and for assessing the validity of the experimental design.
    *   **Model Architectures:** The NER models are described at a very high level. The AP approach uses a "2nd-order MEMM," justified by a questionable claim in footnote 2 regarding CRF training complexity (linear-chain CRF training scales as `O(M^2)`, not `O(M^3)`). The RP approach uses simple feedforward networks (NN1, NN2), which are outdated compared to modern BiLSTM-CRF architectures. Key hyperparameters for these neural models (e.g., context window size, hidden layer dimensions, optimizer) are completely omitted.
    *   **Inconsistent Model Specification:** The description of the source English NER model is confusing. It is described as a CRF in the context of AP (Section 3.2) and a neural network for RP (Section 4.3). This critical detail—that two different source models are used—is not stated clearly upfront and is only discernible upon careful reading of the experiments section.
    *   **Handling of Unknown Words:** The procedure for handling OOV words in Section 4.3 is vague. The paper mentions an "NER-trained unk vector" but provides no information on how this vector is obtained, which is a critical detail in any embedding-based model.

3.  **Minor but Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper exhibits a lack of polish that detracts from its overall quality.
    *   **Formatting:** Equation (2) uses a non-standard `Σ`. The weight function `( 1dist(x,xc) )` in Section 4.1 is poorly formatted.
    *   **Clarity:** Key definitions are sometimes buried in footnotes (e.g., the definition of "conflict" in footnote 3). Entity types like "TITLEWORK" in Table 1 are non-standard and unexplained.
    *   **Analysis:** The paper notes that its method significantly outperforms prior work on German (Table 5) but offers no insight or analysis as to why this might be the case.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Please detail the protocol for tuning the data selection thresholds `(q, n)`. Was a development set used for this? If so, how was a reliable development set created from the noisy, automatically projected data?
2.  Could you justify the use of a 2nd-order MEMM for the target-language AP model over a standard linear-chain CRF or a more powerful neural model (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF)?
3.  Please provide evidence for your claim that word-translation dictionaries larger than 6K pairs harm the performance of representation projection. This is a surprising result that requires substantiation.
4.  For reproducibility, please provide the key architectural and training hyperparameters for your NN1 and NN2 models, including context window size, hidden layer size, activation functions, optimizer, learning rate, and batch size.
