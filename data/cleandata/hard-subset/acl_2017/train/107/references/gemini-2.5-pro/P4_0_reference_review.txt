### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that does not require any annotated data in the target language. The work combines two distinct projection-based methods. The first, an "Annotation Projection" (AP) method, projects NER labels from a source language (English) to a target language using aligned comparable corpora. A key element of this method is a novel heuristic data selection scheme designed to filter out noisy projections by scoring sentences based on the consistency of projected entity labels. The second, a "Representation Projection" (RP) method, performs direct model transfer. It trains a source-language NER model on word embeddings and applies it to the target language by first projecting the target language word embeddings into the source language's vector space via a learned linear mapping. Finally, the paper introduces two "co-decoding" schemes (one confidence-based, one rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, leveraging their complementary strengths to produce a more accurate final prediction. The combined system is evaluated on both in-house, fine-grained NER datasets and standard CoNLL datasets, demonstrating superior performance over previous state-of-the-art weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A strong ensemble system for cross-lingual NER**: The primary contribution is the effective combination of two orthogonal projection methods—one operating in the label space (Annotation Projection) and the other in the feature/representation space (Representation Projection). The proposed co-decoding schemes successfully leverage the different error profiles of the two systems (e.g., high-precision AP and balanced P/R RP) to achieve state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.
2.  **An effective data selection heuristic for Annotation Projection**: The paper introduces a simple yet highly effective heuristic for filtering noisy training instances generated by annotation projection. This method, which scores sentences based on label consistency and entity density, provides substantial improvements, particularly when the underlying corpus alignment quality is low.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The work provides a thorough evaluation across seven target languages from two distinct data sources (in-house and CoNLL), covering both fine-grained (50+ types) and coarse-grained (4 types) NER tasks. This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the proposed methods.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Results**: The paper presents compelling empirical results. The proposed combined system consistently outperforms the individual projection components across all evaluated languages. More importantly, as shown in Table 5, the final system surpasses two relevant state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the standard CoNLL test sets for Spanish, Dutch, and German. This establishes a new, strong baseline for the task.
2.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Approaches**: The motivation for combining the two projection methods is well-founded and validated by the results. The paper observes that the Annotation Projection (AP) system tends to yield high precision but lower recall, while the Representation Projection (RP) system is more balanced. This complementary nature is exploited by the co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based one, which systematically improves F1 scores by significant margins (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house data, Table 3). This demonstrates a principled way to build a more robust system from weaker, diverse components.
3.  **Practical and Impactful Data Selection Method**: The data selection scheme for AP is a valuable contribution. As shown in Table 2, it leads to dramatic F1 score improvements for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), languages for which the alignment quality was noted to be lower. This simple heuristic effectively mitigates a major bottleneck in projection-based methods—noise from imperfect alignments—making the approach more practical and reliable in real-world scenarios where high-quality parallel data may not be available.
4.  **Thorough and Clear Experimental Setup**: The experiments are well-designed and clearly presented. The evaluation on both a fine-grained, multi-type in-house dataset and the standard 4-type CoNLL dataset demonstrates the method's versatility. The paper is transparent about the models used (CRF, MEMM, NN), the data sizes, and the parameter tuning process (using development sets), which enhances the work's reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components**: While the combination is effective, the two core projection methods are based on established techniques. Annotation projection is a standard paradigm in cross-lingual transfer, and the representation projection approach is a direct application of the cross-lingual embedding mapping technique from Mikolov et al. (2013b), which has been previously applied to other NLP tasks. The main novelty resides in the data selection heuristic and the specific co-decoding schemes.
2.  **Reliance on Heuristics and Development Data**: The framework relies on several heuristics that require tuning on a target-language development set. This includes the quality (`q`) and entity number (`n`) thresholds for data selection, and the choice between the two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based vs. rank-based). This reliance on a labeled dev set somewhat undermines the premise of a fully zero-shot setting where no target language annotation of any kind is available. The paper does not discuss how these parameters would be set in a truly zero-shot scenario.
3.  **Lack of Analysis for Neural Network Architectures**: The paper introduces two neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) for the representation projection system, but the description of NN2's "smoothing layer" is brief and lacks technical detail. It is unclear what the "prototype vectors" are, how they are learned, or why this architecture would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper reports that NN2 is better on the in-house data while NN1 is superior on the CoNLL data, but provides no analysis or intuition for this performance difference. This makes the choice of architecture seem arbitrary and reduces the clarity of the RP component.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The data selection and co-decoding methods rely on parameters and choices tuned on a target-language development set. How would you recommend applying your framework in a true zero-shot scenario where no labeled data (not even a dev set) is available for the target language? Are there ways to set the data selection thresholds (`q`, `n`) or choose the co-decoding scheme in an unsupervised manner?
2.  Could you please provide more technical details about the "smoothing layer" in the NN2 architecture? Specifically, what are the "prototype vectors," how are they initialized and learned, and what is the intuition behind this architectural choice? Furthermore, do you have any hypothesis for why NN2 performs better on the fine-grained in-house data while the simpler NN1 performs better on the coarse-grained CoNLL data?
3.  In Section 4.2, you mention that for representation projection, dictionaries of 5K-6K word pairs were used and that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance." This is a counter-intuitive finding. Could you elaborate on this? Did you investigate why this might be the case? Was this phenomenon consistent across languages?
