### Summary

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose two main strategies: 1) an Annotation Projection (AP) approach that uses a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy labels projected from a source language (English) over comparable corpora, and 2) a Representation Projection (RP) approach that performs direct model transfer by mapping target language word embeddings into the source language's vector space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of these two complementary systems. The combined system is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2002/2003 benchmark datasets for Spanish, Dutch, and German, outperforming previous methods.

### Contributions

1.  **An Effective Data Selection Scheme for Annotation Projection:** The primary contribution is the simple yet highly effective heuristic for selecting high-quality training instances from noisily projected annotations. By scoring sentences based on the label consistency of their entities (quality score `q`) and the number of entities they contain (information score `n`), the method successfully filters out poor-quality data. The experimental results, particularly for Japanese and Korean where alignment quality is low, show dramatic improvements (+13.9 and +20.0 F1 respectively), demonstrating this is a practical and impactful solution to a key problem in annotation projection.

2.  **A Strong System Combination Framework:** The paper provides a well-executed case study on combining two distinct and complementary paradigms for cross-lingual transfer (annotation vs. representation projection). The authors empirically analyze the different precision/recall characteristics of each system and design a simple, rank-based co-decoding scheme that leverages these differences to achieve significant gains over either individual system.

3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper establishes a new state-of-the-art for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER on the standard CoNLL benchmarks. This provides a strong new baseline for the field and validates the effectiveness of the proposed combination of methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Practical and Impactful Data Selection Method:** The proposed data selection scheme in Section 3.1 is a major strength. Annotation projection is often hampered by noise from both the source NER model and the word alignment process. This method directly addresses the alignment noise by leveraging a simple, intuitive signal: consistent projection errors are less likely than consistent correct projections. The massive F1 gains reported in Table 2 for languages with poor alignment accuracy strongly validate the method's real-world utility.

2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough. The authors evaluate their methods on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and across two different types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house dataset and a standard public benchmark (CoNLL). This demonstrates the generalizability of their findings. The comparison against strong prior work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and the achievement of SOTA results solidify the paper's contributions.

3.  **Insightful Combination of Complementary Models:** The paper doesn't just present two separate approaches but successfully demonstrates their synergy. The analysis that the AP system tends toward high precision/low recall while the RP system is more balanced is insightful. This observation directly motivates the rank-based co-decoding scheme, which is shown to be highly effective, improving F1 scores by up to 6.2 points on the in-house data. This highlights the value of building and combining diverse systems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The methodology for each component (AP, RP, Co-Decoding) is explained logically, and the experimental results are presented clearly, making the paper's claims easy to verify.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Representation Projection Component:** The representation projection approach is a direct application of existing techniques (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013b) for learning cross-lingual embedding mappings. While its application to NER is valid and well-executed, the method itself is not a novel contribution of this work. The paper's strength lies more in the AP data selection and the combination framework rather than this individual component.

2.  **Lack of Analysis on Data Selection Hyperparameters:** The data selection scheme relies on two key hyperparameters, the quality threshold `q` and the entity count threshold `n`. Table 2 reports results for the best-performing settings, but there is no analysis of how these parameters were tuned or how sensitive the model's performance is to their values. A sensitivity analysis or a plot showing the trade-off between the percentage of data kept and the final F1 score would significantly strengthen this contribution by demonstrating its robustness.

3.  **Simplicity of Co-Decoding Schemes and NER Models:** The co-decoding schemes are effective but are simple heuristics. The rank-based method, for instance, is a fixed rule based on an observed P/R trend. More sophisticated ensemble methods could potentially yield further improvements. Similarly, the neural NER models (NN1/NN2) are relatively simple feed-forward networks. While sufficient for the paper's claims, using more powerful, modern architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) as the source-language model could potentially lead to different outcomes or stronger overall results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the data selection scheme in Section 3.1: Could you elaborate on the process used to tune the thresholds `q` and `n` for each language? Was a target-language development set used, and if so, does this not violate the "no human annotation in the target language" premise? If not, how were these values chosen? Providing a sensitivity analysis for these parameters would be very helpful.

2.  The representation projection method relies on projecting target embeddings into a fixed English space. Have you considered the alternative of training a BiLSTM-based NER model on top of multilingual embeddings (e.g., MUSE, LASER) where languages are already mapped to a shared space? How do you think this would compare to your projection-based approach?

3.  The rank-based co-decoding scheme gives priority to the AP system based on the observation that it has higher precision. Did this characteristic hold true for all languages and datasets? For the CoNLL data (Table 4), the precision of AP and NN1 are quite similar for Spanish, yet co-decoding still provides a benefit. Could you comment on whether a more dynamic or learned combination method might be more robust than the fixed-rank heuristic?
