### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and combine two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, **Annotation Projection (AP)**, uses a source-language NER system to label a comparable corpus, projects these labels onto the target language via word alignments, and then trains a target-language NER model. The key novelty here is a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections based on label consistency and entity density, which proves highly effective for low-quality alignments. The second approach, **Representation Projection (RP)**, is a direct model transfer method. It trains a source-language neural NER model on word embeddings, learns a linear mapping from target-language word embeddings to the source space, and then applies the source model directly to the projected target-language embeddings at test time. Finally, the paper demonstrates that these two approaches are complementary and combines their outputs using two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based). Experiments on both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL dataset show that the combined system outperforms each individual component and achieves new state-of-the-art results compared to previous work.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A highly effective combination of two complementary cross-lingual transfer paradigms.** The paper’s strongest contribution is the successful integration of annotation projection (a data-centric approach that creates target-language training data) and representation projection (a model-centric, zero-shot approach). The authors empirically show that these methods have different precision/recall characteristics and that a simple, well-motivated co-decoding scheme can leverage their respective strengths to achieve significant performance gains over either method alone.
2.  **A simple and effective heuristic for filtering noisy projected annotations.** The proposed data selection scheme for the annotation projection method is a significant practical contribution. By filtering projected sentences based on a quality score derived from label frequency and an entity count threshold, the authors demonstrate dramatic improvements (up to +20 F1 points) in scenarios with noisy word alignments. This addresses a critical bottleneck in traditional annotation projection.
3.  **Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse languages and datasets.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on seven target languages across two very different settings: a proprietary dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types and the standard 4-type CoNLL benchmark. The fact that their method consistently outperforms strong baselines in both settings underscores its robustness and general applicability.

### Strengths
1.  **Sound and well-motivated methodology.** The core idea of combining two different "views" of the cross-lingual transfer problem is excellent. Annotation projection relies on local, token-level alignment evidence, while representation projection relies on a global, semantic vector space alignment. These are fundamentally different sources of information, and the paper rightly identifies their complementary nature (e.g., AP often being high-precision, RP having more balanced P/R). The rank-based co-decoding scheme is a clever way to exploit this observation.
2.  **Strong and convincing empirical results.** The experimental results are a major strength. The proposed data selection scheme for AP yields massive improvements for Japanese and Korean (Table 2), where alignment quality is low, providing clear evidence of its utility. Furthermore, the final combined system sets a new state-of-the-art on the standard CoNLL benchmark (Table 5), outperforming well-regarded previous work by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016).
3.  **Clarity and organization.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of explaining the two projection approaches, motivating their combination, and presenting the results in a comprehensible manner.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of analysis of the data selection hyperparameters.** The data selection scheme for annotation projection introduces two key hyperparameters: the quality score threshold `q` and the entity number threshold `n`. The paper simply states that they "tried different threshold parameters... and reported the best numbers" (Table 2). This is a significant weakness. The review process requires a more rigorous analysis. How sensitive is the model's performance to these parameters? What is the trade-off between the amount of training data filtered out and the final F1 score? A sensitivity analysis or a plot showing this trade-off would make the contribution much stronger and more convincing.
2.  **Limited novelty of individual components.** While the *combination* is novel and effective, the individual projection methods are based on well-established ideas. Annotation projection is a classic technique, and representation projection for cross-lingual model transfer has been explored for various other NLP tasks (as the authors acknowledge in Section 4.4). The paper's contribution lies more in the effective engineering and integration of these ideas for NER rather than in proposing fundamentally new algorithms. This is not a fatal flaw but should be acknowledged.
3.  **Missing details for reproducibility.** Several important experimental details are omitted, which hinders reproducibility.
    *   The paper does not specify the source or size of the monolingual corpora used to train the word embeddings, which is a critical factor for the representation projection approach.
    *   The source of the "comparable corpora" for annotation projection is not mentioned.
    *   In the data selection scheme, the frequency table `T` is described as containing "frequency", but the example in Table 1 shows values that sum to 1, suggesting they are normalized probabilities. The exact normalization method is not specified.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide a sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameters `q` and `n` in your data selection scheme? For instance, for Japanese and Korean, could you show a plot of the final F1 score as a function of the percentage of training sentences retained by the filter? This would help in understanding the robustness of this method.
2.  Can you clarify how the "frequency" in the frequency table `T` (Section 3.1) is calculated and normalized? Is it simply the count of an entity being labeled with a tag `l`, divided by the total number of times that entity appears in the projected corpus?
3.  What were the sources and approximate sizes of the monolingual corpora used for training word embeddings and the comparable corpora used for annotation projection? This information is crucial for assessing the resource requirements of your approach.
4.  You observe that the NN2 architecture (with a smoothing layer) performs better on your in-house data, while the simpler NN1 architecture is superior on the CoNLL data. Do you have any hypothesis for this discrepancy? Could it be related to the much larger and sparser tag set of the in-house data compared to CoNLL?
