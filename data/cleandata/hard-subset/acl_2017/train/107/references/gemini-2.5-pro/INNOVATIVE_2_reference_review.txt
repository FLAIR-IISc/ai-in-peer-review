### Summary of the Paper

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for new languages without any target-language annotations. The authors propose a two-pronged approach. First, they improve upon the standard annotation projection method by introducing a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter out noisy projections, which is particularly effective when alignment quality is low. Second, and more significantly, they introduce a new direct model transfer paradigm called "representation projection." This method involves learning a linear map from a target language's word embedding space to the source language's space, allowing a single source-language NER model (trained on embeddings) to be directly applied to the projected target-language words without any retraining. Finally, they demonstrate that these two conceptually different approaches are complementary and can be combined via simple co-decoding schemes to achieve state-of-the-art results on standard CoNLL benchmarks.

### Contributions

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and elegant paradigm for direct model transfer via representation projection.** The core idea of transforming the input space (word embeddings) rather than the model or labels is a bold and promising direction for cross-lingual transfer tasks. It simplifies the transfer process immensely, requiring only a cross-lingual embedding map and a single source-language model. This stands as a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  **An effective synthesis of two disparate cross-lingual transfer methods.** The paper successfully combines a data-centric approach (annotation projection) with a model-centric one (representation projection). By analyzing their complementary strengths (e.g., high precision vs. balanced recall) and combining them through co-decoding, the authors build a system that is stronger than its parts and achieves state-of-the-art performance.
3.  **A practical and effective filtering technique for noisy annotation projection.** While the idea of filtering noisy data is not new, the proposed frequency-based heuristic is simple, intuitive, and shown to be highly effective, delivering massive improvements in low-quality alignment scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance of Representation Projection:** The standout strength of this paper is the representation projection approach. It is a fundamentally new way to think about cross-lingual NER transfer. The idea is clean, scalable (a new language just needs an embedding map to English), and avoids the need to train a new model for every target language. This kind of bold, simplifying concept is exactly what the field needs to move forward, and I value this contribution very highly.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed combined system is not just a theoretical novelty; it delivers. The paper shows consistent and significant improvements over both individual systems and, more importantly, outperforms prior state-of-the-art methods on the CoNLL test sets. The fact that the system comes close to supervised performance in some cases (e.g., Korean, German in-house data) with zero target-language annotations is a very strong result.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Diverse Settings:** The authors validate their approach on two distinct types of datasets: a fine-grained, in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard, coarse-grained CoNLL dataset. The methods prove effective in both scenarios, demonstrating robustness and generality. This dual evaluation strengthens the paper's conclusions significantly.
4.  **Intelligent System Combination:** The paper doesn't just present two methods but insightfully analyzes their distinct performance profiles (Annotation Projection's high precision, Representation Projection's balanced P/R). This analysis directly motivates the co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based one, which leverages these differences. This demonstrates thoughtful system design rather than just throwing two models together.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper's core ideas, there are areas where the execution could be more rigorous. However, in my view, these are minor weaknesses that do not undermine the significance of the contributions.

1.  **Over-reliance on Simple Heuristics:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection and the co-decoding strategies are effective but highly heuristic. The selection depends on manually tuned thresholds (q, n), and the rank-based co-decoding is a simple rule based on an empirical observation. A deeper analysis of the sensitivity to these parameters or a more principled, model-based approach to data selection or combination would have strengthened the paper.
2.  **Limited Exploration of Representation Mapping:** The paper adopts the linear mapping technique from Mikolov et al. (2013b) for its representation projection. While this is a reasonable starting point, the field of cross-lingual embeddings has advanced since then, with more sophisticated unsupervised and non-linear methods. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the choice of this method over others or showed that this simple approach is sufficient for the task. The contribution lies in the application to NER, but it is unclear how much performance is left on the table by using a relatively dated mapping technique.
3.  **Inconsistent Model Architectures:** The annotation projection pipeline trains an MEMM model on the target side, while the representation projection pipeline uses a neural network. This is justified for practical reasons (training speed), but it creates a confounding variable. It's difficult to fully isolate the effect of the projection *method* from the effect of the underlying model *architecture*. A cleaner comparison would have used the same model family for both, even if it required more engineering effort.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The representation projection approach is very compelling. Could you elaborate on its handling of OOV words in the target language, particularly those that are also OOV in English? The paper mentions mapping to a generic "unk" vector, but how does this affect performance, especially for languages that are morphologically distant from English and might have a high rate of such OOVs?
2.  The effectiveness of the data selection heuristic for annotation projection seems strongly correlated with alignment quality. The F1 gains for low-quality alignment languages (Japanese, Korean) are massive, while they are minimal for high-quality ones (German, Portuguese). Could you provide a more direct analysis to support this conclusion? For example, showing that the distribution of the quality score q(y) is indeed lower or more spread out for the low-quality alignment corpora would provide stronger evidence that your metric is correctly identifying alignment noise.
