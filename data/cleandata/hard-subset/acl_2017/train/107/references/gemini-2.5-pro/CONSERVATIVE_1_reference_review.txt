**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any annotated data. The authors propose and evaluate two primary approaches: an Annotation Projection (AP) method and a Representation Projection (RP) method. The AP method leverages a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy annotations projected from a source language via parallel corpora. The RP method learns a linear mapping between monolingual word embedding spaces to allow a source-language neural NER model to be directly applied to target-language text. Finally, the paper introduces two simple co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a straightforward combination of two established cross-lingual transfer techniques (Annotation Projection and Representation Projection) can achieve state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL benchmark, outperforming previously published methods.
2.  An extensive evaluation across seven target languages and two distinct types of datasets (fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL), which provides valuable benchmarks for future work in this area.
3.  A simple, frequency-based heuristic for filtering projected annotations, which shows significant gains, particularly in low-resource or low-quality alignment scenarios.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its results. The proposed combined systems outperform the established baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark, even with established components, is a noteworthy contribution.
2.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and on two different kinds of NER tasks (a fine-grained 50+ type task and the standard 4-type CoNLL task). This extensive evaluation provides convincing evidence for the general applicability and robustness of the proposed system.
3.  **Well-Motivated System Combination:** The paper effectively leverages two orthogonal views of the cross-lingual transfer problem. Annotation projection works in the label space, while representation projection operates in the feature space. The analysis of their respective precision/recall profiles provides a reasonable motivation for the co-decoding schemes, which demonstrably improve performance over the individual systems in nearly all cases.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core components of the proposed system are applications of well-established techniques. Annotation projection is a classical approach, and the idea of learning linear maps between word embedding spaces for cross-lingual tasks was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b). The main novelties presented—the data selection and co-decoding schemes—are simple, frequency-based heuristics rather than principled, learnable methods. While effective, they feel more like engineering solutions than fundamental methodological advancements.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Key Components:** Several key components of the methodology lack rigorous analysis and justification.
    *   The data selection scheme for AP relies on two thresholds, `q` and `n`. The paper simply states that the best numbers are reported after trying different values. This raises concerns about tuning on the test set. A proper ablation study showing sensitivity to these parameters on a development set would be necessary to validate the robustness of this heuristic.
    *   The NN2 architecture includes an unconventional "smoothing layer" involving prototype vectors. This component is not well-explained, its motivation is unclear, and there is no analysis of why it helps on the in-house dataset but is inferior to the simpler NN1 on the CoNLL data. This inconsistency is concerning and undermines the contribution of this architectural variant.
    *   The rank-based co-decoding scheme is entirely dependent on the observation that the AP system has high precision and low recall. While this holds in the reported experiments, it is a strong assumption that may not generalize to other language pairs, domains, or underlying NER models. The paper does not investigate the limits of this assumption.
3.  **Use of Dated Base Models:** The underlying NER models (MEMMs, simple feed-forward NNs) are not representative of the current state of the art in sequence labeling, which has largely standardized on BiLSTM-CRF architectures with character-level features. While the goal is cross-lingual transfer, it is unclear how the projection methods would interact with more powerful and complex base models. Using older, simpler architectures limits the conclusions that can be drawn about the projection techniques themselves, as performance may be capped by the base models' expressive power.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the data selection scheme for Annotation Projection: Could you clarify the tuning process for the `q` and `n` thresholds? Table 2 reports the best results. Were these thresholds selected using a held-out development set for each language, or were they tuned on the test set? Please provide details on the sensitivity of the model's performance to these hyperparameters.
2.  The NN2 architecture is presented as an alternative to a standard feed-forward network (NN1). Could you please provide a more detailed explanation of the "smoothing layer"? Specifically, how are the prototype vectors initialized and learned, and what is the theoretical motivation for this layer? Furthermore, can you offer a hypothesis for why NN2 outperforms NN1 on the in-house data but underperforms on the CoNLL data?
3.  The co-decoding schemes appear to be a critical part of achieving the reported state-of-the-art results. Have you considered more principled ensemble methods beyond the proposed heuristics? For example, could the outputs of the AP and RP systems be used as features in a final stacking model trained on the projected data itself?
