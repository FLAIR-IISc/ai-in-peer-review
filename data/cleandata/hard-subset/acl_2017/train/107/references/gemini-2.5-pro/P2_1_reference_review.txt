### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any annotated data. The authors propose and evaluate two primary projection-based approaches and a method for combining them.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective combination of two complementary cross-lingual transfer methods.** The paper's strongest contribution is showing that Annotation Projection (AP) and Representation Projection (RP) have different performance characteristics (e.g., AP being high-precision, low-recall) and that combining them via a simple but well-motivated co-decoding scheme leads to state-of-the-art results. This demonstrates a strong system-building methodology.
2.  **A simple and highly effective heuristic for filtering noisy projected annotations.** For the classic Annotation Projection approach, the authors introduce a data selection scheme based on entity label consistency and density. This method provides substantial gains, particularly for language pairs with low-quality word alignments, turning a mediocre baseline into a strong one.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse languages and datasets.** The authors validate their methods on both a fine-grained (50+ entity types) in-house dataset for four languages and the standard CoNLL benchmark datasets for three languages, demonstrating the general applicability and robustness of their approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed combined system demonstrably outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the standard CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets. The improvements, especially for German, are significant. This provides clear evidence of the paper's contribution to the field.

2.  **Insightful Combination of Complementary Approaches:** The paper does not just present two separate methods but intelligently combines them. The analysis of the different precision/recall profiles of the AP and RP systems is a key insight. The rank-based co-decoding scheme, which prioritizes the high-precision AP system's outputs and uses the RP system to improve recall, is well-motivated by this analysis and empirically successful, yielding gains of 2.9 to 6.2 F1 points on the in-house data.

3.  **Practical and Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed heuristic for data selection in Section 3.1 is a standout feature. While conceptually simple, its effectiveness is striking. The results in Table 2 show massive F1 improvements for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), languages for which the alignment quality was reported to be low. This demonstrates that filtering noisy projected data is a critical step, and the paper offers a simple and effective way to do it. This is a valuable practical contribution for anyone working with projected annotations.

4.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The experiments are extensive. Testing on seven target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) across two very different types of NER tasks (fine-grained in-house vs. coarse-grained CoNLL) lends significant credibility to the findings. This shows that the methods are not brittle or over-tuned to a specific dataset or tagset.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty of Individual Components:** While the overall system is effective, the core ideas of Annotation Projection and Representation Projection are not new. Annotation projection is a classic cross-lingual transfer technique, and projecting word embeddings via a linear map was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been applied to other NLP tasks. The novelty lies in the specific application to NER, the filtering heuristic, and the combination, but not in the fundamental projection mechanisms themselves.

2.  **The Data Selection Heuristic Lacks Deeper Analysis:** The data selection scheme uses two thresholds, `q` and `n`, which are tuned per language. The paper reports the best-performing values but offers little insight into *why* these values were chosen or how they might be set without a development set. For example, why is a high quality threshold of `q=0.9` needed for Korean but a moderate `q=0.5` is sufficient for German? Does this correlate directly with alignment accuracy? A more principled approach or a deeper analysis of this sensitivity would strengthen the contribution.

3.  **Lack of Reproducibility for In-House Experiments:** A significant portion of the paper's results (Table 3) relies on proprietary in-house data and models. The 50+ entity type task is an interesting and challenging testbed, but since the data, source NER model, and alignment models are not public, these results are not verifiable or directly reproducible by the research community. While the CoNLL experiments mitigate this, it remains a significant limitation.

4.  **Unexplained Discrepancy in Representation Projection Models:** The paper presents two neural network architectures, NN1 and NN2, for the Representation Projection approach. Curiously, NN2 (with a smoothing layer) performs better on the in-house data, while NN1 is superior on the CoNLL data. The paper notes this but provides no explanation for this inconsistency. This raises questions about the stability and generalizability of the proposed neural architectures and when one should be preferred over the other.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the data selection heuristic in Section 3.1: Can you provide more intuition on how the optimal thresholds `(q, n)` correlate with properties of the language pair, such as alignment accuracy or corpus size? Is there a more principled way to set these hyperparameters in a scenario where a target-language dev set is truly unavailable?

2.  Could you elaborate on the performance discrepancy between the NN1 and NN2 architectures on the in-house versus CoNLL datasets? What is the hypothesis for why the "smoothing layer" in NN2 helps for the fine-grained, 50+ type task but hurts performance on the coarse-grained 4-type CoNLL task?

3.  The paper mentions that for learning the cross-lingual embedding mappings, "larger dictionaries might harm the performance." This is an interesting finding. Could you provide a brief hypothesis as to why this might be? Does it relate to overfitting the linear transformation on noisy or ambiguous word pairs that are more prevalent in larger, less-filtered dictionaries?
