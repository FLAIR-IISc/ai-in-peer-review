### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where no labeled data is available in the target language. The authors propose and combine two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, an "Annotation Projection" method, improves upon the standard technique by introducing a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projected labels from comparable corpora. The second, a novel "Representation Projection" method, performs direct model transfer by training an NER model on source-language word embeddings and applying it to target-language words after projecting their embeddings into the source space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A strong empirical demonstration that combining two orthogonal cross-lingual transfer paradigms—label-level projection (Annotation Projection) and feature-level projection (Representation Projection)—can yield state-of-the-art results for weakly supervised NER.
2.  An extensive evaluation across multiple languages and two distinct datasets (a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL benchmark), showing consistent improvements over individual systems and outperforming previously published baselines.
3.  A simple yet effective data filtering heuristic for annotation projection that provides significant gains, particularly in low-resource or noisy alignment scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results on Standard Benchmarks:** The primary strength of this paper is its performance on the CoNLL test data. The proposed combined system clearly outperforms the established and highly relevant baselines of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). Achieving improvements of this magnitude on a standard shared task is a significant result and provides a strong new baseline for future work in this area.

2.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Methods:** The paper makes a convincing case for combining two fundamentally different transfer methods. The analysis showing that Annotation Projection (AP) tends to yield high precision while Representation Projection (RP) offers more balanced performance is insightful. This observation provides a solid rationale for the co-decoding schemes, which successfully leverage the complementary strengths of each model to achieve a final result superior to either one in isolation.

3.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The authors validate their methods across a wide range of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and on two very different types of NER tasks (a fine-grained 50+ type in-house task and the standard 4-type CoNLL task). This extensive testing increases confidence that the proposed techniques are robust and generally applicable, rather than being tailored to a specific language pair or label set.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Reliance on Heuristics:** While the results are strong, the core components of the proposed methods are adaptations of existing work. Cross-lingual embedding projection for model transfer has been established in other NLP tasks, and annotation projection is a classic technique. The primary novelties are the specific heuristics for data selection and model combination. These heuristics, however, lack rigorous justification. The data selection scheme, based on frequency counts and hard thresholds, feels ad-hoc. The paper states that for Table 2, "we have tried different threshold parameters q and n and reported the best numbers," which suggests that these crucial hyperparameters may have been tuned on the test set, a methodologically unsound practice. A proper validation set should be used for all tuning.

2.  **Questionable Choices in NER Model Architecture:** The paper employs a simple feedforward neural network for its representation projection model. This is a significant deviation from the established state-of-the-art architecture for NER, which for several years has been a BiLSTM-CRF model. The authors provide no justification for using a simpler, locally normalized model that is less capable of capturing long-range sequential dependencies. It is unclear if the gains from representation projection are truly maximized with this architecture. Furthermore, the "smoothing layer" in the NN2 model is poorly motivated and explained; its introduction seems arbitrary without a clearer intuition or ablation study.

3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Ablation:** The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of its components. For the data selection heuristic, a sensitivity analysis showing how the F1 score varies with the thresholds `q` and `n` would be far more insightful than simply reporting the single best configuration. This would demonstrate the robustness of the method. Similarly, while the co-decoding results are good, the analysis is superficial. An error analysis comparing the outputs of the AP and RP systems would provide concrete evidence for their complementarity and better explain *why* the combination works. For example, what specific types of entities does one system consistently identify that the other misses?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the procedure used to tune the data selection thresholds `(q, n)` for the experiments in Table 2? Were these parameters selected based on performance on a held-out development set, or were they optimized directly on the test set results presented?

2.  What was the rationale for choosing a feedforward neural network over a more standard and powerful sequential architecture like a BiLSTM-CRF for the representation projection NER model? Given the importance of sequence information in NER, this choice seems counter-intuitive and warrants a more detailed justification.

3.  Could you provide more detail on the "prototype vectors" used in the NN2 model's smoothing layer? How are they initialized and trained? What is the underlying intuition for this mechanism, and how does it contribute to a more "balanced precision and recall" as claimed?
