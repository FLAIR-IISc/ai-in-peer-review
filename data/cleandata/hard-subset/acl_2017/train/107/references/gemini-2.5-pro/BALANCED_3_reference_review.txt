### **Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where the goal is to build an NER system for a target language using resources from a source language (English) but no annotated data in the target language. The authors propose and combine two distinct approaches. The first is an annotation projection method that uses a novel and effective heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy labels projected across comparable corpora, thereby improving the quality of the training data for a target-language NER model. The second is a representation projection method that enables direct model transfer by learning a linear mapping from a target language's word embedding space to the source language's space, allowing a single source-language neural NER model to be applied directly to the target language. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two complementary systems, leveraging their different precision/recall characteristics. Extensive experiments on both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL benchmark show that the combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A strong framework for combining complementary cross-lingual transfer methods.** The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating that annotation projection and representation projection, which have different error profiles, can be effectively combined. The proposed rank-based co-decoding scheme is a simple yet powerful method to leverage the high-precision nature of the annotation projection system and the balanced performance of the representation projection system, leading to significant and consistent gains over either individual method. This provides a very strong baseline for future work in this area.
2.  **An effective heuristic for filtering noisy data in annotation projection.** While annotation projection is a known technique, the proposed data selection scheme is a simple and practical solution to the critical problem of noisy labels arising from imperfect alignments. By scoring projected sentences based on label consistency and entity density, the method is shown to yield dramatic improvements (up to +20 F1 points) in low-quality alignment scenarios, making the annotation projection approach much more robust.

### **Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results.** The paper demonstrates superior performance on the standard CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets for Spanish, Dutch, and German, outperforming two well-regarded previous approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016). The strong results across multiple languages validate the effectiveness of the proposed combined system and establish a new state of the art for this task.
2.  **Novel and Effective System Combination.** The idea of combining two different views—one based on projecting discrete labels (AP) and the other on projecting continuous representations (RP)—is compelling. The authors provide an insightful analysis of the differing precision/recall trade-offs of the two systems. The rank-based co-decoding scheme is a direct and intuitive consequence of this analysis, providing a simple yet highly effective way to fuse the systems' outputs. This focus on building a robust system from complementary components is a significant strength.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation.** The experiments are extensive, covering seven languages across two very different settings: an in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types and the public CoNLL dataset with 4 coarse-grained types. This dual evaluation strengthens the paper's conclusions, showing that the methods are effective under different conditions. The inclusion of supervised baselines also provides important context for interpreting the results of the weakly supervised models.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Core Projection Methods.** While the overall framework is effective, the two underlying projection methods are largely applications of existing techniques. Annotation projection is a classic approach in cross-lingual transfer, and representation projection via linear mapping of embedding spaces has been explored for several other NLP tasks. The paper's main novelty lies in the engineering of the data selection heuristic and the system combination, rather than in a fundamental methodological advance.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of the Representation Projection Component.** The representation projection approach could be strengthened with more analysis. For instance, the NN2 architecture, with its "smoothing layer" and "prototype vectors," is not explained in sufficient detail, making it difficult to understand its mechanism. The paper reports that NN2 is better for the in-house data while NN1 is better for CoNLL, but offers no hypothesis for why this might be the case. A qualitative analysis of projection errors or a study on the effect of dictionary size would have provided deeper insights.
3.  **Over-reliance on Heuristics and Hyperparameter Tuning.** Both the data selection scheme and the rank-based co-decoding are effective but highly heuristic. The data selection requires tuning two thresholds (`q`, `n`) for each language, which presumes the existence of a development set. The rank-based combination relies on a fixed assumption about the precision/recall profiles of the component systems. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the sensitivity to these hyperparameters or explored more principled, learnable combination methods as an alternative.
4.  **Missing Implementation Details Affecting Reproducibility.** Several key details required for full reproducibility are missing. The feature template for the MEMM model used in the annotation projection system is not described. For the neural models, details like the hidden layer size, context window size, and the specific learning process for the "prototype vectors" in NN2 are absent. This makes it difficult for other researchers to precisely replicate the work or build upon it.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the NN2 architecture? Specifically, how are the "prototype vectors" initialized and learned during training, and how many are used? Could you also offer a hypothesis for why this "smoothing layer" proved beneficial for the fine-grained in-house task but detrimental for the coarse-grained CoNLL task?
2.  The data selection method for annotation projection depends on two hyperparameters (`q` and `n`) that require tuning. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of these parameters? Does the F1 score vary smoothly around the optimal values, or are the results highly dependent on precise tuning?
3.  The rank-based co-decoding scheme is motivated by the observation that the AP system typically has higher precision. However, in some cases (e.g., Spanish dev set in Table 4), the precision difference between AP and NN1 is minimal. How robust is this rank-based heuristic when its underlying precision/recall assumption is not strongly met? Have you considered a softer, learnable combination scheme?
