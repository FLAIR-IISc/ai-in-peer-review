### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The core idea is to leverage a high-resource source language (English) and transfer knowledge through two distinct and complementary projection methods. The first, **Annotation Projection (AP)**, projects NER tags from an English NER system onto aligned sentences in a target language. The second, **Representation Projection (RP)**, learns a mapping between monolingual word embedding spaces, allowing an English NER model to be directly applied to target language text. The authors then propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems, demonstrating that the combined model consistently outperforms the individual components.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and well-motivated data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** The proposed method filters noisy projected data based on label consistency across a large corpus. This is a simple yet powerful technique that proves highly effective, especially in scenarios with low-quality word alignments, yielding significant F1 score improvements (e.g., +20 for Korean).
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of combining complementary cross-lingual transfer methods.** The paper shows that AP (high precision, low recall) and RP (more balanced) provide different "views" of the data. By systematically combining them, the authors achieve state-of-the-art results on standard CoNLL benchmarks, outperforming previous work by a notable margin. This highlights the value of a multi-pronged approach to cross-lingual transfer.
3.  **A thorough evaluation across diverse settings.** The authors validate their framework on both in-house data with a large, fine-grained tagset (>50 types) and standard CoNLL data (4 types), across a total of six target languages. This extensive experimentation demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of their proposed methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Results:** The most compelling strength of this paper is its results. The proposed combined system outperforms strong, recently published baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL test sets (Table 5). This is a clear and impactful result that provides a new, strong baseline for the field.
2.  **Novelty and Practicality of the Data Selection Scheme:** The heuristic for filtering projected annotations is a key strength. It is intuitive, computationally inexpensive, and addresses a critical bottleneck in annotation projection: noise amplification from upstream errors (source NER and alignment). The dramatic improvements shown in Table 2 for Japanese and Korean, where alignment quality is lowest, provide convincing evidence of its utility. This is a practical technique that future work in this area can easily adopt.
3.  **Intelligent Combination of Complementary Systems:** The paper provides a clear analysis of the different characteristics of the AP and RP systems (e.g., AP's high precision vs. RP's more balanced profile). The co-decoding schemes, while simple, are designed based on this observation. The success of the rank-based scheme, which prioritizes the high-precision AP system, is a testament to this thoughtful design. This "ensemble" approach is a valuable contribution to the engineering of robust cross-lingual systems.
4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is extensive. Using both in-house and public datasets with vastly different characteristics (tagset size, domain) strengthens the paper's claims. The inclusion of supervised baselines provides a useful upper bound and context for the performance of the weakly supervised methods, showing that for some languages (e.g., Korean, German), the gap is reasonably small.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Representation Projection Component:** The representation projection method is a direct application of the linear mapping technique proposed by Mikolov et al. (2013b). While its application to NER is valid and effective, the method itself is not novel. The paper's contribution here is more in the successful integration of this existing technique into a larger cross-lingual NER framework rather than a new method for creating or transferring representations.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Ablation:** The paper presents strong results but misses opportunities for deeper analysis that would strengthen its scientific contribution.
    *   The difference in performance between the NN1 and NN2 architectures on the in-house vs. CoNLL datasets is not explained. Why does the "smoothing layer" in NN2 help for the fine-grained task but not the 4-class CoNLL task? A brief analysis or hypothesis would be very insightful.
    *   The paper states that larger dictionaries (for learning the embedding mapping) can *harm* performance, a counter-intuitive finding that warrants explanation. An ablation study showing performance as a function of dictionary size would have been valuable.
    *   The data selection heuristic for AP depends on two thresholds (`q` and `n`). The paper reports the best-performing configuration but does not show how sensitive the model is to these hyperparameters. A sensitivity analysis would better characterize the robustness of this method.
3.  **Ambiguity Regarding Data and Resources:** The methodology relies heavily on pre-existing resources like comparable corpora and alignment models. While the paper mentions the alignment F1 scores, it is not entirely clear what corpora were used to train these alignment models and extract the dictionaries for representation projection. For reproducibility and for assessing the method's applicability, a more detailed description of the size and nature of these parallel/comparable resources would be beneficial. It is unclear how much parallel data is a prerequisite for these methods to be effective.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more intuition about the "smoothing layer" in the NN2 architecture? What is the motivation behind this design, and can you hypothesize why it yields a more balanced precision/recall, particularly on the fine-grained in-house dataset, while the simpler NN1 performs better on the CoNLL data?
2.  You make the interesting claim that using larger dictionaries (beyond 5-6K pairs) for learning the cross-lingual embedding mapping can be detrimental. Could you elaborate on this? Is this because larger dictionaries introduce more noise from low-frequency or ambiguous word pairs?
3.  The data selection scheme is very effective. How did you tune the thresholds `q` and `n`? Was it on a held-out development set? A brief sensitivity analysis would be helpful to understand the trade-offs involved (e.g., a plot of F1 score vs. the percentage of data retained).
