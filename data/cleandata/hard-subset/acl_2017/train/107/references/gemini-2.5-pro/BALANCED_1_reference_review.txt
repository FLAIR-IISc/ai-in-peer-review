### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate a framework built on two complementary projection-based approaches. The first, an **Annotation Projection (AP)** method, projects NER labels from a source language (English) to a target language via word-aligned corpora. Its main contribution is a novel data selection heuristic that filters the noisy projected data based on the global consistency of entity-tag assignments, proving particularly effective for low-quality alignments. The second approach is **Representation Projection (RP)**, a direct model transfer technique. An English NER model is trained using only word embeddings, and a linear transformation is learned to map target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space. This allows the English model to be applied directly to the projected target-language representations without retraining. Finally, the paper introduces two **Co-Decoding** schemes (rank-based and confidence-based) to combine the outputs of the high-precision AP system and the more balanced RP system. The combined framework is evaluated on both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL 2002/03 datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art results compared to previous work.

**Main Contributions**
1.  **A comprehensive and effective framework for weakly-supervised cross-lingual NER.** The primary contribution is the successful integration of two different cross-lingual transfer paradigms (annotation and representation projection) into a single system via a co-decoding mechanism. This combined system leverages the complementary strengths of each component to establish a new, strong state-of-the-art baseline for the task.
2.  **A simple and practical heuristic for filtering noisy projected annotations.** The proposed data selection scheme for the annotation projection method is an important contribution. By measuring the quality of projected sentences based on the consistency of entity labels across a large corpus, it provides an effective, unsupervised way to mitigate noise from imperfect source NER and word alignments. Its significant impact on languages with poor alignment quality highlights its practical value.
3.  **A solid empirical demonstration of representation projection for NER.** While the technique of mapping embedding spaces has been used for other NLP tasks, this work provides a thorough application and evaluation for NER. It shows that even with a simple neural model, direct model transfer via representation projection is a competitive approach on its own and a valuable, complementary component in an ensemble system.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Evaluation:** The paper presents compelling results, outperforming strong, established baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the standard CoNLL test sets. The evaluation is robust, spanning multiple languages (German, Dutch, Spanish, Japanese, Korean, Portuguese) and two distinct types of datasets: the standard 4-type CoNLL data and an in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types. This demonstrates the general applicability and effectiveness of the proposed framework.
2.  **Well-Motivated Combination of Complementary Methods:** The insight that the annotation and representation projection methods have complementary error profiles (AP having high precision and low recall, RP being more balanced) is a key strength. The co-decoding schemes are a direct and effective way to exploit this observation. The rank-based scheme, in particular, is an intuitive heuristic that prioritizes high-confidence predictions from one system while using the other to improve recall, leading to consistent gains across nearly all experiments.
3.  **Practicality and Simplicity of the Proposed Techniques:** The methods proposed, particularly the data selection heuristic for AP, are simple, intuitive, and appear easy to implement. The heuristic provides a practical solution to a common and critical problem in annotation projection—dealing with noise. The significant F1 score improvements for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), where alignment quality was noted to be low, provide strong evidence for its real-world utility.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combined framework is novel and effective, the core building blocks are adaptations of well-known techniques. Annotation projection is a long-standing approach, and representation projection via linear mapping of embedding spaces was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been applied to other cross-lingual tasks. The NER models themselves (MEMM, feedforward NNs) are also standard. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer positioning of its contributions in the context of this extensive prior work, emphasizing that the novelty lies primarily in the specific data selection heuristic and the successful combination of these two paradigms for NER.
2.  **Analysis of the Representation Projection Method is Underdeveloped:** The representation projection component could benefit from deeper analysis.
    *   The NN2 architecture, with its "smoothing layer," shows inconsistent performance (better on in-house data, worse on CoNLL) that is not adequately explained. Its motivation and behavior warrant more discussion.
    *   The paper states that using more than 5-6K word pairs for training the embedding mapping "might harm the performance." This is a counter-intuitive and important finding that is presented without supporting evidence or ablation studies. An analysis of why this occurs (e.g., noise in low-frequency pairs from the phrase table) would be highly valuable.
3.  **Use of Dated NER Architectures:** The NER models employed (MEMM and simple feedforward networks) are not representative of the state-of-the-art from the period when this paper was likely written (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF models). While using simpler models can be a valid choice to isolate the effects of the projection methods, it leaves open the question of whether these techniques are equally effective when paired with more powerful, modern sequence labeling architectures. A comparison using a stronger base model would make the conclusions about the projection methods themselves more robust.
4.  **Implicit Reliance on Parallel Resources:** The paper frames the work as requiring "no human annotation in the target language," which is accurate. However, both methods rely on significant parallel resources: word-aligned comparable corpora for AP, and a seed dictionary (extracted from a phrase table, typically built from parallel corpora) for RP. The performance is therefore dependent on the availability and quality of these resources. A discussion on the sensitivity of the methods to the size or quality of these underlying bilingual resources would provide a more complete picture of the methods' applicability and limitations.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The data selection scheme for annotation projection is shown to be very effective when alignment quality is low. Have you conducted any analysis to directly validate the quality score `q(y)`? For instance, does this score correlate well with the actual sentence-level F1 score of the projected labels on a held-out, manually annotated set?
2.  Could you elaborate on the inconsistent performance of the NN2 architecture? Why might the smoothing layer be beneficial for a task with 50+ entity types (in-house data) but detrimental for a task with 4 types (CoNLL)?
3.  Regarding the optimal seed dictionary size for representation projection, could you provide the experimental data or an analysis that supports the claim that larger dictionaries (>6K pairs) can be harmful? Is this due to noise in the automatically extracted pairs, and does it suggest a trade-off between dictionary size and quality?
