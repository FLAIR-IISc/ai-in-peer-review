### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any annotated data. The authors propose a framework that combines two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, an **Annotation Projection (AP)** approach, uses aligned corpora to project NER tags from a source language (English) to a target language. The key novelty here is a heuristic data selection scheme that filters the projected data based on a quality score derived from the consistency of entity-tag assignments. The second approach, **Representation Projection (RP)**, is a direct model transfer method. An NER model (a neural network using only word embeddings) is trained on the source language. For inference on a target language, word embeddings are projected from the target language's vector space to the source's space via a learned linear mapping, allowing the source-language NER model to be applied directly. Finally, the paper introduces two **co-decoding** schemes to combine the outputs of the high-precision AP system and the more balanced RP system, demonstrating that the combined system consistently outperforms the individual components. The framework is evaluated extensively on both fine-grained in-house datasets and standard CoNLL benchmark datasets across six target languages.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive and effective framework combining two complementary projection methods (annotation and representation) for cross-lingual NER.** The primary contribution is not just the individual methods but their systematic integration and evaluation. The analysis of the different precision/recall characteristics of each method and the design of co-decoding schemes to exploit these differences form a robust and well-motivated framework that achieves state-of-the-art results.
2.  **A simple and demonstrably effective data selection heuristic for improving annotation projection.** While annotation projection is a known technique, the proposed method for scoring and filtering noisy projected sentences is a significant practical contribution. By leveraging projection consistency to estimate quality, the method substantially improves performance, especially in scenarios with low-quality word alignments.
3.  **A strong empirical study of direct model transfer for NER via representation projection.** The paper provides a thorough application and evaluation of representation projection for the NER task across multiple languages. This establishes a strong baseline and demonstrates the viability of this approach as a component in a more complex cross-lingual system.

### Strengths

1.  **Comprehensive Evaluation and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper's claims are supported by extensive experiments on a diverse set of six target languages and two different types of datasets: fine-grained in-house data with over 50 entity types and standard coarse-grained CoNLL data. The proposed combined system achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL benchmark, outperforming previously published methods by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). This thorough evaluation provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
2.  **Well-Motivated Combination of Complementary Approaches:** The paper intelligently combines two distinct and complementary sources of cross-lingual signal. Annotation projection leverages sentence-level parallel data to create a target-specific model, while representation projection uses word-level lexical translation knowledge (from a dictionary) and monolingual data for direct transfer. The authors provide a clear analysis showing that AP tends to yield high precision and low recall, while RP is more balanced. This observation provides a strong motivation for the co-decoding schemes, which are shown to successfully leverage the strengths of both systems.
3.  **High Practical Utility of the Data Selection Scheme:** The heuristic for filtering projected annotations is a key strength. It is intuitive, simple to implement, and highly effective. As shown in Table 2, the scheme provides dramatic improvements for Japanese (+13.9 F1) and Korean (+20.0 F1), where word alignment quality is reported to be lower. This demonstrates the method's value in practical, real-world scenarios where parallel data and alignments are often noisy.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors describe each component of their framework—the two projection methods and the co-decoding schemes—with sufficient detail. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently, facilitating understanding and potential reproduction of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Representation Projection Method:** The core technique for representation projection—learning a linear transformation between pre-trained monolingual word embedding spaces using a seed dictionary—is directly adopted from prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b). This approach has also been applied to other cross-lingual transfer tasks like parsing and POS tagging. While its application to NER is valid and the results are strong, the paper does not sufficiently articulate the novel technical contributions of this component beyond its integration into the larger framework.
2.  **Lack of Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection relies on two crucial hyperparameters: a quality threshold `q` and an entity count threshold `n`. Similarly, the representation projection method depends on a carefully selected dictionary size (5K-6K pairs). The paper reports results using the best-performing settings found on a development set but provides no discussion on how these parameters could be set in a true zero-shot setting where no target-language labeled data is available. This limits the practical applicability of the method for new languages without a development set for tuning.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance and Vague Description of NN Architectures:** The paper introduces two neural network architectures, NN1 and NN2, for the representation projection component. The description of NN2's "smoothing layer" with "prototype vectors" is brief and lacks detail on how these prototypes are learned or what they represent. Furthermore, the relative performance of the two models is inconsistent: NN2 is superior on the in-house datasets, while the simpler NN1 is better on the CoNLL datasets. The paper offers no analysis or hypothesis for this discrepancy, which makes it difficult to draw clear conclusions about the benefit of the more complex NN2 architecture.
4.  **Superficial Analysis of Co-Decoding Performance:** The co-decoding schemes are shown to be effective, but the analysis is limited to reporting aggregate P/R/F1 scores. A more in-depth qualitative or quantitative error analysis would strengthen the paper. For example, an analysis of cases where the combination fails, or where one co-decoding scheme is superior to the other, would provide deeper insights into the remaining challenges and the specific interactions between the two underlying systems.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the procedure for setting the data selection hyperparameters `q` and `n`? In a scenario without a target-language development set, how would you advise a practitioner to choose these values? For example, could they be set based on the distribution of quality scores or by targeting a certain percentage of the projected data to retain?
2.  The relative performance of the NN1 and NN2 architectures is inconsistent across the in-house (fine-grained) and CoNLL (coarse-grained) datasets. Do you have a hypothesis for why the NN2 model with the smoothing layer performs better on one type of dataset but worse on the other?
3.  The paper makes the counter-intuitive claim that larger dictionaries (beyond 5K-6K pairs) can harm the performance of representation projection. Could you provide further analysis or intuition as to why adding more, presumably correct, translation pairs to the dictionary would degrade the quality of the learned embedding projection?
