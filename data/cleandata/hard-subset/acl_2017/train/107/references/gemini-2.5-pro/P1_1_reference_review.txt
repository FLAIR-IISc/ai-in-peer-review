### Summary
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, Annotation Projection (AP), projects NER labels from a source language (English) to a target language via word-aligned corpora. Its main contribution is a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections based on label consistency, which proves highly effective for languages with low-quality alignments. The second approach, Representation Projection (RP), trains an NER model on source language word embeddings and directly applies it to the target language by projecting target word embeddings into the source embedding space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, leveraging their complementary strengths. Experiments on both in-house and CoNLL datasets show that the combined systems outperform the individual components and achieve state-of-the-art results compared to previous work.

### Contributions
1.  **An effective and simple data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** The paper's most significant contribution is the proposed method for filtering noisy projected data in the AP approach. By calculating a quality score based on the frequency of an entity being assigned a particular label across the corpus, the method significantly boosts performance (+14 to +20 F1 points) in low-resource settings where word alignments are poor. This is a practical and impactful technique for anyone using annotation projection.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of combining complementary cross-lingual transfer methods.** The paper systematically compares two fundamentally different transfer paradigms: AP, which relies on token-level syntactic alignment, and RP, which relies on semantic similarity in a shared vector space. The authors show these methods have different precision/recall profiles and that combining them via a simple rank-based co-decoding scheme yields substantial performance gains and state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. This provides a very strong and well-vetted baseline for future work in this area.

### Strengths
1.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The paper's experimental setup is a major strength. The authors evaluate their methods on a wide range of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and across two very different types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard CoNLL benchmark with 4 types. This demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of their findings.
2.  **Highly Effective Data Selection Scheme:** The proposed data selection scheme for AP is simple, intuitive, and remarkably effective. The results in Table 2 clearly show its value, especially for Japanese and Korean, where low-quality alignments produce noisy projected data. This technique addresses a critical and often overlooked weakness of the annotation projection paradigm.
3.  **Intelligent Combination of Complementary Models:** The analysis of the two projection systems is insightful. The observation that AP tends to be high-precision/low-recall while RP is more balanced provides a strong motivation for combining them. The proposed rank-based co-decoding scheme is a clever heuristic that directly leverages this observation, leading to consistent improvements across almost all languages tested.
4.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The final combined system outperforms two strong, contemporary baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the standard CoNLL test data. Achieving SOTA results provides clear evidence of the paper's contribution to the field.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Projection Methods:** While the application and combination are well-executed, the two core approaches (annotation projection and representation projection) are not new concepts in cross-lingual transfer. The novelty lies primarily in the data selection heuristic and the specific combination strategy. The paper is more of a strong engineering and empirical contribution than a proposal of a fundamentally new algorithm.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Motivation for Some Design Choices:**
    *   The NN2 architecture includes a "smoothing layer" with "prototype vectors" that is claimed to improve the precision/recall balance. However, this component is not well-explained, motivated, or analyzed. It is unclear how these prototypes are learned or why this specific architecture has the observed effect.
    *   The paper states that for the RP approach, dictionaries larger than 5K-6K pairs "might harm the performance." This is a critical hyperparameter and a counter-intuitive finding that warrants more explanation or an ablation study. Without further evidence, this claim is difficult to assess.
    *   The paper observes that the best co-decoding scheme differs between the two datasets (rank-based for in-house, confidence-based for CoNLL) but does not offer a deep analysis as to why. This could be related to the number of entity types, the dataset characteristics, or the specific P/R profiles of the base models, and exploring this would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
3.  **Potential Reproducibility Challenges:** The experiments rely on several non-public components, including the in-house dataset, proprietary alignment models, and specific word embedding models. While the CoNLL portion is more reproducible, the exact replication of the SOTA-achieving system would be difficult without access to these resources or more detailed descriptions of their creation.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide more details and intuition behind the NN2 "smoothing layer"? How are the prototype vectors initialized and learned, and what is the hypothesis for why this architecture leads to a more balanced precision and recall compared to the standard NN1 model?
2.  You found that the optimal co-decoding scheme varied between your in-house and the CoNLL datasets. Do you have a hypothesis for this? For example, could it be that with a very large number of entity types (50+), the confidence scores from the weakly supervised models become less reliable, making the rank-based heuristic more robust?
3.  The claim that larger bilingual dictionaries (beyond 6K pairs) can harm representation projection performance is interesting. Could you elaborate on this finding? Was this a consistent trend, and do you have a hypothesis for why adding more (presumably correct) alignment pairs would degrade performance?
