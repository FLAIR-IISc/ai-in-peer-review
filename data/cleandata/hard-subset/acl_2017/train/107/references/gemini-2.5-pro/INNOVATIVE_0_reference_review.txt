### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a weakly supervised setting, where no annotated data is available in the target language. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct projection-based approaches and show that combining them leads to state-of-the-art performance.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel direct model transfer approach for NER via representation projection.** This is the most significant contribution. The paper proposes training an NER model solely on source language word embeddings and then applying it directly to a target language by projecting the target word embeddings into the source embedding space. This is an elegant and bold approach that sidesteps the noisy process of projecting labels and retraining a new model for each target language.
2.  **An effective heuristic for improving annotation projection.** The authors introduce a simple yet powerful data selection scheme to filter noisy labels generated by the standard annotation projection pipeline. By scoring projected entities based on their label frequency and selecting sentences that exceed quality and density thresholds, they achieve substantial improvements, particularly when word alignments are poor.
3.  **A demonstration of the complementary nature of label-level and representation-level transfer.** The paper shows that the two proposed methods, which operate at different levels of abstraction (labels vs. features), have different precision/recall characteristics. By intelligently combining them through co-decoding, the authors achieve results superior to either individual method.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Boldness:** The core idea of representation projection for NER is a significant conceptual leap. While cross-lingual embeddings were being explored for other tasks, their application for direct model transfer in a structured prediction task like NER is a compelling and innovative direction. It elegantly reframes the cross-lingual transfer problem from one of label transfer to one of creating a universal representation space. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-potential idea that the field needs.

2.  **Pragmatic and Effective Solution to a Known Problem:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a highlight. Annotation projection is notoriously sensitive to alignment errors, and this paper provides a simple, intuitive, and remarkably effective heuristic to mitigate this issue. The massive F1 score gains for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), where alignment quality was lowest, provide strong evidence for the utility of this method. It is a valuable practical contribution for anyone working with projected annotations.

3.  **Synergy of Orthogonal Approaches:** The paper doesn't just propose two methods in isolation; it astutely observes their different performance profiles (high-precision annotation projection vs. balanced representation projection) and leverages this via co-decoding. The rank-based co-decoding, in particular, is a clever engineering solution that capitalizes on the strengths of each model. This systems-level contribution, demonstrating how to effectively combine two very different transfer paradigms, is a major strength.

4.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors validate their methods across a wide range of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and on two very different types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard CoNLL benchmarks. The fact that their combined system outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the CoNLL test data convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of the Representation Projection Method:** While the representation projection approach is conceptually exciting, its analysis feels somewhat shallow. For instance, the paper notes that the NN2 architecture (with a "smoothing layer") works better for the in-house data while NN1 is better for CoNLL, but offers little intuition as to why. A more detailed analysis of the projection quality, the types of errors the model makes, or the sensitivity of the performance to the quality of the seed dictionary used for learning the mapping would have significantly strengthened this core contribution. As it stands, it's presented more as a black box that works well.

2.  **Over-reliance on Heuristics and Parameter Tuning:** Both the data selection scheme (with its `q` and `n` thresholds) and the rank-based co-decoding scheme are heavily heuristic. The paper reports results with tuned parameters but does not discuss how one would set these in a true zero-shot scenario without a target-language development set. While the results are strong, the dependence on such heuristics slightly undermines the generalizability and robustness of the proposed pipeline.

3.  **Inconsistent Model Architectures:** The annotation projection approach uses an MEMM, while the representation projection approach uses a neural network. The justification (CRF/MEMM scaling) is practical, but it complicates the comparison. It is unclear if the performance differences are solely due to the projection strategy or if the choice of the underlying sequence model is a major confounding factor. Using a consistent neural architecture for both approaches would have enabled a cleaner ablation and a more direct comparison of the transfer methods themselves.

### Overall Recommendation

Despite some weaknesses, mostly related to a lack of deeper analysis and a reliance on heuristics, this is a strong paper with significant contributions. The core idea of direct NER model transfer via representation projection is highly novel, elegant, and forward-thinking. This, combined with a very effective and practical method for improving the classic annotation projection pipeline, makes for a compelling submission. The authors demonstrate convincingly that these two orthogonal ideas can be combined to push the state of the art.

In line with my reviewing philosophy, I value the paper's bold new direction and conceptual novelty far more than I am deterred by the methodological imperfections. The representation projection idea opens up a new avenue for research in cross-lingual structured prediction. The paper presents a strong new baseline and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
