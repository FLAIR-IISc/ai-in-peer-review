### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data, by leveraging resources from a source language (English). The authors propose and evaluate two primary projection-based approaches and then combine them for improved performance.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **An effective heuristic for filtering projected annotations.** The authors' primary contribution is a simple yet highly effective data selection scheme for the annotation projection approach. By calculating a quality score for each projected sentence based on the consistency of entity-tag assignments across a large comparable corpus, they can effectively filter out noisy training instances. This is shown to yield substantial improvements, especially when the underlying word alignments are poor.
2.  **A systematic comparison and combination of two complementary projection paradigms.** The paper presents a comprehensive study that frames annotation projection and representation projection as two distinct "views" for cross-lingual transfer. It empirically demonstrates that these methods have different characteristics—annotation projection often yields high precision but lower recall, while representation projection can be more balanced. The paper then proposes simple but effective co-decoding schemes to combine these complementary strengths.
3.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new state-of-the-art baseline.** The proposed combined system is thoroughly evaluated on both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL benchmark datasets. It achieves significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods like those of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), providing a strong new baseline for the field.

### Strengths

1.  **The data selection scheme is practical and highly effective.** The core idea of using the frequency of a projected (entity, tag) pair as a proxy for its quality is intuitive and well-motivated. It cleverly exploits the redundancy available in large comparable corpora to mitigate noise from both the source NER system and the alignment model. The results in Table 2 are compelling evidence of its success, showing a dramatic +20 F1 point increase for Korean, where alignment quality was lowest. This is a valuable, practical technique for anyone using annotation projection.

2.  **The dual-approach framework and co-decoding are well-conceived.** Rather than advocating for a single method, the authors intelligently leverage the strengths of two orthogonal approaches. The analysis of the precision/recall trade-offs of the annotation projection (AP) and representation projection (RP) systems is insightful. The rank-based co-decoding scheme, which prioritizes the high-precision AP system and uses the RP system to fill in gaps, is a direct and logical consequence of this analysis and proves to be very effective. This demonstrates a thoughtful approach to system building.

3.  **The evaluation is extensive and convincing.** The paper's claims are backed by rigorous experiments across a wide range of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and two very different types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house set with over 50 entity types and the standard 4-type CoNLL benchmark. This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the methods. Crucially, the comparison against strong, relevant baselines and the clear outperformance on the CoNLL test data validate the paper's claims of advancing the state of the art.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited technical novelty in the individual projection methods.** While the combination and filtering heuristics are new and effective, the core projection mechanisms themselves are adaptations of existing work. Annotation projection is a well-established paradigm. The representation projection method directly follows the linear transformation approach of Mikolov et al. (2013b), with a minor weighting modification. The neural network models (NN1, NN2) are standard feed-forward networks. The paper's strength lies more in its clever engineering and empirical insights than in fundamental algorithmic innovation.

2.  **Lack of deeper analysis into model behaviors and choices.** The paper presents strong results but misses opportunities for deeper insights.
    *   The difference in performance between the rank-based and confidence-based co-decoding schemes across datasets (rank-based is better for in-house, confidence-based for CoNLL) is noted but not explained. Is this due to the number of entity types (50+ vs 4), which might make confidence scores from the MEMM/NN models less comparable? A discussion of this would strengthen the paper.
    *   The claim that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance" for representation projection is presented without evidence or analysis. An ablation study on dictionary size would be necessary to substantiate this and provide guidance for future work.
    *   The justification for using an MEMM instead of a CRF is based on the large number of entity types (50+) in the in-house data, which is reasonable. However, this justification does not apply to the CoNLL data (4 types), where a CRF would be computationally feasible and is the more standard, powerful model. The use of an MEMM in the CoNLL experiments is not justified and may be a suboptimal choice.

3.  **Insufficient detail for reproducibility.** Certain aspects of the methodology are described too vaguely. The "smoothing layer" in the NN2 architecture is a key difference from NN1, but its description is unclear. It is described as returning a "weighted average of these prototype vectors", but the mechanism for learning these prototypes and computing the weights is not specified. Is this related to prototypical networks or another established method? Without a more formal definition or reference, this component is not reproducible.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed, perhaps mathematical, description of the "smoothing layer" in your NN2 model? Specifically, how are the prototype vectors initialized and learned during training, and how is the weighted average computed?

2.  You justify the use of an MEMM for your in-house dataset due to the large number of entity types. Could you explain why an MEMM was also used for the CoNLL experiments, where the number of types is small (4) and a linear-chain CRF would be both computationally tractable and is generally considered to perform better due to avoiding the label bias problem?

3.  You observed that different co-decoding schemes worked best for the different datasets (rank-based for in-house, confidence-based for CoNLL). Do you have a hypothesis for why this is the case? Could it be related to the granularity of the entity type set, the characteristics of the base models trained on those datasets, or another factor?
