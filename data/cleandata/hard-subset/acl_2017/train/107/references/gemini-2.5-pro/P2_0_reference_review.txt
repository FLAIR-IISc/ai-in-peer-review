### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where no annotated data is available in the target language. The authors propose and evaluate two primary projection-based approaches and a method for combining them.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **An effective ensemble method for cross-lingual NER:** The paper's strongest contribution is demonstrating that two distinct, weakly supervised views—instance-level annotation projection (AP) and type-level representation projection (RP)—are complementary. The proposed co-decoding schemes, though simple, effectively leverage the different precision/recall characteristics of the individual models to achieve state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (CoNLL 2002/03).
2.  **A comprehensive evaluation of representation projection for NER:** The paper presents a direct model transfer approach for NER based on projecting target language word embeddings into a source language space. While cross-lingual embedding projection is not a new concept, this work provides a solid application and evaluation of it for the NER task, showing it to be a competitive standalone method.
3.  **A practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection:** The authors propose an intuitive and effective method for filtering noisy data generated by annotation projection. By scoring projected sentences based on entity-tag frequency consistency and entity density, they show significant improvements, especially when alignment quality is low.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed combined system achieves impressive results, outperforming two strong, contemporary baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL test data for Spanish, Dutch, and German. This is a clear and significant result that pushes the state-of-the-art in this area.
2.  **Thorough Evaluation Across Diverse Datasets:** The authors evaluate their methods on both the standard, coarse-grained CoNLL dataset and a fine-grained in-house dataset with over 50 entity types. This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of their approach across different languages and task granularities.
3.  **Well-Motivated Combination of Complementary Methods:** The paper provides a clear analysis of the differing behaviors of the two projection systems (AP having high precision/low recall vs. RP being more balanced). This observation provides a strong motivation for the co-decoding schemes, which are designed to harness the strengths of both. This demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the problem space.
4.  **Clarity and Simplicity of Methods:** The proposed methods are elegant, well-explained, and relatively simple to implement. The data selection heuristic for AP is intuitive, and the representation projection approach avoids the need for retraining on new languages, making it highly practical and scalable.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination and application are effective, the underlying techniques are largely adaptations of existing work. Annotation projection is a well-established method, and cross-lingual word embedding mapping was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b). Furthermore, the sequence labeling models used (CRF, MEMM, simple Feedforward NN) are somewhat dated; state-of-the-art NER models at the time of publication often used recurrent architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF), which are not explored here.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important methodological decisions lack clear motivation.
    *   The choice of a 2nd-order MEMM for the target-language AP model is justified by stating that CRFs are too slow for large datasets. This is a strong claim that needs more evidence or discussion, as efficient CRF toolkits exist.
    *   The NN2 architecture includes a "smoothing layer" with "prototype vectors," but the paper offers no theoretical justification for its design, only an empirical observation that it balances precision and recall.
    *   The paper states that using more than 5-6K word pairs for training the embedding mapping harms performance. This is a crucial and counter-intuitive finding that is mentioned without any analysis or hypothesis as to why more supervision would be detrimental.
3.  **Reproducibility Challenges:** A significant portion of the analysis, particularly for the data selection scheme, is performed on a proprietary in-house dataset. The parallel corpora used for both AP and for learning the embedding mappings are not described in sufficient detail (e.g., source, domain, size) to allow for full reproduction of the experiments. This makes it difficult for other researchers to build upon or directly compare with this work.
4.  **Unclear Comparison Conditions:** It is not specified whether the competing methods from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) were run using the same external resources (e.g., parallel and monolingual corpora) as the proposed method. The proposed AP method, for instance, is trained on millions of projected tokens. A fair comparison requires ensuring all systems have access to the same amount and type of data, which is not guaranteed or discussed in the paper.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  For the representation projection approach, did you experiment with using a more powerful neural architecture, such as a BiLSTM-CRF, as the source-language NER model? Given the success of such models in monolingual NER, it seems like a natural choice that could potentially capture target-language sequence dependencies more effectively after projection.
2.  Could you please elaborate on the intuition behind the "smoothing layer" in the NN2 model? How are the prototype vectors learned, and what is the underlying mechanism by which this layer helps to balance precision and recall?
3.  Your finding that larger dictionaries for learning the embedding mapping hurt performance is very interesting. Do you have a hypothesis for this? Could it be related to an increase in noisy or ambiguous translations from the phrase table as the frequency threshold is lowered?
4.  To aid in reproducibility and ensure a fair comparison, could you please specify the source and size of the parallel corpora used for the CoNLL experiments (for both annotation projection and learning the embedding mapping)?
