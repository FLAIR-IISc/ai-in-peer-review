### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without annotated data by leveraging resources from a source language (English). The authors propose and combine two distinct projection-based approaches:

1.  **Annotation Projection (AP):** This method projects NER tags from an English NER system onto aligned sentences in a target language. The primary contribution here is a novel heuristic data selection scheme that filters the noisy, automatically-labeled target data to retain higher-quality samples for training. This scheme scores sentences based on the consistency of entity-label projections across the corpus.
2.  **Representation Projection (RP):** This method involves direct model transfer. An NER model is trained on English word embeddings. For a target language, word embeddings are projected into the English embedding space via a learned linear transformation. The English NER model can then be applied directly to these projected representations without retraining.
3.  **Co-Decoding:** The paper proposes two schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, leveraging their complementary strengths to produce a final, more accurate output.

From my perspective, the main contributions are, in order of strength:

1.  The effective combination of two complementary views of the cross-lingual transfer problem—symbolic label projection (AP) and distributional representation projection (RP). The proposed co-decoding schemes, though simple, demonstrate significant empirical gains and lead to state-of-the-art results.
2.  The heuristic data selection method for annotation projection. This is a simple but highly effective technique for noise reduction in projected data, showing dramatic improvements in low-resource settings where alignment quality is poor.
3.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation on both fine-grained, in-house datasets and standard CoNLL benchmarks, demonstrating the robustness and superiority of the proposed combined system over strong, recent baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper's strongest point is its empirical success. The combined system consistently outperforms strong, published baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the standard CoNLL test sets (Table 5). This is a clear and significant result that pushes the state of the art in this important area.

2.  **Effective and Complementary System Combination:** The paper presents a well-designed system that intelligently combines two orthogonal approaches. Annotation projection relies on high-quality alignments and a source-language tagger, while representation projection relies on the quality of cross-lingual embeddings. The authors correctly identify the different error profiles of the two systems (AP: high precision, RP: better recall) and design a simple rank-based co-decoding scheme that effectively exploits this complementarity. The consistent improvements from co-decoding (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house data) validate this design.

3.  **High-Impact Data Selection Heuristic:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection is simple but powerful. The results in Table 2 are striking: for Japanese and Korean, where word alignment quality is low, this filtering method boosts F1 scores by +13.9 and +20.0 points, respectively. This demonstrates a practical and effective way to handle the noise that plagues projection-based methods, making the approach much more viable in real-world scenarios with imperfect resources.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on two very different types of data: in-house data with a large, fine-grained tagset (>50 types) and the standard CoNLL data with 4 coarse types. The fact that the methods perform well in both settings demonstrates their robustness and general applicability. The inclusion of supervised baselines provides important context for the performance of the weakly supervised models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty in Core Components:** The primary weakness is that the two core approaches, AP and RP, are based on well-established ideas. Annotation projection via parallel corpora is a classic method, and direct model transfer via cross-lingual embedding projection has been explored for other NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, POS tagging) following the work of Mikolov et al. (2013b). The paper's main novelty lies in the specific heuristics for data selection and co-decoding, and the successful application and combination of these ideas for NER. While the engineering and empirical results are strong, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new cross-lingual transfer paradigm.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Empirical Findings:** The paper presents several interesting results but often stops short of providing a deep analysis. For instance:
    *   The authors state that for RP, "larger dictionaries might harm the performance," leading them to use only 5-6K word pairs. This is a counter-intuitive and important finding that warrants further investigation. Is this due to noise in the phrase table for less frequent words? Does it cause overfitting in the transformation matrix? A deeper analysis would be very insightful.
    *   The NN2 architecture outperforms NN1 on the in-house data, while the reverse is true for the CoNLL data. The paper does not comment on or analyze this discrepancy. Does the smoothing layer in NN2 help specifically with larger, more confusable tagsets, or is there another factor at play?
    *   The data selection heuristic, while effective, could potentially amplify biases in the source NER system or alignment model. An error analysis discussing its failure modes would strengthen the contribution.

3.  **Reliance on Heuristics and Manual Tuning:** The data selection scheme depends on two manually tuned thresholds (`q` and `n`), and the co-decoding is rule-based. While empirically effective, these components are not learned in a principled, data-driven manner. A more integrated approach, for instance, learning a model to combine the two views or framing the data selection in a more probabilistic way, could be more elegant and potentially more robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the finding that larger dictionaries (beyond 5-6K pairs) harm the performance of representation projection? What is your hypothesis for why this occurs, and did you perform any analysis to confirm it (e.g., by examining the quality of the learned mapping or the impact of noisy word pairs)?

2.  You observed that the NN2 architecture (with smoothing) was superior for your in-house, fine-grained NER task, while the simpler NN1 was better for the CoNLL task. Do you have an explanation for this performance difference across datasets? Does the smoothing layer provide a regularizing effect that is more beneficial for larger, sparser label sets?

3.  The rank-based co-decoding scheme is motivated by the observation that AP has high precision and RP has better recall. Is this pattern consistent across all languages and datasets you tested? How sensitive is the performance of the rank-based scheme if this P/R characteristic does not hold for a new language pair?
