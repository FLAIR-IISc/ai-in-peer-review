### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents two weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), designed for scenarios with no annotated data in the target language. The first method, based on annotation projection (AP), uses aligned corpora to project NER tags from a source language (English) to a target language. Its main novelty lies in a heuristic data selection scheme to filter out noisy projections based on a confidence score derived from entity-tag frequencies. The second method, based on representation projection (RP), trains an NER model on English word embeddings and then applies it directly to target language text by learning a linear mapping from the target to the source embedding space. Finally, the authors propose two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems. The authors evaluate their methods on both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL benchmark, demonstrating that their combined system outperforms previous state-of-the-art approaches.

The primary contribution of this work is a strong, empirically-driven system that effectively combines two distinct paradigms of cross-lingual transfer to achieve state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark. A secondary contribution is the heuristic for filtering noisy projected annotations, which proves effective, particularly for low-quality alignments.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results on Standard Benchmarks:** The proposed combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2002/2003 NER test sets for Spanish, Dutch, and German, outperforming the systems of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). This provides a clear and significant result, establishing a new strong baseline for this task.

2.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Diverse Conditions:** The authors validate their methods on two very different types of datasets: the standard CoNLL data with 4 entity types, and a proprietary in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types. Evaluation across seven target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) demonstrates the general applicability and robustness of the proposed framework.

3.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Approaches:** The paper successfully demonstrates that annotation projection and representation projection offer complementary strengths (e.g., high precision for AP, balanced precision/recall for RP). The proposed co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based one, effectively leverage these differences to produce a combined output superior to either individual system, with F1 gains of up to 6.2 points on the in-house data. This highlights the value of multi-view learning in this setting.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Methodological Rigor and Justification:** Several key methodological choices lack sufficient justification or detail, hindering reproducibility and casting doubt on the robustness of the findings.
    *   **Data Selection Hyperparameters:** The thresholds `q` and `n` for the annotation projection data selection scheme are critical. The paper states, "we have tried different threshold parameters q and n and reported the best numbers in Table 2." This implies tuning directly on the test set for the in-house experiments, as no development set is mentioned. This is a serious methodological flaw that may lead to overestimated performance.
    *   **Representation Projection Hyperparameters:** The choice of 800 dimensions for target language embeddings is attributed to Mikolov et al. (2013b) but feels arbitrary without specific ablation or sensitivity analysis. More critically, the claim that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance" is presented without any supporting experimental evidence. Such a counter-intuitive finding requires strong justification.
    *   **Circular Logic in Quality Score:** The quality score `q(y)` for a sentence is derived from a frequency table `T` that is built from the entire set of (noisy) projected data. This creates a potential circularity where common errors could be reinforced, leading to systematically biased data selection. The dynamics of this process are not analyzed.

2.  **Confounding Variables in Model Comparison:** The experimental design makes it difficult to isolate the source of performance differences. The Annotation Projection (AP) approach uses a 2nd-order MEMM, while the Representation Projection (RP) approach uses a neural network. The rationale provided (footnote 2) explains why MEMMs were chosen over CRFs, but not why the same neural network model used for RP was not also applied to the AP-generated data. This architectural difference is a significant confounding variable, making it unclear whether performance differences between AP and RP stem from the projection method itself or the underlying sequence labeling model (MEMM vs. NN).

3.  **Insufficient Explanation of Novel Components:** The paper introduces novel components without adequate description. The NN2 architecture includes a "smoothing layer" with "prototype vectors," but the number of vectors, their initialization, the learning mechanism, and the theoretical motivation for this layer are entirely omitted. The claim that it "tends to have a more balanced precision and recall" is an empirical observation stated without analysis or explanation. This lack of detail makes the contribution of this architectural novelty impossible to assess or reproduce.

4.  **Superficial Analysis of Results:** The paper presents many results but lacks a deep analysis of *why* they occur. For instance, the performance gap between weakly supervised and fully supervised systems is much larger for the fine-grained in-house data than for CoNLL data. This is likely a key finding related to the difficulty of transferring fine-grained semantics, but it is not discussed. Similarly, the observation that NN1 outperforms NN2 on CoNLL data while the reverse is true on in-house data is noted but not explored.

5.  **Lack of Polish and Precision:** The paper suffers from numerous minor but distracting issues in presentation.
    *   Phrasing such as "in a smart way" appears multiple times (abstract, Section 5, conclusion) and is unscientific.
    *   Formatting is inconsistent (e.g., list markers in Section 3, citation styles for `et al.`).
    *   Technical descriptions can be imprecise. For example, footnote 1 contains a confusing typo (`l1` instead of `l`), and footnote 2 states that CRF training time "grows at least quadratically," which is imprecise hedging. These small issues accumulate and detract from the overall quality of the submission.
