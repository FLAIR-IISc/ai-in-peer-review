### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without annotated data. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct projection-based approaches and show that combining them yields state-of-the-art performance.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel "representation projection" framework for direct model transfer.** This is the paper's most significant and boldest idea. Instead of projecting discrete annotations or engineering universal features, the authors learn a linear mapping from a target language's word embedding space to a source language's space. An NER model trained solely on source language embeddings can then be directly applied to the projected target language embeddings. This is an elegant and powerful conceptual shift for cross-lingual transfer.
2.  **An effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** The paper introduces a simple but highly effective method to filter noisy training instances generated by traditional annotation projection. By calculating a quality score based on the label consistency of projected entities, the method dramatically improves the performance of the resulting NER model, particularly when word alignments are poor.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance:** The core idea of representation projection is a major strength. It reframes the cross-lingual transfer problem in a clean and principled way within the vector space model paradigm. Moving the projection from the label space (annotation projection) or feature space (previous direct transfer methods) to the representation space itself is a compelling and forward-thinking approach. This is exactly the kind of bold, high-level idea that can inspire new research directions.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The proposed methods, particularly the combined system, achieve state-of-the-art results on the standard CoNLL benchmarks, outperforming prior work by a noticeable margin. The evaluation is thorough, covering multiple languages and two distinct types of NER datasets (fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL), which demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the approaches.

3.  **Demonstrated Practical Impact:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection provides a powerful and practical solution to a well-known problem. The reported improvements of +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean are massive and show that this simple heuristic can turn a barely-usable projection-based system into a highly effective one, especially in challenging low-alignment-quality scenarios.

4.  **Simplicity as a Virtue:** The methods proposed are surprisingly simple. The representation projection uses a basic linear map, the data selection is a straightforward heuristic, and the co-decoding schemes are simple rules. Yet, this simple toolkit leads to SOTA performance. This suggests that the underlying concepts are powerful and do not require overly complex machinery to be effective, which is a significant plus.

### Weaknesses

While I am very positive about this paper, there are areas where it could be strengthened. I view these as minor limitations in the context of the paper's novel contributions, rather than fundamental flaws.

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Representation Projection:** The paper convincingly shows *that* representation projection works, but offers little insight into *why* it works so well or what its failure modes are. A qualitative analysis would have been highly beneficial. For instance, visualizing how certain entity types (e.g., GPEs in German, Spanish, and English) cluster in the shared space after projection would provide valuable intuition. How sensitive is the performance to the size and quality of the seed dictionary used for learning the mapping? Such analysis would elevate the paper from a strong empirical result to a deeper scientific contribution.

2.  **Somewhat Dated Base Models:** The NER architectures used (CRF, MEMM, and a simple feedforward neural network) are not state-of-the-art for sequence labeling, even for the time of writing. While the focus is rightly on the cross-lingual transfer mechanisms, it leaves open the question of how these projection techniques interact with more powerful and contextualized models like BiLSTMs. One could argue that direct embedding projection is less critical if a powerful contextual encoder can smooth over minor projection errors, or conversely, that better encoders might be more sensitive to projection noise. I do not see this as a reason for rejection, as the current results are strong, but it limits the scope of the conclusions.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of Combination:** The co-decoding schemes are simple, effective heuristics. However, the choice between the rank-based and confidence-based scheme seems to be dataset-dependent and requires a development set for tuning. The rank-based scheme relies on an observation about the precision/recall profiles of the two systems that may not generalize to all languages or domains. A more principled combination method or a discussion of the trade-offs would have been welcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The representation projection idea is very compelling. Do you have any intuition on how its effectiveness might change when paired with more powerful, context-aware NER architectures like BiLSTM-CRFs? Does the projection of individual word embeddings become less critical when the model can learn rich contextual representations on its own?

2.  Could you provide some error analysis for the representation projection method? Are the errors primarily due to imperfect word-level projection (e.g., OOV words, poor dictionary mapping for certain word types), or are they more related to structural/syntactic differences between languages (e.g., word order) that the simple projection of context windows cannot capture?
