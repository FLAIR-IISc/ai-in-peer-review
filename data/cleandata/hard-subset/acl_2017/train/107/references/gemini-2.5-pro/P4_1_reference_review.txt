### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where no annotated data is available in the target language. The authors propose and evaluate a framework that combines two distinct projection-based approaches. The first approach, based on annotation projection, transfers NER tags from a source language (English) to a target language using aligned corpora. The second approach, based on representation projection, trains a single NER model on source language word embeddings and applies it directly to the target language by projecting target word embeddings into the source language's vector space. The outputs of these two systems, which exhibit different precision/recall characteristics, are then combined using co-decoding schemes to produce a final, more accurate output. The combined system is evaluated on seven target languages across in-house and public CoNLL datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance compared to previous projection-based methods.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **An effective framework for combining complementary cross-lingual transfer methods.** The paper demonstrates that annotation projection and representation projection have complementary strengths (the former being high-precision, the latter more balanced) and that a simple co-decoding scheme can successfully leverage these differences to achieve significant performance gains over either individual system. This pragmatic system-building contribution is supported by strong empirical results.
2.  **A practical data selection heuristic to improve annotation projection.** The authors introduce a simple but effective method for filtering noisy training instances generated through annotation projection. By scoring sentences based on the consistency of projected labels and the number of entities, the method significantly improves the quality of the weakly labeled data, leading to large F1 score improvements, especially for language pairs with low-quality word alignments.
3.  **A thorough empirical evaluation of projection-based methods for cross-lingual NER.** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental study across a diverse set of languages and two distinct NER tasks (one with 50+ fine-grained entity types, one with 4 coarse types). This evaluation provides strong evidence for the proposed framework's effectiveness and establishes a solid baseline for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Results.** The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. The final combined system consistently outperforms the individual projection methods across all evaluated languages. Crucially, it achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets, surpassing previously published work by Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) by a clear margin (e.g., +4.6 F1 for Spanish, +7.6 F1 for German over Täckström et al.). This demonstrates a tangible advancement in the field.
2.  **Well-Motivated Combination of Complementary Approaches.** The paper cleverly combines two orthogonal approaches to cross-lingual transfer. Annotation Projection works in the label space, while Representation Projection works in the feature/representation space. The authors correctly identify that these methods produce different types of errors and have different precision/recall profiles. The design of the co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based one, is well-justified by this observation and proves highly effective in practice, yielding improvements of up to 6.2 F1 points over the best individual system.
3.  **Effective and Simple Method for Handling Projection Noise.** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection directly addresses a critical weakness of this method: its sensitivity to alignment errors. The heuristic is intuitive, easy to implement, and shown to be highly effective. The results in Table 2 are compelling, with a +20 F1 point gain for Korean and a +13.9 F1 point gain for Japanese, demonstrating that this simple filtering step can turn a low-performing baseline into a competitive system when dealing with noisy alignments.
4.  **Clarity and Thoroughness.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The methods are described with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is transparent. The evaluation on both a fine-grained, in-house dataset and a standard, coarse-grained public dataset adds to the robustness of the conclusions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of Individual Components.** While the overall framework and its empirical success are significant, the core technical components are adaptations of existing work. Annotation projection is a long-established technique for cross-lingual transfer. Representation projection via learning a linear map between embedding spaces was popularized by Mikolov et al. (2013b) and has been applied to several other NLP tasks. The novelty thus lies not in inventing new projection algorithms but in the specific application to NER, the data selection heuristic, and the effective combination of these methods.
2.  **Lack of Analysis on Data Selection Hyperparameters.** The data selection heuristic introduces two important hyperparameters: the quality threshold `q` and the entity number threshold `n`. The paper reports results using the best-performing values for these parameters but provides no analysis of their sensitivity or a principled method for setting them. It is unclear how these were tuned (e.g., on a development set) and how performance degrades with suboptimal choices. A sensitivity analysis would significantly strengthen the claims regarding this heuristic's robustness and practical utility.
3.  **Insufficient Error Analysis.** The evaluation is based entirely on aggregate metrics (Precision, Recall, F1). A more detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights. For instance, what are the primary sources of error for each projection method? Why does the neural network architecture NN1 outperform NN2 on CoNLL data, while the reverse is true for the in-house data? Understanding these behaviors would offer a deeper understanding of the models' strengths and weaknesses beyond their combined performance.
4.  **Absence of Comparison with Multilingual Pre-trained Models.** The paper compares its methods against projection-based work from 2012 and 2016. However, it omits discussion or comparison with the now-dominant paradigm of zero-shot transfer using large multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., mBERT). While this work may have been developed concurrently, such models represent a powerful and highly relevant baseline for zero-shot cross-lingual tasks. Contextualizing the proposed approach against this class of models would be necessary to accurately position its contribution in the current research landscape.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The data selection scheme's thresholds (`q` and `n`) appear crucial for the performance of the Annotation Projection system. Could you please clarify how these hyperparameters were tuned for each language? Was a held-out development set from the target language used, which would violate the "no human annotation" assumption, or was another heuristic employed?
2.  The relative performance of the two neural network architectures, NN1 and NN2, is inconsistent across the two datasets. NN2, with its smoothing layer, is superior on the fine-grained in-house data, while the simpler NN1 is better on the coarse-grained CoNLL data. Do you have a hypothesis for this behavior? Could it be related to the size of the label set (50+ vs. 4) or other domain characteristics?
3.  In Section 4.2, you mention that using larger seed dictionaries for learning the cross-lingual embedding mapping "might harm the performance." This is a counter-intuitive finding, as larger, high-quality dictionaries are typically assumed to improve mapping quality. Could you provide more details on this observation or a potential explanation for this phenomenon?
