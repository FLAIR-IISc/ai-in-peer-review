**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without annotated data. The authors propose a system that combines two distinct projection-based approaches. The first is an annotation projection (AP) method, which projects labels from a source-language NER system to a target language via aligned corpora. The main contribution here is a heuristic data selection scheme to filter the resulting noisy annotations based on projection frequency and entity count. The second approach is representation projection (RP), where a source-language NER model trained on word embeddings is directly applied to target-language words after their embeddings are mapped into the source language's vector space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the high-precision AP system and the higher-recall RP system.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a simple, heuristic-based data selection method can significantly improve the quality of standard annotation projection, particularly when alignment quality is low.
2.  A comprehensive evaluation of a combined system (AP + RP + Co-decoding) across seven languages and two different NER datasets, showing that this combination of established techniques can serve as a very strong baseline, outperforming prior state-of-the-art systems on the CoNLL benchmark.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its empirical results. The final combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard CoNLL 2002/2003 cross-lingual NER task, outperforming the systems of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). This provides the community with a new, strong, and reproducible baseline for this important task.
2.  **Effective and Practical Data Selection:** The proposed data selection heuristic for annotation projection, while simple, is shown to be highly effective. The improvements reported in Table 2 for Japanese (+13.9 F1) and Korean (+20.0 F1) are substantial and demonstrate the practical value of filtering noisy projected data, a common problem in this domain. This is a useful contribution for practitioners working with annotation projection.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods on a diverse set of target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and on two distinct types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard 4-type CoNLL benchmark. This breadth of evaluation lends significant credibility to the findings and demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed combination.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Outdated Choice of Neural Model:** A significant methodological weakness is the choice of a simple feedforward neural network for the representation projection component. The paper cites Lample et al. (2016), yet opts for a much simpler, locally-normalized model reminiscent of Collobert et al. (2011), instead of the then-standard BiLSTM-CRF architecture. It is well-established that globally normalized sequence models like CRFs (or BiLSTM-CRFs) are better suited for NER than locally normalized models (like the MEMM-style NN used here) due to the label bias problem. This choice of a suboptimal architecture makes it difficult to assess the true potential of the representation projection approach and weakens the paper's methodological contribution.
2.  **Limited Novelty in Core Methods:** The paper's core components—annotation projection, representation projection via embedding mapping, and ensemble decoding—are all well-established techniques. The representation projection method is a direct application of the mapping technique from Mikolov et al. (2013b). The co-decoding schemes are simple heuristics based on confidence scores or observed precision/recall trade-offs. The main novelty lies in the data selection heuristic and the specific combination of these parts. While the engineering is successful, the paper lacks a core, novel methodological contribution that advances our fundamental understanding of cross-lingual transfer.
3.  **Lack of Rigorous Analysis for Heuristics:** The proposed data selection scheme is governed by two hyperparameters, `q` (quality threshold) and `n` (entity count threshold). The paper reports results for the best-performing settings without any discussion of how these were tuned or how sensitive the model's performance is to their values. A robust validation of a new heuristic requires a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate that its effectiveness is not confined to a narrow, difficult-to-find parameter range. Without this, the reproducibility and generalizability of the data selection method are questionable.
4.  **Unclear Rationale for NN2 Architecture:** The paper introduces a second neural network, NN2, with a "smoothing layer." The motivation and mechanism for this layer are not clearly explained, and its empirical contribution is inconsistent: it helps on the in-house dataset but hurts performance on the CoNLL dataset. This suggests the component is not well-understood and its inclusion feels ad-hoc, detracting from the paper's overall methodological clarity.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a justification for using a simple, locally-normalized feedforward network for the representation projection model instead of a more powerful and standard BiLSTM-CRF architecture? Given that the latter is a much stronger baseline for NER, it would be important to understand if there was a specific reason for this choice.
2.  The effectiveness of your data selection scheme for annotation projection appears to be a key result. Could you provide more detail on the tuning process for the `q` and `n` thresholds? A brief sensitivity analysis, perhaps in the appendix, showing how performance changes as you vary these thresholds around the optimal values would greatly strengthen this contribution.
3.  The NN1 and NN2 models exhibit opposing performance trends across the two experimental setups (in-house vs. CoNLL). Do you have an explanation for this discrepancy? Could it be related to the significant difference in the size of the label set (50+ vs. 4)?
