**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), where no annotated data is available in the target language. The authors propose and evaluate two primary approaches. The first, Annotation Projection (AP), projects NER labels from an English source to a target language via aligned corpora and introduces a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections based on entity-tag frequency scores. The second, Representation Projection (RP), trains an English NER model on word embeddings and directly applies it to target language text by projecting the target embeddings into the English space via a learned linear map. Finally, the paper proposes two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems. Experiments on in-house and CoNLL datasets show that the combined systems outperform previous state-of-the-art methods.

**Contributions**
1.  An empirical demonstration that combining two distinct views of cross-lingual transfer—annotation projection and representation projection—yields state-of-the-art results for weakly supervised NER. The strength of the work lies in this comprehensive evaluation and combination.
2.  A simple data selection heuristic for annotation projection that provides substantial improvements, particularly in settings with low-quality word alignments.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper tackles a well-motivated and significant problem: enabling NER for new languages without the expensive cost of manual annotation. The proposed approaches are practical and do not require extensive engineering of language-specific features.
2.  The core idea of combining two fundamentally different projection paradigms (symbolic label projection vs. continuous representation projection) is sound. These methods have different error profiles, and the results convincingly show that their combination, via simple co-decoding, is more effective than either approach in isolation.
3.  The evaluation is extensive, covering seven target languages across two distinct datasets: a fine-grained (50+ entity types) in-house dataset and a standard coarse-grained (4 types) CoNLL benchmark. This demonstrates the general applicability of the methods and provides a strong set of baselines. The reported results outperform prior published work on the CoNLL test set.

**Weaknesses**
Despite its sound high-level ideas and strong empirical results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological and presentational flaws. The lack of crucial details and rigorous analysis undermines the scientific contribution.

1.  **Critical Methodological Details are Missing, Impeding Reproducibility.** The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, NN2, which features a "smoothing layer." The description of this layer is entirely insufficient: "computes the cosine similarity between a word embedding and a fixed set of prototype vectors (learned during training) and returns a weighted average of these prototype vectors as the input." This description raises more questions than it answers. What are these prototypes? How many are there? How are they initialized and learned? Without these details, the NN2 model is a black box and cannot be reproduced or properly assessed. This is a major weakness. Similarly, the authors mention using a "variant of the CBOW word2vec model" with a custom distance-based weighting, but provide no citation or further details.

2.  **Reliance on Poorly Justified Heuristics and Lack of Ablation.** Several key components of the methodology are presented as simple heuristics without adequate justification or analysis.
    *   The data selection scheme (Eq. 6) relies on thresholds `q` and `n` for which only the "best numbers" are reported. This constitutes tuning on the test set. A proper evaluation would involve a development set for tuning or at least a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters.
    *   The claim in Section 4.2 that "larger dictionaries might harm the performance" is a significant and counter-intuitive finding. However, it is presented without any empirical evidence, ablation study, or even a plausible hypothesis. It remains an unsubstantiated assertion.
    *   The co-decoding schemes are based on an observation ("AP tends to have a high precision") that is not consistently true across all experiments (e.g., Spanish in Table 4). The choice of which system combination (AP+NN1 vs AP+NN2) and which scheme (Confidence vs Rank) to use seems arbitrary and data-dependent.

3.  **Superficial Analysis of Results.** The experimental results are presented with minimal interpretation. A key finding is that the best-performing system configuration is inconsistent: AP+NN2 (Rank) is optimal for the in-house data, while AP+NN1 (Confidence) is best for the CoNLL data. The paper offers no discussion as to why this might be the case. Is it related to the number of entity types, the domain, or the quality of the alignments? This lack of analysis makes the proposed "solution" feel like a toolkit of ad-hoc methods that require extensive tuning for each new task, rather than a robust, generalizable framework.

4.  **Minor but Numerous Technical and Presentational Inaccuracies.**
    *   Footnote 1 contains a typo: "(yj , yj+1, ..., yj+q) is labeled with (B-l, I-l,...,I-l)1". The trailing subscript '1' appears to be a typo of the entity type 'l'. *Correction*: I see the footnote says "labeled with l1", not "(B-l, ... I-l)1". This is also a typo; it should be `l`.
    *   Footnote 2 claims that "the training time of CRFs grows at least quadratically in the number of entity types." For a standard linear-chain CRF, training complexity is O(T * N * M^2), where M is the number of labels. The claim is imprecise and potentially misleading.
    *   The phrasing "in a smart way" in the abstract and conclusion is unprofessional for a scientific publication.
    *   Citation formatting is inconsistent, with diacritics missing or malformed (e.g., `Vulić`, `Täckström`).

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents promising results on an important task, the accumulation of methodological opaqueness, reliance on unevaluated heuristics, superficial analysis, and minor errors is too substantial to warrant acceptance in its current form. The lack of reproducibility for the NN2 model is a particularly critical flaw. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors must provide the missing methodological details, rigorously analyze the behavior of their heuristic components (e.g., through sensitivity analyses and ablations), and offer deeper insights into why different system configurations excel in different settings.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Please provide a complete and precise description of the "smoothing layer" in the NN2 architecture, including the number of prototype vectors, their initialization, and the learning procedure.
2.  Could you present evidence (e.g., a table or graph) to support your claim that dictionaries with more than 6K word pairs harm representation projection performance for this task?
3.  Why does the optimal combination of systems (AP+NN1 vs AP+NN2) and co-decoding schemes (Rank vs Confidence) change between the in-house and CoNLL datasets? Can you provide any analysis that would help a practitioner choose the right combination for a new language or dataset?
4.  For the data selection scheme in Section 3.1, could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how F1 score changes as the thresholds `q` and `n` are varied around the optimal reported values for one of the languages?
