### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, an **Annotation Projection (AP)** system, projects NER tags from English to a target language via aligned corpora. Its key novelty is a heuristic data selection scheme that filters the projected data based on the consistency of entity-label assignments, significantly improving robustness to alignment noise. The second approach, **Representation Projection (RP)**, trains an embedding-based NER model in English and applies it directly to the target language by projecting target-language word embeddings into the source (English) embedding space. Finally, the paper demonstrates that these two approaches have complementary strengths (AP being high-precision, RP being more balanced) and introduces two co-decoding schemes (rank-based and confidence-based) to combine their outputs, achieving state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong and practical framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.** The primary contribution is not a single algorithm but a complete, well-engineered system that effectively combines two different cross-lingual transfer paradigms (annotation and representation projection). The final co-decoding system serves as a very strong baseline for future work in this area.
2.  **An effective data selection heuristic for robust annotation projection.** The proposed method for scoring and filtering noisy projected sentences is simple, intuitive, and shown to be highly effective, especially for languages with poor-quality alignments. This is a valuable practical technique for improving a widely used cross-lingual transfer method.
3.  **Thorough and convincing empirical evaluation.** The paper's claims are supported by extensive experiments across seven target languages and two very different types of datasets (an in-house dataset with 50+ fine-grained entity types and the standard CoNLL benchmark with 4 types). The strong performance against previous state-of-the-art systems validates the effectiveness of the proposed framework.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Analysis.** The paper achieves new state-of-the-art results on the standard CoNLL benchmark, outperforming strong prior work. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering a diverse set of languages and both coarse- and fine-grained entity sets, which demonstrates the general applicability of the methods. The analysis of the precision/recall trade-offs of the AP and RP systems is insightful and provides a clear motivation for the co-decoding strategy.
2.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Methods.** The core idea of combining annotation and representation projection is very well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that these two methods leverage different resources (parallel vs. monolingual corpora) and have different error profiles. The proposed co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based one derived from the observation that AP has higher precision, are simple, elegant, and demonstrably effective at improving performance over the individual systems.
3.  **Practical Solution to a Key Problem in Annotation Projection.** The data selection heuristic for the AP approach is a significant strength. Projection-based methods are notoriously sensitive to alignment errors, and this work provides a practical and effective way to mitigate this issue. The huge improvements shown in Table 2 for Japanese (+13.9 F1) and Korean (+20.0 F1) are compelling evidence of the heuristic's value.
4.  **Clarity and Organization.** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors describe their methods with sufficient detail to understand the key components and motivations. Design choices, such as using MEMMs over CRFs for scalability reasons, are clearly justified.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components.** While the overall framework is effective, the individual building blocks are adaptations of well-known techniques. Annotation projection, cross-lingual embedding mapping via a seed dictionary (Mikolov et al., 2013b), and neural sequence labeling models are all established methods. The paper's primary novelty lies in the data-selection heuristic and the specific way these components are combined for NER. The work could be strengthened by more clearly positioning itself with respect to the broader cross-lingual transfer literature, which has explored similar projection and combination ideas for other tasks like parsing and POS tagging.
2.  **Dependence on Multiple Resources and Hyperparameters.** The final system is fairly complex, requiring parallel/comparable corpora, large monolingual corpora, and a seed dictionary derived from a phrase table. Its performance also depends on several hyperparameters (e.g., the selection thresholds `q` and `n`, the dictionary size, the choice of co-decoding scheme) that were tuned on held-out data. This might limit the method's applicability in truly low-resource settings and raises questions about how to set these parameters without a development set for the target language.
3.  **Unexplained Differences in Model Performance.** The paper reports that different system configurations work best for different datasets. The NN2 architecture and rank-based co-decoding excel on the in-house data, while the simpler NN1 and confidence-based decoding are better for CoNLL. The paper does not provide a satisfying explanation for this discrepancy. Is it related to the number of entity types (50+ vs. 4), domain differences, or language properties? Without this analysis, it is difficult for a reader to know which configuration to choose for a new application.
4.  **Absence of Qualitative Error Analysis.** The paper presents strong aggregate scores but would benefit greatly from an error analysis. A qualitative discussion of the types of errors each system (AP and RP) makes would provide deeper insight into their complementary nature. For instance, does RP perform better on non-cognate entities? Does AP handle multi-word expressions more robustly due to phrase-based alignment? Understanding what errors are fixed by co-decoding—and which ones persist—would be very valuable for motivating future work.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide some intuition as to why the more complex NN2 architecture and the rank-based co-decoding scheme performed better on the fine-grained in-house dataset, while the simpler NN1 and confidence-based scheme were superior for the coarse-grained CoNLL data? Do you believe this is tied to the tagset size, data domain, or another factor?
2.  The data selection thresholds `(q, n)` for annotation projection are critical to its success, but they were tuned on development data. In a true zero-shot setting where no labeled data is available for the target language, how would you recommend setting these parameters?
3.  You note that using a seed dictionary larger than 5-6K word pairs for the representation projection mapping was detrimental to performance. This is a surprising finding, as one might expect more data to be helpful. Can you elaborate on this? Was this potentially due to an increase in noise or ambiguity in the larger dictionaries?
