### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose a framework that develops and combines two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, an Annotation Projection (AP) method, projects labels from a source-language NER system onto a target language through a comparable corpus. Its key contribution is a novel data selection heuristic that filters the noisy projected data based on annotation consistency, significantly improving performance. The second, a Representation Projection (RP) method, performs direct model transfer. It trains a neural NER model on source-language word embeddings and applies it directly to the target language by learning a linear map to project target-language embeddings into the source vector space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (rank-based and confidence-based) to combine the outputs of these two complementary systems. The proposed framework is evaluated extensively on seven languages across both a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL benchmark datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A comprehensive and effective framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.** The paper's primary contribution is not a single method but a robust framework that successfully integrates two orthogonal approaches: projecting annotations (labels) and projecting representations (embeddings). By thoughtfully combining the outputs of these two systems via co-decoding, the framework leverages their complementary strengths to achieve state-of-the-art results.
2.  **A simple yet highly effective heuristic for filtering noisy projected annotations.** The proposed data selection scheme for the annotation projection method is a significant contribution. It uses entity-tag frequency across the entire projected corpus as a proxy for annotation quality, allowing it to filter out unreliable sentences. This technique proves crucial for making annotation projection viable, yielding massive F1 score improvements (up to 20 points) on languages with noisy word alignments.
3.  **A strong and thorough empirical evaluation.** The authors provide convincing evidence of their framework's effectiveness through extensive experiments on a diverse set of seven target languages and two very different NER tasks (a fine-grained 50+ type set and the standard 4-type CoNLL set). The detailed results, including comparisons to previous state-of-the-art systems, clearly validate the proposed methods.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks.** The proposed combined system establishes a new state of the art on the CoNLL 2002/2003 cross-lingual NER tasks. As shown in Table 5, it outperforms the strong prior systems of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on all three languages, with particularly large gains on German (+8 F1 over Täckström et al.). Achieving such significant improvements on well-established benchmarks is a major strength.
2.  **Well-motivated and effective system components.** Each part of the proposed framework is well-designed and empirically justified. The data selection scheme for AP is a standout example, directly addressing the critical problem of noise in projection-based methods. The results in Table 2 clearly demonstrate its value. Furthermore, the co-decoding schemes are not a simple ensemble; they are motivated by an insightful analysis of the differing precision/recall characteristics of the AP and RP systems, leading to more intelligent combination strategies.
3.  **Thoroughness of the experimental setup and analysis.** The evaluation is a key strength of this paper. Testing on seven languages across two different domains (in-house and CoNLL) demonstrates the approach's generality. The paper presents a clear ablation-like structure, showing results for each individual system (AP, RP-NN1, RP-NN2) and their combinations. This allows the reader to understand the contribution of each component. The analysis is transparent and provides a clear picture of how the different parts of the framework interact.
4.  **Clarity and potential for reproducibility.** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors describe their methods, models (CRF, MEMM, NN architectures), and experimental settings with sufficient detail to facilitate replication.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the core projection techniques.** While the overall framework is novel and effective, the two foundational pillars—annotation projection and representation projection—are established paradigms in cross-lingual transfer learning. Annotation projection is a classic technique, and direct model transfer via cross-lingual embedding mapping has been successfully applied to other NLP tasks like parsing and POS tagging. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly contextualized its work, clarifying why this specific combination and the data selection heuristic are novel and crucial for the NER task.
2.  **High complexity of the final system.** The best-performing model is an ensemble of two separate and complex pipelines. The AP pipeline requires a source NER system, a comparable corpus, a word alignment model, and a target-language model training step. The RP pipeline requires large monolingual corpora, word embedding training, a bilingual dictionary, and a source neural model. This complexity raises questions about the practical deployment and computational cost of the full approach versus a simpler, standalone model. A discussion of this performance-complexity trade-off would be beneficial.
3.  **Insufficient analysis of key heuristics and hyperparameters.** The data selection heuristic for AP is a central contribution, but its analysis is limited. The thresholds `q` and `n` are presented as the "best numbers" (Table 2), which suggests they were tuned on the test set. It is unclear how sensitive the model is to these hyperparameters and how they would be set in a real-world scenario without labeled target data. A more in-depth analysis of what types of sentences or entities are filtered (e.g., those with rare entities vs. common ones) would also strengthen the contribution. Similarly, the finding that dictionaries larger than 5-6K pairs "might harm performance" for RP is counter-intuitive and warrants further investigation.
4.  **Some design choices lack strong justification.** Several modeling decisions appear ad-hoc. For instance, the NN2 architecture includes a "smoothing layer" that helps on in-house data but hurts on CoNLL data. The explanation that it helps balance precision and recall is an observation of its effect, not a justification for its design. What is the intuition behind this layer? Secondly, the choice to use an MEMM for the target-language AP model (citing CRF training time with >50 types) but a CRF for the source model is inconsistent, especially for the CoNLL task which only has 4 entity types, where a CRF would likely be feasible and potentially stronger.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The data selection heuristic in Section 3.1 is highly effective, but the selection of thresholds `(q, n)` is critical. Could you clarify how these would be tuned in a realistic setting where no labeled target data is available? Furthermore, how sensitive is the performance to variations in these thresholds?
2.  The rank-based co-decoding scheme is motivated by the observation that the AP system generally exhibits high precision. However, on the CoNLL data (Table 4), the RP system (NN1) has higher precision than the AP system for Dutch and German. This seems to align with the finding that the confidence-based scheme performed better on CoNLL. Does this imply that the choice of co-decoding strategy should be adapted based on the observed P/R characteristics of the base models on a small, unlabeled target sample?
3.  The final system combines an MEMM (from AP) and a neural network (from RP). Have you considered using a consistent model architecture for both streams? For instance, could the filtered projected annotations from the AP pipeline be used as weak supervision to train a neural network, potentially allowing for more seamless integration or joint training with the RP approach?
