**Summary**
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without annotated data. The authors propose and evaluate two primary approaches. The first is an annotation projection (AP) method, which improves upon established techniques by introducing a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections from comparable corpora. The second is a representation projection (RP) approach, a form of direct model transfer where a source-language NER model (trained on word embeddings) is applied to target-language text by first projecting the target-language word embeddings into the source space via a learned linear map. Finally, the paper introduces two simple co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, demonstrating that the combined system outperforms each individual component and achieves new state-of-the-art results on CoNLL benchmark datasets.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an effective and intuitive data selection heuristic for the established annotation projection framework. This method is shown to yield substantial improvements, especially in scenarios with low-quality word alignments, thereby making annotation projection more robust.
2.  The paper presents a thorough empirical comparison of two different cross-lingual transfer paradigms (annotation projection and representation projection) on a wide range of languages and two distinct evaluation settings (a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard CoNLL benchmark).
3.  The work demonstrates that a simple combination of these two orthogonal approaches leads to state-of-the-art performance on standard cross-lingual NER benchmarks, providing a strong and reproducible baseline for future work.

**Strengths**
1.  **Rigorous Improvement on an Established Method:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection is a well-motivated and logical extension to a classic cross-lingual transfer technique. It directly addresses the known issue of projection noise from imperfect alignments. The experiments clearly validate this contribution, showing dramatic F1 score improvements for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), languages for which the alignment quality was reported to be lowest. This is a solid, incremental contribution that improves the practicality of annotation projection.
2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is extensive. The authors evaluate their methods on seven target languages across two very different NER tasks: one with over 50 fine-grained entity types and another with the 4 standard CoNLL types. This demonstrates the generalizability of their findings. The comparison against strong prior work (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and the inclusion of supervised upper bounds provide necessary context and convincingly establish the effectiveness of the proposed combined system.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The final combined system sets a new state-of-the-art on the CoNLL Spanish, Dutch, and German test sets. These are significant improvements over previously published results. This demonstrates the practical value of the work and provides a strong baseline for the community.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While empirically successful, the core ideas behind the representation projection and co-decoding components are not novel. The representation projection approach is a direct application of the cross-lingual embedding mapping technique from Mikolov et al. (2013b), which has been previously applied to other NLP tasks. Framing this as a "new approach for direct NER model transfer" is an overstatement. Similarly, the co-decoding schemes are straightforward heuristics (confidence-based and rank-based) rather than principled combination methods. The paper's strength lies in its careful engineering and evaluation, not in fundamental methodological innovation.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Key Components and Hyperparameters:** The paper lacks a deeper analysis of several important design choices.
    *   The performance of the data selection scheme for annotation projection is only reported for the "best" threshold parameters (`q` and `n`). A sensitivity analysis is needed to understand how robust this heuristic is to the choice of these thresholds.
    *   The choice to use 800-dimensional embeddings for target languages versus 300 for English is asserted but not justified. It is unclear if this was systematically tuned or based on prior work.
    *   The two neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) perform inconsistently across datasets, with NN2 being better for in-house data and NN1 for CoNLL. The paper offers no hypothesis for this behavior, which is critical for understanding the role of the "smoothing layer" in NN2.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Error Analysis:** The paper notes that the AP system tends to have high precision and the RP system has more balanced precision/recall, which motivates the rank-based co-decoding. However, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. What kinds of errors does each system make? Are there systematic differences in the types of entities or contexts that each approach handles well? This would provide stronger justification for the combination strategy and offer more insight than simply observing aggregate precision/recall scores.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a sensitivity analysis for the data selection thresholds `q` and `n`? Showing how the F1 score changes as these parameters are varied would give a better sense of the robustness of the annotation projection improvements.
2.  The performance of the NN1 and NN2 architectures is inconsistent across the in-house (fine-grained) and CoNLL (coarse-grained) datasets. Do you have a hypothesis for why the smoothing layer in NN2 helps in the fine-grained setting but appears to be detrimental in the coarse-grained setting?
3.  In Section 4.2, you mention that larger dictionaries (beyond 5-6K word pairs) "might harm the performance" of representation projection. This is a counter-intuitive finding. Can you elaborate on this observation? Did you investigate why this might be the case?
