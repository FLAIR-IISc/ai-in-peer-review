Summary and contributions
This paper proposes weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual NER that rely on projecting information from English into a target language with no target-side annotation. The authors present: (1) an annotation projection approach on comparable corpora with a simple but effective heuristic for selecting higher-quality projected sentences; (2) a representation projection approach that maps target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space to enable direct model transfer; and (3) two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these systems. The experimental evaluation spans multiple languages and both in-house and CoNLL datasets, and the combined systems outperform two prior cross-lingual NER methods on CoNLL test sets.

As I see it, the primary contribution is empirical: demonstrating that a straightforward data selection heuristic substantially improves projection-based NER under noisy alignments, and that simple combination rules consistently boost accuracy over either component alone. Methodologically, the advances are incremental extensions of existing ideas rather than conceptually novel techniques.

Strengths
- Broad, careful evaluation: Results are reported on several target languages with both in-house data (fine-grained types) and CoNLL datasets (coarse types), and include precision/recall analyses. The improvements from data selection (e.g., large gains for Japanese and Korean where alignments are noisy) are clearly supported by Table 2. The combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual baselines on CoNLL test data.
- Simple, practical methods: The sentence-level quality score based on entity/tag frequency and the linear embedding mapping are straightforward to implement and likely to reproduce. This is valuable for practitioners who need workable cross-lingual NER without target annotations.
- Complementary systems and effective combination: The paper documents that annotation projection tends to be high-precision/low-recall, while representation projection is more balanced, and shows that combining them (especially the rank-based scheme prioritizing annotation projection) yields consistent F1 gains across languages.
- Competitive performance: In-house results sometimes approach supervised baselines (with modest target-language supervision), which underscores the practical impact of the approach in low-resource settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The overall paradigm (annotation projection and cross-lingual embedding mapping) is well known. The proposed contributions—data selection and simple co-decoding rules—feel incremental. For an ACL venue, I would have expected more methodological innovation.
- Insufficient detail on data selection tuning: Table 2 reports results for selected thresholds, but the paper only states that “different thresholds were tried.” It is unclear how q and n were chosen (e.g., via a target-language development set, cross-validation on projected data, or heuristics). Without a clear tuning protocol, reproducibility and portability to new language pairs are uncertain.
- Unclear description of co-decoding: Despite multiple readings, the co-decoding procedures (exclude-O confidence-based vs. rank-based with conflict resolution) remain insufficiently specified for reproduction. For instance, how are entity-level confidence scores computed and compared across systems? How exactly are conflicts defined and resolved step-by-step?
- Method variants and system selection: The paper presents multiple variants (NN1 vs. NN2; confidence- vs. rank-based co-decoding), but the criteria for choosing among them per language/dataset are not clearly articulated. This complicates application to new settings.
- Limited information about the in-house data: The paper would benefit from details on the genres/domains, source composition, and annotation guidelines of the in-house datasets (beyond the note on >50 types), to help interpret generalization and comparability with public benchmarks.

Questions to authors
- Data selection tuning: How were the thresholds (q, n) selected for Table 2? Was there a held-out development set in the target language, or did you use a development split of the projection-labeled data itself? Please specify the protocol so that others can replicate and apply it to new language pairs. Did you observe stability of the chosen thresholds across runs or subsets?
- Co-decoding specification: Could you provide a precise, step-by-step description (or pseudocode) of both co-decoding schemes? In particular:
  - How are token- or span-level confidence scores computed and made comparable across models?
  - In the exclude-O scheme, how are ties and overlaps handled?
  - In the rank-based scheme, what is the exact conflict resolution rule when spans overlap or share boundaries with different labels?
- Combining more than two systems: Since the paper includes multiple weakly supervised variants (e.g., annotation projection, NN1, NN2), did you try combining more than two systems simultaneously? If so, how did performance compare to pairwise combinations? If not, do you anticipate the rank-based strategy extends naturally to more than two inputs?
- In-house datasets: Please describe the text sources (newswire, web, conversational, etc.), domains, and any preprocessing; the distribution of entity types; and whether the English source training data are genre-matched to the in-house target test sets. This context would help interpret the cross-domain robustness of the methods.
