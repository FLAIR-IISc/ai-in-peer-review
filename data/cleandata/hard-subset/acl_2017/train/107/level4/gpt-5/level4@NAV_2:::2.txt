This paper presents an approach to cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER). The authors employ conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network–based NER systems. They further propose two strategies to fuse system outputs (a confidence-based and a rank-based scheme), as well as a heuristic for selecting high-quality training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. The cross-lingual transfer relies on a variant of Mikolov’s embedding projection method. Overall, the paper is readable, well organized, and written in clear English. The combined annotations yield interesting results.

Detailed comments:
I would like to understand the motivation for introducing a modified Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model. The paper provides few details on this component (and on the hyperparameters). Did the standard CBOW (or Continuous Skip-gram) underperform? Please also report results with the unmodified CBOW baseline so readers can assess the gains from your variation. Since you use distance-based decay for context, consider the exponential decay discussed in [1].

Along the same lines, please show a comparison between Mikolov’s original cross-lingual projection and your frequency-weighted mapping. The contribution would be stronger if readers could see clear evidence that your weighting improves over the standard formulation.

“The proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant.” Have you run statistical significance tests? It would be helpful to know whether the observed differences throughout the paper are statistically significant.

I suggest moving the content of Section 4.4 to the beginning of Section 4.2 for better flow. I also recommend relocating the evaluation corresponding to Table 2 to the main evaluation section.

I miss a dedicated related work section. While some of this appears in the introduction, consider splitting the introduction into two parts to separate background/positioning from contributions.

The evaluation is quite brief (about 1.5 pages including the conclusion). Given the state-of-the-art results, a deeper discussion and analysis of the outcomes would be valuable.

Suggested references:
[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (Vol. 1, pp. 897-907).
