Summary and contributions
This paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation. It explores two projection-based approaches and their combination:
- Annotation projection from English to a target language with a simple, frequency-based data selection heuristic to filter noisy projected sentences.
- Representation projection: learn monolingual embeddings, train a small bilingual mapping to project target words into English space, and run an English NER without retraining.
- Two co-decoding schemes to combine the two systems, leveraging complementary precision/recall profiles.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that a simple, language-agnostic selection heuristic can materially improve annotation projection under noisy alignments (e.g., large gains for Japanese and Korean).
2) A practical representation-projection transfer pipeline requiring only a small bilingual dictionary, producing competitive cross-lingual NER without target training.
3) A lightweight co-decoding strategy that consistently improves over either system alone and outperforms prior weakly supervised methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test sets.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across languages and settings. The data selection heuristic yields substantial gains when alignment quality is low, e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean. The best combined systems beat prior cross-lingual NER approaches on CoNLL test data, notably for German, while narrowing the gap to supervised models.
- Complementary methods with clear behavior. The analysis that annotation projection tends to high precision/low recall while representation projection balances precision and recall motivates the rank-based co-decoding and explains where the gains come from.
- Practicality and low resource requirements. The approach avoids target-language annotation, relies on comparable/parallel corpora with alignments and a modest bilingual dictionary (~5–6K word pairs), and reuses a single English NER via representation projection. This makes the methods appealing for new languages.
- Breadth of evaluation. The paper evaluates on both in-house data with a fine-grained label set (>50 types) and standard CoNLL datasets (4 types), across multiple languages, models (MEMM, NN1/NN2), and combination schemes, providing a solid empirical picture.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. Both components are extensions of well-known ideas: annotation projection with quality filtering and linear cross-lingual embedding mappings. The main novelty lies in a simple selection heuristic and in engineering the combination. For an ACL venue, more principled modeling or learning (e.g., confidence calibration, joint training, or end-to-end co-training) would strengthen the contribution.
- Insufficient detail on tuning the data selection thresholds. Table 2 reports “tried different thresholds” for q and n, but it is unclear how these were chosen without target annotations. Was there a development set per language? If no labeled dev set was used, what criterion guided tuning? How sensitive is performance to q and n, and how transferable are settings across languages/domains?
- Co-decoding procedure is under-specified. The paper mentions “exclude-O” confidence-based and rank-based schemes, but critical implementation details are missing: exact conflict resolution rules for overlapping spans, how tag sequence consistency (e.g., valid IOB) is enforced after merging, whether probability calibration or normalization is applied across models, and the computational procedure (per-span vs per-token vs lattice-level decisions).
- Reproducibility gaps in resource descriptions. The “in-house” datasets (domains, genres, sizes per language, and detailed label inventory) are not sufficiently described. The selection and construction of comparable/parallel corpora and alignment systems, and how alignment quality is measured and leveraged, are only briefly summarized. Without clearer data and resource documentation, replication by others will be challenging.
- Comparisons and ablations could be broader. While the paper compares to two strong prior cross-lingual NER systems, it omits comparisons to other alignment/mapping methods (e.g., Procrustes with orthogonality constraints, joint multilingual embeddings) and deeper ablations (dictionary size, embedding dimensionality, mapping regularization). It would help to know whether combining more than two systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2) yields further gains.

Questions to authors
- How were the thresholds q and n selected for each language in Table 2 and subsequent experiments? Was any labeled target-language development data used? If not, what unsupervised criterion did you optimize? Please include a sensitivity analysis showing F1 versus q and n and discuss transferability across languages.
- Please clarify the co-decoding algorithms with pseudocode: how do you detect and resolve conflicts (overlapping spans, differing types, boundary disagreements)? How do you ensure valid IOB sequences after merging? Do you perform any score calibration or scaling for comparability across models?
- Did you try combining more than two systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2), or ensembling multiple bilingual dictionaries/mappings? If yes, what were the results?
- What are the domains/genres and sizes of the in-house datasets per language? Can you describe the >50-type schema and its overlap with CoNLL types? Are these datasets or trained models planned for release?
- How sensitive is representation projection to dictionary size and embedding dimensionality (300 vs 800), and to the choice of mapping (e.g., weighted vs unweighted least squares, orthogonality constraints)? Please provide ablations.
- How is the “NER-trained unk vector” obtained, and what is its impact on performance for OOVs? What fraction of tokens/entities use it at test time?

Additional comments
- The paper would benefit from a short complexity discussion (training/inference times, memory) for MEMM/CRF and NN models, and for the co-decoding step.
- Minor clarity: Please state explicitly whether the sentence pairs are parallel or comparable per language, how alignments are obtained (sentence vs word level), and whether your selection metric requires word-level alignments.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid empirical paper with practical value and strong results, but the methodological novelty is modest and several key implementation/tuning details are missing. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can clarify threshold tuning and co-decoding, strengthen reproducibility (dataset/resource descriptions), and add ablations to support design choices.
