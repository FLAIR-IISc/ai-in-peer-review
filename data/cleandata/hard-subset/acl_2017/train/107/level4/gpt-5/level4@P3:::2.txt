Summary
This paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) without target-language annotation. It studies two complementary transfer strategies: (1) annotation projection from English to a target language with a heuristic for selecting high-quality projected sentences from noisy comparable corpora; and (2) representation projection that maps target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space via a weighted linear mapping, enabling zero-shot application of an English NER model. The authors further propose two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based with exclude-O and rank-based) to combine the two systems. Experiments on in-house data (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and CoNLL (Spanish, Dutch, German) show that the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple and effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection that substantially improves performance in the presence of noisy alignments, especially for low-alignment-quality language pairs.
2) A practical representation projection approach that uses a small, frequency-weighted bilingual dictionary to learn linear mappings into English embedding space, enabling direct reuse of an English NER model across languages without retraining.
3) Co-decoding strategies that exploit complementary precision/recall of the two systems, yielding consistent gains over either system alone and improving upon prior cross-lingual baselines.

Strengths
- Effective handling of noisy projection data: The proposed sentence-level quality score q(y) and entity count n(y) criteria yield large gains where alignments are weak. Reported improvements are substantial (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean), indicating the selection heuristic is impactful when noise is high.
- Complementary systems and principled combination: The empirical observation that annotation projection has higher precision and lower recall while representation projection is more balanced is leveraged well. The rank-based co-decoding (prioritizing annotation projection) produces the best weakly supervised results on multiple in-house languages, with gains of 2.9–6.2 F1 over the best single system. On CoNLL, the confidence-based variant performs best for some languages.
- Strong empirical coverage and competitiveness: The work evaluates across seven languages and two settings (fine-grained in-house labels and CoNLL PER/ORG/LOC/MISC). The combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test data, closing the gap to supervised models in several cases.
- Practicality and extensibility: The representation projection approach relies on monolingual embeddings and a relatively small bilingual dictionary (~5–6k pairs) with frequency weights. Mapping all languages into English supports a single universal NER model with no target-specific retraining.
- Clarity and organization: The paper clearly explains the models (CRF/MEMM, NN1/NN2), the selection heuristics, mapping procedure, and co-decoding schemes. The rationale for using MEMMs over high-order CRFs for scalability is also well motivated.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablation and methodological analysis: Key design choices are not empirically dissected. In particular: (a) the CBOW variant with distance-based weighting is proposed without reporting results against standard CBOW and skip-gram; (b) the frequency-weighted mapping is not compared to an unweighted least squares mapping; (c) sensitivity to dictionary size/quality and minimal pair count is only anecdotally discussed. These ablations would strengthen claims of methodological contribution.
- Lack of statistical significance testing: Multiple comparisons (e.g., with vs. without selection, co-decoding vs. individual systems, prior baselines) are presented without significance tests, making it hard to assess robustness of improvements.
- Sparse analysis of errors and calibration: There is little qualitative or quantitative error analysis (e.g., boundary vs. type errors, entity length effects, domain differences). Given the emphasis on confidence-based co-decoding, calibration analysis across systems (especially O vs. non-O) would be informative. Learning-based ensembling (e.g., stacked sequence models) is not explored.
- Missing or underspecified details: Important configuration details are light, including: hyperparameters of the CBOW variant (context window, decay function/parameters), embedding training epochs and corpora across languages, size and selection of prototype vectors for NN2, unk-vector training, and tokenization policies for languages with segmentation challenges.
- Paper structure and evaluation depth: Related work on cross-lingual embeddings (Section 4.4) would be more helpful upfront in Section 4.2. Some experimental content (e.g., Table 2) appears before the main evaluation section. The evaluation/discussion is brief relative to the breadth of results; additional analysis (e.g., per-entity-type performance, effect of alignment quality, dictionary coverage) would enhance insights.

Questions to authors
- CBOW variant: What was the motivation and exact formulation of the distance-based weighting? Please report baselines with standard CBOW and skip-gram, and consider exponential decay as in Iacobacci et al. (2016). What improvements (if any) does your variant yield?
- Weighted mapping: How much does frequency weighting in the bilingual dictionary contribute vs. an unweighted mapping? Please include an ablation and a sensitivity study on dictionary size and frequency threshold. How would the approach fare when only a small lexicon (e.g., Wiktionary) is available?
- Significance: Are the gains from data selection and co-decoding statistically significant across runs? Please provide significance tests.
- NN2 details: How many prototype vectors are used, how are they initialized/learned, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- Co-decoding: How are conflicts resolved to ensure valid BIO sequences? Have you tried simple learned meta-decoding (e.g., per-entity features with a classifier) as a baseline?
- Robustness: How does performance vary with alignment quality, OOV rates, and tokenization errors? Can the data selection thresholds (q, n) be tuned on a small development set without overfitting?

Additional comments
- Consider moving Section 4.4 into 4.2 and relocating Table 2 to the evaluation section for flow.
- A dedicated related work section (separate from the introduction) would improve readability.
- Please include more discussion of failure cases, per-type results, and language-specific issues (e.g., morphology, scripts).
- If possible, release code/models and mapping dictionaries to facilitate reproducibility.

Suggested reference
- Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. ACL 2016. (Relevant for distance-weighted context aggregation.)
