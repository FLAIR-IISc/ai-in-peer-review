Summary
The paper presents a cross-lingual NER system that combines three model families (CRF, MEMM, and neural) via two ensemble strategies (probability-based and ranking-based). It further proposes a data selection method for projection-labeled instances from comparable corpora, and performs cross-lingual projection using a variation of Mikolov’s approach. The paper is easy to follow, well structured, and written in clear English. The combined-annotation results are interesting and appear to advance the state of the art.

Strengths
- Tackles an important and timely problem in cross-lingual NER.
- Sensible use of heterogeneous models and multiple ensembling strategies.
- Practical focus on improving the quality of projection-labeled data via selection.
- Generally clear exposition.

Areas for Improvement and Clarifications
1) Motivation and details for the CBOW variant
- Please explain the motivation for proposing a modified CBOW model. What limitations of the original CBOW (or skip-gram) does your variant address in this setting?
- Provide implementation details (context window, negative sampling, embedding dimensionality, training epochs, decay function/parameters).
- Include results for the original CBOW and skip-gram baselines to quantify the gains of your variant. Since you use a decay factor for surrounding embeddings, consider comparing against an exponential decay scheme such as that used in Iacobacci et al. (2016) and report sensitivity to the decay rate.

2) Cross-lingual projection method and ablations
- The frequency-weighted projection is a key contribution. Please contrast it directly with the original Mikolov mapping: report results with and without your weighting, and specify whether you enforce an orthogonality constraint or use simple linear regression.
- Include ablations on dictionary size/quality and frequency thresholds. If possible, provide a proxy measure of mapping quality (e.g., bilingual lexicon induction accuracy) to link improvements in projection to downstream NER gains.

3) Ensemble design and calibration
- Clarify how sequence-level and token-level scores are made comparable across CRF, MEMM, and neural models. If probabilities are combined, are they calibrated (e.g., temperature scaling or isotonic regression)? How are ties handled in the ranking-based method?
- Discuss when the probability-based vs. ranking-based ensemble works best and provide per-language/per-entity-type breakdowns to illustrate complementary strengths.
- Consider an additional baseline such as feature stacking (feeding model outputs into a meta-CRF) to contextualize the benefit of your two proposed schemes.

4) Data selection scheme
- Precisely define the scoring function used to select “good-quality” projection-labeled sentences, including all hyperparameters and thresholds.
- Add learning curves showing performance as a function of the number of selected instances, and compare against random selection and simple heuristics (e.g., sentence length, dictionary coverage).
- Analyze the error profile of selected vs. discarded instances to substantiate the “quality” claim.

5) Statistical significance and stability
- The manuscript states that improvements are significant. Please report statistical significance tests (e.g., approximate randomization or paired bootstrap over sentences/documents) and include variance across multiple training runs with different random seeds.

6) Evaluation depth and analysis
- The evaluation is comparatively brief given the strong results. Please expand with:
  - Per-language and per-entity-type results.
  - Ablations isolating the contributions of each component: base models, projection variant, data selection, and ensemble method.
  - Robustness tests to projection noise and dictionary sparsity.
  - Qualitative error analysis with examples showing typical cross-lingual projection errors and how the ensemble mitigates them.

7) Organization and presentation
- Integrate the content of Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 to improve narrative flow.
- Move the evaluation associated with Table 2 to the Evaluation section for consistency.
- Add a standalone Related Work section. The current introduction can be split into (i) Background/Related Work and (ii) Contributions/Overview.

8) Reproducibility
- Please provide or reference all hyperparameters and training settings for the CRF, MEMM, and neural models, as well as for the embedding training and projection steps.
- If possible, release code, learned mappings, and selected training subsets to facilitate replication.

Minor comments
- Define all notation at first use and ensure consistent terminology (e.g., “projection-labeled data”).
- Clarify whether comparable corpora are domain-matched with the evaluation data; if not, discuss domain effects.
- Briefly state computational costs for training and ensembling.

Reference suggestion
Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of ACL 2016 (Vol. 1, pp. 897–907). Their use of exponential decay for context weighting provides a useful comparison point for your decay scheme.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper addressing an important problem with a solid set of techniques. The work would be significantly strengthened by clearer motivation and details for the CBOW variant, direct comparisons to the original Mikolov projection, proper significance testing, and a deeper, more diagnostic evaluation. With these additions and minor reorganization, the paper would make a strong contribution.
