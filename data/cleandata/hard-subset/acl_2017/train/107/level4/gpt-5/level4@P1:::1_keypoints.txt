- Paper proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: annotation projection with a sentence-quality heuristic, representation projection via linear embedding mapping, and two co-decoding schemes to combine them.  
- Main contribution is empirical: a simple data-selection heuristic substantially improves noisy projection and combining systems consistently boosts F1; methods are incremental rather than novel.  

Strengths
- Broad evaluation across multiple languages, in-house fine-grained and CoNLL coarse-grained datasets, with precision/recall analyses.  
- Simple, practical methods (entity/tag-frequency sentence score and linear embedding map) that are easy to implement and reproduce.  
- Systems are complementary: annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall, representation projection is more balanced, and combining them yields consistent gains.  
- Competitive results: combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual baselines and sometimes approach supervised baselines on in-house data.  

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: approaches build on well-known paradigms and contributions feel incremental for a top venue.  
- Insufficient detail on data-selection tuning (how thresholds q and n were chosen), harming reproducibility and portability.  
- Co-decoding procedures are under-specified (how confidences are computed, how ties/overlaps/conflicts are resolved).  
- Multiple method variants are presented without clear criteria for selecting among them per language/dataset.  
- In-house data lack description (genres/domains, annotation guidelines, type distribution), limiting interpretability and comparability.  

Questions for authors
- How were thresholds (q, n) selected and were they stable across runs or subsets?  
- Please provide exact pseudocode/specification for both co-decoding schemes, including confidence computation and conflict-resolution rules.  
- Did you try combining more than two systems and does the rank-based strategy generalize to >2 inputs?  
- Please describe in-house dataset sources, domains, preprocessing, entity-type distribution, and genre match to English training data.
