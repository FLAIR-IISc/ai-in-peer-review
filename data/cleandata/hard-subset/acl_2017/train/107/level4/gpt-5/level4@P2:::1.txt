Summary
The paper investigates weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation. It studies two projection-based approaches—annotation projection from English to target texts and representation projection via cross-lingual embeddings—and proposes two co-decoding strategies to combine their outputs. Experiments on in-house datasets (four languages, >50 types) and CoNLL (three languages, 4 types) show that the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER methods and approach supervised baselines in some cases.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
- A simple and effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection on comparable corpora, which substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy.
- A direct model transfer method via linear mapping of target-language embeddings into English space, enabling reuse of a single English NER without retraining.
- Two co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) that leverage complementary precision/recall behaviors to improve final predictions.

Strengths
- Empirical rigor and breadth: Results across multiple languages and datasets, with comparisons to strong prior work (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.). The combined systems consistently improve over individual components and often set new cross-lingual baselines.
- Practicality and scalability: No target-language annotation is required; representation projection only needs monolingual embeddings and a small bilingual seed, and the approach supports adding new languages without retraining the NER.
- Insightful analysis of system behaviors: The paper identifies precision/recall characteristics of the two approaches and uses them to design co-decoding schemes that produce measurable gains.
- Impact of data selection: The proposed heuristic yields large improvements (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese and Korean), demonstrating that careful filtering of noisy projection labels is crucial in low-alignment settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core ideas—annotation projection, linear embedding mapping, and system combination—are well-established. The contributions read as incremental refinements rather than fundamentally new techniques for ACL standards.
- Insufficient guidance on parameter tuning for data selection: Thresholds for quality and entity count (q, n) are “tried” but the paper does not specify whether a development set was used, how sensitive results are to these choices, or how one would select them for a new language/domain. This hampers reproducibility and practical deployment.
- Unclear co-decoding specification: The description of both the exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based schemes is hard to follow. It is not clear how confidence scores across systems are calibrated, how conflicts are resolved under BIO/IOB constraints, or whether sequence-level consistency is guaranteed.
- Underexplored ensembling: The paper considers combining only two systems at a time, despite presenting several variants (AP, NN1, NN2). It is natural to ask whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2) or using alternative ensemble strategies would yield further gains.
- Dataset transparency: The in-house datasets are insufficiently described (domains, genre mix, annotation guidelines for >50 types, alignment sources). Without this information, it is difficult to assess generalizability or reproduce comparable settings.

Questions to Authors
- Data selection thresholds: How exactly were q and n chosen (development set, cross-validation, heuristics)? How sensitive are results to these thresholds? Can you provide guidance or an automatic tuning procedure (e.g., optimizing projected label consistency) for new languages?
- Co-decoding details: Please provide a precise algorithm (or pseudo-code) for both co-decoding schemes, including confidence calibration, conflict resolution, and enforcement of BIO/IOB validity. How are ties handled? Do you re-score spans or operate tokenwise?
- Ensembling beyond two systems: Did you attempt combining AP with both NN1 and NN2, or other ensemble approaches (e.g., majority vote, stacking, CRF lattice merging)? If so, how did they compare to the proposed co-decoding methods?
- In-house data: What are the domains (news, web, conversational), language varieties, and annotation guidelines (entity set definitions, nested entities) for the >50 types? Is there domain shift between English source data and target test sets?
- Cross-lingual mapping robustness: What dictionary sizes were used per language, and how sensitive is performance to dictionary size/quality? Did you consider constraints (e.g., orthogonality) or normalization to improve stability across languages?
- Resource requirements: Can you report training/inference time and memory footprints for AP (MEMM) and representation projection (NNs), and any constraints that influenced the choice of MEMM over CRF for large label sets?
