This paper presents a cross-lingual NER framework with two weakly supervised approaches: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme on comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection via cross-lingual word embedding mappings. The authors also propose two methods to combine system outputs (confidence/probability-based and rank-based co-decoding). The cross-lingual embedding mapping follows a variant of Mikolov’s approach. The paper is generally clear, well structured, and well written. The combined systems yield interesting and competitive results.

Detailed comments:
- Motivation and details of the CBOW variant:
  - Please clarify the motivation for introducing a variant of the CBOW model and provide more details on the architecture and training parameters. Was the original CBOW (or the Continuous Skip-gram) insufficient in your setting?
  - I recommend including results with the standard CBOW model (and, if feasible, Skip-gram) to quantify the gains from your variant.
  - Since you use a decay factor for surrounding embeddings, consider discussing or testing the exponential decay used in [1] to see whether it improves performance.

- Cross-lingual projection: frequency-weighted vs. original mapping:
  - It would strengthen the contribution to show a direct comparison between the original Mikolov mapping and your frequency-weighted least squares formulation, ideally as an ablation. This would help demonstrate that your weighting leads to consistent improvements across languages and settings.

- Statistical significance:
  - You state that “the proposed data selection scheme is very effective… and the improvement is significant.” Have you conducted statistical significance tests? Please report significance for key comparisons, including:
    - With vs. without data selection in annotation projection.
    - The two representation-projection architectures (NN1 vs. NN2).
    - Co-decoding vs. the individual systems.
  - Clarifying whether gains are statistically significant will make the evaluation more convincing.

- Organization and presentation:
  - Consider integrating the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2 for a cleaner flow.
  - The results shown in Table 2 would be more appropriate in the evaluation section.
  - I recommend adding a dedicated Related Work section. While the introduction touches on related literature, splitting it into an Introduction and a Related Work section would improve readability.

- Evaluation depth:
  - The evaluation is relatively brief (about 1.5 pages including the conclusion). Given the strong results, additional discussion would be valuable. Suggestions include:
    - A deeper analysis of why annotation projection tends to be high-precision/low-recall and how NN2 achieves more balanced precision/recall.
    - More detailed error analysis by entity type and language (e.g., how alignment quality correlates with specific error patterns).
    - A breakdown of the co-decoding contributions (e.g., how many entities are added by the second system without conflicts, and their correctness).
    - Clarification of hyperparameters and training details for NN1/NN2 and the projection mappings to improve reproducibility.

Suggested reference:
[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of ACL 2016 (Vol. 1, pp. 897–907).
