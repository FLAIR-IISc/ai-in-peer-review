This paper investigates weakly supervised methods for building NER systems via projection from English into other languages. The work explores several incremental improvements over existing projection-based approaches, introduces a data selection scheme, and proposes combining outputs from two weakly supervised systems. While the problem is important and the empirical results indicate the value of careful data selection, the paper’s novelty and clarity are limited relative to ACL standards.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and challenging setting (low/no supervision for NER in target languages).
- Presents multiple variants and an empirical study showing that data selection strongly affects performance.
- The data selection formulation is simple and potentially practical.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Limited novelty: The overall paradigm (projection-based weak supervision for NER) is well established, and the proposed techniques appear to be incremental extensions rather than fundamentally new methods. For ACL, I would expect a more substantial conceptual advance or deeper analysis that yields generalizable insights.
- Data selection thresholds: Table 2 relies on thresholds chosen by “trying different values,” but the protocol is unspecified. Were thresholds tuned on a held-out development set per language? If so, how was leakage avoided? If not, what criterion governed selection? Without a clear tuning strategy, it is hard to reproduce the gains or apply the method to new language pairs.
- Generalization and portability: The paper demonstrates that data selection is important, but offers limited guidance on how to set thresholds and other hyperparameters for new datasets or languages. A sensitivity analysis or principled procedure (e.g., heuristics based on alignment quality, sentence length, or coverage) would improve practicality.
- System combination: The description of how the two systems’ outputs are combined is difficult to follow. Is the combination token-level voting, span-level reconciliation, confidence-based selection, or a learned meta-classifier? How are conflicts resolved (e.g., overlapping spans, differing label sets, BIO inconsistencies)? A clearer algorithmic description (with examples or pseudo-code) is needed for reproducibility and to understand why the gains occur.
- Scope of combination: Given multiple variants, it seems natural to ask whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems would help. Did the authors try ensembles beyond two systems, or diversity-inducing strategies (e.g., different aligners, projection filters, or base models) to improve complementarity? Even limited experiments or a discussion would be informative.
- “In-house” dataset: The paper provides insufficient detail about the in-house corpus. What domains and genres does it contain (e.g., news, web, conversational, mixed)? How large is it, and how is it distributed across languages? Are annotation guidelines consistent with the evaluation benchmark? Such details are important for interpreting results and for assessing domain mismatch.
- Reproducibility details: The paper would benefit from more explicit information on:
  - The exact alignment/projection pipeline and its parameters;
  - How noisy spans are filtered or repaired (e.g., handling discontinuities or label conflicts);
  - Hyperparameter settings, tuning criteria, and whether a single setting is shared across languages;
  - Random seeds and training budgets;
  - Whether code, scripts, and any non-proprietary data will be released.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous tuning protocol for data selection thresholds: specify development splits, selection criteria, and per-language vs. universal settings. Include a sensitivity plot showing performance as a function of the threshold(s) to guide practitioners.
- Clarify the combination method with a concise algorithmic description, including:
  - The decision unit (token vs. span),
  - Conflict resolution rules,
  - Use of confidence scores or calibration,
  - Handling of BIO schema inconsistencies.
  An ablation comparing simple voting, confidence-based selection, and a learned meta-combiner would help justify the chosen approach.
- Explore ensembles beyond two systems, and quantify how diversity (different projection rules, aligners, base models, or training seeds) correlates with ensemble gains.
- Expand on the in-house dataset: domain composition, size, language coverage, and any preprocessing or normalization. If possible, release a portion or provide a detailed datasheet to aid reproducibility.
- Strengthen the case for novelty by articulating what new insight the paper provides about projection-based NER (e.g., a general principle for selecting training instances, a robustness criterion, or a method that consistently reduces projection noise). Alternatively, include deeper analysis (error typology, cross-language transfer patterns, or theoretical justification for the selection score).
- Report results with clearer guidance for new settings: e.g., default thresholds that work reasonably well across languages, and when to deviate from them based on observable data characteristics (alignment quality, dictionary coverage, sentence length distribution).
- Include an error analysis that ties improvements to specific error categories (boundary errors, label confusions, OOV entities), helping readers understand the mechanisms behind the gains.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem and provides empirical evidence that data selection and system combination can improve weakly supervised NER. However, the contributions feel incremental, and key methodological details—especially the threshold tuning and the combination procedure—are insufficiently specified. With clearer algorithms, a principled tuning strategy, stronger analysis, and broader or more innovative ensembles, the work would be considerably more compelling. In its current form, I find the novelty and clarity below the bar for ACL, but the line of investigation is promising and could be strengthened with the revisions suggested above.
