This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER). The authors employ conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network–based NER methods. They also propose two ensemble strategies to combine their outputs (probability-based and ranking-based), along with a method to select the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is performed using a variant of Mikolov’s approach. Overall, the paper is clear, well structured, and written in solid English. The results from combining annotations are interesting.

Detailed comments:
- Motivation and details for the CBOW variant: Please clarify the motivation for proposing a variation of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model and provide more implementation details (including hyperparameters). Did the original CBOW or the Continuous Skip-gram model underperform in your setting? It would be helpful to include results for the standard CBOW model so readers can assess the improvements introduced by your variation.
- Decay factor for surrounding embeddings: Since you use a decay factor for surrounding embeddings, I suggest considering the exponential decay scheme discussed in [1], which may provide a principled baseline or alternative. Please also specify the exact functional form and parameters of your decay.
- Cross-lingual projection: I would like to see a direct comparison between Mikolov’s original cross-lingual projection method and your frequency-weighted projections. These contributions would be more compelling if readers could clearly observe the superiority of your method across languages and settings. Please include ablation results isolating the effect of frequency weighting.
- Statistical significance: The paper states that “the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant.” Have you conducted statistical significance tests? Please report significance (e.g., via paired bootstrap or approximate randomization) for key comparisons, including the gains from data selection and from the ensemble methods.
- Organization: I recommend integrating the content of Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 to improve flow. Also, consider moving the evaluation related to Table 2 to the main evaluation section for consistency.
- Related work: The introduction partially covers related work, but a dedicated Related Work section would strengthen the paper by situating your contributions more clearly with respect to prior NER transfer methods, cross-lingual projection techniques, and embedding alignment approaches. Alternatively, split the introduction into Background and Contributions versus Related Work.
- Evaluation depth: The evaluation is quite brief (approximately 1.5 pages, including the conclusion). Given that you achieve state-of-the-art results, a deeper analysis would be valuable. Suggestions include:
  - Per-entity-type breakdowns and error analysis to identify which categories benefit most from the proposed methods.
  - Language-wise analysis to show robustness across typologically diverse languages.
  - Ablations for each ensemble component (probability-based vs. ranking-based) and for the data selection scheme.
  - Sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters (e.g., decay factor, projection frequency thresholds).
  - Discussion of computational cost and training/inference efficiency for each component and for the combined system.
- Reproducibility details: Please report all major hyperparameters, training data sizes per language, embedding dimensions, optimization settings, and any preprocessing steps. This is particularly important for your CBOW variant and for the frequency-weighted projection method.

Suggested reference:
[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (Vol. 1, pp. 897–907).
