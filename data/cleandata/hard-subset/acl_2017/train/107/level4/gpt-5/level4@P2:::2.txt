Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation by combining two projection-based approaches and two decoding strategies. First, it builds weakly labeled target data via annotation projection from English onto comparable corpora, with a heuristic to select high-quality projected sentences. Second, it transfers an English NER model directly to new languages by projecting target word embeddings into the English space using a frequency-weighted linear mapping. Finally, it combines both systems through co-decoding (confidence-based and rank-based).
- As I see it, the main contributions are:
  1) A simple, effective co-decoding framework that consistently improves over either projection method alone across multiple languages.
  2) A practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection that yields large gains when alignments are noisy.
  3) A frequency-weighted cross-lingual embedding mapping (and a CBOW variant) enabling direct model transfer without retraining in the target language.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains and breadth. The combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test sets, and the co-decoding improves over individual systems by 1.3–6.2 F1 depending on language. On in-house data, the selection heuristic brings large improvements when alignment is weak (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
- Clear trade-off analysis and design. The paper identifies complementary error profiles: annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall, while representation projection is more balanced. The rank-based co-decoder exploits this by prioritizing entities from the high-precision system and adding non-conflicting entities from the other, leading to consistent gains.
- Practicality and scalability. The representation projection approach maps all target languages into English, allowing a single English NER model to be reused without retraining. The mapping relies on small bilingual dictionaries (~5–6K pairs) and standard monolingual embeddings, making the approach easy to extend.
- Clarity and organization. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, with a clear description of models and training procedures. The empirical section covers both proprietary and public datasets.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations on representation learning and mapping (high priority). The paper introduces a CBOW variant with distance-based decay and a frequency-weighted linear mapping, but does not report comparisons to (a) standard CBOW/skip-gram or (b) the unweighted Mikolov mapping. Without these baselines, it is hard to quantify the contribution of these choices.
- No statistical significance testing (high priority). Claims such as “the improvement is significant” are not supported by statistical tests. Please report paired significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling over sentences or approximate randomization) for key comparisons.
- Evaluation and analysis are brief (medium priority). Given the strong results, the discussion is short. Missing pieces include per-entity-type breakdowns, error analysis (e.g., boundary vs. label errors), sensitivity to thresholds (q, n), dictionary size, embedding dimensionality, and alignment quality. These would help readers understand when each component helps most.
- Presentation/organization issues (medium priority). Related work is interleaved; a dedicated section would improve readability. Section 4.4 (related cross-lingual embeddings) would better fit before 4.2 to motivate design choices. Some experimental material (e.g., Table 2) would be clearer in the evaluation section.
- Incomplete training details (lower priority). The CBOW variant’s parameters and decay function are not fully specified, and hyperparameters for the NER models are only partially described. This hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the effect of design decisions.

Questions to authors
- CBOW variant: What motivated your CBOW modification over standard CBOW or skip-gram? Please report direct comparisons (same data, same dimensionality, same epochs), and consider exponential distance decay (see Iacobacci et al., 2016).
- Mapping ablations: How much do frequency weights in the bilingual dictionary help versus the unweighted Mikolov mapping? Please provide results across languages. Also report sensitivity to dictionary size, selection thresholds, and embedding dimensionality mismatch (800→300).
- Significance: Which improvements in Tables 2–5 are statistically significant? Please include tests and p-values.
- Confidence calibration: For the confidence-based co-decoder, did you normalize scores across systems (e.g., temperature scaling, Platt/Dirichlet calibration) to improve comparability of O vs. non-O tags?
- Threshold selection without target labels: How were q and n chosen in practice? Can they be tuned without target-language dev data (e.g., via proxy metrics)?
- Unknown words: How is the “NER-trained unk vector” obtained, and how sensitive are results to its training?

Additional comments and references
- Consider moving Section 4.4 before 4.2 and relocating Table 2 to the evaluation section. Splitting the introduction into Introduction and Related Work would improve flow.
- Please expand the discussion of when annotation vs. representation projection is preferable (e.g., as a function of alignment quality or domain mismatch).
- Suggested reference: Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. ACL 2016. Their use of exponential distance decay may be relevant to your CBOW variant.
