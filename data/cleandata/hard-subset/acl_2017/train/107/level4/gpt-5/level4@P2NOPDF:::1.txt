This paper proposes several weakly supervised methods for building NER systems by projecting from English into other languages. While the empirical results are useful, the overall strategy—cross-lingual projection with weak supervision—is well established, and most components appear to be incremental refinements of prior work. For an ACL submission, I would expect stronger conceptual novelty or a clearer articulation of what is fundamentally new.

A central contribution is the data selection scheme. The quality score formula is simple and reasonable, which is not a drawback. However, the thresholds reported in Table 2 are insufficiently motivated. The paper only notes that “different thresholds were tried.” Were these tuned on a held-out development set? If so, how was that set constructed for each language pair, and what was the tuning protocol? If not, what principled procedure guided the choice of thresholds? Because the results show that data selection is crucial, readers need guidance on how to set these parameters for new datasets or language pairs.

Another contribution is the combination of the outputs from the two proposed systems. I found this part difficult to follow. Please clarify precisely how the combination works: what outputs are combined (token labels, entity spans, or full sequence hypotheses), how conflicts are resolved, whether confidence scores or calibration are used, and at what stage (training vs. inference) the combination occurs. As written, the method is hard to reproduce.

The paper also introduces multiple variants for each method. Given this breadth, does it make sense to combine more than two weakly supervised systems? Did you explore stacking, voting, or other ensembling strategies across additional variants, and if so, what were the results?

Finally, please provide more detail on the “in-house” dataset: the types of texts and domains included, collection and preprocessing procedures, and how it differs from standard benchmarks. This context would help readers assess the transferability of the approach and interpret the data selection behavior.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and presents solid empirical work, but the novelty claims and methodological clarity need strengthening. Clearer explanations of threshold tuning, the combination mechanism, and the composition of the in-house data would significantly improve the paper’s clarity and reproducibility.
