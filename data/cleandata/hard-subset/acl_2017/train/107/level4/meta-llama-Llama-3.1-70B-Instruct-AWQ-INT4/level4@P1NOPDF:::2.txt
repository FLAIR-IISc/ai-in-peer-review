This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that combines the strengths of conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER methods. The authors propose two methods for combining the output of these approaches, namely probability-based and ranking-based methods, as well as a method for selecting the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is achieved using a variant of Mikolov's proposal. Overall, the paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with good English quality. The results of the combined annotations are also noteworthy.

Detailed comments:

I was curious about the motivation behind proposing a Continuous Bag-of-word (CBOW) model variation. The authors do not provide sufficient details about this variation or the parameters employed. It would be helpful to know whether the original model (or the Continuous Skip-gram model) yielded suboptimal results. I suggest including the results obtained with the CBOW model, allowing readers to assess the improvements of the proposed approach.

The use of a decay factor for the surrounding embeddings prompts a comparison with the exponential decay used in [1]. Considering this alternative approach could provide additional insights.

Similarly, I would like to see a comparison between the original Mikolov's cross-lingual projections and the proposed frequency-weighted projections. Demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method would enhance the value of these contributions.

The authors claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant." However, it is unclear whether a test of statistical significance was conducted. I would appreciate knowing whether the differences in results are statistically significant.

From a structural perspective, I suggest integrating the text of Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2, as this would improve the organization of the paper. Additionally, I recommend moving the evaluation of Table 2 to the evaluation section.

A related work section is noticeably absent. While the introduction provides some relevant information, I suggest dividing the introduction into two sections to address this omission.

The evaluation section is relatively brief (1.5 pages, including the conclusion). Given that the authors achieve state-of-the-art results, I would appreciate a more in-depth discussion and analysis of the results.

Suggested references:
