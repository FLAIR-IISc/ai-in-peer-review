This paper proposes a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that integrates conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov, and neural network-based NER methods. The authors also introduce two methods to combine the output of these methods (probability-based and ranking-based) and a method to select the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is achieved using a variant of Mikolov's proposal. Overall, the paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and written in correct English. The results of the combined annotations are interesting and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.

Detailed comments:

The motivation behind proposing a Continuous Bag-of-word (CBOW) model variation is not entirely clear. The authors should provide more details about this choice, including the parameters employed. It would be beneficial to include the results obtained with the CBOW model to allow readers to analyze the improvements of the proposed approach. Additionally, the authors may consider exploring the exponential decay used in [1] as an alternative to the decay factor for surrounding embeddings.

A comparison between the original Mikolov's cross-lingual projections and the proposed frequency-weighted projections would be valuable. By presenting the differences between these two approaches, the authors can demonstrate the superiority of their method. This would strengthen the contribution of the paper and provide more insight into the proposed approach.

The claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant" should be supported by a statistical significance test. The authors should provide evidence that the differences between the results obtained with and without the proposed data selection scheme are statistically significant.

The text in Section 4.4 could be integrated into the beginning of Section 4.2 to improve the overall structure of the paper. Furthermore, the evaluation of Table 2 could be moved to the evaluation section to make the paper more cohesive.

A related work section is missing, and the introduction could be divided into two sections to address this issue. The introduction currently includes some information about related work, but a separate section would provide a clearer overview of the existing research in the field.

The evaluation section is relatively short (1.5 pages with the conclusion section) and could be expanded to provide more discussion and analysis of the results. Given that the authors obtain state-of-the-art results, a more in-depth examination of the results would be beneficial.
