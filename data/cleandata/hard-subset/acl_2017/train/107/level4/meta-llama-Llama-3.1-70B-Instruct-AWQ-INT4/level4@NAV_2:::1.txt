This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognizers (NERs). The methods rely on some form of projection from English into another language. While the overall approach is not new, the individual methods proposed are improvements of existing methods.

The main contributions of the paper are the data selection scheme and the combination of the outputs of the two systems developed in the paper. The data selection scheme is a significant contribution, as it effectively selects good-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora. The formula used to calculate the quality score is straightforward, which is a positive aspect. However, it is unclear how the thresholds were calculated for Table 2, and it would be beneficial to provide more information on this process. The evaluation results demonstrate the importance of data selection, but it may be challenging to tune the parameters for a new dataset or language pair.

The combination of the outputs of the two systems is another notable contribution. However, the description provided is not entirely clear, and further clarification would be helpful. Additionally, it would be interesting to explore the possibility of combining more than two weakly supervised systems, as this could potentially lead to further improvements in performance.

The paper presents a number of variants for each of the methods proposed. The authors evaluate the performance of these variants on both in-house and open NER datasets for multiple target languages. The results show that the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL test data.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the types of texts in the "in-house" dataset are not well described. Providing more information about the dataset, such as the genres, styles, and sources of the texts, would be beneficial for understanding the results and generalizing the methods to other datasets.

In terms of strengths, the paper presents a thorough evaluation of the proposed methods, including a comparison with state-of-the-art approaches. The authors also provide a detailed description of the methods, including the data selection scheme and the combination of the outputs of the two systems.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the methods proposed are not entirely novel, as they build upon existing approaches. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more information on the parameter tuning process, particularly for the data selection scheme.

Questions to Authors:

1. How were the thresholds calculated for Table 2, and was this done on a development set?
2. Can you provide more information on the types of texts in the "in-house" dataset?
3. Did you try combining more than two weakly supervised systems, and if so, what were the results?
4. Can you provide more clarification on the combination of the outputs of the two systems, particularly with regards to the confidence-based and rank-based co-decoding schemes?
