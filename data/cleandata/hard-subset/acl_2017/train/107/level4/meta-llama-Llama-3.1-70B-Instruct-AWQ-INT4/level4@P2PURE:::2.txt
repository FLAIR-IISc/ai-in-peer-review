This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) by leveraging conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER methods. The authors propose two methods for combining the output of these methods, namely probability-based and ranking-based approaches, as well as a method for selecting the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is performed using a variant of Mikolov's proposal. Overall, the paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and written in good English. The results of the combined annotations are also noteworthy.

Detailed comments:

I would appreciate more insight into the motivation behind proposing a Continuous Bag-of-word (CBOW) model variation. The authors could provide more details about this variation, including the parameters employed. It would be helpful to know whether the original model or the Continuous Skip-gram model yielded suboptimal results. Including the results with the CBOW model would allow readers to analyze the improvements of the proposed approach. 

Considering the use of a decay factor for the surrounding embeddings, it might be beneficial to examine the exponential decay used in [1]. 

Similarly, I would like to see a comparison between the original Mikolov's cross-lingual projections and the proposed frequency-weighted projections. This would enable readers to assess the superiority of the proposed method. 

The statement "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant" raises the question of whether a test of statistical significance was conducted. It would be valuable to know if the differences in results are statistically significant.

From a structural perspective, integrating the text of Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 would improve the organization of the paper. Additionally, moving the evaluation of Table 2 to the evaluation section would enhance clarity. 

The paper would benefit from a separate related work section, as the introduction currently includes some of this information. Consider dividing the introduction into two sections to address this.

The evaluation section is somewhat brief (1.5 pages, including the conclusion section). Given that the paper achieves state-of-the-art results, a more in-depth discussion and analysis of the results would be appreciated.
