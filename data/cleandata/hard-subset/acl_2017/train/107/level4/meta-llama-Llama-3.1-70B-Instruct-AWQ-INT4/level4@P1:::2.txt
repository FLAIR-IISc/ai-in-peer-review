This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that employs conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER methods. The authors propose two methods to combine the output of these methods, a probability-based and a ranking-based approach, as well as a method to select the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. The cross-lingual projection is done using a variant of Mikolov's proposal. In general, the paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with correct English quality. The results of the combined annotations are interesting.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER based on effective annotation and representation projection. The first approach is an annotation projection method that uses a heuristic data selection scheme to select good-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora. The second approach is a representation projection method that projects word representations in vector space (word embeddings) from a target language to a source language, allowing the source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches. The main contributions of the paper are:

1. A heuristic data selection scheme for selecting good-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora.
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer based on cross-lingual word embedding mappings.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches to produce an output that is more accurate than the outputs of individual systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to cross-lingual NER that does not require human annotation in the target language.
2. The authors evaluate their approach on both in-house and open NER data sets for multiple target languages, demonstrating its effectiveness.
3. The paper provides a thorough comparison of the proposed approach with state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks a clear motivation for proposing a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-word (CBOW) model. It would be helpful to provide more details about the parameters employed and the results with the original CBOW model.
2. The authors do not provide a thorough comparison of their cross-lingual projection approach with the original Mikolov's proposal. It would be helpful to include a discussion of the differences between the two approaches and the results with the original proposal.
3. The paper does not provide a test of statistical significance for the improvement in results obtained with the proposed data selection scheme.
4. The evaluation section is quite short and could benefit from more discussion and analysis of the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the parameters employed in the variant of the CBOW model and the results with the original CBOW model?
2. How do the results of the proposed cross-lingual projection approach compare to the original Mikolov's proposal?
3. Have you conducted a test of statistical significance for the improvement in results obtained with the proposed data selection scheme?
4. Can you provide more discussion and analysis of the results, including a comparison of the proposed approach with state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches?
