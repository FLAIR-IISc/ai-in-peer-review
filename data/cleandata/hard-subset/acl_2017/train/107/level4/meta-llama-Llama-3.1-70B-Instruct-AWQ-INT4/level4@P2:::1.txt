This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognition (NER) systems. The methods rely on some form of projection from English into another language. While the overall approach is not new, the individual methods proposed are improvements of existing methods.

The paper's contributions can be summarized as follows:

1. A data selection scheme for improving the accuracy of annotation projection-based NER systems. The scheme uses a quality score to filter out low-quality projection-labeled data, which can significantly improve the performance of the target-language NER system.
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer. This approach uses cross-lingual word embedding mappings to project word representations from a target language to a source language, allowing the source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems to produce an output that is more accurate than the outputs of individual systems.

The strengths of the paper include:

1. The proposed data selection scheme is effective in improving the accuracy of annotation projection-based NER systems, especially when the alignment accuracy is low and the projection-labeled data are noisy.
2. The representation projection approach provides a new way for direct NER model transfer, which can be useful for building NER systems for low-resource languages.
3. The co-decoding schemes are able to combine the strengths of the two projection-based systems, achieving higher accuracy than both systems.

However, there are also some weaknesses in the paper:

1. The formula used to calculate the quality score in the data selection scheme is quite straightforward, and it is unclear how the thresholds were calculated for Table 2. The paper does not provide enough information on how to tune the parameters for a new data set or a new language pair.
2. The description of the co-decoding schemes is not clear, making it difficult to understand how they work.
3. The paper does not provide enough information about the types of texts in the "in-house" dataset, which makes it difficult to evaluate the generalizability of the proposed methods.

Questions to authors:

1. How did you tune the parameters for the data selection scheme, and what development set was used for this purpose?
2. Can you provide more details on how the co-decoding schemes work, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each scheme?
3. Did you try combining more than two weakly supervised systems, and what were the results?
4. Can you provide more information about the types of texts in the "in-house" dataset, and how do you think the proposed methods will generalize to other types of texts?
