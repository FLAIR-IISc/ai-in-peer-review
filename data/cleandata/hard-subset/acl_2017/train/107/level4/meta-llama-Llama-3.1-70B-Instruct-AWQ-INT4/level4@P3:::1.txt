This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognition (NER) systems. The methods rely on some form of projection from English into another language. The overall approach is not new, and the individual methods proposed are improvements of existing methods. For an ACL paper, I would have expected more novel approaches.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: annotation projection and representation projection. The annotation projection approach uses a heuristic data selection scheme to select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from comparable corpora. The representation projection approach projects word representations in vector space (word embeddings) from a target language to a source language, creating a universal representation of the words in different languages. The paper also proposes two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems.

**Contributions**

1. The data selection scheme for annotation projection, which effectively selects good-quality projection-labeled data from noisy comparable corpora.
2. The representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer, which projects word embeddings from a target language to a source language.
3. The co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems, improving the accuracy of the NER system.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the proposed approaches on both in-house and open NER data sets for multiple target languages.
2. The results show that the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL test data.
3. The paper provides a detailed description of the methods and the experimental setup, making it easy to replicate the results.

**Weaknesses**

1. The overall approach is not new, and the individual methods proposed are improvements of existing methods.
2. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the thresholds were calculated for the data selection scheme, which may make it difficult to tune the parameters for a new data set or language pair.
3. The description of the co-decoding schemes is not clear, making it difficult to understand how they work.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How were the thresholds calculated for the data selection scheme? Was this done on a development set?
2. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the co-decoding schemes, including examples of how they work?
3. Did you try combining more than two weakly supervised systems? If so, what were the results?
4. Can you provide more information about the types of texts in the "in-house" dataset?
