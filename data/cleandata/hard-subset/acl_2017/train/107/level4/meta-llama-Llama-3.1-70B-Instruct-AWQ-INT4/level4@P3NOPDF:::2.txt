This paper proposes a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) by combining conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov, and neural network-based NER methods. The authors also introduce two methods to integrate the output of these approaches (probability-based and ranking-based) and a method to select the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is achieved using a variant of Mikolov's proposal. Overall, the paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and written in good English. The results of the combined annotations are interesting and contribute to the field.

However, there are several areas that require improvement to strengthen the paper. Firstly, the authors should provide a clear motivation for proposing a Continuous Bag-of-word (CBOW) model variation. Currently, the details of this variation and its parameters are not adequately explained. It would be beneficial to include the results obtained using the CBOW model, allowing readers to analyze the improvements achieved by the proposed approach. Additionally, the authors may consider comparing their method with the original CBOW model or the Continuous Skip-gram model to demonstrate the superiority of their approach.

The use of a decay factor for the surrounding embeddings is an interesting aspect of the paper. However, the authors may want to explore the exponential decay method used in [1] as an alternative approach. Similarly, a comparison between the original Mikolov's cross-lingual projections and the proposed frequency-weighted projections would be valuable. This would enable readers to assess the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant" requires further support. Specifically, the authors should conduct a test of statistical significance to confirm that the differences in results are indeed significant.

From a structural perspective, the text in Section 4.4 could be integrated into the beginning of Section 4.2 to improve the flow of the paper. Furthermore, the evaluation of Table 2 would be better placed in the evaluation section. A related work section is noticeably missing, and the introduction could be divided into two sections to provide a clearer background and motivation for the research.

Finally, the evaluation section is somewhat brief, spanning only 1.5 pages, including the conclusion. Given that the paper achieves state-of-the-art results, a more in-depth discussion and analysis of the results would be appreciated. This would provide readers with a deeper understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.
