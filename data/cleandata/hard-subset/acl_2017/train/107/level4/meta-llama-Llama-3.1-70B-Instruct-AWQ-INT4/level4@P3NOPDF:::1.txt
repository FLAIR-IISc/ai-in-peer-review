This paper proposes several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognizers (NERs) that rely on projecting information from English to another language. While the overall approach is not new, the individual methods presented are improvements upon existing techniques. However, for an ACL paper, I would have expected more innovative approaches that push the boundaries of the field.

One of the contributions of the paper is the data selection scheme, which utilizes a straightforward formula to calculate a quality score. The simplicity of this formula is not inherently problematic, but it is unclear how the thresholds presented in Table 2 were determined. The paper mentions that different thresholds were tried, but it does not specify whether this was done using a development set. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to understand how to tune the parameters for a new dataset or language pair, despite the evaluation results clearly demonstrating the importance of data selection.

Another contribution of the paper is the combination of the outputs of the two systems developed in the paper. Unfortunately, I found the description of this combination to be unclear, and I had to make a concerted effort to understand how it works. Providing a more detailed explanation of this process would greatly enhance the readability of the paper.

The paper presents multiple variants for each of the proposed methods, which raises the question of whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems would be beneficial. It would be interesting to know if the authors explored this possibility and, if so, what the results were.

Finally, it would be helpful to have more information about the types of texts included in the "in-house" dataset. This would provide valuable context for understanding the results and potential applications of the proposed methods.

In summary, while the paper presents some useful improvements to existing methods, it falls short of expectations for an ACL paper in terms of novelty and innovation. Addressing the above concerns would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of the proposed approaches.
