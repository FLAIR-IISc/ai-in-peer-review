## Review for ACL 2017

This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), leveraging Conditional Random Fields (CRFs), Maximum Entropy Markov Models (MEMMs), and neural network-based NER approaches. The authors contribute two methods for combining the outputs of these systems – a probability-based and a ranking-based approach – alongside a method for selecting high-quality training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is achieved using a modification of Mikolov’s original technique. Overall, the paper is well-written, clearly structured, and demonstrates good English. The results obtained through combined annotations are promising and warrant further investigation.

**Strengths:**

The paper tackles an important problem in cross-lingual NLP. The combination of multiple NER systems and the intelligent selection of training data from comparable corpora are valuable contributions. The paper is generally easy to follow, and the experimental setup appears sound. Achieving state-of-the-art results is a significant accomplishment.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

Several aspects of the paper could be strengthened through further elaboration and analysis.

* **CBOW Variation:** The motivation for employing a variation of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is not fully clear. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of this modification, including the parameters used and a rationale for their selection. Furthermore, including the results of experiments using the original CBOW model (and potentially the Continuous Skip-gram model) would allow readers to directly assess the improvements gained through the proposed variation. 

* **Frequency-Weighted Projections:** While the authors mention a frequency-weighted projection approach based on Mikolov’s work, a more direct comparison between the original and proposed methods is needed. Quantifying the benefits of the frequency weighting – demonstrating that it demonstrably improves performance – would significantly strengthen this contribution.

* **Statistical Significance:** The claim that “the proposed data selection scheme is very effective” and that the “improvement is significant” lacks supporting evidence. A statistical significance test should be conducted to substantiate this claim and provide a more rigorous evaluation of the results. Reporting p-values would be highly beneficial.

* **Organization and Flow:** The current placement of Section 4.4 feels disjointed. Integrating the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2 would improve the logical flow and readability of the paper.  Additionally, moving the detailed evaluation of Table 2 to a dedicated evaluation section would create a more structured presentation of results.

* **Related Work:** The paper currently lacks a dedicated Related Work section. While the introduction touches upon related research, a more comprehensive overview of the existing literature on cross-lingual NER, data selection, and embedding projection is essential.  Consider dividing the current introduction into two sections – one for introducing the problem and motivation, and another for reviewing relevant prior work.

* **Evaluation and Discussion:** The evaluation section is relatively concise (1.5 pages, including the conclusion). Given the state-of-the-art results achieved, a more in-depth discussion and analysis of the findings would be highly appreciated. Exploring the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach, analyzing error cases, and discussing potential avenues for future research would enhance the impact of the paper.

**Minor Comments:**

* Consider referencing Iacobacci et al. (2016) [1] when discussing the use of decay factors in embedding weighting. Their work on exponential decay in word sense disambiguation may be relevant.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. While the paper is well-written and the results are promising, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly enhance its clarity, rigor, and impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing these points, particularly the addition of statistical significance testing, a more detailed comparison of the proposed methods with existing approaches, and an expanded discussion of the results.



**References:**

[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (Vol. 1, pp. 897-907).
