This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), leveraging conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER techniques. The authors further introduce two methods for combining the outputs of these systems – probability-based and ranking-based approaches – alongside a method for selecting optimal training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is achieved using a modified version of Mikolov’s approach. Overall, the paper is clearly written, well-organized, and demonstrates a good standard of English. The results obtained with the combined annotation methods are particularly noteworthy.

However, I have several detailed comments. First, the motivation behind the proposed Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model variation could be elaborated upon. The paper lacks specific details regarding this adaptation (and its parameters). It would be beneficial to understand if the original model, or the Continuous Skip-gram model, yielded unsatisfactory results. Including the CBOW model's performance figures would allow readers to better assess the improvements achieved by the proposed approach.  Given the use of a decay factor for surrounding embeddings, I recommend considering the exponential decay method described in Iacobacci et al. [1]. 

Second, a more thorough comparison between the original Mikolov cross-lingual projections and the frequency-weighted projections employed here would strengthen the contribution. Demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method with concrete comparisons would be valuable. 

Regarding the claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective…and the improvement is significant," a statistical significance test is necessary to substantiate this assertion.  Providing evidence of statistical significance will reinforce the findings.

From an organizational perspective, integrating the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2 would improve the paper's flow. Furthermore, moving the evaluation of Table 2 to the dedicated evaluation section would create a more logical structure. 

A dedicated related work section is missing; much of this information is currently embedded within the introduction. I suggest restructuring the introduction into two distinct sections to address this.

Finally, the evaluation section is relatively brief (1.5 pages including the conclusion).  Given the state-of-the-art results achieved, a more in-depth discussion and analysis of these findings would be appreciated.

Suggested reference:

[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (Vol. 1, pp. 897-907).
