This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), exploring a combination of traditional and neural network-based approaches. The authors investigate Conditional Random Fields (CRFs), Maximum Entropy Markov Models (MEMMs), and neural NER methods, alongside two novel combination strategies – probability-based and ranking-based – and a data selection method for leveraging cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection utilizes a modified version of Mikolov’s approach. Overall, the paper is clearly written, well-structured, and demonstrates a good command of English. The results achieved through combined annotation strategies are particularly noteworthy.

However, several points could be addressed to strengthen the paper. The motivation behind the proposed variation of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model lacks sufficient detail. The paper would benefit from clarifying the rationale for this adaptation and including the CBOW model’s performance in the results for direct comparison. Was the initial implementation of the standard CBOW or Skip-gram model underperforming? Furthermore, considering the use of a decay factor for surrounding embeddings, exploring related work such as the exponential decay method described in Iacobacci et al. (2016) [1] would be valuable. 

Similarly, a more detailed comparison between the original Mikolov cross-lingual projection technique and the proposed frequency-weighted projection would better highlight the contribution of the latter. Demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method through quantitative and qualitative analysis is crucial.

The claim that “the proposed data selection scheme is very effective” should be supported by statistical significance testing. Including p-values or other statistical measures would strengthen the validity of the reported improvements.

Regarding organization, integrating the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2 would improve the flow and readability. Additionally, moving the evaluation of Table 2 to a dedicated evaluation section would present a more coherent structure.

A dedicated related work section is currently missing. While the introduction touches upon relevant prior work, a separate, comprehensive section would provide necessary context and position the current work more effectively within the field. Consider restructuring the introduction into two sections – one providing general background and motivation, and the second dedicated to related work.

The evaluation section is relatively concise (1.5 pages including the conclusion). Given the state-of-the-art results obtained, a more in-depth discussion and analysis of these results are expected. Exploring the strengths and limitations of the model, potential error sources, and the impact of different components would significantly enhance the evaluation.
