## Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

This paper presents a comprehensive approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), addressing the challenge of limited annotated data in target languages. The authors explore two primary strategies: annotation projection from comparable corpora with a novel data selection heuristic, and direct model transfer via cross-lingual word embedding projection. They further propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The paper is generally well-written, clearly structured, and technically sound. The experimental results, demonstrating improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods, are a significant positive aspect of this work.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The most substantial contribution lies in the heuristic data selection scheme for improving the quality of weakly labeled data generated through annotation projection. This addresses a critical issue – the inherent noise in projection-labeled data – and demonstrably improves NER performance, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. This is a practical and impactful contribution.
2. **Cross-Lingual Embedding Projection with Weighted Frequency:** The proposed method for creating cross-lingual embedding mappings, utilizing a weighted least squares approach based on translation frequency, is a solid contribution.  While the core concept of embedding projection is not novel, the incorporation of weighted frequencies from a phrase table appears to be a beneficial refinement.
3. **Co-Decoding Strategies for System Combination:** The exploration of co-decoding schemes – specifically the rank-based approach – to intelligently combine the outputs of the annotation projection and embedding projection methods is a valuable addition. This demonstrates a thoughtful approach to leveraging the strengths of both techniques. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently reports performance gains across multiple target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.  Outperforming established cross-lingual NER approaches is a significant accomplishment.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a comprehensive experimental setup, including multiple target languages, various model configurations (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks), and ablation studies (e.g., different co-decoding schemes). This strengthens the credibility of the findings.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methods are explained in a straightforward manner, and the experimental results are presented concisely.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The proposed methods address a critical bottleneck in multilingual NLP – the scarcity of labeled data. The techniques presented have the potential to be applied to a wide range of languages and NER applications.
5. **Detailed Error Analysis & Justification:** The authors provide reasoning for architectural choices and parameter settings, like the choice of CBOW and the smoothing layer in NN2, providing valuable context for the experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of CBOW Variation:** While the authors introduce a variation of the CBOW model, the justification for this choice and a comparative analysis against the standard CBOW or Skip-gram models are lacking. The impact of this variation on performance remains unclear. A more detailed discussion and inclusion of results using standard models would strengthen this aspect.
2. **Insufficient Comparison of Embedding Projection Methods:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth comparison between the proposed frequency-weighted embedding projection method and standard Mikolov-style projection. Quantifying the performance improvement specifically attributable to the weighting scheme would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** While the reported improvements appear promising, the absence of statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) weakens the claims of improvement. Establishing statistical significance is crucial for demonstrating the robustness of the results.
4. **Incomplete Related Work:** The related work section is somewhat limited. Expanding this section to include more recent advancements in cross-lingual NER, representation learning, and weakly supervised learning would provide a more complete context for the presented work. A dedicated section, separated from the introduction, would improve readability.
5. **Concise Evaluation & Analysis:** The evaluation section, while thorough, is relatively concise. A more in-depth discussion of the results, including error analysis and insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach for different languages, would be beneficial.  

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a comparative analysis of your CBOW variation against the standard CBOW and Skip-gram models?
2. Can you quantify the performance gain specifically attributable to the frequency weighting in your embedding projection method compared to standard Mikolov-style projection?
3. Have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate the reported improvements? If so, please provide the results.
4. What are the limitations of your approach, particularly regarding languages with significantly different word order or morphological structures?
5. Regarding the rank-based co-decoding scheme, what criteria were used to prioritize the annotation projection system? Is this prioritization language-dependent?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider moving Section 4.4 (Related Work regarding embedding projection) to the beginning of Section 4.2 for improved flow.
*   Move the evaluation tables (currently interspersed within the method descriptions) to a dedicated “Experiments” section.
*   The reference to Iacobacci et al. (2016) regarding exponential decay is helpful; please cite it in the main text where the decay factor is introduced.
