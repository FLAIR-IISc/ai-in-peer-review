This paper describes a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER). The authors employ conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov, and neural network-based NER methods. In addition, the authors propose two methods to combine the output of those methods (probability-based and ranking-based), and a method to select the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. The cross-lingual projection is done using a variant of Mikolov's proposal. In general, the paper is easy to follow, well-structured, and the English quality is also correct. The results of the combined approaches are promising.

The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive study of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection and representation projection techniques with novel co-decoding strategies. A secondary contribution lies in the proposed heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which demonstrably improves accuracy when alignment quality is low. Finally, the exploration of frequency-weighted cross-lingual embedding mappings offers a practical adaptation of existing methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive System:** The paper presents a well-integrated system combining multiple techniques—annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding—addressing the challenge of cross-lingual NER without relying on expensive human annotation. This holistic approach is a significant strength.
2. **Effective Data Selection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrably improves performance, especially in scenarios with noisy alignment data. This is a practical contribution that directly addresses a common issue in cross-lingual transfer learning. The results in Table 2 clearly show the improvements achieved through this selection process across multiple languages.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed system achieves competitive results, outperforming existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL data (as shown in Table 5). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods and provides a solid empirical basis for the claims made in the paper.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The explanations of the various techniques are concise and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Justification for CBOW Variation:** The rationale for using a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is not sufficiently explained. The paper lacks details about the original model's performance and why the proposed modification was necessary. Including a comparison to standard CBOW or Skip-gram results would strengthen this aspect of the work.
2. **Insufficient Comparison of Embedding Methods:** The paper does not provide a detailed comparison between the proposed frequency-weighted cross-lingual embedding mappings and the original Mikolov's projections. Quantitatively demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method is crucial for establishing its value.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The claim of "significant" improvement in the annotation projection data selection scheme is not supported by statistical significance tests. Providing p-values or confidence intervals would bolster the validity of the reported results.
4. **Organization of Section 4:** The placement of related work (Section 4.4) within the methodology section (Section 4.2) disrupts the flow of the paper. Relocating this context to the beginning of Section 4 would improve readability. Moving the experimental results from section 3.2 to the evaluation section would improve the structure.
5. **Limited Discussion and Analysis:** The evaluation section is relatively brief given the promising results. A more in-depth discussion and analysis of the results, including error analysis and potential future directions, would enhance the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the CBOW variant and include a comparison with standard CBOW or Skip-gram models?
2. Can you present a quantitative comparison between the proposed frequency-weighted embedding mappings and Mikolov's original projections, demonstrating the improvements achieved by your method?
3. Have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate the reported improvements, particularly for the annotation projection data selection scheme? If so, could you please provide the results?
4. What types of errors does the combined system still make, and what potential strategies could be employed to address these limitations?
