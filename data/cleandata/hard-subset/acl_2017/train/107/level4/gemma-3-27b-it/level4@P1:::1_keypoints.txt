1. Paper proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER via annotation projection and representation projection but lacks novelty—methods are incremental improvements.  
2. Data selection scheme (simple quality score) is a practical contribution that improves handling of noisy comparable corpora.  
3. Thresholds for data selection (q and n) are not clearly justified or tuned—no explanation of development set or procedure.  
4. Data selection is shown to be crucial for performance, especially when alignment accuracy is low.  
5. Co-decoding to combine two systems is a contribution, with a rank-based scheme outperforming a confidence-based scheme.  
6. Description of how co-decoding resolves conflicts and combines outputs is unclear and needs more detail.  
7. Many method variants are presented; it’s unclear whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems was explored.  
8. Evaluation is comprehensive across multiple languages and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), enabling comparative analysis.  
9. Empirical comparisons to prior state-of-the-art (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) provide valuable context.  
10. Models (CRFs, MEMMs, neural nets) are clearly presented, aiding reproducibility.  
11. Lack of detail about the “in-house” dataset (source, domain, size) reduces reproducibility and interpretability.  
12. Overall contributions are incremental: heuristic data selection and co-decoding exploration, with a minor contribution being broad empirical evaluation.  
13. Key questions for authors: how thresholds were chosen, detailed co-decoding mechanics (conflict resolution), whether more than two systems were combined, and full in-house dataset details.
