This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognition (NER) systems, relying on projection from English into another language. While the overall approach builds upon existing methods, the paper offers incremental improvements and a thorough evaluation across multiple languages. For an ACL paper, a higher degree of novelty would be desirable, but the practical contributions related to data selection and system combination are valuable.

The primary contribution of this work is a data selection scheme for improving annotation projection. The quality score formula is straightforward, which is beneficial for interpretability. However, the paper lacks detail regarding the threshold selection process (q and n in Table 2). It is unclear if these thresholds were tuned using a development set, and the paper would benefit from clarifying this. While the evaluation results demonstrate the importance of data selection, guidance on parameter tuning for new datasets or language pairs would enhance the practical applicability of this contribution.

A second contribution lies in the combination of the two developed systems via co-decoding. The description of the schemes, particularly the "rank-based" approach, is somewhat unclear and could be elaborated upon. Specifically, how conflicting entities are resolved within the rank-based scheme is not fully explained.

The paper presents numerous variants of each method. While comprehensive, it prompts the question of whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems could yield further improvements. Exploring this direction, even with preliminary results, would strengthen the work.

Finally, providing additional details about the “in-house” dataset—specifically the types of texts included—would provide valuable context for the evaluation results. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper evaluates the proposed approaches across a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and established (CoNLL) datasets. This breadth of evaluation is a significant strength, demonstrating the generalizability of the methods.
2. **Practical Data Selection Scheme:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is a valuable contribution. The results clearly show its effectiveness, particularly in low-resource scenarios or with noisy data. This addresses a key challenge in cross-lingual NER.
3. **Effective System Combination:** The co-decoding schemes, while needing clarification in their description, demonstrate the potential for improving performance by intelligently combining the strengths of different approaches. The rank-based scheme consistently outperforms the confidence-based approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core approach of projection from English is not novel. The proposed methods represent refinements and combinations of existing techniques, rather than a fundamentally new direction.
2. **Lack of Clarity in Co-Decoding Description:** The explanation of the co-decoding schemes, particularly the "rank-based" scheme, is insufficient. A more detailed explanation of how conflicting entity detections are handled would be beneficial.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Threshold Tuning:** The paper does not adequately describe the process used to determine the optimal thresholds (q and n) for the data selection scheme. The statement that “different thresholds were tried” lacks rigor and reproducibility.
4. **Limited Exploration of System Combination:** The paper focuses on combining two systems. Investigating the potential of combining more systems, even briefly, could reveal further improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on how the thresholds (q and n) for the data selection scheme were determined? Was a development set used, and if so, what metric was optimized?
2. Please provide a clearer explanation of the “rank-based” co-decoding scheme, specifically addressing how conflicts between entity detections from the annotation projection and representation projection systems are resolved.
3. Did you explore combining more than two weakly supervised systems? If so, what were the results? If not, can you comment on the potential benefits or challenges of doing so?

**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the characteristics of the "in-house" datasets (e.g., domain, size, entity type distribution).
*   Consider discussing the computational cost of the different approaches, particularly the representation projection method.
*   The citation style is inconsistent. Ensure that all citations adhere to ACL formatting guidelines.
