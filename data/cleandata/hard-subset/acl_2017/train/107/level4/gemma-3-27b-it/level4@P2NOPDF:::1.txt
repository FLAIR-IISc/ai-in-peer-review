This paper explores weakly supervised methods for Named Entity Recognition (NER), leveraging cross-lingual projection from English. While the core approach of cross-lingual transfer is established, the presented methods represent incremental improvements upon existing techniques. For a conference of ACL’s caliber, I anticipated a more substantially novel contribution.

A key contribution of this work lies in the proposed data selection scheme. The quality scoring formula is commendably simple and transparent. However, the methodology for determining the thresholds used in Table 2 remains opaque. The paper states that thresholds were selected through experimentation, but lacks detail regarding whether a development set was utilized for this tuning process. This omission hinders reproducibility and practical applicability. The evaluation results convincingly demonstrate the importance of effective data selection, yet the paper does not offer sufficient guidance on how to adapt the parameter tuning process to new datasets or language pairs. 

Another noteworthy contribution is the combination of the two NER systems developed. Unfortunately, the description of this combination process is unclear and requires further elaboration. Understanding the specific mechanism by which the system outputs are integrated is crucial for assessing the approach’s validity and potential. 

The paper investigates numerous variants for each proposed method. This thoroughness is appreciated, but raises a natural question: is there diminishing returns from combining more than two weakly supervised systems? It would be valuable to know if the authors explored combinations involving a larger number of systems, and if so, what the results indicated. 

Finally, further detail regarding the composition of the "in-house" dataset would be beneficial. Knowing the characteristics and source of this data would allow for a more complete understanding of the experimental setup and the generalizability of the findings.
