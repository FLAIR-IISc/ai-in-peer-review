This paper explores weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging projection techniques from English to other languages. While the overall approach builds upon existing work, the paper introduces refinements and combinations aimed at improving performance. The core contributions are a data selection scheme for annotation projection and a co-decoding strategy to integrate the outputs of different projection methods. However, the novelty presented may be limited for an ACL publication.

The data selection scheme, using a frequency-based quality score, is a sensible approach. Its simplicity is a strength. However, the paper lacks clarity regarding parameter tuning – specifically, how the thresholds used in Table 2 were determined. Was a development set employed for optimization? The results convincingly demonstrate the importance of data selection, but the absence of guidance on parameter tuning for new datasets or language pairs hinders reproducibility and practical application. 

The combination of system outputs via co-decoding is another contribution. The description of this process, however, is somewhat unclear. A more detailed explanation of the methodology would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper does not explore the potential of combining more than two weakly supervised systems, a direction that warrants investigation.

The evaluation is performed on both in-house and standard CoNLL datasets. The results demonstrate improvements over existing cross-lingual NER approaches, particularly for German. However, more information about the characteristics of the “in-house” datasets would provide valuable context. What type of texts are included? What is the domain?

**Strengths:**

1. **Data Selection Effectiveness:** The proposed data selection scheme demonstrably improves performance, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. The experimental results (Table 2) clearly highlight the positive impact of filtering noisy projection data, showing significant F1 score gains across multiple languages. This is a practical contribution for real-world applications where perfect alignment is often unattainable.
2. **Co-Decoding Improvement:** The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, consistently improve performance compared to the individual projection methods. This suggests a successful integration of complementary strengths from annotation projection (precision) and representation projection (recall). The gains reported in Tables 3-5 strengthen this argument.
3. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on both in-house and standard datasets (CoNLL), demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed methods. The comparison with state-of-the-art approaches in Table 5 indicates a competitive performance level.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core approach of leveraging projection techniques is well-established. The incremental improvements presented, while valuable, may not meet the threshold for a top-tier ACL publication. The paper would benefit from a more compelling argument for the novelty of the combined approach.
2. **Lack of Parameter Tuning Details:** The paper does not provide sufficient detail on how the thresholds for the data selection scheme were determined. The statement "different thresholds were tried" is insufficient. A description of the development set used for tuning, or the methodology employed, is essential for reproducibility and generalizability.
3. **Co-Decoding Clarity:** The explanation of the co-decoding schemes lacks clarity. A more precise description of the algorithms, potentially with pseudocode, would facilitate understanding and replication.
4. **In-House Dataset Description:** The paper provides insufficient information about the characteristics of the “in-house” datasets. Details regarding the text type, domain, and size would be valuable for interpreting the evaluation results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide details on the development set used to tune the parameters for the data selection scheme, or describe the methodology used for parameter optimization?
2. Please provide a more detailed explanation of the co-decoding schemes, potentially including pseudocode or a step-by-step description.
3. Was the exploration of combining more than two weakly supervised systems considered? If so, what were the results? If not, why?
4. Could you provide more information regarding the composition and characteristics of the “in-house” datasets used for evaluation (e.g., text type, domain, size)?
