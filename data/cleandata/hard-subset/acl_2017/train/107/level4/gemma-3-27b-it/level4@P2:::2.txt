This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), leveraging both annotation projection and representation projection techniques. The authors explore methods for improving the quality of projected annotations using a data selection scheme, and for transferring knowledge across languages by projecting word embeddings. They further propose co-decoding strategies to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The work is clearly written and well-structured. The results demonstrating improvements over existing cross-lingual NER systems are promising.

The primary contribution of this work is the development and evaluation of a practical system for cross-lingual NER that requires no human annotation in the target language. This is achieved through a combination of effective strategies: a heuristic data selection scheme for improving the quality of annotation projection, a representation projection approach using weighted cross-lingual word embeddings, and co-decoding methods for combining the strengths of both approaches. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, their combination and the demonstrated performance gains represent a significant contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical analysis demonstrating the benefits of careful data selection and the advantages of combining different projection techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets. The gains are particularly notable for languages with lower alignment accuracy. This empirical validation provides strong support for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Practical Approach:** The presented system offers a practical solution for low-resource languages where annotated data is scarce. By relying on comparable corpora and pre-trained embeddings, the method avoids the costly and time-consuming process of manual annotation.
3. **Comprehensive Combination of Techniques:** The authors thoughtfully combine multiple techniques – annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding – to create a robust and effective system. This holistic approach addresses different challenges in cross-lingual NER and leverages the strengths of each component.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The technical details are clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components (annotation projection, word embedding projection) are not entirely novel. A more in-depth comparison to existing methods for each component would strengthen the argument for the overall approach. Specifically, a clear discussion on why the proposed data selection scheme outperforms simpler alternatives would be valuable.
2. **Insufficient Detail on CBOW Variation:** The rationale behind the specific variation of the CBOW model used for word embeddings is not fully explained. A more detailed explanation of the changes made to the original model, along with a comparison of its performance to the standard CBOW and Skip-gram models, would provide valuable insights.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports performance improvements but does not provide statistical significance testing to confirm whether these improvements are statistically significant. Including p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the validity of the conclusions.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are promising, a more detailed error analysis would help to identify the remaining challenges and potential areas for future improvement. Investigating the types of errors made by the system could provide valuable insights into its limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed data selection scheme to simpler alternatives (e.g., random sampling, selecting sentence pairs with the highest alignment accuracy)? What are the advantages of your heuristic approach?
2. What were the specific parameters used for the CBOW model variation, and how did its performance compare to the original CBOW and Skip-gram models?
3. Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for the reported performance improvements?
4. Could you provide a brief error analysis, identifying the most common types of errors made by the system and suggesting potential strategies for addressing them?
