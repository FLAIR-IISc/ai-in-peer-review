This paper investigates weakly supervised Named Entity Recognition (NER) through cross-lingual projection, presenting several methods that build upon existing techniques. While the approaches demonstrate incremental improvements, the overall strategy of leveraging cross-lingual transfer for weak supervision is not novel, and for a conference of ACL’s caliber, I anticipated a more innovative contribution.

A key contribution of this work lies in the proposed data selection scheme. The quality score formula is commendably straightforward, prioritizing clarity. However, the methodology for determining the thresholds used in Table 2 remains opaque. The paper states that thresholds were selected through experimentation, but lacks detail regarding the experimental setup. Specifically, it is unclear whether a development set was employed for this tuning process. Its omission hinders reproducibility and limits the practical applicability of the method – readers are left without guidance on how to adapt these thresholds to new datasets or language pairs, despite the clear demonstration of data selection’s significant impact on performance. Providing insights into this tuning process would substantially strengthen the paper.

Another contribution centers on the combination of the outputs from the two developed systems. Unfortunately, the explanation of this combination process is unclear and requires further elaboration. A more detailed description, potentially with a clarifying example, is necessary to fully understand the implementation and evaluate its effectiveness.

The paper explores a range of variants for each proposed method. This thoroughness is appreciated; however, it raises a natural question: does the benefit plateau with only two combined systems, or is there potential for further performance gains through the integration of more weakly supervised models? Exploring the scalability of this combination approach – even with preliminary results – would be a valuable addition. Did the authors investigate the impact of incorporating additional, diverse weakly supervised signals?

Finally, greater context regarding the “in-house” dataset would be beneficial. Details regarding the text genres, domains, and general characteristics of this dataset would allow for a more informed assessment of the results and their generalizability. Knowing the nature of these texts would help to understand the potential biases inherent in the evaluation.

In summary, this paper presents a solid, incremental contribution to the field of weakly supervised NER. The data selection scheme is a worthwhile improvement, but its practical application is limited by the lack of clarity regarding threshold tuning. While the results are encouraging, the limited novelty and incomplete methodological descriptions prevent this paper from achieving a higher rating. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns outlined above regarding threshold selection, system combination clarity, and dataset description.
