This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing NER systems. The methods rely on some form of projection from English into another language. The overall approach is not novel, and the individual methods proposed are improvements of existing methods. For an ACL paper, I would have expected more innovative approaches.

One of the contributions of the paper is the data selection scheme. The formula used to calculate the quality score is straightforward, which is a positive aspect. However, it is unclear how the thresholds were determined for Table 2. The paper states only that different thresholds were tried. Was this done on a development set? There is no mention of this in the paper. The evaluation results clearly show that data selection is crucial, but it remains unclear how to tune the parameters for a new dataset or language pair.

Another contribution of the paper is the combination of the outputs of the two systems developed. I found it difficult to fully understand how this works; the description provided is not sufficiently clear. 

The paper presents numerous variants for each of the proposed methods. Does it make sense to combine more than two weakly supervised systems? Did the authors explore this possibility?

It would be helpful to know more about the nature of the texts included in the “in-house” dataset.

**Summary:** The paper introduces two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER – annotation projection and representation projection – and explores co-decoding schemes to combine their outputs. While the individual techniques build on existing work, the combination and data selection strategy represent incremental contributions.

**Contributions:** I identify two primary contributions. First, the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection offers a practical improvement for handling noisy comparable corpora. Second, the exploration of co-decoding schemes to integrate different projection-based approaches demonstrates a thoughtful effort to maximize performance. A third, minor contribution is the empirical evaluation across several languages, providing a comparative analysis of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Data Selection Scheme:** The proposed data selection scheme addresses a critical issue in annotation projection – the presence of noisy data. The simplicity of the quality score calculation is an advantage, and the empirical results (Table 2) clearly demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. The improvement in F1 scores across various languages highlights the scheme’s robustness.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering multiple target languages. This breadth of evaluation strengthens the validity of their findings and provides a more comprehensive understanding of the approaches’ performance. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) provides valuable context.
3. **Thoughtful Combination of Approaches:** The investigation of co-decoding schemes shows a good understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the individual approaches. The rank-based co-decoding scheme consistently outperforms the confidence-based scheme, suggesting a more effective way to leverage the complementary information from annotation projection and representation projection.
4. **Clear Presentation of Models:** The paper provides a detailed description of the NER models used (CRFs, MEMMs, and neural networks), facilitating reproducibility and understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Threshold Tuning:** The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the thresholds for the data selection scheme (q and n) were determined. Simply stating that “different thresholds were tried” is insufficient. It’s crucial to explain whether a development set was used and how the optimal thresholds were identified, as this impacts the generalizability of the approach.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Co-Decoding:** The description of the co-decoding schemes remains somewhat vague. A more detailed explanation of the decision-making process within the co-decoding scheme would be beneficial. For example, clarifying how conflicts between entities detected by different systems are resolved would improve understanding.
3. **Limited Exploration of System Combination:** While the paper explores combining two systems, it does not investigate the potential benefits of incorporating more than two weakly supervised methods. Addressing whether extending the combination to include additional systems yields further improvements could strengthen the research.
4. **Dataset Description:** The paper lacks a detailed description of the “in-house” dataset, including its source, domain, and size. Providing this information would enhance the reproducibility and interpretability of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the process used to determine the thresholds (q and n) for the data selection scheme? Was a development set utilized, and if so, how were the optimal values identified?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the co-decoding schemes, particularly how conflicts between entities identified by different systems are resolved?
3.  Did you explore combining more than two weakly supervised systems? If not, could you briefly discuss the challenges or considerations that led to focusing on a combination of only two?
4.  Could you please provide more details about the "in-house" dataset, including its source, domain, and size?
