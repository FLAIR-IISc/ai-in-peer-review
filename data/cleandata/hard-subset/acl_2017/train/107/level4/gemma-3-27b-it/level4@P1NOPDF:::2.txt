This paper describes a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER). The authors employ conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER methods. They also propose two methods for combining the outputs of these systems – a probability-based approach and a ranking-based approach – as well as a method for selecting the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is performed using a variant of Mikolov’s method. Overall, the paper is easy to follow, well-structured, and clearly written. The results obtained through combined annotations are particularly interesting.

Detailed comments:

I was curious about the motivation behind proposing a variation of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model. The paper lacks detail regarding this (and the parameters used). Did the original model, or the Continuous Skip-gram model, yield unsatisfactory results? I suggest including the results achieved with the CBOW model, allowing readers to assess the improvements offered by your approach. 

Given your use of a decay factor for the surrounding embeddings, I recommend considering the exponential decay used in [1].

Similarly, I would appreciate a more detailed comparison between the original Mikolov cross-lingual projections and your frequency-weighted projections. The value of these contributions would be enhanced by demonstrating the clear superiority of your method.

The statement "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant" raises a question: have you conducted a statistical significance test? Knowing whether the differences in results are statistically significant would be valuable.

I suggest integrating the text from Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 for improved flow and clarity. I also recommend moving the evaluation of Table 2 to the dedicated evaluation section.

The paper is missing a related work section. While the introduction contains some of this information, I suggest dividing the introduction into two sections – one for background and motivation, and another for related work.

The evaluation section is rather short (1.5 pages including the conclusion). Given that you achieve state-of-the-art results, I would appreciate a more in-depth discussion and analysis of the findings.

Suggested reference:

[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (Vol. 1, pp. 897-907).
