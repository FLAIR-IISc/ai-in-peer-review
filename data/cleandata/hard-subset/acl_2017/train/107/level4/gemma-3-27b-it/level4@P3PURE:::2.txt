This paper describes a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), employing conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER methods. The authors further propose two methods to combine the outputs of these systems – probability-based and ranking-based approaches – and a method for selecting the best training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is performed using a variant of Mikolov’s original proposal. Overall, the paper is easy to follow, well-structured, and clearly written. The results achieved through the combination of annotations are particularly interesting.

Detailed comments:

I am curious about the motivation behind proposing a variation of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model. The paper lacks detailed information regarding this (or the parameters used). Were lower results observed with the original model, or alternatively, with the Continuous Skip-gram model? I suggest including the results obtained using the CBOW model to allow readers to analyze the improvements offered by your approach. 

Regarding the decay factor used for surrounding embeddings, I recommend considering the exponential decay approach presented in [1]. 

Similarly, I would appreciate a more thorough comparison between your frequency-weighted projections and the original cross-lingual projections proposed by Mikolov. Demonstrating the superiority of your method with a direct comparison would enhance the value of these contributions.

The statement “the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant” raises the question of statistical significance. Providing a statistical significance test would strengthen this claim and clarify the robustness of the reported improvements.

I suggest integrating the text of Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 for improved clarity and flow. Furthermore, I recommend moving the evaluation of Table 2 to the dedicated evaluation section.

The paper lacks a dedicated related work section. While the introduction includes some relevant information, I suggest dividing it into two sections – an introduction and a related work section – for better organization.

The evaluation section is relatively short (1.5 pages including the conclusion). Given the state-of-the-art results obtained, I would appreciate a more detailed discussion and analysis of the findings.

Suggested reference:

[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics (Vol. 1, pp. 897-907).
