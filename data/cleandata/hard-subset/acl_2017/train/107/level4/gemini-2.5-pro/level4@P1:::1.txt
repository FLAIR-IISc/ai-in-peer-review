### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents two weakly supervised methods for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that do not require manually annotated data in the target language. Both methods leverage a resource-rich source language (English) to build an NER system for a resource-poor target language.

The first method is based on **annotation projection**. It uses an English NER system to tag sentences in a comparable corpus and then projects these annotations onto aligned target-language sentences. The primary contribution here is a heuristic data selection scheme designed to filter the noisy projected data, retaining only higher-quality sentences to train the target-language model.

The second method is based on **representation projection**. It learns a linear mapping to project word embeddings from a target language into the English word embedding space. This allows a single NER model, trained only on English data and word embeddings, to be directly applied to the projected target-language representations without any retraining.

Finally, the authors propose two **co-decoding schemes** (one confidence-based, one rank-based) to combine the outputs from the annotation projection and representation projection systems, leveraging their complementary strengths. The combined system is evaluated on both in-house and standard CoNLL datasets, demonstrating superior performance over previous state-of-the-art weakly supervised cross-lingual NER systems.

### Contributions

1.  **An effective heuristic for filtering noisy projected data:** The paper's most significant contribution is a simple yet highly effective data selection scheme for the annotation projection approach. By filtering the training data based on a quality score derived from annotation consistency, the method achieves substantial performance gains (up to 20 F1 points), particularly for language pairs with lower-quality alignments.
2.  **A successful combination of two complementary projection approaches:** The paper demonstrates that combining an annotation projection system (which tends to have high precision) with a representation projection system (which has more balanced precision and recall) via a simple co-decoding scheme consistently improves performance over either individual system. The resulting combined system establishes a new and strong state-of-the-art for this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong empirical results and a new state-of-the-art:** The proposed combined system outperforms two recent and competitive cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL 2002/2003 benchmark datasets. The gains are particularly impressive for German. This work provides a strong new baseline for the field.
2.  **Demonstrates the critical importance of data selection:** The experiments clearly show that for annotation projection, filtering the noisy auto-generated training data is crucial. The paper presents a straightforward heuristic that leads to dramatic improvements in model performance, offering a valuable and practical lesson for weakly supervised learning from projected data in general.
3.  **Comprehensive evaluation:** The proposed methods are evaluated on two distinct types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard coarse-grained CoNLL datasets. The consistent effectiveness of the methods across these different settings demonstrates their robustness and general applicability.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the core methods:** The two main pillars of the work—annotation projection and representation projection (or model transfer via embeddings)—are well-established paradigms for cross-lingual tasks. The paper's contribution lies in the effective engineering, refinement, and combination of these existing ideas, rather than in proposing a fundamentally new approach. While the results are excellent, the conceptual novelty is somewhat incremental for a top-tier conference.
2.  **Lack of clarity on hyperparameter selection for data filtering:** The data selection scheme is a key contribution, yet the paper offers little insight into how its crucial thresholds (`q` and `n`) were chosen for the experiments in Table 2. It simply states that "different threshold parameters ... were tried and the best numbers ... reported." For the method to be reproducible and useful for other researchers, a more principled explanation of the tuning process is needed. For example, was a development set used? Without this information, it is difficult to know how to apply this otherwise promising technique to a new language or dataset.
3.  **The co-decoding schemes are not clearly described:** The descriptions of the two co-decoding methods in Section 5 are brief and difficult to fully understand. The "rank-based" scheme, in particular, is described in an ambiguous manner. A more precise, perhaps algorithmic, description of how the outputs of the two systems are merged would greatly enhance the clarity and reproducibility of this important component.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the process used to select the data selection thresholds (`q` and `n`) for the results presented in Table 2? Was a development set used for tuning? Could you offer any general guidance on how to set these parameters for a new language pair?
2.  The paper presents several architectural variants for each of the two main approaches (e.g., NN1 and NN2 for representation projection). Did you experiment with combining more than two systems? For instance, would a three-way combination of the annotation projection system (AP) and both neural network variants (NN1, NN2) yield further improvements?
3.  Could you provide more information about the in-house dataset? Specifically, what is the domain or genre of the text (e.g., newswire, web text)? This context is important for interpreting the results and understanding the settings in which the methods are most applicable.
