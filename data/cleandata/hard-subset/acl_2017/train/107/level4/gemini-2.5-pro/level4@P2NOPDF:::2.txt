This paper presents a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The proposed system leverages an ensemble of three distinct NER models: a Conditional Random Field (CRF), a Maximum Entropy Markov Model (MEMM), and a neural network-based model. The authors' primary contributions include two novel ensemble methods (one probability-based and one ranking-based) to combine the outputs of these models, a data selection strategy to identify high-quality training instances from comparable corpora, and a modified cross-lingual projection technique based on Mikolov et al.'s work.

Overall, the paper is well-structured and clearly written. The approach of combining multiple NER systems and the results achieved are promising.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Motivation for Model Variants:** The motivation for proposing a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is not sufficiently explained. The paper lacks details regarding the model's architecture and the hyperparameters used. To justify this contribution, it would be beneficial to include an ablation study or a direct comparison with the original CBOW and Continuous Skip-gram models. This would allow readers to quantify the improvement gained from the proposed modification. Additionally, given the use of a decay factor for context word embeddings, the authors might consider citing and comparing their approach to the exponential decay function used in Iacobacci et al. (2016) [1].

*   **Comparison of Projection Methods:** In a similar vein, the paper introduces a frequency-weighted projection method but does not empirically compare it against the original cross-lingual projection method from Mikolov et al. Demonstrating the superiority of the proposed variant through a direct comparison would significantly strengthen this contribution.

*   **Statistical Significance:** The paper claims that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant." However, it is unclear if this claim is supported by statistical significance testing. Reporting the results of such tests would provide stronger evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods and validate claims of significant improvements.

*   **Paper Organization:** The paper's organization could be improved with a few adjustments. The content of Section 4.4 seems to describe the experimental setup and would logically fit better at the beginning of Section 4.2. Furthermore, the discussion and analysis of the results presented in Table 2 would be more appropriately placed within the main evaluation section.

*   **Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a dedicated "Related Work" section. While the introduction currently touches upon some prior work, creating a separate section would allow for a more comprehensive literature review and better contextualize the paper's contributions within the field.

*   **Evaluation and Analysis:** The evaluation section feels condensed, spanning only 1.5 pages along with the conclusion. Given that the paper reports state-of-the-art results, the work would be more impactful with an expanded discussion. A more detailed error analysis or a deeper investigation into the behavior of the different model components and ensemble methods would be highly valuable to the reader.

**Suggested References:**
