This paper presents a framework for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), where annotations from a resource-rich source language are projected onto a target language using comparable corpora. The authors employ a suite of NER models, including Conditional Random Fields (CRF), Maximum Entropy Markov Models (MEMM), and a neural network-based approach. The core contributions of the paper are: (1) two ensemble methods (probability-based and ranking-based) to combine the outputs of the individual NER models; (2) a data selection scheme to identify high-quality training instances from the potentially noisy projected data; and (3) a variant of Mikolov et al.'s cross-lingual word embedding projection.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the proposed methods for combining model outputs and selecting training data achieve strong empirical results.

### **Strengths**

*   The proposed ensemble methods for combining diverse NER models are well-motivated and demonstrate a clear benefit over using any single model.
*   The data selection scheme for handling noisy projected labels is a practical and effective contribution to the cross-lingual transfer problem, addressing a key challenge in this domain.
*   The paper is easy to follow, and the experimental setup is clearly described.

### **Major Comments**

1.  **Justification and Ablation for Model Modifications:** The paper introduces two notable modifications to existing methods: a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model for word embeddings and a frequency-weighted cross-lingual projection. However, the motivation for these changes is not sufficiently justified. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation for why these modifications were necessary and, crucially, present ablation studies. A direct comparison with the standard CBOW model and the original Mikolov projection method would allow readers to quantify the specific improvements gained from these novel components and better appreciate their value. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the impact of these specific contributions.

2.  **Statistical Significance of Results:** The authors claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective... and the improvement is significant." To substantiate such claims, it is standard practice to perform statistical significance tests (e.g., a paired t-test or bootstrap resampling). The authors should report the results of these tests to confirm whether the observed improvements over baselines and model variants are statistically significant.

3.  **Insufficient Depth in Results Analysis:** While the paper achieves state-of-the-art results, the evaluation section is relatively brief. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth discussion and error analysis. For example, what types of entities are most challenging for the cross-lingual projection? How do the different combination schemes (probability vs. ranking) behave on different entity types or with varying levels of label noise? A deeper analysis would provide valuable insights beyond the aggregate F1 scores and offer a more complete picture of the system's performance.

### **Minor Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Related Work:** The introduction currently contains background information that would be better placed in a dedicated "Related Work" section. Creating a separate section would improve the paper's structure and allow for a more comprehensive review of prior art in cross-lingual NER, projection-based methods, and ensemble techniques.

*   **Paper Organization:** The paper's clarity could be further improved with minor restructuring. The content of Section 4.4 (describing the dataset) seems more appropriate at the beginning of Section 4.2 (Experiments). Additionally, the discussion of the results in Table 2 should be consolidated within the main evaluation section to keep all empirical analysis together.

*   **Embedding Decay Factor:** In the description of the modified CBOW model, the authors use a decay factor for surrounding word embeddings. They might find it useful to reference or compare their approach with similar techniques, such as the exponential decay used in [1].

In summary, this is a solid paper with promising results and several interesting contributions. Addressing the points above—particularly regarding the justification for model modifications, the inclusion of significance testing, and a deeper analysis of the results—would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its impact.
