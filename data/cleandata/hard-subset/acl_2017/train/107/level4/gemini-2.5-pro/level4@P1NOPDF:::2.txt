This paper presents an approach to cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) using a combination of different models. The authors employ Conditional Random Fields, Maximum Entropy Markov Models, and a neural network-based method. The paper also introduces two novel techniques (one probability-based, one ranking-based) for ensembling the outputs of these models, as well as a method for selecting high-quality training instances from comparable corpora. The cross-lingual projection is performed using a variant of the method proposed by Mikolov et al.

In general, the paper is well-structured and easy to follow, and the quality of the writing is high. The results from the combined models are particularly interesting and promising.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   The motivation for proposing a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is not fully explained. The paper provides few details about this model or the parameters used. It would be helpful to understand why this new variant was necessary. For instance, did the original CBOW model or the Continuous Skip-gram model yield poor results in this setting? To properly evaluate this contribution, I strongly suggest including baseline results from the original CBOW model. This would allow readers to directly assess the improvement gained from the proposed modifications.
*   Since the proposed model uses a decay factor for the surrounding embeddings, I suggest the authors also look at the exponential decay method used in [1].
*   Similarly, a direct comparison between the original cross-lingual projection method from Mikolov et al. and the proposed frequency-weighted variant is needed. The paper's contributions would be significantly strengthened by empirically demonstrating that the proposed method is superior to the original.
*   The paper claims that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant." Have statistical significance tests been conducted to support this claim? It is important to clarify whether the reported differences in results are statistically significant.
*   The paper's organization could be improved. For better readability and logical flow, I suggest integrating the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2. Additionally, the evaluation and discussion of the results in Table 2 seem more appropriate for the main evaluation section rather than their current location.
*   The paper lacks a dedicated "Related Work" section. While some of this information is currently included in the introduction, creating a separate section would provide a more structured and comprehensive overview of prior research, improving the paper's self-containedness.
*   The evaluation section is quite brief (approximately 1.5 pages, including the conclusion). Given that the paper reports state-of-the-art results, the work would be much stronger with a more extensive discussion and analysis of these results. For example, an error analysis could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed combination methods.
