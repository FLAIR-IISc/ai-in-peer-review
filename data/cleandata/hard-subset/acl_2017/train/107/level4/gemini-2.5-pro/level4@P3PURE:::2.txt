This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that combines the outputs of Conditional Random Fields, Maximum Entropy Markov Models, and neural network-based methods. The authors propose two novel techniques for this combination (one probability-based and one ranking-based), a method for selecting high-quality training instances from comparable corpora, and a variant of Mikolov et al.'s word embedding projection for cross-lingual transfer. The paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and clearly written. The results achieved by combining multiple NER systems are particularly interesting.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   The motivation for proposing a variation of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is not sufficiently explained. The authors do not provide much detail about this variant or the parameters used. It would be helpful to know if the original CBOW or Continuous Skip-gram models were tested and found to produce poor results. I suggest including baseline results from the original CBOW model to allow readers to analyze the improvement offered by the proposed approach. Given the use of a decay factor for surrounding embeddings, the authors might also consider the exponential decay approach in [1].

*   Similarly, a direct comparison between the original Mikolov et al.'s cross-lingual projection method and the proposed frequency-weighted projection is missing. The value of this contribution would be significantly strengthened by an empirical demonstration that the proposed method is superior to the original.

*   The authors claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant." Have tests of statistical significance been conducted to support this claim? Clarifying whether the reported differences in results are statistically significant would make the conclusions more robust.

*   The paper's structure could be improved. The content of Section 4.4 seems better suited to the beginning of Section 4.2, which would improve the overall flow. Additionally, the analysis of Table 2 should be moved from the model description to the evaluation section.

*   The paper would benefit from a dedicated "Related Work" section. While the introduction includes some of this information, creating a separate section would allow for a more comprehensive literature review and better contextualize the paper's contributions.

*   The evaluation section is quite short, spanning approximately 1.5 pages including the conclusion. Given that the paper reports state-of-the-art results, a more extensive discussion and in-depth analysis of the findings would be appreciated.
