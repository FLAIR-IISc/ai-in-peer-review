### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that do not require any human-annotated data in the target language. The first approach, Annotation Projection (AP), projects NER labels from a source language to a target language using comparable corpora. The second, Representation Projection (RP), projects target-language word embeddings into the source language's vector space, allowing a single source-language NER model to be applied directly. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of these two distinct systems.

I see the main contributions of this work as follows:

1.  **Effective Combination of Diverse Weak Supervision Signals:** The paper's strongest contribution is demonstrating that two different projection-based approaches—one operating in the label space (AP) and the other in the feature space (RP)—can be effectively combined to achieve state-of-the-art results. The co-decoding methods are well-motivated by the differing precision/recall characteristics of the individual systems.

2.  **A Heuristic for Noisy Data Selection:** The paper introduces a simple yet effective heuristic for selecting higher-quality training instances from the noisy data generated by annotation projection. This method proves particularly valuable when dealing with low-quality word alignments, significantly boosting performance.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work is its impressive results. The combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2002/2003 datasets for Spanish, Dutch, and German, outperforming the strong baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed combination strategy.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Practical Methods:** The two core approaches and their combination are well-motivated. The Annotation Projection system is shown to benefit greatly from a novel data selection scheme, which is crucial for handling the noise inherent in using comparable (rather than parallel) corpora. The Representation Projection method offers a simple and efficient way to perform direct model transfer. Finally, the co-decoding schemes are cleverly designed to leverage the complementary strengths of the two systems (e.g., the high precision of the AP system and the balanced performance of the RP system).

3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods across a wide range of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and on two different types of datasets: a fine-grained in-house dataset and the standard coarse-grained CoNLL benchmark. This thorough evaluation demonstrates the general applicability and robustness of the proposed techniques.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the proposed methods and experiments easy to follow and understand.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Ablation Studies for Methodological Choices:** The paper introduces custom variations of existing methods without sufficient justification or comparison against the original versions.
    *   In Section 4.1, the authors propose a variant of the CBOW model that uses a distance-decay weighting scheme. However, they provide no comparison to the standard CBOW or Skip-gram models. It is unclear whether this modification provides a tangible benefit for this task.
    *   Similarly, in Section 4.2, the cross-lingual embedding mapping is a weighted version of the one proposed by Mikolov et al. (2013b). An ablation study comparing this weighted approach to the original unweighted version would be necessary to validate this contribution and demonstrate its superiority. Without these comparisons, the necessity of these modifications is questionable.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Discussion of Results:** While the paper presents strong results, the evaluation section is brief and lacks in-depth analysis. The authors obtain state-of-the-art results, but there is little discussion about *why* the models perform as they do. For instance, why does the confidence-based co-decoding scheme work best for the CoNLL data, while the rank-based scheme is superior for the in-house data? An error analysis or a discussion of the types of entities each system correctly or incorrectly identifies would provide valuable insights and significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports improvements in F1 scores, but it is not stated whether these improvements are statistically significant. Given that some of the gains are modest (e.g., 1-3 F1 points), significance testing is crucial to confirm that the observed differences are not due to random chance.

4.  **Minor Structural Issues:**
    *   The paper lacks a dedicated "Related Work" section. While the introduction covers some previous work, a separate section would provide a more structured and comprehensive overview of the field.
    *   The inclusion of Table 2, an evaluation table, in the middle of the "Annotation Projection Approach" methods section (Section 3) is unconventional. This table would be more appropriately placed in the main "Experiments" section (Section 6) to maintain a clear separation between methodology and evaluation.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide justification or comparative results for your modified CBOW model and the weighted cross-lingual mapping? How do they perform against the standard implementations from Mikolov et al. (2013a, 2013b)? This would help clarify the impact of these specific modifications.

2.  Have you performed statistical significance tests to validate your results? It would be helpful to know if the improvements of the combined systems over the individual systems, as well as over prior state-of-the-art work, are statistically significant.

3.  Could you offer more insight into why different co-decoding schemes (confidence-based vs. rank-based) perform best on different datasets? Does this relate to the number of entity types, the nature of the data, or the relative performance of the individual projection systems on those datasets?
