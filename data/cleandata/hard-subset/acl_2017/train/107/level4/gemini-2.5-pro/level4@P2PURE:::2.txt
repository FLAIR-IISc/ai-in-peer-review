This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that utilizes Conditional Random Fields, Maximum Entropy Markov Models, and neural network-based methods. The authors also propose two techniques for ensembling the outputs of these models (one probability-based and one ranking-based) and a method for selecting optimal training instances from comparable cross-lingual corpora. Cross-lingual projection is performed using a variant of the method proposed by Mikolov et al. Overall, the paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and clearly written. The results achieved by combining the different model annotations are particularly interesting.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   The motivation for proposing a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is unclear. The paper provides limited detail about this variant and the parameters used. Did the original CBOW model or the Continuous Skip-gram model yield unsatisfactory results? To allow readers to better analyze the benefits of the proposed approach, I suggest including baseline results from the standard CBOW model.

*   Regarding the decay factor used for surrounding word embeddings, the authors might consider discussing or comparing their approach with the exponential decay method presented in [1].

*   Similar to the previous point, the paper would be strengthened by a direct comparison between the original cross-lingual projection method from Mikolov et al. and the proposed frequency-weighted projection. Such a comparison would better demonstrate the value of this contribution by showing that the proposed method is empirically superior.

*   The paper claims that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant." Have statistical significance tests been performed to support this claim? It is important to clarify whether the reported performance differences are statistically significant.

*   For improved clarity and structure, I suggest integrating the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2. Furthermore, the evaluation related to Table 2 seems misplaced; I recommend moving it to the main evaluation section to consolidate all results.

*   The paper lacks a dedicated Related Work section. While the introduction touches upon some related work, creating a separate section would provide a more comprehensive and structured overview of prior research in this area.

*   The evaluation section is quite brief, spanning only 1.5 pages including the conclusion. Given that the paper reports state-of-the-art results, a more in-depth discussion and analysis of these findings would be highly beneficial to the reader.
