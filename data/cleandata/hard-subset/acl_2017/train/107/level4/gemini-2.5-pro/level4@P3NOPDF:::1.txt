This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognition (NER) systems by projecting information from English to a target language using parallel corpora. While the paper addresses a significant and practical problem, its contributions are primarily incremental improvements upon well-established techniques.

**Strengths:**

The paper's main strength lies in its effective demonstration of data selection for projection-based NER. The results clearly show that filtering noisy projected annotations is crucial for building a high-quality system. The proposed quality score, while straightforward, proves to be a valuable component, leading to substantial performance gains. This result reinforces an important lesson for practitioners working with cross-lingual projection methods.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Novelty:** The overall approach of using cross-lingual projection via word alignments to bootstrap NER systems is not new. The individual methods proposed in the paper, such as data selection and system combination, represent incremental refinements of existing ideas rather than fundamentally novel approaches. For a paper at a premier conference like ACL, a stronger case for conceptual novelty would be expected, or alternatively, a more extensive and insightful analysis that yields new understanding.

2.  **Clarity on Data Selection Hyperparameters:** A significant concern is the lack of transparency in the experimental methodology for the data selection scheme. The paper presents a quality score formula but does not explain how the selection thresholds, which are critical for the method's success (as shown in Table 2), were chosen. The authors state only that "different thresholds were tried." It is essential to clarify whether these thresholds were tuned on a dedicated development set. Without this information, it is difficult to rule out the possibility of overfitting to the test data and, more importantly, it makes the method difficult to reproduce. The paper would be much stronger if it included details on the tuning process and offered guidance on how one might set these parameters for a new language pair or domain.

3.  **Clarity on System Combination:** The description of the method for combining the outputs of the two weakly supervised systems is unclear and difficult to follow. Despite careful reading, the mechanics of this algorithm remain elusive. To be a valuable contribution, the method must be described in enough detail for the reader to understand and reimplement it. The authors should consider providing a more formal definition, pseudocode, or a worked example to elucidate the process.

4.  **Experimental Scope and Analysis:** The paper develops several variants of weakly supervised systems but only explores the combination of two of them. A natural extension would be to investigate the effect of combining more than two systems. Did the authors explore this possibility? A more comprehensive analysis of different combination strategies could provide deeper insights into the complementary strengths of the various models.

5.  **Dataset Details:** The experiments are conducted on an "in-house" dataset. For the results to be properly contextualized and for the community to understand their generalizability, more information about this dataset is needed. Details about the domain, genre, and general characteristics of the texts would be highly beneficial for interpreting the findings.

In summary, while the paper tackles an important problem and demonstrates the effectiveness of careful data selection, its contributions are hampered by a lack of novelty and significant clarity issues in the description of its core methods and experimental setup. Addressing the points above would substantially improve the quality and impact of this work.
