This paper presents a framework for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The methodology leverages three distinct NER approaches: Conditional Random Fields, Maximum Entropy Markov Models, and neural network-based systems. The authors introduce two novel ensemble techniques (one based on probability, the other on ranking) to integrate the outputs from these models. Furthermore, they propose a data selection strategy for identifying optimal training instances within cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection is achieved through a modified version of the approach originally proposed by Mikolov. Overall, the paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a high standard of English. The performance gains from the proposed ensemble methods are particularly noteworthy.

**Detailed comments:**

The motivation for developing a variant of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model is not entirely clear. The paper would benefit from additional details regarding this modification, including the parameters used. It is also unclear whether standard CBOW or Skip-gram models yielded unsatisfactory results. To better contextualize the contribution, I recommend including baseline results from the original CBOW model, which would allow readers to quantify the improvement offered by the proposed variant.

Regarding the use of a decay factor for contextual embeddings, the authors might find the exponential decay method described in [1] relevant to their work.

In a similar vein to the comment on the CBOW model, the paper's claims would be strengthened by a direct comparison between the original Mikolov cross-lingual projection and the proposed frequency-weighted variant. Demonstrating the tangible benefits of the proposed method over the established baseline is crucial for validating its contribution.

The paper claims that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and the improvement is significant." To substantiate this claim, have statistical significance tests been performed? Clarifying whether the observed differences in performance are statistically significant would add rigor to the analysis.

For improved clarity and flow, I suggest merging the content of Section 4.4 into the beginning of Section 4.2. Additionally, the discussion pertaining to Table 2 seems more appropriate for the main evaluation section rather than its current location.

The paper lacks a dedicated Related Work section. While the introduction covers some relevant prior work, creating a separate section would provide a more structured and comprehensive literature review.

The evaluation section, which spans approximately 1.5 pages including the conclusion, feels brief. Given that the paper achieves state-of-the-art results, a more in-depth discussion and error analysis would be highly valuable to the reader.

**Suggested Reference:**
