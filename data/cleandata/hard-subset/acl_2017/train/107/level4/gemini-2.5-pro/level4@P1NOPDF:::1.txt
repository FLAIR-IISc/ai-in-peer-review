This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing NER systems, which rely on projecting annotations from English into a target language. The overall approach builds upon established techniques, and the individual methods proposed are incremental improvements to existing work. While the results are promising, the paper's contributions may not be sufficiently novel for a top-tier conference like ACL.

One of the paper's main contributions is its data selection scheme. The formula used to calculate the quality score is commendably straightforward. However, the methodology lacks clarity regarding the selection thresholds used for Table 2. The authors state only that "different thresholds were tried" but do not specify whether this tuning was performed on a development set. This is a critical omission. The evaluation results clearly demonstrate the importance of this data selection step, yet without guidance on how to tune these parameters, it is difficult for other researchers to reproduce the work or apply the method to a new dataset or language pair.

Another contribution is the method for combining the outputs of the two systems developed in the paper. Unfortunately, the description of this combination strategy is unclear, making it difficult for the reader to fully understand the proposed technique.

Given that the paper presents several variants for each of the proposed methods and shows the benefit of combining two systems, a natural question is whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems could yield further improvements. It would be valuable to know if the authors explored this possibility.

Finally, the paper would be strengthened by providing more details about the "in-house" dataset. Information about the domain or genre of these texts would help readers better contextualize the results.
