### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any human-annotated data. The authors propose and evaluate two primary projection-based approaches. The first, **Annotation Projection**, transfers NER tags from an English source text to an aligned target language text. The second, **Representation Projection**, maps word embeddings from a target language into the English embedding space, allowing a single English NER model to be applied directly to the projected target language representations. The authors also introduce methods to combine the outputs of these two systems.

The main contributions of this work, in order of significance, are:

1.  **Effective System Combination for State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper's strongest contribution is demonstrating that combining two distinct weakly supervised approaches (annotation projection and representation projection) via simple co-decoding schemes leads to significant performance gains. The resulting system outperforms two recent state-of-the-art methods on the standard CoNLL benchmark datasets, establishing a new strong baseline for the task.
2.  **A Heuristic Data Selection Scheme for Annotation Projection:** The authors propose a simple yet highly effective heuristic for filtering noisy training instances generated by annotation projection. This method provides substantial improvements, particularly for language pairs with lower-quality alignments, addressing a critical and practical challenge in projection-based methods.
3.  **A Thorough Evaluation of Representation Projection for NER:** The paper provides a solid implementation and evaluation of a representation projection approach for cross-lingual NER. While the core idea of projecting embeddings is not new, its application to NER and its direct comparison and combination with the more traditional annotation projection approach provide valuable insights for the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its experimental results. The proposed combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets for Spanish, Dutch, and German, outperforming the strong baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) (Table 5). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods on a well-established benchmark.

2.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Approaches:** The paper intelligently combines two methods with different characteristics. The annotation projection system tends to achieve high precision but low recall, while the representation projection system offers a more balanced profile. The co-decoding schemes are well-motivated by this observation and are shown to consistently improve upon the individual systems across multiple languages and datasets (Tables 3 and 4), yielding F1 score improvements of up to 6.2 points. This provides a practical and effective recipe for building robust cross-lingual systems.

3.  **Simple and Impactful Data Selection Method:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a valuable contribution. It is based on a straightforward and intuitive quality metric. The results in Table 2 clearly show its importance: for languages with noisy alignments like Japanese and Korean, this filtering step dramatically improves performance (+13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean). This finding highlights that for annotation projection, data quality is more important than quantity, a useful lesson for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core ideas of annotation projection and representation projection are well-established in the field of cross-lingual NLP. The paper's contribution is more in the effective engineering, refinement (e.g., the data selection heuristic), and combination of these existing paradigms rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new approach. While the results are excellent, the conceptual novelty is somewhat incremental for a top-tier conference.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper does not specify how the crucial thresholds for the data selection scheme (`q` and `n` in Table 2) were selected. The text states, "we have tried different threshold parameters... and reported the best numbers," which raises concerns about potential tuning on the test set. For a truly unsupervised method, it is essential to describe how these parameters could be set for a new language without access to any labeled data (e.g., a development set). Without this information, the method's applicability and the reproducibility of the results are diminished.

3.  **Insufficient Details on In-House Datasets:** A significant portion of the evaluation is performed on in-house datasets with a fine-grained set of over 50 entity types. However, the paper provides very few details about this data, such as the domain of the text (e.g., newswire, web text), size, or the principles behind the annotation schema. This lack of information makes it difficult to fully interpret the results on these datasets and assess the generalizability of the findings to other fine-grained NER tasks.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the data selection scheme in Section 3.1: How were the quality and entity number thresholds (`q` and `n`) reported in Table 2 determined? Was a development set used for tuning? If not, could you elaborate on how a practitioner might select appropriate values for these crucial hyperparameters for a new target language where no labeled data is available?

2.  Could you please provide more details about the in-house datasets? Specifically, what is the domain or genre of the texts, and could you briefly explain the motivation for using such a fine-grained (50+) entity type set? This context would be very helpful for interpreting the results in Table 3.

3.  Your co-decoding schemes effectively combine two systems. Given that you developed two variants of the representation projection model (NN1 and NN2), did you experiment with combining more than two systems, for instance, by ensembling the outputs of AP, NN1, and NN2?
