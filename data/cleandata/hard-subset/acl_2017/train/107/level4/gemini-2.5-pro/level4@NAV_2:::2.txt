This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), designed to function without any human-annotated data in the target language. The work introduces two primary methods: an annotation projection approach that uses a novel heuristic scheme to select high-quality training instances from noisy comparable corpora, and a representation projection approach that maps target-language word embeddings into a source-language space, allowing a source-language NER model to be applied directly. Furthermore, the authors propose two co-decoding schemes to effectively combine the outputs of these complementary systems.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A robust, combined system for cross-lingual NER that integrates two distinct projection methodologies (annotation and representation). The proposed co-decoding schemes effectively leverage the complementary strengths of each method, achieving state-of-the-art results on standard benchmark datasets.
2.  An effective heuristic for data selection in annotation projection. This scheme filters noisy, automatically projected labels, demonstrating significant performance gains, particularly in scenarios where corpus alignment quality is low.
3.  A successful application of representation projection for direct model transfer in the NER task. This approach creates a universal NER system that can be applied to new languages without retraining by mapping their word embeddings to a common space.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The proposed combined system achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL test data, outperforming two strong, recent baselines. The evaluation is thorough, covering both standard academic benchmarks (CoNLL) and in-house datasets with a much larger, fine-grained entity set, which demonstrates the method's applicability and robustness across different domains and label granularities.
2.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Methods:** A key strength is the intelligent combination of two different cross-lingual transfer paradigms. The paper shows that annotation projection tends to yield high precision while representation projection offers a more balanced precision-recall profile. The proposed co-decoding schemes are simple but powerful, effectively merging these two views to produce a final output superior to either individual system.
3.  **Practical and Well-Motivated Data Selection Heuristic:** The proposed scheme for selecting high-quality training data from noisy projections is a practical solution to a critical problem in annotation projection. The experiments in Table 2 clearly validate its effectiveness, showing dramatic improvements (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean) where the underlying alignments are weakest, highlighting its real-world utility.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The methods are described logically, and the motivations behind the different components are well-explained, contributing to the work's potential impact and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Methodological Novelties:** The paper introduces modifications to established techniques, such as a variant of the CBOW model and a weighted version of Mikolov et al.'s cross-lingual mapping. However, these choices are not sufficiently justified, and no ablation studies or comparisons against the original, standard methods are provided. Without this analysis, it is difficult to assess whether these modifications offer a genuine advantage and what their specific contribution is to the overall performance.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis in the Evaluation:** Although the empirical results are strong, the evaluation section is relatively brief and lacks in-depth analysis. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the results, including an error analysis to shed light on the remaining challenges, or a qualitative examination of why one co-decoding scheme outperforms another on different datasets. Given the state-of-the-art claims, such an analysis is crucial for providing deeper insights to the community.
3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The authors claim that improvements are "significant," particularly for the data selection scheme. However, the paper does not report any statistical significance tests. This makes it challenging to determine if the observed performance gains are statistically meaningful or simply due to random variance in the evaluation sets.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for developing a variant of the CBOW model and a weighted version of the cross-lingual embedding mapping? To better isolate their contributions, could you provide a comparison against the standard, un-weighted mapping from Mikolov et al. (2013b) and the original CBOW or Skip-gram models?
2.  Have you conducted any tests of statistical significance to confirm that the reported improvements—particularly the large F1 gains from the data selection scheme (Table 2) and the advantages over prior work (Table 5)—are statistically significant?
