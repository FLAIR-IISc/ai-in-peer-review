### Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that do not require any human-annotated data in the target language. The first approach, based on annotation projection, creates training data for a target language by projecting labels from an English NER system onto comparable corpora. The second approach, based on representation projection, maps target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space, allowing a single English NER model to be applied directly to the target language. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems. The proposed methods are evaluated on both in-house and public CoNLL datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL benchmark.

### Contributions
1.  **An effective heuristic data selection method for annotation projection.** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful method for filtering noisy, automatically-labeled training data generated via annotation projection. This selection scheme is shown to provide substantial improvements, particularly for language pairs with low-quality word alignments, where the projected data is inherently noisier. For instance, the method yields F1 score improvements of +20.0 for Korean and +13.9 for Japanese on the in-house dataset.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical study and a strong combined system for cross-lingual NER.** The paper presents a thorough comparison of two distinct projection methodologies (annotation vs. representation) and their variants across multiple languages and datasets. The proposed co-decoding schemes effectively combine the complementary strengths of the two base systems, resulting in a final model that outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems on the standard CoNLL cross-lingual NER task.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. The combined system surpasses two recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL test data for Spanish, Dutch, and German (Table 5). Achieving a new state-of-the-art on a well-established benchmark is a significant contribution and provides a strong baseline for future work.
2.  **Demonstrated Effectiveness of the Data Selection Scheme:** The proposed heuristic for selecting high-quality training data from a noisy, projected corpus is a valuable contribution. As shown in Table 2, the gains from this filtering step are dramatic for languages like Japanese and Korean, where alignment quality is lower. This highlights the practical importance of addressing the noise inherent in projection-based methods and provides a simple, effective solution.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation Across Diverse Datasets:** The authors validate their methods on two distinct types of NER datasets: a fine-grained, in-house dataset with over 50 entity types and the standard, coarse-grained CoNLL dataset. Evaluating across seven different target languages and different annotation schemes strengthens the paper's conclusions and demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed techniques.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity in Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of a clear explanation for how the data selection thresholds (quality score `q` and entity number `n` in Table 2) were chosen. The paper states that "we have tried different threshold parameters," which raises concerns about potential overfitting to the test set. Without a description of a principled tuning process on a held-out development set, the reported gains, while impressive, are difficult to interpret and the method's reproducibility is questionable. One cannot be certain how to set these crucial parameters for a new language or dataset.
2.  **Limited Novelty in Core Methodologies:** The core ideas of annotation projection and representation projection (cross-lingual embeddings) are not new to the field of cross-lingual NLP. While the paper's implementation and combination of these methods are effective, the contributions are more incremental improvements and strong engineering rather than the introduction of a fundamentally novel paradigm. This might be seen as a limitation for a top-tier conference like ACL.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on the Co-Decoding Scheme:** The description of the co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based one, is brief. The text explains that the scheme "will first include all the entities detected by the annotation projection system" and then add non-conflicting entities from the other system. However, the exact mechanism for resolving different types of conflicts (e.g., partial overlaps with different labels) is not fully elaborated, which could make reimplementation difficult. A more detailed explanation or a worked example would significantly improve the clarity of this component.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the data selection scheme in Section 3.1 and the results in Table 2, could you please clarify how the thresholds for the quality score (`q`) and entity number (`n`) were selected for each language? Was a dedicated development set used for tuning these hyperparameters? If so, details on this process would be crucial for assessing the validity of the results.
2.  Could you provide more detail on the domain and genre of the texts in the "in-house" dataset? This information would provide valuable context for interpreting the results in Table 3 and understanding potential domain effects.
3.  The paper focuses on combining the outputs of two systems (one annotation-based and one representation-based). Did you experiment with combining more than two systems, for example, by ensembling the Annotation Projection system with both the NN1 and NN2 variants of the Representation Projection system?
