### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), designed to function without any human-annotated data in the target language. The overall system is built on two complementary projection methods.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **An effective ensemble system for cross-lingual NER.** The main strength of the paper lies in its successful combination of two distinct and complementary weakly supervised methods: annotation projection and representation projection. The proposed co-decoding schemes intelligently leverage the differing precision/recall characteristics of each method, leading to state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks.
2.  **A novel data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** The paper introduces an intuitive and effective heuristic for filtering noisy data generated through annotation projection on comparable corpora. This method is shown to yield substantial performance gains, particularly for language pairs with low-quality word alignments, making the annotation projection approach more robust.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The most compelling argument for acceptance is the strong performance of the proposed system. On the CoNLL 2002/2003 test data, the combined model outperforms two recent and strong cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), particularly for German. This establishes a new state-of-the-art and provides a robust baseline for future work in this area.

2.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Approaches:** The authors thoughtfully design a system that combines two methods with different underlying principles and error profiles. The annotation projection (AP) system tends towards high precision, while the representation projection (RP) system offers a more balanced profile. The paper demonstrates that the proposed co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based one, consistently improve upon the individual components, achieving F1 score gains of up to 6.2 points on the in-house datasets. This highlights a well-engineered and synergistic system design.

3.  **Robustness via Effective Data Selection:** The proposed data selection heuristic for the annotation projection component is a valuable contribution. As evidenced by Table 2, this simple scheme provides dramatic improvements for languages like Japanese (+13.9 F1) and Korean (+20.0 F1), where the underlying word alignment quality is lower. By filtering out low-confidence projections, the method prevents noisy data from degrading the performance of the target-language NER model, a critical step for making annotation projection practical.

4.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The two core approaches are presented in distinct sections, their combination is logically motivated, and the experimental setup is straightforward. This clarity makes the work accessible and facilitates reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Methodological Modifications:** The paper introduces variants of two established methods—a weighted version of the CBOW model and a weighted formulation for learning cross-lingual embedding mappings. However, the motivation for these changes is not fully substantiated with empirical evidence. The paper would be significantly stronger if it included ablation studies comparing these proposed variants against their original, un-weighted counterparts (e.g., the standard word2vec CBOW model and Mikolov et al.'s original projection method). Without this comparison, the impact of these specific modifications remains unclear.

2.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports numerous results and claims that improvements are "significant" (e.g., in the conclusion of Section 3.2). However, no statistical significance tests are reported. Given the number of experiments and the relatively small differences between some systems, it is crucial to establish whether the observed improvements are statistically meaningful or could be due to random variation. This is a notable omission for a strongly empirical paper.

3.  **Limited Analysis and Discussion of Results:** While the paper presents comprehensive results across multiple datasets and languages, the evaluation section (Section 6) is largely descriptive and lacks in-depth analysis. For instance, why does the NN1 architecture outperform NN2 on the CoNLL datasets, while the reverse is true for the in-house data? What kinds of errors are corrected by the co-decoding schemes? A more thorough error analysis or discussion would provide deeper insights into the behavior of the models and offer more valuable takeaways for the community.

4.  **Minor Organizational Issues:** The paper's structure could be slightly improved. The related work discussion in Section 4.4 would be more naturally placed at the beginning of Section 4 to provide context before detailing the method. Similarly, the initial evaluation of the data selection scheme (Table 2) feels disconnected from the main evaluation in Section 6; integrating it into the main experiments section would improve the paper's narrative flow. Finally, a dedicated "Related Work" section would be beneficial for contextualizing the contributions more broadly.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide results comparing your proposed variant of the CBOW model to the original implementation from Mikolov et al. (2013a)? Similarly, can you show an empirical comparison between your weighted cross-lingual mapping (Equation 7) and the original unweighted formulation? This would help clarify the impact of these specific modifications.

2.  Have you performed any tests of statistical significance for your main results, particularly for the improvements gained from the data selection scheme (Table 2) and the co-decoding methods (Tables 3, 4, and 5)? Reporting p-values would greatly strengthen your claims.
