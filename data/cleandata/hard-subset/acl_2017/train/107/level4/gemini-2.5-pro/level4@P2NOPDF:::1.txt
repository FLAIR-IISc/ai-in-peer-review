This paper presents several weakly supervised methods for developing Named Entity Recognizers (NERs) based on cross-lingual projection from English. While the paper explores a number of variations, the overall approach is not new, and the individual methods are largely incremental improvements on existing work. For a paper at a premier venue like ACL, a more substantial novel contribution would be expected.

One of the paper's primary contributions is its data selection scheme. The formula for calculating the data quality score is commendably straightforward. However, the methodology lacks crucial details. It is unclear how the filtering thresholds presented in Table 2 were determined; the paper only states that "different thresholds were tried." There is no mention of whether a development set was used for this tuning process. This omission is significant, as the evaluation results clearly demonstrate the critical importance of data selection. Without a clear protocol for parameter tuning, it is difficult for other researchers to reproduce the work or apply the method effectively to new datasets or language pairs.

Another contribution is the combination of outputs from the two systems developed in the paper. Unfortunately, the description of this combination method is not sufficiently clear to allow for a full understanding of the technical details.

The paper presents a number of variants for each of the proposed methods. Given this, a natural question arises: did the authors investigate combining more than two of these weakly supervised systems? An analysis in this direction could provide further insights into the potential and limitations of such an ensemble approach.

Finally, the paper would be strengthened by providing more details about the "in-house" dataset. Information regarding the domain, genre, and other characteristics of the texts is essential for interpreting the experimental results and assessing their generalizability.
