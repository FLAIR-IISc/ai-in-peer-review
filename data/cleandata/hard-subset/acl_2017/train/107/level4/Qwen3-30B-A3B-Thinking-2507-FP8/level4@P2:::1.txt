

**Summary and Contributions**  
The paper introduces two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations: (1) *annotation projection* with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy comparable corpora, and (2) *representation projection* via cross-lingual word embedding mapping. The primary contribution is the data selection scheme, which significantly improves NER accuracy when alignment quality is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese). The second contribution is the representation projection approach, enabling direct NER model transfer without retraining. The third contribution is the co-decoding schemes that fuse outputs from both methods, yielding consistent gains over individual systems (up to +6.2 F1).  

**Strengths**  
1. **Data selection scheme demonstrates clear empirical impact.** The heuristic quality metric (based on entity tag frequency) effectively filters noisy projection data. Table 2 shows dramatic improvements: for Japanese (alignment F1 69.4), data selection boosts F1 from 41.1 to 55.0 (+13.9), while Korean (alignment F1 62.0) sees a +20.0 gain. This addresses a critical gap in prior work, which ignored alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs.  
2. **Co-decoding achieves robust gains over baselines.** The rank-based scheme (prioritizing high-precision annotation projection outputs) consistently outperforms individual systems across languages (Table 3). For German, it achieves 69.3 F1 (vs. 66.4 for annotation projection alone), reducing the gap to supervised learning (72.6 F1) by 3.3 points.  
3. **Strong SOTA comparison on CoNLL data.** The combined system (co-decoding AP+NN1) outperforms Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL test sets (Table 5), particularly for German (+2.7 F1 over Tsai et al.). This establishes a compelling new baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Threshold selection lacks methodological rigor.** The data selection scheme (Equation 6) uses arbitrary thresholds (e.g., *q*=0.8, *n*=4 for Japanese), but the paper provides no justification for these values. Was tuning performed on a development set? Without this detail, the approach is impractical for new language pairs. The claim that "different thresholds were tried" (p. 6) is insufficient; a clear procedure (e.g., grid search on dev data) is needed.  
2. **Co-decoding mechanism is underspecified.** The rank-based scheme’s "priority" mechanism (Section 5) is described vaguely: "it gives higher priority to the annotation projection system" without explaining *how* conflicts are resolved beyond excluding overlapping entities. For instance, if annotation projection detects "United States" as GPE and representation projection detects it as ORGANIZATION, which tag is chosen? The paper lacks pseudocode or algorithmic detail, hindering reproducibility.  
3. **Limited exploration of system combinations.** The authors propose combining only two systems (annotation + representation projection) but do not test: (a) integrating more than two weakly supervised methods (e.g., adding a third projection approach), or (b) using different neural architectures (e.g., NN1 vs. NN2) in co-decoding. This omission limits the scope of their fusion contribution.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. For the data selection scheme, please clarify whether thresholds (*q*, *n*) were tuned on a development set. If not, how should practitioners set these parameters for a new language pair?  
2. Provide a concrete example of how the rank-based co-decoding resolves conflicting entity tags (e.g., "United States" labeled as GPE vs. ORGANIZATION). Include the exact decision rule.  
3. Did you experiment with combining the representation projection system (NN2) with the annotation projection system in the rank-based scheme? If not, why was NN1 preferred (as in Table 3)?  

**Additional Comments**  
The "in-house" dataset’s text types (e.g., news, social media) are not described. This context is critical for assessing generalizability, especially since CoNLL data (Table 4) shows different performance patterns (e.g., NN1 outperforms NN2 for CoNLL German). Clarify this to contextualize results.
