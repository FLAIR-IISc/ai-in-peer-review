

Summary: The paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation. The primary contributions are: (1) a heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves accuracy on noisy comparable corpora (especially when alignment is poor), (2) a representation projection method using language-independent word embeddings to transfer NER models directly, and (3) two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two approaches for better performance. The authors demonstrate that their combined systems outperform prior work on CoNLL data.

Strengths:
1. The data selection scheme (Section 3.1) is a substantial practical contribution. By leveraging entity frequency distributions to filter noisy projection-labeled data, it achieves +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean—dramatic gains where alignment quality is low (Table 2). This addresses a critical flaw in prior annotation projection work that ignored alignment accuracy during data selection.
2. The representation projection approach (Section 4) provides a clean, scalable solution for model transfer. Projecting target-language embeddings to English via weighted least squares (Equation 7) avoids retraining for new languages and achieves strong results (Table 3), particularly for German (F1 69.3 vs. 64.9 for NN2 alone).
3. The rank-based co-decoding scheme (Section 5) is well-motivated and effective. It leverages the complementary strengths of the two approaches (annotation projection’s high precision vs. representation projection’s balanced recall) to achieve the highest F1 scores across all languages (e.g., +6.2 F1 for Portuguese over the best single system).

Weaknesses:
1. **Statistical significance is unverified.** The paper claims "significant improvement" for the data selection scheme (Table 2), but provides no statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to confirm these gains are not due to chance. This is critical for NLP evaluation and must be addressed.
2. **Insufficient comparison to foundational work.** The representation projection method builds on Mikolov et al. (2013b), but the paper lacks a direct comparison of their weighted least squares formulation (Equation 7) versus the original method. Without this, it’s unclear whether the improvements stem from the weighting scheme or other factors.
3. **Missing related work section.** The introduction covers some prior work but fails to systematically categorize it (e.g., annotation projection vs. model transfer). A dedicated related work section would better contextualize the novelty of the data selection scheme and representation projection.

Questions to Authors:
1. The CBOW variant (Section 4.1) uses a distance-weighted context but doesn’t justify why this outperforms standard CBOW or skip-gram. Please report results for both baselines to clarify the contribution of the weight decay.
2. Section 4.4 claims flexibility in mapping to English, but the method still requires a seed dictionary for each target language. How does this compare to dictionary-free approaches like those in Vulić & Moens (2015)? Could the dictionary size (5K–6K entries) become a bottleneck for low-resource languages?
3. The rank-based co-decoding (Section 5) prioritizes annotation projection outputs. Given that annotation projection has lower recall, how does this scheme avoid suppressing true entities that representation projection detects but annotation projection misses? Please elaborate on the conflict resolution mechanism.
