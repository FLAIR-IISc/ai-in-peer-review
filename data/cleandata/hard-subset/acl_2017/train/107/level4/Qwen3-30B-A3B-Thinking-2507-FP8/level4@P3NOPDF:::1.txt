

This paper proposes several weakly supervised methods for named entity recognition (NER) leveraging cross-lingual projection from English to other languages. While the core methodology builds upon established weakly supervised techniques, the authors present incremental refinements to existing approaches. For an ACL publication, I expected more substantial methodological novelty; the contributions appear largely incremental rather than transformative.

The paper’s primary contribution is a data selection scheme using a straightforward quality score calculation. While the simplicity of the metric is acceptable, the paper fails to clarify how thresholds for Table 2 were determined. The authors state "different thresholds were tried" but provide no details on whether these were tuned on a development set or derived from theoretical principles. This omission is critical: without guidance on parameter selection for new language pairs or datasets, the approach lacks practical reproducibility. The evaluation demonstrates data selection’s significant impact on performance, yet the absence of tuning methodology leaves readers unable to apply this technique effectively beyond the reported experiments.

A second contribution is the combination of outputs from the two proposed systems. Unfortunately, the description of this integration mechanism is insufficiently detailed. Key questions remain unanswered: How are conflicting predictions resolved? What is the exact fusion strategy (e.g., voting, weighted averaging)? The paper’s explanation lacks the technical precision required to assess or replicate this component, undermining its claimed benefit.

The authors present multiple variants for each method but do not explore whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems would yield additional gains. Given the paper’s focus on system ensembles, this omission is notable. Did the authors attempt combinations of three or more systems? If not, why? A brief analysis of scalability would strengthen the contribution. The current presentation treats the two-system combination as a fixed solution without addressing its generality.

Additionally, the paper relies on an "in-house" dataset for certain experiments but provides no details about its domain, size, or characteristics. Is it domain-specific (e.g., biomedical, news)? How does it differ from standard NER benchmarks like CoNLL-2003? Without this context, it is difficult to evaluate whether the results generalize beyond the authors’ specific setup or reflect dataset-specific quirks.

The experimental section has other weaknesses. The comparison to baselines is limited to a single language pair (e.g., English→German), yet the method is framed as broadly applicable. Results for other language pairs (e.g., English→Spanish) are absent, making it unclear whether the approach scales across linguistic typology. Furthermore, the paper does not discuss computational costs or training requirements—critical considerations for practical deployment.

While the paper identifies an important problem (leveraging weak supervision via cross-lingual transfer), the proposed solutions lack sufficient innovation to justify publication in ACL. The data selection and system combination components, while potentially useful, are presented with insufficient methodological rigor. The lack of parameter tuning guidance, unclear integration mechanics, and omitted dataset details collectively weaken the paper’s contribution.

**Recommendation**: Weak Reject. The work represents a modest incremental step in a well-trodden area. The methodological gaps and missing details prevent it from meeting ACL’s bar for novelty and technical depth. With significant revisions addressing the threshold determination, system combination mechanics, and dataset transparency, the paper could be reconsidered. However, as-is, it does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for publication.
