

Review:  
This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model combining conditional random fields (CRFs), maximum entropy Markov models (MEMMs), and neural network approaches. The authors propose two ensemble methods (probability-based and ranking-based) for integrating these models, alongside a novel data selection strategy for identifying high-quality training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projections leverage a frequency-weighted variant of Mikolov’s method. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the results demonstrate promising improvements over baselines.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **CBOW Adaptation Motivation:** The paper introduces a modified Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model but provides insufficient justification or comparative results. It is unclear why CBOW (instead of skip-gram) was chosen, or whether the original Mikolov model underperformed. The authors should include ablation results comparing CBOW, skip-gram, and the proposed variant to validate the design choice. Without this, the contribution of the CBOW adaptation remains ambiguous.  

2. **Cross-lingual Projection Comparison:** The frequency-weighted projection method is presented as an improvement over Mikolov’s original approach, but the paper lacks a direct comparison of their method against the baseline. Readers cannot assess whether the proposed weighting scheme genuinely outperforms the original. A table or analysis contrasting both projections (e.g., on a small validation set) would strengthen this contribution.  

3. **Statistical Significance:** The claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is very effective" requires statistical validation. The authors should report significance tests (e.g., t-tests or bootstrap resampling) for the reported performance gains. Without this, the magnitude of improvement remains unverified.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- **Section Structure:** Section 4.4 (on data selection) should be moved to the beginning of Section 4.2 for logical flow. Similarly, Table 2’s evaluation details belong in the dedicated results section (Section 4.3), not Section 4.2.  
- **Related Work:** The paper lacks a dedicated related work section. The current introduction overlaps with this content; splitting it into "Introduction" and "Related Work" would improve organization and contextualize contributions.  
- **Evaluation Depth:** The evaluation (1.5 pages) is concise but superficial. Given the state-of-the-art results, the authors should elaborate on *why* their approach outperforms prior work. For instance: How do the ensemble methods specifically mitigate cross-lingual noise? What error types are reduced? A deeper analysis of failure cases or linguistic phenomena would add substantial value.  
- **Technical Clarifications:**  
  - The phrase "maximum entropy Markov" should be corrected to "maximum entropy Markov models" (MEMMs).  
  - The decay factor for surrounding embeddings should be explicitly compared to exponential decay (e.g., as in [1]). This contextualizes the design choice.  

**Suggested Improvements:**  
- Integrate Section 4.4 into Section 4.2 to streamline the methodology description.  
- Add a dedicated "Related Work" subsection early in the paper.  
- Expand the evaluation to discuss *how* the proposed methods address cross-lingual NER challenges (e.g., lexical gaps, morphological differences).  
- Include statistical significance tests for all reported improvements.  
- Provide CBOW ablation results and a direct comparison of projection methods.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in cross-lingual NER with a practical ensemble approach. The core ideas (data selection, projection weighting) hold promise, but the current presentation lacks sufficient evidence to fully validate their superiority. Addressing the above concerns—particularly the CBOW justification, statistical validation, and deeper result analysis—would significantly strengthen the contribution. The writing is clear, but structural and technical gaps currently limit the paper’s impact.
