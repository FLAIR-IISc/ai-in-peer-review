

Summary: This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to improve the quality of weakly labeled data, and (2) representation projection that maps target language word embeddings to a source language space to enable direct application of the source language NER system. The primary contribution is the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low. The secondary contribution is the representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer. The tertiary contribution is the co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems.

Strengths: The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrates substantial improvements in NER performance (up to +20.0 F1 score for Korean) when alignment accuracy is low, addressing a key limitation of existing annotation projection methods. The combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on CoNLL test data, establishing a strong baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and both in-house and open NER datasets.

Weaknesses: The overall approach is incremental rather than novel, with the individual methods being improvements of existing techniques rather than fundamentally new contributions. For a top-tier conference like ACL, more novel approaches would be expected. The threshold selection for the data selection scheme (Table 2) is not clearly explained, and it is unclear how these thresholds would generalize to new language pairs or datasets. The co-decoding schemes are not sufficiently well-described, making it difficult to understand how they combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems.

Questions to Authors: How were the threshold parameters (q, n) for the data selection scheme determined? Was this tuning performed on a development set, and if so, how was the development set selected? Could you provide more details about the "in-house" dataset, particularly the types of texts used (e.g., newswire, social media, etc.) and how they compare to the CoNLL datasets? The paper mentions multiple variants of the methods but doesn't explore combining more than two weakly supervised systems. Have the authors considered or tried combining more than two systems, and if so, what were the results?
