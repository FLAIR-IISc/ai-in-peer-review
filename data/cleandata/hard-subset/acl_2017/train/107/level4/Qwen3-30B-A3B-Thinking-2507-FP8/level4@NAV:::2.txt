

This paper presents a model for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER). The authors utilize conditional random fields, maximum entropy Markov models, and neural network-based NER approaches. Additionally, they propose two methods for integrating these model outputs (probability-based and ranking-based fusion) and a technique for selecting optimal training instances from cross-lingual comparable corpora. Cross-lingual projection employs a variant of Mikolov's method. Overall, the paper is well-structured, clearly written, and maintains strong English quality. The results from combined annotations are promising.  

Detailed comments:  
The motivation for proposing a Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model variation requires clarification. Insufficient details are provided regarding this adaptation or its parameters. Was the original model (or Continuous Skip-gram) yielding suboptimal results? Including CBOW performance metrics would enable readers to assess the efficacy of this modification.  
Regarding the decay factor applied to surrounding embeddings, consider incorporating the exponential decay mechanism described in [1].  
For the frequency-weighted cross-lingual projections, please explicitly compare them against Mikolov's original approach. Demonstrating tangible superiority through comparative results would significantly strengthen this contribution.  
The claim that "the proposed data selection scheme is highly effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled data and yields significant improvement" requires statistical validation. Please confirm whether the observed performance differences are statistically significant.  
I recommend relocating Section 4.4 content to the beginning of Section 4.2 for improved flow, and moving Table 2's evaluation to the dedicated evaluation section.  
A dedicated related work section is missing. The current introduction partially covers this; splitting it into distinct introduction and related work sections would enhance organization.  
The evaluation (1.5 pages including conclusion) is concise despite achieving state-of-the-art results. A more thorough discussion and analysis of these results would be valuable.  

Suggested reference:  
[1] Iacobacci, I., Pilehvar, M. T., & Navigli, R. (2016). Embeddings for word sense disambiguation: An evaluation study. In *Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics* (Vol. 1, pp. 897–907).
