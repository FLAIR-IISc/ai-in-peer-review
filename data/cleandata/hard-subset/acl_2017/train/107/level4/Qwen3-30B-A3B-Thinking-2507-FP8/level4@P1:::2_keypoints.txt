- Summary: Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods—an annotation-projection data selection scheme and a representation-projection model transfer—plus two co-decoding schemes; combined system outperforms prior work on CoNLL.

- Strength 1: Data selection scheme filters noisy projection labels using entity frequency distributions and yields large F1 gains (e.g., +13.9 Japanese, +20.0 Korean) when alignment is poor.

- Strength 2: Representation projection maps target embeddings to English via weighted least squares, enabling scalable transfer without retraining and improving results (e.g., German F1 69.3).

- Strength 3: Rank-based co-decoding effectively combines annotation and representation projection outputs, leveraging high precision vs. balanced recall to produce highest F1 across languages (e.g., +6.2 Portuguese).

- Weakness 1: Statistical significance of reported gains is not verified; no tests (e.g., paired t-tests) are reported for claimed improvements.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient comparison to foundational work—no direct comparison of the weighted least squares mapping to the original Mikolov et al. (2013b) method to isolate the source of improvements.

- Weakness 3: Missing a dedicated related work section that systematically categorizes prior approaches (e.g., annotation projection vs. model transfer) to better contextualize contributions.

- Question 1: Why does the distance-weighted CBOW variant outperform standard CBOW/skip-gram—please report baseline results to clarify the effect of weight decay.

- Question 2: The mapping requires a 5K–6K seed dictionary per target language—how does this compare to dictionary-free methods and could dictionary size be a bottleneck for low-resource languages?

- Question 3: In rank-based co-decoding, how are conflicts resolved so that annotation projection’s prioritization does not suppress true entities detected only by representation projection?
