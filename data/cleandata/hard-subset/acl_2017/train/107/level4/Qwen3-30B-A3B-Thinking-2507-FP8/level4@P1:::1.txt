

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language. The first approach uses annotation projection from comparable corpora with a novel data selection scheme to filter noisy labels. The second approach projects word embeddings across languages to enable direct NER model transfer. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The main contributions are: (1) an effective data selection scheme for annotation projection that improves accuracy when alignment is imperfect; (2) a representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer using word embeddings; and (3) co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems.

**Strengths**  
1. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is well-motivated and empirically validated. The authors demonstrate that it significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean), which is a substantial contribution given the common challenge of imperfect alignment in comparable corpora.  

2. The representation projection approach offers a novel alternative to existing cross-lingual transfer methods. By projecting word embeddings into a common space rather than building inter-lingual representations, the authors enable a single English NER system to be applied to multiple target languages with minimal adaptation.  

3. The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, effectively combine the complementary strengths of the two projection-based systems (annotation projection's high precision and representation projection's balanced precision-recall), resulting in significant improvements over individual systems (up to +6.2 F1).  

**Weaknesses**  
1. The novelty of the overall approach is limited. The paper builds upon existing annotation projection and model transfer methods rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts. For ACL, I would have expected more novel approaches rather than incremental improvements on established techniques.  

2. The data selection scheme's parameter tuning process is inadequately described. The paper states that "different thresholds were tried" for Table 2, but it's unclear whether this was done on a development set. Without this information, it's difficult to assess how to apply this technique to new languages or datasets.  

3. The co-decoding schemes are not clearly explained. The description of how the rank-based scheme works is vague, making it difficult to understand the exact mechanism for combining the outputs of the two systems. More detailed explanation would be necessary for reproducibility.  

4. The paper does not explore combining more than two weakly supervised systems, despite presenting multiple variants of each approach (e.g., NN1 and NN2). It would be valuable to know whether combining additional systems could yield further improvements.  

5. The in-house dataset is not sufficiently characterized. The paper refers to "in-house" human-annotated NER data for four languages but provides no details about the text types, domain, or annotation guidelines. This lack of information makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could you clarify the parameter tuning process for the data selection scheme? Specifically, was the threshold selection performed on a development set, and if so, what were the development set sizes for each language?  

2. The rank-based co-decoding scheme is described as "giving higher priority to the annotation projection system," but the exact mechanism for resolving conflicts between the two systems is not clear. Could you provide more detail on how the scheme handles overlapping or conflicting entity predictions?  

3. Given that the paper presents multiple variants of each approach (e.g., NN1 and NN2 for representation projection), why not explore combining more than two systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2) to potentially achieve better results?  

4. What are the characteristics of the in-house dataset (e.g., text domain, size, annotation guidelines)? How does it differ from standard NER datasets like CoNLL?
