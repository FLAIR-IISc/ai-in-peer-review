

Summary: The paper presents two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER requiring no target-language human annotation. Primary contributions are: (1) a heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves accuracy when alignment is noisy; (2) a representation projection method for direct NER model transfer via word embedding mapping; (3) two co-decoding schemes combining the two approaches. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL data, with combined systems outperforming prior work.

Strengths:
1. The heuristic data selection scheme effectively filters noisy projection-labeled data, yielding substantial accuracy gains (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean) when alignment quality is low. This addresses a critical limitation in prior annotation projection work that ignored alignment accuracy during data selection.
2. The representation projection approach provides a novel, efficient solution for direct model transfer without retraining, leveraging cross-lingual embeddings to create universal word representations. This avoids the need for language-specific feature engineering required by prior methods.
3. The rank-based co-decoding scheme successfully combines the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (high-precision annotation projection + balanced representation projection), achieving 2.9–6.2 F1 point improvements over individual systems across four languages.

Weaknesses:
1. Statistical significance testing for performance improvements (e.g., data selection gains, co-decoding benefits) is absent. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it is unclear whether observed F1 score differences (e.g., +13.9 for Japanese) are meaningful beyond random variation.
2. The paper inadequately compares the proposed frequency-weighted cross-lingual mapping (Section 4.2) against Mikolov's original method (2013b), making it difficult to assess the novelty and magnitude of the contribution. The claim of "superior" performance requires explicit ablation results.
3. The evaluation section (Section 6) is insufficiently detailed for a conference paper. Despite reporting state-of-the-art results, the authors omit critical analysis of failure modes, error types, and comparative runtime/memory costs versus prior methods, limiting the practical insights for readers.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for all performance improvements claimed over baselines (e.g., data selection gains, co-decoding enhancements).
2. Include ablation results comparing the frequency-weighted mapping (Section 4.2) against Mikolov's unweighted approach on the same datasets to quantify the contribution of the weighting scheme.
3. Clarify why the rank-based co-decoding outperforms confidence-based schemes (Table 3), particularly given the different precision/recall characteristics of the two component systems.
