

Summary and Contributions: This paper addresses weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in target languages. The authors present two main approaches: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme for filtering noisy data from comparable corpora (not requiring parallel corpora), and (2) representation projection that maps target-language word embeddings to source language for direct NER system application. The strongest contribution is the data selection scheme, which significantly improves NER performance for low-alignment-accuracy languages. The second strongest contribution is the representation projection approach, which enables direct application of source-language NER systems to target languages without retraining. The third contribution is the co-decoding schemes that combine outputs from the two projection-based approaches.

Strengths:
1. The data selection scheme is exceptionally well-motivated and addresses a critical challenge in cross-lingual NER: effectively filtering noisy data from comparable corpora. The authors' frequency-based metric for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data demonstrates significant improvements (up to +20 F1 points) for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese, Korean), showing this is a substantial practical contribution.

2. The representation projection approach offers a simple yet powerful solution for cross-lingual NER that avoids the need for retraining NER systems for new languages. By projecting word embeddings from target to source language, the authors enable direct application of a single source-language NER system to multiple target languages, which has clear practical advantages for multilingual NLP systems.

3. The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, are well-designed and empirically validated. The rank-based method effectively leverages the complementary strengths of the two projection-based approaches (high precision of annotation projection vs. more balanced precision/recall of representation projection), consistently improving performance over individual systems by 2.9-6.2 F1 points across languages.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient evidence demonstrating the novelty of the data selection scheme compared to existing approaches. While the authors claim their scheme is effective, they don't clearly show how it differs from or improves upon selection methods used in prior annotation projection work (e.g., Zitouni & Florian, 2008; Ehrmann et al., 2011), making it difficult to assess the true contribution.

2. The evaluation of the representation projection approach is limited to word embeddings without sufficient comparison to other cross-lingual transfer methods. The paper claims state-of-the-art results but doesn't thoroughly analyze why the approach works better than alternatives, leaving open questions about its generalizability and limitations.

3. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the reported performance improvements. Given that the authors claim significant improvements (e.g., +20 F1 points for Korean), statistical validation would strengthen their claims and help readers assess the practical significance of their results.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your data selection scheme specifically improve upon existing methods for filtering noisy projected data in comparable corpora? Could you provide a more detailed comparison with prior work's selection approaches?

2. The paper mentions a CBOW variant with distance-weighted context but doesn't present results comparing it to standard CBOW or skip-gram. Could you include these results to better demonstrate the value of your modification?

3. The paper claims the combined system outperforms previous work but lacks statistical significance tests. Could you add these to confirm that the reported improvements are meaningful and not due to random variation in the data?
