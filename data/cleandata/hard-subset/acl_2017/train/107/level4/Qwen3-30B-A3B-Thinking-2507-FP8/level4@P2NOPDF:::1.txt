

This paper proposes weakly supervised methods for Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging cross-lingual projection from English to other languages. While the core methodology is not novel, the authors present incremental refinements of existing techniques. Given ACL's emphasis on significant contributions, the paper would benefit from more innovative approaches rather than incremental improvements.

The data selection scheme, which employs a straightforward quality scoring formula, is a notable contribution. However, the methodology for threshold selection in Table 2 remains unclear. The paper states thresholds were "tried" but does not specify whether they were tuned on a development set or via another procedure. Without this detail, it is impossible to assess how to adapt the method to new datasets or language pairs, undermining practical reproducibility.

The combination of outputs from the two proposed systems is another key contribution, but the description lacks sufficient clarity. The mechanism for merging predictions is inadequately explained, making it difficult to evaluate the technique's validity or replicate the results. A more detailed account or illustrative example would substantially strengthen this section.

The paper explores multiple variants of each method but does not address whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems could yield additional gains. This omission is significant, as it leaves unanswered whether the approach scales beyond the two-system framework tested. The authors should clarify whether such extensions were attempted or why they were deemed unnecessary.

Additionally, the paper references an "in-house" dataset without describing its linguistic characteristics, domain, or text types. Understanding the nature of this dataset is critical for assessing the method's applicability to real-world scenarios and generalizability across domains. Without this context, the evaluation's broader implications remain uncertain.

While the work demonstrates practical utility for cross-lingual NER, its incremental nature and methodological gaps limit its suitability for an ACL publication. The paper would benefit from deeper theoretical insights, clearer technical descriptions, and a stronger justification for its contributions relative to existing work. Addressing these issues would significantly elevate the paper's impact and alignment with ACL's standards.
