

This paper proposes several weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), leveraging projection from English to other languages. While the core idea is reasonable, the overall contribution lacks sufficient novelty for an ACL venue; the methods primarily refine existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new approaches.  

The data selection scheme represents a key contribution, but its implementation raises concerns. The quality score formula is straightforward, which is acceptable, yet the paper fails to specify how thresholds for Table 2 were determined. The authors state only that "different thresholds were tried," without indicating whether this involved a development set or other validation procedure. This omission is problematic: while the results clearly demonstrate the importance of data selection, readers cannot replicate the parameter tuning for new datasets or language pairs without additional guidance.  

The paper’s proposed method for combining outputs from the two systems is another critical weakness. The description of this integration is insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the technical rationale or implementation. Without a clear explanation, it remains unclear how the combination actually functions or why it outperforms individual systems.  

Additionally, the paper introduces multiple variants for each method but does not address whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems would yield further gains. Given the focus on ensemble approaches, it is surprising that the authors did not explore this direction. A brief discussion of scalability or limitations in system combination would strengthen the work.  

Finally, the paper references an "in-house" dataset but provides no details about its characteristics. Information on text types, domain coverage, or linguistic properties would help contextualize the results and assess their generalizability. For instance, are the texts domain-specific, informal, or noisy? Such details are essential for evaluating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.  

In summary, while the empirical results demonstrate that data selection and system combination improve performance, the paper’s incremental nature and lack of methodological transparency limit its suitability for ACL. The authors should address the gaps in threshold determination, system combination explanation, and dataset characterization to strengthen their contribution.
