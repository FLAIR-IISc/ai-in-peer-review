

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses cross-lingual NER without target-language annotations through two projection-based approaches: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme for selecting high-quality weakly labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection via cross-lingual word embedding mappings to transfer a source-language NER system directly to target languages. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The primary contribution is the data selection scheme, which significantly improves annotation projection performance by filtering noisy data (evidenced by +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 F1 for Korean). The representation projection approach is a novel method for direct model transfer, and the co-decoding schemes—particularly the rank-based variant—provide a practical way to combine complementary strengths of the two approaches.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Effective data selection for annotation projection**: The paper demonstrates that the heuristic data selection scheme substantially improves NER performance when alignment quality is low (e.g., Japanese, Korean), outperforming no-data-selection baselines by over 13 F1 points. This addresses a critical gap in prior work, which overlooked alignment accuracy in data selection.  
2. **Novel representation projection approach**: The method of projecting word embeddings from target to source language (English) to enable direct application of the source NER system avoids retraining and handles multiple languages flexibly. This is a clean, scalable solution for cross-lingual transfer, particularly valuable for low-resource settings.  
3. **Effective co-decoding schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme (prioritizing annotation projection outputs) consistently outperforms individual systems, improving F1 by 2.9–6.2 points across languages. This demonstrates a practical way to leverage complementary strengths (high precision of annotation projection vs. balanced precision-recall of representation projection).  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unclear parameter tuning for data selection**: The paper states thresholds (q, n) were "tried" but provides no details on how they were determined (e.g., development set validation). Without this, readers cannot replicate the method for new languages or datasets. The authors should clarify whether thresholds were language-specific and how they were optimized.  
2. **Insufficient explanation of co-decoding**: The description of the co-decoding schemes (especially the "rank-based" method) is ambiguous. For instance, it is unclear how the "priority" for annotation projection is implemented beyond "including all entities detected by AP first." A pseudocode or more concrete example would clarify this critical contribution.  
3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper compares against only two prior approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) but omits more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., transfer learning with multilingual BERT). The CoNLL results (Table 5) show marginal gains over prior work (e.g., +1.6 F1 for German), which may not be significant given the simplicity of the approach.  
4. **Lack of analysis for the in-house dataset**: The in-house NER data (used for Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) is described as having "over 50 entity types" but lacks details on text domains (e.g., news, social media) or linguistic properties. This limits understanding of the method's generalizability.  
5. **No exploration of combining more than two systems**: The paper focuses on merging two approaches but does not investigate whether adding more weakly supervised systems (e.g., including direct model transfer via other methods) would yield further gains, which is a missed opportunity.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How were the thresholds (q, n) for data selection determined? Was this done via development set optimization, and if so, what was the development set size and language?  
2. The rank-based co-decoding scheme is described as "giving higher priority to annotation projection." Please clarify the exact algorithmic steps for resolving conflicts (e.g., how "non-conflicting" entities from representation projection are selected).  
3. The paper states that "the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches." However, the CoNLL results show only modest gains (e.g., +1.6 F1 for German vs. Tsai et al., 2016). How do the authors reconcile this with the claim of "strong baseline" status?  
4. What are the specific characteristics of the in-house dataset (e.g., domain, genre, sentence length)? How might these affect the method's performance in real-world scenarios?
