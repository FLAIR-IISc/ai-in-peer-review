- Introduces two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods: annotation projection with a novel heuristic data selection and representation projection via cross-lingual embedding mapping.  
- Main contribution: data selection scheme substantially improves NER when alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).  
- Representation projection enables direct model transfer without retraining.  
- Co-decoding schemes fuse the two methods and yield consistent gains (up to +6.2 F1).  

- Strength: Data selection scheme shows clear empirical impact, dramatically boosting F1 for languages with noisy alignments (examples: JP +13.9, KR +20.0).  
- Strength: Rank-based co-decoding consistently outperforms individual systems and narrows gap to supervised models (e.g., German 69.3 vs. 66.4).  
- Strength: Combined system sets a strong baseline on CoNLL, outperforming prior work (notably German +2.7 F1 over Tsai et al.).  

- Weakness: Threshold selection for data filtering is arbitrary and lacks a clear tuning procedure or guidance for new language pairs.  
- Weakness: Rank-based co-decoding is underspecified—conflict resolution and exact prioritization rules are not detailed, harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of fusion choices—no experiments with more than two systems or different neural architectures in co-decoding.  

- Question: Were the data-selection thresholds (q, n) tuned on a development set, and if not, how should they be set for new languages?  
- Question: Provide a concrete decision rule/example for resolving conflicting entity tags in rank-based co-decoding (e.g., GPE vs. ORG).  
- Question: Did you try combining the representation projection NN2 with annotation projection in rank-based co-decoding, and why is NN1 preferred?  

- Additional comment: The “in-house” dataset’s text types (news, social media, etc.) are not described, limiting assessment of generalizability.
