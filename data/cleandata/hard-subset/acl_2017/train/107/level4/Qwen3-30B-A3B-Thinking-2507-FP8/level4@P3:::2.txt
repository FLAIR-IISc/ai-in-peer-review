

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses the challenge of cross-lingual NER without human annotations in target languages. The primary contributions are:  
1. A data selection scheme for annotation projection that identifies high-quality weakly labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, significantly improving performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).  
2. A representation projection approach that maps target-language word embeddings to a source language (English), enabling direct application of the source-language NER system without retraining.  
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine outputs from the annotation and representation projection approaches, achieving state-of-the-art results on CoNLL data.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical impact on low-resource languages**: The data selection scheme addresses a critical gap in cross-lingual NER—handling noisy alignment data. The results demonstrate substantial improvements for languages with low alignment accuracy (e.g., Japanese, Korean), making the method highly applicable to real-world scenarios where parallel data is scarce.  
2. **Novel representation projection approach**: The method avoids the need for retraining NER models for new languages, a significant advantage over prior work. By projecting embeddings to a universal space (English), it simplifies system deployment and scales naturally to new languages.  
3. **Effective fusion of complementary strengths**: The co-decoding schemes (particularly rank-based) leverage the high precision of annotation projection and balanced recall of representation projection, achieving consistent gains (+2.9–6.2 F1) over individual methods across four languages.  
4. **Rigorous evaluation**: The paper evaluates on both in-house data (with 50+ entity types) and standard CoNLL datasets, demonstrating robustness across diverse language pairs and entity types. The results clearly show the combined system outperforms prior state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims "significant" improvements (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese) without statistical validation. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it is unclear whether these differences are meaningful or due to random variation.  
2. **Insufficient comparison to baseline projection methods**: The representation projection approach is framed as novel, but the paper does not compare it directly to Mikolov et al.’s (2013b) original cross-lingual embedding mapping. Without this, the contribution’s novelty and superiority are not fully justified.  
3. **Underdeveloped motivation for CBOW variation**: The paper uses a weighted CBOW variant for word embeddings but does not explain why the standard CBOW or Skip-gram would fail. Including ablation results (e.g., comparing CBOW vs. Skip-gram) would strengthen the methodological justification.  
4. **Limited discussion of results**: The evaluation section (1.5 pages) is concise but lacks depth. For example, it does not analyze *why* the rank-based co-decoding outperforms confidence-based (e.g., trade-offs between precision/recall), nor does it discuss failure cases or error analysis.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for the reported F1 improvements across languages? This is critical for validating the claimed "significant" gains.  
2. How does your weighted cross-lingual mapping (Section 4.2) compare to Mikolov et al.’s (2013b) unweighted approach in terms of performance and robustness? Including this comparison would clarify the novelty of your method.  
3. The CBOW variant uses a distance-decay weight (Section 4.1). Could you share results for a standard CBOW model (without weights) to demonstrate the necessity of this modification?  
4. Why does the rank-based co-decoding scheme (Section 5) outperform confidence-based in most cases, despite the latter being more intuitive? A brief analysis of the precision-recall trade-offs would enhance the paper’s impact.
