

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER without human annotation in target languages: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme that filters noisy labels from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection that maps target-language word embeddings to source language space for direct model transfer. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine these approaches. The main contributions are: (1) a data selection scheme that significantly improves annotation projection accuracy when alignment is noisy (especially for Japanese and Korean), (2) a representation projection approach enabling direct application of source-language NER systems to new languages without retraining, and (3) co-decoding schemes that combine the two approaches to achieve higher accuracy than either approach alone.

Strengths: The data selection scheme for annotation projection is highly practical and effective, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese: +13.9 F1, Korean: +20.0 F1), addressing a critical challenge in cross-lingual NER where alignment quality varies across language pairs. The representation projection approach is conceptually elegant and operationally simple, allowing direct application of a source-language NER system to target languages without retraining—a significant practical advantage for multilingual systems. The rank-based co-decoding scheme, which prioritizes the annotation projection system's output while incorporating representation projection's higher recall, demonstrates intelligent combination of complementary strengths and achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL data.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks statistical significance tests for the improvements claimed by the data selection scheme, which is essential for establishing the robustness of the results. The motivation for the proposed CBOW variation is unclear; the paper introduces a distance-weighted CBOW but doesn't compare it with standard CBOW or skip-gram models to demonstrate the improvement. The paper doesn't compare the proposed frequency-weighted cross-lingual projection with the original Mikolov approach (unweighted dictionary pairs), making it difficult to assess the contribution of the weighting scheme. The evaluation section is brief despite achieving state-of-the-art results; more discussion of results (e.g., why Portuguese shows less improvement from data selection than German) would add depth. The paper would benefit from a standalone Related Work section rather than embedding related content in the Introduction.

Questions to Authors: Can you provide statistical significance tests for the data selection scheme's improvements? How does your CBOW variation compare to standard CBOW and skip-gram in terms of embedding quality and downstream NER performance? Can you include a comparison between your frequency-weighted projection and the original Mikolov approach? Could you provide more discussion on why certain languages (e.g., Portuguese) show less improvement from data selection than others (e.g., German)?

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the combined results are interesting and competitive with state-of-the-art. The presentation of the co-decoding schemes and the data selection scheme are particularly strong. However, addressing the weaknesses above would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and impact. The work provides a valuable baseline for building cross-lingual NER systems with minimal human annotation, and the representation projection approach in particular has clear practical utility for multilingual NLP systems.
