

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper presents two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER with no target-language human annotation. The primary contribution is a heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves accuracy when alignment quality is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese). The second contribution is a representation projection method that maps target-language word embeddings to English via frequency-weighted linear mappings, enabling direct application of the source-language NER system. The third contribution is a rank-based co-decoding scheme combining the two approaches, which achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL data.

**Strengths**  
1. **Effective data selection scheme**: The paper demonstrates a clear, empirically validated solution to noisy projection data. For low-alignment languages (Japanese, Korean), the scheme improves F1 by 13.9–20.0 points over no selection, directly addressing a critical limitation in prior work (e.g., Zitouni & Florian 2008). The frequency-based metric for entity reliability is intuitive and well-motivated.  
2. **State-of-the-art results**: The combined system (AP + NN2 via rank-based co-decoding) outperforms Täckström (2012) and Tsai (2016) on CoNLL test data, achieving F1 scores within 4–6 points of supervised baselines (e.g., 69.9% vs. 75.6% for Portuguese). This establishes a strong practical benchmark for zero-shot cross-lingual NER.  
3. **Practical applicability**: The approach requires no human annotation in target languages and avoids language-specific retraining. The representation projection method is particularly scalable—adding new languages only requires mapping to English, not retraining the NER model.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims "significant improvements" from data selection (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese) but provides no statistical tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) to validate these claims. This undermines confidence in the reported gains.  
2. **Inadequate comparison of projection methods**: The frequency-weighted mapping (Section 4.2) is not compared against the original Mikolov (2013b) approach. Without this, it’s unclear whether the improvement stems from the frequency weighting or other factors. The paper should include a direct comparison (e.g., Table 2 vs. Mikolov baseline).  
3. **Missing ablation studies**: The CBOW variant (Section 4.1) lacks comparison to skip-gram (Mikolov 2013a) or standard CBOW. The motivation for the "distance-weighted" CBOW is unclear, and the paper omits results for standard CBOW/skip-gram to assess the improvement.  
4. **Poor organization**: Section 4.4 (Related Work) is embedded in Section 4.2, disrupting flow. A standalone Related Work section (as suggested in key points) would better contextualize the contribution.  
5. **Superficial evaluation**: Despite achieving SOTA, the evaluation lacks depth (e.g., error analysis, failure cases). The paper should discuss *why* the rank-based co-decoding outperforms confidence-based methods (e.g., precision-recall trade-offs in Table 3).  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Are the data selection improvements statistically significant? Please provide p-values or bootstrap confidence intervals for the F1 gains (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).  
2. How does the frequency-weighted mapping (Eq. 7) compare to the original Mikolov (2013b) method? Include a baseline result for the standard mapping in Table 2.  
3. What are the F1 scores for standard CBOW/skip-gram in the representation projection approach? This is critical to assess the value of the proposed CBOW variant.  
4. Could you clarify the motivation for the distance-weighted CBOW (Section 4.1)? Is it necessary for cross-lingual transfer, or would standard CBOW suffice? (Consider citing Iacobacci et al. 2016 on exponential decay for context.)
