- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods: annotation projection with data selection and representation projection via embedding mapping, plus co-decoding to combine them.
- Claims contributions: data selection scheme, representation projection for direct NER transfer, and co-decoding schemes.
- Strength: Data selection yields meaningful gains when alignment is poor (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).
- Strength: Representation projection is a reasonable alternative to existing cross-lingual transfer methods.
- Strength: Combined systems consistently outperform individual approaches on the in-house dataset.
- Weakness: Threshold determination for data selection (Table 2) is unclear and may hurt reproducibility.
- Weakness: Co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based approach, are poorly explained and hard to follow.
- Weakness: No guidance on tuning parameters for new languages or datasets, limiting practical applicability.
- Weakness: Overall approach lacks novelty, appearing as incremental extensions of prior work (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).
- Weakness: In-house dataset lacks description of text content and domain, hindering assessment of generalizability.
- Question: How were threshold parameters (q, n) in Table 2 determined—was there development set tuning?
- Question: Please clarify the exact implementation of the rank-based co-decoding scheme.
- Question: What is the domain and content of the "in-house" dataset?
- Question: How would the approach scale to languages without comparable corpora or with very different lexical structures?
