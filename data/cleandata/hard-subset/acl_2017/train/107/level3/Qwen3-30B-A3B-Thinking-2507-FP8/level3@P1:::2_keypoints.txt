- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approaches: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection mapping target embeddings to source space.  
- Designs two co-decoding schemes to combine annotation projection and representation projection.  
- Contribution: a data selection scheme that substantially improves annotation projection accuracy under noisy alignment (notably Japanese +13.9 F1, Korean +20.0 F1).  
- Contribution: a representation projection method enabling direct application of source-language NER models to target languages without retraining.  
- Contribution: co-decoding schemes that combine approaches to outperform each individually and achieve state-of-the-art on CoNLL.  
- Strength: the data selection scheme is practical and highly effective for languages with poor alignment.  
- Strength: the representation projection approach is conceptually simple and operationally useful for multilingual deployment.  
- Strength: the rank-based co-decoding intelligently leverages projection precision and model recall to boost results.  
- Weakness: no statistical significance tests are reported for the claimed improvements.  
- Weakness: unclear motivation and no empirical comparison for the proposed distance-weighted CBOW versus standard CBOW or skip-gram.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison between the frequency-weighted cross-lingual projection and the original unweighted Mikolov dictionary projection.  
- Weakness: evaluation discussion is brief and lacks deeper analysis of language-specific effects (e.g., why Portuguese benefits less than German).  
- Weakness: Related Work is embedded in the Introduction instead of a standalone section.  
- Question: please provide statistical significance tests for the data selection improvements.  
- Question: how does the distance-weighted CBOW compare to standard CBOW and skip-gram in embedding quality and NER performance?  
- Question: include a comparison between frequency-weighted projection and Mikolov’s original unweighted projection.  
- Question: can you explain why some languages (e.g., Portuguese) show less improvement from data selection than others (e.g., German)?  
- Overall: paper is well-structured and clearly written, presents competitive and practically valuable results, but addressing the above weaknesses would strengthen its contribution.
