

**Summary**  
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with heuristic data selection from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection via cross-lingual word embedding mapping. The primary contributions are the data selection scheme for filtering noisy projection-labeled data and the co-decoding framework that combines outputs from both systems. The paper claims these approaches outperform prior work on CoNLL and in-house datasets with no target-language annotation.  

**Main Contributions**  
1. **Data selection for annotation projection**: A heuristic scheme using entity frequency tables to filter low-quality projection-labeled sentences, significantly improving accuracy when alignment is noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).  
2. **Co-decoding framework**: A rank-based method that prioritizes the high-precision annotation projection system while incorporating recall from the representation projection system, boosting F1 by 2.9–6.2 points.  
3. **Representation projection**: A novel direct model transfer approach using cross-lingual embeddings (avoiding retraining), with NN2 (smoothing layer) achieving competitive results.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical impact**: The data selection scheme directly addresses a critical bottleneck in annotation projection (noisy alignments), yielding consistent gains across languages (e.g., +20.0 F1 for Korean). This is the strongest contribution, as it enables robust weak supervision where prior work ignored data quality.  
2. **Effective co-decoding**: The rank-based scheme (combining annotation and representation projection) significantly outperforms individual systems (e.g., 69.3 F1 for German vs. 66.4 for best single system). The empirical validation across four languages and two datasets (in-house, CoNLL) is thorough and convincing.  
3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: Results on diverse languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (fine-grained in-house, CoNLL’s 4-class) demonstrate robustness. The gap between weakly supervised (69.9 F1 for Portuguese) and supervised (75.6 F1) systems is narrow, proving practical viability.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of transparency in data selection**: Thresholds (q, n) in Table 2 are unexplained (e.g., why (0.8,4) for Japanese?). Were they tuned on a development set? Without this, reproducibility is compromised, and the method’s generalizability to new languages is unclear.  
2. **Unclear co-decoding justification**: The paper claims "rank-based" co-decoding is superior but fails to explain *why* it outperforms confidence-based schemes (Table 3). The description is vague (e.g., "higher priority to annotation projection"), and it’s unclear if other combinations (e.g., AP+NN2) were tested or why rank-based was chosen.  
3. **Insufficient novelty**: The core ideas (annotation projection, embedding mapping) are incremental extensions of prior work (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012; Mikolov et al., 2013). The data selection scheme is straightforward, and representation projection is a minor adaptation of existing cross-lingual embedding methods. The paper does not convincingly argue why this combination is novel.  
4. **Inadequate dataset description**: The "in-house" dataset’s text type (e.g., news, web, social media) and entity type distribution (beyond "over 50 types") are omitted. This hinders assessment of the results’ real-world relevance.  
5. **No guidance for new languages**: The method assumes access to comparable corpora and alignment models, but the paper offers no advice for languages without these resources (e.g., low-resource languages).  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How were the thresholds (q, n) in Table 2 selected? Were they tuned on a development set, or are they fixed across languages? If fixed, why do they vary per language (e.g., (0.8,4) for Japanese vs. (0.5,4) for Portuguese)?  
2. The in-house dataset uses "over 50 entity types." Please specify the most frequent types and the text genre (e.g., news, biomedical) to contextualize results.  
3. Why was the rank-based co-decoding scheme chosen over confidence-based? Did you test combinations beyond AP+NN1/NN2 (e.g., AP+NN1+NN2), and if so, what were the results?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper’s empirical results are strong and well-structured, but the novelty gap and methodological gaps (thresholds, dataset details) prevent it from being a significant advance. The co-decoding contribution is valuable but under-explained. For acceptance, the authors must clarify threshold tuning, justify co-decoding, and better contextualize the in-house data. The work is a useful incremental step but not a breakthrough for ACL.
