- Paper proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approaches: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection via cross-lingual embedding mapping.  
- Primary contributions: data selection scheme for filtering noisy projection-labeled data and a co-decoding framework combining both systems.  

Main contributions:
- Data selection for annotation projection: uses entity frequency tables to filter noisy projected sentences, substantially improving accuracy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).  
- Co-decoding framework: rank-based method that prioritizes high-precision annotation projection and adds recall from representation projection, boosting F1 by 2.9–6.2 points.  
- Representation projection: direct model transfer with cross-lingual embeddings and a smoothing NN2 layer achieving competitive results without retraining.  

Strengths:
- Practical impact: data selection addresses noisy alignments and yields consistent gains across languages (e.g., +20.0 F1 for Korean).  
- Effective co-decoding: rank-based combination outperforms individual systems and is validated across languages and datasets.  
- Comprehensive evaluation: experiments cover diverse languages and datasets, showing weak supervision approaches close supervised performance in some cases.  

Weaknesses:
- Lack of transparency in data selection: thresholds (q, n) are unexplained, raising reproducibility and generalizability concerns.  
- Unclear co-decoding justification: paper does not explain why rank-based outperforms confidence-based schemes or whether other combinations were tested.  
- Insufficient novelty: methods are incremental extensions of prior work; novelty of combination and heuristics is not convincingly argued.  
- Inadequate dataset description: in-house dataset lacks text genre and entity distribution details, limiting result interpretation.  
- No guidance for new/low-resource languages: assumes comparable corpora and alignment models with no advice for languages lacking these resources.  

Questions to authors:
- How were thresholds (q, n) in Table 2 selected—tuned per development set or fixed—and why do they vary by language?  
- What is the text genre and most frequent entity types in the in-house dataset to contextualize results?  
- Why choose rank-based co-decoding over confidence-based methods, and were combinations beyond AP+NN1/NN2 evaluated?  

Additional comment:
- Empirical results are strong and structured, but methodological gaps (threshold tuning, co-decoding rationale, dataset details) and limited novelty reduce the paper’s impact.
