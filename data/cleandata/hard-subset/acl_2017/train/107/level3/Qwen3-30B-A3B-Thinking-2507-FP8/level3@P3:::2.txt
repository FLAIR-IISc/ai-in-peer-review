

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in target languages: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy weakly labeled data from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection that maps target language word embeddings to a source language (English) space. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine outputs from these approaches. The methods are evaluated on in-house multilingual NER data and CoNLL test sets, with results showing state-of-the-art performance.

## Main Contributions
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).
2. A representation projection approach with frequency-weighted cross-lingual mappings that outperforms standard projection methods.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine outputs from the two projection-based approaches, achieving higher accuracy than individual systems.

## Strengths
1. The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written, making complex technical concepts accessible. The motivation for each contribution is well-explained, and the English is of high quality throughout.

2. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a significant practical contribution. It effectively addresses a key limitation in previous work (ignoring alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs), and the results demonstrate substantial improvements when alignment quality is poor, which is a common real-world scenario.

3. The combined systems achieve state-of-the-art results on CoNLL test data, outperforming previous approaches by 1.3-3.6 F1 points. The results are particularly impressive for German (69.3 F1 vs. previous 65.0 F1), showing the practical value of the proposed methods.

## Weaknesses
1. The CBOW variation proposed in Section 4.1 lacks sufficient justification and evaluation. The paper states the authors developed "a variant of the CBOW word2vec model" with distance-weighted context words but doesn't explain why this variant is better than standard CBOW or skip-gram. Crucially, the paper doesn't provide results comparing their variant to standard CBOW/skip-gram, making it impossible to assess the value of this modification.

2. The paper doesn't compare the frequency-weighted projection (Section 4.2) with the original Mikolov approach (which uses unweighted word pairs). Without this comparison, it's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the frequency weighting versus the projection method itself. The authors should include this comparison to demonstrate the specific contribution of their weighting scheme.

3. The evaluation section is too brief and lacks sufficient analysis. While the results are state-of-the-art, the paper doesn't thoroughly discuss *why* the methods work well, analyze error cases, or provide deeper insights into the performance differences across languages. The authors should expand this section with more detailed analysis.

4. The paper lacks statistical significance tests for claims about improvements from the data selection scheme and co-decoding approaches. Without these tests, it's difficult to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant rather than due to random variation.

5. Section 4.4 (Related Work) is embedded in Section 4.2 rather than being a standalone section. This makes the paper less organized and harder to follow, as related work should be presented separately from the main technical contributions.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide results comparing the standard CBOW and skip-gram models with your proposed CBOW variant for the representation projection task? This would help assess the specific contribution of your CBOW modification.

2. Please compare the frequency-weighted projection (Section 4.2) with the original Mikolov projection (without weights) to demonstrate the specific contribution of the weighting scheme.

3. Have you considered using exponential decay for surrounding embeddings as suggested by Iacobacci et al. (2016)? If not, could you discuss why this approach wasn't used in your work?

4. Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for the improvements from the data selection scheme and the co-decoding approaches?
