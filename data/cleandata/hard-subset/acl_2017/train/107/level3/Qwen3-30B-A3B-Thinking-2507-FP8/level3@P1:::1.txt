

Summary: The paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with a data selection scheme for selecting quality projection-labeled data from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection that maps target language word embeddings to the source language space. The authors also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The main contributions in order of strength are: (1) the co-decoding schemes for combining projection-based systems (though the description is unclear), (2) the data selection scheme for annotation projection (which shows significant improvements in low-alignment-accuracy scenarios), and (3) the representation projection approach (which builds on prior work).

Strengths: First, the data selection scheme demonstrates significant improvements in cases with low alignment accuracy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean), which addresses a real challenge in cross-lingual NER. Second, the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on CoNLL test data, providing a strong baseline for future work. Third, the representation projection approach offers a practical solution for direct model transfer without retraining, which is valuable for multilingual NER deployment.

Weaknesses: First, the overall contribution lacks novelty as the methods represent incremental improvements over prior work on annotation projection (e.g., Zitouni and Florian, 2008; Ehrmann et al., 2011) and representation projection (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013b). The data selection scheme's quality-score formula is straightforward, and the representation projection approach closely follows existing cross-lingual embedding mapping techniques. Second, the paper fails to clarify how the data selection thresholds (q and n) were determined for Table 2, making reproducibility difficult. It's unclear whether these thresholds were tuned on a development set, which is essential for proper experimental design. Third, the co-decoding schemes are described in an unclear manner, making it difficult to understand how the combination of the two projection-based systems actually works. The paper claims the rank-based scheme "gives higher priority to the annotation projection system," but the exact implementation details are missing. Fourth, the paper lacks guidance for tuning parameters for new datasets or language pairs, which limits the approach's generalizability. Fifth, the "in-house" NER dataset's content and text types are not described, making it hard to assess the evaluation's relevance to real-world applications.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Were the data selection thresholds (q and n) tuned on a development set or selected based on heuristics? If tuned, please specify how the development set was constructed.
2. Could you provide more detailed implementation details for the rank-based co-decoding scheme, particularly how entities detected by both systems are resolved when they conflict?
3. What specific characteristics of the "in-house" NER dataset (e.g., domain, text types, entity distribution) make it suitable for evaluating this approach, and how does it differ from standard NER datasets like CoNLL?
4. Have you explored combining more than two weakly supervised systems, and if so, what were the results? If not, why is combining just two systems sufficient?

The paper presents a practical approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER that shows promising results, particularly in low-alignment-accuracy scenarios. However, the incremental nature of the contributions and lack of clarity in key methodological details weaken the paper's impact. The authors should strengthen their contribution claims by better differentiating their work from prior art and providing more comprehensive methodological details to support reproducibility. The evaluation on both in-house and CoNLL datasets is thorough, but the unclear co-decoding schemes and missing parameter-tuning details limit the paper's technical contribution.
