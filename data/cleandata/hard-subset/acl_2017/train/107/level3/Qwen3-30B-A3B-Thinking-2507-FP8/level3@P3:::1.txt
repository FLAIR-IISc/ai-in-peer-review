

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Summary:** This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach (annotation projection) creates weakly labeled NER data for a target language by projecting English NER tags to comparable corpora, using a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy data. The second approach (representation projection) projects target-language word embeddings to English vector space, allowing the English NER system to be applied directly. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**
1. The data selection scheme for annotation projection (though straightforward, it demonstrates clear effectiveness)
2. The representation projection approach (which builds on existing work but provides a viable alternative to parallel data)
3. The combination of two projection-based approaches (though the description is problematic)

**Strengths:**
1. The data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrates clear empirical value, especially for languages with low alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean), improving F1 scores by 13.9 and 20.0 points respectively. This practical contribution addresses a real problem in annotation projection where noisy data can severely degrade performance.

2. The representation projection approach provides a viable alternative to traditional parallel corpus requirements for cross-lingual NER, which is valuable given the scarcity of parallel data for many languages. The paper demonstrates that this approach can produce reasonable results without requiring parallel data.

3. The combined systems consistently outperform previous state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on CoNLL test data (Table 5), showing that combining complementary approaches can yield better results than individual methods. This is a meaningful empirical contribution that could serve as a useful baseline for future work.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The overall approach lacks novelty. The paper builds incrementally on existing work (annotation projection and representation projection are well-established concepts), with no significant theoretical contributions. The data selection scheme is straightforward and the representation projection approach is a direct application of existing embedding mapping techniques. The authors fail to adequately distinguish their work from prior art.

2. The threshold determination for data selection (Table 2) is unclear and lacks justification. The paper does not specify whether thresholds were tuned on a development set, making reproducibility difficult. The absence of this information undermines the credibility of the results, as it's unclear whether the reported improvements are due to the method itself or overfitting to the test data.

3. The co-decoding schemes are poorly described and hard to follow. The paper claims these are a contribution, but the explanation (Section 5) is vague, and the implementation details are insufficient. It's unclear why the rank-based scheme outperforms the confidence-based approach in some cases but not others, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about how these schemes were implemented or why they work.

4. The paper lacks guidance for applying the method to new languages or datasets. While the data selection scheme is shown to be important, the paper provides no clear guidance on how to tune the quality score and entity number thresholds for new language pairs or different domains. This severely limits the practical applicability of the work.

5. The "in-house" dataset is inadequately described. The paper doesn't provide details about the content, domain, or characteristics of the texts in this dataset (Section 6.1), making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results. The lack of this information is a significant omission in the evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. How were the thresholds (q, n) in Table 2 determined? Were they tuned on a development set, and if so, which development set was used?
2. Could you provide more details about the "in-house" dataset's content, domain, and text characteristics to help assess the generalizability of your results?
3. Why did you choose to combine only two systems rather than exploring combinations of more than two weakly supervised approaches?
4. Could you clarify the implementation details of the co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based scheme, to make the method more reproducible?
