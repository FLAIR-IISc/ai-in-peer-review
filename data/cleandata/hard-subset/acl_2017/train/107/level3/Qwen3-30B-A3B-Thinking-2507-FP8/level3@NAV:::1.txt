

**Review**

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER: (1) annotation projection with a data selection scheme, and (2) representation projection via word embedding mapping. It claims to combine these approaches using co-decoding schemes. The primary contributions as I see them are: (1) a data selection scheme for annotation projection (though not novel), (2) representation projection for direct NER model transfer, and (3) co-decoding schemes to combine the two projection-based systems.

Strengths: The data selection scheme demonstrates meaningful improvements over no selection in cases with low alignment accuracy (Table 2, +13.9 F1 for Japanese). The representation projection approach provides a reasonable alternative to existing cross-lingual transfer methods. The combined systems do show consistent improvement over individual approaches on in-house data (Table 3).

Weaknesses: First, the data selection scheme's threshold determination (Table 2) is unclear—no explanation of whether thresholds were tuned on a development set, undermining reproducibility. Second, the co-decoding schemes are poorly explained and hard to follow, with the rank-based approach being particularly confusing (Section 5). Third, the paper makes no guidance for tuning parameters for new languages or datasets, limiting practical applicability. Fourth, the overall approach lacks novelty—the methods are incremental improvements over existing work (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), with the "innovative" aspects being straightforward extensions. Fifth, the in-house dataset lacks details about text content and domain, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of results.

Questions to Authors: How were the threshold parameters (q, n) in Table 2 determined—was there development set tuning? Please clarify the exact implementation of the rank-based co-decoding scheme. What is the domain and content of the "in-house" dataset? How would your approach scale to languages without comparable corpora or with very different lexical structures?
