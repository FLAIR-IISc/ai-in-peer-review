## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

This paper presents a comprehensive approach to cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme and representation projection via word embeddings. The authors explore various combinations of CRF, MEMM, and neural network models, culminating in co-decoding strategies to leverage the strengths of each approach. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow, presenting a significant body of experimental work. The combined system results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL test data, are a notable contribution.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the systematic exploration and effective combination of existing techniques – annotation projection and representation projection – specifically tailored for cross-lingual NER. The proposed data selection heuristic for annotation projection demonstrably improves performance, particularly when alignment quality is low. Additionally, the co-decoding schemes are a practical and effective method for integrating diverse system outputs.  A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the extensive experimental evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, providing a robust assessment of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over baseline systems and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL datasets. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
2. **Comprehensive Approach:**  The combination of annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding offers a holistic solution to the cross-lingual NER problem, addressing challenges in both data scarcity and domain adaptation.
3. **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:**  The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, facilitating a clear understanding of the methodology and results.  The inclusion of ablation studies (varying model types) enhances the clarity of contributions.
4. **Extensive Experimentation:** Evaluating on multiple languages and datasets strengthens the generalizability of the findings and provides a more complete picture of the approach's strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Motivation for CBOW Variation:** The paper mentions a variation of the CBOW model but lacks a clear justification for this specific modification. Providing a rationale, perhaps drawing on limitations of standard CBOW in this context, would strengthen the paper. Details about the parameters used in this variant are also needed.
2. **CBOW Baseline Comparison:** To fully appreciate the proposed CBOW variation, the paper should include results for the original CBOW and skip-gram models as baselines. This would enable a direct assessment of the improvement achieved through the proposed modification.
3. **Embedding Decay Consideration:**  The use of distance-weighted context in the CBOW model is promising. The authors should acknowledge and potentially compare their approach to exponential decay schemes for surrounding embeddings, referencing Iacobacci et al. (2016).
4. **Statistical Significance:** Claims of improvement should be supported by statistical significance tests. This is crucial for establishing the robustness of the results and demonstrating that observed gains are not due to chance.
5. **Organization of Section 4:** Integrating Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 and moving Table 2 to the evaluation section would improve the paper’s flow and readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific rationale behind the proposed CBOW variation, and provide detailed parameters used for the modified model?
2. Would you be able to provide results for the original CBOW and skip-gram models as baselines for comparison?
3.  What statistical significance tests were used to validate the reported improvements, and what were the p-values?
