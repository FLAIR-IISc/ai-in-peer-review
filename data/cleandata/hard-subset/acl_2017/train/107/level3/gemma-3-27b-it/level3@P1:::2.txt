This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging both annotation projection and representation projection, coupled with novel co-decoding schemes to combine their outputs. The authors address a critical problem in multilingual information extraction – the scarcity of annotated data in many languages – by transferring knowledge from English to target languages without requiring human annotation. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and demonstrates strong experimental results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on several datasets. The main contributions are: (1) a heuristic data selection scheme for improving the quality of annotation projection by filtering noisy data; (2) a representation projection approach using a modified word embedding model and cross-lingual mapping; and (3) effective co-decoding strategies to combine the outputs of both approaches, leading to improved performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The most compelling strength of this work lies in its empirical performance. The proposed methods consistently outperform existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.  The gains are particularly notable in scenarios with low alignment accuracy, demonstrating the effectiveness of the data selection scheme. The results on CoNLL specifically position this work as a significant advancement in the field.
2. **Comprehensive Approach:** The paper tackles the problem of cross-lingual NER from multiple angles, combining annotation projection, representation projection, and sophisticated co-decoding strategies. This multifaceted approach is a significant strength, allowing the system to leverage the benefits of each individual technique and mitigate their limitations. The exploration of different co-decoding schemes (confidence-based vs. rank-based) shows careful consideration of the nuances of combining probabilistic outputs.
3. **Practical Data Selection Scheme:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is a valuable contribution. Recognizing that projection-labeled data can be noisy, the authors propose a method for selecting high-quality examples based on frequency analysis of entity tags.  This addresses a common weakness in annotation projection approaches and provides a practical solution for improving performance. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of this scheme.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is remarkably clear and well-organized.  The problem is articulated effectively, the proposed methods are described in detail, and the experimental setup is clearly explained. The English is excellent, making the paper easy to understand and follow.  This clarity is crucial for reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty of CBOW Variation:** The paper introduces a variant of the CBOW word2vec model by incorporating a weighted context, but the motivation for this specific weighting scheme isn't fully explained. While the results are positive, a more thorough justification for the proposed modification, and a detailed description of the hyperparameters and training procedure (beyond just the number of epochs), would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Limited Comparison of Embedding Models:**  The authors evaluate their representation projection approach using the proposed CBOW variant, but lack a comparison to the original CBOW and skip-gram models. Reporting results for these baselines would provide valuable context and allow readers to assess the actual improvement offered by the proposed model. This comparison is essential to demonstrate the benefit of the specific architectural choices.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** While the experimental results demonstrate performance gains, the paper does not include statistical significance tests. Without statistical tests, it is difficult to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant or simply due to chance. Including p-values would significantly strengthen the validity of the claims.
4. **Organization and Related Work:** The inclusion of related work within the introduction feels somewhat fragmented.  A dedicated “Related Work” section would provide a more coherent overview of the existing literature and better position the contributions of this paper. Furthermore, integrating Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 and moving the evaluation presented in Table 2 to the evaluation section would improve the paper's flow.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the specific weighting function used in your modified CBOW model? What motivated the choice of this particular function over alternatives, such as an exponential decay (e.g., Iacobacci et al., 2016)?
2.  How do the performance results compare when using the original CBOW and skip-gram models for representation projection? Please provide results for these baselines.
3.  Could you provide p-values for the reported performance improvements, to demonstrate their statistical significance?
4.  What is the computational cost associated with the data selection scheme? Does it significantly increase the training time of the annotation projection approach?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, the reliance on comparable corpora with alignment information could be a significant constraint in some scenarios.
*   The authors should also discuss the potential impact of the entity type granularity on the performance of the system. The use of over 50 entity types in the in-house datasets might pose challenges for the weakly supervised learning setting.
*   The reference to Iacobacci et al. (2016) would be valuable in the context of exploring alternative weighting schemes for contextual embeddings.
