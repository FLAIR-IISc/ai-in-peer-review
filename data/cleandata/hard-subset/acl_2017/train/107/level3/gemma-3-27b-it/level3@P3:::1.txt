This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using annotation projection and representation projection, combined with co-decoding schemes. While the work presents a comprehensive empirical study, the contributions feel incremental, building upon established techniques rather than offering fundamentally new approaches. The extensive experimentation is a strength, but the lack of clarity around certain implementation details and parameter tuning hinders reproducibility and generalizability.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of Data Selection:** The study demonstrates the importance of data selection when employing annotation projection, particularly in low-resource scenarios with noisy alignment data. The quality score, while simple, provides a practical means to filter projection data.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation of Combined Approaches:** The thorough evaluation of different combinations of projection techniques (annotation & representation) and co-decoding strategies provides a useful benchmark and demonstrates the benefits of combining diverse weakly supervised signals.
3. **Extensive Experimental Results:** The paper presents a substantial number of experiments across multiple languages and datasets, providing a broad assessment of the proposed methods' performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Experimentation:** The authors evaluate their methods on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), demonstrating the broad applicability of their approaches. This is a significant undertaking and provides robust empirical evidence.
2. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages and provides a strong motivation for exploring weakly supervised techniques.
3. **Practical Data Selection Approach:** The quality score formula for data selection, while straightforward, appears reasonable and practical. It’s a sensible way to prioritize higher-quality projection data.
4. **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to existing cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) demonstrates the competitiveness of the proposed methods.
5. **Detailed Implementation:** The paper provides a reasonably detailed description of the NER models used (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) and the procedures for representation projection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core ideas – annotation projection and representation projection – are well-established in the literature. The paper’s contribution lies primarily in their combination and application to NER, rather than proposing novel algorithms. The methods feel like incremental improvements rather than a breakthrough.
2. **Unclear Threshold Determination:** The selection of thresholds (q and n) for the data selection scheme (Table 2) is not justified. The paper states “we have tried different threshold parameters q and n and reported the best numbers,” but there’s no discussion of how these parameters were chosen or optimized. This significantly impacts reproducibility.
3. **Reproducibility Concerns:** The lack of development set tuning for these thresholds, and the absence of explicit reporting on parameter search, raise concerns about reproducibility. It's unclear if the reported results are due to genuine improvements or lucky parameter settings.
4. **Limited Guidance for Generalization:** While the results are extensive, the paper offers limited guidance on how to tune the parameters (particularly the data selection thresholds) for new datasets or language pairs. There’s no discussion of how alignment quality influences optimal threshold values.
5. **Ambiguous Co-Decoding Description:** The description of the co-decoding schemes (especially the “rank-based” scheme) is somewhat unclear and difficult to follow. It's hard to understand the precise decision-making process for combining the outputs of the two systems.
6. **Limited Exploration of System Combination:** The paper focuses on combining only two weakly supervised systems. It remains unclear whether combining more than two systems would yield further improvements, or if the benefits diminish with increased complexity.
7. **Insufficient Dataset Details:** The description of the “in-house” dataset is lacking. Knowing the content and types of texts included (e.g., news articles, scientific papers, social media) would help assess the generalizability of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How were the thresholds *q* and *n* determined for the data selection scheme? Was a development set used for tuning, and if so, what were the results of the parameter search?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the "rank-based" co-decoding scheme? A concrete example illustrating the decision-making process would be helpful.
3.  Did you experiment with combining more than two weakly supervised systems? If not, why? Were there any practical or performance-related limitations that prevented you from doing so?
4.  Could you elaborate on the content and characteristics of the "in-house" dataset? What types of texts are included, and what is the domain/topic distribution?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the English NER system used for annotation projection? Would using a more accurate (but potentially more complex) English NER system significantly improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "conflict" in the co-decoding description (footnote 3) needs clarification.
*   Consider adding an ablation study to assess the contribution of each component of the proposed approach.
