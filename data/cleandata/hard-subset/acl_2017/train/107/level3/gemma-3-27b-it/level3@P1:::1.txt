This paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER): annotation projection from comparable corpora and direct model transfer via representation projection of word embeddings. The authors further propose two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. While the work builds upon existing techniques, the contributions lie primarily in the specific implementation details and empirical evaluation across multiple languages.

The most substantial contribution appears to be the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection. The authors address the inherent noisiness of projection-labeled data by developing a quality score based on entity frequency, aiming to select higher-confidence examples. This is a sensible and practical approach to improve performance when alignment accuracy is low. The subsequent representation projection approach, while not entirely novel, provides an alternative pathway for cross-lingual transfer, leveraging word embeddings to map target language words into the source language's feature space. The co-decoding schemes are attempts to intelligently combine these two distinct outputs. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL). This is a key strength, demonstrating the applicability and potential of the proposed approaches beyond a single language pair. The consistent evaluation framework allows for meaningful comparisons between the different components (annotation projection, representation projection, co-decoding).
2. **Practicality of Data Selection:**  The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection is a practical contribution. Addressing the noise inherent in projected data is crucial for the success of this approach.  The frequency-based quality score offers a reasonable heuristic for identifying higher-confidence examples. The demonstrated improvements in F1 score (particularly for Japanese and Korean) highlight the effectiveness of this component.
3. **Combined System Performance:**  The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, show promising results in improving overall performance by leveraging the complementary strengths of the two projection-based systems. The gains observed on both in-house and CoNLL datasets suggest that combining these techniques can be more effective than relying on either method alone.
4. **Detailed Implementation:** The paper provides a relatively detailed description of the implemented models (CRFs, MEMMs, neural networks) and embedding techniques, allowing for some degree of reproducibility. The specification of embedding dimensions (300 for English, 800 for target languages) and training epochs provides useful information for potential reimplementation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core ideas – annotation projection and representation projection – are well-established in the cross-lingual NER literature. The paper primarily presents incremental improvements and specific implementations of these techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach. The contributions, while valuable, feel more evolutionary than revolutionary.
2. **Threshold Tuning and Reproducibility:** The selection of parameters *q* and *n* for the data selection scheme is not thoroughly explained.  Table 2 shows different (q,n) values for each language, but it’s unclear whether these were tuned on a development set or chosen empirically. Without a clear description of the tuning process, replicating these results is difficult. More details on how the thresholds were determined would strengthen the findings.
3. **Generalizability of Data Selection:** While the data selection scheme improves performance, the paper doesn’t offer much guidance on how to effectively tune the *q* and *n* parameters for *new* languages or datasets. It is unclear whether the optimal thresholds are highly language-specific or dependent on the characteristics of the comparable corpora used.
4. **Co-Decoding Scheme Clarity:** The description of the co-decoding schemes, especially the "rank-based" approach, is somewhat unclear. It's difficult to fully understand the precise logic for combining the outputs of the two systems. A more detailed explanation or an illustrative example would be beneficial.  Was it explored whether combining more than two systems would yield further improvements?
5. **In-House Dataset Information:** The description of the "in-house" datasets is limited. More information about the source of these data (e.g., news articles, social media, specific domains) and the types of named entities included would be helpful for understanding the scope and limitations of the evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the *q* and *n* parameters were tuned for the data selection scheme? Was a development set used, and if so, what was its size and composition?
2. Could you elaborate on the rank-based co-decoding scheme? Can you provide a concrete example illustrating how it resolves conflicts between the outputs of the annotation projection and representation projection systems?
3.  What steps were taken to ensure that the parameters and hyperparameters used across all experiments (for each language) were adequately tuned, rather than relying on default values?
4. Was the performance of the methods sensitive to the size of the training dictionary used for learning the cross-lingual embedding mappings? 

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive and includes relevant work in cross-lingual NER.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   A brief discussion of the computational cost of each approach would be valuable.
*   Consider adding an ablation study to determine the individual contribution of each component (e.g., data selection, co-decoding) to the overall performance.
