## Review for ACL 2017 – Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

This paper presents a comprehensive approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), combining annotation projection with representation projection and employing co-decoding schemes for improved performance. The work leverages CRFs, MEMMs, and neural network models, alongside a modified CBOW word embedding model, to achieve state-of-the-art results on both in-house and CoNLL datasets. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The experimental results demonstrate a significant contribution to the field.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in the effective combination of annotation projection and representation projection techniques for cross-lingual NER. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection demonstrably improves performance in noisy scenarios. A secondary contribution is the successful adaptation of cross-lingual word embedding mappings, utilizing a frequency-weighted approach, to facilitate direct model transfer without target-language retraining. Finally, the novel co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, effectively integrate the outputs of these disparate methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL datasets (as shown in Table 5) and achieves competitive results on in-house datasets (Table 3). This is a significant accomplishment, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods. The consistent gains across multiple languages underscore the robustness of the approach.

2. **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors explore a variety of techniques – different NER models (CRF, MEMM, Neural Networks), embedding projection methods, and co-decoding schemes – providing a thorough investigation of the problem space. This breadth is valuable and offers insights into the interplay between these different components.

3. **Practical Data Selection:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection addresses a critical issue in weakly supervised learning – the presence of noisy labels. The heuristic, based on frequency analysis, is intuitively sound and demonstrably effective, particularly when alignment accuracy is low.

4. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The detailed descriptions of the models and the co-decoding schemes contribute to the reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Motivation for CBOW Variation:** The paper introduces a variant of the CBOW word2vec model using a weighted sum of context words based on distance. However, the motivation for this specific weighting scheme isn’t fully explained.  A more thorough justification, detailing why this variation is expected to outperform the standard CBOW implementation, is needed.

2. **Comparative Embedding Analysis:** While the frequency-weighted projection is presented, the paper lacks a direct comparison with the original Mikolov cross-lingual embedding projections. Reporting results for both the original and the proposed method would provide a stronger validation of the improvements achieved through the frequency weighting.

3. **Statistical Significance:** The claim that the data selection scheme yields significant improvements (mentioned in Section 3.2 and supported by Table 2) lacks statistical significance testing. Performing statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) would strengthen the validity of these claims.

4. **Organization & Related Work:** The paper interleaves related work throughout the introduction. A dedicated, standalone “Related Work” section would provide a more cohesive overview of the existing literature and better contextualize the contributions of this work. Integrating Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 and moving Table 2 to the Evaluation section would also improve the paper’s organization.

5. **Limited Evaluation Discussion:** While the paper achieves excellent results, the discussion of the experimental findings is relatively brief. A more in-depth analysis of the errors made by the system and potential areas for future improvement would be beneficial. For instance, exploring the impact of different alignment qualities or language pairs would add valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed rationale for the weighting scheme used in your CBOW variant?  What specific properties of the weighting function are intended to improve embedding quality for cross-lingual transfer?

2.  Have you conducted experiments comparing the performance of your frequency-weighted cross-lingual embedding projection with the original Mikolov projection method? If so, could you share the results?

3. Have you performed statistical significance testing to confirm that the improvements reported for the data selection scheme are statistically significant?

**Additional Comments:**

- Considering the work of Iacobacci et al. (2016) on embeddings for word sense disambiguation and discussing its relevance to the proposed weighting scheme in the CBOW variant would be valuable.
- Exploring the impact of different co-decoding weights, beyond the rank-based and confidence-based schemes, could potentially yield further improvements.
