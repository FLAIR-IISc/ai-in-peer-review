## Review for ACL 2017 - Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

This paper presents two approaches to weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER): annotation projection with a data selection heuristic, and representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings. The authors further propose co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems. While the work demonstrates improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods on the CoNLL dataset, the contributions feel incremental and the presentation lacks clarity regarding experimental setup and reproducibility.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper addresses the challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages by leveraging resources from a high-resource language (English). It explores two main avenues: transferring labels from English via comparable corpora alignment (annotation projection) and transferring representations via cross-lingual word embeddings. A key component of the annotation projection approach is a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projection labels. Finally, the authors investigate combining the outputs of both approaches using co-decoding strategies. 

**Main Contributions (as perceived by the reviewer):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Data Selection:** The most significant contribution lies in showing the importance of careful data selection when using annotation projection for cross-lingual NER. The proposed quality score, while straightforward, highlights the need to mitigate noise in projection-labeled data, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. This is substantiated by the reported improvements on Japanese and Korean.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Combined Approaches:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of combining the annotation projection and representation projection methods, demonstrating that a combined system can outperform individual approaches, particularly with the proposed co-decoding schemes.
3. **Baseline Performance for Cross-Lingual NER:** The work establishes a strong baseline for weakly-supervised cross-lingual NER, surpassing previously published results on the CoNLL datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conduct experiments on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) using both in-house and publicly available datasets.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource NER and motivates the need for weakly supervised approaches.
* **Positive Results:** The reported improvements over existing methods on the CoNLL data demonstrate the potential of the proposed techniques.
* **Exploration of Combination Strategies:** Investigating ways to combine the strengths of different approaches is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Novelty:** The individual techniques—annotation projection and representation projection—are well-established in the cross-lingual NLP literature. The proposed methods are incremental improvements rather than fundamentally new approaches.
* **Unclear Threshold Tuning:** The selection of thresholds *q* and *n* for the data selection scheme is not well-explained. The paper mentions finding the "best numbers" (Table 2), but doesn't specify whether these were tuned on a development set or via some other methodology.  This significantly impacts reproducibility.
* **Limited Generalizability of Data Selection:** While the data selection scheme shows promise, the paper provides little guidance on how to tune the thresholds *q* and *n* for new datasets or language pairs.  The optimal values seem specific to the current experimental setup.
* **Unclear Co-Decoding Description:** The description of the co-decoding schemes is vague and difficult to follow. The "exclude-O confidence-based" and "rank-based" schemes are not clearly differentiated in terms of their implementation details.
* **Method Variant Proliferation:** The paper presents numerous variations of the methods (NN1 vs. NN2, different co-decoding schemes). It’s unclear if attempts were made to combine more than two weakly supervised systems, or whether such an approach would be beneficial.
* **Insufficient Dataset Description:** The paper lacks sufficient details about the "in-house" dataset, including the types of texts included (e.g., news, social media) and the characteristics of the entity types. This makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How were the thresholds *q* and *n* for the data selection scheme determined? Was a development set used for tuning? If so, please provide details.
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the “exclude-O confidence-based” and “rank-based” co-decoding schemes, including pseudocode or a more concrete description of the decision-making process?
3.  Were any experiments conducted to combine more than two weakly supervised systems? If not, why?
4.  Can you provide more information about the “in-house” dataset, including the source of the data, the genre/domain, and the distribution of entity types?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, especially recent advancements in weakly supervised NER and cross-lingual transfer learning.
* The figures are limited, and a diagram illustrating the co-decoding process would be helpful.
* Please clarify how the alignment information was obtained for the comparable corpora used in annotation projection.



Overall, this paper presents a solid empirical study of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. However, the lack of significant novelty and the clarity issues regarding the experimental setup and reproducibility hinder a stronger recommendation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
