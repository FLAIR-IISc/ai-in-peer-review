This paper presents a comprehensive study of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with representation projection and novel co-decoding schemes. The work is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL datasets. The paper's primary contribution lies in the systematic exploration and effective combination of existing techniques, alongside thoughtful data selection strategies, rather than proposing radically new algorithms. A secondary, valuable contribution is the thorough evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, providing a strong empirical basis for the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the combined approach. The performance gains, while not always massive, are consistent across languages and represent a solid improvement in the field. The in-house dataset results also provide a broader view of performance beyond the standard benchmarks.
2. **Comprehensive Approach:** The paper doesn't focus on a single technique but rather investigates and combines multiple strategies (CRF/MEMM, Neural Networks, different projection methods, and co-decoding schemes). This holistic approach highlights the benefits of synergistic combinations and provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each component.
3. **Practical Data Selection Scheme:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is a practical and effective addition. The motivation is clearly explained, and the results demonstrate the significant improvement achievable by filtering noisy projection data. This is a crucial element for real-world applications where alignment accuracy can be questionable.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical details are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-documented. This makes the work accessible and facilitates reproducibility.
5. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation across multiple languages (including in-house datasets) is a significant strength. This demonstrates the generalizability of the approach and provides a more robust assessment of its performance than relying solely on standard benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, the individual components (CRFs, MEMMs, neural networks, Mikolov word embeddings, projection methods) are well-established. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty within each component, and how these specific implementations differ from existing work.
2. **Insufficient Detail on CBOW Variation:** The paper mentions a variant of the CBOW model but lacks sufficient detail regarding its specific implementation and parameters. A clear explanation of the differences from the standard CBOW model, the motivation behind these differences, and a justification for the chosen parameters is needed. Presenting results for the original CBOW and skip-gram models would provide a crucial baseline for evaluating the proposed improvement.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Claims of improvement are made without accompanying statistical significance tests. It’s essential to demonstrate that the observed performance gains are statistically significant and not due to random chance. Reporting p-values for the comparisons would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Organization Concerns:** Integrating Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 and moving Table 2’s evaluation to the evaluation section would indeed improve the paper’s flow and clarity, as suggested.
5. **Limited Analysis & Discussion:** While the paper achieves SOTA results, the analysis and discussion remain somewhat superficial. A deeper investigation of error cases, ablation studies (e.g., removing components of the co-decoding scheme), and a more thorough analysis of the impact of data quality on performance would add significant value.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a detailed description of the CBOW variation, including the specific modifications made to the standard model and the rationale behind these changes? Please also provide the hyperparameters used for training this model.
2. Could you present the results of the original CBOW and skip-gram models on the tasks evaluated in the paper? This would provide a baseline for assessing the effectiveness of the proposed CBOW variation.
3. Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to support the claims of improvement in performance.
4. How sensitive are the results to the threshold parameters (q and n) used in the data selection scheme? Could you provide an ablation study analyzing the impact of different parameter settings?
5. Can you elaborate on the specific error cases encountered by the system? Are there particular entity types or linguistic phenomena that pose a significant challenge?

**Additional Comments:**

* Consider citing Iacobacci et al. (2016) and exploring the use of exponential decay for surrounding embeddings, as suggested.
* A more detailed comparison of the proposed frequency-weighted projections with the original Mikolov projections would be beneficial. Quantify the differences in performance resulting from this weighting scheme.
* A dedicated Related Work section would improve the organization and context of the paper.
