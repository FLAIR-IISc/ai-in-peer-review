## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using two main approaches: annotation projection and representation projection. The authors propose a data selection scheme to improve the quality of projected annotations and investigate combining the outputs of both approaches via co-decoding. While the work is a reasonable extension of existing techniques, it lacks significant novelty and suffers from reproducibility concerns.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the empirical demonstration of the importance of data selection in the annotation projection approach. The quality-score formula is straightforward and intuitive, and the observed improvements highlight a practical consideration for this technique. However, the secondary contributions, regarding representation projection and co-decoding, feel incremental. Representation projection is a well-established technique, and the described co-decoding schemes lack clear explanation and justification for their design. The paper presents many method variants, but the rationale behind exploring this broad space without more in-depth analysis is unclear.

**Strengths:**

1. **Demonstrates Data Selection Benefit:** The experimental results clearly show that careful data selection in annotation projection can significantly improve performance, particularly when alignment quality is low. This is a practical and valuable finding.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering multiple target languages, providing a reasonable breadth of assessment.
3. **Clear Presentation of Baseline Approaches:** The paper provides a concise overview of existing techniques for cross-lingual NER, setting the stage for their proposed improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core ideas (annotation projection, representation projection) are not novel. The proposed methods are largely incremental improvements to existing techniques, lacking a breakthrough contribution.
2. **Threshold Determination Opacity:** The selection of thresholds for the data selection scheme (Table 2) is unclear. There’s no mention of a development set used for tuning, making it difficult to understand how these values were chosen and impacting reproducibility.
3. **Limited Generalizability Guidance:** While data selection is shown to be effective, the paper lacks guidance on how to tune parameters (e.g., quality threshold) for new datasets or language pairs. This limits the practical applicability of their method.
4. **Unclear Co-decoding Description:** The description of the co-decoding schemes is vague and difficult to follow. The rationale behind the "exclude-O" confidence-based approach and the rank-based scheme is not fully explained. It's also unclear if exploring combinations of more than two weakly supervised systems was considered.
5. **Insufficient Dataset Details:** More information about the "in-house" dataset is needed. What types of texts are included? Knowing the domain and characteristics of this data would help contextualize the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Was a development set used to tune the thresholds for the data selection scheme? If so, please provide details. If not, please explain the rationale for choosing these specific values.
2. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the co-decoding schemes, including the intuition behind the “exclude-O” approach?
3. Please provide more information about the content and characteristics of the "in-house" dataset.
4. Was there an attempt to combine more than two of the weakly supervised systems? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, but the paper could benefit from more concise explanations and a stronger focus on the key contributions. Table and figure captions would be more helpful with brief summaries of the results/content.
