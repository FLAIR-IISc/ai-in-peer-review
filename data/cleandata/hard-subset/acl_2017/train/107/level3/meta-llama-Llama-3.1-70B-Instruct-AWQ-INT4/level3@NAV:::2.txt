**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach is based on annotation projection, which uses a heuristic data selection scheme to select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora. The second approach is based on representation projection, which projects word representations in vector space from a target language to a source language, allowing the source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training. The paper also presents two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches to produce an output that is more accurate than the outputs of individual systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Effective annotation projection approach**: The paper proposes a heuristic data selection scheme that can effectively select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora, which improves the accuracy of the target-language NER system.
2. **Representation projection approach**: The paper proposes a new approach for direct NER model transfer based on representation projection, which projects word representations in vector space from a target language to a source language, allowing the source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training.
3. **Co-decoding schemes**: The paper presents two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches to produce an output that is more accurate than the outputs of individual systems.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-art results**: The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL test data, outperforming two previous approaches for cross-lingual NER.
2. **Effective data selection scheme**: The paper's heuristic data selection scheme is effective in selecting good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora.
3. **Flexible representation projection approach**: The paper's representation projection approach is flexible and can be applied to multiple languages without re-training.
4. **Improved accuracy**: The paper's co-decoding schemes improve the accuracy of the individual projection-based approaches.
5. **Well-organized and easy to follow**: The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approaches and experiments.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The paper's evaluation is limited to a few languages and datasets, and more experiments are needed to fully assess the effectiveness of the proposed approaches.
2. **Lack of novelty in the CBOW variation**: The paper's variation of the CBOW word2vec model is not novel and has been proposed before.
3. **Insufficient details on the CBOW variation**: The paper does not provide sufficient details on the CBOW variation, including the parameters and training settings.
4. **No comparison with the original CBOW and skip-gram**: The paper does not compare the proposed CBOW variation with the original CBOW and skip-gram models.
5. **No statistical significance tests**: The paper does not provide statistical significance tests to assess the improvements of the proposed approaches.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the CBOW variation, including the parameters and training settings?
2. How do you plan to extend the proposed approaches to more languages and datasets?
3. Can you provide more insights on why the rank-based co-decoding scheme is more effective than the confidence-based co-decoding scheme?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in the representation projection approach?
5. Can you provide more details on the training dictionary used in the representation projection approach?
