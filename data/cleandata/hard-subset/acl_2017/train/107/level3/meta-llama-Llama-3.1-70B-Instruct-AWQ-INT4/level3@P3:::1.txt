**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) via effective annotation and representation projection. The first approach is based on annotation projection, where a heuristic data selection scheme is developed to select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora. The second approach is based on representation projection, where word representations in vector space (word embeddings) from a target language are projected to a source language, allowing a universal representation of words in different languages. Two co-decoding schemes are also designed to combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems. The proposed approaches are evaluated on both in-house and open NER data sets for several target languages, and the results show that the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. A heuristic data selection scheme for selecting good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora.
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer based on word embeddings.
3. Two co-decoding schemes for combining the outputs of the two projection-based systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel data selection scheme that can effectively select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora, which is a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER.
2. The representation projection approach is a new and innovative way to transfer NER models across languages, and the results show that it can achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art approaches.
3. The two co-decoding schemes are well-designed and can effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems, leading to improved performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. The overall approach is not novel, and the methods are incremental improvements over existing approaches. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of existing approaches and how the proposed methods address these limitations.
2. The quality-score formula is straightforward, but the threshold determination for Table 2 is unclear. It is not clear whether the thresholds were tuned on a development set, which makes reproducibility unclear.
3. The paper could benefit from more information about the content/types of texts in the "in-house" dataset, which would help to better understand the results and their generalizability.
4. The combination of outputs from the two systems is claimed as a contribution, but the description is unclear and hard to follow. More details about the co-decoding schemes and their implementation would be helpful.
5. Many method variants are presented, but it is unclear whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems was tried or makes sense. More discussion about the potential benefits and limitations of combining multiple systems would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the threshold determination for Table 2 and how the thresholds were tuned?
2. Can you provide more information about the content/types of texts in the "in-house" dataset?
3. Can you provide more details about the co-decoding schemes and their implementation?
4. Have you tried combining more than two weakly supervised systems, and if so, what were the results?
5. How do you plan to extend the proposed approaches to other languages and datasets?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the results are clearly presented. However, some of the figures and tables could be improved for better clarity and readability. Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER, and the proposed approaches have the potential to be widely adopted.
