This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) based on effective annotation and representation projection. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes that combine the two projection-based systems in a smart way. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. A data selection scheme for annotation projection that can effectively select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora.
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer that projects word representations in vector space (word embeddings) from a target language to a source language.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based systems to produce an output that is more accurate than the outputs of individual systems.

The strengths of this submission are:

1. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approaches on both in-house and open NER data sets for a number of target languages, demonstrating the effectiveness of their methods.
2. The data selection scheme for annotation projection is shown to be important, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it works and why it is effective.
3. The representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer is a novel contribution that has the potential to be applied to other NLP tasks.

However, there are also some weaknesses in this submission:

1. The overall approach is not novel, and the methods are incremental improvements over existing work.
2. The quality-score formula for data selection is straightforward, but the threshold determination for Table 2 is unclear, and it is not explained whether the thresholds were tuned on a development set.
3. The description of the combination of outputs from the two systems is unclear and hard to follow, making it difficult to understand how the co-decoding schemes work.
4. Many method variants are presented, but it is unclear whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems was tried or makes sense.
5. More information is needed about the content/types of texts in the "in-house" dataset.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more information about the content/types of texts in the "in-house" dataset?
2. How did you determine the thresholds for the data selection scheme, and were they tuned on a development set?
3. Can you provide a clearer explanation of how the co-decoding schemes work, and why they are effective?
4. Did you try combining more than two weakly supervised systems, and if so, what were the results?
5. How do you plan to address the issue of reproducibility, given that the threshold determination for Table 2 is unclear?
