### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build an NER system for a target language without any human-annotated data. The authors propose two main methods: (1) an annotation projection (AP) approach that projects labels from an English NER system to a target language via comparable corpora, enhanced by a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections; and (2) a representation projection (RP) approach that maps target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space, allowing a single English NER model to be applied directly to the target language. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective data selection method for annotation projection.** The paper demonstrates that filtering noisy, automatically generated labels is crucial for the success of annotation projection, especially when alignment quality is low. The proposed quality scoring mechanism, while simple, leads to very large performance gains (e.g., +20 F1 on Korean), providing a strong and practical takeaway for future work in this area.
2.  **A successful framework for combining complementary projection-based systems.** The paper shows that combining a label-space projection method (AP) with a feature-space projection method (RP) yields state-of-the-art results. By analyzing the different precision/recall characteristics of the two systems, the authors motivate a co-decoding approach that consistently outperforms the individual components and achieves better results than previous work on the standard CoNLL benchmark.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Comprehensive Evaluation.** The proposed combined system is shown to outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER systems on the CoNLL test data. The evaluation is thorough, covering six target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and two distinct datasets: an in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types and the standard CoNLL dataset. This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the proposed methods.
2.  **Demonstrates the Critical Importance of Data Selection.** The experiments in Section 3.2 provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed data selection scheme. For languages with low-quality alignments like Japanese and Korean, filtering the projected data results in dramatic improvements in F1 score (+13.9 and +20.0, respectively). This is a valuable insight, highlighting that for annotation projection, data quality is more important than quantity.
3.  **Effective Combination of Complementary Approaches.** The paper cleverly combines two orthogonal approaches to cross-lingual transfer. The annotation projection method trains a new model on projected labels, while the representation projection method applies a source model to projected features. The analysis showing that one system (AP) tends to be high-precision and the other (RP) more balanced provides a solid rationale for combining them. The results confirm this, with the co-decoding schemes consistently improving F1 scores across all languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Novelty.** The core ideas behind both annotation projection and representation projection are well-established in the literature. Annotation projection for NER has been explored in prior work, and mapping word embeddings across languages using a seed dictionary is a standard technique (Mikolov et al., 2013b) that has been applied to other cross-lingual tasks. The contributions are therefore incremental improvements and effective combinations of existing paradigms rather than the proposal of a fundamentally new approach.
2.  **Key Details for Reproducibility are Missing.** Several aspects of the methodology are unclear, which hinders reproducibility and raises questions about the validity of some results.
    *   The tuning of the data selection thresholds (q, n) in Table 2 is not explained. The paper states that the best numbers were reported after trying different values, but it is not specified whether this was done on a development set or on the test set itself. If the latter, the reported improvements are not valid. Furthermore, the paper provides no guidance on how to set these crucial hyperparameters for a new language or dataset where no labeled data is available, limiting the practical utility of the method.
    *   The descriptions of the co-decoding schemes in Section 5 are too high-level and lack precision. A more formal, algorithmic description is needed to understand exactly how the confidence scores are compared in the "exclude-O" scheme and how conflicts are resolved in the "rank-based" scheme.
3.  **Analysis Could Be Deeper.** While the paper presents many experiments, the analysis of the results is somewhat superficial. For example, the paper notes that the NN2 architecture is better for the in-house data while NN1 is better for CoNLL data, but offers no hypothesis for why this might be the case. Is it related to the number of entity types, the domain of the text, or some other factor? A more in-depth discussion would strengthen the paper. Additionally, since the theme is combining systems, a discussion on whether combining more than two systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2) was considered or would be beneficial is missing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how the data selection thresholds (q, n) reported in Table 2 were chosen? Were they tuned on a held-out development set, or were they selected based on performance on the test set? Crucially, how would a practitioner choose these values for a new language where no labeled data is available for tuning?
2.  The description of the co-decoding schemes is brief. Could you provide a more formal or algorithmic description of both the "exclude-O confidence-based" and "rank-based" schemes to ensure the work is reproducible?
3.  Could you provide more information about the domain and genre of the in-house dataset? This context would be very helpful for interpreting the results, particularly the differing performance of the NN1 and NN2 architectures on the in-house versus the CoNLL datasets.
