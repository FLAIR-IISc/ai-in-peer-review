### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and combine two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, an Annotation Projection (AP) approach, projects NER tags from a source language (English) to a target language using comparable corpora. Its key feature is a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy, low-quality projected annotations. The second is a Representation Projection (RP) approach, which learns a linear mapping to project target-language word embeddings into the source-language embedding space. This allows a single, pre-trained source-language NER model to be directly applied to the target language. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems. Experiments on both in-house and CoNLL datasets show that the combined system achieves state-of-the-art performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive and effective framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.** The primary contribution is the successful integration of two complementary projection methods (annotation and representation) with a simple yet powerful co-decoding strategy. This combined framework establishes a new state-of-the-art on standard CoNLL benchmarks, providing a very strong baseline for future work.
2.  **A practical and effective heuristic for filtering projected annotations.** The proposed data selection scheme, which leverages entity-tag co-occurrence frequency to score and filter projected sentences, is shown to be highly effective, especially for language pairs with noisy alignments. The massive F1-score gains reported for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0) underscore the real-world value of this contribution.
3.  **An empirical demonstration of the complementary nature of annotation and representation projection.** The paper clearly shows that the two approaches have different characteristics (e.g., AP having high precision, RP having more balanced precision/recall). The authors effectively leverage this by designing co-decoding schemes that outperform either individual system, highlighting the benefits of this dual-view approach.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance.** The paper's strongest argument for acceptance is its empirical results. The proposed system outperforms two recent state-of-the-art approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL test data across multiple languages. This is a significant achievement and makes a clear, measurable contribution to the field.
2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Methodology.** The overall framework is methodologically sound. Combining two different paradigms (data-level projection vs. model-level transfer) is an intelligent way to leverage different signals and mitigate the weaknesses of each. The design of the co-decoding schemes is well-motivated by the observed precision/recall trade-offs of the individual systems.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining each component of their system, from the base NER models to the projection methods and the final combination schemes. This clarity makes the work accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification and Ablation for Proposed Model Variants.** The paper introduces several new variants of existing methods, but their benefits are not empirically validated. For example, a variant of the CBOW word embedding model is proposed (Section 4.1) and a weighted least squares objective is used for cross-lingual mapping (Section 4.2). However, there are no experiments comparing these variants to their standard, widely-used counterparts (i.e., the original CBOW/skip-gram models and the unweighted mapping from Mikolov et al., 2013b). Without these ablation studies, the contributions of these specific methodological choices are unsubstantiated.
2.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing.** The paper claims "significant improvement" from the data selection scheme and presents multiple tables comparing different systems. However, no statistical significance tests are reported. This is a critical omission, as it is unclear whether many of the observed F1 score differences (e.g., the 0.7 F1 gain for German in Table 2 or the small differences between co-decoding schemes) are statistically meaningful or simply due to random variation.
3.  **Superficial Analysis of Results.** While the results are strong, the evaluation section is brief and lacks depth. The paper would be much stronger with a more detailed analysis of the results. For example, why does the NN2 model perform better on in-house data while NN1 is better on CoNLL data? What kinds of errors are corrected by the co-decoding schemes? An error analysis or a discussion of language-specific phenomena would provide valuable insights beyond just reporting final F1 scores.
4.  **Minor Organizational Issues.** The paper's structure could be improved. Related work is currently scattered in the Introduction and Section 4.4; a consolidated "Related Work" section would be more appropriate. Additionally, results are presented prematurely in the methodology section (Table 2 in Section 3.2), which disrupts the paper's flow. Moving all evaluation results to the main "Experiments" section would create a cleaner separation between methods and results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You propose a variant of CBOW with distance-based decay weights. Could you elaborate on the motivation for this choice? More importantly, could you provide results comparing it against the standard word2vec CBOW and skip-gram models to demonstrate its necessity and advantage for this task?
2.  Your cross-lingual mapping uses a weighted least squares objective based on translation probabilities. How does this perform compared to the original, unweighted formulation from Mikolov et al. (2013b)? An ablation study quantifying the impact of this weighting would be very helpful.
3.  Have you performed statistical significance tests for your main results? Specifically, are the improvements from your data selection scheme (Table 2) and your co-decoding schemes (Tables 3-5) statistically significant compared to the respective baselines?
