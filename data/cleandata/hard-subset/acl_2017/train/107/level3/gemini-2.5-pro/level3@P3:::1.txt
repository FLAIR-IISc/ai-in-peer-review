### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a weakly supervised approach for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that does not require any human-annotated data in the target language. The authors propose two main methods: 1) an annotation projection (AP) approach that projects NER labels from English to a target language via comparable corpora, enhanced by a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections; and 2) a representation projection (RP) approach that maps target-language word embeddings into the English embedding space, allowing a single English NER model to be applied directly to the target language. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs from the AP and RP systems. The proposed methods are evaluated on both an in-house dataset with fine-grained entity types and the standard CoNLL datasets, demonstrating that the combined systems outperform previous state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.

### Main Contributions
1.  **Strong Empirical Results via System Combination:** The paper's primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that combining two distinct weakly supervised transfer methods—annotation projection and representation projection—can achieve state-of-the-art performance on standard cross-lingual NER benchmarks. This provides a strong new baseline for the task.
2.  **Effective Heuristic for Data Filtering in Annotation Projection:** The paper introduces a simple but effective heuristic for selecting high-quality training instances from noisy, automatically projected data. This method yields significant performance gains, particularly for language pairs with lower-quality word alignments, highlighting the importance of data quality control in this setting.

### Strengths
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The key strength of this work is its strong empirical performance. The combined systems outperform two strong, contemporary cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL test data. This is a significant result and provides a valuable data point for future research in this area.
2.  **Thorough Evaluation Across Diverse Languages and Datasets:** The authors conduct experiments on a wide range of target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and across two distinct evaluation settings: a proprietary in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types and the standard 4-type CoNLL datasets. This extensive evaluation demonstrates the general applicability and robustness of the proposed methods.
3.  **Demonstrated Importance of Data Selection:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection, while straightforward, is shown to be highly effective. The large F1 score improvements for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0) in Table 2, where alignment quality is lower, clearly illustrate the practical benefit of filtering noisy projected data. This is an important and practical finding for anyone working with annotation projection.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core ideas of annotation projection and representation projection via embedding mapping are well-established in cross-lingual NLP. The paper’s contribution lies primarily in the effective engineering and combination of these existing paradigms. The quality score for data selection is a simple frequency-based heuristic, and the co-decoding schemes are straightforward rules. As such, the work feels more like an incremental improvement rather than a novel methodological advance.
2.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility in Data Selection:** The paper's data selection method, while effective, is described with insufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper does not explain how the quality and entity count thresholds `(q, n)` reported in Table 2 were determined. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters were tuned on a development set for each language, which is a critical detail. Without this information or general guidance on setting these parameters, it is difficult for others to apply this technique to new languages or datasets.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on Key Components:** Several key aspects of the methodology are underspecified. The description of the co-decoding schemes in Section 5 is very brief. The "rank-based" scheme is motivated by an empirical observation about precision/recall, which may not generalize, and the rules for handling conflicting entity spans from the two systems are not fully detailed. Furthermore, the evaluation on the "in-house" dataset is difficult to interpret, as no information is provided about the domain of the text or the nature of the 50+ fine-grained entity types. This lack of transparency limits the community's ability to understand the results and the challenges posed by this dataset.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the data selection thresholds `(q, n)` used for the annotation projection results in Table 2: How were these specific values chosen for each language? Were they tuned on a development set? If so, could you please provide details about the tuning process and the range of values explored?
2.  The co-decoding schemes combine the output of one annotation projection system and one representation projection system. Did you experiment with combining the outputs of more than two systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2)? Given that the two neural network models (NN1 and NN2) exhibit different performance characteristics, it seems plausible that a three-way combination could yield further improvements.
3.  Could you please provide more information about the in-house dataset used in Section 6.2? Specifically, what is the domain of the text (e.g., news, web documents), and could you give some examples of the 50+ entity types? This context is crucial for interpreting the results and understanding the system's performance on a fine-grained NER task.
