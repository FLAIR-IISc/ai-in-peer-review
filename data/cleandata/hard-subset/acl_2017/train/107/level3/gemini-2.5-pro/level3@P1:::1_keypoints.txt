- Paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using two projection-based approaches: annotation projection from comparable corpora and representation projection via linear embedding mapping.
- Main contribution 1: combining annotation and representation projection with simple co-decoding yields state-of-the-art results on CoNLL benchmarks.
- Main contribution 2: a heuristic data selection method that filters noisy projected annotations and substantially improves performance, especially with poor alignments.
- Strength: Strong empirical results, outperforming recent baselines and giving large gains for languages like German.
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (CoNLL and an in-house multi-entity dataset).
- Strength: The simple data selection heuristic is highly effective, producing large F1 gains for languages with low-quality alignments (e.g., +13.9 JP, +20.0 KO).
- Weakness: Core methods (annotation projection and embedding mapping) are not novel and are incremental applications of existing techniques.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter tuning for data selection (thresholds q and n) is unclear and potentially flawed—uncertain whether test-set tuning occurred, threatening reproducibility and validity.
- Weakness: No practical guidance on choosing q and n in a true zero-shot setting; optimal thresholds vary greatly across languages.
- Weakness: Co-decoding scheme descriptions lack detail (how confidences across models are normalized; how partial overlap conflicts are resolved), hindering reproducibility.
- Question: How were the data selection thresholds (q, n) chosen for each language—were labeled dev sets used, and what were the data splits?
- Question: How should a practitioner pick q and n for a new target language with no labeled data; do optimal settings correlate with observable properties like alignment accuracy?
- Question: Please provide details about the in-house NER dataset (domain/genre and examples of the 50+ entity types) to contextualize Table 3 results.
- Question: In rank-based co-decoding, how are partial boundary conflicts resolved (e.g., [Barack Obama] vs. [Obama])—discarded, merged, or otherwise handled?
