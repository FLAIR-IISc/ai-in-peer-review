**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a weakly supervised framework for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without requiring any annotated data in those languages. The authors present two primary approaches. The first, "Annotation Projection," uses an existing NER system in a source language (English) to label comparable corpora. These labels are then projected to the aligned target language sentences. The key contribution here is a novel heuristic data selection scheme that filters this noisy, auto-generated training data by scoring sentences based on the consistency of entity-label assignments, which proves particularly effective when alignment quality is low.

The second approach, "Representation Projection," is a direct model transfer method. An NER model (a neural network) is trained on the source language using only word embeddings as features. For a target language, its monolingual word embeddings are mapped to the source language's embedding space via a learned linear transformation. This allows the source-language NER model to be applied directly to the projected target-language representations without retraining.

Finally, the paper introduces two "co-decoding" schemes (one confidence-based, one rank-based) to combine the outputs of these two complementary systems. Experiments on both fine-grained in-house datasets and standard CoNLL benchmarks show that the combined system outperforms previous state-of-the-art weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods.

**Contributions**

1.  **A powerful and effective combined system for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER:** The primary contribution is the complete framework that intelligently combines two distinct projection methodologies (annotation projection and representation projection). This combined system leverages the high-precision nature of the annotation projection model and the more balanced performance of the representation projection model to achieve new state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL benchmark datasets for Spanish, Dutch, and German. This is a strong, practical contribution for a challenging task.

2.  **An effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection:** The paper introduces a simple but well-motivated heuristic for filtering noisy training data generated via annotation projection. By measuring the consistency of projected labels for each entity across the corpus, the method effectively prunes low-quality sentence pairs. The experiments show this technique yields substantial improvements (up to 20 F1 points) for languages with poor alignment quality, making the annotation projection approach more robust.

**Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-art empirical results:** The paper demonstrates superior performance over two strong, recent baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on standard CoNLL test data. The gains are consistent across multiple languages, validating the effectiveness of the proposed combined approach. Achieving SOTA results provides a compelling argument for acceptance.

2.  **Comprehensive and well-designed approach:** The work is thorough in its exploration. It investigates two orthogonal yet complementary methods for cross-lingual transfer—one operating at the label level (annotation projection) and the other at the feature level (representation projection). The further step of designing co-decoding schemes to combine their strengths demonstrates a thoughtful system-building process.

3.  **Clarity and readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivations behind each component are well-explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient justification and ablation for proposed model variants:** The paper introduces several modifications to existing methods without empirically justifying their necessity or superiority over the original versions.
    *   A variant of the CBOW word2vec model is proposed, but no motivation is given for its design, nor is it compared against the standard CBOW or Skip-gram models.
    *   The cross-lingual embedding mapping (Eq. 7) adds frequency weights to the formulation of Mikolov et al. (2013b), but no experiment shows that this weighting actually improves performance over the unweighted original.
    Without these baseline comparisons, it is difficult to attribute the performance gains to these specific innovations versus the overall framework.

2.  **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The paper claims that the data selection scheme provides "significant" improvements, particularly for Japanese and Korean (Table 2). However, no statistical significance tests are reported to support these claims. Given the potential for variability in results, especially with smaller improvements on German and Portuguese, statistical validation is necessary to confirm that the observed gains are not due to chance.

3.  **Limited analysis of results and model behaviors:** While the paper achieves excellent results, the evaluation section is primarily a presentation of scores. There is little qualitative analysis or discussion. For instance, why does the rank-based co-decoding work better for the in-house data, while the confidence-based one is better for CoNLL data? What are the common error types? A deeper analysis would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the different projection and combination methods, strengthening the paper's contribution beyond just a new SOTA score.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the motivation for your CBOW variant and, more importantly, provide results comparing it to the standard CBOW and/or Skip-gram models to demonstrate its contribution?

2.  For the representation projection, could you provide an ablation study comparing your weighted linear mapping (Eq. 7) against the original unweighted formulation from Mikolov et al. (2013b)? This would help isolate the impact of this modification.

3.  Have you performed statistical significance tests on the improvements gained from your data selection scheme (Table 2)? Reporting these would strengthen your claim that the method is effective.
