**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) without target-language annotations. The authors propose and evaluate two main approaches: 1) an annotation projection method that uses a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy labels generated from comparable corpora, and 2) a representation projection method that maps target-language word embeddings into the source language space, allowing a source-language model to be applied directly. Finally, they introduce co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems, demonstrating improved performance over individual models and prior state-of-the-art on CoNLL datasets.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that a simple, frequency-based data selection heuristic can substantially improve the quality of projected annotations for weakly supervised NER, particularly when alignment quality is low.
2.  A secondary contribution is the systematic comparison and combination of two distinct projection paradigms (annotation vs. representation), showing that a simple ensemble can outperform stronger individual systems.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper presents strong empirical results. The proposed data selection scheme yields significant gains (+20.0 F1 for Korean), and the final combined system surpasses previous state-of-the-art baselines on the CoNLL test set. This provides a strong new baseline for the task.
2.  The work effectively highlights the critical importance of managing noise in annotation projection. While the quality-score formula is straightforward, its demonstrated effectiveness provides a valuable, easy-to-implement technique for practitioners.
3.  The evaluation is conducted across multiple languages and two different types of datasets (a fine-grained in-house set and a standard public benchmark), which lends credibility to the findings.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The overall approach lacks novelty. Both annotation projection and representation projection are well-established methods for cross-lingual transfer. The proposed methods, such as the data selection quality score and co-decoding schemes, are incremental improvements rather than novel techniques.
2.  The methodology lacks clarity and raises concerns about reproducibility. The process for determining the quality `(q)` and entity `(n)` thresholds for data selection in Table 2 is not explained. Without knowing if these were tuned on a development set, the robustness of these key results is unclear.
3.  Key components of the work are poorly described. The explanation of the co-decoding schemes is too brief to be easily understood or reimplemented. Furthermore, the lack of detail about the "in-house" dataset (e.g., domain, content) makes it difficult to fully interpret the results presented in Table 3. The paper also fails to provide general guidance on how to tune its parameters for new language pairs or datasets.
