- Tackles cross-lingual NER without target annotations by combining annotation projection and representation (embedding) projection plus co-decoding.  
- Annotation projection uses English→target projection with a per-entity frequency-based data selection heuristic.  
- Representation projection linearly maps target embeddings into English space and decodes with an English-only neural NER model.  
- Two co-decoding schemes introduced: exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based prioritization.  
- Evaluated on four in-house languages (>50 types) and CoNLL (4 types); co-decoding outperforms prior methods, especially when alignment quality is low.  
- Contribution: Empirical combination of AP and embedding transfer yields SOTA cross-lingual NER on CoNLL without target supervision.  
- Contribution: Simple, effective per-entity frequency data selection giving large gains on low-alignment pairs.  
- Contribution: Lightweight weighted linear mapping learned from a small seed dictionary, requiring no target retraining.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results across multiple languages, datasets, and entity inventories, improving over baselines.  
- Strength: Practical weak supervision—realistic resource requirements (alignments/phrase tables, small dictionary, monolingual corpora).  
- Strength: Interpretable and inexpensive selection heuristic that substantially reduces projection noise.  
- Strength: Co-decoding leverages complementary error profiles (AP high-precision/low-recall; representation projection higher recall).  
- Strength: Clear methodological descriptions for core components (mapping, NN architectures, AP pipeline).  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—mostly incremental/engineering contributions rather than new methods.  
- Weakness: Data selection threshold tuning (q, n) and protocol are under-specified, hindering reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Co-decoding description lacks detail on score computation, calibration, conflict resolution, and sequence consistency.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablations on key design choices (dictionary size/weighting, orthogonality, NN variants, calibration sensitivity).  
- Weakness: In-house dataset details (domains, label inventory) are sparse and statistical significance is not reported.  
- Q: How were data selection thresholds chosen—target dev set or other protocol—and what defaults for new pairs?  
- Q: How are confidence scores computed and calibrated across AP and NN models; any calibration methods tried?  
- Q: In rank-based co-decoding how are span/type conflicts and BIO validity resolved and tie-broken?  
- Q: For representation mapping, were orthogonal/Procrustes mappings, mean-centering, or normalization tried; sensitivity to dictionary size/weights?  
- Q: What are domains and label inventories of the in-house datasets and how do they match English training data?  
- Q: Were more-than-two system combinations or ensembling of AP variants attempted, and if not why?  
- Q: What resources produced alignments/phrase tables (bitext vs. comparable corpora) and how feasible is this for truly low-resource pairs?  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — solid, practical recipe with modest novelty; clarifying tuning and co-decoding details would improve impact and reproducibility.
