Summary
This paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation. It explores two projection-based approaches: (1) annotation projection from English via aligned comparable corpora, augmented with a heuristic data selection scheme; and (2) representation projection by mapping target embeddings to English via a linear transformation and directly decoding with an English NER. The paper also proposes two co-decoding heuristics to combine the outputs. Experiments on in-house data for four languages and on CoNLL 02/03 show gains over prior cross-lingual baselines.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: data selection for annotation projection matters a lot, especially when alignment is noisy. The proposed sentence-level quality score (+ entity count threshold) provides a simple, effective filter that improves F1 substantially.
2) Practical baseline: a straightforward linear cross-lingual embedding mapping for direct model transfer, plus simple co-decoding heuristics that yield consistent improvements over individual systems on multiple languages.
(While the methods are mostly incremental, the combined empirical package is a useful reference baseline.)

Strengths
- Clear empirical value of data selection: The results in Table 2 and Table 3 show very large gains (+13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean) when applying the quality-based selection on noisy alignments. This convincingly demonstrates that selection is critical and can outweigh model capacity.
- Simple, broadly applicable methods: Both the linear embedding mapping and the selection heuristic are lightweight and easy to adopt in low-resource settings without heavy reliance on language-specific resources or re-training target-specific models.
- Consistent improvements via co-decoding: Across both in-house and CoNLL setups, combining the two weakly supervised systems increases F1 versus either component alone, and the paper offers an intuition for complementary error profiles (AP high-precision vs. RP balanced).
- Wide coverage and comparisons: The paper evaluates several languages and both internal and public datasets, and compares against two recognized cross-lingual NER baselines, achieving better test performance on CoNLL languages.
- Sensible engineering choices: The use of weighted least-squares for embedding mapping, a dictionary size cap, and MEMM for scalability with large label inventories are reasonable and well-motivated by practical constraints.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: All core components (annotation projection, linear embedding mapping, and simple output fusion) are known techniques; the proposed quality score and co-decoding are incremental refinements rather than conceptual advances. The paper reads mainly as a careful engineering study rather than a methodological breakthrough.
- Unclear threshold selection and reproducibility for data selection: The quality score q(y) is intuitive, but Table 2 reports “best” (q, n) without explaining how these were chosen. For in-house languages, it is unclear whether thresholds were tuned using held-out development data, or via the test sets, or via unsupervised proxy criteria. Without a precise tuning protocol, reproducing Table 2 is difficult and there is a risk of inadvertent test-set peeking.
- Lack of guidance to tune on new languages: Although data selection is shown to be important, the paper provides little actionable guidance (e.g., starting ranges for q and n, sensitivity analyses, or rules tied to alignment quality) to help practitioners pick thresholds on new datasets/language pairs without labeled dev sets.
- Co-decoding description is underspecified: The exclude-O confidence scheme and rank-based scheme are described at a high level, but the paper does not detail score calibration, span reconciliation, and tie-breaking. Given that confidence scores across models are not directly comparable, the implementation details are crucial for reproducibility and for understanding when each scheme is preferable. It is also unclear whether combining more than two weakly supervised systems was explored, or whether fusing AP with both NN1 and NN2 (or other variants) yields further gains.
- Insufficient dataset transparency: The “in-house” datasets are only briefly characterized (>50 types, 30–45K tokens). The paper needs more detail on domains, text genres, annotation guidelines and consistency, and the relationship between these label sets and CoNLL labels. This limits interpretability of cross-domain effects and transferability of the findings.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Data selection thresholds:
  - How were (q, n) thresholds chosen for Table 2 per language? Was there a target-language dev set? If not, what unsupervised criterion or cross-validation procedure was used?
  - Can you provide a sensitivity analysis of F1 versus q and n, and guidance on how to pick thresholds given measured alignment quality?
- Frequency table and quality score:
  - Are T(e, l) values normalized per entity (as Table 1 suggests)? Are alignment confidences or source NER confidences incorporated, or are counts unweighted?
  - How are multi-token entity variants, casing variants, and transliterations handled when constructing e?
- Co-decoding details:
  - How are confidence scores computed and compared across the MEMM and NN models? Are they calibrated (e.g., temperature scaling, isotonic)?
  - How are overlapping spans merged, and how are exact ties resolved? Please include pseudo-code or a deterministic description.
  - Did you try combining AP with both NN1 and NN2 simultaneously, or fusing more than two weakly supervised systems? If not, why not?
- Reproducibility of input resources:
  - What are the sources and domains of the “comparable corpora” and the “in-house” test sets for each language? Please specify tokenization/segmentation tools (esp. for Japanese/Korean), alignment toolkit and settings, and English training corpora domains.
  - For embedding mappings: exact dictionary sizes per language, how “larger dictionaries might harm performance” was measured, and sensitivity to dictionary size.
- Tuning/setup:
  - For CoNLL experiments, which hyperparameters (including co-decoding choice) were tuned on dev sets, and which were fixed from prior experiments?
  - For MEMM/NN models, please provide feature templates, context windows, learning rates, regularization, and early stopping criteria.

Additional comments
- A small ablation on using only q(y) vs. only n(y) vs. both would strengthen the selection story.
- Error analysis by entity type and boundary errors could clarify when each approach helps and why.
- Consider reporting per-language OOV rates and the impact of the NER-trained unk vector choice.
- If possible, release scripts for building T(e, l) and computing q(y), and provide a minimal recipe for selecting (q, n) without target dev data (e.g., via alignment scores or unsupervised stability metrics).
