Summary
The paper tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection strategies: (i) annotation projection with a heuristic training-instance selection, trained with MEMMs; and (ii) representation projection, mapping target embeddings into English using a frequency-weighted variant of Mikolov’s linear mapping, powering neural NER. Two output combination schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) exploit complementary precision/recall profiles. Experiments on in-house and CoNLL datasets show strong gains and state-of-the-art results among weakly supervised methods.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An effective and simple rank-based co-decoding that consistently improves over individual systems and prior cross-lingual NER baselines.
2) A practical data selection method for annotation projection that substantially boosts performance under noisy alignments.
3) A frequency-weighted cross-lingual embedding mapping for direct model transfer; the proposed CBOW variant is potentially useful but under-specified.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance: Combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER (e.g., Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) and approach supervised baselines in some settings, with clear, multi-language evidence (e.g., large F1 gains for JA/KR via data selection; consistent improvements from co-decoding).
- Complementarity exploited well: The analysis of precision-recall trade-offs between AP (high precision) and representation projection (balanced) motivates a rank-based combiner that yields sizable F1 gains.
- Clarity and reproducibility of the pipeline: The paper is easy to follow; modeling choices (CRF/MEMM/NN1/NN2) and training regimes are generally described; evaluation spans in-house and public benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient detail and validation of the CBOW variant: Motivation and all training parameters are missing; no comparison to vanilla CBOW and skip-gram, preventing assessment of the claimed improvement.
- No ablation against the original (unweighted) Mikolov mapping to justify the frequency-weighted projection.
- Lack of statistical significance testing, despite claims of “significant” improvements from data selection; this weakens the empirical claims.
- Evaluation discussion is brief; more error analysis (entity types, boundary errors, language-specific failure modes) would strengthen insights.
- Organization: Section 4.4 (Related Work) should be a standalone section placed earlier; move Table 2’s evaluation to the main evaluation section for consistency.

Questions to Authors
- Please detail the CBOW variant: decay functional form (e.g., 1/d vs exponential), window sizes, negatives, subsampling, learning rates, epochs, vocab thresholds, and dimensions per language; report results vs standard CBOW and skip-gram.
- Provide an ablation comparing weighted vs unweighted cross-lingual mappings and report which languages benefit most.
- Run and report statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap or approximate randomization) for the data selection and co-decoding gains.
- Have you tried exponential decay over context contributions as in Iacobacci, Pilehvar & Navigli (2016)? If not, please compare or discuss.
