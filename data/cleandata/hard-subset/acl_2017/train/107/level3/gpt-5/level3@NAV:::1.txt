Summary: The paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with two English-projection strategies—annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection via cross-lingual embedding mappings—and proposes simple co-decoding rules to combine them. Results are reported on multiple in-house and CoNLL datasets, showing the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual baselines.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
- Empirical demonstration that data selection in annotation projection substantially improves performance, especially under noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9/+20.0 F1 on Japanese/Korean in Table 2).
- Simple, effective co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) that consistently improve over individual weakly supervised systems across languages (e.g., +2.9–6.2 F1 in Table 3; +1.3–3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev in Table 4).
- A straightforward representation-projection transfer baseline using monolingual embeddings and a weighted linear mapping, yielding competitive performance without target-language annotation.

Strengths:
- Breadth of evaluation: multiple target languages and both in-house (50+ types) and CoNLL (4 types) settings. This breadth supports claims about generality.
- Clear evidence that data selection matters: the simple frequency-based quality score and entity-count thresholds yield large gains where alignment is weak (Table 2), isolating the value of selection.
- Combination beats components: co-decoding reliably improves F1 across datasets (Tables 3–4) and achieves better CoNLL test scores than prior cross-lingual methods, indicating practical utility.
- Practicality: methods rely on widely available ingredients (alignments, monolingual embeddings, small dictionaries) and avoid target-language supervision.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: both projection mechanisms are known; contributions are mainly incremental heuristics and system engineering. The quality-score formula and linear mapping are standard.
- Reproducibility gaps: threshold choices (q, n) in Table 2 are “best numbers” with no procedure; unclear whether tuning used a dev set in each target language. Co-decoding details (score calibration, conflict resolution ordering) are hard to follow and may be sensitive.
- Insufficient guidance for new settings: no principled method to set q, n, dictionary size thresholds, or co-decoding preferences for new language pairs/domains.
- Method proliferation without ablation: many variants (AP, NN1, NN2, two co-decoders), but no analysis of combining more than two weakly supervised systems or when NN1 vs NN2 is preferable.
- Dataset transparency: the in-house corpora and test sets lack description (domains, genre mix, entity-type distribution), making results hard to interpret and replicate.

Questions to Authors:
- How were q and n chosen per language? Was there a target-language dev set or cross-validation on projected data? Please provide a tuning protocol and sensitivity plots.
- What exactly is the co-decoding procedure? How are O vs non-O scores calibrated, and how are overlaps resolved deterministically?
- Did you try ensembling more than two weakly supervised systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2), or calibrating scores to enable confidence-based fusion?
- Please detail the in-house datasets (domains, sources, type inventories, label distributions) and release any resources or synthetic proxies to aid reproducibility.
