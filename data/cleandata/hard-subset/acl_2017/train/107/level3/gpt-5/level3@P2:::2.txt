Summary
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language via two complementary projection-based approaches and simple output combination: (a) annotation projection from English onto comparable corpora with a heuristic sentence selection based on entity-tag frequency consistency, and (b) representation projection that linearly maps target-language embeddings into English space to directly run an English NN-based NER. Two co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) combine the outputs. Across four in-house languages (with >50 entity types) and CoNLL languages, the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), sometimes approaching supervised baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection that substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
- A practical representation-projection transfer for NER: weighted linear mapping (dictionary-based) from target to English embedding space and decoding with an English-only NN.
- Empirically strong, lightweight co-decoding schemes that reliably improve over either projection alone and surpass prior cross-lingual baselines on CoNLL.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results and breadth: The paper evaluates on multiple languages and settings (in-house fine-grained and CoNLL coarse-grained). The combined systems consistently outperform prior cross-lingual approaches, and the gains over the single-view systems are nontrivial (e.g., +2.9–6.2 F1 in-house; +1.3–3.6 F1 on CoNLL dev).
- Practicality and simplicity: Both the data selection and the cross-lingual mapping are simple to implement and computationally light. The approach scales to new languages without target-side labels or retraining of the NER model (for representation projection).
- Clear characterization of complementary error profiles: The observation that annotation projection yields high precision/low recall while representation projection is more balanced is insightful and well-exploited in the rank-based co-decoder.
- Sensible engineering choices: Using a linear mapping with a small, frequency-thresholded dictionary and tuning sentence selection by quality and entity-count thresholds are pragmatic, portable design decisions that practitioners can reuse.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is easy to follow, the methods are well-described, and the empirical section—though it could be expanded—addresses both absolute performance and the combination effects.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in representation projection: The core transfer relies on Mikolov-style linear mappings with a frequency-weighted least-squares variant. The paper should explicitly compare to the unweighted mapping (and to orthogonally-constrained variants) to justify the weighted choice and quantify its impact on NER.
- Insufficient analysis of the proposed CBOW variant: The text proposes a CBOW variant with distance-decayed contexts but does not motivate design choices, hyperparameters, or show comparative results versus standard CBOW and skip-gram. Without ablations, it is hard to attribute gains to this choice.
- Missing statistical significance tests: Claims that the data selection scheme “significantly” improves F1 are not backed by statistical testing. Given the headline gains (particularly for Japanese/Korean), significance tests (e.g., paired bootstrap on entity-level F1) are necessary to substantiate the claims.
- Evaluation/analysis could go deeper: Error breakdowns (by entity type, boundary vs. type errors), effect of dictionary size/quality, sensitivity to q and n thresholds, and ablations on co-decoding design (exclude-O vs. pure confidence; rank-ordering rationale) would improve understanding and reproducibility.
- Organizational issues: Related work is interleaved within the method sections; Section 4.4 fits better as lead-in to 4.2; some evaluation content (e.g., Table 2) appears mid-method rather than in a unified evaluation section, which slightly disrupts flow.
- Reproducibility concerns for in-house setup: Details about the comparable corpora, alignment pipeline, and release status of resources are insufficient. Given the reliance on alignment quality (and >50 types), more transparency is needed.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Representation projection:
  - Please compare (i) unweighted vs. frequency-weighted linear mapping; (ii) unconstrained vs. orthogonal Procrustes mapping; and report the impact on NER. How large is the gain from weighting?
  - What is the dictionary size sensitivity curve and how do you select the threshold? Does performance saturate around 5–6K pairs?
- CBOW variant:
  - What context window, decay function, and hyperparameters are used? Please report comparisons to standard CBOW and skip-gram. Why is the proposed variant preferable for NER transfer?
  - Did you try exponential vs. reciprocal distance decay (cf. Iacobacci, Pilehvar & Navigli, 2016)? Any effect?
- Data selection:
  - Provide significance tests for the gains in Table 2. How robust are results to q and n? Show curves vs. thresholds and discuss selection–quantity trade-offs.
  - How is the frequency table T smoothed? How are rare entities handled to avoid biasing toward frequent entities?
- Co-decoding:
  - How do you ensure BIO validity after merging? Are conflicts resolved to preserve sequence constraints?
  - Why does exclude-O confidence sometimes underperform rank-based? Any calibration attempts across models?

Additional comments
- Please introduce a standalone Related Work section earlier, and move Section 4.4 there or as a lead-in to 4.2. Move Table 2 and its discussion to the evaluation section for cleaner structure.
- Expand the evaluation with ablations (CBOW variant, mapping choices, dictionary size/weights, q/n sensitivity), per-type analysis, and qualitative error cases.
- Consider citing and comparing to embedding strategies with positional/decay weighting (e.g., Iacobacci, Pilehvar & Navigli, 2016), and more recent cross-lingual mappings (e.g., CCA-based, BilBOWA/MultiCCA).
- Minor clarifications: specify the number of prototype vectors in NN2, regularization/dropout settings, handling of OOV/UNK vectors, and how comparable corpora are aligned (sentence- and word-level) and evaluated.
