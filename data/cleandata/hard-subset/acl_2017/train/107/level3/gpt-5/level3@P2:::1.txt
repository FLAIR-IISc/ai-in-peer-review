Summary and contributions (as I see them)
The paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation. It evaluates two projection-based strategies: (1) annotation projection from English onto comparable corpora coupled with a heuristic data-selection scheme; (2) direct model transfer via linear mapping of target-language word embeddings into English, enabling an English-only neural NER to decode target text. It further proposes two simple co-decoding heuristics (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) to ensemble the two systems. Experiments on four in-house languages (fine-grained types) and CoNLL Spanish/Dutch/German show consistent gains from data selection and co-decoding, with test-time performance competitive with or better than prior cross-lingual NER work.

Contributions (1–3, in order of strength)
1) Empirical demonstration that simple, alignment-aware selection of projection-labeled sentences yields large gains when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
2) A practical, competitive direct-transfer baseline: linear cross-lingual embedding mapping plus an English NN NER, requiring no target retraining.
3) A lightweight co-decoding heuristic that reliably improves over either component and outperforms prior cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL test sets.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across diverse languages and setups. On in-house data, the rank-based co-decoder improves over the best component by 2.9–6.2 F1 (e.g., Japanese 55.0→59.6; Korean 45.6→51.8; German 66.4→69.3; Portuguese 64.7→69.9), at times approaching supervised systems trained on 90–173K tokens. On CoNLL, the co-decoder improves the best component by 1.3–3.6 F1 on dev and surpasses Täckström (2012) and Tsai (2016) on test.
- Clear evidence that data selection matters. When alignment quality is low, the proposed quality/coverage filter yields large improvements (Table 2), making a compelling case for careful selection in projection-based NER.
- Simple, resource-light techniques with broad applicability. The methods require standard resources (a phrase table/alignment model and monolingual embeddings) and avoid language-specific features, making them attractive for low-resource settings and new languages.
- Sensible analysis of precision–recall tradeoffs guides the ensemble design. The observation that annotation projection is high-precision/low-recall while representation projection is more balanced is borne out in the numbers and motivates the rank-based combiner that prioritizes high-precision spans.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty; methods are incremental. Annotation projection and embedding-based direct transfer are established ideas. The data-selection metric (mean of entity-tag frequencies) and the co-decoding heuristics (exclude-O confidence; priority merge) are straightforward. The contribution is primarily empirical/practical rather than methodological.
- Reproducibility and tuning unclear for data selection. Table 2 reports best thresholds (q, n) per language, but the paper does not specify: how thresholds were tuned (on what dev data); whether any target-language annotation was used for tuning; the search space; or sensitivity curves. Without guidance, it is hard to reproduce or apply the method to new languages without risking overfitting to test data.
- Insufficient detail on in-house datasets. The paper mentions “over 50 entity types” and token counts, but lacks domain/genre, source, labeling guidelines, and train/dev/test splits. This prevents assessing domain mismatch, generality, and fairness of comparisons (e.g., English training domain vs target).
- Incomplete ablations and justification for design choices. Key choices are asserted but not studied: dictionary size is “~5–6K” pairs; linear mapping unconstrained (no orthogonality/centering/regularization); using MEMM instead of CRF due to complexity (no timing/accuracy tradeoff); the effect of the weighting in Eq. 7; OOV handling beyond an “unk vector.” No statistical significance tests are reported.
- Co-decoding description and calibration are underspecified. The exclude-O confidence scheme presumes comparable per-token probabilities across models; no probability calibration or normalization is discussed. The rank-based scheme’s conflict resolution is briefly described, but behavior on overlapping spans or disagreements in tag type is not deeply analyzed. It is unclear whether learning-based stacking or more than two systems were explored.

Questions to authors
- Data selection: How were (q, n) chosen per language? What development signal was used, given the target-language “no annotation” setting? Please provide a principled tuning protocol and sensitivity analyses (F1 vs q, n).
- In-house data: What domains/genres and label inventory were used? Are train/dev/test splits fixed across experiments? Can you release a description or a subset?
- Alignment resources: What corpora and tools produced the phrase tables/alignments? How do coverage and alignment quality correlate with q, n and downstream F1?
- Representation projection: Did you try orthogonal Procrustes or centered/length-normalized embeddings? Any regularization on Eq. 7? Sensitivity to dictionary size beyond 5–6K?
- Co-decoding: Were any calibration methods (e.g., temperature scaling) tried to make confidence scores comparable? Did you attempt a simple learned meta-decoder on dev data?
- Comparisons: Why not include a multilingual/bilingual LSTM-CRF baseline with character features (which often helps NER), at least on CoNLL?

Additional comments
- Please clarify exact rules for conflict resolution in rank-based decoding (BIO/BILOU constraints, nested entities, boundary disagreements).
- Report the number of selected sentences/tokens after data selection for all (q, n) tried, and provide practical guidance (e.g., target proportion retained or thresholds as a function of observed alignment F1).
- The complexity discussion for CRFs could be nuanced with empirical training times for your label set. A small CRF experiment on a subset would strengthen the MEMM choice.
- Eq. 7 may benefit from L2 regularization and embedding centering; noting whether these were tried would help practitioners.
- Consider reporting significance tests on CoNLL test gains and providing code/data to enhance reproducibility.
