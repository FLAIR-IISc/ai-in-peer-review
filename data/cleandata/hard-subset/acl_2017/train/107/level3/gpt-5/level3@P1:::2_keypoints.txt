- Paper addresses cross-lingual NER without target annotations using: annotation projection with a frequency-based instance filter, embedding-based direct transfer via a frequency-weighted linear mapping, and two co-decoding schemes to combine them.  
- Contribution: effective, simple instance-selection heuristic for annotation projection that yields large F1 gains when alignments are noisy.  
- Contribution: practical direct-transfer pipeline using embedding projection that needs no per-target retraining and uses only word embeddings as features.  
- Contribution: co-decoding schemes (confidence-based exclude-O and rank-based) that consistently improve over single systems and beat prior cross-lingual baselines.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance across CoNLL and in-house datasets, with notable gains on German and large improvements for Japanese/Korean.  
- Strength: the frequency-table-based sentence-quality metric is simple, intuitive, efficient, and effective when alignments are weak.  
- Strength: the two systems are complementary (AP high-precision/low-recall, representation-projection more balanced) and the rank-based combination leverages this asymmetry well.  
- Strength: practical and scalable approach—single English NER model plus dictionary-based linear mapping with small seed dictionaries is realistic for new languages.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written with sensible baselines and reproducible methodology.  
- Weakness: core techniques show limited novelty—representation projection and NER models are variants of existing methods; main novelties are heuristics and combination strategy.  
- Weakness: missing ablation studies comparing CBOW variant and NN2 smoothing to standard CBOW/skip-gram and comparing frequency-weighted mapping to unweighted/orthogonal variants.  
- Weakness: statistical significance of reported improvements is not established (no paired bootstrap/McNemar tests reported).  
- Weakness: co-decoding lacks analysis of probability calibration and justification for exclude-O/ranking decisions; calibration study would strengthen claims.  
- Weakness: organization issues—related work (Section 4.4) interrupts flow and some evaluation details are presented prematurely.  
- Weakness: limited analysis depth — lacks per-entity/type error breakdown, sensitivity to dictionary/alignment quality, and detailed error taxonomy.  
- Question: please report all embedding hyperparameters (window, context concat vs. average, 1/dist weighting, negative sampling, subsampling, epochs) and results for standard CBOW/skip-gram.  
- Question: for NN2 smoothing, report number of prototypes, how they’re learned, window/hidden sizes, runtime cost, and regularization details.  
- Question: mapping specifics—dimensions of M_fe, sensitivity to dictionary size/frequency threshold, and comparisons to unweighted least squares and orthogonal mappings.  
- Question: provide statistical significance tests for key gains (Table 2 and co-decoding improvements).  
- Question: how are overlapping/conflicting boundaries resolved in co-decoding, and did you try union/stacked/learned meta-combination or evaluate cross-model probability calibration?  
- Additional comment: consider exponential decay for context weighting (cite related embedding work) and test if 1/dist is optimal.  
- Additional comment: reorganize paper (move related work earlier, consolidate evaluation tables) for readability.  
- Additional comment: expand analysis with breakdowns by entity type/language, error types (boundary vs. type), OOV effects, alignment/domain sensitivity, and compute/training time.  
- Overall: paper achieves strong state-of-the-art results; with ablations, significance tests, calibration study, and reorganized presentation it would be an even stronger baseline for cross-lingual NER.
