Summary
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation via two projection-based approaches and a combination strategy. (1) Annotation projection from English onto comparable corpora is strengthened by a heuristic selection metric that filters noisy instances using entity-tag frequency statistics. (2) Direct model transfer is achieved by projecting target-language word embeddings into English via a frequency-weighted linear mapping, enabling a single English NN-based NER model to decode target text. (3) Two co-decoding schemes—confidence-based (exclude-O) and rank-based—combine outputs of the two systems, exploiting their complementary precision/recall profiles. Experiments on in-house data (four languages) and CoNLL (three languages) show the combined systems outperform prior cross-lingual baselines.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An effective, simple instance selection scheme for annotation projection that yields large gains when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese and Korean).
2) A practical direct-transfer pipeline using cross-lingual embedding projection that requires no retraining per target language and only uses word embeddings as features.
3) Co-decoding schemes that consistently improve over either system alone and beat prior cross-lingual NER systems on CoNLL.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and breadth: The combined systems outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL test data, with especially notable gains on German. On in-house data, rank-based co-decoding (AP+NN2) improves the best single system by 2.9–6.2 F1 and, for Korean/German, approaches supervised models trained on 90K/125K tokens, underscoring practical utility.
- Simple, robust data selection for projection: The frequency-table-based sentence quality metric is intuitive, easy to compute, and highly effective when alignments are weak (Japanese/Korean), addressing a known pain point in projection approaches.
- Complementarity well leveraged: The characterization that AP is high-precision/low-recall while representation projection is more balanced is supported by reported P/R numbers, and the rank-based scheme is tailored to this asymmetry, leading to consistent improvements.
- Practicality and scalability: The universal English NER with embedding projection avoids target-specific retraining. The dictionary-based linear mapping with small seed (∼5–6K pairs) is realistic and extensible to new languages.
- Paper quality: The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, with sensible baselines and comparisons; the methodology is described sufficiently to be broadly reproducible.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in core techniques: The representation projection is a minor variant of Mikolov’s mapping (weighted least squares), and the NER models (CRF/MEMM/FFNN) are standard. The main novelty lies in the effective heuristics and combination strategy rather than fundamentally new modeling.
- Missing ablations on embeddings and mappings: The proposed CBOW variant (distance-weighted concatenation) and NN2 “smoothing” lack direct comparisons to standard CBOW and skip-gram; similarly, the frequency-weighted mapping is not compared to unweighted Mikolov or orthogonal Procrustes variants. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the proposed modifications.
- Significance not established: Several improvements are described as “significant,” but no statistical tests (e.g., paired bootstrap on entity-level F1 or McNemar at token level) are reported, particularly for the large gains in Table 2 and for co-decoding.
- Calibration and combination details: The exclude-O confidence co-decoding is plausible, but cross-model probability calibration is not analyzed. Rank-based priority is justified heuristically; a brief study of calibration (e.g., temperature scaling/isotonic) could strengthen the combination story.
- Organization and related work placement: Section 4.4 (related work) interrupts the flow; moving it earlier would improve readability. Some evaluation details (e.g., Table 2) are introduced before the main Evaluation section, which could be consolidated for clarity.
- Limited analysis depth: Despite solid results, the discussion is brief. Deeper error analyses by entity type/language, sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, and alignment quality would clarify when each component helps most.

Questions to Authors
- Embedding models: Please specify all hyperparameters for the CBOW variant (window size, context concatenation vs. averaging, weighting schedule 1/dist exactly, negative sampling, subsampling, epochs per language). Could you report results with standard CBOW and skip-gram to quantify the benefit of your variation?
- Smoothing layer (NN2): How many prototypes are used, how are they learned, and what is the additional runtime cost? What window size and hidden layer size are used? Any regularization/dropout?
- Mapping details: What are the exact dimensions of Mfe (e.g., 300×800)? How sensitive are results to dictionary size and frequency threshold? Please compare frequency-weighted vs. unweighted least squares, and, if possible, an orthogonal constraint baseline.
- Significance: Can you provide statistical significance tests for the key improvements (Table 2, co-decoding gains on dev/test)?
- Co-decoding: How are conflicts resolved when boundaries overlap across systems? Did you try simple union/CRF stacking or learned meta-combining? Any evidence on probability calibration quality across models?

Additional Comments
- Consider exponential decay for context weighting (e.g., as explored in embedding work such as Iacobacci, Pilehvar & Navigli, 2016) and cite accordingly; a small comparison could clarify whether 1/dist is optimal.
- Reorganize: Move Section 4.4 to the start of Section 4 and relocate preliminary evaluation tables to a unified Evaluation section. A standalone Related Work section would help situate the paper more clearly.
- Expand analysis: Include breakdowns by entity type and language, error typology (boundary vs. type errors), OOV effects, and sensitivity to alignment quality and domain mismatch. Also discuss compute/training time for each component.
- The paper already achieves state-of-the-art results; with the ablations, significance tests, and improved organization, it would set an even stronger baseline for cross-lingual NER without target supervision.
