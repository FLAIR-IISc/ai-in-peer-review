Summary
The paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language supervision via two complementary projections and their combination. (1) Annotation projection: English NER labels are projected to target sentences through alignments in comparable corpora; a simple frequency-based instance selection scheme filters noisy projections. (2) Representation projection: monolingual word embeddings are learned and a (frequency-weighted) linear mapping projects target vectors into English, enabling direct model transfer of an English-only neural NER. Two co-decoding strategies—exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based—combine outputs. Experiments on in-house multi-entity-type data and CoNLL 02/03 show strong gains, with combined systems outperforming prior state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical and effective data selection method for annotation projection that substantially improves NER when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese and Korean).  
2) A simple, scalable representation projection pipeline for direct model transfer using a frequency-weighted variant of Mikolov et al.’s linear embedding mapping; coupled with light neural NER architectures that use only word embeddings.  
3) Co-decoding schemes (especially the rank-based one) that consistently improve over either projection alone and achieve state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER on CoNLL.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and breadth: The combined systems set a new state-of-the-art on CoNLL (vs. Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) and deliver sizeable gains across four additional languages with >50 types. The rank-based fusion yields 2.9–6.2 F1 improvements on in-house data; the confidence-based fusion is best on CoNLL dev for Spanish/Dutch.
- Practicality and scalability: The approach requires no target-language annotation, uses lightweight resources (comparable corpora with alignments, small seed dictionaries), and a single English NER model suffices for new languages via linear mappings.
- Effective instance selection: The frequency-table–based quality and coverage criteria are simple yet impactful, especially under low alignment quality (Japanese/Korean), demonstrating a valuable engineering contribution for projection-based NER.
- Clear, well-structured presentation: The paper is easy to follow, English is good, and the methodological components and experimental setup are described cleanly. The co-decoding intuition (AP high precision, RP balanced) is convincing.

Weaknesses
- Missing ablations for key design choices: The representation learning uses a “variant of CBOW” with inverse-distance weighting, but there is no comparison to standard CBOW and skip-gram. Likewise, the frequency-weighted cross-lingual mapping is not contrasted with the original unweighted Mikolov mapping. Without these, the marginal benefit of the proposed modifications is unclear.
- Insufficient detail on the CBOW variant and neural models: The context weighting is described ambiguously (“concatenates … using a weight”), and important hyperparameters are missing (context window, decay form and normalization, subsampling/negative sampling, learning rates, batch size, orthogonality/regularization for M, etc.). NN1/NN2 training details and prototype-layer specifics (number of prototypes, initialization, temperature) are sparse.
- No statistical significance testing: Several claims (e.g., “significantly improve” via data selection; improvements from co-decoding) are not supported by significance tests across runs or via standard paired tests.
- Limited analysis of errors and transfer conditions: The evaluation section is short on qualitative/quantitative analysis (e.g., error types by entity class, OOV/rare words, effect of alignment noise, sensitivity to dictionary size). Understanding when rank vs. exclude-O fusion wins would strengthen the takeaways.
- Organization and related work: Related work is embedded in 4.4 rather than as a standalone section; Section 4.4 would fit better before 4.2. Table 2’s evaluation appears early; moving all evaluations to Section 6 would improve flow.

Questions to Authors
- CBOW variant: Please specify the exact architecture and objective. Do you concatenate or average context vectors? How is the inverse-distance weight applied (linear vs. exponential decay, normalization across the window)? What window size, negative samples, subsampling, learning rate, epochs, and vocabulary cutoff did you use?
- Baselines: Can you report results with standard CBOW and skip-gram for the monolingual embeddings, holding everything else constant, so the contribution of your CBOW variant is clear?
- Cross-lingual mapping: Please compare the proposed frequency-weighted least squares to the original unweighted mapping (and, if possible, to orthogonal Procrustes). Does weighting by P(x|y) consistently help? Any regularization used?
- Significance: Are the gains from data selection and co-decoding statistically significant (e.g., paired bootstrap/randomization on F1)? Over how many runs?
- Fusion behavior: Under what conditions does rank-based outperform exclude-O confidence-based fusion? Any analysis by precision/recall trade-offs or conflict types?
- Dictionary size: You cap at ~5–6K pairs. Can you provide a sensitivity curve vs. size/frequency threshold? How robust are results to domain mismatch?
- OOV and rare words: How are OOVs handled in representation projection (UNK embedding learned where?), and what is their impact on recall by entity type?

Additional Comments
- Consider comparing linear vs. exponential decay for context weighting and citing Iacobacci, Pilehvar & Navigli (2016), who use decay functions in context representations. This could clarify whether exponential decay is preferable.
- Please add a standalone Related Work section (moving 4.4) and integrate it near the start of Section 4; move Table 2 into the main evaluation section for cleaner organization.
- Expand the evaluation with error analysis (by entity type, length, capitalization, OOV) and sensitivity studies (alignment quality, dictionary size, mapping regularization).
- If feasible, include a comparison to character-aware or BiLSTM-CRF baselines to contextualize gains relative to common NER architectures, even if you avoid char/gazetteers in transfer.
