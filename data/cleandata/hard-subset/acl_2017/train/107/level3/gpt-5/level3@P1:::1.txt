Summary
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation by combining two projection-based methods: (1) annotation projection from English onto comparable corpora with a heuristic data selection mechanism; and (2) direct model transfer via representation projection, where target word embeddings are linearly mapped into the English embedding space and decoded by an English-only neural NER model. Two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) combine outputs from these systems. Experiments on four in-house languages (>50 types) and CoNLL (4 types) show that the co-decoding systems outperform prior cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), with especially large gains when alignment quality is low.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An effective empirical combination (co-decoding) of annotation projection and embedding-based model transfer that consistently improves over either component, leading to state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER performance on CoNLL test sets without target-language supervision.
2) A simple, pragmatic data selection strategy for annotation projection using per-entity frequency-derived quality scores, yielding large gains on low-alignment pairs (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese and +20.0 for Korean in Table 2).
3) A lightweight representation projection pipeline for direct NER transfer using a weighted linear mapping learned from a small seed dictionary, requiring no target-language retraining.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across settings. The combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL test sets, and on in-house data the rank-based co-decoding AP+NN2 improves by 2.9–6.2 F1 over the best single system and approaches supervised baselines (e.g., Korean and German). This breadth (multiple languages, two evaluation suites, diverse entity inventories) increases confidence in robustness.
- Practical weak supervision with minimal target resources. Both approaches avoid target-language annotation. The data demands (comparable/parallel data with word alignment; small seed dictionary/phrase table; monolingual corpora for embeddings) are realistic for many languages, making the method useful in applied settings.
- Simple, interpretable selection heuristic with large impact. The per-entity frequency-based quality scoring is easy to implement and delivers substantial improvements where projection noise is high (Table 2). The intuition—aggregating across independent noise sources (English NER and alignment)—is sound, and the metric is inexpensive to compute.
- Complementary error profiles exploited effectively. The paper documents that AP is high-precision/low-recall, while representation projection is more balanced (NN2 higher recall). The co-decoding designs reflect this (rank-based prioritizes AP, exclude-O confidence mitigates calibration issues), and the empirical selection between schemes by dev performance is reasonable.
- Methodological clarity for core components. The representation projection is described cleanly (weighted least squares with ~5–6K pairs; mapping all target words into English space; NN1/NN2 architectures), and the AP pipeline is standard with a clearly defined projection and tagging format.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty; contributions are incremental. Annotation projection and embedding mapping are well known; the data selection metric (average of empirical tag frequencies per entity) and the co-decoding heuristics (exclude-O confidence, AP-first rank-based) are reasonable but not conceptually new. The paper’s value is largely in engineering and evaluation rather than methodological innovation.
- Threshold selection and reproducibility gaps for data selection. Table 2 reports best thresholds (q, n) but does not specify how they were chosen (development data? cross-validation? held-out slice of projection-labeled data?). Without a clear tuning protocol or guidance, reproducing the gains and applying to new language pairs is difficult.
- Co-decoding description is under-specified. “Exclude-O confidence” and “rank-based” are presented at a high level. It’s unclear how token/segment-level scores are computed, whether probabilities are calibrated across models, how conflicts are resolved with equal boundaries but different types, and whether global sequence consistency is enforced. This hinders reproducibility and assessment of generality beyond two systems.
- Many variants, but limited ablations and guidance. The paper explores CRF/MEMM/NN1/NN2, two co-decoding schemes, and multiple language datasets, yet key design choices are not thoroughly ablated: (a) effect of dictionary size/quality and weighting in Eq. (7); (b) whether orthogonality constraints or normalization improve mapping; (c) how NN1 vs. NN2 trade-offs vary by language/domain; (d) sensitivity of co-decoding to calibration. Readers lack principled guidance for new deployments.
- In-house dataset details are sparse. The paper does not describe the domains, genre mix, or label inventory beyond “>50 types,” nor whether train/test are news, web, or mixed. This makes it hard to interpret generalization, domain shift effects, and why precision/recall behave differently across languages. Also, statistical significance of improvements is not reported.

Questions to Authors
- How exactly were the data selection thresholds (q, n) chosen per language? Was there a target-language dev set, or were thresholds tuned on English-aligned statistics only? Please provide a tuning protocol and default recommendations for new language pairs.
- How are confidence scores computed and calibrated across AP and NN models? Did you try temperature scaling, Platt scaling, or CRF/MEMM normalization tricks to improve comparability?
- In the rank-based scheme, how are conflicts resolved when spans match but types differ? Is there any tie-breaking by confidence, or a global decoding step to ensure BIO/IOB validity?
- For representation projection, did you try orthogonal (Procrustes) mapping, mean-centering, or unit normalization? How sensitive are results to the 5–6K dictionary size and the weighting wi in Eq. (7)?
- What are the domains and label inventories of the in-house datasets, and how closely do they match the English training data? Any domain adaptation effects observed?
- Did you attempt combining more than two systems (e.g., AP + NN1 + NN2), or ensembling multiple AP models (different aligners or selection thresholds)? If not, why?
- What resources produce the alignments/phrase tables (bitext vs. comparable corpora)? How feasible is this for truly low-resource pairs without good aligners?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. While the methodological novelty is modest, the paper presents a well-executed, practical recipe that yields strong cross-lingual NER results with no target annotations, with clear empirical gains from a simple data selection heuristic and judicious co-decoding. Clarifying tuning protocols and co-decoding details would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact and reproducibility.
