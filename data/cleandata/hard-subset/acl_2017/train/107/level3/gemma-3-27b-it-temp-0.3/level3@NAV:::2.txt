## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

This paper presents a comprehensive approach to cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme and representation projection using word embeddings. The authors explore several decoding strategies to integrate the outputs of these two approaches, achieving state-of-the-art results on both in-house and CoNLL datasets. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The experimental setup is thorough, and the results are promising.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the effective combination of annotation projection and representation projection, coupled with the proposed data selection heuristic for improving the quality of projected annotations. The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, demonstrate a clear benefit in integrating the strengths of each projection method. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the detailed exploration of different neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) for representation projection and their impact on performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance on the evaluated datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The improvements over existing cross-lingual NER approaches are significant, particularly for languages with limited resources.
2. **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors address multiple aspects of cross-lingual NER, from data selection and representation learning to model combination, providing a holistic solution.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The structure is logical, and the experiments are clearly described.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on both in-house and publicly available datasets, providing a robust assessment of its generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **CBOW Variation Justification:** The motivation for proposing a specific variation of the CBOW model is not sufficiently explained. A clearer rationale for the changes and a comparison to the original CBOW and skip-gram models are needed to assess the value of this modification.
2. **Statistical Significance:** The paper lacks statistical significance tests to support the claims of improvement. Reporting p-values for the performance differences would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Organization of Section 4:** The suggestion to integrate Section 4.4 at the beginning of Section 4.2 and move Table 2’s evaluation to the evaluation section is valid and would improve the flow of the paper.
4. **Limited Analysis:** While the results are strong, the discussion and analysis of the results are somewhat limited. A deeper dive into the types of errors made by each system and the reasons for the success of the co-decoding schemes would be beneficial.
5. **Missing Related Work Section:** Embedding a related work section within the introduction is not ideal. A dedicated "Related Work" section would provide a more comprehensive overview of the field.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind the CBOW variation and present a comparison of its performance against the original CBOW and skip-gram models?
2. Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the claims of improvement over baseline methods and different configurations.
3. Can you elaborate on the types of errors made by each system (annotation projection, representation projection) and how the co-decoding schemes mitigate these errors?
