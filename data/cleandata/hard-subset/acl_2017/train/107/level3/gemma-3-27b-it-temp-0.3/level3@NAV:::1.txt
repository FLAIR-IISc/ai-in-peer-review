## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using two primary approaches: annotation projection and representation projection, combined with co-decoding schemes. The core idea of leveraging comparable corpora for annotation projection and projecting word embeddings is not novel; however, the paper attempts to improve upon existing methods through data selection and combination strategies.

The primary contribution, as I see it, lies in the empirical investigation of data selection for annotation projection. While the quality score formula is relatively straightforward, the experiments demonstrate that careful selection of projection data can significantly improve performance, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. A secondary contribution is the exploration of combining the outputs of the two projection-based systems, although the description of the co-decoding schemes remains somewhat unclear.

**Strengths:**

*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation across multiple languages (both in-house and CoNLL datasets). This provides some evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   **Demonstrated Improvement:** The results consistently show improvements over baseline systems, and in some cases, approach the performance of supervised systems, highlighting the potential of weakly supervised approaches.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – cross-lingual NER with limited resources – is a significant challenge in NLP, and the proposed methods offer a potential solution.
*   **Clear Presentation of Background:** The introduction provides a good overview of existing approaches to cross-lingual NER.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The fundamental approaches (annotation projection and representation projection) are well-established. The paper presents incremental improvements rather than a fundamentally new technique.
*   **Threshold Determination:** The method for determining the thresholds (q and n) for data selection (Table 2) is unclear. It is not stated whether these thresholds were tuned on a development set, which significantly impacts reproducibility. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the generalizability of the data selection scheme.
*   **Limited Guidance for New Datasets:** While data selection is shown to be important, the paper lacks guidance on how to tune the parameters for new datasets or language pairs. This limits the practical applicability of the approach.
*   **Unclear Co-Decoding Description:** The description of the co-decoding schemes is vague and difficult to follow. More detail is needed to understand how the outputs of the two systems are combined. It’s also unclear why more than two systems weren’t considered for combination.
*   **Incomplete Dataset Description:** More information is needed about the content and characteristics of the "in-house" dataset. Knowing the domain and type of text would help assess the generalizability of the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How were the thresholds (q and n) for data selection determined? Were they tuned on a development set? If so, please provide details.
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the co-decoding schemes, including pseudocode or a more precise description of the algorithm?
3.  Please provide more information about the "in-house" dataset, including its domain, size, and characteristics.
4.  Did you experiment with combining the outputs of more than two weakly supervised systems? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods.
*   Consider including an ablation study to assess the contribution of each component of the proposed approach.
