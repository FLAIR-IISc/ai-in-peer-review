Paper proposes two transfer methods for cross-lingual NER: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection using frequency-weighted cross-lingual embeddings.  
Introduces two co-decoding schemes (probability-based and ranking-based) to combine annotation and representation projection outputs.  
Claims combined system outperforms state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER on multiple datasets.  
Contribution: novel heuristic data selection that improves noisy projection-labeled data, especially for low-alignment languages.  
Contribution: frequency-weighted embedding mapping as an enhancement of Mikolov’s cross-lingual mapping for better direct model transfer.  
Contribution: co-decoding strategies that effectively leverage strengths of both projection approaches.  
Strength: innovative combination of methods yielding consistent improvements across languages and datasets.  
Strength: comprehensive evaluation on both in-house and public datasets showing robust gains.  
Strength: practical relevance by removing need for human-annotated target-language data, increasing scalability.  
Strength: clear organization and explanations with detailed experimental results.  
Weakness: no comparison to standard CBOW, making relative improvements unclear.  
Weakness: insufficient analysis of frequency-weighted projection differences from Mikolov’s method.  
Weakness: evaluation section is brief and lacks deeper error analysis and component-wise performance discussion.  
Weakness: no statistical significance tests reported to validate results.  
Weakness: related work placement and table organization are suboptimal (Section 4.4 and Table 2 placement).  
Question: provide quantitative comparison and ablation isolating impact of frequency-weighted projection vs Mikolov’s method.  
Question: explain omission of CBOW results and include them to contextualize embedding-variant improvements.  
Question: whether exponential decay for embeddings was considered and how it affects performance.  
Recommendation: paper is a strong contribution but needs minor revisions (statistical tests and deeper analyses) before final acceptance.
