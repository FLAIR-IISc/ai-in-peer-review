### Summary of the paper
This paper tackles the problem of building named entity recognition (NER) systems for a target language without any manually annotated data, by leveraging resources from a well-annotated source language (English). The authors propose and evaluate two distinct approaches. The first is an annotation projection (AP) method, which uses comparable corpora and word alignments to project NER labels from English to the target language. The key contribution here is a novel heuristic data selection scheme that filters the noisy, automatically-labeled target data based on a quality score derived from entity-label consistency across the corpus. The second approach is based on representation projection (RP), where monolingual word embeddings are trained for both languages, and a linear mapping is learned to project the target language's embedding space into the source language's space. This allows a single NER model trained on source language embeddings to be directly applied to the projected target language text. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the AP and RP systems, exploiting their complementary strengths (high precision for AP, better recall for RP). The combined system is shown to achieve state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2002/2003 datasets, outperforming previous cross-lingual NER methods.

### Main Contributions
From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection.** The idea of scoring projected sentences based on the global frequency of entity-label pairs is intuitive and shown to be highly effective, especially in scenarios with low-quality word alignments. This simple method yields significant performance gains (+20 F1 for Korean) and is a valuable contribution for making annotation projection more robust.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical study combining two orthogonal cross-lingual transfer methods for NER.** The paper presents a strong case for combining annotation projection and representation projection. The analysis of the different precision/recall profiles of the two systems is insightful and provides a solid justification for the proposed co-decoding schemes. This combination and analysis, leading to state-of-the-art results, is a significant contribution.
3.  **A strong benchmark for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.** By thoroughly evaluating two distinct projection paradigms on both fine-grained in-house data and standard public benchmarks, and demonstrating superior performance over previous work, this paper provides a new and strong baseline for future research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Evaluation:** The paper's claims are backed by extensive experiments on seven different target languages across two very different types of datasets (fine-grained in-house and standard CoNLL). The fact that the proposed combined system outperforms strong, contemporary baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL benchmark is a major strength and clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
2.  **Practical and Intuitive Methods:** The proposed techniques, especially the data selection heuristic for AP, are simple to understand and implement, yet highly effective. The paper shows that significant gains can be achieved through clever data filtering, which is a very practical result for researchers and practitioners working on low-resource languages. The co-decoding schemes are also well-motivated by empirical observations of the component systems' behaviors.
3.  **Complementary Approach to the Problem:** The paper cleverly explores two different and complementary views of the cross-lingual transfer problem: projecting labels (AP) vs. projecting representations (RP). Investigating both, analyzing their trade-offs, and then combining them for an improved result is a methodologically sound and compelling narrative.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and explaining their methods and results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Core Techniques:** While the application and combination are well-executed, the core ideas of annotation projection and representation projection (cross-lingual embeddings) are not new. The main novelty lies in the data filtering heuristic for AP and the co-decoding combination. The paper could be strengthened by acknowledging this more directly and focusing the narrative more on the novel refinements and the powerful synergy achieved by their combination, rather than presenting them as two entirely new approaches.
2.  **Ambiguity Regarding Hyperparameter Tuning:** The data selection scheme relies on thresholds (`q`, `n`), and the final system's performance depends on choosing the best representation projection model (NN1 vs. NN2) and the best co-decoding scheme. The authors state they use the CoNLL development set for this tuning. However, in a true zero-shot scenario for a new language, such a development set would not exist. The paper does not discuss how these crucial parameters would be set in a real-world, low-resource setting. It is also not specified how tuning was performed for the in-house datasets, which makes it harder to assess the true "no human annotation" claim.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Analysis for RP Models:** The neural network architectures for representation projection (NN1 and NN2) show inconsistent performance: NN2 is better for the in-house data, while NN1 is superior for CoNLL. The authors note this but provide no analysis or strong intuition as to why this might be the case. The "smoothing layer" in NN2 is mentioned but not deeply explained, leaving the reader to wonder about its effect on fine-grained vs. coarse-grained entity types or on different languages. This lack of analysis weakens the conclusions that can be drawn about the representation projection approach.
4.  **Unclear Handling of Shared Vocabulary / OOV in RP:** In the representation projection approach, it is unclear how words that exist in both source and target vocabularies (e.g., named entities like "Google") are handled. Are their target language embeddings projected via the learned mapping, or are their source language embeddings used directly? This is a crucial detail, as named entities are often the most important tokens for NER and frequently appear untranslated. Clarification on the handling of these cases is needed to fully understand the model's behavior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How were the data selection thresholds `q` and `n` for the annotation projection method chosen for the in-house datasets (Tables 2 & 3)? Was a target-language development set used, and if so, how large was it? If not, could you please elaborate on any heuristics used to set these values in the absence of labeled target data?
2.  In the representation projection approach, for words shared between the source and target vocabulary (e.g., proper nouns, loanwords), does the system use the projected target embedding or the original source embedding? Could you clarify the logic for handling such tokens, as they are central to the NER task?
3.  Could you offer more intuition or analysis regarding the inconsistent performance of the NN1 and NN2 architectures across the different datasets? For instance, does the smoothing layer in NN2 perhaps hurt performance on the coarse-grained CoNLL task by collapsing distinctions that are otherwise useful?
