### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any labeled data. The authors propose and evaluate two main approaches. The first, an Annotation Projection (AP) approach, projects NER tags from a source language (English) onto a target language using aligned comparable corpora. The second, a Representation Projection (RP) approach, learns a linear mapping from a target language's word embedding space to the source language's space, allowing a source-language NER model to be applied directly to the projected target-language representations. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these complementary systems.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection.** The paper introduces a simple yet highly effective method to filter noisy data generated by annotation projection. This method scores projected sentences based on entity-tag frequency statistics and prunes low-quality examples. This directly tackles a key weakness of annotation projection and demonstrates substantial empirical gains, particularly when alignment quality is low.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of combining complementary projection methods.** The authors show that annotation projection and representation projection have different characteristics (e.g., high precision vs. balanced precision/recall). They design two simple co-decoding schemes that leverage these differences, leading to a combined system that consistently outperforms either individual component and achieves state-of-the-art results on standard CoNLL benchmarks.
3.  **A thorough evaluation across multiple languages and domains.** The proposed methods are validated on seven target languages using two distinct datasets: an in-house dataset with over 50 fine-grained entity types and the standard CoNLL dataset with 4 coarse types. This comprehensive evaluation strengthens the paper's claims of generalizability.

### Strengths

1.  **Practical and Highly Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The core novelty of the AP approach lies in the data selection scheme. This method is intuitive, simple to implement, and its impact is significant. The results in Table 2 are compelling: for Japanese and Korean, where alignment accuracy is low, the F1 score improves by +13.9 and +20.0 points, respectively. This demonstrates that the proposed filtering method is a valuable and practical technique for making annotation projection a much more robust approach for cross-lingual transfer.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The paper presents a very thorough empirical study. The evaluation on seven languages across two different NER tasks (fine-grained and coarse-grained) shows the robustness of the proposed methods. Critically, the comparison in Table 5 shows that the proposed combined system outperforms two recent state-of-the-art systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL test sets. This provides strong evidence that the work advances the state of the art in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.
3.  **Insightful Combination of Complementary Systems:** The authors provide a clear analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of their two base systems, noting that the AP system tends to have high precision while the RP system has more balanced performance. This observation directly motivates the design of the co-decoding schemes. The rank-based co-decoding method, in particular, is a simple but clever way to leverage the high-precision output of one system to anchor the combined prediction, while using the second system to improve recall. The consistent improvements from co-decoding across all experiments validate this design.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Representation Projection Method:** The paper presents representation projection as a "new approach for direct NER model transfer". However, the method itself—learning a linear map between pre-trained monolingual embeddings using a bilingual dictionary—is very similar to the approach of Mikolov et al. (2013b). The application of this technique for cross-lingual model transfer has also been explored for other NLP tasks like parsing and POS tagging, which the authors cite. While its application to NER is valuable and it serves as a strong component in the final system, the methodological contribution in this part of the paper is limited. The primary contribution is more empirical than novel.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Data Selection Hyperparameters:** The data selection scheme relies on two key hyperparameters: the quality score threshold `q` and the entity number threshold `n`. The paper reports results for the "best" setting of `(q, n)` without providing any details on how these were tuned or how sensitive the performance is to their values. An ablation study or a plot showing the trade-off between F1 score and the amount of training data retained as these thresholds are varied would significantly strengthen this contribution. Without this analysis, it is difficult for a reader to understand the behavior of the method or how to apply it in a new setting.
3.  **Missing Experimental Details for CoNLL Annotation Projection:** For the in-house datasets, the paper provides details on alignment accuracy, which helps explain why the data selection scheme is so effective for Japanese and Korean. However, for the CoNLL experiments on Spanish and Dutch, key details are omitted. The paper does not specify which comparable corpora or alignment models were used to generate the projected data. This information is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the results, such as why confidence-based co-decoding outperformed the rank-based scheme on the CoNLL data, which was the opposite of the trend on the in-house data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the tuning process for the data selection hyperparameters `(q, n)`? Was a held-out development set used? It would be very helpful to see an analysis of how performance changes as you vary these thresholds, as this would provide better insight into the trade-off between data quality and quantity.
2.  Your results show that rank-based co-decoding works best for the in-house data, while confidence-based co-decoding is superior for the CoNLL data. Do you have a hypothesis for this difference? Could it be related to the characteristics of the datasets (e.g., 50+ entity types vs. 4), or perhaps the precision/recall profiles of the base AP and RP systems were different across the two sets of experiments?
3.  For the annotation projection on the CoNLL data for Spanish and Dutch, could you please specify the comparable corpora and alignment systems used? This would greatly improve the reproducibility of your work.
