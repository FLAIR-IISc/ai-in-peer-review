### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER), aiming to build NER systems for target languages without any annotated data. The authors propose and combine two distinct projection-based approaches. The first, an **Annotation Projection (AP)** method, projects NER labels from a high-resource source language (English) to a target language using aligned comparable corpora. Its key innovation is a heuristic data selection scheme that filters the noisy projected data based on the consistency of entity-label pairings across the corpus, proving highly effective for languages with lower-quality alignments. The second approach, **Representation Projection (RP)**, is a direct model transfer technique. It learns a linear mapping from a target language's word embedding space to the source language's space, allowing a single source-language neural NER model to be directly applied to the target language. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the outputs from the AP and RP systems, leveraging their complementary strengths. The combined system is shown to achieve state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2002/2003 datasets, outperforming previous work.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  An effective and practical heuristic for filtering noisy data in annotation projection. The method is well-motivated and the experiments demonstrate its significant impact, especially in challenging low-resource or poor-alignment scenarios. This is a valuable contribution to the annotation projection literature.
2.  A comprehensive empirical study demonstrating that combining two orthogonal cross-lingual transfer methods—data-level projection (AP) and model-level projection (RP)—leads to state-of-the-art performance. The analysis of the complementary precision/recall profiles of the two systems and the simple but effective co-decoding schemes are insightful.
3.  A strong and thoroughly evaluated baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The system's performance on standard benchmarks and across seven different languages (from two distinct datasets) makes it a significant result and a valuable point of comparison for future work.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results, outperforming two strong and contemporary baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the standard CoNLL 2002/2003 NER test sets (Table 5). The improvements are consistent across Spanish, Dutch, and German. This is a significant achievement and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed combined approach. The evaluation is extensive, covering seven languages in total, which underscores the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

2.  **Novel and Highly Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed data selection scheme for the AP approach is a standout contribution. The intuition that consistently projected entity-label pairs are more reliable is simple yet powerful. The results in Table 2 are striking, showing F1 score gains of +13.9 for Japanese and +20.0 for Korean, languages for which the paper reports lower alignment accuracy. This demonstrates that the filtering method is not just a minor tweak but a critical component for making annotation projection viable when dealing with noisy alignments, a common real-world scenario.

3.  **Insightful Combination of Complementary Methods:** The paper successfully integrates two fundamentally different cross-lingual transfer paradigms. The authors astutely observe that their AP system tends to produce high-precision, low-recall output, while the RP system is more balanced. This diagnosis directly motivates the rank-based co-decoding scheme, which leverages AP for high-confidence entities and RP to improve recall. This thoughtful combination of diverse methods is a key reason for the system's success and provides a valuable lesson for future work in cross-lingual learning.

4.  **Clarity and Methodological Soundness:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The proposed methods are explained with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is sound. By evaluating on both a private, fine-grained NER dataset and public CoNLL benchmarks, the authors demonstrate the applicability of their methods to different NER formalisms.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Representation Projection Component:** The RP method is a straightforward application of existing techniques for learning cross-lingual embedding mappings (Mikolov et al., 2013b), which had already been applied to other cross-lingual transfer tasks like parsing. While its application to NER is novel within this paper, the work does not explore or compare against more advanced cross-lingual embedding methods that were emerging at the time (e.g., joint training, adversarial methods). The contribution here is primarily empirical and integrative, rather than a new core technique for representation transfer.

2.  **Reliance on Hyperparameter Tuning on Target Dev Data:** The framework's claim of requiring "no human annotation in the target language" is slightly weakened by its reliance on parameter tuning using target-language development sets. Specifically, the data selection thresholds (`q`, `n`) for AP are tuned per language, and the choice between co-decoding strategies is made based on dev set performance. In a truly zero-shot scenario, such a development set would not be available. The paper would be stronger if it included an analysis of parameter sensitivity or proposed a heuristic for setting these thresholds without target supervision.

3.  **Lack of Deeper Error Analysis:** The paper presents strong aggregate F1 scores but misses an opportunity for a deeper analysis of the systems' behaviors. For instance, what kinds of errors are characteristic of the AP versus the RP approach? Do they fail on different entity types? Is one better at identifying entity boundaries while the other is better at classification? Such an analysis would provide more profound insights into why their combination works well and where future improvements could be focused. Additionally, the NN2 architecture's "smoothing layer" is introduced without a strong theoretical motivation, and its inconsistent performance (better on in-house data, worse on CoNLL) is not explained.

4.  **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** The strong baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) are only compared against on the CoNLL data. Their performance on the in-house dataset, with its fine-grained (50+) entity types, is not reported. Including these baselines on the in-house data would have provided a more complete picture and strengthened the claims about the proposed system's effectiveness on more complex, non-standard NER tasks.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The data selection scheme for annotation projection relies on thresholds (`q`, `n`) that were tuned on target-language development data. In a scenario where no such data is available, how would you suggest setting these parameters? Have you considered strategies for automatic or unsupervised threshold selection, for example, by analyzing the distribution of the quality scores `q(y)`?

2.  The NN2 architecture with a "smoothing layer" improves performance over NN1 on the in-house data but is detrimental on the CoNLL data. Can you offer more intuition for this behavior? Do you suspect it is related to the vast difference in the number of entity types (50+ vs. 4), or perhaps data sparsity in the fine-grained setting?

3.  The rank-based co-decoding scheme is a hard rule that gives absolute priority to the AP system's output. Have you explored "softer" combination methods? For instance, one could try to calibrate the confidence scores from the two different models (MEMM and NN) to make them more comparable, or learn a simple gating function to decide which system's output to trust for a given token or span.
