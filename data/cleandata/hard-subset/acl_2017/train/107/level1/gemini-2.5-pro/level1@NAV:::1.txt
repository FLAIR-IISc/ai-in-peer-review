**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where no human-annotated data is available for the target language. The authors propose two complementary approaches. The first, Annotation Projection (AP), projects labels from a source language NER system onto aligned sentences in a target language and introduces a novel heuristic scheme to filter this noisy data. The second, Representation Projection (RP), learns a linear mapping between word embedding spaces, allowing a source-language neural NER model to be applied directly to the target language. Finally, the paper presents co-decoding methods to combine the outputs of these two systems.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A simple yet highly effective heuristic for selecting high-quality training instances from noisy, projection-labeled data. This method substantially improves the performance of the annotation projection approach, especially in settings with poor quality corpus alignments.
2.  An effective combination of two distinct paradigms for cross-lingual transfer (data-level AP and model-level RP). The proposed co-decoding schemes leverage the complementary strengths of each system to achieve state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.

**Strengths**
1.  The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is a strong practical contribution. It directly addresses the critical issue of noise in projected labels, a major bottleneck for this class of methods. The significant F1 score improvements shown for Japanese (+13.9) and Korean (+20.0), languages with lower-quality alignments, provide compelling evidence of its effectiveness.
2.  The paper presents a comprehensive and strong empirical evaluation. The methods are tested across seven target languages using both a private, fine-grained dataset and the public CoNLL benchmarks. The proposed combined system is shown to outperform two strong, established state-of-the-art baselines, setting a new benchmark for the task.
3.  The combination of the two projection approaches is well-motivated and effective. The authors correctly identify the different precision/recall characteristics of the AP and RP systems and design a rank-based co-decoding scheme that effectively leverages these differences, consistently yielding improvements over either individual system.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The novelty of the representation projection method is limited, as it is a direct application of previously established techniques for learning cross-lingual embedding mappings (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013b). While its use for NER is valid, the contribution is more engineering than research, and a deeper analysis of why this simple linear map is sufficient for NER would strengthen the paper.
2.  The proposed data selection heuristic relies on thresholds (q, n) that require tuning. The paper does not describe a method for setting these hyperparameters in a true zero-shot setting where a target-language development set is unavailable. This somewhat undermines the "no human annotation" claim and limits the method's out-of-the-box applicability.
3.  The analysis could be deeper. For example, a qualitative error analysis comparing the outputs of the AP and RP systems would provide valuable insight into why their combination is so effective. It is also unclear why the NN2 architecture outperforms NN1 on the in-house data but underperforms on CoNLL data; a more thorough explanation would be beneficial.
