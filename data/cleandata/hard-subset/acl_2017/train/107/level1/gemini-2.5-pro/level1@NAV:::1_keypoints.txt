- Task & approach: tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with two methods—Annotation Projection (AP) with a noise-filtering heuristic, and Representation Projection (RP) using a linear embedding map—plus co-decoding to combine them.
- Contribution: introduces a simple, effective heuristic to select high-quality training instances from noisy projection-labeled data, substantially improving AP performance, especially with poor alignments.
- Contribution: combines data-level AP and model-level RP via co-decoding schemes that leverage their complementary strengths to achieve state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.
- Strength: the data selection heuristic is a strong practical contribution, yielding large F1 improvements (e.g., +13.9 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
- Strength: comprehensive empirical evaluation across seven target languages on both private and CoNLL datasets, outperforming strong baselines.
- Strength: the rank-based co-decoding is well-motivated and effective, exploiting AP and RP’s differing precision/recall trade-offs to improve results.
- Weakness: RP’s novelty is limited—it’s a direct application of known linear cross-lingual embedding mappings and lacks deeper analysis of why this works for NER.
- Weakness: the data selection heuristic depends on tunable thresholds (q, n) with no method given for setting them in true zero-shot (no dev set) scenarios, weakening the “no human annotation” claim.
- Weakness: analysis is shallow—missing qualitative error analysis comparing AP and RP, and lacking explanation for NN2 vs NN1 performance differences across datasets.
