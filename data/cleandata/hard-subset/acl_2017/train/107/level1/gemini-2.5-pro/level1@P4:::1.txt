### Summary of the paper
This paper addresses the task of weakly supervised cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where no labeled data is available for the target language. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct approaches. The first, an Annotation Projection (AP) method, projects NER labels from a source language (English) onto a target language using sentence-aligned comparable corpora. Its main novelty lies in a heuristic data selection scheme that filters the noisy projected data by assessing the consistency of entity-tag assignments across the entire corpus. The second approach, Representation Projection (RP), is a direct model transfer technique. It trains an NER model on English using only word embeddings and then applies it to a target language by learning a linear mapping from the target language's embedding space to the English space. Finally, the paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (rank-based and confidence-based) to combine the complementary outputs of the AP and RP systems. The proposed methods are evaluated on both in-house, fine-grained NER datasets and standard CoNLL datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance by outperforming previous systems.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A simple and effective heuristic for filtering noisy projected data in annotation projection.** The proposed data selection scheme, which uses the global consistency of projected labels to compute a quality score for each sentence, is shown to be highly effective at improving the performance of the resulting NER model, particularly for language pairs with low-quality word alignments. This provides a significant practical improvement to a standard cross-lingual transfer technique.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical study of two orthogonal cross-lingual NER approaches and their combination.** The paper systematically investigates an annotation projection method and a representation projection method across a wide variety of languages and two different NER task settings (fine-grained and coarse-grained). The analysis reveals the complementary nature of the two systems (e.g., high precision vs. balanced P/R) and demonstrates that a simple co-decoding scheme can effectively combine their strengths to achieve further performance gains.
3.  **State-of-the-art results on standard cross-lingual NER benchmarks.** The final combined system is shown to outperform two recent and strong cross-lingual NER systems on the CoNLL 2002/2003 test sets, establishing a new and robust baseline for the task.

### Strengths
1.  **The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a strong contribution.** The method is intuitive, well-motivated, and empirically powerful. The results presented in Table 2 are compelling, showing F1 score gains of +13.9 for Japanese and +20.0 for Korean. This demonstrates that the filtering method is a crucial component for making annotation projection work in practice, especially when dealing with the noisy alignments common in real-world comparable corpora.
2.  **The evaluation is extensive and convincing.** The experiments cover six target languages and two very different types of NER datasets (in-house fine-grained with 50+ types, and CoNLL coarse-grained with 4 types). This breadth lends strong support to the generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, by comparing against and outperforming strong baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), the paper clearly demonstrates its advance over the state of the art.
3.  **The combination of the two projection methods is well-motivated and effective.** The paper provides a clear analysis of the differing characteristics of the Annotation Projection (high precision) and Representation Projection (more balanced) systems. This observation motivates the co-decoding schemes, which are shown to consistently improve performance over the best individual system across nearly all experimental settings. This highlights the value of combining diverse, weakly supervised signals.
4.  **The paper is clearly written and well-structured.** The methods are described with sufficient detail for replication, the experimental setup is clear, and the results are presented in an easy-to-interpret manner.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The novelty of the Representation Projection (RP) approach is limited.** The RP method is a straightforward application of the cross-lingual word embedding mapping technique proposed by Mikolov et al. (2013b), with the minor addition of weights to the objective function. While its application to NER is sound, the underlying technique is not new and has been used for model transfer in other NLP tasks. The paper could be improved by more accurately framing this as an adaptation of existing work rather than a primary novel contribution.
2.  **The paper lacks a qualitative error analysis.** While quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of the models' behavior. For example, what kinds of entities or contexts are particularly difficult for each projection method? Why does the NN2 architecture outperform NN1 on the in-house data, while the reverse is true for the CoNLL data? Such an analysis would provide more valuable insights into the remaining challenges and the specific strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3.  **Key details regarding hyperparameter selection are underspecified.** The paper states that the data selection thresholds `(q, n)` were tuned to achieve the best results, but it is unclear how this tuning was performed in a weakly supervised setting without access to a labeled development set for the target language. Similarly, the process for selecting the bilingual dictionary size for the RP approach is not fully detailed, beyond a statement that larger dictionaries "might harm the performance." The sensitivity of the models to these hyperparameters is an important practical consideration that is not adequately addressed.
4.  **The resource requirements for the Annotation Projection approach limit its applicability.** The AP method, including the novel data selection scheme, relies on large, sentence-aligned comparable corpora. This is a significant prerequisite that is not available for many low-resource language pairs. The paper could better discuss this limitation and the trade-off between the two proposed approaches in terms of their data requirements.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the procedure for tuning the data selection thresholds `(q, n)` for the Annotation Projection approach? In a true weakly supervised scenario, one would not have access to a target-language annotated development set, so how were the optimal values reported in Table 2 determined?
2.  You observe that the NN2 architecture works better for your in-house data while NN1 is better for the CoNLL data. Do you have a hypothesis for this difference in performance? Could it be related to the task granularity (50+ entity types vs. 4 types), the domain of the data, or some other factor?
3.  In Section 4.2, you state that bilingual dictionaries larger than 5-6K word pairs could harm the performance of the Representation Projection method. This is a counter-intuitive finding. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case and provide any analysis you performed to reach this conclusion?
