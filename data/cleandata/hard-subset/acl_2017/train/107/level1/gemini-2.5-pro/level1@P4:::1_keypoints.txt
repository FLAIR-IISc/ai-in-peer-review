- Summary: Tackles weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with two methods — Annotation Projection (AP) with a global-consistency data selection heuristic, and Representation Projection (RP) via linear embedding mapping — plus co-decoding; reports state-of-the-art results.

Main contributions:
- Proposes a simple, effective global-consistency heuristic to filter noisy projected labels in annotation projection.
- Provides a comprehensive empirical comparison of AP and RP across multiple languages and fine/coarse-grained NER, showing their complementary behavior.
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2002/2003 by combining AP and RP with co-decoding.

Strengths:
- The data selection scheme for AP is intuitive, well-motivated, and yields large F1 gains (e.g., +13.9 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
- Evaluation is extensive and convincing, covering six target languages and two diverse NER settings and outperforming strong baselines.
- Combining AP (high precision) and RP (balanced P/R) via co-decoding is well-motivated and consistently improves performance.
- Paper is clearly written, well-structured, and reproducible.

Weaknesses:
- RP novelty is limited, essentially applying existing bilingual embedding mapping (Mikolov et al.) with minor weighting additions.
- Lacks qualitative error analysis explaining which entities/contexts each method struggles with and architecture performance differences.
- Hyperparameter selection (e.g., AP thresholds q,n and RP bilingual dictionary size) is underspecified for a truly weakly supervised setting.
- AP requires large sentence-aligned comparable corpora, limiting applicability to low-resource language pairs.

Questions to authors:
- How were AP data selection thresholds (q,n) tuned without a target-language labeled development set in a true weakly supervised scenario?
- Why does NN2 outperform NN1 on in-house fine-grained data while NN1 is better on CoNLL; is this due to type granularity, domain, or other factors?
- Why do larger bilingual dictionaries (>5–6K pairs) harm RP performance, and what analysis supports this counter-intuitive finding?
