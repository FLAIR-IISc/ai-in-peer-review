- Paper addresses zero-resource cross-lingual NER by transferring from English using annotation projection (AP) and representation projection (RP).

- AP method projects English labels via comparable corpora and word alignments and introduces a heuristic data selection filter based on entity-label consistency.

- RP method trains monolingual embeddings and learns a linear mapping to project target embeddings into English space so a single English-trained NER model can be applied.

- Two co-decoding schemes are proposed to combine AP (high precision) and RP (better recall) outputs, yielding complementary gains.

- Combined system achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2002/2003 benchmarks, outperforming prior cross-lingual NER methods.

- Main contribution: an effective, practical data selection heuristic for annotation projection that yields large F1 gains, especially with low-quality alignments.

- Main contribution: comprehensive empirical study showing that combining AP and RP is beneficial and explaining their precision/recall trade-offs.

- Main contribution: provides a strong benchmark for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER through extensive evaluation on in-house and public datasets.

- Strength: strong empirical results across seven target languages and multiple datasets, convincingly outperforming prior baselines.

- Strength: methods are simple, practical, and easy to implement, with the data-filtering heuristic particularly effective.

- Strength: methodologically sound exploration of two complementary transfer paradigms and a well-motivated combination strategy.

- Strength: paper is well-written, clearly structured, and situates the work within existing literature.

- Weakness: core techniques (annotation projection, cross-lingual embeddings) are not novel; novelty mainly in data filtering and co-decoding.

- Weakness: ambiguity on hyperparameter tuning (thresholds q, n, RP model and co-decoding choices) — unclear how to set them in true zero-shot without a dev set.

- Weakness: inconsistent performance between NN1 and NN2 RP architectures across datasets with little analysis of why this occurs.

- Weakness: unclear handling of shared vocabulary / OOV cases in RP (e.g., named entities that appear untranslated), which is critical for NER.

- Question: How were AP data selection thresholds q and n chosen for in-house datasets, and were target-language dev sets used?

- Question: For words shared across source and target (proper nouns, loanwords), does RP use projected target embeddings or original source embeddings?

- Question: Can the authors provide intuition or analysis for NN1 vs NN2 performance differences, and the effect of NN2’s smoothing layer on different entity granularities?
