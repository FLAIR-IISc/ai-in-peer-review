Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based approaches and their combination:
- Annotation projection with data selection: project English NER tags through alignments to target sentences in comparable corpora, then select higher-quality projected sentences using a reliability score built from entity-tag frequency statistics and an entity-count threshold.
- Representation projection for direct model transfer: learn linear mappings from target word embeddings into English embedding space using a small weighted bilingual lexicon, and apply a single English-only NN NER model directly to mapped target inputs (no retraining).
- Co-decoding: combine outputs via (a) an exclude-O confidence-based scheme and (b) a rank-based scheme that prioritizes high-precision annotation-projection entities and augments with non-conflicting entities from representation projection.

My view of the primary contributions (strongest first)
1) A simple, effective, and well-motivated data selection heuristic for annotation projection that materially improves robustness under noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean in Table 2).
2) A clean instantiation of zero-shot NER via cross-lingual embedding mapping coupled with an English-only NN tagger, showing competitive performance across several languages and complementarity with annotation projection.
3) Co-decoding strategies that exploit complementary error profiles to consistently outperform either component on both in-house and CoNLL settings.

Strengths
- Strong empirical coverage and consistent gains: The paper evaluates on seven languages across two settings (in-house with 50+ types; CoNLL with 4 types), and demonstrates that combination improves over both components in almost all cases. Notably, rank-based AP+NN2 yields the best F1 across 3/4 in-house languages (Table 3), with improvements of 2.9–6.2 F1 over the better component; confidence-based AP+NN1 achieves the best dev/test results on Spanish/Dutch (Table 4) and outperforms prior cross-lingual methods (Table 5, per text).
- Practicality and simplicity: Both projection techniques are conceptually straightforward, resource-lean (no target labels), and decoupled, which makes them easy to port. The representation mapping uses a small weighted lexicon (~5–6K pairs) and a linear projection, enabling fast addition of new languages. The annotation data selection uses only corpus-internal statistics.
- Complementarity characterized and exploited: The paper observes AP’s high precision/low recall versus more balanced NN2 (Table 3). The rank-based scheme is tailored to this empirical profile and yields consistent F1 gains, demonstrating thoughtful system design rather than naïve score fusion.
- Clear ablations for data selection: Table 2 provides a direct comparison with/without selection across languages with varying alignment quality; the magnitude of gains correlates with alignment noise, supporting the motivation for the heuristic.
- Competitive baselines and relevance: Comparisons against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) are appropriate for ACL-2017, and the reported improvements on CoNLL are meaningful. The work provides a strong practical baseline for zero-resource NER.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of core mechanisms: Annotation projection and bilingual embedding mapping are established; the selection heuristic (frequency-based reliability and entity-count threshold) and co-decoding rules, while effective, are relatively simple. The representation mapping follows Mikolov et al. (2013b)-style regression; no exploration of constraints (e.g., orthogonality), normalization, or hubness mitigation that might further help sequence tagging.
- Tuning and supervision boundary unclear: For data selection (q, n) and for choosing co-decoding variants (confidence vs. rank, NN1 vs. NN2), it is unclear how thresholds/choices are set without target supervision in the in-house setting. In Table 2 the “best numbers” are reported—how were these selected if no labeled dev set exists? If tuned on test, this inflates gains. Clarification is essential.
- Dependency on resources not fully specified: Both approaches require alignments/phrase tables or comparable corpora with alignment models. The paper does not fully detail the source/availability of these resources, nor report the amount/quality of the bitext/comparable data used to train aligners and phrase tables per language. Reproducibility for practitioners without in-house aligners is uncertain.
- Missing analyses that would strengthen claims: 
  - No statistical significance testing on CoNLL.
  - No per-entity-type breakdowns (especially important in the 50+ type setting to assess whether gains concentrate on frequent types).
  - Limited sensitivity studies: dictionary size (only a heuristic cap), choice of 300/800 dims, effect of orthogonality constraint, or normalization in mapping. 
  - Limited error analysis beyond precision/recall profiles.
- Underutilization of language-agnostic features: The decision to avoid character-level features is debatable; char/subword modeling can be language-agnostic and often improves NER, including across scripts. Given that models use only word embeddings, the comparison to stronger supervised baselines is limited, and the zero-shot baseline might be improved considerably with subword features.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How are the selection thresholds (q, n) chosen without target-language labels? Is there a target-language dev set (even weakly labeled) used for tuning, or are thresholds fixed across languages? Please specify the tuning protocol to rule out test-set peeking.
- For co-decoding and NN architecture choice (NN1 vs. NN2), what supervision or criteria are used to pick the combination on in-house data? On CoNLL dev this is clear; for in-house, please detail the selection procedure.
- What are the exact sources and sizes of the comparable/parallel data and the phrase tables for each language? Are these publicly available resources (e.g., OPUS, Europarl), and could you release the dictionaries used for the mapping?
- Did you try an orthogonal Procrustes mapping and/or vector length normalization for the embedding projection? If so, how did it compare to weighted least squares?
- How sensitive is performance to the bilingual dictionary size and weighting scheme (P(x|y))? Any trends beyond the “larger may harm” observation?
- Can you provide per-type F1 or at least macro/micro breakdowns for the 50+ type setting, and a brief error analysis indicating where each component excels/fails?

Overall assessment
Quality: Solid engineering with clear empirical gains; some missing methodological and tuning details need clarification.
Originality: Moderate; effective combination and selection heuristics on top of known paradigms.
Significance: High practical impact for zero-resource NER; breadth of languages is a plus.
Clarity: Generally clear; add tuning/reproducibility details.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the tuning protocol and resource availability.
