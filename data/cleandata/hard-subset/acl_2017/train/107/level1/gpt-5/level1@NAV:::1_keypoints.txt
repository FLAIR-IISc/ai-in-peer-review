- Paper proposes two projection-based cross-lingual NER approaches (annotation projection with quality-aware selection; representation projection via weighted linear embedding mapping) plus two co-decoding strategies to combine them, showing consistent gains on in-house and CoNLL datasets.
- Contribution: a simple, effective co-decoding framework that leverages complementary precision/recall profiles to improve F1 over individual methods.
- Contribution: a quality-controlled data selection heuristic for annotation projection that reduces alignment noise and yields large gains in low-alignment settings.
- Contribution: a pragmatic representation-projection transfer pipeline using weighted least squares to map embeddings and enable zero-shot use of an English NER model.
- Strength: strong empirical evidence across seven languages and two label inventories, with combined systems outperforming established baselines on CoNLL.
- Strength: intuitive, language-agnostic data selection mechanism that demonstrably mitigates noisy alignment problems.
- Strength: lightweight, modular representation-projection approach requiring only a small seed dictionary and a linear mapping for quick onboarding of new languages.
- Strength: clear characterization of complementary error profiles (high-precision AP vs. balanced NN2) that motivates principled co-decoding.
- Strength: evaluation includes both fine-grained in-house and coarse-grained CoNLL settings, indicating robustness across type sets.
- Weakness: dependence on comparable corpora, alignments, and phrase tables limits applicability to truly low-resource languages with poor or no alignment resources.
- Weakness: embedding mapping is purely linear and unconstrained; no analysis of orthogonal/Procrustes or non-linear alternatives and their effects.
- Weakness: baselines omit stronger modern supervised-transfer or multilingual-pretrained methods (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings or recent multilingual transformers), weakening state-of-the-art claims.
- Weakness: limited statistical significance testing and shallow ablation (sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, thresholds q and n, mapping choices not thoroughly analyzed).
- Weakness: in-house datasets and resources are not released, restricting reproducibility and external verification.
- Question: How sensitive are results to dictionary size/quality and to using orthogonal vs. unconstrained mapping methods?
- Question: Can co-decoding be calibrated to avoid hard preference for non-O tags and better quantify conflicts between systems?
- Question: How does performance change when phrase tables are unavailable—e.g., using induced lexicons or bilingual lexicon induction from monolingual corpora?
