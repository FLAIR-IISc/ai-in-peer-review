Summary
This paper addresses cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation via two projection-based methods and their combination. (1) Annotation projection from English to target sentences in comparable corpora, with a heuristic data selection that scores sentences by the reliability of projected entity labels (entity-level frequency-based reliability) and the number of entities per sentence. (2) Representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target to English word embedding space using a small weighted bilingual dictionary extracted from a phrase table; then directly apply an English NER neural model to the mapped target embeddings. (3) Co-decoding schemes to combine outputs: an exclude-O confidence combiner and a rank-based combiner that prioritizes annotation projection outputs and augments with non-conflicting entities from representation projection. Experiments on in-house (50+ types) and CoNLL (4 types) datasets across several languages show that data selection helps substantially when alignments are noisy, representation projection is competitive, and co-decoding improves over individual systems and prior cross-lingual baselines.

Claimed contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection that improves performance under noisy alignments.
2) A direct model transfer approach for NER via linear embedding mapping from target to English, avoiding retraining per target language.
3) Co-decoding strategies that exploit complementary precision/recall profiles of the two systems to yield consistent gains.

Strengths
- Clear empirical benefit of data selection under noisy alignment: On in-house Japanese/Korean, selecting sentences by (q, n) improves F1 by +13.9 and +20.0 over using all projected data, with alignment F1 in the 62–69 range. This supports the central premise that quality-aware selection mitigates projection noise.
- Complementary methods and effective combination: Annotation projection tends to high precision/low recall; representation projection (especially NN2 on in-house and NN1 on CoNLL) yields more balanced recall. The rank-based and exclude-O confidence co-decoders are simple yet provide consistent gains (often +2–6 F1) over the best single approach across multiple languages and settings.
- Scope and robustness across settings: Results are reported on both fine-grained in-house label sets (50+ types) and standard CoNLL 4-type benchmarks, covering Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, and Dutch. The approach improves upon two established cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test data, notably for German.
- Resource-light and modular design: The representation projection method relies only on monolingual embeddings plus a small bilingual dictionary from a phrase table and allows adding new languages without retraining the NER model. The overall pipeline is conceptually simple and likely to be practical.
- Reasonable modeling choices and analysis: Using MEMMs for large label spaces (to avoid CRF training complexity) and exploring two NN variants (with/without smoothing layer) are well-motivated. The paper reports precision/recall trade-offs and observes where each combiner is preferable.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and resource specification: Key components are insufficiently specified for replication. The paper relies on in-house comparable corpora and alignment systems, but does not detail data sources, sizes, or whether corpora are parallel; how alignment models were trained; or how phrase tables (for dictionaries) were obtained. Without released resources or detailed descriptions, it is difficult to reproduce the results.
- Possible test-set tuning and protocol concerns: For in-house data, the data selection thresholds (q, n) are “tried” and “best numbers” are reported, but there is no mention of a development split. Similarly, the choice of co-decoding scheme for in-house appears unstated. This raises concerns of tuning on test data. Clear separation of train/dev/test and reporting of how thresholds are selected is needed.
- Limited novelty of core methods: The representation projection is a straightforward application of linear cross-lingual embedding mapping (Mikolov et al., 2013b) to NER; the mapping objective is standard weighted least squares; annotation projection and frequency-based sentence selection are heuristic and intuitive; co-decoding is a simple rule-based combination. The novelty lies primarily in the specific sentence selection heuristic and the empirical combination, rather than in new modeling or theory.
- Missing comparisons and ablations: The embedding mapping is unconstrained; there is no comparison to orthogonal/Procrustes mappings (e.g., Xing et al., 2015) or to alternative bilingual embedding methods, which may impact transfer quality. The paper does not ablate the weighting in the mapping objective, dictionary size selection, or the effect of embedding dimensionality. Similarly, there is no analysis of BIO constraint handling in co-decoding or calibration of confidences.
- Claims vs. supervision assumptions: While there is no target-language annotation, the approaches require bilingual resources: aligned/comparable corpora with alignments for annotation projection, and phrase tables/parallel signal for dictionary extraction. The strength and availability of these resources vary by language; the paper should more explicitly state these requirements and discuss applicability to truly low-resource settings.
- Analysis gaps: The sentence selection heuristic likely favors frequent entities (as shown in Table 1), potentially biasing against rare/new entities; there is no analysis of this effect. Statistical significance is not reported. Some design choices (e.g., why NN2 helps on in-house but not on CoNLL) are not analyzed.

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- How were (q, n) thresholds selected for in-house experiments? Was there a held-out development set per language, or were thresholds tuned on test? Similarly, how was the co-decoding scheme chosen for in-house?
- What are the sources, sizes, and nature (parallel vs. comparable) of the English–target corpora and alignment models? Can you quantify how “comparable” they are?
- How are phrase tables obtained without target supervision? Do they rely on parallel data or an MT system? Please specify the data and process used to extract the weighted bilingual dictionaries.
- Is the mapping matrix constrained (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes)? If not, did you try such constraints, and how do they compare?
- How do you enforce BIO constraints during co-decoding when merging spans and resolving conflicts? Is there a post-processing step to ensure valid tag sequences?
- Can you provide ablations on dictionary size/thresholds, mapping weights, and embedding dimensions? Also, report sensitivity to q and n.
- Can you report per-entity-type performance and significance tests? Any analysis of errors on rare vs. frequent entities?
- Are code, trained models, and any non-proprietary resources (e.g., mapping dictionaries) planned for release?
