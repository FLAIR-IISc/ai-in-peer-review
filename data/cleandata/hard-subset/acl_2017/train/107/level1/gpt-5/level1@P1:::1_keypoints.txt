- Paper addresses cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation using annotation projection (AP), representation projection (RP), and simple co-decoding.
- AP: projects English annotations via word alignments and filters noisy projections using an entity-frequency reliability table plus a per-sentence entity-count threshold.
- RP: learns a linear mapping from target to English embeddings using a small weighted bilingual dictionary and applies a single English NN NER model to projected embeddings.
- Co-decoding: two heuristics (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) combine AP (high-precision/low-recall) and RP (more balanced) outputs.
- Main contribution 1: practical, effective data-selection heuristic for AP that markedly improves performance with noisy alignments.
- Main contribution 2: simple RP transfer that avoids per-language retraining by mapping embeddings into English and reusing one NER model.
- Main contribution 3: co-decoding heuristics that consistently improve over individual components and beat prior cross-lingual baselines on CoNLL.
- Strength: strong empirical gains and broad evaluation across multiple in-house and CoNLL languages, approaching supervised performance for some languages.
- Strength: interpretable, domain-agnostic AP data-selection that yields large gains for low-quality alignments (e.g., Japanese, Korean).
- Strength: RP is universal and scalable, requiring only monolingual embeddings and a modest bilingual lexicon, no language-specific features.
- Strength: complementary behaviors of AP and RP are effectively exploited by simple co-decoding rules.
- Strength: resource-light NER models (no gazetteers/character features) clarify projection method contributions.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—corpora nature, alignment pipelines, and in-house datasets are not sufficiently detailed or public.
- Weakness: AP reliability table is computed from noisy projections, risking feedback/errors; limited analysis of failure modes and threshold sensitivity.
- Weakness: RP supervision and mapping choices under-specified—dictionary sourcing, size/quality effects, and lack of comparisons (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) are missing.
- Weakness: limited error analysis and per-type results, especially for the fine-grained in-house schema.
- Weakness: co-decoding is heuristic with no calibration, learning-to-rank, or BIO-consistency analysis beyond brief conflict rules.
- Questions for authors: details on corpora, alignment method, parallel vs. comparable data, and amount of parallel supervision.
- Questions for authors: how phrase tables/dictionaries were obtained, sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, and ablation results for varying dictionary sizes.
- Questions for authors: whether orthogonal mapping or CSLS-style adjustments were tried and how they compare to weighted least squares.
- Questions for authors: how q and n thresholds were tuned, their stability across languages/domains, and performance vs. threshold curves.
- Questions for authors: how BIO validity and span conflicts are handled after co-decoding and statistics on corrected/introduced invalid spans.
- Overall verdict: practical and effective approach with solid results; would be stronger with reproducibility details, ablations, and deeper analysis—reviewer leans positive.
