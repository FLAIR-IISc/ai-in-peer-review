Summary and contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation via two complementary projection strategies and simple co-decoding:
- Annotation projection (AP) from English onto target sentences using word alignments on comparable/parallel data, with a heuristic data-selection scheme that filters noisy projected sentences using an entity-frequency reliability table and a per-sentence entity count threshold.
- Representation projection (RP): learn a linear mapping from target word embeddings to English embeddings using a small weighted bilingual dictionary; apply a single English NN NER model directly to projected target embeddings.
- Two co-decoding heuristics (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine AP (high precision/low recall) and RP (more balanced) outputs.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order):
1) A practical, effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection that substantially improves over using all projected data when alignments are noisy.
2) A simple representation-projection transfer approach that avoids retraining per target language by mapping embeddings into English and applying a single NN NER model.
3) Co-decoding heuristics that consistently yield gains over each component across multiple languages and datasets, outperforming prior cross-lingual NER baselines on CoNLL.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains and breadth of evaluation: The paper reports improvements across four in-house languages (with >50 types) and three CoNLL languages, including significant gains from co-decoding (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev). The combined systems outperform Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on CoNLL test and approach supervised performance for some languages—evidence the methods are robust and practically useful.
- Effective and interpretable data selection for AP: The frequency-based reliability table and sentence-level (quality, entity-count) thresholds are simple, domain-agnostic, and demonstrably valuable, especially with low-quality alignments (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean). This addresses a critical weakness in projection approaches (noise sensitivity) and is a clear, self-contained contribution.
- Universality and scalability of RP: Mapping all target languages into English and reusing one English NER model is elegant and operationally attractive. The approach requires only monolingual embeddings plus a modest bilingual lexicon, and avoids language-specific features or retraining, which is valuable for new or low-resource languages.
- Complementarity leveraged by co-decoding: The observation that AP is high-precision/low-recall while RP is balanced is exploited with simple, effective co-decoding rules. The rank-based variant aligns with this empirical behavior and consistently yields the best combined performance on in-house data.
- Sensible model choices and resource-light features: The NER systems intentionally avoid gazetteers/character features to improve transferability, focusing on embedding-based features. This choice clarifies the contribution of projection methods without confounds from language-specific resources.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and data/alignments opacity: The paper relies on “comparable corpora with alignment information” and reports alignment F1, but does not sufficiently detail the corpora’s nature (parallel vs comparable), how sentence/word alignments are obtained, domain coverage, or availability. The in-house datasets (including >50 types) and alignment pipelines are not public, limiting reproducibility and the community’s ability to validate or extend the work.
- AP quality metric depends on the same noisy projections: The frequency table T(e, l) is computed from projected labels, which can encode systematic errors (e.g., systematic tag/type confusions) and feedback loops. While the intuition is sound and empirically effective, there is limited analysis of failure modes, sensitivity to threshold choices (q, n), and the robustness to skewed entity distributions. A breakdown by entity type/frequency or a controlled noise study would strengthen claims.
- RP supervision and mapping choices under-specified: The bilingual dictionary is extracted from phrase tables; this implicitly requires parallel or aligned data. The size/quality of the dictionary and its domain similarity may crucially impact performance, yet ablations on dictionary size/quality and comparisons to alternative mapping constraints (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) are missing. The use of weighted least squares is reasonable, but prior work suggests orthogonal mappings preserve geometry and often improve downstream performance; a comparison would be informative.
- Limited analysis of errors and types: The paper reports aggregated F1 but lacks qualitative/error analysis or per-type results, especially on the fine-grained in-house schema. Understanding which types benefit from AP vs RP (and when co-decoding helps/hurts) would provide insight and guidance for deployment.
- Co-decoding is heuristic and lacks calibration discussion: Confidence scores across AP (MEMM) and RP (NN) are not calibrated; the exclude-O and rank-based heuristics are pragmatic but ad hoc. There is no exploration of calibration, learning-to-rank, or stacking approaches that might yield further gains, nor analysis of BIO consistency constraints after merging (beyond a brief conflict rule).

Questions to authors
- Alignment/data: What are the sources, sizes, and domains of the comparable/parallel corpora? Are alignments sentence-level or word-level? How are alignments produced for non-parallel comparable data, and how much parallel supervision is used?
- Dictionary creation: How are phrase tables obtained for each target language (parallel corpora? SMT system)? How sensitive is RP performance to dictionary size/quality? Please provide ablations varying dictionary size (e.g., 1k–20k pairs) and frequency thresholds.
- Mapping constraints: Did you try orthogonal (Procrustes) constraints or CSLS-like retrieval adjustments for the mapping? If so, how do they compare to your weighted least squares?
- Thresholds and stability: How were q and n tuned? Are the chosen thresholds stable across languages or domains? Could you report development curves showing performance vs. q and n and the trade-off between quantity and quality?
- BIO validity and conflicts: After co-decoding, how do you ensure valid BIO sequences? Is there a final CRF/Viterbi pass, or purely span-level merging? Any statistics on invalid spans corrected/introduced?

Overall, this paper proposes practical and effective methods for cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation, with solid empirical results and useful insights into combining complementary projections. Addressing reproducibility, adding ablations and analysis (especially around alignment/dictionary requirements and mapping choices), and clarifying co-decoding/consistency would further strengthen the contribution. I lean positive given the demonstrated gains and practicality.
