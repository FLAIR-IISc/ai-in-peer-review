- Paper proposes two projection-based cross-lingual NER methods: annotation projection with sentence selection, and representation projection via linear mapping, plus two co-decoding schemes.
- Main empirical contributions: a simple sentence-selection heuristic improves noisy projection, representation projection without retraining works well, and co-decoding consistently boosts F1 and beats prior systems on CoNLL.
- Strength: clear, practical weakly-supervised recipe with strong empirical gains, especially large F1 improvements for noisy alignments (e.g., Japanese/Korean).
- Strength: rank-based co-decoding reliably improves over individual systems and outperforms contemporaneous cross-lingual baselines.
- Strength: representation-transfer approach is practical for rapid deployment — maps target embeddings into English and decodes with a single English model using a small weighted dictionary.
- Strength: broad evaluation across scripts, languages, tag granularities, and datasets with precision/recall analysis motivating the combination.
- Strength: sensible engineering baselines reported (alignment quality, training sizes, CRF/MEMM/NN variants) to expose trade-offs.
- Weakness: limited novelty — selection and mapping ideas echo prior distant supervision and cross-lingual embedding work and lack comparison to those alternatives.
- Weakness: no comparison to joint multilingual embeddings or orthogonal/Procrustes mappings; mapping choice (800→300 linear regression) is under-justified.
- Weakness: insufficient ablations and details on the selection heuristic — handling of inflection, casing, aliases, and frequency effects are not analyzed.
- Weakness: no analysis of dictionary size/quality, weighting, or sensitivity to noisy bilingual seeds for mapping.
- Weakness: supervised/monolingual baselines are weaker than 2016 state-of-the-art (no char/subword or BiLSTM-CRF), limiting context for absolute performance.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to other cross-lingual NER resources (e.g., Wikipedia-supervised, Polyglot-NER).
- Weakness: limited methodological clarity about the source and construction of "comparable corpora," sentence/token alignment models, and reproducibility.
- Weakness: evaluation gaps — no significance testing for small gains, unclear handling/enforcement of BIO constraints after co-decoding, and little qualitative error analysis.
- Question: what are the sources and alignment methods for the comparable corpora, and can these resources be released?
- Question: how are entity surface forms normalized and aliases handled in the entity frequency table, and how sensitive are results to this normalization?
- Question: why choose non-orthogonal 800→300 linear regression for mapping; were Procrustes, dimensionality matching, or different seed sizes tried?
- Question: does co-decoding enforce BIO constraints or repair invalid spans, and what fraction of outputs are invalid?
- Question: can the authors compare to Wikipedia-supervised NER baselines and joint multilingual embedding transfer methods?
- Overall assessment: solid, practical empirical contribution with useful design choices and consistent gains; methodological novelty is limited and several comparisons/clarity issues should be addressed — leaning toward weak accept conditional on addressing gaps.
