Summary
The paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation through two projection-based approaches and a combination scheme. First, it constructs weakly labeled target data via annotation projection on comparable corpora and proposes a quality-aware data selection heuristic. Second, it performs direct model transfer by projecting target word embeddings into the English embedding space via a weighted linear mapping and decoding with an English NN-based NER model. Finally, two co-decoding strategies (confidence-based with exclude-O, and rank-based prioritizing annotation projection) merge outputs. Experiments on in-house datasets (4 languages, 50+ types) and CoNLL (3 languages, 4 types) show consistent gains, with combined systems outperforming prior cross-lingual NER methods.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, effective co-decoding framework that exploits complementary precision/recall profiles to yield consistent F1 improvements over either projection method alone.
- A quality-controlled data selection heuristic for annotation projection that substantially mitigates alignment noise and yields large gains (e.g., +14 to +20 F1 in low-alignment settings).
- A pragmatic representation-projection transfer pipeline using weighted least squares to map embeddings, enabling zero-shot application of an English NER model to new languages.

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence across seven languages and two label inventories; combined systems consistently improve over individual projections and outperform two established cross-lingual baselines on CoNLL test sets.
- The data selection mechanism is intuitive, language-agnostic, and demonstrably impactful under noisy alignment, addressing a key pain point in projection-based NER.
- The representation projection approach is lightweight and modular (small seed dictionary, linear mapping), facilitating quick onboarding of new languages.
- Clear characterization of error profiles (high-precision AP vs. balanced NN2) informs principled co-decoding design and explains observed gains.
- Comparisons include both in-house fine-grained and CoNLL coarse-grained settings, suggesting robustness across type sets.

Weaknesses
- The approach still depends on alignment artifacts (comparable corpora with alignments, phrase tables for dictionaries), which may be unavailable or poor for truly low-resource languages, limiting applicability.
- The embedding mapping is purely linear and unconstrained; there is no analysis of alternative mappings (e.g., orthogonal/Procrustes) or non-linear projections and their impact.
- Baselines omit stronger modern supervised-transfer or multilingual pretraining methods (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings, or recent multilingual transformers), making the claim of state-of-the-art less compelling today.
- Limited statistical significance testing and ablation depth (e.g., sensitivity to dictionary size/quality, thresholds q and n, and mapping choices).
- In-house datasets and resources are not released, restricting reproducibility and community verification.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive are results to the dictionary size/quality and to using orthogonal vs. unconstrained mappings?
- Can the co-decoding schemes be calibrated to avoid the hard preference for non-O tags and quantify conflicts better?
- What is the performance when phrase tables are unavailable—e.g., with induced lexicons or bilingual lexicon induction from monolingual corpora?
