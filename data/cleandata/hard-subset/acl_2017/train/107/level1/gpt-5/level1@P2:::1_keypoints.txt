- Paper addresses zero-resource cross-lingual NER with annotation projection + data selection, representation projection via linear embedding mapping, and two co-decoding schemes.
- Annotation projection: project English NER through alignments to comparable corpora and select high-quality projected sentences using a frequency-based reliability score and entity-count threshold.
- Representation projection: learn linear mappings from target embeddings to English space using a small weighted bilingual lexicon and apply an English-only NN tagger without retraining.
- Co-decoding: combine outputs with (a) an exclude-O confidence-based scheme and (b) a rank-based scheme favoring high-precision annotation-projection entities and non-conflicting NN entities.
- Primary contribution 1: simple, effective data-selection heuristic that substantially improves robustness under noisy alignments (large F1 gains for Japanese/Korean).
- Primary contribution 2: clean zero-shot NER instantiation using cross-lingual embedding mapping with an English-only NN tagger, competitive across languages.
- Primary contribution 3: co-decoding strategies that exploit complementary error profiles to outperform individual components consistently.
- Strength: strong empirical coverage across seven languages and two settings, with combination usually improving over both components.
- Strength: practical and simple methods that are resource-lean, decoupled, and easy to port to new languages.
- Strength: clear characterization of complementary error profiles (AP high-precision/low-recall vs. NN2 balanced) and tailored rank-based fusion.
- Strength: clear ablation for data selection showing gains correlate with alignment noise.
- Strength: competitive baselines and meaningful CoNLL improvements, providing a strong practical zero-resource NER baseline.
- Weakness: limited novelty—core mechanisms (annotation projection, embedding mapping) are established and heuristics are simple.
- Weakness: unclear tuning and supervision boundary—how thresholds and co-decoding choices are set without target labels is unspecified and may risk test-set tuning.
- Weakness: resource dependence not fully specified—details on alignments/phrase tables, data sizes, and availability are missing, affecting reproducibility.
- Weakness: missing analyses—no significance testing, per-entity-type breakdowns, sensitivity studies (dict size, mapping constraints), or deeper error analysis.
- Weakness: underutilization of language-agnostic features like character/subword modeling, potentially limiting zero-shot baseline strength.
- Question: how are selection thresholds (q, n) chosen without target labels—fixed across languages or tuned on a dev set?
- Question: how are co-decoding and NN variant choices (NN1 vs NN2) selected for in-house data without supervision?
- Question: what are the exact sources, sizes, and availability of comparable/parallel data, phrase tables, and bilingual dictionaries used?
- Question: were orthogonal Procrustes mapping, vector normalization, or hubness mitigation tried and how did they compare to weighted least squares?
- Question: how sensitive is performance to bilingual dictionary size and weighting scheme—any systematic trends?
- Question: can authors provide per-type F1 or macro/micro breakdowns for the 50+ type setting and brief error analysis of component strengths/failures?
- Overall: solid engineering and practical impact with clear gains, moderate originality, but contingent acceptance recommended pending clarification of tuning and resource details.
