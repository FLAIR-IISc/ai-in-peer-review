Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation via two projection-based approaches and their combination:
1) Annotation projection from English onto aligned sentence pairs drawn from comparable corpora, with a heuristic data selection scheme that scores projected sentences by averaging entity-level tag reliability derived from corpus-wide tag frequencies.
2) Representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target-language embeddings into English embedding space using a small weighted seed dictionary, then decode with an English-only neural NER model (no retraining).
3) Two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) to combine outputs.

As I see it, the primary contribution is an empirical framework showing that (i) a simple, scalable sentence selection heuristic materially improves projected-data NER when alignments are noisy; (ii) a practical representation-projection transfer baseline works surprisingly well across several languages and label granularities; and (iii) a lightweight co-decoding policy consistently boosts F1 and outperforms prior cross-lingual NER systems on CoNLL. The methodological novelty is moderate; the impact is mainly in practical design choices and a thorough evaluation across settings.

Strengths
1) Clear, broadly applicable weakly supervised recipe with strong empirical results.
- The data selection heuristic for annotation projection yields large gains when alignment quality is low (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean). This directly addresses a known failure mode of projection (alignment noise) with a simple, implementable metric.
- The rank-based co-decoding policy consistently improves over both individual systems (up to +6.2 F1 on in-house data) and beats two strong contemporaneous cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test.

2) Practical representation transfer that avoids retraining per language.
- Mapping target embeddings into English and decoding with a single English NER model is attractive for rapid deployment to new languages. The weighted dictionary regression is simple and efficient, requiring ~5–6K translation pairs.

3) Breadth of evaluation and insightful precision/recall analysis.
- Experiments cover diverse scripts (Japanese/Korean) and Indo-European languages, two tag granularities (4 and >50 types), and both in-house and standard CoNLL benchmarks. The paper analyzes contrasting precision/recall profiles of AP vs representation projection and uses that to motivate co-decoding.

4) Sensible engineering baselines and reporting.
- The paper reports alignment quality, training sizes, and model choices (CRF/MEMM/NN architectures), and explores two NN variants, exposing the precision/recall trade-offs that drive the final combination.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior work and missing comparisons.
- Annotation projection with selection is not new; similar ideas (noise-aware sentence/entity selection, distant supervision denoising) have been explored in cross-lingual IE/NER and Wikipedia-based distant supervision (e.g., Nothman et al., 2013). The proposed frequency-based selection is intuitive but fairly heuristic; there is no comparison to alternative denoising strategies (e.g., alignment quality features, tagger confidence thresholds, agreement across multiple aligners/taggers).
- For representation projection, mapping embeddings via a linear transform using a seed dictionary follows Mikolov et al. (2013b) and related cross-lingual embedding literature; applying it to NER is reasonable, but lacks comparison to joint multilingual embeddings (e.g., BilBOWA/Gouws et al., 2015, Vulić & Moens, 2015) or more recent orthogonal Procrustes mappings. Without such baselines, it’s hard to attribute gains to the specific mapping choice.

2) Insufficient detail and ablations for the selection heuristic and mappings.
- The entity-level frequency table T(e,l) uses surface forms. How are inflection, casing, and script normalization handled? Ambiguous entities (e.g., Washington) can be truly polysemous; frequency-based “reliability” may overfit popular entities and penalize contextually correct minority senses. A controlled ablation (e.g., bucketing by entity frequency, removing high-frequency entities, or using per-entity entropy) would strengthen the claim.
- There is no analysis of dictionary size/quality or weighting on mapping performance. The unusual dimensionality mismatch (800→300) and choice of linear (non-orthogonal) regression deserve justification; Procrustes with orthogonality and CSLS-type scoring were known to help even by 2016–2017.

3) Fairness and strength of supervised/monolingual baselines.
- Supervised upper bounds use MEMM/CRF/FFNN without character/subword features or BiLSTM-CRF architectures that were state of the art by 2016. While the main thrust is cross-lingual, stronger monolingual baselines would contextualize absolute performance, especially for in-house >50-type NER.
- For CoNLL, comparisons omit other cross-lingual NER sources (e.g., Wikipedia distant supervision/Polyglot-NER) that were competitive at the time.

4) Limited methodological clarity about “comparable corpora” and alignments.
- The paper assumes sentence-level aligned comparable corpora with token-level alignments and reports alignment F1, but the source, construction process, and domain are unclear. How are sentences aligned if the data are not parallel? Which token alignment model is used (features, training data), and how reproducible is it? Since the alignment is pivotal to AP, more detail is needed.

5) Evaluation gaps: significance, BIO consistency, and error analysis.
- Improvements of 1–3 F1 (e.g., on CoNLL dev/test) are not accompanied by significance testing. Given the small margins, it’s important to report paired significance.
- Co-decoding resolves conflicts locally; how are BIO/IOB2 constraints enforced? Are invalid spans produced and then repaired?
- There is little qualitative error analysis (boundary vs type errors, language-specific issues like segmentation in Japanese/Korean, OOV handling), which would inform when each projection method fails.

Questions to authors
1) Comparable corpora and alignments: What is the source of the comparable corpora and how are sentence/token alignments obtained for non-parallel data? Please detail the alignment model, training data, domains, and whether these resources can be released.
2) Entity frequency table: How are entity surface forms normalized (casing, lemmatization, script variants)? Did you collapse aliases (e.g., U.S., USA, Estados Unidos) before counting? How sensitive is performance to this normalization?
3) Mapping choices: Why choose 800→300 linear regression (without orthogonality constraints)? Did you try orthogonal Procrustes, dimensionality matching, or alternative seed sizes? Any results on sensitivity to dictionary noise and frequency threshold?
4) BIO constraints in co-decoding: Do you run a final sequence-level decoding step to enforce BIO validity after merging spans? If not, what fraction of outputs are invalid and how are they handled?
5) Baselines: Can you compare to a Wikipedia-supervised cross-lingual NER baseline (e.g., Nothman et al., Polyglot-NER) and to a joint multilingual embedding transfer approach?

Overall assessment
The paper offers a solid, practical contribution: a simple denoising heuristic for projection, a universal representation-transfer baseline, and an effective co-decoding policy, validated across multiple languages and datasets with consistent gains over strong prior cross-lingual systems. Methodological novelty is limited and several comparisons/ablations are missing, but the empirical insights and ease of deployment are valuable. I lean toward weak accept, conditional on addressing the clarity and baseline gaps.
