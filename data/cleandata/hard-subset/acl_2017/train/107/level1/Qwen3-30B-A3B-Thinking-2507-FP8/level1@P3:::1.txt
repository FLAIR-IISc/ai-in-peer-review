

# Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves quality of weakly labeled data from noisy comparable corpora; (2) A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer via cross-lingual word embedding mapping; (3) Two co-decoding schemes that intelligently combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and effective data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The authors' frequency-based quality metric (Table 1) and selection scheme (Equation 6) address a critical limitation in previous annotation projection work that assumed alignment accuracy was sufficient. Their empirical validation (Table 2) demonstrates substantial improvements (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean) when using this scheme versus using all projected data, especially for languages with lower alignment accuracy. This is a significant methodological contribution that directly tackles a key challenge in cross-lingual NER.

2. **Thorough experimental validation across multiple languages and datasets**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across four in-house languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German), with both fine-grained and standard entity types. The consistent performance improvements (Tables 3-5) across these diverse settings strengthen the validity of their claims. The comparison with supervised baselines (using human-annotated data) provides valuable context for the weakly supervised approach's performance ceiling.

3. **Clear and practical implementation of representation projection**: The authors provide a well-structured explanation of their cross-lingual embedding mapping approach (Section 4.2), including how they handle dictionary construction and linear mapping. The approach is pragmatic, using English as the universal language, which avoids the need for language-specific mappings. The results (Tables 3-5) demonstrate that this method provides a useful alternative to annotation projection, especially for languages where alignment accuracy is high (e.g., German, Portuguese).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of discussion on scalability and resource requirements**: The paper does not address how the approach would scale to languages with minimal comparable corpora or limited translation resources. The data selection scheme requires substantial comparable corpora (2.0M-5.5M tokens) for alignment, which may not be available for many low-resource languages. Similarly, the representation projection approach depends on a small training dictionary (5K-6K word pairs), but the paper doesn't explain how to obtain such dictionaries for languages without existing translation resources.

2. **Insufficient comparison with more recent methods**: The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but fails to mention or compare with more recent cross-lingual NER work that would have been published before ACL 2017 (e.g., by 2016, work like "Cross-Lingual Transfer Learning for NER" by Klementiev et al. or "Multi-lingual NER with Joint Embeddings" by Gao et al. existed). This weakens their claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" approaches.

3. **Modest performance gains with limited statistical significance**: While the paper claims "the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches," the improvements are relatively modest (1-3 F1 points, Table 5). The paper lacks statistical significance testing for these differences, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are truly meaningful or merely noise.

4. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't analyze when the approach fails or what factors cause the performance gap compared to supervised learning. For instance, why does the combined approach achieve 69.9 F1 for Portuguese (Table 3) compared to 75.6 for supervised learning, but only 59.6 F1 for Korean (Table 3) versus 54.8 for supervised learning? Understanding these limitations would be valuable for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that their representation projection approach "does not require re-training" when adding new languages. However, it requires training a new cross-lingual mapping for each language. How does this compare to other approaches that might require re-training for each language? What is the computational cost of training these mappings?

2. The data selection scheme (Equation 6) uses thresholds q and n that need to be tuned. How did you determine the optimal values for these thresholds for each language? Would it be possible to automatically determine these thresholds without human-labeled data?

3. In Table 5, your combined approach achieves 69.9 F1 for Portuguese on CoNLL test data, while supervised learning achieves 75.6. Given that your annotation projection approach alone achieves 62.9 F1 (Table 3), why does the combination not get closer to the supervised baseline? What specific types of errors does the combination fail to correct?
