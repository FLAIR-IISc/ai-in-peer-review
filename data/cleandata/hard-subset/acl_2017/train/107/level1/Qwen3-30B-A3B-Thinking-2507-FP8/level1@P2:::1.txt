

# Review for "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality weakly labeled data from noisy comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection that maps target-language word embeddings to source language space, allowing direct application of source-language NER models. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes to combine these approaches. 

My assessment of main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean), addressing a critical limitation in prior work that didn't consider alignment quality when selecting sentence pairs.
2. The representation projection approach, which provides a clean and effective method for direct model transfer via cross-lingual embedding mapping without retraining.
3. The rank-based co-decoding scheme that intelligently combines the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (annotation projection's high precision/low recall and representation projection's balanced performance), yielding consistently better results.

## Strengths
1. **Practical and effective data selection scheme**: The paper makes a significant practical contribution with its heuristic data selection method. The frequency-based quality metric (T(e,l)) and threshold-based selection (q(y) ≥ q, n(y) ≥ n) are simple yet highly effective, particularly for low-alignment-accuracy languages. The 13.9-20.0 F1 point improvements for Japanese and Korean demonstrate the method's real-world impact, addressing a key limitation in prior annotation projection work that assumed alignment quality was sufficient.

2. **Novel application of representation projection to NER**: While cross-lingual embeddings have been used for other NLP tasks, this paper's application to NER via direct representation projection is novel and elegant. The approach avoids the need for language-specific model retraining, making it highly practical for multilingual deployment. The experimental results (e.g., 69.3 F1 for German with co-decoding) show this approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Effective co-decoding schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme is particularly strong, leveraging the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches. By prioritizing the annotation projection system's high-precision outputs and then adding non-conflicting entities from representation projection, the authors achieve 2.9-6.2 F1 point improvements over individual systems. This is a thoughtful and effective combination strategy that demonstrates deep understanding of the different systems' behaviors.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited theoretical justification for data selection**: While the heuristic works well empirically, the paper lacks theoretical grounding for why the frequency-based quality metric is effective. The intuition about "less likely both systems make consistent errors" is plausible but not rigorously justified. A more detailed analysis of the error correlation between the English NER system and alignment system would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Limited language coverage and evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to 7 languages (4 in-house, 3 CoNLL), with no systematic analysis of how the approach performs across language families or typological differences. For instance, Portuguese (with high alignment accuracy) performs well, but it's unclear how the approach would fare with languages having very different structures (e.g., agglutinative vs. isolating). The paper should address this limitation.

3. **Comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art" approaches but only compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), which are older methods. The 2016 work by Lample et al. on multilingual embeddings for NER is a more relevant comparison point, and the paper should address this gap to properly position their contribution.

4. **Lack of discussion on scalability and limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss the approach's limitations for languages with no comparable corpora to English, or how it would perform when the source language isn't English. This significantly limits the approach's perceived applicability, especially for low-resource language scenarios where English might not be the most natural source language.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but not with Lample et al. (2016) which uses multilingual embeddings for NER. How would your approach compare with this more recent work, and why is your method superior?

2. Your data selection scheme works well for low-alignment-accuracy languages, but how would it perform if the alignment accuracy were very high (e.g., 90%+)? Would the selection threshold need to be adjusted, and how would this affect the trade-off between data quantity and quality?

3. The paper assumes English as the source language throughout. How would your approach generalize to cases where English is not the most suitable source language (e.g., for closely related languages like Spanish and Portuguese)?
