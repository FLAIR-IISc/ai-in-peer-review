

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach uses annotation projection on comparable corpora with a heuristic data selection scheme to select high-quality weakly labeled data. The second approach projects word embeddings from target language to source language for direct model transfer. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The paper evaluates on both in-house and CoNLL NER datasets across multiple languages.

## Main Contributions
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (evidenced by +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean)
2. A novel representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer using cross-lingual word embeddings
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine outputs from the two projection-based approaches

## Strengths

1. **Effective data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The authors identify a critical gap in previous work (not considering alignment accuracy when selecting sentence pairs for projection) and propose a simple yet effective solution. The frequency-based quality metric and threshold-based selection strategy are well-motivated and lead to significant performance improvements (13-20 F1 points) for languages with poor alignment accuracy. The scheme's effectiveness is clearly demonstrated across multiple languages.

2. **Novel representation projection approach**: While cross-lingual embeddings have been used for other NLP tasks, this is a well-executed application to NER. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their mapping approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive experiments. The use of a linear mapping with weighted least squares (with dictionary word pairs) is a reasonable and effective solution.

3. **Well-designed co-decoding schemes**: The authors correctly observe complementary strengths between the two projection approaches (annotation projection has high precision, representation projection has more balanced precision/recall) and design co-decoding schemes accordingly. The rank-based scheme, which prioritizes annotation projection outputs, consistently outperforms individual approaches and confidence-based schemes across multiple languages.

4. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates on both in-house and CoNLL datasets across six languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch), with detailed results showing consistent improvements over baseline approaches. The comparison to supervised learning (using human-annotated data) provides valuable context for the performance of weakly supervised approaches.

5. **Clear technical presentation**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The methodology is described with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility, and the results are presented in a logical manner with appropriate tables.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to more recent work**: The paper only compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but there have been several more recent cross-lingual NER approaches published after these works. The authors should have included comparisons with these more recent state-of-the-art methods to better establish the novelty and significance of their contribution.

2. **Lack of analysis on alignment accuracy threshold**: The data selection scheme's effectiveness is demonstrated for low alignment accuracy cases, but the paper doesn't analyze how the scheme performs when alignment accuracy is already high (e.g., Portuguese with 88.0 F1 alignment accuracy). This limits the understanding of the scheme's full applicability.

3. **Inconsistent performance of neural network architectures**: The paper shows NN2 outperforms NN1 for in-house data but NN1 outperforms NN2 for CoNLL data, without sufficient explanation. This inconsistency needs more thorough analysis to understand the underlying reasons and provide guidance on architecture selection.

4. **Small absolute performance gains**: While the relative improvements are significant, the absolute performance gaps compared to supervised learning are relatively large (e.g., 12-14 F1 points for German), and the improvements over previous work are modest (0.8-3.6 F1 points). The paper should provide statistical significance analysis to confirm these gains are meaningful.

5. **Limited discussion of computational complexity**: The authors don't address the computational cost of the data selection scheme compared to using all projection-labeled data. This is important for practical deployment of the approach, especially given the large corpus sizes used (up to 5.5M tokens).

## Questions to Authors
1. How do your results compare with more recent cross-lingual NER approaches published after 2016 (e.g., those using multilingual BERT or other transfer learning techniques)?
2. Could you provide more insight into why NN2 performs better than NN1 for in-house data but worse for CoNLL data?
3. What are the computational costs (time and memory) of your data selection scheme compared to using all projection-labeled data?
