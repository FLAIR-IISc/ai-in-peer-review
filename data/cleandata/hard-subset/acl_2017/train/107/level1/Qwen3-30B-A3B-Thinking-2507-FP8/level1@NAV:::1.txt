

Summary and Contributions: This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in target languages. The primary contribution is the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves NER accuracy by filtering noisy projection-labeled data from comparable corpora (showing +20 F1 points for Korean when alignment accuracy is low). The second contribution is the representation projection approach, which transfers NER models across languages by mapping word embeddings to a source language vector space. The third contribution is the co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two approaches, with the rank-based scheme demonstrating consistent improvements.

Strengths: The data selection scheme is well-motivated and empirically validated, addressing a critical limitation in prior annotation projection work. The representation projection approach provides a simple yet effective solution for direct model transfer, avoiding the need for language-specific features. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple languages and datasets, with results showing the combined systems outperforming two state-of-the-art methods on CoNLL data. The rank-based co-decoding scheme is particularly insightful, leveraging the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (high precision/low recall vs. balanced precision/recall).

Weaknesses: The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods beyond 2012-2016, which weakens the novelty claim. The reliance on comparable corpora rather than parallel data limits the approach's applicability, as alignment accuracy is generally lower and not fully addressed. The representation projection approach's dependency on word embeddings may fail to capture semantic nuances across languages, particularly for entity types with complex cross-lingual mappings. The computational cost of the data selection scheme isn't discussed, which could be prohibitive for large-scale applications. The absolute performance remains significantly below supervised baselines, raising questions about practical utility.

The authors should clarify how their method compares to recent cross-lingual NER work (e.g., multilingual BERT approaches) and provide more details on the limitations of using comparable corpora.
