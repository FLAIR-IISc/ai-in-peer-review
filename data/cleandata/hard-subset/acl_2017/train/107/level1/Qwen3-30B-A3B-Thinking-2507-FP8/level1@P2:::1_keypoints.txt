- Proposes two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods: annotation projection with heuristic data selection and representation projection via embedding mapping, plus two co-decoding schemes.
- Main contribution: heuristic data selection for annotation projection that greatly improves performance when alignment quality is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 F1 for Korean).
- Main contribution: representation projection that maps target-language embeddings into source space to apply source NER models without retraining.
- Main contribution: rank-based co-decoding that combines annotation- and representation-projection outputs to improve overall F1.
- Strength: practical, effective frequency-based data selection (T(e,l) and thresholds) addressing alignment-quality issues in annotation projection.
- Strength: novel and elegant application of representation projection to NER, enabling multilingual deployment without language-specific retraining.
- Strength: effective co-decoding (rank-based) that leverages annotation projection’s high precision and representation projection’s balanced performance, yielding 2.9–6.2 F1 gains.
- Weakness: limited theoretical justification for the frequency-based data selection and lack of analysis of error correlations between NER and alignment systems.
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope (7 languages) with no systematic analysis across language families or typological differences.
- Weakness: comparisons miss more recent relevant baselines (e.g., Lample et al. 2016) to properly position state-of-the-art claims.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of scalability and applicability for languages lacking comparable corpora or when the source language is not English.
- Question: How does the approach compare to Lample et al. (2016) and why is it superior?
- Question: How would the data selection scheme behave with very high alignment accuracy (e.g., 90%+), and would thresholds need adjustment?
- Question: How would the approach generalize when English is not the best source language (e.g., for closely related language pairs like Spanish–Portuguese)?
