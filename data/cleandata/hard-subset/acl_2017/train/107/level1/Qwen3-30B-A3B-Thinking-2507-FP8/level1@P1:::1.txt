

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of building cross-lingual NER systems without human annotation in the target language. The authors propose two main approaches: (1) annotation projection with a novel heuristic data selection scheme for comparable corpora (not requiring parallel corpora), and (2) representation projection that maps target language word embeddings to source language space for direct model application. They also introduce two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection from comparable corpora (which is more widely available than parallel corpora) - this is the strongest contribution as it directly addresses a key limitation of previous work
2. The representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer using cross-lingual word embeddings
3. The co-decoding schemes that intelligently combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches

## Strengths

1. **Novel data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The authors address a critical limitation of previous annotation projection approaches that relied on parallel corpora with alignment information. Their heuristic scheme for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data from comparable corpora (which are more widely available) is a significant contribution. The experimental results in Table 2 clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, with substantial improvements (up to +20 F1 points) for languages with lower alignment accuracy (Japanese and Korean). This is a practical solution that could be widely adopted.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and two different NER datasets (in-house and CoNLL). The comparison of weakly supervised approaches with supervised baselines (using human-annotated data in the target language) shows that their best combined system achieves F1 scores close to supervised learning (e.g., 69.9% vs. 75.6% for Portuguese). This demonstrates the practical viability of their approach.

3. **Effective co-decoding schemes**: The rank-based co-decoding scheme, which leverages the complementary strengths of the two projection approaches (annotation projection has higher precision but lower recall, while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall), is particularly well-motivated and effective. The results in Tables 3-5 show consistent improvements (2.9-6.2 F1 points) over the best individual approach across multiple languages and datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of evidence for comparable corpus quality**: The paper claims to use "comparable corpora" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the corpora are actually comparable in content. For annotation projection to work well, the corpora must cover similar topics. Without demonstrating that the English and target language corpora are comparable in content, the results might be coincidental rather than due to the proposed method. The paper should clarify how the comparable corpora were selected and provide evidence of their comparability.

2. **Limited applicability of representation projection**: The representation projection approach requires a training dictionary of 5K-6K word pairs for each target language to learn the cross-lingual embedding mapping. For very low-resource languages, obtaining such a dictionary might be difficult or impossible, which limits the practical applicability of this approach. The paper should discuss this limitation and how it might be overcome for extremely low-resource languages.

3. **Insufficient comparison with more recent approaches**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches" from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent work that might have emerged after these papers. For a 2017 submission, it would have been valuable to compare with approaches that came out in 2015-2016, which might have improved upon the cited methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about how the comparable corpora were selected to ensure they are truly comparable in content? What metrics or evidence do you have that the English and target language corpora cover similar topics?

2. For languages with extremely limited resources, where obtaining a 5K-6K word pair dictionary might be challenging, what alternatives would you suggest for the representation projection approach?

3. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with recent approaches (post-2015) that might have addressed similar cross-lingual NER challenges, to better contextualize your contribution within the current state of the art?
