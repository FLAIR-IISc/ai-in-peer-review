**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach is based on annotation projection, which projects NER tags from a source language to a target language using comparable corpora. The authors develop a heuristic data selection scheme to select good-quality projection-labeled data from noisy data. The second approach is based on representation projection, which projects word embeddings from a target language to a source language, allowing a single NER system to be applied to multiple languages without re-training. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, which can significantly improve the accuracy of the target-language NER system when the alignment accuracy is low and the projection-labeled data are noisy.
2. A representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer, which can map words in any language into English and use a single English NER system to decode.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, achieving higher accuracy than both individual systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to cross-lingual NER, which can reduce the need for human annotation in the target language.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approaches using both in-house and open NER data sets for multiple target languages.
3. The paper presents a detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, including the impact of alignment accuracy and data selection on the performance of annotation projection.
4. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their co-decoding schemes in combining the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, achieving higher accuracy than both individual systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on the quality of the comparable corpora and the alignment accuracy between the source and target languages.
2. The representation projection approach assumes that the word embeddings in the target language can be accurately mapped to the English vector space, which may not always be the case.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approaches, which may be an important consideration for large-scale NER applications.
4. The authors do not compare their approaches to other state-of-the-art NER systems that use different architectures or techniques, such as deep learning-based approaches.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approaches to handle more complex NER tasks, such as nested entities or entities with multiple types?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the training data and parameters used for the neural network models in the representation projection approach?
3. How do the authors plan to handle out-of-vocabulary words in the target language, which may not have a corresponding word embedding in the English vector space?
4. Can the authors provide more insights on the trade-offs between the annotation projection and representation projection approaches, including their strengths and weaknesses in different scenarios?
