**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach is an annotation projection method that selects good-quality weakly labeled data from comparable corpora using a heuristic data selection scheme. The second approach is a representation projection method that projects word embeddings from a target language to a source language, allowing a source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training. The paper also presents two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches. Experimental results on in-house and open NER data sets show that the combined systems outperform two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Heuristic Data Selection Scheme**: The paper proposes a heuristic data selection scheme that effectively selects good-quality weakly labeled data from comparable corpora for annotation projection. This scheme is a significant contribution to improving the accuracy of the annotation projection approach for cross-lingual NER.
2. **Representation Projection Approach**: The paper presents a new approach for direct NER model transfer based on representation projection, which projects word embeddings from a target language to a source language. This approach allows a source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes**: The paper designs two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches in a smart way, achieving higher accuracy than both individual systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Annotation Projection**: The heuristic data selection scheme significantly improves the accuracy of the annotation projection approach, especially when the alignment accuracy is low and the projection-labeled data are noisy.
2. **Effective Representation Projection**: The representation projection approach achieves competitive results with the annotation projection approach, and the co-decoding schemes further improve the accuracy.
3. **Flexibility and Scalability**: The representation projection approach allows for easy addition of new languages without requiring separate embeddings for each language pair.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed approaches on a limited number of languages and data sets. More extensive evaluation on diverse languages and data sets is needed to demonstrate the generalizability of the approaches.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Accuracy**: The annotation projection approach still relies on the accuracy of the alignment system, which can be a limitation in certain scenarios.
3. **Limited Analysis of Co-Decoding Schemes**: The paper does not provide an in-depth analysis of the co-decoding schemes, and it is unclear how the schemes would perform in different scenarios.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the proposed approaches perform on languages with limited resources or low-quality comparable corpora?
2. Can the representation projection approach be applied to other NLP tasks, such as part-of-speech tagging or dependency parsing?
3. How do the co-decoding schemes perform when combining more than two projection-based approaches?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a well-structured and clear introduction to the problem of cross-lingual NER, and the proposed approaches are well-motivated and effective. However, more extensive evaluation and analysis are needed to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the approaches.
