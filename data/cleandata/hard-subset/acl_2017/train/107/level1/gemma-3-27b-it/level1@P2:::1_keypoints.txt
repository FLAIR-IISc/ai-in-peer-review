- Paper proposes annotation projection + representation projection and co-decoding, with the main contribution being their combination and improved results on CoNLL datasets.
- Secondary contribution: a heuristic data selection method that reduces noise in annotation projection.
- Strength: Strong empirical improvements over prior cross-lingual NER methods, supported by CoNLL and in-house results.
- Strength: Practical and relevant approach for low-resource languages that avoids costly human annotation.
- Strength: Data selection heuristic effectively mitigates projection noise, especially with lower alignment accuracy.
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and target languages with reasonable baselines.
- Weakness: Individual components (annotation projection and representation projection) lack novelty and are largely standard.
- Weakness: Performance heavily depends on sentence/alignment quality; data selection only partially mitigates this.
- Weakness: Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning and selection criteria (e.g., q, n) and unclear rationale for NN1 vs NN2 choices.
- Weakness: Co-decoding schemes (exclude-O and rank-based) are justified somewhat ad-hoc without deeper analysis.
- Question: Provide details on the tuning process and selection strategy for the data selection parameters q and n.
- Question: How sensitive is annotation projection performance to sentence-alignment quality; provide correlation analysis if possible.
- Question: Why was NN2 chosen for in-house experiments while NN1 worked better for CoNLL—deliberate or experimental artifact?
- Question: Beyond empirical precision/recall differences, is there a theoretical rationale for the rank-based co-decoding scheme’s effectiveness?
- Additional: Strengthen related-work discussion to clarify how this approach addresses prior limitations.
- Additional: Shorten or trim the general NER model background, focusing on novel contributions.
- Additional: Improve table readability (font size) in the manuscript.
