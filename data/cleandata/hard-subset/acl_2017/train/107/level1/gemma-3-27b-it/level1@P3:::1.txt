This paper presents a practical approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection and representation projection, with a focus on improving the quality of weakly labeled data and intelligently combining the outputs of the two approaches. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming existing state-of-the-art approaches on the CoNLL test data.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Heuristic Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The development of a data selection scheme for noisy comparable corpora used in annotation projection is the strongest contribution.  Addressing the critical issue of alignment accuracy – often overlooked in previous work – directly impacts the quality of weakly labeled data and substantially improves performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios (demonstrated by the significant gains for Japanese and Korean). This is a significant practical improvement over simply using all projected data.
2. **Effective Combination of Projection Methods:**  The exploration of co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, successfully leverages the complementary strengths of annotation projection (precision) and representation projection (recall).  This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the characteristics of each method and a successful strategy to build a more robust system.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a relatively thorough evaluation using both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering multiple target languages. This strengthens the validity and generalizability of the findings.  The comparisons to existing approaches further solidify the contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Relevance:** The problem addressed – cross-lingual NER without target language annotation – is highly relevant and faces significant practical challenges.  The proposed approach offers a viable solution for low-resource languages.
2. **Clear Methodology:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology behind each component, including the data selection scheme, representation projection details, and co-decoding strategies.  The inclusion of equations and diagrams aids understanding.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent improvements over baseline methods and the demonstrated performance on multiple datasets provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed techniques. The specific gains reported for Japanese and Korean, tied to the data selection scheme, are particularly convincing.
4. **Consideration of System Characteristics:**  The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each projection method and design the co-decoding schemes accordingly. This thoughtful approach to system integration is a strength.
5. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper appropriately positions the work within the context of existing research, referencing relevant approaches for both annotation projection and model transfer.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Embedding Mapping Quality:** While the representation projection approach is presented, the paper lacks detailed analysis of the quality of the learned cross-lingual embeddings.  Metrics to assess the alignment of the embedding spaces (e.g., correlation, nearest neighbor retrieval) would strengthen the evaluation. Knowing *how well* the embedding spaces are aligned would provide more insight into the success of this approach.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** Although the data selection scheme addresses alignment issues, the dependence on comparable corpora with *some* alignment information is a limitation.  The performance is fundamentally bounded by the quality of the alignment model.  A discussion of potential robustness to poor alignment, or alternative strategies for handling alignment errors, would be valuable.
3. **Co-Decoding Scheme Justification:** The justification for choosing the rank-based co-decoding scheme isn't fully compelling. While the empirical results show it performs well, a more detailed explanation of *why* it’s more effective than the confidence-based approach would be beneficial.  For example, is there a systematic bias in the confidence scores generated by each system?
4. **Feature Engineering in CRFs/MEMMs:** The paper mentions using a template of features for the CRFs/MEMMs but doesn’t provide specific details. The choice of features can significantly impact performance, and a more detailed description would enhance reproducibility.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the overall F1 scores are reported, a qualitative error analysis would provide valuable insights. What types of errors are still being made, and could the system be improved by addressing these specific error patterns?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide some quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation, nearest neighbor retrieval) to evaluate the quality of the cross-lingual embedding mappings?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the annotation projection approach to the quality of the underlying English NER system?  Would using a less accurate English NER system significantly degrade performance?
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the rank-based co-decoding scheme, and provide any analysis of the confidence scores generated by the individual systems to support its effectiveness?
4.  Could you provide a brief description of the feature templates used in the CRFs and MEMMs?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase “co-decoding” is somewhat ambiguous. Consider using a more descriptive term like “output combination” or “ensemble method.”
*   In the introduction, it might be useful to briefly mention the challenges of applying supervised NER techniques to morphologically rich languages.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of each approach.
