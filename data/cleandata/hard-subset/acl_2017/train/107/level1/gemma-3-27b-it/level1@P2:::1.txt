This paper presents two approaches to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: annotation projection with data selection, and representation projection via word embeddings. It further proposes co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The core contribution lies in the combination of these techniques and the demonstrated improvement over existing cross-lingual NER methods, particularly on the CoNLL datasets. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the data selection heuristic for the annotation projection approach, addressing the noise inherent in such methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012 and Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL datasets. This is a significant practical result, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The in-house dataset results further support the effectiveness, although the specific utility of a 50+ entity type scheme isn't fully addressed.
2. **Practicality and Relevance:** Addressing the need for NER in low-resource languages is highly relevant. The method avoids costly human annotation, making it practical for many real-world applications. The focus on combining two distinct approaches—annotation projection and representation projection—is a sensible strategy that leverages different strengths and mitigates weaknesses.
3. **Effective Data Selection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is a valuable addition. Recognizing and addressing the noise introduced during annotation projection is crucial for performance, and the proposed method demonstrably improves results when alignment accuracy is lower. This shows a good understanding of the challenges in weakly supervised learning.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are quite thorough, using both in-house and standard datasets (CoNLL) and various target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch). The comparison against baseline methods and a supervised approach provides a good context for evaluating the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, the individual components—annotation projection and representation projection—are not novel in themselves. Both have been explored extensively in prior work. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight how the specific implementation details of these components contribute to the improvement.  For instance, the details of the word embedding training and mapping are relatively standard.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection approach is inherently limited by the quality of the alignment between the source and target languages. While the data selection scheme mitigates some of the noise, poor alignment remains a significant bottleneck. The paper briefly mentions this, but a more in-depth analysis of the relationship between alignment accuracy and performance would be valuable.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning and Reporting:**  The paper mentions tuning parameters, but the details are limited.  For example, the criteria for selecting "q" and "n" in the data selection scheme aren't described in detail beyond stating they were tuned.  More specific details regarding how these values were selected would improve reproducibility and understanding. The choice of architecture NN1 vs NN2 also seems somewhat arbitrary without deeper explanation.
4. **Co-Decoding Scheme Justification:** The justification for choosing the "exclude-O" confidence-based scheme or the rank-based scheme feels somewhat ad-hoc.  A more rigorous analysis of why these schemes outperform simpler combinations would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on the parameter tuning process for the data selection scheme (q and n)? What strategies were used to find optimal values?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the annotation projection approach to the quality of the sentence alignment? Could you provide a correlation analysis between alignment accuracy and NER F1-score?
3. Why was NN2 chosen in the co-decoding schemes in the in-house experiments, while NN1 performed better in the CoNLL experiments? Was this a deliberate choice or simply a result of the experiments?
4. Beyond the observation of different precision/recall characteristics, is there a theoretical rationale behind the effectiveness of the rank-based co-decoding scheme?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, specifically highlighting the limitations of existing methods that the proposed approach addresses.
* The section on NER models (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) feels somewhat like background information and doesn't directly contribute to the novel contributions of the paper. It could be shortened.
* The font size in some tables is quite small, making them difficult to read.
