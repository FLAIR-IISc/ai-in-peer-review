This paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: annotation projection with data selection and representation projection. It further proposes two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The paper claims contributions in developing a heuristic for selecting good-quality projection data, a representation projection approach using word embeddings, and the co-decoding schemes.

The primary contribution of this work is the combination of annotation projection with a well-motivated data selection scheme. The heuristic, based on frequency analysis of projected NER tags, addresses the common issue of noisy labels inherent in annotation projection. This significantly improves performance, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. The second major contribution is a practical demonstration of cross-lingual transfer using word embedding projection, showcasing its viability as a complementary approach. Finally, the exploration of co-decoding schemes, although not revolutionary, provides a sensible way to integrate the strengths of both projection methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a practical problem:** The paper tackles the significant challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages where labeled data is scarce. Weakly supervised approaches are crucial in these scenarios.
2. **Sound methodology for data selection:** The proposed heuristic for filtering projection data is well-reasoned and demonstrably effective. Basing the selection on the frequency of projected tags is intuitive and mitigates the impact of noisy alignments.  The experimental results clearly demonstrate the improvement gained by data selection (see Table 2).
3. **Comprehensive experimentation:** The authors evaluate their approaches on both in-house and publicly available (CoNLL) datasets, across multiple target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch). This broad evaluation strengthens the findings.
4. **Comparison to baselines:** The performance comparison against existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al. 2012 and Tsai et al. 2016) provides a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods. (Table 5 shows improvement over previous state-of-the-art).
5. **Clear presentation of models:** The descriptions of the CRFs, MEMMs, and neural network architectures are clear and concise, enabling reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the combination of approaches is valuable, neither the annotation projection technique nor the representation projection using word embeddings is fundamentally novel. Both techniques are well-established in the literature.  The core novelty lies in *how* they are combined and the specific data selection heuristic.
2. **Hyperparameter tuning details:** The paper mentions tuning parameters for the data selection scheme (q and n) but lacks specific details about how these were determined for each language. Providing this information would enhance reproducibility and clarity.  More detail on the specific architecture and hyperparameters used for the neural networks (NN1 and NN2) would also be valuable.
3. **Lack of error analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the systems fail (e.g., specific entity types, languages, or alignment errors) could inform future improvements.
4. **Co-decoding scheme simplicity:** The co-decoding schemes, particularly the "exclude-O" confidence-based approach, are relatively straightforward. Exploring more sophisticated fusion techniques (e.g., using a learned weighting scheme) could potentially yield further improvements.
5. **Presentation of Results:** The multiple tables showing experimental results, while thorough, are a bit repetitive. Consolidating some of the information into fewer, more focused tables could improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on how the parameters 'q' and 'n' were tuned for the data selection scheme in each language? Was it based on a development set, and if so, what metrics were used for optimization?
2. What specific types of errors are most common in the representation projection approach? Are there particular entity types or languages where it consistently underperforms?
3. Did you explore more complex co-decoding schemes beyond the confidence and rank-based approaches? If so, could you briefly discuss the results?
4. Regarding the neural network architecture, could you provide more specifics on the layer sizes, activation functions, and training details (e.g., learning rate, optimization algorithm)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly with respect to languages with significantly different word order or morphological structures than English.
*   It would be useful to discuss the computational cost of the different approaches, especially the embedding projection.
