This paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: annotation projection with a data selection heuristic, and representation projection via word embeddings. It further proposes two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The paper demonstrates improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Heuristic Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The development of a data selection scheme that intelligently filters noisy, projection-labeled data based on frequency analysis of entity labels is the strongest contribution. This addresses a key challenge in annotation projection – the impact of alignment errors – and demonstrably improves performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios with poor alignment quality.
2. **Combined Projection Approaches via Co-Decoding:** The investigation of co-decoding schemes to integrate the strengths of both annotation and representation projection is a valuable contribution. The rank-based co-decoding scheme, giving priority to the annotation projection system, shows consistent improvement across languages.
3. **Empirical Evaluation & Baseline Establishment:** The extensive experimental evaluation across multiple languages (both in-house and publicly available datasets) provides a solid empirical foundation and establishes a new baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.  The comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods are convincing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of building NER systems for low-resource languages and motivates the need for weakly supervised approaches.
2. **Practical & Novel Data Selection Heuristic:** The data selection heuristic is simple yet effective. Basing selection on frequency of projected labels provides a robust way to mitigate the impact of noisy projections. The ablation studies demonstrate its impact.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including both in-house and CoNLL datasets, and comparisons against established baselines. The experiments explore different combinations of approaches and co-decoding schemes.
4. **Well-Structured & Readable:** The paper is well-organized, logically flowing, and clearly written. The technical details are presented in a way that is easy to understand. The inclusion of tables summarizing the results aids in quick comprehension.
5. **Relevance to the Community:** Cross-lingual NER and low-resource NLP are active areas of research, and this work offers a practical solution with demonstrated improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper demonstrates performance improvements, a deeper analysis of the types of errors made by each approach (and by the combined system) would be beneficial. Understanding *why* errors occur could point to further refinements. Specifically, a breakdown of errors relating to specific entity types might be helpful.
2. **Dependence on English as Pivot:** The reliance on English as the pivot language for representation projection introduces potential bias and limitations. Investigating the impact of different pivot languages or multi-pivot approaches could strengthen the work.
3. **Feature Engineering in MEMM:** The MEMM model's reliance on manually designed features feels somewhat dated given the success of end-to-end neural approaches. A brief justification for this choice, beyond ease of implementation, would be helpful. Is there a specific reason the neural network-based projection approach wasn’t used for the annotation projection portion as well?
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions tuning parameters, but lacks details on *how* these parameters were tuned (e.g., grid search, validation set size). Providing this information would enhance reproducibility.
5. **Dictionary Size Sensitivity:** The authors state that larger dictionaries might harm representation projection performance. While they mention choosing a dictionary size of ~5-6K word pairs, they don’t detail the experimentation process used to determine this optimal range.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, perhaps focusing on the types of entities that are most frequently misclassified by each approach?
2. Did you explore the impact of different pivot languages for the representation projection approach? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3. What criteria guided your selection of features for the MEMM model, and why did you choose this model architecture over a more modern neural network approach for annotation projection?
4. Can you elaborate on the process of hyperparameter tuning, specifically regarding the parameter selection for the data selection heuristic (q and n)?
5. Regarding dictionary size sensitivity, could you share more about the experimentation you performed to determine the optimal dictionary size for representation projection?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "smart way" in the abstract and introduction feels imprecise.
*   Figure 1 is referenced but is missing from the paper.
*   The citation style appears inconsistent (e.g., some are abbreviated, others are not).
