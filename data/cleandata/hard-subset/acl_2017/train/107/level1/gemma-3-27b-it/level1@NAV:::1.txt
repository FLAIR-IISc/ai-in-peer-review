## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

This paper presents two approaches to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER – annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme and representation projection using word embeddings – and explores co-decoding strategies to combine them. The core idea of intelligently filtering projected data and leveraging cross-lingual embeddings to transfer knowledge is promising.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the *data selection scheme for annotation projection*. While annotation projection is a well-established technique, the authors address the critical issue of noisy projections arising from imperfect alignment, proposing a frequency-based heuristic to select higher-quality, projected training data. This is well-motivated and demonstrably improves performance across multiple languages. A secondary, but valuable contribution is the *empirical comparison and combination of annotation projection with representation projection*.  The exploration of co-decoding strategies provides a practical method for benefiting from the complementary strengths of each approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Problem & Clear Motivation:** The paper tackles a significant challenge – building NER systems for low-resource languages – and clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on human annotation.
2. **Effective Data Selection:** The proposed data selection scheme is a practical and effective method for improving the quality of projected data.  The experiments demonstrate consistent gains across various target languages.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), providing a robust assessment of performance. The comparison against existing state-of-the-art methods is valuable.
4. **Well-Established Baseline:** Building on existing NER models such as CRFs and neural networks provides a solid foundation for the proposed techniques.
5. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The paper provides sufficient detail regarding model training, parameter tuning, and data preprocessing, allowing for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Approaches:** Both annotation projection and representation projection are not novel techniques in themselves. The paper’s contribution lies in the *application* and *refinement* of these techniques, rather than groundbreaking algorithmic innovation.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection approach, even with the data selection scheme, remains heavily dependent on the quality of the comparable corpora and alignment information. This limitation isn’t sufficiently discussed in terms of error analysis.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the data selection scheme relies on tuning the thresholds 'q' and 'n'. While the authors show some experimentation, a more systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by each approach and the combined system would provide valuable insights for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the data selection scheme, particularly for very large comparable corpora?
2.  How does the performance of the combined system degrade with lower-quality alignment data?  Do you have any plans to integrate alignment error estimation into the data selection process?
3.  Were any attempts made to incorporate character-level features or other language-independent features into the neural network models to further improve cross-lingual transfer?
