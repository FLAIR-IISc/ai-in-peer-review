

Summary: The paper introduces a new dataset and task for identifying products in online cybercrime marketplaces, which the authors frame as a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition. They annotate 93,924 posts from four different forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled) with product references at the token level, creating a dataset that represents a challenging fine-grained domain adaptation problem. The authors demonstrate that standard domain adaptation techniques have limited effectiveness when applied to this data, with performance dropping significantly when models trained on one forum are applied to another.

Main contributions:
1. Creation of a new dataset with token-level annotations for product references in cybercrime marketplaces across four distinct forums.
2. Formulation of the task as a context-sensitive token-level annotation problem, with careful justification for the annotation scheme.
3. Empirical demonstration that even within a narrow domain (cybercrime), different forums represent distinct fine-grained domains, making standard domain adaptation techniques ineffective.

Strengths:
1. The dataset is valuable and addresses a genuine gap in NLP research. Cybercrime marketplaces represent an important domain for security research, and the authors have already demonstrated its utility for case studies (mentioned in the abstract). The dataset size (93,924 posts) and reasonable inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' Kappa 0.58-0.77) make it a useful resource for future work.

2. The paper provides a clear and important insight into domain adaptation challenges. The authors effectively demonstrate that different cybercrime forums represent different "fine-grained domains" with distinct product inventories and stylistic conventions, which is a significant contribution to understanding the limitations of current domain adaptation techniques.

3. The annotation scheme is well-justified and practical. The authors went through six preliminary rounds of annotation, developed clear guidelines, and provide a thorough explanation for their token-level approach (avoiding the need to decide whether different mentions of a product are distinct entities). The examples in Figure 1 effectively illustrate their annotation scheme.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is not sufficiently supported. While the authors state this is the first work to tackle marketplace forums from an information extraction perspective, they don't adequately compare with related work on forum analysis (e.g., Lui and Baldwin, 2010; Kim et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2011). The paper doesn't clearly explain how their specific task differs from these prior works in terms of the NLP challenge.

2. The experimental evaluation is limited in scope. The paper focuses on simple baselines and two learning-based methods but doesn't compare with more recent approaches that might be better suited for domain adaptation (e.g., adversarial training, contextual embeddings). The authors also don't provide ablation studies to understand why certain techniques (like Brown clusters or gazetteers) provide limited improvement.

3. The paper lacks clear guidance for practitioners. While the authors show that domain adaptation is challenging, they don't provide practical recommendations for applying this to new forums. For example, they don't quantify the minimum amount of labeled data needed for effective adaptation, which would be valuable for researchers who want to apply this work.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper shows that Darkode-trained systems have lower OOV rates and better performance on OOV tokens than Hack Forums-trained systems. Can the authors provide more insight into why Darkode seems to have better product coverage? Is this due to Darkode being a larger forum or having a more standardized product vocabulary?

2. The post-level model achieves better product type F1 and post-level accuracy than the token-level model. Given that both models use the same features, can the authors explain why this approach works better for this task?

3. The paper shows that adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode is more effective than vice versa. Given that Darkode appears to be a more "general" forum, would it be possible to build a system that first trains on Darkode and then fine-tunes on other forums? If so, how much labeled data would be needed for the fine-tuning to be effective?

Overall, this is a valuable contribution that provides a useful dataset and important insights into domain adaptation challenges in a specific but important domain. With some additional experimental comparisons and clarification of the novelty claim, this paper could make a stronger contribution to the field. The authors have done good work creating a dataset and characterizing the domain adaptation problem, and this will be a useful resource for future research.
