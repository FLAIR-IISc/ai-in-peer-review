

Summary: This paper introduces a new dataset and task for identifying products being bought/sold in online cybercrime marketplaces. The authors create a dataset of 93,924 posts from four different cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, and Nulled) with token-level annotations for product mentions. They formulate a task that combines slot-filling information extraction with named entity recognition, and analyze the challenges of domain adaptation across these fine-grained domains. The main contributions are the dataset creation, the task definition, and the analysis of domain adaptation challenges.

Primary Contributions:
1. A new dataset with token-level annotations for products in cybercrime marketplace forums, with detailed statistics and inter-annotator agreement metrics.
2. A novel task definition that blends information extraction and named entity recognition for this specific domain, with well-justified evaluation metrics.
3. A thorough analysis of domain adaptation challenges within the cybercrime domain, showing that even within the same broad domain, forums exhibit significant differences requiring domain adaptation.

Strengths:
1. The dataset is valuable and addresses a clear gap in the literature, as cybercrime forum data is not well-studied in NLP despite its importance for security research. The annotation effort is substantial and well-documented.
2. The task definition is well-motivated and clearly differentiated from existing NER and information extraction tasks, with thoughtful consideration of evaluation metrics appropriate for different use cases.
3. The analysis of domain adaptation challenges is thorough and provides useful insights into the nature of domain differences in this specific context. The OOV analysis (Table 5) effectively demonstrates that product inventory coverage is a significant factor in domain adaptation performance.
4. The paper provides clear evidence that standard domain adaptation techniques have limited effectiveness for this task, which is an important contribution to the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The baseline models are relatively simple (SVM classifiers with basic features), which may not represent state-of-the-art approaches for this task. More recent neural network models might provide better baselines and could potentially yield different insights about domain adaptation.
2. The paper does not sufficiently explore why the domain adaptation methods failed beyond the OOV analysis. A more detailed linguistic or semantic analysis of the differences between forums would strengthen the claims about domain differences.
3. The claim that this is "the first to tackle forum data from an information extraction perspective" is potentially overstated, as prior work has analyzed forum data for syntax (Kaljahi et al., 2015) and discourse (Lui and Baldwin, 2010; Kim et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2011). While the specific focus on marketplace forums is novel, the paper could better acknowledge related work.
4. The domain adaptation experiments do not include more recent techniques (e.g., adversarial domain adaptation methods like Zhang et al., 2017), which were emerging in 2017. This limits the scope of the comparison and may affect the conclusions about the difficulty of the domain adaptation problem.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper notes that adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode is more effective than vice versa. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of the linguistic and semantic differences between these forums that might explain this asymmetry?
2. Given that the Darkode-trained model performs better on Hack Forums than the Hack Forums-trained model performs on Darkode, could the authors speculate about why Darkode might have more generalizable product concepts?
3. How would the results change if more recent domain adaptation techniques (e.g., adversarial methods) were applied to this task? Would they show better performance or reveal additional challenges?

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a well-annotated dataset and clearly articulating the challenges of domain adaptation within a fine-grained domain. The analysis of domain differences is particularly insightful. However, the paper would be strengthened by incorporating more recent domain adaptation methods, providing a more detailed analysis of the domain differences, and more precisely situating the work within the broader literature on forum analysis. The dataset itself is a significant contribution that will likely enable future work in this important area.
