This paper introduces a new dataset and task focused on identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime forums. The authors correctly identify the challenges of applying NLP models to this domain, particularly the significant differences between forums (fine-grained domains). The core contribution is the dataset itself, consisting of posts from four forums with token-level annotations for products. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of the difficulty of adapting models across these forums, even with standard domain adaptation techniques. Finally, the paper provides a detailed analysis of the challenges, motivating further research into this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Dataset:** The released dataset is the strongest aspect of this work. Obtaining data from cybercrime forums is difficult, and the annotation provides a resource for researchers in security, NLP, and information extraction. The dataset statistics (size, annotation effort, inter-annotator agreement) are well-presented.
2. **Well-Motivated Task:** The problem of identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces is relevant and has potential applications in security research (e.g., tracking threats, understanding market trends). The framing of the task as a hybrid of slot-filling and NER is reasonable, and the authors explain the rationale behind token-level annotation.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a reasonably comprehensive evaluation, including multiple metrics (token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, post-level accuracy) and exploring various baselines and domain adaptation techniques. The analysis of cross-forum performance, including OOV rates and the impact of Brown clusters and gazetteers, is insightful. 
4. **Clear Identification of Challenges:** The paper clearly articulates the difficulties of domain adaptation in this setting, showing that even relatively simple techniques struggle to generalize across forums. This sets the stage for future work focused on developing more effective approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** The domain adaptation techniques explored (Brown clusters, gazetteers, simple transfer learning) are fairly standard and haven’t been specifically tailored to the unique characteristics of cybercrime forum data. While demonstrating their limited effectiveness is valuable, the methodological contributions are minimal.
2. **Superficial Analysis of Domain Differences:** While the authors acknowledge the differences between forums, the analysis of *why* these differences exist is somewhat shallow. A deeper dive into the linguistic characteristics, product types, and forum cultures could provide more guidance for developing targeted adaptation strategies. The analysis in Section 5.4 hints at the importance of product coverage, but lacks depth.
3. **Evaluation Metric Choices:** The choice of multiple evaluation metrics is good, but the motivation for certain metrics (e.g., post-level accuracy) feels somewhat arbitrary. A clearer justification for prioritizing these metrics, and a more thorough discussion of their limitations, would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the model predictions. Understanding the types of errors made (e.g., misclassifying non-product terms, failing to identify specific product types) would provide valuable insights for future model development.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the forum access and data collection process? Were there any ethical considerations or challenges associated with obtaining this data?
2. The paper mentions that annotators had varying levels of security expertise. Did you observe any correlation between annotator expertise and annotation quality or disagreements?
3. You note that the post-level accuracy metric is conservative. Could you explore alternative post-level metrics that might be more sensitive to relevant information?
4. In Section 5.4, you highlight the differences in generalization performance between models trained on Darkode vs. Hack Forums. Can you elaborate on the specific characteristics of each forum that might contribute to these differences?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 1 is slightly awkward, making it difficult to quickly compare statistics across forums.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential biases in the dataset, given the nature of the data source.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections could benefit from more concrete examples.
