**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a new task of identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime forums. The authors formulate a hybrid task of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition, and annotate a dataset consisting of 93,924 posts from four different forums. They characterize the domain differences in the context of a learning-based system and find that standard techniques for semi-supervised learning and domain adaptation have limited effectiveness on this data. The authors propose several methods for fine-grained domain adaptation, including using Brown clusters, gazetteers, and token-level annotation.

**Main Contributions**

1. A new dataset of annotated posts from cybercrime marketplaces, which blends information extraction and named entity recognition.
2. A characterization of the domain differences in the context of a learning-based system, highlighting the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation.
3. An exploration of several methods for fine-grained domain adaptation, including using Brown clusters, gazetteers, and token-level annotation.

**Strengths**

1. The paper introduces a new and interesting task that combines information extraction and named entity recognition.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the domain differences and the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation.
3. The paper explores several methods for domain adaptation, providing a comprehensive evaluation of their effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the annotation process and the inter-annotator agreement.
2. The authors could provide more insights into the characteristics of the dataset and the forums, which could help to better understand the results.
3. Some of the methods proposed for domain adaptation, such as using Brown clusters and gazetteers, may not be as effective as expected, and the authors could provide more analysis on why they did not work well.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the annotation process and the inter-annotator agreement?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting when using domain-specific features in the adaptation methods?
3. Can you provide more insights into the characteristics of the dataset and the forums, and how they affect the results?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the task, the dataset, and the methods proposed for domain adaptation. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, making it easy to understand the effectiveness of each method. Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field of natural language processing and domain adaptation.
