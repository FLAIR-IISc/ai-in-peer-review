**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a new dataset of posts from cybercrime marketplaces annotated with product references. The task is a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors formulate a fine-grained domain adaptation task, where each forum constitutes its own domain with different properties. They characterize the domain differences in the context of a learning-based system, showing that supervised models see decreased accuracy when applied to new forums, and standard techniques for semi-supervised learning and domain adaptation have limited effectiveness on this data.

**Main Contributions**

1. A new dataset of 93,924 posts from four different cybercrime forums, with annotations for 1,938 posts, providing a useful testbed for fine-grained domain adaptation tasks.
2. A thorough analysis of the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation in online marketplace data, highlighting the limitations of current methods.
3. An exploration of various methods for improving domain adaptation, including Brown clusters, type-level annotation, and token-level annotation, providing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a unique and challenging task that combines information extraction and named entity recognition in a fine-grained domain adaptation setting.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the dataset and the challenges of domain adaptation, highlighting the limitations of current methods.
3. The paper explores various methods for improving domain adaptation, providing a comprehensive evaluation of each approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper's focus on fine-grained domain adaptation might limit its generalizability to other domains or tasks.
2. Some of the methods explored, such as Brown clusters and type-level annotation, show limited improvement over the baseline system.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the system, providing insights into the specific challenges of the task.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the annotation process, including the guidelines and the inter-annotator agreement?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in the domain adaptation methods, particularly when using small amounts of labeled target-domain data?
3. Have you considered exploring other methods for domain adaptation, such as adversarial training or multi-task learning, and how do you think they might perform on this task?
