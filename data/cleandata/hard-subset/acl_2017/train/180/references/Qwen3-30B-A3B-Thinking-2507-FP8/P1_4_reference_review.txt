

# Peer Review: "Identifying Products in Online Cybercrime Marketplaces: A Dataset and Fine-grained Domain Adaptation Task"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a new NLP task for identifying products being bought and sold in cybercrime marketplaces. The authors create a novel dataset with token-level annotations of product references across four different cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, and Nulled). They frame this as a fine-grained domain adaptation problem, demonstrating that models trained on one forum perform significantly worse on another, even within the same cybercrime domain. The main contributions are:

1. A new dataset for product identification in cybercrime marketplaces with token-level annotations
2. A well-defined task that blends information extraction and named entity recognition
3. An empirical demonstration of the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation in this context

## Strengths

1. **Significant and Well-Constructed Dataset**: The paper provides a valuable resource for the NLP community. The dataset is carefully annotated with reasonable inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' Kappa 0.58-0.77), covering four distinct cybercrime forums with different product types. The annotation guidelines were developed through six rounds of refinement, which demonstrates the authors' commitment to quality. The dataset will be useful for future research on cybercrime analysis, domain adaptation, and information extraction in messy web text.

2. **Clear Task Definition and Justification**: The authors provide a thorough justification for their token-level annotation approach, distinguishing their task from standard NER and slot-filling. They clearly explain why token-level annotation is more practical than post-level (avoiding the need to determine whether multiple mentions refer to the same product) and why it's more appropriate than span-level annotation (to avoid parsing disagreements in ungrammatical text). The task is well-positioned within the literature, with clear connections to information extraction and named entity recognition.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Domain Adaptation Challenges**: The paper provides a thoughtful analysis of why domain adaptation is particularly difficult in this context. The OOV analysis (Table 5) is especially valuable, showing that Darkode has better product coverage than Hack Forums, which explains why adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode is more effective than the reverse. The authors systematically test multiple domain adaptation approaches (Brown clusters, gazetteers, token-level annotation) and provide a clear explanation of why they fail to yield significant improvements, which is a valuable contribution in itself.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novel Methodological Contribution**: The core contribution is the dataset and task definition, but the methods for solving the task are quite basic (binary SVM classifiers with simple features). The domain adaptation approaches tested (Brown clusters, gazetteers, small amounts of labeled data) are standard techniques from the literature, not novel contributions. The paper doesn't present a new method for domain adaptation but rather demonstrates that existing methods don't work well for this task. This makes it difficult to assess whether the poor performance is due to the nature of the task or because the authors didn't use state-of-the-art methods.

2. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares to simple baselines and standard domain adaptation techniques, but doesn't compare to more recent or sophisticated approaches. For example, they don't test neural network-based transfer learning methods, which have become standard for domain adaptation in NLP. The lack of comparison to recent work makes it difficult to determine whether the performance issues they observe are inherent to the task or due to the use of outdated methods.

3. **Underdeveloped Justification for Token-Level Annotation**: While the authors justify token-level annotation, they don't provide strong evidence that it's necessary for their intended applications. The post-level accuracy metric they introduce (which they claim is sufficient for most applications) suggests that token-level might not be needed. The paper mentions that "some analyses might require token-level references," but doesn't provide concrete examples of how token-level annotations would be used in practice, making it difficult to assess the necessity of this annotation scheme.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that posts selling multiple products are "quite rare" (roughly 3% of cases). Can you provide more details on how you determined these were different products versus different ways of describing the same product? What specific criteria did you use for this determination?

2. The paper mentions that "the text exhibits common features of web text: abbreviations, ungrammaticality, spelling errors, and visual formatting." Have you analyzed whether these features impact model performance? For example, does the model perform better or worse on posts with more spelling errors?

3. The authors use Fleiss' Kappa for inter-annotator agreement. Since they have multiple annotators for different subsets of the data, could you explain why you chose Fleiss' Kappa over Cohen's Kappa (which would be appropriate for pairwise agreement)?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution through the creation of a new dataset and task for a challenging domain. The dataset is well-constructed and will be useful for future research on cybercrime analysis and domain adaptation. The paper provides a thoughtful analysis of the domain adaptation challenges in this context, which is a significant contribution in itself. However, the lack of novel methodological contributions and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods weakens the paper's technical contribution. The paper would be stronger with more rigorous evaluation against recent approaches and clearer justification for the token-level annotation scheme.

I recommend acceptance, as the dataset and task definition are significant contributions that will enable future research. The thorough analysis of domain adaptation challenges in this specific context is also valuable, even if the proposed methods are not novel. The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above in the revision, particularly by comparing to more recent domain adaptation methods and providing stronger justification for the token-level annotation approach.
