

# Peer Review: "Identifying Products in Online Cybercrime Marketplaces"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a new NLP task and dataset for identifying product references in cybercrime marketplaces (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled). The authors present a token-level annotation scheme where they identify references to products being bought/sold in forum posts, and explore domain adaptation challenges when transferring models between different cybercrime forums. The main contributions are: (1) a new dataset with token-level annotations of product mentions across multiple cybercrime forums; (2) a well-defined task that blends information extraction and named entity recognition; and (3) an analysis of fine-grained domain adaptation challenges in this specific domain.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Dataset Contribution**: The authors have created a well-annotated, large-scale dataset for a domain that has been largely unexplored in NLP. The dataset includes four distinct cybercrime forums with detailed statistics, annotation guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement metrics. The annotation process was thorough with six rounds of development and training, and the dataset is immediately useful for security researchers who need to analyze these forums. This represents a substantial contribution to the field.

2. **Clear Task Definition and Evaluation Framework**: The authors effectively distinguish their task from standard NER and slot-filling by emphasizing the context-dependent nature of product mentions in forum data. They provide a comprehensive evaluation framework that considers multiple metrics (token-level, phrase-level, product-type, post-level), recognizing that different applications may require different evaluation approaches. This thoughtful consideration of evaluation metrics significantly strengthens the paper.

3. **Useful Analysis of Domain Differences**: The paper provides valuable insights into the domain differences between cybercrime forums, particularly the "fine-grained domain adaptation" challenge. The analysis of out-of-vocabulary rates and performance on seen vs. unseen products (Table 5) is particularly insightful and helps explain why cross-domain transfer is challenging. The finding that Darkode appears to be more "general" than Hack Forums is an important empirical observation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Domain Adaptation Methods**: The domain adaptation approaches explored (Brown clusters, gazetteers, simple domain adaptation) are relatively basic and standard. The authors don't leverage more recent advances in domain adaptation (e.g., adversarial methods, contextual embeddings, or transformer-based approaches), which would have been expected in 2023. The paper misses an opportunity to make a meaningful contribution to the domain adaptation literature by testing more sophisticated methods.

2. **Superficial Analysis of Domain Differences**: While the authors identify two hypotheses about why domain adaptation fails (product inventory vs. stylistic differences), they don't provide a deep linguistic analysis to support these claims. They don't examine specific linguistic features that differ between forums (e.g., vocabulary, syntax, discourse patterns), which would strengthen their argument and provide more concrete insights for future work.

3. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims this is "the first to tackle forum data (and marketplace forums specifically) from an information extraction perspective," but there's significant prior work on forum data analysis (Kaljahi et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2011). The authors don't adequately engage with this literature, which weakens their novelty claim and makes the contribution seem less significant.

4. **Limited Practical Impact Discussion**: The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how the task would be used in real security applications. While they mention it could help characterize forums and identify trends, they don't provide concrete examples of how this would impact security analysis. A case study showing the practical utility of their dataset would strengthen the paper significantly.

## Questions to Authors

1. Given the relatively modest improvements from domain adaptation methods (Table 3), how would you assess the practical utility of these methods for security researchers who need to analyze new cybercrime forums? Are the improvements sufficient for real-world applications?

2. Could you elaborate on why the Darkode-trained model performs better on Hack Forums than vice versa (Table 5)? This seems counterintuitive given the domain differences, and would help clarify your hypothesis about "general" vs. "specific" forums.

3. How do you address the ethical concerns of creating and sharing a dataset of cybercrime marketplace content? Have you consulted with security researchers about the potential risks and benefits of this dataset?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution through its dataset and task definition for analyzing cybercrime marketplaces. The dataset will be immediately useful for security researchers and NLP researchers interested in domain adaptation. However, the domain adaptation methods explored are limited, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the practical utility of the task. The work is solid but incremental, and the domain adaptation analysis would be significantly strengthened with more sophisticated methods and deeper linguistic analysis.

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the domain adaptation challenges, a more thorough engagement with existing forum analysis literature, and a clearer demonstration of the practical impact of this work for security researchers. With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field.
