

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a new dataset of posts from cybercrime marketplaces annotated for product references, along with a token-level annotation task that blends information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors present a fine-grained domain adaptation challenge between different cybercrime forums, exploring various adaptation techniques to improve cross-forum performance. The main contributions are the new dataset, the task definition, and an analysis of domain adaptation challenges in this specific setting.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel dataset of cybercrime marketplace posts annotated for product references at the token level, with careful annotation guidelines and inter-annotator agreement analysis
2. A well-defined task that bridges information extraction and named entity recognition for this specific domain, with clear motivation for the token-level approach
3. A thorough analysis of domain adaptation challenges between different cybercrime forums, including the surprising finding that adaptation from Hack Forums to Darkode is more effective than vice versa

## Strengths

1. **Novel dataset with clear motivation**: The authors have created a valuable resource for the NLP community, addressing a real-world problem in cybersecurity. The dataset is carefully annotated with a detailed annotation scheme and inter-annotator agreement metrics (Fleiss' Kappa). The motivation for the token-level approach (to avoid domain knowledge requirements for distinguishing between multiple product mentions) is sound and well-justified.

2. **Clear task definition and evaluation metrics**: The paper provides a thoughtful discussion of different evaluation metrics (token-level, type-level, post-level) and justifies their use based on potential applications. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the trade-offs between these metrics and how they relate to different use cases.

3. **Important domain adaptation findings**: The paper makes a significant contribution by highlighting the substantial performance degradation when transferring models between different cybercrime forums, even within the same domain. The finding that Darkode is more "domain-general" than Hack Forums (based on lower OOV rates and better performance on OOV tokens) is particularly insightful and has important implications for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Major inconsistencies in the annotation process description**: The paper states that "When the product is a multiword expression (e.g., Backconnect bot), it is almost exclusively a noun phrase, in which case we annotate the head word of the noun phrase (bot)." However, Figure 1 shows "Backconnect bot" as a single underlined phrase, not just "bot" as the head word. This contradiction between the stated annotation principle and the actual example creates significant confusion about the annotation scheme. Additionally, the paper states that for "hack an account" they annotate both "hack" and "account," but according to their stated principle, "hack" should be annotated as part of the method, while "account" is the product - this is inconsistent with the example.

2. **Poorly formatted tables and inconsistent data presentation**: Table 1 has confusing column headers ("Words Products Annotated Annotators Inter-annotator agreement Forum Posts per post per post posts per post 3-annotated all-annotated") that don't clearly indicate what the numbers represent. The description of the table is insufficient to understand the data. Table 3 and Table 4 have inconsistent labeling of training and test sets (e.g., "Trained on Darkode" as a row rather than a column), making it difficult to interpret the results. The "Blackhat" row in Table 1 shows inconsistent numbers (167 posts, 174 words, 3.2 posts per word) that don't align with the other forums' statistics.

3. **Inconsistent and poorly formatted references**: The references section contains numerous formatting errors, including inconsistent use of backslashes, strange formatting with \Q and \E, and incomplete citation information (e.g., "Bamman et al.,? \Q2013\E, Bamman et al\.,"). The references to "III.,? 2007" instead of "Daume III, 2007" are particularly problematic and unprofessional.

4. **Critical errors in data description**: The paper states "We have no training data for those forums" when referring to Blackhat and Nulled, but Table 1 shows that Blackhat and Nulled have annotated data (167 and 39,118 posts, respectively). This is a serious inconsistency that undermines the credibility of the paper.

5. **Misuse of statistical terms and methods**: The paper claims to use "Fleiss' Kappa measurement (Fleiss, 1971)" for token-level annotation, but Fleiss' Kappa is designed for categorical agreement between multiple raters, not for token-level binary classification. The authors should have used a different metric such as Cohen's Kappa (for two raters) or Krippendorff's Alpha (for multiple raters and binary data). Additionally, the paper states "We chose this measure as we are interested in agreement between more than two annotators (ruling out Cohen's kappa)" but then uses a measure that is inappropriate for the task.

## Questions to Authors

1. Please clarify the contradiction between the stated annotation principle (annotating only the head word of noun phrases) and the examples in Figure 1 (where the entire phrase "Backconnect bot" is underlined).

2. Please explain why you claim "We have no training data for those forums" (Blackhat and Nulled) when Table 1 shows annotated data for these forums.

3. Please justify your use of Fleiss' Kappa for token-level binary annotation. If this is not appropriate, please explain what metric you should have used instead.

4. Please clarify the inconsistent labeling in Tables 3 and 4 (e.g., "Trained on Darkode" as a row rather than a column). This makes it very difficult to understand the results.

5. Please explain why you upweighted singleton products (product types that occur only once in the training set) by a factor of 5. What evidence supports this choice?

## Recommendation

Reject

The paper presents a novel dataset and task with potential value for the NLP community, but the numerous inconsistencies, formatting errors, and methodological issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The contradictions between stated principles and examples, the poor table formatting, the inconsistent data description, and the misuse of statistical methods are too severe to overlook. While the dataset itself may be valuable, the paper's presentation and methodological execution are not of sufficient quality for publication at ACL. These issues make it difficult to assess the validity of the results and the significance of the contributions.
