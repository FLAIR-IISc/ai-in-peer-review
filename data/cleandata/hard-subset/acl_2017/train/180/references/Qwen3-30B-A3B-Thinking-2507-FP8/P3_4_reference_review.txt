

# Peer Review: "Identifying Products in Online Cybercrime Marketplaces: A Dataset and Fine-grained Domain Adaptation Task"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a new NLP task for identifying product references in cybercrime marketplaces, accompanied by a well-annotated dataset from four different forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled). The authors define a token-level annotation task where they label references to products being bought or sold, distinguishing their approach from standard NER and slot-filling tasks. The main contributions are: (1) the creation of a valuable, well-annotated dataset for this underexplored domain; (2) a careful justification and definition of the task; and (3) a thorough analysis of domain adaptation challenges between different cybercrime forums.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Dataset Contribution**: This is the paper's strongest contribution. Cybercrime marketplaces represent an important but understudied domain for NLP research. The authors provide a well-constructed dataset with detailed annotation guidelines, inter-annotator agreement statistics (Fleiss' Kappa > 0.58), and careful preprocessing. The dataset fills a critical gap in the literature and will be valuable for future research on domain adaptation, security informatics, and information extraction. The authors' note that the dataset has already "proven enabling for case studies" is a strong testament to its utility.

2. **Clear Task Definition and Justification**: The paper provides a thoughtful justification for their token-level annotation approach, clearly explaining why it's more appropriate than standard NER or slot-filling. Their examples (like the "mod DCIBot" case) effectively demonstrate the context-dependent nature of product references in this domain. The distinction between annotating the product itself versus the method of obtaining it is particularly insightful and well-justified.

3. **Rigorous Domain Adaptation Analysis**: The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding fine-grained domain adaptation challenges. The authors demonstrate that performance drops substantially when moving between different cybercrime forums (e.g., 14.4 F1 point drop when adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode), which is a critical insight for the field. Their analysis of OOV rates (Table 5) and the comparative performance of Darkode versus Hack Forums as training data provides valuable empirical evidence about domain differences.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Methodological Innovation**: While the dataset is valuable, the paper's methodological contributions are limited. The baseline approaches (binary SVM, post-level classifier) are standard, and the domain adaptation techniques tested (Brown clusters, gazetteers, token-level annotation) are well-established in the literature. The authors acknowledge this by stating they "explore several methods for adaptation" but don't provide a compelling argument for why these specific methods should work well in this domain. The failure to find significant improvements is not surprising given the standard nature of the techniques tested.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Domain Differences**: The paper identifies that domain adaptation is difficult but doesn't deeply analyze *why*. While they note that Darkode has better product coverage, they don't investigate the linguistic or structural differences between forums in detail. For example, they could have analyzed differences in sentence structure, product naming conventions, or forum-specific slang. A deeper analysis of these differences would have strengthened their insights about the nature of the domain adaptation problem.

3. **Omission of Recent Domain Adaptation Methods**: Given this is an ACL submission, the omission of recent domain adaptation techniques is notable. The paper doesn't compare their approach to state-of-the-art methods like adversarial domain adaptation (Zhang et al., 2017) or contextualized embeddings (BERT). These methods have shown promise in domain adaptation tasks and could have provided more meaningful insights about whether the problem is solvable with current techniques.

## Questions to Authors

1. You note that Darkode has better product coverage than Hack Forums (Table 5), which explains why adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode is more effective. Could you elaborate on why Darkode might have broader product coverage? Is it because it's a more established forum with a wider range of products, or are there specific characteristics of the Darkode community that lead to more diverse product mentions?

2. Your Table 3 shows that the Hack Forums-trained system performs better on Nulled than the Darkode-trained system. Given that Nulled is a different forum, could this be due to some commonality between Hack Forums and Nulled that isn't shared with Darkode? If so, what might that commonality be (e.g., similar product types, forum structure, or user demographics)?

3. The paper mentions that "product inventories and stylistic conventions both differ across domains" (Hypothesis 2), but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of stylistic differences. Could you provide more specific examples of how the forums differ in writing style, sentence structure, or other linguistic features that might impact model performance?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution through its dataset and careful task definition, which will be useful for future research in domain adaptation and security informatics. However, the methodological contributions are limited, and the paper could have strengthened its analysis by comparing to more recent domain adaptation techniques and providing a deeper exploration of the linguistic differences between forums.

The dataset is well-constructed and the annotation process appears rigorous. The paper's main insight—that even within the same broad domain (cybercrime forums), performance degrades significantly when moving between forums—is important and well-documented. However, the lack of methodological novelty and the omission of recent domain adaptation approaches are significant limitations for an ACL submission.

I would recommend acceptance with major revisions, particularly to address the methodological shortcomings by either: (1) comparing to more recent domain adaptation techniques, or (2) providing a deeper analysis of the linguistic differences between forums to better explain why domain adaptation is difficult in this context. The dataset itself is strong enough to be a valuable resource, but the paper's contribution would be significantly enhanced by addressing these limitations.
