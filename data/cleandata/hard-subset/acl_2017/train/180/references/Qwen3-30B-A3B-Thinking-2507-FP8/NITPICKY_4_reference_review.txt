

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper introduces a new dataset for identifying products being bought and sold in cybercrime marketplaces, presented as a token-level annotation task. The authors annotate posts from four different cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled) and evaluate several methods for adapting models across these different forums. The key claim is that even within cybercrime forums, there are significant domain differences that make cross-forum adaptation challenging.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A new dataset of cybercrime marketplace posts annotated with product references at the token level
2. A novel task definition that blends information extraction and named entity recognition for cybercrime forum analysis
3. A thorough analysis of domain adaptation challenges for this specific context

## Strengths
1. **The dataset itself is potentially valuable**: While small, the dataset fills a gap in the literature for cybercrime forum analysis. The token-level annotation approach is appropriate for the messy, ungrammatical nature of forum posts.

2. **Clear task definition**: The paper clearly articulates the distinction between their task and standard NER or slot-filling, emphasizing the context-dependent nature of product mentions in these forums.

3. **Thorough analysis of adaptation challenges**: The paper provides a detailed exploration of why domain adaptation is difficult in this context, including the OOV analysis in Table 5 and the comparison of different adaptation techniques.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical methodological inconsistencies in annotation guidelines**: The paper claims they "do not annotate Features of products, e.g., Update Cmd in Figure 1" but Figure 1 shows exactly "Update Cmd" as part of the annotated text. This contradiction undermines the credibility of the annotation scheme and makes it impossible to reproduce the dataset consistently. The annotation guidelines must be rigorously consistent to be useful.

2. **Severe formatting and data inconsistencies in Tables 1 and 5**: Table 1 has multiple critical errors:
   - Column headers are confusing and repetitive ("Posts per post" appears twice)
   - Blackhat shows "167 posts" but "174 products" (which is impossible)
   - Inter-annotator agreement values are not clearly labeled (0.62 0.66 for Darkode)
   - The table includes "3-annotated" and "all-annotated" columns but doesn't explain what these mean
   - Table 5 reports "OOV" rates without defining what OOV means in this context (out-of-vocabulary for product types)
   These formatting issues make the paper difficult to read and undermine the credibility of the reported results.

3. **Overstated novelty claims**: The paper claims to be "the first to tackle forum data (and marketplace forums specifically) from an information extraction perspective" but fails to cite relevant work on forum analysis. The paper cites Lui and Baldwin (2010), Kim et al. (2010), and Wang et al. (2011) who have worked on discourse analysis of forums, and Kaljahi et al. (2015) on syntax. The authors need to clarify how their work differs from these existing studies on forum analysis.

4. **Insufficient dataset size and lack of justification**: The training sets are extremely small (Darkode has only 660 training posts), which is inadequate for meaningful domain adaptation studies. The paper claims "our dataset has already proven enabling for case studies" but provides no examples of these case studies. A dataset of this size would be considered too small for most NLP benchmarks.

5. **Incomplete methodological details**: Key technical details are missing that would prevent reproducibility:
   - No explanation of how Brown clusters were formed (did they use target domain data?)
   - No specification of SVM hyperparameters (only "tuned on development data")
   - No details on how the "post-level" model was actually implemented (e.g., what was the latent variable in the latent SVM)
   - The phrase "Binary" in Table 3 is never properly defined

## Questions to Authors

1. In Figure 1, the paper states "We do not annotate Features of products, e.g., Update Cmd in Figure 1" but Figure 1 shows "Update Cmd" as part of the annotated text. Can you clarify this contradiction and explain how "Update Cmd" is being treated in the annotation scheme?

2. Table 1 shows Blackhat has "167 posts" but "174 products" - this is impossible as the number of products cannot exceed the number of posts. Is this a typo, and if so, what is the correct number?

3. The paper states "We form Brown clusters on our unlabeled data from both Darkode and Hack Forums" - did you use unlabeled data from the target forum or only source forum data? This is critical for understanding the domain adaptation setup.

4. The paper claims "our dataset has already proven enabling for case studies on these particular forums" but provides no examples. Can you share at least one concrete example of how this dataset has been used in actual analysis?

5. The paper states "The post-level system achieves an F-score of roughly 80 on product type identification" but the results in Table 3 show an NP-level F1 of 61.4 for the "Post" system. Can you clarify this discrepancy?

## Additional Comments
The reference list has multiple formatting inconsistencies, including inconsistent use of "et al." (some with backslashes like "et al\." and some without), inconsistent years, and strange formatting like "\Q2013\E". This needs to be fixed before publication. The paper also contains several grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "outof-domain" should be "out-of-domain", "fo-" in "forum" should be "forum").

The paper presents a potentially useful dataset but is undermined by numerous methodological inconsistencies, formatting errors, and overstated claims. While the task is interesting, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about novelty or the significance of the dataset. The small size of the dataset and lack of detailed methodology make it difficult to assess the value of the work for the NLP community.
