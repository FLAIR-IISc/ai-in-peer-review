

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

The paper introduces a new dataset for identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime marketplaces, presenting a token-level annotation task that blends aspects of information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors provide a dataset from four cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, and Nulled), evaluate several approaches to product extraction, and explore domain adaptation challenges when applying models trained on one forum to another. They claim that data from one cybercrime forum is out of domain with respect to another, making domain adaptation challenging.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel dataset for cybercrime marketplace analysis** (primary contribution) - The paper provides the first dataset for product identification in cybercrime marketplaces, which is a previously unstudied domain in NLP.
2. **Clear definition of a token-level product identification task** - The paper establishes a well-defined NLP task with specific annotation guidelines.
3. **Analysis of domain adaptation challenges in this specific domain** (secondary contribution) - The paper explores why models trained on one forum don't generalize well to another.

## Strengths

1. **Novel dataset for an important domain**: The dataset is valuable because it addresses a niche but important application area (cybercrime analysis) that hasn't been studied in NLP before. The authors have already used this dataset for case studies on the forums, demonstrating its utility.

2. **Thorough annotation process**: The paper describes a detailed annotation process with six preliminary rounds of annotation, discussion, and resolution of disagreements. The authors also provide inter-annotator agreement statistics (Fleiss' Kappa), which adds credibility to their annotation.

3. **Clear task definition**: The paper effectively distinguishes their task from standard NER by emphasizing the context-sensitivity of product mentions ("only care about the specific product being bought or sold in a post, not other products that might be mentioned").

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and incomplete data presentation**: Table 1 contains fundamental inconsistencies that undermine the credibility of the paper:
   - "Words Products Annotated" shows "3,368" for Darkode, but the number of posts is "660/100/100" (train/dev/test). At 60 words per post (as indicated in the table), Darkode should have ~39,600 words, not 3,368.
   - "Annotators" shows "3.2" for Darkode, which is nonsensical (number of annotators must be integer).
   - The inter-annotator agreement columns show "0.62 0.66" but don't clarify what these numbers represent (agreement for 3-annotated posts vs. all-annotated posts?).
   - The paper mentions "a subset labeled by all annotators" but doesn't clarify how many posts were labeled by all annotators.

2. **Methodological contradictions**: The paper contains fundamental contradictions that make it difficult to understand the actual task:
   - The paper states "we do not annotate noun phrases" but then describes a phrase-level evaluation that projects annotations to noun phrase level.
   - The paper claims "the product is almost exclusively a noun phrase" but then says "we annotate the head word of the noun phrase (bot)" - this is contradictory. If the product is a noun phrase, why are they only annotating the head word?
   - The paper says "we annotate tokens when they are either the product that will be delivered or are an integral part of the method leading to the delivery of that product" but then claims "the product is almost exclusively a noun phrase" - this is inconsistent with the examples provided (e.g., "mod DCIBot" is annotated as the core product, but "coder" is also annotated because it's "inextricably linked to the service").

3. **Superficial domain adaptation analysis**: The paper claims "data from one cybercrime forum is even out of domain with respect to another cybercrime forum" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence beyond the performance drop in cross-forum evaluation. The analysis of why domain adaptation is difficult is limited to two hypotheses without strong evidence:
   - They claim "Darkode seems to be more 'general' than Hack Forums" based on lower OOV rates, but Darkode is actually a more specialized forum (focusing on cybercriminal wares) while Hack Forums is a more general forum (mixing cyber-security and computer gaming). This contradicts their claim.
   - The paper doesn't explain why a Darkode-trained model performs better on Hack Forums than vice versa (as shown in Table 3), which would be a key insight.

4. **Inadequate baselines and comparison**: The paper uses very simple baselines (frequency, dictionary, first noun phrase) and doesn't compare with state-of-the-art methods in NLP for similar tasks. The binary classifier and post-level classifier are relatively basic approaches that don't leverage recent advances in NLP (e.g., transformer-based models). The paper doesn't compare with existing domain adaptation techniques (e.g., adversarial training, adversarial domain adaptation) that have been shown effective in other NLP tasks.

5. **Lack of statistical rigor**: The paper claims "only one result improves significantly" for Brown clusters but doesn't provide the statistical tests or p-values to back this up. The paper also doesn't provide confidence intervals or error bars for the reported metrics, making it difficult to assess the significance of the results. The bootstrap resampling test mentioned is not described in detail.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Table 1, "Words Products Annotated" shows "3,368" for Darkode, but the number of posts is "660/100/100" (train/dev/test). Could you clarify what "3,368" represents? Is this the number of words in the annotated data, or something else? Given the number of posts and words per post, this number seems inconsistent with the data.

2. The "Annotators" column shows "3.2" for Darkode. This doesn't make sense as a number of annotators. Could you clarify what this number represents?

3. The paper states "we do not annotate noun phrases" but then describes a phrase-level evaluation that projects annotations to noun phrase level. Could you clarify the relationship between the token-level annotations and the phrase-level evaluation? Why would you project token-level annotations to noun phrase level if you don't annotate noun phrases?

4. The paper claims "Darkode seems to be more 'general' than Hack Forums" based on lower OOV rates. However, Darkode is a more specialized cybercrime forum (focusing on cybercriminal wares) while Hack Forums is a more general forum (mixing cyber-security and computer gaming). Could you explain why Darkode would be considered more "general" in this context? This contradicts the typical understanding of the forums.

5. The paper uses "Fleiss' Kappa" for inter-annotator agreement but doesn't explain why they chose it over other measures. Could you provide a justification for this choice? Specifically, why not use Cohen's Kappa for the 3-annotator cases and Krippendorff's Alpha for the all-annotated cases?

## Additional Comments

The paper's claim that "data from one cybercrime forum is even out of domain with respect to another cybercrime forum" is significant but not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The paper should provide more detailed analysis of the differences between the forums (e.g., vocabulary, writing style, forum structure) to support this claim.

The paper mentions "Available upon publication, along with all models and code from this work" but doesn't provide a URL or reference to where this will be available. This is important for reproducibility.

The paper has inconsistent formatting (e.g., "fine-grained domain adaptation" vs. "fine-grained domain adaptation problem") and grammatical errors (e.g., "data is outof-domain" should be "data is out-of-domain").

The paper states "the product is almost exclusively a noun phrase" but provides no evidence for this claim. This is a key assumption that should be supported with data.

The paper claims "posts selling multiple types of products are actually quite rare (roughly 3% of cases overall)" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. This is a critical point for their evaluation metric choice.

Overall, while the dataset is novel and valuable, the methodological inconsistencies, data presentation issues, and superficial analysis of domain adaptation significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these issues before it could be accepted.
