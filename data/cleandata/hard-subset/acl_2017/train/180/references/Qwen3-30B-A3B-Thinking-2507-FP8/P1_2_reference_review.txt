

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel dataset and task for identifying products in online cybercrime marketplaces, where posts describe products being bought or sold. The authors annotate token-level references to products, distinguishing this from standard NER by focusing on context-specific product mentions. They demonstrate that even within the same domain (cybercrime forums), different forums present significant domain differences, making standard domain adaptation methods ineffective. The paper provides thorough evaluation of multiple adaptation techniques and offers insightful analysis of why domain adaptation is challenging in this context.

## Main Contributions

1. **Novel dataset creation**: The authors provide a new dataset with token-level annotations for product references in cybercrime marketplaces, including detailed statistics, inter-annotator agreement measures, and clear annotation guidelines. This dataset fills an important gap in NLP research, as cybercrime forums represent a challenging but underexplored domain.

2. **Clear task definition and analysis**: The paper effectively defines the task as a blend of information extraction and named entity recognition, with careful consideration of nuances like when to annotate verbs or multi-word phrases. The analysis of why domain adaptation fails (showing both product coverage and stylistic differences matter) is particularly insightful.

3. **Thorough evaluation framework**: The authors propose multiple evaluation metrics (token-level, type-level, post-level) and provide a nuanced analysis of results across different forums, offering valuable insights into the nature of the domain adaptation challenge.

## Strengths

1. **Significant dataset contribution**: The dataset is a substantial contribution to the field. Cybercrime marketplaces represent a critical but understudied domain, and the token-level annotation with inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' Kappa = 0.62-0.77) provides a solid foundation for future research. The dataset has already proven useful for case studies (as noted in the paper), suggesting immediate applicability.

2. **Insightful analysis of domain adaptation**: The paper makes a crucial observation that even within a single domain (cybercrime), different forums represent different "fine-grained domains" with significant performance differences (e.g., Hack Forums-trained model on Darkode: 63.2 vs. 77.6 F1). The analysis in Section 5.4, particularly Table 5 showing OOV rates and performance on seen vs. novel products, provides valuable theoretical grounding for the domain adaptation challenge.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper evaluates multiple metrics (token, type, post-level) and multiple models (baselines, binary classifiers, post-level classifiers), providing a thorough understanding of the task's challenges. The exploration of Brown clusters, gazetteers, and small-scale target-domain annotation demonstrates a thoughtful approach to domain adaptation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited methodological novelty**: The paper primarily applies standard NLP techniques (SVMs with basic features) to a new domain rather than proposing novel methods. The domain adaptation approaches tested (Brown clusters, gazetteers) are well-established in NLP literature, not novel for this context. A comparison with more recent domain adaptation techniques (e.g., adversarial training, multi-task learning) would have strengthened the paper.

2. **Small dataset size**: The dataset sizes are relatively modest (Darkode: 3,368 posts), which limits the potential for training more complex models. The paper doesn't discuss how dataset size might impact the results or whether the findings would generalize to larger datasets.

3. **Lack of ethical considerations**: Given the sensitive nature of the data (cybercrime marketplaces), the paper omits discussion of ethical considerations regarding data collection, consent, and potential misuse of the dataset. This is particularly concerning as the data involves illegal activities.

## Questions to Authors

1. How was the data collected from these forums? Was it through public access, and if so, what measures were taken to ensure ethical collection? How did you address potential risks of facilitating cybercrime?

2. Have you considered using neural network models (e.g., BERT) to see if they can better handle the domain adaptation challenge compared to the SVM baselines? If not, what would be the expected benefits and challenges?

3. The paper states that "posts selling multiple types of products are actually quite rare (roughly 3% of cases overall)." How did you determine this threshold of 3%? Is there a way to quantify the impact of this rarity on the model's performance?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution through its new dataset and insightful analysis of domain adaptation challenges in cybercrime marketplaces. The dataset is well-constructed and fills an important gap in the literature. However, the methodological contributions are limited, as the paper primarily applies standard NLP techniques to a new domain rather than proposing novel approaches. The small dataset size and lack of ethical considerations are notable weaknesses.

The paper would be strengthened by comparing with more recent domain adaptation techniques, discussing how to expand the dataset size, and addressing ethical considerations. Nevertheless, the dataset and task definition represent a significant contribution that will enable future research in this challenging domain.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the dataset contribution is strong, but the methodological novelty is limited)
