

# Review of "Identifying Products in Online Cybercrime Marketplaces: A Dataset and Fine-grained Domain Adaptation Task"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a new dataset for identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces and introduces a fine-grained domain adaptation task. The authors define a token-level annotation task for product references in forum posts, which they argue is distinct from standard NER and slot-filling tasks. They provide statistics on four forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled), detail their annotation process, and evaluate several models for product extraction with a focus on domain adaptation challenges.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. A new dataset for product identification in cybercrime marketplaces with detailed token-level annotations
2. A clear definition of a novel token-level product identification task that blends information extraction and NER
3. An insightful analysis of domain adaptation challenges in this specific context, particularly the difference between Darkode and Hack Forums

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Important Dataset**: The dataset addresses a significant gap in NLP research, providing the first annotated resource for product identification in cybercrime marketplaces. This dataset has clear practical applications for cybersecurity research and could enable future studies on cybercriminal behavior.

2. **Clear Task Definition**: The authors effectively distinguish their task from standard NER and slot-filling by emphasizing the context-sensitive nature of product references in forum posts. Their justification for token-level annotation over post-level is compelling and well-argued.

3. **Thorough Annotation Process**: The paper details the annotation guidelines development process (six rounds of annotation), inter-annotator agreement statistics, and the specific challenges of annotating this messy forum data. This level of detail is valuable for reproducibility.

4. **Systematic Evaluation**: The paper provides multiple evaluation metrics (token-level, type-level, post-level) and evaluates across different forums, which is crucial for understanding the domain adaptation challenges. The analysis of OOV rates (Table 5) provides important insights into the nature of the domain shift.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Formatting and Terminology Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors that undermine its credibility and readability. Most notably:
   - Table 1 contains multiple inconsistencies: "Words Products Annotated" should be separate columns; "Posts per post" is mislabeled; the "Annotators" column has inconsistent formatting (e.g., "3.2" vs. "3/8/8").
   - In the text, "fo-rum" appears instead of "forum" (Section 1).
   - In Table 4, "N P-" should be "NP-" and the formatting is inconsistent with the rest of the paper.
   - The paper uses inconsistent capitalization ("Darkode" vs. "darkode" in different places).
   - These formatting issues suggest a lack of attention to detail that would be unacceptable in an ACL publication.

2. **Insufficient Methodological Detail**: The paper lacks critical details necessary for reproducibility:
   - The SVM parameters (C value, kernel type) are not specified.
   - AdaGrad parameters (learning rate, etc.) are omitted.
   - The paper states "we use AdaGrad to speed convergence" but doesn't provide the specific hyperparameters used.
   - The reference to "bootstrap resampling test" for statistical significance lacks details about the number of resamples or the exact test procedure.

3. **Superficial Analysis of Domain Adaptation**: The paper claims to analyze why domain adaptation methods fail but provides insufficient depth:
   - Section 5.4 states "Darkode seems to be more 'general' than Hack Forums" based on OOV rates, but doesn't provide statistical tests to confirm this difference is significant.
   - The analysis of why the Darkode-trained system performs better on Darkode than the Hack Forums-trained system on Darkode is superficial; the paper doesn't explore why Darkode has better product coverage.
   - The paper states "we find little improvement from these methods" but doesn't provide a systematic comparison of why each method fails rather than just stating the results.

4. **Inconsistent Evaluation Metrics**: The paper switches between evaluation metrics (token-level, NP-level, type-level) without clear justification for why different metrics are used in different sections:
   - The paper states "Throughout the rest of this work, we focus on NP-level evaluation and post-level NP accuracy" but the tables and text inconsistently reference different metrics.
   - In Table 3, they report "NP-level F1" but in the text, they sometimes refer to "token-level" results.
   - This inconsistency makes it difficult to follow the evaluation and compare results across different sections.

5. **Reproducibility Concerns**: The paper mentions "Our full annotation guide will be available in supplementary material" but fails to provide any details about:
   - What specific information will be in the supplementary material
   - When it will be available
   - How to access it
   - This is a common issue in ACL papers, but as a perfectionist reviewer, I find it unacceptable that the paper doesn't provide even a placeholder for this information.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify the inconsistency in Table 1 between "Posts per post" (which appears to be a mislabeling) and the actual numbers provided? What does this column represent?

2. The paper states "we take all (lowercased, stemmed) product tokens and keep those occurring at least 4 times in the training dataset" but also mentions "these datasets are ≈ 700 posts" in Section 5.2. However, Table 1 shows Darkode has 3,368 posts. Please clarify the dataset size used for creating the gazetteers.

3. The paper claims "only one result improves significantly" with p < 0.05 but doesn't provide details about the bootstrap resampling test (number of resamples, confidence intervals, etc.). Please provide these details.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a valuable dataset and addresses an important problem, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, lack of methodological detail, and superficial analysis of domain adaptation issues are unacceptable for an ACL publication. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these minor flaws collectively undermine the paper's credibility and make it difficult to assess the significance of the contributions. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues before reconsideration.

The dataset itself is valuable, but the paper's presentation and methodological rigor fall short of ACL standards. The authors have a strong foundation but need to address these fundamental issues before the work can be considered for publication.
