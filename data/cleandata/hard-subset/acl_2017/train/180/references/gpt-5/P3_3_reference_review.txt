Summary and contributions
- New dataset and task: The paper introduces a token-level annotation task for identifying products being bought/sold in cybercrime marketplace forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled), with detailed guidelines and moderate IAA. This is the primary contribution, filling a real gap for security-focused IE on messy, forum text.
- Evaluation protocol and analysis: The paper proposes multiple evaluation views (token-, type-, and post-level; token vs NP-projected spans) and provides a careful cross-forum analysis, highlighting “fine-grained domain” shifts and the role of OOV product vocabularies. The negative results and diagnostics (OOV analysis, seen vs unseen recall) are themselves valuable.
- Baseline modeling and domain adaptation study: The authors benchmark simple SVM-based token and latent post-level models and test Brown clusters, type-level gazetteers, and “frustratingly easy” domain adaptation with small amounts of labeled target data. While improvements are limited, the systematic study clarifies what does and doesn’t help.

Strengths
- Timely, novel dataset with practical impact: Product extraction in underground forums is important for security research and difficult for general-purpose NLP. The dataset is non-trivial (tens of thousands of posts; multiple forums; messy text) and the annotation guidelines address hard context-sensitive decisions (e.g., annotating method tokens when integral to the deliverable). Making data/code available will likely enable follow-on work across NLP and security.
- Thoughtful task and metrics design: The paper recognizes different downstream needs and evaluates at token-, type-, and post-levels, plus NP-projected spans. The post-level latent SVM targeting the “one salient product per post” assumption is a meaningful modeling choice aligned with the forum setting.
- Solid cross-domain experimental protocol and honest reporting: The within-forum vs cross-forum gaps are large and clearly quantified. The authors report limited gains from Brown clusters and gazetteers with significance testing and provide helpful analysis (seen vs OOV products, asymmetric transfer, Darkode’s broader coverage).
- Clear annotation process and inter-annotator agreement: Multiple rounds of guideline refinement and moderate IAA (Fleiss’ κ ≈ 0.58–0.77) on challenging data provide confidence the task is learnable but non-trivial. The decision to annotate heads simplifies consistency.
- Useful negative results and implications: The work convincingly argues this setting is a “fine-grained domain adaptation” problem where lexical OOV is significant but not the only factor; this gives a concrete, realistic benchmark for future adaptation methods.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling baselines relative to current standards: The strongest models are linear SVMs with local features. Absent are modern sequence labeling baselines that could help both in-domain and cross-forum settings (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character CNN/LSTM; contextual representations like ELMo/BERT; domain-adaptive pretraining on forum text). Given the paper’s framing as an adaptation benchmark, stronger baselines (including adversarial or invariant representation learning, instance weighting, pivots/SCL) would sharpen conclusions.
- Potential evaluation bias from NP projection and sentence selection: Phrase-level evaluation relies on an off-the-shelf news-trained parser over noisy forum text. Parser errors may distort both gold NP spans and system scoring. Further, excluding product mentions outside the first/last 10 lines may bias the dataset toward title/closing language and inflate the utility of “first NP” or post-level models; the paper notes coverage statistics, but a deeper analysis of affected posts and the error profile would help.
- Ambiguity in annotation semantics (product vs. method/agent): Annotating method verbs (hack) and agents (coder) when “integral to delivery” introduces subjectivity, which likely contributes to moderate κ and modeling confusion. A clearer operationalization or hierarchical labels (e.g., product, method, agent) could reduce ambiguity and help adaptation by decomposing stylistic vs lexical shifts.
- Adaptation methods are shallow and under-explored: Brown clusters and type-level gazetteers yield small gains; frustratingly easy domain features sometimes hurt under low-resource target data. Missing are stronger unlabeled-target methods (domain-adaptive LM pretraining; self-training with post-level constraints; posterior regularization using title-body consistency; instance reweighting), and systematic ablations (e.g., effect of dependency features, position features, singleton upweighting).
- Sparse error analysis and ablations: The paper would benefit from a deeper qualitative breakdown of cross-forum errors (false positives like account/website; polysemy; title vs body; spelling variants; code snippets/formatting) and ablations of key design choices (head-only annotation vs full span; dependency features; singleton upweighting).

Questions to authors
- Can you report results with a standard sequence model (e.g., CRF over tokens/NPs) and at least one neural baseline (BiLSTM-CRF with char features)? If contextual encoders are infeasible, please clarify constraints and consider domain-adaptive LM pretraining on unlabeled forum text as a non-task-specific adaptation.
- How robust is the NP projection? Could you provide parser accuracy diagnostics on a sample and sensitivity of F1 to projection errors (e.g., varying NP length cap, using a chunker, or reporting token-level results alongside)?
- How often did “method/agent” tokens (e.g., coder, hack) get annotated, and how do systems perform on these vs noun products? Would a multi-label schema (product/method/agent) improve agreement and cross-domain transfer?
- For the “first/last 10 lines” rule, can you analyze the subset of posts affected: proportion where core product is indeed outside that window; performance with/without this restriction?
- Could you test a simple constraint-based self-training: pick the single highest-scoring product in each unlabeled post (title-weighted), add to training, iterate? This aligns with your post-level modeling assumption and may address OOV/lexical variation.

Additional comments
- Please clean up references (several malformed entries) and clarify licensing/ethical handling of the forum data (de-identification, terms of use, IRB/ethics review).
- Consider releasing a small, fully span-annotated subset to decouple parser errors from evaluation and to support research on span boundary recovery.
- The dataset and clear negative results make this a valuable benchmark; strengthening baselines and clarifying evaluation choices would make it a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance primarily on the dataset and analysis merits.
