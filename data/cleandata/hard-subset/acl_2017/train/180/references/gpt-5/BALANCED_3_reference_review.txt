Summary and main contributions
This paper introduces a token-level product identification task for posts in cybercrime marketplaces, accompanied by an annotated dataset spanning four forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled). The authors define multiple evaluation metrics (token-, type-, and post-level; token vs NP projection), propose simple SVM-based token and post-level models, and study “fine-grained” cross-forum domain adaptation via Brown clusters, gazetteers (type-level supervision), and feature augmentation (Daumé). Cross-forum performance degrades substantially; explored adaptations yield modest gains. The dataset, code, and models are promised for release.

As I see them, the contributions (strongest first):
1) A new annotated dataset and task definition for product extraction in cybercrime forums, with clear evaluation protocols and analyses (including NP projection and post/type/post-level metrics).
2) An empirical characterization of fine-grained domain shift across forums with concrete evidence (OOV analyses, asymmetric transfer), showing limitations of standard adaptation tricks.
3) Baseline models and publicly released resources to seed future work.

Strengths
- Valuable, hard-to-obtain dataset in an impactful domain. The forums covered represent distinct sub-communities with different product inventories and styles; this supports the paper’s claim of fine-grained domain shift. The dataset enables research at the intersection of NLP and cybersecurity, a relatively under-resourced area.

- Careful task framing and metrics. The authors provide multiple, well-motivated evaluation views (token F1, per-post type F1, post-level accuracy; token vs NP projection). This aligns with real downstream goals (forum characterization and trend analysis) and reveals trade-offs (e.g., post-level accuracy ~90% in-domain).

- Thorough cross-domain analysis with concrete diagnostics. The paper does more than report drops: it quantifies OOV rates across forums and separates seen vs unseen product performance, explaining asymmetric adaptation (Darkode → others is easier than the reverse). This provides a useful baseline understanding for future adaptation research.

- Transparent baselines and ablations. The paper evaluates dictionary baselines, token vs post-level SVMs, Brown clusters, and type-level gazetteers, and reports statistical significance where applicable. The observation that simple post-level selection can be strong for post-level accuracy is informative.

- Annotation process and agreement reporting. Multiple annotators, majority-vote merging, and Fleiss’ Kappa are provided, along with rationale for token-level heads and projection to NPs. Agreement is moderate but reasonable for noisy forum text.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and missing strong baselines. The modeling choices (linear SVMs with hand-crafted features; Brown clusters; Daumé feature augmentation) are conservative for ACL 2025. Absent are widely adopted baselines likely to change the picture: BiLSTM-CRF/CRF with character features; contextual encoders (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa) with domain-adaptive pretraining; adversarial/domain-invariant methods; self-training/pseudo-labeling; multi-domain or meta-learning. Without these, it is hard to assess how “hard” the adaptation is today.

- Small annotated scale and potentially biased annotation policy. Training data are modest (≈700–800 posts per main forum; only 80–100 posts for smaller forums). The policy to annotate only first/last 10 lines may bias distributions, and annotating only NP heads (with parser-projected evaluation) introduces parser error into evaluation without quantifying parser robustness on this noisy domain.

- Moderate and uneven IAA, and annotator pool composed of authors. Fleiss’ Kappa ranges ~0.58–0.77. Given the context-sensitive task, this is understandable, but more detail on adjudication, difficult categories, and a small expert vs non-expert comparison would help. Having only authors annotate raises potential bias concerns.

- Ethical/legal and data-governance aspects are underdeveloped. The paper lacks a clear ethics statement: data acquisition/compliance, licensing/redistribution, PII handling, redaction, potential harms, and safeguards when releasing data/models about illicit activity. This is critical for a cybersecurity resource paper.

- Clarity and reproducibility gaps. Some references appear malformed; footnote markers and formatting issues persist. The training details are sparse (e.g., hyperparameters, exact regularization strengths, feature vocab thresholds). The quality of NP projection (parser accuracy on this domain) is not quantified. It is unclear whether time-based splits were considered to avoid temporal leakage.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong modern baselines: contextualized sequence models (BiLSTM-CRF + character CNN/LSTM; BERT-style encoders), domain-adaptive pretraining on unlabeled forum text, adversarial/domain-invariant training (e.g., gradient reversal), and self-training across forums. Character-level and subword features should directly address OOV issues.

- Evaluate parser robustness and the impact of NP projection errors; provide an end-to-end metric that does not depend on parser accuracy, or use chunkers tuned for noisy text.

- Expand or diversify annotated data, or augment with weak supervision (distant patterns, lexicon bootstrapping, thread-title heuristics) and report how much each helps.

- Provide a detailed ethics/data-governance statement (collection legality, consent, redactions, release terms, potential misuse mitigations).

- Strengthen reproducibility: full hyperparameter grids, random seeds, feature lists, and scripts to replicate the evaluation metrics.

Questions to authors
- What is the exact license and access policy for the dataset? Are posts redacted/anonymized? How are PII and potential harms handled?

- Did you try contextual encoders (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa) or character-aware models? If so, please report results; if not, why?

- How accurate is the parser on your domain? Can you quantify the effect of NP projection errors on the final metrics?

- Are splits time-aware to avoid temporal leakage (e.g., new product types appearing later)? If not, could you provide such splits?

- Can you share more details on hyperparameters (regularization strengths, feature thresholds) and the exact significance testing protocol?

Overall, this is a timely dataset contribution with thoughtful task design and analyses. To meet ACL standards, the paper would benefit from stronger modern baselines, clearer ethics and reproducibility details, and deeper evaluation of parser dependence.
