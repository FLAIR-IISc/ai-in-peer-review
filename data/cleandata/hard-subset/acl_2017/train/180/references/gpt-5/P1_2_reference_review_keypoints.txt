- Paper introduces a new IE task/dataset for extracting product mentions from cybercrime forums and benchmarks token- and post-level models across multiple forums, showing strong within-forum but poor cross-forum performance.
- Contribution: a curated, token-level annotated dataset across multiple cybercrime forums with detailed guidelines, IAA, and multiple evaluation protocols.
- Contribution: an empirical evaluation framework with token, NP-projected phrase, type-per-post, and post-level metrics plus a post-level latent SVM tailored to “one primary product.”
- Contribution: systematic cross-domain analysis of domain shift using Brown clusters, small gazetteers, and limited token-level supervision, with diagnostic analysis of OOV effects.
- Strength: high-impact, socially relevant resource addressing noisy, high-stakes text and enabling study of fine-grained domain shift across forums.
- Strength: transparent annotation and evaluation design with reasonable IAA and pragmatic token-to-NP projection for multiple analytic needs.
- Strength: strong empirical characterization and useful baselines showing practical within-domain performance (≈80% NP / ≈90% post-level on Darkode).
- Strength: careful domain-adaptation experiments that quantify degradation, demonstrate modest gains from simple methods, and identify product inventory/OOV coverage as a dominant factor.
- Strength: commitment to release data, code, and models to support reproducibility and future work.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty and reliance on dated linear SVM baselines without exploring modern neural/contextualized methods or domain-adversarial approaches.
- Weakness: annotation/evaluation choices (head-token annotation, news-trained NP parser projection, NP length cap, truncating posts) may bias phrase-level results and dataset representativeness.
- Weakness: moderate inter-annotator agreement (κ ≈ 0.58–0.77) and annotator pool comprised of authors raise concerns about ambiguity handling and generalizability.
- Weakness: domain adaptation methods tested are narrow (Brown clusters, gazetteers, feature augmentation) and omit alternatives like self-training, multi-source learning, representation alignment, or meta-learning.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of ethics, privacy, anonymization, and potential harms or approvals for releasing sensitive cybercrime forum data.
- Question: clarify exactly what will be released (raw text, tokenized text, IDs) and how sensitive data (user IDs, URLs) are handled and ethically justified.
- Question: how sensitive are NP-level results to parser errors—was gold NP span evaluation or multiple parser comparison performed?
- Question: were stronger baselines (BiLSTM-CRF, contextual encoders, frozen pretrained models) tried, and if not, why not given domain-shift framing?
- Question: how does the post-level model handle multi-product posts (~3%), and would selecting up to K mentions improve performance?
- Question: did you try multi-source training or domain-balanced sampling, and can you explain why word vectors underperformed Brown clusters (hyperparams/corpora)?
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept as a resource/empirical paper—valuable dataset and analysis but needs clearer ethical handling and ideally at least one modern neural baseline or released code to enable such comparisons.
