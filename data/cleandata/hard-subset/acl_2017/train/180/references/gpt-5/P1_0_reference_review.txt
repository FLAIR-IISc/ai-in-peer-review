Summary and contributions
This paper introduces a new IE task and dataset for identifying the products being bought/sold in online cybercrime marketplaces. The authors annotate token-level product mentions (including integral method tokens) across four forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled), propose multiple evaluation views (token/type/post-level; token vs. NP-projected phrases), and benchmark simple models: (i) a token/NP SVM classifier and (ii) a post-level latent SVM that selects one product mention per post. They study cross-forum “fine-grained domain adaptation” using Brown clusters, small type-level gazetteers, and frustratingly easy domain features, and analyze OOV effects.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) Dataset and task design for product extraction in cybercrime forums, with clear guidelines, multiple evaluation metrics (token/type/post-level; NP projection), and inter-annotator agreement. Primary contribution.
2) Empirical characterization of fine-grained domain shift across forums, including negative results for common adaptation methods and an informative OOV/coverage analysis that explains asymmetric adaptation. Strong empirical contribution.
3) A simple post-level latent SVM that achieves high post-level accuracy and is practically useful for whole-forum analysis. Secondary methodological/engineering contribution.

Strengths
- Novel and impactful domain + released resources: The dataset targets a high-stakes, understudied domain (cybercrime marketplaces), enabling downstream security/social-science analyses. The promise to release data, models, and code greatly enhances impact and reproducibility.
- Careful task formulation and metrics: The authors thoughtfully define multiple evaluation views aligned with different use cases (token-level, product type per post, and post-level accuracy), and motivate NP-level projection to capture multiword products. This clarity helps interpret trade-offs and informs system design.
- Thorough empirical study with honest negative results: The paper systematically compares within-forum vs. cross-forum transfer, shows large cross-domain drops, and probes classic adaptation (Brown clusters, gazetteers, domain features). The OOV-based analysis is insightful (e.g., Darkode’s broader product coverage drives asymmetric adaptation), and statistical testing is used appropriately.
- Practical post-level modeling: The latent SVM that selects a single product per post yields ~80 F1 on product types and ~90% post-level accuracy within-forum, which is strong enough to enable automatic forum-wide analyses; it also illustrates that extracting one salient product often suffices.
- Annotation process and agreement: Iterative guideline development, multiple annotators, adjudication, and moderate agreement (Fleiss’ κ≈0.58–0.77) on noisy data suggest reasonable reliability for a difficult, context-sensitive task.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and baselines: The core models (linear SVMs; Daumé ’07; Brown clusters) are standard. The paper asserts an SVM performs similarly to a CRF but does not report CRF or stronger neural sequence labeling baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character features; contextualized encoders), nor domain-adversarial or self-training methods. This weakens claims about the difficulty of adaptation and the generality of findings.
- Annotation scope conflates concepts: The guidelines annotate products and sometimes “integral” method/agent tokens (e.g., hack, coder). This blurs the target concept, increases ambiguity, and plausibly harms cross-domain generalization. No analysis separates these categories or quantifies how often non-product method/agent tokens are labeled.
- Evaluation via NP projection relies on an automatic news-trained parser: No parser accuracy is reported on this noisy domain. The NP length cap and projection heuristics may both introduce errors and partially inflate NP-level performance by erasing token distinctions. A sanity check on parser quality or manual span evaluation would strengthen confidence.
- Dataset scale and sampling details: The annotated sets are modest (≈700–900 posts for training per main forum; test-only for two forums), and all annotators are authors, which may introduce bias. The sampling strategy (subforums, time) is not described; temporal or topical drift might confound generalization. Agreement is reported only for token-level binary labels; span-level or category-specific agreement is unknown.
- Adaptation space underexplored: The negative results for Brown clusters (50 clusters) and small gazetteers could stem from limited feature capacity. The study does not explore richer unlabeled-data use (e.g., contextual embedding pretraining/fine-tuning on target forums, self-training/co-training, label propagation), multi-source adaptation, adversarial/domain-invariant encoders, or normalization of product lexicons, all of which are natural fits for the stated hypotheses.

Questions to authors
- Release: Will datasets (including raw text or tokenized/anonymized versions), detailed guidelines, and code be released, and under what license/access constraints given forum TOS and ethics?
- Sampling: How were posts selected for annotation (by subforum/topic/time)? Are splits temporally stratified? Could temporal drift be driving some cross-forum degradation?
- Model comparisons: Can you report results for a CRF and/or a BiLSTM(-CRF) with character features, and a simple contextual embedding baseline (e.g., fine-tuned encoder with a token/post head)? This would help bound how much of the gap is due to model capacity vs. domain shift.
- Annotation categories: Can you provide breakdowns separating “product” vs. “method/agent” tokens (and their frequencies) and report performance per category? Would a label inventory that distinguishes these improve consistency and transfer?
- Parser sanity check: Do you have any manual assessment of NP projection quality on this data, or an alternative span evaluation to validate NP-level metrics?
- Exclusion rule: What is the estimated recall loss from excluding mentions outside the first/last 10 lines? Any evidence this rule doesn’t bias the models toward title-like mentions?

Additional comments
- The reference list has formatting issues and incomplete entries; please clean up. 
- Consider reporting per-forum error analyses (e.g., POS, morphology/orthography, title vs. body) and including an ablation on character features given misspellings.
- Normalization to canonical product types (where possible) and demonstrating end-task utility (e.g., trend analysis, price linkage) would further strengthen impact.

Overall, this is a valuable dataset and empirical study of a timely problem. Strengthening baselines and clarifying annotation/evaluation choices would make it a strong accept; as is, it is a solid resource paper with insightful analysis.
