Summary and main contributions
- A new annotated dataset of marketplace posts from multiple cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled), with token-level labels marking references to the primary product(s) being bought/sold. The task is framed as fine-grained, context-sensitive product identification.
- An evaluation protocol with multiple granularities (token-level, NP-level via parser projection, per-post product-type sets, and post-level “any correct product” accuracy), arguing for application-driven metrics.
- Empirical study of within-forum vs cross-forum performance using simple supervised models and lightweight adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, gazetteers from frequent product types, and feature duplication per Daumé 2007), concluding that cross-forum adaptation remains challenging.

Strengths
- Dataset novelty and potential impact: Curating annotated data from underground cybercrime marketplaces is non-trivial and valuable to both NLP and security research. The focus on context-sensitive, product-specific mentions (rather than generic NER) addresses a real need for downstream analyses (market composition, trends, user profiling).
- Clear, multi-view evaluation: The paper thoughtfully defines multiple metrics reflecting different application needs (token vs NP vs post-level), and shows how design choices (e.g., post-level latent selection) align with evaluation goals.
- Transparent negative/limited adaptation results: The study carefully documents that straightforward semi-supervised signals (Brown clusters) and small gazetteers yield only modest gains, and that token-supervised adaptation yields asymmetric benefits depending on source/target forum. This is useful empirical evidence for the community.
- Reasonable annotation process and agreement reporting: The guidelines, multi-round adjudication, use of multiple annotators, and explicit reporting of Fleiss’ Kappa (0.58–0.77 across conditions) demonstrate attention to annotation quality in a messy domain.
- Release plan of data, models, and code (stated): If followed through, this will provide a practical testbed for domain adaptation and IE under distribution shift.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological rigor and outdated baselines: The modeling is restricted to linear SVMs and a latent SVM selector. There is no comparison to established strong sequence labeling baselines (CRF), nor to widely adopted neural taggers (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with pretrained embeddings, character features) or contextual encoders (ELMo/BERT). Similarly, domain adaptation baselines omit standard, well-studied techniques (e.g., instance reweighting/importance weighting, self-training, tri-training, pivot-based feature transfer, adversarial domain-invariant representations). Without such comparisons, the strength of the conclusions about adaptation difficulty is unclear.
- Annotation design compromises utility and introduce confounds: Only heads of product NPs are annotated, then projected to phrases using an out-of-domain parser to evaluate at NP level. This creates a dependency on parser errors (especially on noisy forum text) and blurs whether gains come from tagging vs parsing. Span-level annotation would remove this confound and make the dataset more broadly useful. Furthermore, limiting annotation to the first/last 10 lines may systematically exclude product mentions embedded in long posts; the justification is empirical but the potential bias is not fully quantified.
- Scale and coverage are modest, with moderate IAA: Training data per domain is small (hundreds of posts), and kappas around 0.6 indicate only moderate agreement for a binary token task. The authors acknowledge domain expertise was uneven across annotators and that all annotators were authors, which raises concerns about bias and reproducibility. The small annotated sets also constrain the strength of conclusions regarding domain adaptation.
- Ethical/legal considerations are not addressed: The paper does not discuss IRB/ethics review, data acquisition legality, potential PII, de-identification, terms-of-service compliance, and conditions for dataset release. Given the sensitive nature of cybercrime forums, this omission is serious and must be remedied before public release.
- Incomplete analysis and ablations: While OOV analyses are informative, the paper lacks category-wise error analysis (e.g., product classes, verbs vs nouns), impact of the parser on NP projection accuracy, and per-forum lexical vs stylistic differences. Statistical significance is reported only sporadically; many claims rely on small absolute differences.

Questions to authors
- Data release and ethics: What IRB/ethics approvals and legal reviews were obtained? How is PII handled? What de-identification, redaction, or access controls will be applied upon release? Are forum ToS respected?
- Span vs head annotation: Why not annotate product spans directly? Can you quantify how often parser projection changes gold boundaries and how much it affects scores? Could you provide span-level annotations (even for a subset) to benchmark parsing-induced noise?
- Stronger baselines: Can you include comparisons to CRF and BiLSTM-CRF with pretrained embeddings and character features, and to a contextual model (ELMo/BERT) fine-tuned on source domains? These are established baselines in IE/NER and necessary to interpret the difficulty of cross-forum transfer.
- Domain adaptation: Why were standard methods like self-training on unlabeled target, importance weighting, pivot-based feature induction, or adversarial representation learning not evaluated? Given you have substantial unlabeled data, these seem directly applicable and well-established.
- Annotation scope: The “first/last 10 lines” heuristic excludes content in many long posts. Can you quantify the fraction of product mentions excluded by this rule on a manually inspected subset?
- Reproducibility details: Please clarify exact training sizes per split, SVM/regularization hyperparameters, dictionary/gazetteer construction (e.g., stemming algorithm), and whether Brown clusters used unlabeled data from target test forums. If so, report results when clusters are trained only on source-domain unlabeled data.

Overall, the dataset addresses an important, under-resourced domain and the evaluation framing is sensible. However, the current submission falls short on methodological rigor and ethical transparency. Strengthening baselines with established modern methods, expanding analysis, and providing a clear ethics/data-release plan would substantially improve the paper.
