- Presents a novel annotated dataset of product mentions from four cybercrime forums for token-level information extraction.  
- Frames the task as challenging due to noisy text and significant fine-grained domain shift between forums.  
- Empirically evaluates several baseline and adaptation methods, finding limited success and characterizing domain adaptation difficulty.

- Main contribution: creation and public release of a valuable cybercrime forum dataset filling a resource gap.  
- Main contribution: clear problem formulation and characterization of fine-grained domain adaptation even within the cybercrime domain.  
- Main contribution: thorough empirical evaluation that highlights why existing adaptation techniques struggle.

- Strength: dataset is hard-to-obtain, well-annotated, and useful for the research community.  
- Strength: task is well-motivated and appropriately framed as token-level IE for cybercrime product identification.  
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation with clear metrics and insightful analyses (e.g., OOV rates).  
- Strength: honest, realistic assessment of limitations and challenges for future work.  
- Strength: paper is well-written, organized, and includes a useful ablation study.

- Weakness: methods explored are relatively standard and lack methodological novelty.  
- Weakness: insufficient detailed error analysis explaining why specific methods fail and which product types are misclassified.  
- Weakness: inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' kappa 0.58–0.77) is only moderate, suggesting annotation ambiguity.  
- Weakness: lack of comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., fine-tuned BERT or other pre-trained LMs).  
- Weakness: limited scope of adaptation techniques; more advanced methods (adversarial training, meta-learning) were not explored.

- Question: please detail types of annotation disagreements and particularly challenging product categories or constructions.  
- Question: what is the computational cost and scalability of the gazetteer approach as forums increase?  
- Question: have you performed error analysis to identify consistent error patterns across forums?  
- Question: why was Stanford CoreNLP chosen for tokenization/sentence splitting, and were alternative tokenizers considered for forum text?  
- Question: given limited success of current adaptation methods, what future directions, methods, or data augmentation strategies do you recommend?

- Additional: decision to release dataset and code is commendable.  
- Additional: paper is readable and well-structured.  
- Additional: ablation study in Section 4.1 is helpful.

- Overall assessment: accept-worthy with minor revisions; dataset and evaluation are significant despite limited methodological novelty.
