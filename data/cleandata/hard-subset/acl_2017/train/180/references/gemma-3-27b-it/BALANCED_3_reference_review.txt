This paper introduces a new dataset and task focused on identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime marketplaces. The authors annotate token-level references to products across four forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, and Nulled) and explore the challenges of domain adaptation when applying models trained on one forum to another. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, along with a thorough empirical characterization of the domain adaptation problem in this setting. A secondary contribution lies in highlighting the difficulties of applying standard domain adaptation techniques to this specific task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Valuable Dataset:** The created dataset is a significant contribution. Data from cybercrime forums is notoriously difficult to obtain and analyze, and a labeled dataset focused on product identification fills a crucial gap. This dataset will likely serve as a valuable resource for researchers in cybersecurity, NLP, and information extraction. The descriptive statistics provided in Table 1 are helpful and demonstrate the dataset’s characteristics.
2. **Realistic and Challenging Task:** Identifying products in these forums is a naturally complex task. The authors correctly point out the messiness of the text, the context-dependence of product mentions, and the fine-grained domain differences between forums. These characteristics make the task genuinely challenging and relevant to real-world security applications.
3. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, considering different evaluation metrics (token-level, type-level, post-level) and exploring several baseline models and adaptation techniques. The analysis of performance across different forums and the investigation of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) product terms provide valuable insights into the challenges of domain adaptation.
4. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and motivates its importance. The connection to real-world applications (characterizing forums, identifying trends, associating users with activity) is well-established.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement with Adaptation Techniques:** While the paper demonstrates that standard domain adaptation methods struggle, the lack of substantial improvement from techniques like Brown Clusters or type-level annotation is somewhat disappointing. The analysis of *why* these methods fail is insightful, but a stronger exploration of alternative adaptation strategies would have been beneficial. The results suggest that the domain shift is particularly severe, but the paper doesn’t fully capitalize on investigating solutions specifically tailored to this magnitude of shift.
2. **Evaluation Metric Complexity:** The presentation of multiple evaluation metrics, while comprehensive, can be confusing. The rationale for each metric is explained, but the reader needs to carefully consider the implications of each one when interpreting the results. Streamlining the evaluation or focusing on a primary metric with secondary analyses would improve clarity.
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the errors made by the models. Identifying common types of errors (e.g., misidentification of product features, handling of ambiguous terms) would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the current approaches and suggest directions for future research.
4. **Annotation Guidelines Detail:** While the authors state that annotation guidelines will be available in supplementary material, a brief overview within the paper itself would be beneficial. Understanding the nuances of the annotation process is crucial for interpreting the results and assessing the quality of the dataset.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper states that annotators had expertise in either NLP or computer security. How did this expertise distribution impact the annotation process, and were there any observed differences in annotation patterns based on background?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific reasons why the standard domain adaptation techniques (Brown Clusters, type-level annotation) failed to provide significant improvements? Were there any unexpected challenges or limitations encountered during these experiments?
3.  Given the importance of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) product terms, did you consider any techniques for handling these terms, such as subword modeling or character-level embeddings?
4.  You mention that many posts contain multiple annotated tokens that are variations of the same product. How robust are the evaluation metrics to these variations, and what steps were taken to mitigate potential biases?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to “anon. work in press” is vague. Providing more detail, if possible, would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of each forum (e.g., type of products, level of technical expertise, forum rules) to provide context for the domain adaptation results.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The dataset is a significant resource, and the empirical analysis provides important insights into the challenges of applying NLP techniques to the domain of online cybercrime. While the lack of substantial improvement with adaptation techniques is a limitation, the authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the underlying reasons. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
