This paper presents a new dataset of posts from online cybercrime marketplaces, annotated with product references, and frames this as a fine-grained domain adaptation task. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, which offers a valuable resource for researchers interested in analyzing cybercrime activity. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of the challenges inherent in applying NLP models trained on one cybercrime forum to another, highlighting the domain-specific nature of this data. Finally, the exploration of several adaptation methods, while ultimately unsuccessful, provides a useful benchmark and motivation for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Dataset:** The annotated dataset is the strongest aspect of this work. The cybercrime domain is notoriously difficult to access, and a publicly available, well-annotated dataset will undoubtedly be useful for a variety of research tasks beyond the specific product identification task presented here. The detailed statistics provided (Table 1) and the example annotations (Figure 1) are helpful for understanding the characteristics of the data.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly define the task and motivate its relevance to cybersecurity research. The connection to information extraction tasks like slot filling and NER is well-articulated, and the justification for token-level annotation is reasonable.
3. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation, comparing several baselines and adaptation methods across multiple forums. The use of multiple evaluation metrics (token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, post-level accuracy) demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the task.
4. **Honest Assessment of Results:** The authors are refreshingly honest about the limited success of their adaptation attempts. They acknowledge the challenges and offer plausible explanations for why existing methods fail to generalize well to this domain. This realism is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** The adaptation methods explored (type-level annotation, token-level annotation, semi-supervised approaches) are all well-established techniques in NLP. While their application to this specific domain is valuable, the methodological innovation is minimal. The paper lacks a compelling justification for *why* these methods were expected to succeed, given their known limitations.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While several baselines are included, they are relatively simple. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art NER or information extraction models (even without adaptation) would provide a stronger baseline for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. Why not adapt a strong pre-trained language model (BERT, RoBERTa) using simple fine-tuning as a baseline?
3. **Overemphasis on Token-Level Evaluation:** While token-level accuracy is a valid metric, the authors acknowledge that type-level or post-level information may be more relevant for many applications. The paper spends considerable effort optimizing for token-level performance, which feels somewhat disconnected from the stated goals of characterizing forums and identifying trends.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the different systems. Understanding *why* the models fail to identify products in certain cases could provide valuable insights for future research. What types of products are particularly difficult to identify? Are there specific linguistic patterns that cause confusion?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the domain expertise of the annotators? What specific security knowledge did they possess, and how did this influence the annotation process?
2.  You mention that the annotation guide will be available in supplementary material. Could you provide a high-level overview of the key decisions and edge cases covered in the guidelines?
3.  Why were more sophisticated domain adaptation techniques (e.g., adversarial training, domain-specific embeddings) not explored? 
4.  Given the limitations of the adaptation methods, what are the most promising directions for future research in this area?
