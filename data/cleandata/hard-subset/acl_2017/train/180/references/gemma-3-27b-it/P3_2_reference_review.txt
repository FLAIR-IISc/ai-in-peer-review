## Review for "Identifying Products in Online Cybercrime Marketplaces: A Dataset and Fine-grained Domain Adaptation Task"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a new dataset of posts from four online cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, and Nulled) annotated with product mentions. The authors frame this as a token-level information extraction task, similar to NER but with a strong reliance on context and significant data messiness. They explore the challenges of domain adaptation, finding that models trained on one forum perform poorly on others, and investigate several adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, type-level annotation, semi-supervised learning) with limited success. The primary contribution of this work is the publicly available dataset, alongside a characterization of the difficulties inherent in applying NLP techniques to this sensitive and rapidly evolving domain.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a labeled dataset of cybercrime forum posts is the most significant contribution.  This dataset will be invaluable for researchers in cybersecurity, NLP, and potentially criminology. The task itself is well-motivated and the annotation guidelines, while messy to create (as evidenced by the iterative process), appear reasonable.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Domain Shift:** The paper clearly demonstrates the substantial domain shift between even relatively similar cybercrime forums.  This is a crucial finding, highlighting the limitations of simply transferring models across platforms.
3. **Benchmark & Initial Exploration of Adaptation Techniques:**  The authors provide a baseline evaluation of several domain adaptation techniques. While the results are largely negative, they establish a benchmark for future research and a useful starting point for investigating more sophisticated methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **High Relevance & Impact:** The task addressed is highly relevant to security research. Automated analysis of cybercrime forums can provide valuable insights into emerging threats and criminal activities.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation using multiple metrics (token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, post-level accuracy) and a well-defined experimental setup, including train/dev/test splits. The analysis of OOV rates is particularly insightful.
3. **Detailed Dataset Description:** The paper provides sufficient detail about the dataset, including forum statistics, annotation process, and inter-annotator agreement.  The decision to annotate at the token level, while increasing complexity, is well-justified.
4. **Realistic Problem Framing:** The authors acknowledge and address the challenges inherent in working with noisy, informal text characteristic of online forums. The careful consideration of annotation nuances (e.g., annotating verbs as methods) demonstrates a good understanding of the data.
5. **Open Science:** The commitment to releasing the dataset, models, and code is commendable and will greatly benefit the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** The domain adaptation techniques explored are relatively standard and have been applied extensively in other NLP settings. The paper doesn’t present a novel adaptation method, and the lack of significant improvement suggests the standard techniques are insufficient for this unique domain. The analysis of *why* these methods fail is more valuable than the results themselves, but a more exploratory approach to adaptation might have been beneficial.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** While the authors mention the difficulty of handling ambiguous product mentions, a more in-depth error analysis would be helpful.  Understanding the types of errors the models make (e.g., confusion between similar products, misidentification of features as products) could guide the development of more effective techniques.
3. **Small Dataset Size (for some forums):**  Blackhat and Nulled have extremely small datasets (167 and 39,118 posts respectively). This limits the generalizability of the findings and the effectiveness of the learned models.  The small size of Blackhat makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the performance of the adaptation techniques on that forum. While gathering data from these forums is inherently difficult, the imbalance should be acknowledged more prominently.
4. **Focus on NP-level evaluation *after* token-level annotation:**  The decision to annotate at the token level is justified, but the shift to NP-level evaluation feels somewhat *post hoc*. If the primary goal is type-level product extraction, a span-based annotation scheme might have been more efficient and directly applicable to evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the difficulty of annotating multi-word expressions.  Could you provide more detail on how you handled cases where a product name spans multiple sentences?
2.  The paper states that annotators were researchers in NLP *or* computer security. Were there noticeable differences in annotation consistency between annotators with different backgrounds?
3.  Given the limitations of the explored adaptation techniques, what specific characteristics of cybercrime forum data (beyond messiness and domain shift) do you believe pose the greatest challenges for NLP? 
4.  The analysis points to Darkode being a more “general” forum.  Do you have hypotheses as to *why* this is the case (e.g., broader product range, more standardized language)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of evaluation metrics is excellent and demonstrates a careful consideration of the different perspectives on this task.
*   The introduction could be strengthened by providing concrete examples of how this research could be used in real-world cybersecurity applications.
*   Consider providing a more detailed breakdown of the annotation time per post to give readers a sense of the effort involved in creating the dataset.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a valuable contribution to the field. The dataset is a significant resource, and the paper provides a compelling analysis of the challenges of applying NLP techniques to cybercrime forum data. While the methodological contributions are limited, the clear presentation, rigorous evaluation, and open science approach make this a strong submission worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
