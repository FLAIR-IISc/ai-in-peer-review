This paper presents a new dataset of posts from online cybercrime marketplaces annotated with product references, framing the task as a fine-grained domain adaptation problem. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, along with a detailed analysis of the challenges associated with applying NLP techniques to this domain, specifically the significant variability between different cybercrime forums. A secondary contribution is the thorough evaluation of several domain adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, type-level annotation, and target-domain data augmentation) and a clear demonstration of their limited effectiveness in this setting. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation metrics (token-level, type-level, and post-level) provide a useful framework for future work in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Valuable Dataset:** The creation of a labeled dataset for identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces is a significant contribution. This type of data is difficult to obtain and highly valuable for security researchers. The dataset statistics (size, annotation agreement) are well-presented and indicate a reasonable quality. The authors explicitly state the dataset and code will be made available, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.
2. **Realistic Task Formulation and Analysis:** The framing of the task as a token-level information extraction problem, acknowledging the messiness of forum data and the context-dependence of product identification, is well-motivated and reflects the realities of the domain. The detailed discussion of the challenges—domain shift between forums, noisy data, and the need for nuanced understanding—is insightful.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper goes beyond standard metrics (token-level F1) and introduces type-level and post-level evaluations that better reflect the potential downstream applications of this technology (e.g., identifying trends in product offerings). The ablation studies with different adaptation techniques and clear presentation of results are valuable.
4. **Clear Error Analysis:** The analysis revealing that adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode is more successful than vice-versa, linked to the broader product coverage of Darkode, provides crucial insights into the nature of the domain adaptation problem. This highlights the importance of the source domain selection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Effectiveness of Adaptation Techniques:** While the paper thoroughly *evaluates* adaptation techniques, the results are largely negative (minimal improvement). This isn't necessarily a flaw, but the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *why* these standard techniques fail. More exploration of potentially more effective adaptation strategies (e.g., adversarial training, meta-learning) could strengthen the work.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set used in the binary classifier and post-level classifier is relatively basic. While the paper focuses on domain adaptation, exploring more sophisticated features (e.g., forum-specific lexicons, embeddings trained on forum data) might improve performance and reduce the need for extensive adaptation.
3. **Gazetteer Construction:** The method for constructing the gazetteer (frequent tokens in the training set) is simplistic and could be improved. For example, filtering by part-of-speech or using a more sophisticated term extraction method might yield a more effective gazetteer.
4. **Limited Scope of Adaptation:** The domain adaptation experiments focus solely on transferring between forums. It would be valuable to investigate adaptation from more general domains (e.g., e-commerce websites) to these specialized cybercrime forums.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. You mention that the annotation guide will be available in supplementary material. Could you provide some examples of the types of edge cases or ambiguities that were addressed in the annotation guidelines?
2. Given the limited success of standard domain adaptation techniques, what is your intuition for why this task is particularly challenging, beyond the domain shift between forums? Are there inherent characteristics of cybercrime language that make it difficult to model?
3. In Section 5.3, you found that adding labeled data from Hack Forums to Darkode improved performance, while the reverse was not true. Is this due to the inherent characteristics of the forums themselves (e.g., the types of products being sold, the language used), or is it simply a matter of dataset size?
4. Could you elaborate on why the domain-specific features (Daume III, 2007) harmed performance when adapting from Darkode to Hack Forums? What specific features seemed to be most detrimental?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The related work section is adequate, but could be expanded to include more recent work on domain adaptation and information extraction from noisy text.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of the ethical considerations of working with data from cybercrime forums.
