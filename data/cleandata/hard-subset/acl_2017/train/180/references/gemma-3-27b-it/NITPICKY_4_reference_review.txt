The paper presents a new dataset of posts from online cybercrime marketplaces, annotated with product references. The authors define a token-level annotation task and explore the challenges of applying models trained on one forum to another, highlighting a fine-grained domain adaptation problem. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, along with a thorough empirical analysis of the challenges in adapting models across these domains. A secondary contribution is the exploration of several domain adaptation techniques, demonstrating their limited effectiveness in this setting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Dataset:** The dataset is a significant contribution. Data from cybercrime forums is notoriously difficult to obtain and analyze, and making this resource publicly available will be beneficial to researchers in cybersecurity, NLP, and information extraction. The dataset’s size and the detailed token-level annotations are particularly commendable.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The task of identifying products being bought and sold in these forums is well-defined and relevant. The authors convincingly argue that this task is distinct from standard NER and slot filling, due to the context-dependent nature of product references and the noisy nature of forum text.
3. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation, exploring various baselines and adaptation techniques. The analysis of performance across different forums and the investigation of OOV rates provide valuable insights into the challenges of domain adaptation in this context. The inclusion of both token-level and post-level evaluation metrics demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of different application scenarios.
4. **Realistic Assessment:** The authors are refreshingly honest about the limitations of the explored adaptation methods. They acknowledge the lack of significant improvements and offer plausible explanations for why these methods fail to have a larger impact, which suggests a deep understanding of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Novelty in Methods:** While the empirical evaluation is thorough, the methods explored for domain adaptation (Brown clusters, type-level annotation, simple binary classifiers) are relatively standard and lack significant novelty. The paper doesn’t propose any new techniques to address the specific challenges of this domain. The “frustratingly easy” domain adaptation from Daume III (2007) is a well-known approach and its application here doesn’t constitute a significant advance.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. While the authors identify OOV rates, they don’t provide specific examples of the types of errors the models make and the reasons behind these errors. Understanding these errors could lead to more targeted improvements. Why are specific products misidentified, and what features are contributing to these mistakes?
3. **Annotation Guidelines Detail:** The paper mentions extensive development of annotation guidelines, but the full guidelines are only available in supplementary material. Important details regarding complex annotation cases (e.g., distinguishing between products and features) should be integrated directly into the main text to enhance reproducibility and clarity. Specifically, the examples provided feel a bit arbitrary and more details on edge cases would be beneficial.
4. **Minor Formatting/Presentation Issues:** Table 1 is slightly confusing with the presentation of train/development/test splits. The use of slashes is not immediately clear. The layout and readability could be improved. The phrase “Overall, our results…” in the conclusion feels abrupt and could be better integrated into a more summarizing statement.
5. **Inconsistent Reporting of Statistical Significance:** The text refers to "statistically significant gains" (e.g. in Section 5.2) without consistently reporting p-values or significance levels.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more examples of challenging cases in the annotation process and explain how the annotation guidelines address these challenges?
2. What specific types of errors are the models making most frequently, and what features seem to be contributing to these errors?
3. Given the limited effectiveness of the explored adaptation techniques, what are your thoughts on alternative approaches, such as leveraging pre-trained language models or exploring more sophisticated domain adaptation methods?
4. Could you clarify the train/development/test splits in Table 1 with a more explicit presentation?
