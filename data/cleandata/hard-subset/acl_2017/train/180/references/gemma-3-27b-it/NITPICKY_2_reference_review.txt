This paper presents a new dataset of posts from online cybercrime marketplaces annotated with product references, framing the task as a fine-grained domain adaptation problem. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, alongside a thorough empirical analysis demonstrating the challenges of applying models trained on one cybercrime forum to another. A secondary contribution is the exploration of several domain adaptation techniques – type-level annotation, Brown clustering, and limited target-domain labeling – and a clear demonstration of their limited effectiveness in this setting. A final, though less substantial, contribution is the detailed evaluation metrics proposed, acknowledging the nuances of this task beyond standard NER or IE evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Value:** The dataset is genuinely valuable. Access to data from these forums is difficult, and a labeled dataset specifically targeting product identification is a significant resource for the security research community. The dataset statistics (size, annotation agreement) appear reasonable.
2. **Thorough Empirical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous empirical evaluation across multiple forums and adaptation strategies. The authors don’t simply present a method; they systematically investigate *why* existing approaches fail, offering insights into the specific challenges of this domain. The analysis of OOV rates is particularly insightful.
3. **Realistic Task Formulation:** Framing the task as fine-grained domain adaptation accurately reflects the real-world scenario. The observation that even different cybercrime forums exhibit distinct linguistic characteristics is important and well-supported by the experimental results.
4. **Careful Consideration of Evaluation:** The authors thoughtfully discuss and propose multiple evaluation metrics, recognizing that the optimal metric depends on the downstream application. The move toward post-level evaluation is justified and practical.
5. **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction motivates the problem effectively, and the experimental setup is described in detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methods:** The domain adaptation methods explored (Brown clusters, type-level annotation) are well-established and not particularly novel. While the paper’s value lies in the *application* to this specific domain and the analysis of results, the methodological contribution is minimal. Simply stating these methods "fail to have a larger impact" isn't sufficient; a more detailed explanation of *why* they fail given the specific characteristics of the data would be stronger.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The baselines presented are extremely simple (frequency, dictionary lookup, first noun phrase). A stronger baseline, such as a pre-trained language model fine-tuned on a related corpus, would provide a more challenging benchmark and better contextualize the performance of the proposed approaches.
3. **Annotation Granularity & Consistency:** The annotation principle of annotating "either the product that will be delivered or are an integral part of the method" seems prone to subjectivity and inconsistencies. The example in Figure 1 is illustrative, but the distinction between "product" and "method" appears blurry in other potential scenarios. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion but do not offer a quantitative assessment of annotation disagreements related to this ambiguity.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper analyzes OOV rates, a more detailed error analysis identifying common types of errors (e.g., misidentification of product features, inability to handle slang or abbreviations) would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. **Minor Formatting Issues:** Table 1 uses inconsistent formatting for agreement columns (all-annotated vs 3-annotated) and should be standardized. The footnote referencing supplementary material is awkwardly placed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more examples of annotation disagreements, specifically focusing on cases where the line between “product” and “method” is unclear? How were these disagreements resolved?
2. What is the average inter-annotator agreement when only considering posts with multiple product mentions?
3. Could you elaborate on the specific reasons why the "frustratingly easy" domain adaptation method of Daume III (2007) *harmed* performance when adapting from Darkode to Hack Forums? Is this related to overfitting, as you suggest, or other factors?
4. Given the limitations of the explored adaptation techniques, what are your thoughts on exploring more sophisticated methods like adversarial domain adaptation or meta-learning?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper refers to “anon. work in press.” Please provide more details or a placeholder citation if possible.
* The discussion of Fleiss’ Kappa and the justification for its use seems overly detailed and slightly defensive.
* The phrase "frustratingly easy" is parenthetical and unnecessary.



Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a valuable resource. However, the lack of methodological novelty and the aforementioned weaknesses prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing these concerns, particularly a more robust baseline comparison and a more detailed error analysis, is needed.
