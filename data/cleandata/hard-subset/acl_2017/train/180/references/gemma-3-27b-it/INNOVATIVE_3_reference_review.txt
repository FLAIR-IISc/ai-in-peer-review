This paper introduces a new dataset of annotated posts from online cybercrime marketplaces, focusing on identifying products being bought and sold. The primary contribution is this dataset itself, providing a challenging and unique resource for NLP research in a domain largely unexplored – the dark web. A secondary, though less impactful, contribution is the demonstration of the significant domain adaptation challenges present even *between* different cybercrime forums. The authors also provide a reasonable exploration of baseline methods and adaptation techniques, though these are not the focus.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Dataset:** The dataset is genuinely novel. Existing NLP datasets rarely address the specific characteristics of cybercrime forums – their unique language, structure, and security concerns. This dataset fills a critical gap and will undoubtedly enable research in areas like threat intelligence, security monitoring, and understanding criminal economies.
2. **Interesting Domain Adaptation Challenge:** The finding that even seemingly similar cybercrime forums present significant domain adaptation challenges is insightful. This highlights the need for robust, adaptable NLP models for this domain and provides a compelling motivation for further research.  The observation that simple transfer learning fails underlines the depth of the problem.
3. **Clear Problem Definition and Annotation Scheme:** The authors clearly define the task – token-level product identification – and provide a detailed description of their annotation process.  The annotation guidelines, even in summary form, appear well-considered, balancing precision with practicality (e.g., annotating the head of noun phrases).
4. **Practical Relevance:**  The ability to automatically identify products in these marketplaces has direct real-world applications. Understanding what is being traded, tracking trends, and identifying actors are all valuable for law enforcement and security professionals.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Adaptation Techniques:** While the authors explore some adaptation methods, their evaluation feels somewhat cursory. The failure of the techniques tested is noted, but the reasons are not deeply investigated.  More advanced adaptation strategies (e.g., adversarial training, meta-learning) could be explored, even if only briefly.
2. **Evaluation Metric Complexity:** The paper presents a variety of evaluation metrics, some of which appear overly complex and may not reflect the primary practical use cases. While acknowledging the nuances of the task is good, focusing on a few key, interpretable metrics would strengthen the results presentation. The reliance on token-level F1 alongside post-level accuracy feels somewhat disjointed.
3. **Superficial Analysis of Errors:** While the authors note the OOV issue, a deeper dive into the *types* of out-of-vocabulary products and the reasons for misclassification would be valuable.  Are there specific categories of products that are particularly difficult to identify? What linguistic features contribute to the errors?
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The baselines used (frequency, dictionary lookup) are relatively weak. Comparing against a pre-trained language model fine-tuned on a related (though not directly cybercrime) corpus would provide a more informative comparison point.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more detail on the types of errors made by the models? Specifically, what categories of products are most frequently misclassified, and why?
2. Given the high prevalence of specialized terminology in these forums, have you considered incorporating external knowledge resources (e.g., cybersecurity glossaries) into your models?
3. What are the limitations of the annotation process? Were there any specific challenges or ambiguities that arose during annotation that might affect the quality or consistency of the dataset?
4. The paper mentions "anonymized case studies." Could you provide a high-level overview of how this dataset has been used to enable these studies, without compromising confidentiality?

**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material containing the full annotation guidelines is critical. Please ensure this is comprehensive and clearly explains all annotation decisions. The mention of "all models and code" being available is a strong positive.
