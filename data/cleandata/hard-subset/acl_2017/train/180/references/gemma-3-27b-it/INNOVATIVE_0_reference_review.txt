This paper introduces a novel dataset of posts from online cybercrime marketplaces, annotated with references to products being bought and sold. The core contribution is framing this as a fine-grained domain adaptation problem, highlighting the significant distributional differences *between* cybercrime forums. While the proposed solutions for adaptation don’t yield substantial improvements, the paper clearly articulates the challenge and provides a valuable resource for future research. A secondary, but important, contribution is the careful analysis of annotation challenges in this messy, informal text, and the resulting reasonable inter-annotator agreement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task and Dataset:** The task of identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces is genuinely novel. This isn’t just another NER or IE task; the domain-specificity and adversarial nature of the text create unique challenges. The dataset itself is a significant contribution – access to this type of data is difficult, and the annotation is valuable. The authors correctly point out the distinct challenges presented by this data (ungrammaticality, abbreviations, context dependence) which differentiates it from typical IE corpora.
2. **Clear Articulation of the Domain Adaptation Problem:** The paper persuasively demonstrates that even within the relatively narrow domain of cybercrime forums, significant domain shifts exist. This is a crucial observation, and framing the problem as “fine-grained” domain adaptation is insightful.  The empirical results showing performance degradation across forums are compelling evidence.
3. **Rigorous Annotation Process:** The authors clearly detail their iterative annotation process, including multiple rounds of refinement and discussion to establish clear guidelines.  The reported Fleiss’ Kappa scores indicate reasonable agreement, despite the inherent ambiguity of the task. The considerations made during annotation (e.g. annotating verbs as methods) show a thoughtful approach.
4. **Realistic Evaluation Metrics:** The proposal of multiple evaluation metrics – token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, and post-level accuracy – acknowledges the different requirements of potential applications and allows for a nuanced assessment of performance.  The justification for each metric is sound.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement with Adaptation Methods:** While the paper correctly identifies domain adaptation as a key challenge, the explored methods (type-level annotation, Brown clusters, gazetteers) offer only marginal improvements.  The authors acknowledge this, but it leaves the reader wanting more.  The exploration feels somewhat cursory. The lack of significant gains calls into question the effectiveness of the chosen approaches in this specific domain.
2. **Shallow Analysis of Adaptation Failure:**  The paper discusses *why* the adaptation methods failed but lacks a deep dive into error analysis. What types of errors are occurring? Are there specific product categories that are particularly challenging to adapt? Understanding the *nature* of the failures could inform more targeted adaptation strategies.
3. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The baselines are relatively simple. While this is common, stronger baselines (e.g., pre-trained language models fine-tuned on a small amount of labeled data) might provide a more challenging benchmark. A comparison to recent domain adaptation techniques from other areas of NLP (e.g., adversarial training) could also strengthen the analysis.
4. **Focus on English Only:** The cybercrime landscape is global.  Acknowledging the limitations of focusing solely on English language forums and potentially suggesting future work on multilingual models would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detail on the specific errors made by the adaptation methods? Are there particular product types that consistently cause problems?
2.  You mention that Darkode seems more “general” than Hack Forums. Can you elaborate on the specific linguistic characteristics of these forums that might explain this difference?
3.  Were there any attempts to incorporate external knowledge sources (e.g., cybercrime threat intelligence feeds) to improve product identification? If so, what were the results?
4.  Could you comment on the potential for active learning strategies to reduce the annotation burden in new domains?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's clarity and organization are excellent. The inclusion of example posts and annotation details is helpful. The decision to provide the dataset and code upon publication is commendable. I think the dataset is the major contribution here; the domain adaptation exploration is less compelling, but serves as a good starting point for future work.
