This paper introduces a new dataset of product listings from online cybercrime marketplaces, along with a token-level annotation scheme for identifying product references within these listings. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, which presents a challenging information extraction task due to the specialized language and domain shift inherent in this data. A secondary contribution is the exploration of domain adaptation techniques, though the results are largely negative. A tertiary contribution is the careful analysis of the challenges present in this domain, demonstrating the difficulties of adapting models across even subtly different cybercrime forums.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Valuable Dataset:** The dataset is a significant contribution. Data from these types of forums is difficult to obtain and valuable for security research. The annotation scheme, while complex, is well-motivated and allows for both post-level and token-level analysis. This resource is likely to be useful for a range of research beyond the specific task addressed in the paper.
2. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces and effectively motivates why automated analysis is important for security research. The connection to real-world applications (characterizing forums, identifying trends, user profiling) is well-established.
3. **Thorough Error Analysis:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the challenges presented by this data and provide a reasonable analysis of why standard domain adaptation techniques fail to achieve substantial improvements. This level of critical self-assessment is commendable.
4. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors employ a variety of evaluation metrics, acknowledging the different perspectives on what constitutes successful extraction. They also conduct thorough experiments, including cross-forum evaluation to assess generalization performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** The methodological contributions are weak. The explored domain adaptation techniques (type-level annotation, Brown clusters, gazetteers) are well-established and their failure to dramatically improve performance is not surprising. The paper lacks exploration of more recent or specialized domain adaptation methods. Simply demonstrating that existing methods *don’t* work isn't a strong research contribution in itself.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The baselines are relatively simple. While the paper acknowledges the importance of comparison to established methods, the chosen baselines (frequency, dictionary lookup) are easily outperformed and do not provide a stringent test of the proposed approach. A comparison to state-of-the-art NER or IE models, even if fine-tuned, would have been more informative.
3. **Overstated Claims of Generalizability:** The analysis of forum-specific differences is insightful, but the authors sometimes imply that these differences are insurmountable. The observation that adapting from Hack Forums to Darkode is easier than vice-versa, while interesting, does not invalidate the possibility of developing more robust domain adaptation techniques. The claim that the dataset provides a "useful testbed" feels somewhat unsupported given the limited success of the explored methods.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The impact of individual features within the binary classifier isn’t clearly established. An ablation study would help determine which features are most important and contribute most to the observed performance gains.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the potential for utilizing pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as a starting point for your models? Have you experimented with fine-tuning these models on your dataset, and if so, what were the results?
2. Given the observation that Darkode seems to be more "general" than Hack Forums, could you provide more details about the specific characteristics of the data that contribute to this difference? (e.g., vocabulary size, average sentence length, prevalence of specific product types)
3. You mention that the annotation guide will be available in supplementary material. Could you provide a brief overview of the key annotation guidelines, particularly those that were most challenging for annotators to agree on?
4. Why was the post-level system formulated as a latent SVM rather than a simpler approach, such as a binary classification model? What benefits did the latent SVM provide in this context?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of evaluation metrics is thorough and well-reasoned.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   The inclusion of inter-annotator agreement is a positive aspect of the work.
