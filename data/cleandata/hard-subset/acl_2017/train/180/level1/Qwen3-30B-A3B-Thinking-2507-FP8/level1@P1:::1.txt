

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel task of identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime marketplaces, which requires a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors create a dataset of 93,924 posts from four different cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled), with annotations for 1,938 posts. They characterize these forums as representing "fine-grained domains" within the broader cybercrime domain, showing that models trained on one forum perform poorly when applied to another. The authors evaluate several domain adaptation techniques and find they have limited effectiveness for this specific problem.

My assessment of the main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. The creation of a substantial, well-annotated dataset for a challenging NLP task in a domain with significant real-world relevance (93,924 posts, 1,938 annotated posts across four forums).
2. The characterization of fine-grained domain differences within the cybercrime domain, demonstrating that even within a narrow domain, different forums represent distinct domains requiring specialized models.
3. The identification of limitations of standard domain adaptation techniques for this specific fine-grained domain adaptation problem, which provides a clear research direction for future work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Dataset Contribution**: The dataset is a major contribution to the field, providing a rich resource for studying fine-grained domain adaptation in a challenging real-world domain. The authors demonstrate careful annotation process with multiple rounds of annotation, discussion, and resolution of disagreements, resulting in reasonable inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' Kappa 0.58-0.77). The dataset's size (93,924 posts) and coverage of four distinct forums make it valuable for future research on domain adaptation.

2. **Clear Characterization of Domain Differences**: The paper makes an important contribution by demonstrating that even within the same broad domain (cybercrime), different forums represent distinct "fine-grained domains" with different product types and stylistic conventions. The authors provide concrete evidence through performance degradation when models trained on one forum are applied to another (e.g., Hack Forums-trained model is 14.4 F1 points worse on Darkode than a Darkode-trained model). This insight goes beyond previous work on domain adaptation and provides a specific challenge for the field.

3. **Thorough Evaluation of Domain Adaptation Techniques**: The paper evaluates multiple domain adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, type-level annotation, token-level annotation) in a systematic way, providing valuable insights into why standard methods fail for this problem. The analysis of product OOV rates (Table 5) and the demonstration that Darkode is more "domain-general" than Hack Forums (explaining why adaptation from Hack Forums to Darkode is more effective) is particularly insightful.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Task Differentiation**: The paper does not sufficiently distinguish their task from existing NER or information extraction tasks. While the authors note the data is messier than classical NER corpora, they don't clearly articulate why standard NER approaches are unsuitable for this specific problem. The task description is somewhat vague, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing NER work in similar domains (e.g., social media or forum data).

2. **Limited Contextualization Within Domain Adaptation Literature**: The paper doesn't adequately situate its work within the broader literature on domain adaptation in NLP. While they cite some domain adaptation techniques, they don't compare their findings to previous work on domain adaptation in other domains (e.g., sentiment analysis, named entity recognition in different genres). This makes it difficult to assess the novelty of their findings about the limitations of domain adaptation techniques.

3. **Superficial Analysis of Domain Differences**: While the paper identifies that product inventory differences and stylistic conventions both matter, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of which factor is more important or how to address each. The paper would be stronger with a more nuanced exploration of specific stylistic differences (e.g., sentence structure, common phrases) that impact model performance across forums.

4. **Limited Domain Adaptation Techniques Evaluated**: The paper only evaluates three domain adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, type-level annotation, token-level annotation), missing opportunities to explore more recent approaches that might be better suited for this problem (e.g., adversarial training, multi-task learning, contextual embeddings). This limits the paper's contribution to the domain adaptation literature.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you think this task would compare to standard NER tasks on similar messy text (e.g., social media)? Would the same domain adaptation challenges exist, or is the cybercrime domain uniquely challenging?

2. The paper states that "posts selling multiple types of products are actually quite rare (roughly 3% of cases overall)." How does this impact the choice of evaluation metrics (e.g., type-level vs. token-level), and how would your approach change if this percentage were higher?

3. The paper identifies two hypotheses for domain differences (product inventories vs. stylistic conventions). Could you provide specific examples of stylistic differences between forums that impact model performance, and how these might be addressed in future work?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their findings relate to previous work on domain adaptation. While the authors cite some domain adaptation techniques, they don't engage with the broader literature on why these methods might fail in this specific context. A comparison to domain adaptation work in other challenging domains (e.g., biomedical text, legal documents) would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The evaluation of domain adaptation techniques is limited to a small number of methods. The authors could have explored more recent approaches, such as adversarial training or contextual embeddings, which have shown promise for domain adaptation in other settings.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution with its dataset and characterization of fine-grained domain differences in cybercrime forums. However, it could be strengthened by better situating its work within the domain adaptation literature and providing more nuanced analysis of the specific challenges. The dataset itself is a significant contribution that will likely be useful for future research on domain adaptation.
