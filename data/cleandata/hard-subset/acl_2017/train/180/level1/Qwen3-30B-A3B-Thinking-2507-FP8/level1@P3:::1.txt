

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel dataset and task for identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces, where posts from four different forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled) represent distinct fine-grained domains within the broader cybercrime domain. The authors formulate a token-level annotation task that blends slot-filling information extraction with named entity recognition, providing detailed annotation guidelines and statistics. The main contributions are:

1. **A comprehensive dataset** of 93,924 posts from four cybercrime forums with 1,938 annotated posts (5-10 posts per forum with full inter-annotator agreement), representing a significant resource for studying fine-grained domain adaptation in NLP.

2. **A new task formulation** that demonstrates the challenge of domain adaptation even within a narrow domain (cybercrime), showing that standard techniques have limited effectiveness when moving between different cybercrime forums.

3. **Empirical analysis** demonstrating that product inventories and stylistic conventions both differ across domains, with OOV product rates as high as 52% in cross-forum settings, and a detailed analysis of why standard domain adaptation techniques fail.

## Strengths

1. **Highly valuable dataset**: The dataset fills a critical gap in NLP research by providing a well-annotated corpus for a domain that has been largely unexplored. The diversity of forums (covering different product types and community cultures) is particularly valuable for studying fine-grained domain adaptation. The dataset is large enough (93,924 posts) to enable meaningful analysis, and the detailed annotation guidelines with inter-annotator agreement scores (0.58-0.77) demonstrate careful annotation effort.

2. **Strong empirical analysis of domain adaptation challenges**: The paper effectively demonstrates that even within the narrow cybercrime domain, different forums represent distinct fine-grained domains. The analysis of OOV rates (up to 52%) and performance differences between forums (e.g., Hack Forums-trained model performs 14.4 F1 worse on Darkode than Darkode-trained model) provides compelling evidence that this is a significant challenge. The authors' exploration of why standard adaptation techniques fail (e.g., Darkode-trained system better at handling Hack Forums than vice versa) is insightful.

3. **Clear task motivation and justification**: The authors thoroughly justify the need for their specific task formulation, explaining why it's different from standard NER (context sensitivity, messy forum data) and slot-filling (post-level vs. token-level). The examples provided (e.g., "Backconnect bot" vs. "mod DCIBot") effectively illustrate the nuances of their annotation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Superficial exploration of domain adaptation methods**: The paper only tests a few basic adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, type-level annotation, token-level annotation with domain-specific features) without exploring more sophisticated or recent approaches. For example, the authors don't consider adversarial training methods (e.g., Zhang et al., 2017) or contextual embedding-based adaptation that might be better suited for this task. This limits the paper's contribution to the domain adaptation literature.

2. **Inconsistent evaluation metrics**: The paper switches between token-level and NP-level evaluation without clear justification, making it difficult to compare results across sections. For instance, Table 2 reports token-level results while Table 3 reports NP-level results, and the paper doesn't adequately explain why this shift occurs or how it affects the interpretation of results.

3. **Limited analysis of linguistic differences between forums**: While the paper identifies that domain differences exist, it doesn't provide a detailed linguistic analysis of what makes these forums distinct. A deeper exploration of the specific linguistic features (e.g., differences in product naming conventions, forum-specific jargon, syntactic patterns) that cause the domain adaptation problem would strengthen the paper significantly.

4. **Lack of comparison to modern neural models**: The authors use relatively simple SVM-based models rather than exploring state-of-the-art neural sequence labeling approaches (e.g., BiLSTM-CRFs) that might better handle the challenges of this task. This is particularly surprising given the paper's focus on NLP methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you elaborate on why the Darkode-trained model performs better on Hack Forums than vice versa, given that Darkode has better overall product coverage? Is this because Darkode's product vocabulary is more general, or are there other factors at play?

2. The paper mentions that "product mentions outside of the first and last 10 lines of each post" were not annotated. How does this decision affect the task's complexity and the model's ability to generalize? Would including these mentions significantly change the results?

3. Have you considered applying more recent domain adaptation techniques such as adversarial training (Zhang et al., 2017) or contextualized embeddings (Devlin et al., 2019) to this task? If so, how would these approaches differ from the methods tested in the paper?

4. The paper states that "posts selling multiple types of products are actually quite rare (roughly 3% of cases overall)." How does this compare to other marketplace datasets (e.g., e-commerce), and what implications does this have for the generalizability of the task formulation?
