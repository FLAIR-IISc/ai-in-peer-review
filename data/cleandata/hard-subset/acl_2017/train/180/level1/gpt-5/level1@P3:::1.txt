Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces a new task and dataset for identifying the product(s) being bought/sold in cybercrime forum posts, positioned between NER and slot-filling. The authors annotate token-level product mentions (including verbs when part of the deliverable/method) across four distinct forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled), and examine within- and cross-forum generalization. They propose multiple evaluation views (token F1, per-post type-level F1, post-level accuracy), simple SVM-based token and latent post-level models, and explore fine-grained domain adaptation via Brown clusters, type-level gazetteers, and “frustratingly easy” feature augmentation, with analysis on OOV effects and domain differences.

As I see them, contributions (in order of strength):
1) A novel, publicly released dataset of cybercrime marketplace posts with fine-grained token-level product annotations across multiple “fine-grained domains” (forums), plus carefully motivated evaluation metrics.
2) An empirical characterization of cross-forum degradation and analysis showing the role of lexical OOVs and domain-specific stylistic differences; a useful, challenging testbed for domain adaptation.
3) Baseline modeling including a post-level latent SVM and evaluations of simple semi-/weakly-supervised adaptation techniques.

Strengths
1) Valuable dataset and task definition addressing a societally relevant domain. The released corpus (93,924 posts; 1,938 labeled) and clear task formulation fill a gap at the intersection of IE and NER in noisy, high-impact security data. The annotation guidelines reflect domain realities (context-sensitive “product of transaction”), and the multiple evaluation views align with practical use cases (e.g., post-level accuracy to characterize market composition).

2) Thorough and transparent evaluation protocol. The paper defines complementary metrics (token, NP/projected phrase, type-per-post, post-level accuracy), and shows how metric choice influences modeling decisions (e.g., latent post-level classifier improves post-level accuracy and type F1 despite lower token F1). Evaluations across four forums, with within- vs. cross-forum splits, show clear, quantified domain gaps (e.g., ∼14 F1 NP-level drop Darkode↔HF).

3) Careful analysis of domain shift and OOV. The OOV breakdown (Table 5) is informative: Darkode-trained models face lower OOV rates and better OOV recall than HF-trained ones, suggesting Darkode’s broader product coverage. This explains asymmetric adaptation gains (HF→Darkode easier than Darkode→HF) and supports the hypothesis that product inventory shift is a major factor.

4) Baselines and adaptation techniques are well-motivated and reproducible. SVM token and latent post models with transparent features (position, POS, dependency, char n-grams), Brown clusters on unlabeled forum text, and simple gazetteers constitute solid baselines. The post-level latent SVM is a sensible formulation given the near-single-product-per-post observation, achieving strong within-domain post-level accuracy (~90%).

Weaknesses
1) Limited methodological novelty and dated baselines for ACL’17. While the empirical characterization is valuable, the modeling is limited to linear SVMs; CRFs are asserted “similar,” but neural sequence models (BiLSTM-CRF with char/subword, which were state-of-the-art by 2016–2017) are not attempted. Given the focus on domain adaptation, omitting strong contemporary baselines (e.g., adversarial/domain-invariant representations, pivot-based SCL, instance reweighting) weakens the case that standard techniques “have limited effectiveness.” Suggest adding at least one modern domain-adaptive neural baseline (e.g., DANN/adversarial, aspect-based per Zhang et al., 2017) and a pivot-feature baseline (SCL).

2) Annotation choices introduce potential bias and evaluation confounds. Token-level labels mark only heads of product NPs and sometimes verbs; then phrase-level labels are produced by projecting with a WSJ-trained parser on noisy forum text. This may compound errors and bias evaluation in favor of systems that correlate with the parser’s mistakes. The decision to ignore content beyond the first/last 10 lines (while pragmatic) removes many mentions (particularly in long HF posts) and could penalize systems that correctly extract mid-post products. More quantitative analysis of how these choices affect recall and agreement would strengthen the work.

3) Inter-annotator agreement is modest and annotators are authors. Fleiss’ κ ranging from 0.58 to 0.77 indicates fair-to-moderate agreement; for a context-sensitive task this may be acceptable, but there is risk of bias since all annotators are authors. The paper would benefit from reporting adjudication protocol, per-forum κ confidence intervals, and examples of frequent disagreements to calibrate task difficulty.

4) Type-level supervision and gazetteer simulation are not entirely clean. The “type-level” adaptation uses a gazetteer extracted from labeled data in the target forum (≥4 occurrences), which presumes some existing annotation or careful manual curation. This is realistic but mixes supervision regimes. It would be helpful to simulate a truly cheap supervision setting (e.g., 15–30 minutes of expert skim labeling) and quantify gains vs. cost, and to separate performance when no target-labeled products are available (Blackhat/Nulled) from when a small curated lexicon exists.

5) Missing related work and ablations on normalization/noise. Prior domain adaptation methods in NLP are under-cited (e.g., structural correspondence learning, instance weighting, feature augmentation variants) and no error analysis on normalization issues (spelling, obfuscation) is given. Given heavy misspellings/slang, stronger subword modeling, lexicon normalization, or forum-specific tokenization could substantially help OOV recall; an ablation on normalization would be valuable.

Questions to authors
- Data release and ethics: What is the licensing, anonymization, and ethical review status of the released forum data? Are user handles, PII, or links redacted? How will you mitigate dual-use concerns?
- Parsing for NP projection: What is parsing accuracy on this domain? Did you try a robust/NN parser or NP chunker trained on noisy text? How sensitive are NP-level results to parser errors and the length ≤7 cap?
- Gazetteer supervision: How expensive is constructing the type-level lexicon in a truly new forum (minutes per forum)? Can you quantify improvements vs. annotation time?
- Disagreement patterns: What are the dominant sources of annotation disagreement (e.g., method vs. product, human agent vs. deliverable)? Any forum-specific trends?
- Why was SCL/pivot features or adversarial DA not tried? Any constraints that precluded a stronger DA baseline?

Additional comments
- Consider character/subword-rich neural baselines (BiLSTM-CRF with char CNN/LSTM), domain-adversarial training, and pivot features; evaluate with and without normalization (e.g., spelling correction).
- Provide per-forum class priors and product distribution overlap (JS divergence) to quantify domain similarity beyond OOV.
- Report post-level accuracy in cross-domain for the latent SVM vs. simple title-based baselines; posts’ titles might be a strong heuristic.
- Clarify dataset splits and counts in Table 1 (the “758/140 3/4” formatting is hard to parse).
- Minor: Some references are malformed; ensure complete citations.

Recommendation: Weak accept as a dataset-and-analysis paper. The resource and empirical characterization of fine-grained domain shift are valuable and likely to spur research. The paper would be stronger with at least one contemporary domain adaptation baseline and deeper analysis of annotation and normalization.
