## Review of “Identifying Products in Online Cybercrime Marketplaces: A Dataset and Fine-grained Domain Adaptation Task”

This paper presents a new dataset of posts from four online cybercrime forums, annotated for product mentions, and explores the challenges of applying NLP models across these distinct forums – framed as a fine-grained domain adaptation problem. The authors correctly identify the difficulty of transferring knowledge even within the cybercrime domain, where forums specialize in different products. The main contribution is the creation and release of this dataset, which is a valuable resource for researchers interested in analyzing online illicit markets. A secondary contribution lies in empirically demonstrating the surprisingly strong domain shift between these forums and the limited effectiveness of standard domain adaptation techniques. Finally, the detailed error analysis and discussion of why existing methods fail provide useful insights for future research.

The strengths of this submission are significant. First, the dataset itself is a substantial contribution. The annotation scheme, described in detail, appears well-considered, balancing granularity with practicality. The dataset size (93,924 posts, 1,938 annotated) is commendable and will enable meaningful studies. Second, the empirical demonstration of domain shift *within* a seemingly narrow domain (cybercrime) is crucial and highlights a real-world challenge for NLP. The consistent degradation in performance when applying models across forums is clearly shown. Third, the detailed analysis of the challenges, including the role of out-of-vocabulary products and stylistic differences, provides a solid foundation for future work. The inclusion of baseline models and exploration of Brown clusters and type-level adaptation, while not highly successful, offers a valuable benchmark for comparison.

However, the paper has weaknesses. While the authors explore several domain adaptation techniques, these are relatively standard approaches. The lack of more innovative adaptation methods or in-depth investigation of the specific characteristics driving the domain shift is a missed opportunity. Additionally, the evaluation is somewhat limited. While the authors consider token and post-level accuracy, a more nuanced evaluation considering the *types* of errors made across forums could provide deeper insights. Finally, the writing in some sections is dense and could benefit from clearer explanations of the methods and results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of each forum (beyond the broad descriptions provided) that you believe contribute most to the observed domain shift?
2.  Given the observed limitations of standard domain adaptation techniques, what specific avenues for future research do you believe hold the most promise for addressing this fine-grained domain adaptation problem?
3.  You mention that many posts contain multiple references to the same product described in slightly different ways. How might this ambiguity be better handled in future annotation schemes or evaluation metrics?
