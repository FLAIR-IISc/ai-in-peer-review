**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a new dataset of online cybercrime marketplace posts annotated with product references, a task that combines information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors formulate a fine-grained domain adaptation task, where the goal is to identify products being bought and sold in different forums. They evaluate the performance of various machine learning models, including simple baselines and learning-based approaches, on this task. The results show that while the models perform well on in-domain data, their performance degrades significantly when applied to out-of-domain data. The authors explore several methods for domain adaptation, including using Brown clusters, gazetteers, and token-level annotation, but find that effective methods for this task are still an open question.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Dataset**: The authors release a new dataset of 93,924 posts from four different cybercrime forums, with annotations for 1,938 posts. This dataset provides a useful testbed for evaluating fine-grained domain adaptation methods.
2. **Task Formulation**: The authors formulate a fine-grained domain adaptation task that combines information extraction and named entity recognition. This task is particularly challenging due to the out-of-domain nature of the data.
3. **Evaluation of Domain Adaptation Methods**: The authors evaluate the performance of various domain adaptation methods, including Brown clusters, gazetteers, and token-level annotation. While these methods show some promise, they are not effective in fully addressing the domain adaptation challenge.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Task Formulation**: The authors introduce a new task that combines information extraction and named entity recognition, which is particularly challenging due to the out-of-domain nature of the data.
2. **Large-Scale Dataset**: The authors release a large-scale dataset of annotated posts from cybercrime forums, which provides a useful testbed for evaluating fine-grained domain adaptation methods.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors evaluate the performance of various machine learning models and domain adaptation methods, providing a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Success of Domain Adaptation Methods**: The authors find that existing domain adaptation methods are not effective in fully addressing the domain adaptation challenge, highlighting the need for further research in this area.
2. **Overfitting**: The authors note that some of the domain adaptation methods they evaluate suffer from overfitting, particularly when using small amounts of labeled data from the target domain.
3. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the domain adaptation methods they evaluate, which would be useful in understanding the underlying reasons for their performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the annotation process and the guidelines used to annotate the dataset?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in the domain adaptation methods you evaluate?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the performance of the different domain adaptation methods on different types of products (e.g., nouns vs. verbs)?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a useful contribution to the field of natural language processing, particularly in the area of domain adaptation. The authors' evaluation of various machine learning models and domain adaptation methods provides a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. However, further research is needed to develop more effective domain adaptation methods that can fully address the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation.
