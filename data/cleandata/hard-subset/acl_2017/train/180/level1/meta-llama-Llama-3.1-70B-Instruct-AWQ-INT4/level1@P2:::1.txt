**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a new task of identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime forums, which is a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors annotate a dataset of 93,924 posts from four different forums and release it for public use. They also propose several learning-based methods for product extraction, including binary classification and post-level classification, and evaluate their performance using various metrics. The authors investigate the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation in this task and explore several methods to improve results in cross-forum settings.

**Contributions**

1. **New dataset and task**: The authors introduce a new dataset and task of identifying products in online cybercrime forums, which is a valuable contribution to the NLP community.
2. **Evaluation metrics**: The authors propose several evaluation metrics for the task, including token-level accuracy, type-level product extraction, and post-level accuracy, which provide a comprehensive assessment of system performance.
3. **Learning-based methods**: The authors propose several learning-based methods for product extraction, including binary classification and post-level classification, which achieve better performance than baseline methods.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel task and dataset**: The paper introduces a new task and dataset that is relevant to the NLP community, particularly in the area of information extraction and domain adaptation.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of system performance using various metrics, which gives a thorough understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed methods.
3. **In-depth analysis**: The authors provide an in-depth analysis of the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation in this task and explore several methods to improve results in cross-forum settings.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited domain adaptation methods**: The authors only explore a limited number of domain adaptation methods, which may not be sufficient to address the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation in this task.
2. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The authors do not compare their proposed methods with state-of-the-art methods in the area of information extraction and domain adaptation, which makes it difficult to assess the competitiveness of their approach.
3. **Limited discussion of error analysis**: The authors do not provide a detailed error analysis of their proposed methods, which would be helpful in understanding the limitations of their approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the annotation process and the guidelines used to annotate the dataset?
2. How do you plan to address the challenges of fine-grained domain adaptation in this task, and what additional methods do you propose to explore?
