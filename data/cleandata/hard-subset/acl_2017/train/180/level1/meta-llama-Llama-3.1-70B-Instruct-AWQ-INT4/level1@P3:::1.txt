**Summary**

This paper introduces a new task of identifying products being bought and sold in online cybercrime forums, which is a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition. The authors create a dataset of 93,924 posts from four different forums, with annotations for 1,938 posts. They evaluate several machine learning models on this task, including simple baselines and learning-based methods, and find that the models perform poorly when applied to out-of-domain data. The authors then explore several methods for fine-grained domain adaptation, including using Brown clusters, type-level annotation, and token-level annotation, but find that these methods have limited effectiveness.

**Main Contributions**

1. The creation of a new dataset for the task of identifying products in online cybercrime forums, which is a valuable resource for the research community.
2. The evaluation of several machine learning models on this task, which provides a baseline for future work.
3. The exploration of several methods for fine-grained domain adaptation, which is an important problem in natural language processing.

**Strengths**

1. The paper tackles a challenging and important problem in the field of natural language processing, and the creation of a new dataset is a significant contribution.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of several machine learning models on this task, which provides a baseline for future work.
3. The exploration of several methods for fine-grained domain adaptation is a valuable contribution, and the authors provide a clear and detailed analysis of the results.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why the authors chose to focus on online cybercrime forums, and how this task is relevant to the broader field of natural language processing.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by the machine learning models, which would be helpful in understanding why the models perform poorly on out-of-domain data.
3. The paper does not provide a clear conclusion or summary of the main findings, which makes it difficult to understand the implications of the research.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more information on why you chose to focus on online cybercrime forums, and how this task is relevant to the broader field of natural language processing?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the machine learning models, including examples of errors and an explanation of why the models made these errors?
3. Can you provide more information on the potential applications of this research, and how the dataset and models could be used in real-world scenarios?
