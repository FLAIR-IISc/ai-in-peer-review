This paper presents a new dataset of product annotations from online cybercrime forums and explores the task of identifying products being bought and sold in these forums. The authors formulate this task as a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition. The dataset consists of 93,924 posts from four different forums, with annotations for 1,938 posts. The authors also examine basic supervised learning approaches to the product extraction problem and explore several methods for fine-grained domain adaptation.

The paper is clear and well-written, and the experiments are thorough, with hypotheses tested and compared. The authors provide a detailed description of the dataset and annotation process, which is helpful for understanding the task and the challenges involved. The use of multiple evaluation metrics, including token-level accuracy, type-level product extraction, and post-level accuracy, provides a comprehensive view of the performance of the models.

One of the main contributions of this paper is the release of a new dataset for product extraction in online cybercrime forums. This dataset can be useful for researchers working on information extraction, named entity recognition, and domain adaptation. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the challenges involved in this task, including the variability in product inventories and stylistic conventions across different forums.

However, there are some limitations to this paper. The authors manipulated the abstract formatting to fit the content into the 8-page limit, which makes it difficult to read and understand the abstract. Additionally, the paper is miscategorized as a tagging/chunking/parsing paper, when in fact it is more related to information extraction.

Some of the annotations in the dataset are unclear or seem unrelated to products. For example, the annotation of "sombody" in Figure 1 is not clear. The authors should provide more guidance on how to annotate such cases.

The baseline models used in the paper are overly basic, and stronger, readily available NLP baselines could have been included with little extra effort. The authors should consider using more robust baselines to evaluate the performance of their models.

The domain adaptation results are expected and reiterate known differences in user-generated forum data, offering no novel insights. The authors should consider exploring more advanced domain adaptation techniques, such as those that take into account the variability in product inventories and stylistic conventions across different forums.

Overall, this paper provides a good starting point for researchers working on product extraction in online cybercrime forums. However, there are some limitations that need to be addressed, and the authors should consider exploring more advanced techniques to improve the performance of their models.
