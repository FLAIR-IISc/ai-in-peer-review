This paper presents a new dataset of posts from four online cybercrime marketplaces, annotated with references to products being bought and sold. The authors formulate this as a token-level annotation task, resembling a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition, and analyze the challenges of domain adaptation across these forums. The primary contribution is the dataset itself, which offers a valuable resource for researchers interested in analyzing cybercrime-related text data and exploring domain adaptation techniques. A secondary contribution lies in the characterization of these forums as “fine-grained domains,” demonstrating that significant performance degradation occurs even when transferring models between different cybercrime forums. Finally, the paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of several baseline models and domain adaptation strategies, highlighting the difficulties of this specific task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Dataset:** The released dataset is a significant contribution. High-quality, annotated data in the domain of online cybercrime is scarce due to the sensitive nature of the content. The size of the dataset (93,924 posts, with 1,938 annotated) and the detailed annotation scheme are commendable. This dataset will undoubtedly serve as a valuable testbed for future research in information extraction, domain adaptation, and cybersecurity.
2. **Clear Problem Formulation and Thorough Evaluation:** The authors clearly define the product identification task and present a comprehensive evaluation of several baseline methods and domain adaptation techniques. The use of multiple evaluation metrics (token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, post-level accuracy) provides a nuanced understanding of the system performance. The careful experimentation and analysis demonstrate a rigorous approach to addressing the research question.
3. **Insightful Domain Characterization:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the “fine-grained domain” nature of cybercrime forums. The observation that models trained on one forum perform poorly on others underscores the challenges of applying NLP techniques to this domain. This finding highlights the need for developing more robust domain adaptation strategies. 
4. **Well-written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to understand. The authors provide sufficient context and motivation for their work, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The inclusion of example annotations (Figure 1) is helpful for understanding the annotation scheme.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Domain Adaptation:** While the domain adaptation experiments are well-executed, the findings are largely expected. The observed performance degradation when transferring models between forums reiterates known challenges in applying NLP techniques to user-generated content with varying stylistic conventions and vocabularies. The paper lacks novel insights into *why* these adaptation techniques fail, and doesn’t offer any particularly innovative solutions.
2. **Basic Baseline Models:** The baseline models employed (dictionary-based approaches, binary classifiers) are relatively simple and do not represent the state-of-the-art in information extraction. Including stronger, readily available NLP baselines (e.g., pre-trained language models fine-tuned for NER/IE) would have provided a more meaningful comparison and established a clearer benchmark for evaluating the proposed methods.
3. **Annotation Ambiguity & Consistency:** Some of the annotation choices seem questionable. The inclusion of tokens like "sombody" (Figure 1) as part of the product reference is unclear and potentially weakens the quality of the annotations. Greater clarity in the annotation guidelines, along with inter-annotator agreement analysis on a larger subset of the data, would strengthen the dataset’s reliability. The authors mention minor inconsistencies in annotation in the paper.
4. **Formatting Issues:** The authors explicitly state they manipulated abstract formatting (font size and line spacing) to fit the content within the 8-page limit. This suggests potential compromises in presentation and readability, which could detract from the overall impact of the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used to determine the inclusion of non-product tokens, such as "sombody" in Figure 1? Providing more specific examples and justifications would clarify the annotation process.
2.  Given the limitations of the explored domain adaptation techniques, what specific features of the cybercrime forum data do you believe pose the greatest challenge to generalization? 
3.  What are the implications of the observed differences in OOV rates between the Darkode and Hack Forums training sets? Does this suggest that Darkode is a more representative or generalizable domain?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a discussion of the ethical considerations surrounding the collection and analysis of data from online cybercrime forums. The authors should address potential risks related to privacy, security, and the potential for misuse of the dataset. The paper’s categorization as “tagging, chunking and parsing” is inaccurate; it aligns more closely with Information Extraction.
