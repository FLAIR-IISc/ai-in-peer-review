- Presents a new dataset of product references from four cybercrime marketplaces, framing the task as a blend of information extraction and NER.  
- Dataset: 93,924 posts total, 1,938 annotated with token-level labels; dataset released to the community.  
- Empirically characterizes domain shift: models trained on one forum suffer substantial performance drops when applied to others.  
- Thorough, well-motivated experimentation with clear hypotheses, ablation studies, and token- and post-level evaluations.  
- Systematic exploration of adaptation methods (Brown clusters, type-level, token-level); results largely negative with analysis of failure modes.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-presented, and includes informative figures and tables.  
- High relevance and potential real-world impact for security, law enforcement, and threat intelligence.  
- Authors provide an honest discussion of limitations and negative results.  
- Baselines are weak; stronger off-the-shelf baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with pre-trained embeddings or transformer models) are missing.  
- Annotation guidelines allow subjective interpretations; examples raise concerns about annotation consistency.  
- The domain-shift finding is not surprising and offers limited novel explanation beyond confirming expected challenges.  
- Misclassification of the task as tagging/chunking/parsing rather than information extraction, affecting framing and comparisons.  
- Authors admit to manipulating formatting to meet page limits, suggesting issues with conciseness and presentation.  
- Requests for more ambiguous annotation examples and explanations of resolution during annotation.  
- Asks whether stronger NLP baselines were considered and why they were excluded.  
- Seeks authors’ views on promising directions to address domain shift given limited success of evaluated techniques.  
- Asks whether knowledge graphs or ontologies could aid product identification and domain adaptation.  
- Overall: dataset release is valuable and will spur research; recommend clarifying annotations, using stronger baselines, and reclassifying the task as IE.
