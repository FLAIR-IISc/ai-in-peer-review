Summary
The paper introduces a token-level product identification task in online cybercrime forums, accompanied by a new multi-forum dataset and a thorough empirical study of cross-forum domain shift. The authors define multiple evaluation views (token-, NP-, and post-level) and test simple supervised models with standard adaptation techniques.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A publicly released dataset of cybercrime marketplace posts with product annotations across four forums, plus clear guidelines and IAA analysis.
2) A careful empirical characterization and evaluation protocol for product extraction in noisy, fine-grained domains (metrics and OOV analyses highlighting cross-forum degradation).
3) A task formulation at the intersection of IE and NER with practical evaluation scaffolding (token/NP/post-level).

Strengths
- Dataset value and clarity: The dataset fills a gap in a high-impact, hard-to-access domain; annotation protocol, adjudication, and IAA are well-documented, making it a credible resource for the community.
- Thorough evaluation: The paper presents multiple complementary metrics (token/NP/post), strong analyses (seen vs. OOV products), and clear takeaways about fine-grained domain shift.
- Writing and reproducibility: The paper is well-written, hypotheses are explicit, and experiments are easy to follow; the promise to release data/code enhances impact.
- Practical relevance: Product identification enables downstream security analyses (market composition, trends, user profiling), increasing the work’s utility beyond NLP.

Weaknesses
- Baselines are too weak: The study omits readily available strong baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, CRF+contextual embeddings, span-based neural IE, pretrained transformers), which could be added with modest effort and might substantially change conclusions.
- Limited novelty in adaptation: The adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, gazetteers, Daumé features) are standard; results largely re-confirm known domain-shift behavior without novel methodological insights.
- Annotation ambiguity: Some labels conflate services/agents with products (e.g., annotating “sombody/coder” as part of the deliverable), risking inconsistent semantics and questionable generalizability; more justification and reliability analysis are needed.
- Area fit: The work is primarily an information extraction problem rather than tagging/chunking/parsing, and appears miscategorized for the track.
- Formatting violation: Manipulating abstract font size/line spacing to fit the page limit is a serious compliance issue.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Please justify and quantify the annotation of human agents/methods as products (frequency, IAA, error patterns); can you provide a cleaner product-vs-service schema?
- Why were stronger neural baselines and pretrained models not included? Can you provide preliminary results (even small-scale) to contextualize current findings?
- Can you release the full guidelines and all adjudicated labels at submission time, along with an ethics statement and licensing terms?
- Did NP projection via a parser distort evaluation? Could you add BIO span-level evaluation to remove parser dependence?
- Will you resubmit a version compliant with formatting rules?
