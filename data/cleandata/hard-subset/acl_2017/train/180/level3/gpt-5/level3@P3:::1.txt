Summary
This paper introduces a new token-level annotation task and dataset for identifying products being bought or sold in cybercrime forums. It covers four distinct forums, positions each forum as a fine-grained domain, and studies cross-forum generalization. The authors propose simple SVM-based models at token and post levels, evaluate with multiple metrics (token-, NP-, type-, and post-level), and analyze standard semi-supervised and domain adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, gazetteers, feature augmentation). They find large out-of-domain drops and limited gains from standard adaptation, arguing this setting remains challenging.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Resource: A new annotated dataset of cybercrime marketplace posts with clear task definition, multiple evaluation protocols, and release plans for data and code.
2) Empirical characterization: A careful, multi-metric evaluation showing substantial cross-forum domain shift, including analyses of OOV rates and performance on seen vs. unseen products.
3) Baseline modeling and adaptation study: Token-level and post-level SVMs with experiments on Brown clusters, simple gazetteers, and “frustratingly easy” domain features, showing limited improvements out of domain.

Strengths
- Valuable dataset and task framing: The data are hard to obtain and important for security research. The annotation targets a practically useful goal (what is the product being transacted) and the evaluation suite (token, NP projection, type, post-level) is thoughtfully designed to support different downstream uses. The cross-forum setting is realistic and timely.
- Clear, careful writing and thorough experimentation: The paper articulates hypotheses, defines metrics, and reports results across forums and metrics, with significance tests and error/OOV analyses. The per-forum breakdown and post-level vs. token-level comparison are informative.
- Fine-grained domain adaptation perspective: Treating each forum as its own domain and quantifying asymmetries (e.g., Darkode’s broader product coverage) is helpful and will seed follow-up work on domain adaptation in noisy, user-generated markets.
- Practical utility: Even with modest performance, post-level accuracy is high enough to enable forum-scale analyses. Releasing data and code will likely stimulate research and applications beyond the paper’s scope.

Weaknesses
- Formatting noncompliance: The abstract appears to use reduced font size and/or line spacing to fit within the 8-page limit. This violates ACL formatting policy and should be corrected; if confirmed, it is a serious issue for the ACs to consider.
- Task framing and track miscategorization: Despite references to NER, the problem is more naturally an information extraction/slot-filling task with provenance, plus a resource contribution. The work seems better suited to IE/Resources than Tagging/Chunking/Parsing. This mismatch contributes to a perception of limited modeling novelty relative to track expectations.
- Annotation scheme clarity and face validity: Some annotations are hard to reconcile with “product” semantics (e.g., annotating “sombody/somebody” or “coder” as part of the method/deliverable in Fig. 1). This collapses roles (agent/method vs. deliverable) and introduces ambiguity. The decision to mark only NP heads at token level, exclude mid-post spans, and mix verbs/nouns under a single product label may inflate apparent agreement while conflating different phenomena. More evidence of consistency across such cases (e.g., IAA split by “core product” vs. “method”) is needed.
- Baselines are underpowered for 2017: The modeling choices (token-level SVM; post-level latent SVM) are basic. The paper explicitly notes CRF parity but does not report strong sequence-labeling baselines available in 2016–2017 (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with char/subword features; pre-trained embeddings; CRF with lexicons). Given the resource focus, including such baselines would be straightforward and set a more useful bar for future work.
- Domain adaptation adds little beyond known results: The finding that cross-forum performance drops, OOV drives errors, and Brown/gazetteers/feature augmentation give small gains is expected for user-generated forums. The adaptation study reiterates well-known patterns without offering new techniques or qualitative insights (e.g., pivot features, adversarial invariance, multi-task across forums, field-aware features for titles vs. bodies). The asymmetry analysis is interesting but feels incomplete without stronger baselines.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Formatting: Please confirm whether the abstract adheres to ACL font/spacing rules and commit to fixing any violations. If adjustments are needed, does the text still fit within the page limit?
- Annotation policy:
  - What fraction of annotated tokens are “core products” vs. “methods/agents” (e.g., coder, somebody)? Provide IAA broken down by these categories.
  - How often are human agents annotated? Why not separate labels (e.g., PRODUCT, METHOD, AGENT) to preserve role distinctions?
  - How sensitive are results to excluding the “method/agent” category?
  - What is the impact of the “first/last 10 lines” rule on recall and on IAA?
- Modeling/baselines:
  - Did you try a BiLSTM-CRF with char/subword features and pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) on forum text? If so, please report; if not, can you add it?
  - For post-level prediction, how often does the “exactly one product per post” inductive bias misfire (given ≈3% multi-product posts)? Does that model suppress secondary correct mentions?
  - Can you report a span-level (phrase) extractor trained directly at phrase level (e.g., BIO tagging) rather than projecting from head tokens with a parser?
- Domain adaptation:
  - Did you try stronger DA methods (e.g., pivot-based SCL, adversarial domain-invariant representations, or multi-task learning over forum IDs)? Any qualitative analysis of which features fail to transfer besides OOV?
  - Field-aware features: do per-field models (title vs. body) improve transfer?
  - How do your results change with subword/character features to mitigate OOV?

Additional comments
- Please release the full annotation guidelines and per-post IDs for reproducibility, along with scripts to compute all metrics (token, NP, type, post-level).
- Minor: Consider standardizing terminology (e.g., “product,” “method,” “agent”) in figures and text; the example with “sombody” could be clarified or replaced to avoid confusion.
- If accepted, I strongly encourage adding at least one modern sequence-tagging baseline and a brief qualitative error analysis of cross-domain failures beyond OOV.
