Summary
This paper introduces a new task and dataset for identifying the products being bought/sold in online cybercrime forums. The annotation is token-level but evaluated at both token and NP levels, with multiple downstream-motivated metrics (token F1, type-level per-post F1, and post-level accuracy). The authors provide data from four forums (≈94k posts, with 1,938 posts annotated) and analyze cross-forum generalization as a fine-grained domain adaptation challenge. Baselines include simple lexical heuristics and learned SVM models (binary token/NP classification and a latent-SVM post-level selector). Brown clusters, gazetteers, and limited target supervision are explored for adaptation; these yield modest gains but do not close the cross-forum gap.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Dataset and task formulation: A publicly released dataset and clear IE-style task definition for product extraction in cybercrime marketplaces, with detailed guidelines and multi-granularity evaluation metrics tailored to realistic analyses.
2) Empirical characterization of fine-grained domain shift: A careful set of within- vs cross-forum experiments that quantify large performance drops and analyze OOV effects and transfer asymmetries across forums.
3) Negative results on straightforward adaptation baselines: Evidence that common semi-supervised and light-weight adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, gazetteers, frustratingly-easy feature augmentation) offer only limited improvements, highlighting the difficulty of this setting.

Strengths
- Valuable dataset and realistic task. The forum data is messy and security-relevant, and the product-extraction task bridges NER and slot filling. The release of ≈94k posts with 1,938 annotated posts across four distinct forums should enable follow-on work in IE and domain adaptation.
- Clear, thorough experimental design and analysis. The paper defines multiple evaluation views (token, NP, type-level, post-level) and justifies them by intended use cases. The results include within- and cross-forum settings, with concrete numbers that make the domain shift salient (e.g., NP-level F1 drops by 14.4 points when training on Hack Forums and testing on Darkode, 63.2 vs. 77.6). The OOV analysis and asymmetry between Darkode and Hack Forums are insightful.
- Reproducibility and transparency. The authors promise to release data, code, and models, and they document preprocessing, annotation workflow, and inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ ≈ 0.58–0.77 across subsets).
- Practical evaluation choice and rationale. The post-level selector model and post-level accuracy metric (≈90% within-forum) align with the real need to identify a single salient product per post/thread, which is a useful perspective beyond pure token labeling.

Weaknesses
- Formatting/style noncompliance. The abstract appears to use reduced font size/line spacing to fit within the 8-page limit, which violates the conference formatting guidelines. This must be rectified for a fair page budget comparison.
- Track miscategorization. The work is fundamentally information extraction and domain adaptation rather than tagging/chunking/parsing. This misplacement may have limited the comparison set and baseline choices; it should be revised to an IE or domain adaptation track.
- Some annotation choices are unclear. The guidelines annotate human agents and methods as part of the “product” (e.g., “sombody/coder” in Fig. 1), which seems inconsistent with the stated goal of identifying the good/service delivered. This conflation can confuse learning and evaluation. The κ values are reasonable, but more evidence about category-specific agreement (core product vs. method/agent) and error breakdowns would help.
- Baselines are too modest given the 2017 state of the art. Beyond the SVMs and Brown clusters, there is no comparison to standard sequence models (linear-chain CRF), neural baselines (BiLSTM-CRF with char features), or simple domain-adversarial training, which were readily available and could be plugged in with minimal engineering. The claim that a CRF with a binary tagset performs “similarly” deserves empirical confirmation. Stronger baselines are essential to validate the difficulty claims.
- Limited novelty in adaptation insights. The observed behavior (OOV-driven degradation, forum-specific lexicons/stylistics, limited gains from Brown clusters/gazetteers) largely reiterates known challenges in user-generated/forum data. The paper stops short of proposing or evaluating more targeted adaptation strategies (e.g., lexicon induction with distant supervision, adversarial representation learning, multi-task objectives), so the adaptation contribution feels incremental.

Questions to Authors
- Annotation scope: Please clarify and justify when human agents or methods (e.g., “coder,” “somebody,” “hack”) are labeled as products. Can you separate “deliverable” vs. “method/agent” labels or at least report per-category agreement and performance?
- NP projection: How sensitive are NP-level results to parsing errors? Have you tried simpler span heuristics (e.g., head-word expansion based on dependency arcs) to reduce parser-induced noise?
- Baselines: Can you add a linear-chain CRF, a BiLSTM-CRF with pretrained word vectors and character CNN/LSTM, and a simple domain-adversarial baseline? Even a small ablation on Darkode/Hack Forums would strengthen claims.
- Type-supervision: How robust are gazetteers to near-synonyms and misspellings? Did you try distant induction (e.g., bootstrapping product dictionaries from thread titles) to expand the lexicon across forums?
- Ethics and release: What steps ensure ethical/IRB compliance and safe release of this sensitive data (e.g., redaction, TOS compliance)?

Additional Comments
- Please fix the abstract formatting to comply with the style file.
- Consider moving the paper to an IE or domain adaptation track.
- Tighten references (several entries have formatting issues) and correct a few typos in examples.
- Providing the full annotation guide and more qualitative examples (success/failure) in the appendix would aid reuse.

Overall, I see the primary contribution as the dataset and task framing, supported by a careful empirical characterization. With stronger baselines, clearer annotation justification, and corrected formatting, this would be a solid asset for the community.
