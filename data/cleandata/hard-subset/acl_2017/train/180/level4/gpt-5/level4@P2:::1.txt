Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces a dataset and task for identifying products being bought or sold in cybercrime marketplace forums. The authors annotate token-level product mentions in 1,938 posts drawn from four forums (93,924 posts total), then evaluate supervised models under within- and cross-forum settings.
- Primary contributions (as I see them):
  1) A new dataset and task definition for product identification in noisy, domain-specific forums, with clear evaluation protocols (token-, type-, and post-level; token-to-NP projection).
  2) An empirical characterization of “fine-grained” domain shift across forums and a systematic evaluation showing that standard semi-supervised and domain adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, type-level gazetteers, small labeled target sets, feature-duplication) provide limited gains.
  3) Baseline models (token-level SVM and post-level latent SVM) and analyses that reveal why cross-forum transfer degrades (notably product OOV and stylistic differences).

Strengths
- Valuable dataset and task framing: The corpus spans four distinct cybercrime forums and focuses on a practically relevant IE task. The annotation scheme, inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ kappa ~0.58–0.77), and release plan suggest this will be a useful benchmark for both NLP and security research.
- Careful evaluation design: The paper motivates multiple metrics (token F1, per-post type-level F1, and post-level accuracy) and justifies projecting token labels to NP spans via a parser to better capture product phrases. This makes the results interpretable for different downstream goals.
- Solid within-forum performance enabling downstream use: On Darkode, the post-level NP system achieves about 80 F1 on product types and ~90% post-level accuracy, suggesting the approach is sufficiently accurate to support forum-level analyses.
- Thorough experimental comparison: The paper contrasts within- vs cross-forum performance, tests Brown clusters, gazetteers from small type-level supervision, and limited token-level supervision with and without domain-specific feature duplication. The OOV analysis (e.g., Darkode’s lower OOV rates and more consistent OOV recall) helps explain asymmetric transfer and provides actionable insight for future work.
- Reproducibility: The authors state that data, models, and code will be released, which will benefit the community.

Weaknesses
- Formatting noncompliance: The abstract appears to use smaller font or tighter line spacing to extend its length and fit within the page limit. This should be corrected to comply with ACL formatting rules.
- Track fit: The work aligns more with information extraction and domain adaptation than with tagging/chunking/parsing per se. While token-level tagging is used, the framing, metrics, and models are IE-centric.
- Annotation scope is debatable: Labeling human-agent terms (e.g., “somebody/coder” in Fig. 1) as product-related may conflate providers with products or methods. This choice could complicate both learning and evaluation and may not align with many IE use cases focused strictly on the deliverable.
- Baselines are limited relative to current practice: Given readily available tools, stronger baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with pretrained embeddings and character features; CRFs with richer context; simple neural sentence/post models for post-level prediction; leveraging title/body structure) could likely be implemented with modest effort and may change conclusions about absolute performance.
- Limited novelty in adaptation findings: The cross-forum degradation and modest gains from Brown clusters/gazetteers largely corroborate known challenges in user-generated, noisy domain transfer. The paper’s adaptation techniques are standard, and negative results dominate; the work would be stronger with a more targeted modeling contribution or deeper causal analysis (e.g., disentangling product lexicon OOV vs stylistic/structural shifts).

Questions to authors
- Formatting: Did you alter the abstract’s font size or line spacing? Please ensure the camera-ready version strictly follows ACL formatting guidelines.
- Annotation policy: What fraction of annotations are agent/method tokens (e.g., “somebody,” “coder”)? Can you report performance and downstream utility if such tokens are excluded or split into a separate label (product vs method/agent)?
- Span vs head annotation: Why not annotate minimal NP spans directly? How sensitive are results to the NP projection heuristic and parser errors in noisy text? Any analysis comparing head-only vs span-level gold?
- Stronger baselines: Can you add results for a BiLSTM-CRF (with pretrained embeddings and character-level encoders), a CRF with global features, and/or a simple neural post-level selector that leverages titles? These would better calibrate the difficulty of the task.
- Release details: Will the full unlabeled corpus, annotation guidelines, and code be released upon publication? Under what license and with what access constraints, given the sensitive domain?
- Domain adaptation analysis: Beyond OOV rates, can you isolate stylistic vs lexical factors (e.g., mask product tokens during training/testing; swap titles only; control for thread structure)? This could clarify whether failures stem mainly from product inventory mismatch or broader discourse/style differences.

Overall, I find the dataset and task valuable and the empirical study careful and transparent, but I am concerned about formatting compliance, the choice of venue track, the annotation scope for agent tokens, and the modest strength of baselines and adaptation novelty.
