Summary
The paper introduces a new dataset annotated for products mentioned in online cybercrime forums and evaluates extraction models and cross-forum domain adaptation. The paper is clearly written, hypotheses are systematically tested, and the experimental setup is generally sound. The dataset addresses an important and under-resourced domain and has potential value for the community.

Strengths
- Timely and impactful problem setting (cybercrime forums) with clear practical relevance.
- Clear articulation of hypotheses and comparisons across experimental conditions.
- The dataset could become a useful benchmark if released with sufficient documentation and tooling.
- The writing is mostly clear and the experiments are reproducible at a high level.

Main Concerns and Suggestions
1) Formatting and policy compliance
- The abstract appears to use a smaller font size and reduced line spacing relative to the ACL template, effectively extending the abstract and fitting more text into the 8-page limit. This is a serious formatting violation. Please restore the official template’s default font size and spacing for all sections, including the abstract, and ensure the paper meets the page limits accordingly.

2) Fit to track/scope
- As framed, the work is closer to information extraction (entity/product mention detection and related attributes) than to tagging/chunking/parsing in the traditional sense. If submitted to a track focused on tagging/chunking/parsing, please (a) explicitly cast the core task as sequence labeling with a well-defined tagging scheme, or (b) consider submitting to an information extraction or NLP-and-security–oriented venue/track. Clear positioning will help reviewers assess novelty and technical contribution appropriately.

3) Annotation clarity and validity
- It is unclear why certain spans are labeled as products (e.g., “somebody” in Fig. 1). The paper needs crisper definitions and boundary guidelines for “product” versus related concepts (services, users/actors, contact handles, escrow, etc.).
- Please provide:
  - A formal definition of “product” with decision rules for borderline cases (e.g., offers, requests, services, human labor).
  - The label schema (e.g., product types, attributes) and whether services are included.
  - Inter-annotator agreement per label and for span boundaries (e.g., token vs. span F1, Cohen’s κ), along with the adjudication protocol.
  - Representative positive/negative examples and counterexamples illustrating the guidelines.
  - Dataset statistics (span lengths, label distribution, forum/topic coverage).
- Clarifying these points will increase trust in the dataset and the validity of subsequent modeling results.

4) Baselines are too limited
- Given current NLP tools, more competitive yet still straightforward baselines are expected. Please consider:
  - Strong sequence labelers: CRF with lexical/morphological/gazetteer features; BiLSTM-CRF with character-level encoders for OOV handling.
  - Pretrained embeddings (e.g., word2vec/GloVe/fastText) and subword features.
  - Simple rule-based or distant-supervision heuristics as a complementary baseline (e.g., marketplace lexicons, pattern-based triggers).
  - Ablations to assess the contribution of domain-specific resources vs. generic features.
  - Clear evaluation for both token-level and span-level metrics with exact/partial-match and statistical significance testing.
- Without these, it is hard to gauge progress or to position the dataset results relative to standard IE practice.

5) Domain adaptation analysis lacks novelty and depth
- The cross-forum results confirm well-known observations about user-generated content: forums differ in user bases, topics, and styles, resulting in domain shift. To provide new insight, consider:
  - Stronger adaptation baselines: feature augmentation (e.g., “frustratingly easy” domain adaptation), instance reweighting/importance sampling, pivot features/SCL, domain-adversarial training, or shared–private representations.
  - Diagnostics: topic and lexical overlap between forums; divergence measures (e.g., JS divergence on n-grams), label distribution shift vs. covariate shift; per-label transfer performance; learning curves for few-shot adaptation.
  - Temporal splits to assess stability over time; per-forum error analysis with a taxonomy of common errors (boundary errors, type confusions, OOV slang).
- Such analyses would move beyond confirming prior knowledge and offer concrete guidance for practitioners working across heterogeneous forums.

Additional Comments
- Reproducibility: Please include preprocessing steps, train/dev/test splits, hyperparameters, random seeds, and code links. If the dataset will be released, state licensing, any redactions, and access procedures.
- Ethics and data governance: Given the cybercrime context, please discuss data provenance, legality of collection, privacy protections (e.g., de-identification of PII), potential risks of misuse, and mitigation steps. Include IRB/ethics review status if applicable.
- Presentation: Improve figure legibility and ensure that all examples follow the final annotation guidelines. In particular, revisit Fig. 1 to resolve the “somebody” labeling issue or explain it explicitly.

Overall Assessment
The paper tackles an important domain and contributes a potentially valuable dataset. The writing is clear and the experimental comparisons are carefully laid out. However, significant issues remain: noncompliant formatting, unclear annotation criteria (with at least one questionable example), underpowered baselines, and a domain adaptation section that largely confirms prior findings without deeper analysis. Addressing these concerns—especially (i) restoring template compliance, (ii) tightening and validating the annotation scheme with robust agreement metrics and examples, and (iii) strengthening baselines and domain adaptation analyses—would substantially improve the paper’s rigor and impact.

I encourage the authors to revise accordingly; with these changes, the dataset and accompanying analyses could make a strong contribution to information extraction and the study of NLP in cybercrime contexts.
