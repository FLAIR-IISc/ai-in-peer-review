Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a new task and dataset for identifying products being bought/sold in cybercrime forums, framing it as a token-level annotation problem with both IE and NER characteristics and evaluating generalization across “fine-grained domains” (individual forums). The authors provide multiple evaluation views (token, type-per-post, post-level; token vs NP projection) and compare within-forum vs cross-forum performance, including several domain adaptation techniques.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A publicly released dataset of 93,924 posts (1,938 annotated) from four cybercrime forums (Darkode, Hack Forums, Blackhat, Nulled) with token-level annotations and guidelines. This offers a realistic, messy testbed for IE/domain adaptation in user-generated security text.
2) Careful task design and evaluation protocol, including post-level accuracy and NP projection to better reflect downstream needs, and a post-level latent SVM that targets “one product per post.”
3) An empirical characterization of fine-grained domain shift across forums, with diagnostic analysis (e.g., OOV breakdowns) showing why standard semi-supervised features (Brown clusters) and type-level supervision (gazetteers) only modestly help.

Strengths
- Valuable dataset and task definition: The corpus spans diverse market segments (exploit kits, SEO, account trading, etc.) and realistic forum artifacts (noise, typos, titles vs body). Inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ ≈ 0.58–0.77) is reasonable for noisy text. The commitment to release data, code, and guidelines meaningfully advances research in security-focused IE and domain adaptation.
- Thoughtful evaluation views aligned with applications: Token-, type-, and post-level metrics clarify different use cases. The NP projection addresses a gap between token-level annotation and phrase-level utility (e.g., “Backconnect bot” vs “bot”).
- Solid baselines and a useful post-level model: The token/NP SVMs and a latent SVM that selects one product per post are well-motivated. Within-forum results are strong (NP F1 ≈ 78 on Darkode; post-level accuracy ≈ 90%), sufficient to enable forum-scale analysis.
- Thorough cross-domain evaluation and diagnostics: The paper documents severe cross-forum degradation and probes causes via OOV analysis, revealing Darkode’s broader product coverage and directionality effects in adaptation (HF→D helps more than D→HF). This makes a concrete case for “fine-grained” domain shifts even within cybercrime.
- Reproducibility details: Preprocessing choices, annotation process, and clear train/dev/test splits help replicability; statistical testing is used in several comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and missing stronger baselines: The learning methods are mostly linear SVMs with hand-crafted features. Given the 2017 landscape, stronger baselines are expected (e.g., CRF sequence tagging; BiLSTM-CRF with pre-trained embeddings and character features; neural phrase scoring). On the adaptation side, more modern techniques (e.g., domain-adversarial training, multi-task or feature-augmentation with neural encoders, self/tri-training) would better contextualize the difficulty of the task.
- Annotation scope is potentially ambiguous: The guideline that annotates method/agent tokens (e.g., “coder/somebody” in Fig. 1) as part of the product can conflate deliverables, services, and actors. This may blur the target label and hinder generalization. The paper should quantify how often such tokens are annotated, their inter-annotator agreement vs “core product” tokens, and the impact on system errors (e.g., precision on agent/method vs product nouns).
- Moderate agreement and potentially biasing constraints: κ values around 0.58–0.66 for much of the data suggest non-trivial ambiguity; more error analysis would help. The rule to ignore middle lines beyond the first/last 10 may reduce effort but could bias products that occur later or in long posts; reporting how many true products are excluded and sensitivity analyses would increase confidence.
- Domain adaptation findings largely confirm prior expectations: While the analysis is careful, the main conclusion (strong within-forum, sharp cross-forum drop; modest gains from Brown clusters/gazetteers; small labeled target helps) largely aligns with established observations on user-generated text. Additional insights (e.g., linguistic/stylistic divergences, error taxonomies, or adaptation methods that succeed/fail for identifiable reasons) would strengthen the contribution beyond the dataset.
- Formatting/policy compliance: The abstract appears to use altered font size/line spacing to fit within the page limit, which violates submission guidelines. This should be corrected.

Questions to Authors
- Annotation policy: How frequently are agent/method tokens annotated, and what is their κ compared to core product tokens? Do models over-predict these categories? Could you report results when excluding such tokens from the target label?
- Sequence and neural baselines: How do CRF sequence tagging (token and NP-span) and BiLSTM-CRF with pre-trained embeddings and character features perform, especially cross-forum? Do they narrow the gap or change the precision/recall balance?
- Adaptation methods: Have you tried domain-adversarial training, self-training/tri-training, or label distribution shift correction? Any results with larger or in-domain unlabeled embeddings vs Brown clusters?
- Bias from line-window rule: What proportion of gold products fall outside the first/last 10 lines? Does performance change if that constraint is removed in annotation/evaluation?
- Release details: Will you release the NP projections, gazetteers, and code to reproduce all metrics and splits? Any redaction/ethical safeguards for sensitive content?

Additional Comments
- The task likely fits better under information extraction/domain adaptation than “tagging/chunking/parsing”; this is a matter of track assignment rather than technical merit.
- Several reference entries contain formatting errors and placeholders; please fix for camera-ready.
- Please address the abstract formatting to comply with the conference guidelines.

Overall recommendation
A strong dataset-and-analysis paper with careful evaluation and clear evidence of fine-grained domain shift in cybercrime forums. Methodological novelty is limited, and several stronger baselines/adaptation techniques are missing. If the camera-ready can add at least one contemporary sequence/neural baseline and clarify annotation policy impacts, I would be inclined to support acceptance primarily on the value of the dataset, task framing, and empirical analysis.
