This paper introduces a new dataset with product annotations derived from online cybercrime forums. The writing is clear, and the experiments are generally solid: the authors articulate hypotheses and evaluate them systematically and comparatively. Nevertheless, I have several concerns:

1. The abstract appears to use a reduced font size and tighter line spacing, effectively extending its length and helping the paper fit within the 8-page limit. This seems to violate formatting guidelines and should be corrected.

2. The paper does not fit well within the “tagging, chunking, parsing” area. The core contribution and tasks are more accurately framed as information extraction.

3. The annotation rationale is sometimes unclear. For instance, in Fig. 1 it is difficult to see why certain tokens, such as “somebody,” would be labeled as products. More explicit guidelines or justification are needed to clarify such cases.

4. The baselines are quite minimal. Given the availability of modern NLP tools, it seems feasible to include stronger, more informative baselines without excessive additional effort.

5. The domain adaptation experiments largely confirm what is already well established for user-generated content: different forums (e.g., even within the same topic such as video games) can attract distinct user populations and produce markedly different text, leading to domain shifts. As a result, the findings do not provide new insights specific to this problem.

Overall, the dataset is potentially useful and the paper is well organized, but the formatting issue, the topical mismatch, the unclear annotation examples, and the limited baselines reduce the impact. Clarifying the annotation scheme, strengthening baselines, and repositioning the work within information extraction would improve the paper.
