Summary and contributions:
This paper introduces a new dataset for identifying products bought and sold in cybercrime forums and studies cross-forum domain adaptation. The task blends slot-filling information extraction and NER, with token-level annotations projected to NP-level for evaluation, and multiple evaluation metrics (token, type, and post-level).

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A new dataset spanning four cybercrime forums (≈94k posts total, 1,938 annotated posts), with carefully developed guidelines, inter-annotator agreement analysis, and multiple evaluation views (token/NP/post level). This dataset is likely to be valuable to both the NLP and security communities.
2) An empirical characterization of “fine-grained domain” differences across forums: substantial performance degradation in cross-forum transfer, and limited gains from standard semi-supervised and adaptation techniques (Brown clusters, gazetteers from type-level supervision, limited token-level target data with feature augmentation).
3) Baseline models and metrics for the product identification task, including a token-level SVM, a post-level latent SVM, and reasonable baselines (dictionary, first-NP), along with a clear evaluation protocol.

Strengths:
- Clarity and thoroughness: The paper is clearly written and well structured. The task definition, annotation choices, and evaluation metrics are presented carefully (token-, type-, and post-level views; token-to-NP projection).
- Dataset value and documentation: The collection covers multiple forums with distinct market sectors; the annotation process is detailed, with IAA reported (Fleiss’ Kappa). This resource should facilitate future research on messy forum data and security applications.
- Careful evaluation design: The inclusion of multiple metrics (token vs NP; type vs post-level accuracy) is thoughtful and aligns with different downstream needs. The post-level latent SVM and the finding that extracting a single representative product can be sufficient are practical insights.
- Systematic empirical analysis: The paper evaluates several adaptation hypotheses using standard techniques (Brown clusters over unlabeled target data, type-level gazetteers, limited token-level supervision with “frustratingly easy” domain features). The negative results are informative and help define a challenging testbed for fine-grained domain adaptation.
- Potential impact: Automating product identification in cybercrime forums is useful for characterizing market composition, tracking trends, and linking to user and price information. The dataset and baselines can enable both NLP research and security analyses.

Weaknesses:
- Formatting issue: It appears the abstract’s font size and line spacing have been altered, allowing a longer abstract and helping fit content into the 8-page limit. This is a serious formatting concern and should be rectified.
- Track fit: I do not think this paper fits a tagging/chunking/parsing area; the core is an information extraction task (product slot identification) with domain adaptation. It would be better placed in an IE or domain adaptation track.
- Annotation scope for “product”: I find it hard to see why certain tokens such as “somebody” in Fig. 1 are annotated as product-related. The guidelines say method tokens may be included when integral to delivery, but the boundary between products, methods, and agents needs clearer justification and examples. The decision to annotate verbs (e.g., “hack”) and certain human-agent terms seems to blur categories and may complicate learning and evaluation.
- Baseline strength: The “basic results are very basic indeed.” Given available tools, stronger baselines would seem feasible without much extra effort, e.g., CRF or BiLSTM-CRF sequence taggers with character features, pretrained embeddings, and lexicon features; or neural span predictors for NP-level extraction. This would better contextualize the difficulty of the task and the value of the proposed approaches.
- Limited novelty in domain adaptation findings: The cross-forum degradation largely corroborates known challenges with user-generated content and heterogeneous communities (e.g., different user bases lead to stylistic and lexical variation). Brown clusters and small gazetteers yield only small gains; limited token-level supervision helps asymmetrically. While the dataset is valuable, the adaptation insights themselves are not particularly new.

Questions to authors:
- Annotation definition: Could you clarify the criteria for annotating human-agent terms (e.g., “somebody,” “coder”) and method verbs as part of the “product”? Would a stricter focus on deliverables (excluding agents/methods) simplify the task and improve agreement/transfer?
- Baseline choices: Why not include standard sequence labeling baselines (CRF, BiLSTM-CRF with char features and pretrained embeddings) or simple neural span predictors for NP extraction? Do you anticipate these would change the cross-domain conclusions?
- Domain adaptation methods: Did you consider feature-space or representation-learning approaches (e.g., adversarial domain adaptation, shared-private representations) beyond Brown clusters and Daumé’s feature augmentation? If not, can you elaborate on constraints that prevented these?
- Gazetteer construction: The type-level gazetteers were built from training data with a frequency threshold. How sensitive are results to the threshold, stemming/lowercasing choices, and NP-level normalization (e.g., including modifiers like “backconnect”)? Would phrase-level gazetteers help?
- Evaluation framing: Given that most posts feature one core product, would a pure post-level prediction setup (select-one) be a better primary task definition, with token/NP provenance as secondary? Do you see better cross-forum robustness under this formulation?

Overall, this is a clear and useful dataset paper with careful evaluation and a well-motivated task. The main concerns are the formatting issue, the fit to the area, the annotation boundary for “product,” the limited strength of baselines, and the lack of new insights in domain adaptation beyond confirming known challenges.
