- Introduces a new dataset for product identification in four cybercrime forums (~94k posts, 1,938 annotated), with token→NP projection and multiple evaluation views (token, type, post).
- Empirically characterizes fine-grained cross-forum domain differences, showing substantial cross-forum degradation and limited gains from standard semi-supervised/adaptation methods.
- Provides baseline models and evaluation protocol: token-level SVM, post-level latent SVM, and simple baselines (dictionary, first-NP).

- Clear, well-structured presentation with careful task definition, annotation guidelines, and evaluation metrics.
- Dataset covers multiple distinct markets, includes IAA (Fleiss’ Kappa), and is well-documented for reuse by NLP and security communities.
- Thoughtful evaluation design (token vs NP; type vs post-level) and a practical insight that a single representative product per post can suffice for some tasks.
- Systematic empirical analysis testing Brown clusters, gazetteers, and feature-augmentation; negative results highlight a challenging testbed for fine-grained domain adaptation.
- Potential impact: enables automation of product identification to support market analysis, trend tracking, and linking to users/prices.

- Formatting concern: altered abstract font/spacing to fit 8 pages, a serious submission issue that must be corrected.
- Track fit: paper aligns more with information extraction/domain adaptation than tagging/chunking/parsing.
- Annotation scope: unclear justification for labeling agents/method verbs (e.g., “somebody”, “hack”) as products; boundaries between products, methods, and agents need clearer rules and examples.
- Baseline strength: reported baselines are weak—stronger, standard sequence-labeling models (CRF, BiLSTM-CRF, neural span predictors) should be evaluated to contextualize difficulty.
- Limited novelty in adaptation findings: cross-forum degradation and small gains from Brown clusters/gazetteers largely confirm known issues with user-generated content.

- Clarify criteria for annotating human-agent terms and method verbs as products, and whether restricting to deliverables would improve agreement and transfer.
- Explain omission of standard sequence-labeling baselines (CRF, BiLSTM-CRF, span predictors) and whether they might alter cross-domain conclusions.
- Did you try representation-learning/adversarial domain adaptation or shared-private models beyond Brown clusters and Daumé-style features?
- How sensitive are gazetteer results to frequency thresholds, normalization (stemming/lowercasing), and phrase-level vs NP-level construction?
- Given most posts contain one core product, would framing the task as a post-level select-one prediction improve robustness and be a better primary evaluation?
