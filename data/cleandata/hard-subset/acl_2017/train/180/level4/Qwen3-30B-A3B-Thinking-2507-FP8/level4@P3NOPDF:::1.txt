

This paper introduces a novel dataset of product annotations sourced from online cybercrime forums, presenting a clear methodology and well-executed experiments that rigorously test all hypotheses against appropriate baselines. The contribution of a specialized dataset for cybercrime analysis is valuable, and the writing is generally strong. However, several significant concerns regarding scope, technical execution, and scholarly contribution warrant careful consideration.  

First, the paper violates ACL formatting guidelines by altering the abstract’s font size and line spacing to extend its length and meet the 8-page limit. This is a procedural error that undermines the paper’s professionalism and adherence to submission standards. The abstract should be concise and formatted as specified; such adjustments are unacceptable and require correction prior to publication.  

Second, the paper’s core focus—annotating products within cybercrime forum discussions—aligns more closely with information extraction (IE) or security informatics than with the core themes of ACL, which emphasize tagging, chunking, and parsing. The work lacks theoretical or methodological contributions to these NLP subfields, and the annotations (e.g., product mentions) do not inherently relate to syntactic or semantic structure analysis. The authors should reconsider the venue fit or substantially reframe the contribution to justify its relevance to ACL’s scope.  

Third, the annotation scheme contains ambiguities that weaken the dataset’s utility. For instance, the label "somebody" in Figure 1 appears to refer to a user (e.g., "somebody posted a link"), yet the annotation treats it as a product-related entity. The relationship between such labels and the product-centric task is unclear. The authors must clarify the annotation guidelines and provide explicit examples demonstrating how these labels contribute to product identification. Without this, the dataset’s coherence and usefulness for downstream tasks are questionable.  

Fourth, the baseline results are disappointingly basic. Given the availability of advanced NLP tools (e.g., contextual embeddings, transformer-based models), the authors could have implemented more sophisticated baselines with minimal additional effort. The current baselines (e.g., simple rule-based or bag-of-words approaches) fail to demonstrate the dataset’s potential or provide meaningful benchmarks. This omission significantly limits the paper’s scientific value, as it does not advance the state of the art or offer actionable insights for the community.  

Finally, the domain adaptation experiments confirm well-established phenomena in user-generated content: forum-specific linguistic patterns (e.g., due to differing demographics in video game forums) lead to performance variations. While the results are logically consistent, they do not yield novel insights into cybercrime text analysis or domain adaptation strategies. The paper merely replicates known challenges without proposing new methods or theoretical frameworks to address them, diminishing its contribution.  

In summary, while the dataset itself has potential utility for security-focused research, the paper’s misalignment with ACL’s scope, technical formatting violations, ambiguous annotations, underdeveloped baselines, and lack of novel insights into domain adaptation collectively weaken its suitability for publication. The work would be better positioned in a venue focused on information extraction, security, or digital forensics.  

**Recommendation**: Weak Accept (with major revisions). To strengthen the submission, the authors must: (1) correct formatting violations; (2) reframe the contribution to explicitly address an ACL-relevant NLP problem (e.g., by proposing a parsing model for product mentions); (3) clarify annotation guidelines with concrete examples; (4) implement stronger baselines using modern NLP techniques; and (5) reframe the domain adaptation discussion to highlight novel methodological contributions. Without these changes, the paper does not meet ACL’s standards for technical novelty or scope relevance.
