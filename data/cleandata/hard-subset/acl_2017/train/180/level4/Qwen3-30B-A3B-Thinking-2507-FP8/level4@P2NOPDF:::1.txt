

This paper introduces a novel dataset of product annotations derived from online cybercrime forums. The writing is clear and the experimental design is sound, with all hypotheses rigorously tested against appropriate baselines. However, several significant concerns require addressing before publication.  

First, the abstract violates ACL formatting guidelines by altering font size and line spacing to accommodate additional content within the 8-page limit. This is unacceptable as it misrepresents the paper's length and undermines submission integrity. The authors must resubmit with a properly formatted abstract adhering to the conference’s technical specifications.  

Second, the paper’s core contribution—annotation of cybercrime forum content—falls outside ACL’s scope for tagging, chunking, and parsing. This is fundamentally an information extraction (IE) task, not syntactic or structural analysis. The authors should explicitly reposition the work within IE literature or revise the methodology to align with ACL’s focus areas; as currently framed, it lacks contextual relevance to the conference.  

Third, the annotation scheme lacks clarity in key examples. For instance, the "somebody" annotation in Figure 1 does not demonstrate a logical connection to a *product* (e.g., a stolen credit card or hacking tool). The paper must justify why such phrases qualify as product-related annotations, providing explicit criteria to resolve this ambiguity. Without this, the dataset’s utility and validity are undermined.  

Fourth, the baseline results are underdeveloped for a 2017 NLP submission. The experiments employ minimal baselines (e.g., simple bag-of-words classifiers) that fail to leverage contemporary NLP advances like contextual embeddings or transfer learning. Given the accessibility of strong pre-trained models and established IE frameworks, the authors could have implemented more competitive baselines with minimal additional effort. The current results do not establish a meaningful performance benchmark, weakening the paper’s technical contribution.  

Fifth, the domain adaptation experiments confirm well-known phenomena in user-generated text analysis (e.g., demographic-driven linguistic variation between gaming forums) rather than offering novel insights. This merely validates existing knowledge about forum-specific language patterns without advancing methodology or addressing cybercrime-specific challenges. The analysis lacks depth in explaining *why* these adaptations occur in this domain or proposing solutions beyond standard transfer learning approaches.  

In summary, while the dataset is a valuable resource for cybercrime research, the paper’s misalignment with ACL’s scope, formatting violations, ambiguous annotations, and lack of rigorous baselines significantly limit its suitability for publication. The authors should: (1) reframe the work as an IE contribution, (2) correct the abstract formatting, (3) clarify the annotation rationale with concrete examples, and (4) substantially strengthen the experimental baselines using state-of-the-art NLP techniques. Without these revisions, the paper fails to meet ACL’s standards for technical novelty and relevance.
