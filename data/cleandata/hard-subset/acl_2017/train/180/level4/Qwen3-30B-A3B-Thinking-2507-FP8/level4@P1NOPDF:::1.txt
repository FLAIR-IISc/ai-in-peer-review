

This paper introduces a novel dataset annotating products from online cybercrime forums. The writing is clear, the experiments well-executed, and all hypotheses are rigorously tested against appropriate baselines. However, I have several concerns that require attention:

1.  The authors altered the abstract's font size and line spacing to extend its length, circumventing the 8-page limit. This adjustment undermines the stated page constraint and appears inappropriate for a conference submission.

2.  I question whether this work aligns with ACL's focus on tagging, chunking, and parsing. The core contribution—annotating products within forum discussions—more accurately falls under information extraction (IE), not the specified NLP tasks. The paper should clarify its scope or justify its fit within ACL's purview.

3.  The rationale for certain annotations, such as "someone" in Figure 1, is unclear. It is difficult to see how these labels directly relate to the product being discussed, raising questions about annotation consistency and the defined task.

4.  The baseline results are surprisingly basic. Given the current state of NLP tools, more sophisticated baselines could have been established with minimal additional effort. The paper does not leverage available techniques (e.g., contextual embeddings, advanced sequence models) to demonstrate a stronger baseline, which weakens the comparative significance of the proposed method.

5.  The domain adaptation experiments confirm well-known challenges in user-generated content (e.g., differing user demographics across video game forums leading to stylistic variations). This outcome does not provide novel insights into the specific problem of cybercrime forum analysis and fails to advance the understanding of domain adaptation in this context.
