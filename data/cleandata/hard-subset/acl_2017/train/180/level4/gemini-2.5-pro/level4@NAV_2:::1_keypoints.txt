- Introduces token-level task for identifying products in cybercrime forums, combining NER and slot-filling.
- Presents a new dataset: >90k posts from four forums with ~2k manually annotated posts, released publicly.
- Demonstrates that models trained on one forum generalize poorly to others, defining a fine-grained domain adaptation challenge.
- Evaluates multiple baselines and standard adaptation techniques and finds only marginal improvements.
- Contribution: a valuable, high-impact annotated dataset for product identification in noisy, non-standard forum text.
- Contribution: systematic empirical analysis that documents cross-forum performance degradation and limited adaptation gains.
- Strength: provides a challenging real-world resource with clear security relevance.
- Strength: clear task formulation, careful annotation choices, and multiple evaluation metrics (token/type/post/phrase).
- Strength: well-structured experiments with detailed analysis (seen vs unseen products, cross-forum comparisons).
- Weakness: limited novelty—findings largely confirm known domain-adaptation behavior without proposing new solutions.
- Weakness: reliance on SVMs with engineered features—no modern neural baselines like BiLSTM-CRF or BERT.
- Weakness: some annotation guidelines appear subjective (e.g., "sombody"), and inter-annotator agreement is not very high.
- Question: please clarify the "integral part of the method" annotation principle and the "sombody" vs unannotated human agent cases.
- Question: have you evaluated standard neural sequence-tagging models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) as stronger baselines?
