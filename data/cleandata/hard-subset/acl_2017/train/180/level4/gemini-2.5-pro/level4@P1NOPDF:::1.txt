This paper presents a new dataset of annotated product information extracted from online cybercrime forums. The paper is clearly written and the experiments are well-designed, with each hypothesis being systematically tested and compared. However, I have several concerns that I believe should be addressed:

1.  It appears that the font size and line spacing in the abstract have been altered, which allowed for a longer abstract and may have been used to ensure the paper meets the 8-page limit. Authors must adhere strictly to the conference formatting guidelines.

2.  The paper's fit with the "Tagging, Chunking, and Parsing" track is questionable. The work is fundamentally an information extraction task and would seem to be more appropriately placed in an IE or applications-focused track.

3.  The rationale behind some annotation decisions is unclear. For example, in Figure 1, it is not obvious why a term like "somebody" is annotated as being product-related. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed explanation of the annotation guidelines to clarify such cases.

4.  The baselines used for comparison are quite simplistic. Given the wide range of sophisticated and readily available NLP tools, it would have been possible to include more advanced baselines with minimal additional effort. Stronger baselines would provide a more meaningful context for evaluating the proposed model's performance and would significantly strengthen the paper's experimental contributions.

5.  The domain adaptation experiments largely corroborate a well-established finding: language use can vary significantly across different user-generated content platforms, even those covering similar topics (e.g., two different video game forums), due to differing user demographics. As this is a known characteristic of online communities, these results do not offer significant new insights into the problem of domain adaptation.
