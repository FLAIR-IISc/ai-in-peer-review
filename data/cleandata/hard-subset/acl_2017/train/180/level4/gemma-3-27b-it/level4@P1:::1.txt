This paper presents a new dataset with annotations of products from online cybercrime forums. The paper is clear and well-written, and the experiments are thorough. Each hypothesis is tested and compared to others. However, I do have some concerns.

1. The authors appear to have adjusted the font size and line spacing of the abstract to maximize space and meet the page limit.
2. I question whether this paper best fits the tagging, chunking, or parsing area; it more closely aligns with information extraction.
3. I find it unclear why certain annotations, such as “somebody” in Figure 1, are considered related to a product.
4. The baseline results are relatively basic. Given the current state of NLP, I believe more sophisticated baselines could have been implemented with reasonable effort.
5. The domain adaptation experiments largely confirm existing knowledge about user-generated data, where variations in user demographics (e.g., age, gender) across forums like those dedicated to video games can lead to significant textual differences. Therefore, this work doesn’t offer substantial new insights into this well-known problem.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the creation and release of a novel, annotated dataset of posts from four online cybercrime forums, specifically focused on identifying products being bought and sold. This dataset is valuable due to the unique challenges posed by the domain – namely, the fine-grained differences even *between* cybercrime forums. A second, and related, contribution is the characterization of these domain differences through empirical evaluation of supervised learning and domain adaptation techniques. The results demonstrate the difficulty of adapting models across these forums, highlighting the need for improved methods. Finally, the paper provides a clear formulation of the task as a blend of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition, which may be useful for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The dataset itself is a significant contribution. Cybercrime forums are a difficult data source to obtain and analyze, and the annotated data provides a valuable resource for researchers in NLP, security, and related fields. The detailed statistics in Table 1 are helpful for understanding the dataset’s characteristics.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of various baseline and adaptation methods. They demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges and nuances of the task and consider multiple evaluation metrics (token-level, type-level, post-level), providing a nuanced picture of the performance.
3. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the task and its relationship to existing NLP problems like NER and slot filling. The justification for token-level annotation, to ensure generality, is well-reasoned.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The discussion of the domain adaptation experiments and the analysis of OOV rates (Table 5) provide valuable insights into the specific challenges of this domain and potential directions for future research.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to releasing the dataset, models, and code, which will greatly facilitate further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Baseline Complexity:** While the baselines are functional, they are relatively simple, especially considering the state-of-the-art in NLP. Exploring more advanced baselines (e.g., utilizing pre-trained word embeddings or more sophisticated feature engineering) could have provided a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed methods.
2. **Domain Adaptation Results:** The domain adaptation experiments largely confirm known challenges with user-generated content. While the results are important for documenting the difficulty of the task, they do not present significantly novel findings in the domain adaptation literature itself. The analysis of why existing methods fail to perform well could be strengthened.
3. **Annotation Justification:** The rationale for certain annotation choices, specifically the inclusion of terms like “somebody” in Figure 1, is not entirely convincing. A more detailed explanation of the annotation guidelines and examples would improve clarity.
4. **Presentation of Results:** The results are presented in numerous tables. While comprehensive, this can make it challenging to quickly grasp the key findings. A more concise and visually-driven presentation of the most important results would be beneficial.
5. **Formatting Issue:** The author’s comment regarding the altered abstract formatting is concerning. While it's understandable to meet page limits, altering formatting to increase content raises questions about adherence to conference guidelines.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the annotation guidelines, including specific examples that clarify the rationale behind potentially ambiguous annotations (e.g., "somebody" in Figure 1)?
2. Were any more sophisticated baselines considered during the experimentation phase? If so, why were they ultimately excluded from the final paper?
3. What specific characteristics of cybercrime forums make domain adaptation particularly challenging compared to other types of online forums?
4. Could you elaborate on potential avenues for future research to address the limitations of existing domain adaptation techniques in this context?
