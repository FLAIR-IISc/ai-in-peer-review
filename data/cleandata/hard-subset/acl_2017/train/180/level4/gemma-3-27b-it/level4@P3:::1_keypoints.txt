- Presents a large-scale, token-level annotated dataset (93,924 posts, 1,938 annotated) from four cybercrime forums for product extraction.  
- Annotation scheme captures explicit product mentions and related services, offering a valuable resource for studying online criminal marketplaces.  
- Demonstrates substantial fine-grained domain shift between different cybercrime forums, causing significant cross-forum performance degradation.  
- Systematically evaluates baseline and domain adaptation methods (including Brown clusters, gazetteers, token-level adaptation) and provides a comprehensive empirical benchmark.  
- Releases dataset, models, and code to promote reproducibility and further research.  
- Clearly motivates the task and frames it as a hybrid of slot-filling and NER relevant to security and law enforcement.  
- Employs rigorous evaluation using multiple metrics (token-level, type-level, post-level) and within- and cross-domain experiments.  
- Provides a detailed annotation process with refinement rounds and inter-annotator agreement measures, enhancing dataset credibility.  
- Offers insightful analysis of results and limitations, including an OOV analysis that sheds light on domain shift characteristics.  
- Overall contribution is valuable and the work is clearly written and well-motivated.  
- Limited novelty in domain adaptation methods; findings largely echo known challenges with user-generated content.  
- Some annotation decisions (e.g., labeling tokens like "somebody") lack clear justification and need better explanation.  
- Baselines are relatively basic; stronger baselines using pre-trained/contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT) are missing.  
- Minor formatting deviation (font size/line spacing in abstract) from conference guidelines.  
- Task classification comment: fits more naturally under information extraction than tagging/chunking/parsing, but not a major flaw.  
- Question: Please justify annotation choices for ambiguous tokens (e.g., "somebody") and clarify criteria for product-relatedness.  
- Question: What prevented implementing stronger baselines using pre-trained language models?  
- Question: Based on the OOV analysis, which features or modeling approaches do authors think could best address domain shift?  
- Question: Since authors performed annotation, how was potential researcher bias mitigated during guideline development?  
- Figures/tables are clear and well-organized, and the evaluation metrics discussion is well-motivated.
