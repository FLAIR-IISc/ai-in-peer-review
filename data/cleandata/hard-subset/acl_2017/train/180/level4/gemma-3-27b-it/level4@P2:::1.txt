This paper introduces a novel dataset of posts from online cybercrime forums, annotated with references to products being bought and sold. The work is clearly presented and well-written, with thorough experimentation and evaluation of proposed hypotheses. The primary contribution is the creation and release of this dataset, which provides a valuable resource for research into information extraction from a challenging and important domain. A secondary contribution lies in the characterization of the difficulties in adapting models across different cybercrime forums, demonstrating the fine-grained domain shift even within this specialized context.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Valuable Dataset:** The dataset itself is a significant contribution. The annotation scheme, while complex, allows for both post-level and token-level analysis, making it versatile for a range of information extraction tasks. The size of the dataset (93,924 posts, 1,938 annotated) is substantial and will likely enable further research in this area. The authors clearly articulate the potential applications of the dataset, including characterization of forum composition, trend identification, and user profiling.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors systematically evaluate several baseline models and domain adaptation techniques. They test a variety of metrics – token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, and post-level accuracy – providing a comprehensive picture of model performance. The inclusion of both within-forum and cross-forum evaluation is critical for understanding the challenges of domain adaptation in this context.
3. **Realistic Problem Setting:** The task addresses a real-world problem with significant implications for cybersecurity. Analyzing online cybercrime marketplaces can provide valuable insights into emerging threats and criminal activity. The study acknowledges the unique characteristics of this data, such as ungrammaticality, spelling errors, and forum-specific jargon, making the work relevant and impactful.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The analysis of OOV (out-of-vocabulary) rates and the discussion of why certain adaptation techniques fail provide valuable insights into the nature of the domain shift. Recognizing that Darkode may be a more general domain than Hack Forums is a useful observation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Domain Adaptation Results:** While the paper demonstrates the difficulty of domain adaptation, the findings largely corroborate existing knowledge about user-generated content and the challenges of transferring models across different online communities. The observation that forums with different user demographics exhibit different linguistic characteristics is not particularly surprising. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *why* existing domain adaptation techniques fail in this specific context and proposing more innovative solutions.
2. **Annotation Ambiguity:** The example in Figure 1 raises questions about the annotation criteria. The inclusion of "sombody" as a product-related token is unclear and could lead to inconsistencies. A more rigorous justification for such annotations is needed. It’s important to ensure the annotation guidelines are consistently applied and well-defined.
3. **Basic Baselines:** The baselines employed are relatively simple. While providing a starting point, the authors acknowledge that more sophisticated baselines are feasible. Exploring more advanced techniques, such as pre-trained language models or more comprehensive product lexicons, could provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed models.
4. **Formatting Concerns:** The authors' adjustment of font size and line spacing to meet page limits, while understandable, is not ideal. It potentially compromises readability and may be viewed negatively by reviewers.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind annotating tokens like “sombody” in Figure 1?  Please clarify the criteria used to determine when a person or service provider is considered integral to the product being sold.
2. What specific challenges inherent in the cybercrime domain make existing domain adaptation techniques less effective? Are there unique characteristics of this data (e.g., deliberate obfuscation, code-switching) that contribute to the difficulty?
3. Have you considered using pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as a starting point for your models? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?



**Additional Comments:**

* The phrase "frustratingly easy" domain adaptation (Daume III, 2007) could be phrased more neutrally.
* The distinction between type-level and token-level evaluation is well-explained and justified.
