This paper introduces a novel dataset comprising product mentions annotated from online cybercrime forums. The writing is clear and the experiments are well-executed, with each hypothesis systematically tested and compared. However, I have several concerns regarding the work.

First, the authors appear to have manipulated the formatting of the abstract – specifically, font size and line spacing – to exceed the standard length constraints and accommodate additional content within the eight-page limit. This is a concerning practice.

Second, the paper’s categorization within the tagging, chunking, and parsing area seems misaligned. The core task more closely resembles information extraction, given the focus on identifying and classifying product mentions within unstructured text. A more appropriate venue might be a conference focused on information extraction or similar fields.

Third, the relevance of certain annotations is unclear. For example, as illustrated in Figure 1, the connection between annotations like “somebody” and the identification of a product is not readily apparent, raising questions about annotation consistency or the schema's clarity. Further justification for these types of annotations would be beneficial.

Fourth, while the baseline results are reported, they are rather limited in scope. Given the wealth of readily available NLP tools and techniques, more sophisticated baselines could likely have been established with reasonable effort. Exploring stronger baselines would provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach and dataset.

Finally, the domain adaptation experiments largely confirm existing findings regarding the variability of user-generated text. The observation that forums dedicated to different topics (e.g., video games) attract distinct user demographics, leading to divergent textual patterns, is already well-established. While the experiments demonstrate this phenomenon within the context of the new dataset, they do not offer significant new insights into the challenges of domain adaptation itself.
