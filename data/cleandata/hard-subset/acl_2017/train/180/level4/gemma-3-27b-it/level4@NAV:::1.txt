This paper introduces a novel dataset comprising product listings sourced from online cybercrime forums, accompanied by detailed annotations. The manuscript is well-structured and clearly written, and the experimental evaluation is sound. The authors thoroughly investigate their hypotheses through comparative analysis. However, I have several points of concern.

First, the abstract appears to have been adjusted—through alterations to font size and line spacing—to exceed typical length constraints and meet the page limit. Second, I question the paper’s categorization within the tagging, chunking, and parsing area; the core task appears more aligned with information extraction. Third, the relevance of certain annotations, such as the example provided in Fig. 1, to the product itself is unclear. Fourth, while the presented results are valid, they are relatively straightforward. Given the current state of NLP tools, more sophisticated baselines could reasonably have been implemented with limited additional effort. Finally, the domain adaptation experiments largely confirm established findings regarding user-generated data – specifically, that variations in user demographics across platforms (e.g., differing age or gender distributions in video game forums) naturally lead to textual differences. Consequently, these results do not offer substantial new insights into this well-known phenomenon.
