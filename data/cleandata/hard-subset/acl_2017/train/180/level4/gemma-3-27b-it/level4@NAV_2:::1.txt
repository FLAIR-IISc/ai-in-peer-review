This paper presents a new dataset with annotations of products from online cybercrime forums. The paper is clear and well-written, and the experiments are thorough. Each hypothesis is tested and comparatively analyzed. However, I have several concerns.

The primary contribution of this work is the creation and release of a novel, annotated dataset of posts from four cybercrime forums, specifically focused on identifying products being bought and sold. A secondary contribution is the empirical characterization of the challenges associated with domain adaptation in this setting, demonstrating that even within the cybercrime domain, significant differences exist between forums. Finally, the paper provides a formulated task that represents a hybrid of slot-filling information extraction and named entity recognition.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Dataset:** The dataset is a significant contribution. The annotation scheme, covering four distinct forums, offers a valuable resource for researchers in cybersecurity and NLP. The size of the dataset (93,924 posts, 1,938 annotated) is substantial and provides a solid basis for further research.
2.  **Realistic Task Formulation:** The task of identifying products in cybercrime marketplaces is a practically relevant problem with real-world applications in threat intelligence and law enforcement. Framing it as a blend of slot-filling and NER is a sensible approach, acknowledging the complexities of the data.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, evaluating various baseline and learning-based methods for product extraction and domain adaptation. The inclusion of multiple evaluation metrics (token-level accuracy, type-level extraction, post-level accuracy) provides a nuanced understanding of the system performance.
4.  **Clear Demonstration of Domain Shift:** The results clearly demonstrate the difficulties of applying models trained on one forum to another, highlighting the importance of domain adaptation techniques in this specific context. This empirical evidence strengthens the motivation for further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Formatting Irregularities:** The authors altered the abstract’s formatting (font size and line spacing) to meet page limits. While seemingly minor, this practice is unconventional and raises concerns about adherence to conference guidelines.
2.  **Discipline Alignment:** The categorization of the paper within the “tagging, chunking, parsing” area seems inaccurate. The core problem is fundamentally information extraction, and positioning it elsewhere might attract a more relevant audience and facilitate more targeted feedback.
3.  **Annotation Ambiguity:** The rationale behind certain annotations, such as "somebody" in Figure 1, is unclear. A more rigorous justification for inclusion criteria would strengthen the dataset's reliability and reproducibility.
4.  **Limited Baseline Strength:** The baselines employed are relatively simple. Given the current state-of-the-art in NLP, more sophisticated baselines (e.g., pre-trained language models with fine-tuning) could provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed methods.
5.  **Expected Domain Adaptation Results:** The observed performance degradation across forums aligns with established findings regarding user-generated content and domain shift. While the empirical validation is valuable, the results do not offer substantial new insights beyond what is already known in the field.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the annotation of terms like “somebody” in Figure 1? What criteria were used to determine whether a non-product term is relevant to the product being sold?
2.  What steps were taken to ensure consistency in annotation across all four forums, given the differing characteristics of each forum?
3.  Were any pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) considered as baselines for the product extraction task? If not, why?
