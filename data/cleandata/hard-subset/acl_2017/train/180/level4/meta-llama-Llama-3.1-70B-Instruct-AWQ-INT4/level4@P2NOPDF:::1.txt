This paper presents a new dataset with annotations of products from online cybercrime forums. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, and the experiments are thorough, with each hypothesis tested and compared to others. However, I have several concerns that impact my overall assessment of the paper.

Firstly, I noticed that the authors have altered the font size and line spacing in the abstract, allowing for a longer abstract that fits within the 8-page limit. While I understand the desire to include more content, I question the decision to modify the formatting in this way.

Furthermore, I am not convinced that this paper aligns with the tagging, chunking, and parsing area, as it appears to be more focused on information extraction. The authors may want to consider whether their work is a better fit for a different area of focus.

Upon reviewing the annotations, I struggled to understand the relevance of certain labels, such as "somebody" in Figure 1, to the product being described. The authors may want to provide additional clarification or context to help readers understand the reasoning behind these annotations.

The baseline results presented in the paper are somewhat underwhelming, particularly given the wealth of tools and resources available in the field of NLP. I believe that the authors could have easily developed more sophisticated baselines with minimal additional effort.

Finally, the domain adaptation experiments, while thorough, do not provide significant new insights into the problem of user-generated data. The finding that different online forums (e.g., video game forums with varying demographics) exhibit distinct linguistic characteristics is not particularly surprising, and the authors may want to consider whether this aspect of the paper adds substantial value to the overall contribution.
