## Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging. The authors explore multi-task learning to further improve performance. The core finding is that while dependency parsing proves ineffective, a simpler BiLSTM-based sequence tagging model with distance encoding performs robustly across classification scenarios, and benefits from jointly learning related subtasks.

The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. The authors convincingly demonstrate the limitations of a dependency parsing approach for this task, a result that challenges previous suggestions.  A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the demonstration of the effectiveness of a relatively straightforward sequence tagging model, and the benefit of incorporating auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup. Finally, the thorough experimental setup and comparison against a strong baseline (ILP model) provide a solid foundation for future research.

Strengths of the paper include the clear problem definition, the comprehensive experimental evaluation across different model architectures, and the insightful analysis of results. The ablation studies, particularly regarding multi-task learning, are well-executed and provide valuable guidance. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the challenges of long-range dependencies in longer texts. The inclusion of detailed results for both paragraph and essay levels allows for a nuanced understanding of model performance.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The motivation for exploring dependency parsing feels somewhat underdeveloped, given the authors’ eventual finding that it is ineffective. A more thorough discussion of *why* it fails would be beneficial. The description of the multi-task learning setup could be more detailed, specifically regarding the selection of auxiliary tasks and the layer integration strategy. While the authors mention LSTM-ER, the discussion of its results feels somewhat cursory, and a deeper comparison to the other models would be valuable. The writing style is occasionally dense and could benefit from more concise phrasing.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific reasons you hypothesized dependency parsing might be a suitable framing for AM, given its eventual poor performance?
2.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, what criteria were used to select the auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification)?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of LSTM-ER’s performance to STagBLCC, particularly focusing on the types of errors each model makes?
