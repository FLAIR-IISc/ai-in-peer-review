Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus. It compares four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) token-level sequence tagging with a label that jointly encodes BIO, component type, distance-to-head component, and stance, (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary tasks for components and relations, and (iv) a span-level entity+relation model (LSTM-ER; Miwa & Bansal, 2016). The authors report that dependency parsing underperforms; BiLSTM-based tagging is robust and competitive; jointly learning natural subtasks in an MTL setup improves performance; and LSTM-ER excels on components and on shorter (paragraph-level) inputs. Several neural models match or surpass a feature-based joint ILP baseline.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) A thorough and well-controlled comparison of neural end-to-end framings for AM at token level, including the design of a novel distance/stance-aware tagging scheme and an MTL variant, with state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset.
2) An empirical analysis separating paragraph- vs essay-level performance and the effect of model inductive biases (local vs global, constrained vs unconstrained) on relation prediction under varying input lengths/entity counts.
3) A negative result: token-level dependency parsing is substantially weaker than tagging for end-to-end AM in this dataset/setting.

Strengths
- Clear and insightful problem framing and model comparison. The paper contrasts local tagging vs global parsing vs modular entity–relation modeling, with careful discussion of constraints and search space. The distance-aware tagging scheme is a practical idea that leverages the empirical distance distribution to bias learning toward plausible links.

- Strong empirical evidence with multiple baselines. Results include feature-based parsers (MST, Mate), neural parsers (Dyer; Kiperwasser & Goldberg), BLCC tagger, MTL tagger variants, LSTM-ER, and ILP. Paragraph vs essay analyses make the findings more informative. Statistical testing is reported for main tables.

- Practical takeaways for AM. The paper articulates when to prefer more constrained local models (long documents/many entities) vs more general relational models (short documents/few entities), and shows that de-coupling components and relations (LSTM-ER; MTL) helps component detection without sacrificing overall end-to-end performance.

- End-to-end, feature-free pipelines. The work reduces reliance on hand-crafted features and ILP constraint design, while achieving competitive or better performance than the ILP approach.

- Sensible MTL findings. Including component and relation auxiliary tasks improves the full tagging objective; the analysis that low-level tasks do not necessarily feed best from the lowest layer is useful and nuanced.

Weaknesses
- Generality beyond a single dataset. All results are on one corpus with a specific near-tree argument structure and paragraph-local relations (except claim–major claim). Claims about the (in)effectiveness of dependency parsing and the superiority of tagging may not generalize to corpora with different structures (e.g., microtexts, multi-party debates, multi-root graphs). A validation on at least one additional AM dataset would strengthen the conclusions.

- Token-level dependency parsing may be a strawman. The negative result may be driven by modeling choices (token-level trees over very long sequences, paragraph-only training for memory, specific transition systems). A component-level/hierarchical parser (predict spans, then parse spans) or biaffine span-dependency parsing might be more appropriate. As framed, the conclusion that “dependency parsing is ineffective” seems too strong.

- Label space explosion and factorization. The joint label (BIO × type × distance × stance) can be large and sparse, especially for rare long distances. While the method works, the paper does not discuss handling unseen distances at test time, capping/truncation of |d|, or alternative factorizations (e.g., CRF on BIO/type with separate heads for distance and stance, or pointer/biaffine link prediction over predicted spans). The MTL section hints at benefits of factorization but does not explore stronger architectures (e.g., BLCC+MTL).

- Limited analysis of errors and constraints. The decoding/post-processing from token labels to component spans and links is not fully specified, nor is the handling of structural violations (multiple heads, conflicting distances within a span, invalid stances for certain component types). A finer-grained error analysis by distance bucket, component type, and paragraph position would better explain where each model fails.

- Fairness of comparisons across levels and resources. Some parsers are trained at paragraph level due to memory constraints, while essay-level results are reported for others; LSTM-ER degrades on essay-level relations partly due to O(m^2) pairing. It would help to standardize training/evaluation regimes (or add hierarchical/paragraph-aware variants) to ensure conclusions are not confounded by input length.

Questions to authors
- How do you decode from token-level labels to component spans and relations in cases of inconsistency (e.g., mixed distance/stance tags within a span, multiple B tags, or no head for a premise)? Do you enforce any global constraints at decoding time?

- Did you cap or bucket the distance d during training/inference? How do you handle unseen d values at test time? What is the size of the effective label set Y in the trained models?

- For MTL, did you try a BLCC (char-CNN + CRF) backbone with the same auxiliary tasks? If not, can you comment on anticipated trade-offs vs BL?

- For dependency parsing, did you consider a two-stage setup (span prediction, then component-level parsing) or a span-based neural parser? If not feasible, could you report memory/runtime constraints to justify the token-level choice?

- How sensitive are the models to pre-trained embeddings and hyperparameters (dropout, tagger depth)? Any variance estimates over multiple runs?

Additional comments
- The discussion of distance distributions is helpful; adding per-bucket precision/recall for relations would further clarify where LSTM-ER vs STagBLCC wins/loses.

- Given the near-tree structure, a light-touch post-hoc constraint (e.g., one parent per premise, preferring short d) might boost relation F1 without sacrificing end-to-end simplicity; worth considering in future work.
