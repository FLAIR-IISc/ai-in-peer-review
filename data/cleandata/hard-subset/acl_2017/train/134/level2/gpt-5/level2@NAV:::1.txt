Summary: The paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on persuasive essays, comparing four framings: token-level dependency parsing, a composite sequence tagging scheme that encodes component BIO/type, relation distance, and stance (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), multi-task tagging with auxiliary component/relation subtasks, and an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) that decouples entity and relation extraction. Using the PE dataset and the C-F1/R-F1 metrics at 100%/50% span match, the authors report that sequence tagging is robust and outperforms a feature-based ILP baseline; token-level dependency parsing lags behind; MTL provides modest gains; and LSTM-ER excels on paragraph-level but degrades on essay-level, especially on relations.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A strong empirical study establishing that distance-augmented BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN) tagging delivers state-of-the-art end-to-end AM on PE, surpassing an ILP baseline without hand-engineered features/constraints.
2) A clear negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, especially on long documents.
3) Evidence that auxiliary multitask supervision (component and/or relation subtasks) yields consistent, if modest, improvements.

Strengths:
- Thorough comparative evaluation across diverse modeling paradigms with significance testing; the tagging model consistently improves both component and relation F1 over ILP and neural parsers, and MTL boosts F1 (e.g., +~4 points on essay-level overall F1 from Y-only to Y+YC).
- Practical and data-driven insight on document length: models that constrain relation search (distance-aware tags) cope better on essays than more general pairwise relation models (LSTM-ER), a valuable guideline for practitioners.
- Reduced reliance on hand-crafted features/constraints while matching or exceeding SOTA baselines; the work meaningfully advances end-to-end AM.
- Sensible analysis of why LSTM-ER underperforms on essays (O(m^2) relation candidates; 92% cross-sentence links) and why label distance biases help.

Weaknesses:
- Limited external validity: all results are on a single dataset with specific structural properties (paragraph-contained structures, claim-to-all-major-claims). Generalization to other AM corpora is untested.
- The composite tag space grows with distance values, risking sparsity; the paper does not quantify label cardinality, truncation/capping, or ablations on distance encoding.
- The LSTM-ER comparison may be disadvantaged by sentence-level dependency TreeLSTM despite predominantly cross-sentence relations; alternative relation architectures (e.g., document-level attention or pointer networks) are not evaluated.
- Dependency parsing is largely evaluated at paragraph level due to memory; cross-level comparability is uneven, and stronger global decoding constraints for taggers (beyond CRF) are not explored.

Questions to authors:
- What is the realized size of the tag set (unique (b,t,d,s)) and how are long-distance values handled (capping/bucketing)? Sensitivity to this choice?
- Do results hold on other AM datasets with different structures? Any domain transfer experiments?
- Can a document-level relation classifier (without sentence-level trees) within LSTM-ER mitigate the essay-level R-F1 drop?
