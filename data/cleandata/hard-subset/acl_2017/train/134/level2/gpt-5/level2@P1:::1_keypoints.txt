- Paper compares four neural end-to-end framings for argumentation mining on the Persuasive Essays corpus and reports tagging and MTL outperform token-level dependency parsing, with LSTM-ER strong on components/short inputs.  
- Main contribution: thorough, controlled comparison of token-level neural framings including a novel distance/stance-aware tagging scheme and MTL variant with SOTA results on PE.  
- Main contribution: empirical analysis separating paragraph- vs essay-level performance and the effect of model inductive biases (local vs global, constrained vs unconstrained) on relation prediction.  
- Main contribution: negative result showing token-level dependency parsing is substantially weaker than tagging for end-to-end AM in this setting.  
- Strength: clear, insightful framing contrasting local tagging, global parsing, and modular entity–relation models, and proposing a practical distance-aware tagging bias.  
- Strength: strong empirical evidence with multiple baselines (feature-based and neural), paragraph vs essay analyses, and statistical testing.  
- Strength: practical recommendations on when to prefer constrained local models (long documents/many entities) vs general relational models (short documents/few entities); decoupling components and relations helps component detection.  
- Strength: end-to-end, feature-free pipelines that reduce reliance on hand-crafted features and ILP while matching or surpassing ILP baselines.  
- Strength: sensible MTL findings showing auxiliary component/relation tasks improve tagging and that low-level tasks don’t always benefit most from the lowest layer.  
- Weakness: all experiments on a single dataset with near-tree, paragraph-local relations, limiting generality to other AM corpora/structures.  
- Weakness: token-level dependency parsing may be a strawman—negative result could stem from modeling choices; span-level or hierarchical parsers might be more appropriate.  
- Weakness: joint label (BIO × type × distance × stance) can explode/sparsify the label space; paper lacks discussion on capping/bucketing unseen distances or alternative factorizations.  
- Weakness: decoding/post-processing from token labels to spans/links is under-specified and lacks fine-grained error analysis by distance, type, and position.  
- Weakness: comparisons may be unfair due to inconsistent training regimes (paragraph vs essay) and resource constraints affecting some models (e.g., LSTM-ER’s O(m^2) pairing).  
- Question: how do you decode token-level labels into spans/relations when labels within a span conflict, and do you enforce global constraints at decoding?  
- Question: did you cap or bucket distance d during training/inference, how are unseen d values handled, and what is the effective label set size?  
- Question: did you try an alternative backbone (BLCC char-CNN + CRF) with the same MTL tasks, and how would that trade off with BL?  
- Question: for dependency parsing, did you consider a two-stage span-then-parse setup or span-based parser, and can you report memory/runtime constraints for the token-level choice?  
- Question: how sensitive are models to pretrained embeddings and hyperparameters, and do you have variance estimates over multiple runs?  
- Additional comment: adding per-distance-bucket precision/recall would clarify where LSTM-ER vs tagging wins or loses.  
- Additional comment: given the near-tree structure, a light post-hoc constraint (e.g., one parent per premise, prefer short distances) might boost relation F1 while retaining simplicity.
