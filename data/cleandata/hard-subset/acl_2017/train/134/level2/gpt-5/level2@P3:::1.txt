Summary and main contributions
This paper studies neural, end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus, comparing several framings: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary tasks for components and relations, and a joint entity–relation model (LSTM-ER). Evaluation is conducted at both paragraph and essay levels using component and relation F1 with exact (100%) and approximate (50%) span matching.

As seen from the submission, the primary contributions are:
1) A thorough empirical study of end-to-end neural AM across multiple modeling paradigms with detailed analysis of length effects (paragraph vs essay), relation distance distributions, and task coupling/decoupling. The study yields consistent, data-backed insights about what works and why.
2) A robust sequence tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distances in the label space, delivering strong and often state-of-the-art performance without hand-crafted features or ILP constraints.
3) Evidence that multi-task learning with “natural” subtasks (component and relation prediction) improves the end-to-end tagger, and that a token-level dependency-parsing framing underperforms substantially in this setting.

Strengths
- Strong empirical comparison with careful baselines and metrics. The paper contrasts four families: feature-based and neural dependency parsers, neural sequence tagging (BLCC), MTL tagging (BL), and LSTM-ER, plus the prior ILP system. Reported numbers are detailed and include exact/approximate matching and global F1. For example, on paragraph level, STagBLCC achieves C-F1 66.69/74.08 and R-F1 39.83/44.02, outperforming the best neural parser (LSTM-Parser: C-F1 58.86/68.20; R-F1 35.63/40.87) and competitive with or better than the ILP baseline (C-F1 62.61/73.35; R-F1 34.74/44.29). On essay level, STagBLCC remains robust (C-F1 60.46/63.23; R-F1 34.82/39.68).
- Clear, data-driven analysis explaining the results. The paper links modeling behavior to structural properties of the data: the distribution of inter-component distances (Figure 2), the combinatorics of entity pairs at essay level (penalizing unconstrained relation models), and long sequence effects (parser deterioration). The probability-of-correctness vs. |d| analysis (Figure 4) concretely illustrates the inductive bias differences between STagBLCC and LSTM-ER.
- New state-of-the-art results without feature engineering or ILP constraints. On paragraph level, LSTM-ER attains C-F1 70.83/77.19 and R-F1 45.52/50.05, substantially exceeding the ILP system, while STagBLCC improves over the best neural parser and is competitive with ILP on relations. The work demonstrates that neural end-to-end AM can match or surpass feature-intensive pipelines.
- Insightful findings about task coupling. Decoupling components and relations while modeling them jointly (LSTM-ER, or STag with auxiliary component/relation tasks) improves component detection; naïvely coupling both in a single label space is suboptimal. MTL ablations (Table 2) show consistent gains from adding YC and/or YR, e.g., Y-3:YC-3 improves C-F1(100%) from 49.59 to 54.58 and R-F1(50%) from 37.00 to 40.30.
- Relevance and timeliness. End-to-end AM is important for downstream applications and the paper addresses practical concerns: avoidance of dataset-specific feature/constraint engineering, and providing models that generalize to long-range dependencies.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset scope and generalization. All experiments are on a single English dataset with specific structural regularities (e.g., relations contained within paragraphs except claim→major-claim; near-tree structures). It remains unclear how well the conclusions transfer to other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts, legal decisions, scientific discourse) with different granularity or graph structures. Essential: add at least one additional corpus or a cross-domain evaluation, or provide stronger evidence via stratified analyses (e.g., per-paragraph vs cross-paragraph relations).
- The “dependency parsing is ineffective” conclusion is tied to a token-level design and resource constraints. Parsers are trained at paragraph level (essay-level training was memory-exhaustive), and heads are defined at token level with span recovery via labels. Component-first parsing (detect spans, then parse components), neural semantic/AM-specific parsers, or span-based dependency/constituency formalisms could yield different outcomes. Essential: temper the claim and, if feasible, include a component-level parsing baseline or a span-based parser.
- Label-space scalability and sparsity in sequence tagging. Encoding relation distances into tags leads to a large label inventory and data sparsity for long distances; this may limit transfer to domains with more distant links. Optional: report the effective tagset size and frequency distribution, ablate distance bucketing/pruning, or replace hard encoding with a separate predicted distance head.
- Constrained decoding and structural validity are under-explored. The evaluation does not quantify structural violations (e.g., multiple parents per premise) or the effect of simple constraints at inference time. For LSTM-ER on essays, introducing light constraints (one parent per premise, same-paragraph candidate pruning) or global decoding might mitigate the R-F1 drop. Essential: include a constrained-decoding ablation for LSTM-ER and/or STag to assess the trade-off between generality and structural validity.
- Reporting of variance and training details. Some models (e.g., LSTM-Parser) show high variance; however, averages over multiple runs and standard deviations are not provided for the main neural results. Essential: report mean±std over several seeds for all neural models; clarify class weighting/negative sampling, early stopping, and any distance cutoff.

Questions to clarify during author response (optional)
- What is the effective size of the STag label set on PE (min/median/max |d| used, any bucketing or pruning)? How are rare distance labels handled?
- Were class imbalances (non-argumentative tokens, support vs. attack) addressed via loss weighting or sampling?
- For LSTM-ER on essays, were constraints such as “one parent per premise” or restricting candidate pairs to the same paragraph tried? If so, how did R-F1 change?
- How many random seeds were used for the main neural models, and what is the variance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Essential: (a) add multi-run statistics; (b) include at least one constrained-decoding experiment for LSTM-ER and/or STag; (c) moderate the dependency-parsing claim or add a component-level/ span-based parsing baseline.
- Optional (nice-to-have): (a) evaluate on an additional AM corpus or provide cross-domain analysis; (b) ablate distance encoding (bucketing vs. exact d) and report tagset size; (c) provide per-class (MC/C/P, support/attack) F1 and structural-validity metrics.

Overall assessment
A solid and insightful empirical contribution that advances end-to-end AM with neural methods and provides actionable modeling guidance. With the essential clarifications and modest additional experiments, this work would be a strong addition to the program.
