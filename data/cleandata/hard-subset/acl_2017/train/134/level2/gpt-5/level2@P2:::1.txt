Summary and contributions
This paper studies neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays (PE) dataset. It compares four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) token-level sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes component BIO/type, relation distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task sequence tagging using natural subtasks (components and relations) as auxiliaries; and (iv) LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016), which decouples entity and relation prediction but models them jointly. The main empirical findings are: (1) neural sequence taggers (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) outperform a feature-based ILP pipeline and neural/feature-based dependency parsers; (2) MTL with natural subtasks yields consistent gains; (3) the LSTM-ER model is very strong on component identification (especially on paragraph-level) but degrades on relations for long documents; and (4) token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1) A thorough comparison of neural end-to-end framings for AM, with clear evidence that local sequence tagging (with distance-coded labels) is a strong, robust baseline and that MTL helps.
2) An empirical analysis of when more general joint entity–relation models (LSTM-ER) help (shorter texts) versus when constrained local models are preferable (longer texts).
3) A negative result for token-level dependency parsing for AM, with plausible explanations grounded in search space and sequence length.

Strengths
- Clear end-to-end framing and baselines: The paper tackles the full AM pipeline from raw tokens to components and relations, avoiding hand-engineered features and ILP constraints. Comparisons include strong neural parsers and a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP pipeline, providing a broad empirical picture.
- Simple, effective sequence tagging design: Encoding relation distance and stance into tags is elegant and yields strong results, especially for long-range dependencies, validating the intuition that BiLSTMs can capture such dependencies better than traditional CRFs.
- Careful analysis of document length: The paragraph vs. essay evaluations are insightful. The observed degradation of LSTM-ER on long texts (O(m^2) relation candidates) and the relative robustness of local taggers is well argued and supported by results and distance/error analyses (e.g., Fig. 4).
- Multi-task learning improves robustness: Using natural subtasks (component labeling C and relation labeling R) as auxiliaries consistently helps, strengthening the claim that joint but decoupled modeling is beneficial.
- Reproducibility considerations: The paper mentions released code/settings and provides multiple model variants, metrics at 100% and 50% span matching, and significance testing across documents.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset scope vs. broad claims: All conclusions are drawn on a single dataset (PE). While this is the most comprehensive end-to-end AM resource, claims about general suitability of dependency parsing vs. tagging and about “first neural end-to-end AM” would be stronger with at least one complementary domain (e.g., microtexts, legal texts, or web discourse). The discussion notes theory-dependent variability but does not test cross-dataset transfer or domain robustness.
- Incomplete control for architecture when assessing MTL: The MTL experiments use a weaker BL tagger (no char-CNN, no CRF), making it hard to isolate the MTL effect from architectural capacity. A controlled comparison (MTL on top of BLCC vs. BLCC alone) would better quantify the auxiliary tasks’ contribution.
- Dependency parsing framing may be suboptimal by design: The token-level head assignment (e.g., all non-argumentative tokens headed by END; premises attached to first token of the target component) arguably induces unnatural structures for parsers. Span-level parsing (segment-level heads) or biaffine graph parsers with span representations could be more appropriate. The current negative result may reflect the specific encoding rather than the general viability of parsing for AM.
- Fairness and clarity of baselines: The ILP system is reported to “operate on both levels,” but Table 1 presents a single line of numbers without clearly mapping to paragraph vs. essay evaluation, making comparisons harder to interpret. Moreover, modern strong neural parsers (e.g., deep biaffine) were not evaluated; while not required, including a stronger parsing baseline could refine the negative conclusion.
- Ablation on distance coding: The core novelty in tagging is the composite label with distance d. There is no ablation comparing (i) tags without d; (ii) coarser bucketing of d; (iii) post-hoc relation scoring using learned token/span representations. This would clarify how much gain is attributable to explicit distance supervision vs. the encoder’s capacity.

Questions to authors
- Can you report ILP results separately for paragraph vs. essay levels for a fair, apples-to-apples comparison with neural models at each level?
- How sensitive are STag results to the maximum distance d allowed? Did you cap unseen distance values at test time, and how do you handle distances outside the training set?
- Could you provide an ablation where the same BLCC backbone is used with and without MTL auxiliaries (YC, YR) to isolate the effect of MTL?
- For dependency parsing, did you try span-level representations (e.g., predicting component spans first, then parsing components) or graph parsers over spans? If not, do you expect these would mitigate the token-level issues you observed?
- For LSTM-ER on essays, did you try pruning candidate relations using distance thresholds or paragraph locality to address the O(m^2) explosion?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider evaluating on at least one additional AM dataset (e.g., microtexts or a subset of legal/web discourse) to assess robustness across theories and domains.
- Provide qualitative error analysis for typical failure modes (over-extended spans, stance confusions, long-distance errors) and how MTL shifts these.
- Explore constrained decoding for tagging (e.g., enforcing valid component BIO transitions and one-parent constraint for premises) to bridge the gap between local taggers and global structural consistency.
- Reporting training/inference time and memory would help practitioners choose between framings in real systems.
- Minor: clarify “Acc.” in tables (token-level BIO accuracy?), and harmonize notation for stance labels (Supp/Att vs. For/Ag) across components and relations to avoid confusion.
