

Summary: This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), proposing four different neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of the LSTM-ER model. The main contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions for AM, (2) demonstrating that sequence tagging models (specifically STagBLCC) outperform dependency parsing approaches, and (3) showing that multi-task learning with component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

Strengths: 
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to explore neural end-to-end approaches for AM, which eliminates the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints required in previous work. This is particularly important as AM is an "arbitrary" problem where the notion of "argument" depends on underlying argumentation theories, making manual feature engineering challenging.
2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with careful comparison across multiple neural approaches and detailed analysis of why certain approaches work better than others. The authors provide clear explanations for the underperformance of dependency parsing (e.g., "the LSTM-Parser's deterioration [from paragraph to essay level] is much more severe than the other models'... due to the very long sequences encountered on essay level").
3. The paper makes a valuable methodological contribution by demonstrating that sequence tagging models with distance encoding (STagT) can effectively capture long-range dependencies inherent to argumentation structures, which is a significant insight for the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims to be the first to explore neural end-to-end approaches for AM but omits Potash et al. (2017), which is mentioned in the paper as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" but not cited in the related work section. This is a significant oversight as Potash et al. (2017) also investigates neural approaches for argumentation mining, including pointer networks, which would be relevant to the authors' claims of novelty.
2. The claim that dependency parsing is ineffective for AM is based primarily on paragraph-level results, while the authors acknowledge that performance drops drastically from paragraph to essay level (e.g., "the performance drop between paragraph and essay level is in any case immense"). The main conclusion about dependency parsing being ineffective should be qualified by noting that the experiments were limited to paragraph level, which is an easier setting than the intended essay-level task.
3. The multi-task learning results are inconsistent with the authors' claim of improvement. The MTL approach (STagBL) performs worse than the single-task approach (STagBLCC) on the essay level (C-F1 100%: 49.59 vs 54.55), suggesting that the multi-task learning setup may not be as beneficial as claimed. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently address why the MTL setup performs worse than the single-task approach.
4. The paper does not compare with more recent neural dependency parsers that might perform better than the ones tested (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser, LSTM-Parser). The authors use Kiperwasser and Goldberg (2016) as a representative neural parser, but the field has advanced since then, and a more comprehensive comparison would strengthen their claim about dependency parsing's ineffectiveness.

Questions to Authors:
1. Potash et al. (2017) presents a neural approach to argumentation mining using pointer networks, which is mentioned in your paper as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work." Why was this work not cited in the related work section, and how does your approach differ from theirs?
2. Your main claim that dependency parsing is ineffective for AM is based primarily on paragraph-level results. How would the results change if dependency parsers were evaluated on the essay level, where the problem is more challenging?
3. The multi-task learning approach (STagBL) performs worse than the single-task approach (STagBLCC) on the essay level, yet you claim that "jointly learning 'natural' subtasks... improves performance." Can you clarify this apparent contradiction and explain why the MTL setup is beneficial despite these results?
