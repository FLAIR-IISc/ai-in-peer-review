

This paper investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM that eliminate the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints; (2) demonstrating that sequence tagging models with distance encoding outperform dependency parsing approaches by 10-13% on standard metrics; and (3) showing that multi-task learning with natural subtasks improves performance.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to explore neural end-to-end approaches for AM, addressing a key limitation in prior work that relied on manual feature engineering. The experimental design is strong, with thorough comparisons across multiple neural architectures (dependency parsers, sequence taggers, multi-task learning) on the PE dataset. The finding that sequence tagging outperforms dependency parsing is particularly valuable, as it contradicts the intuitive assumption that argument structures (which often form trees) would benefit from parsing approaches. The paper also provides a clear explanation for why dependency parsing fails (due to the combinatorial complexity of modeling long sequences) and why sequence tagging works better (as a simpler local model).

Weaknesses: The paper claims dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, but doesn't adequately explore whether specialized parsers designed specifically for argumentation might perform better than standard parsers. The explanation for LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level (due to combinatorial explosion of possible relations) is insufficient; a more detailed analysis would strengthen the argument. The experiments are limited to a single dataset (PE), which may not represent the full diversity of argumentation structures found in other domains, limiting the generalizability of the findings.

The paper is well-written and demonstrates good understanding of the AM literature, but the single-dataset limitation and incomplete explanation of LSTM-ER's degradation are notable weaknesses. The authors should address these concerns in a revision. The insights about sequence tagging outperforming dependency parsing are valuable for the field, and the multi-task learning findings could be broadly applicable to other sequence labeling tasks.
