- Summary: Compares neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) and claims sequence tagging and MTL improve performance.  
- Contribution: Claims first neural end-to-end solutions for computational AM.  
- Contribution: Shows sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing for token-level AM.  
- Contribution: Shows multi-task learning with component and relation detection as auxiliaries improves results.  
- Strength: Thorough empirical comparison addressing gap from prior feature-engineered, pipeline approaches.  
- Strength: Analysis of paragraph vs. essay level performance reveals impact of sequence length on model behavior.  
- Strength: MTL experiments show auxiliary tasks (especially component detection) consistently boost performance.  
- Strength: Detailed analysis arguing dependency parsing struggles due to search-space complexity for long sequences.  
- Weakness: Novelty claim unclear given related end-to-end neural work (e.g., Miwa and Bansal 2016); needs clearer distinction.  
- Weakness: Comparison with ILP is unfair because ILP uses paragraph-level information not given to neural models.  
- Weakness: Robustness claim is overstated because evaluation is limited to a single PE dataset.  
- Weakness: Insufficient evidence explaining why dependency parsing fails; deeper analysis or alternative parsers needed.  
- Question: How does the sequence tagging approach differ from Miwa and Bansal (2016) in architecture and evaluation?  
- Question: How would neural models perform if given the same paragraph-level information as the ILP model?  
- Question: What specific architectural aspects prevent STagBLCC from degrading on essays compared to LSTM-ER?  
- Additional: Paper should discuss PE dataset limitations and generalizability to other argumentation structures.  
- Additional: Authors should better justify novelty versus prior neural approaches and explain why component detection helps more than relation detection.  
- Additional: Clarify how exact-match (100%) and approximate-match (50%) metrics for components were computed.
