- Paper explores neural end-to-end argumentation mining, proposing four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER adaptation.
- Contribution: presents first neural end-to-end solutions for AM.
- Contribution: shows sequence tagging (STagBLCC) outperforms dependency parsing approaches.
- Contribution: demonstrates multi-task learning with component detection and relation identification can improve performance.
- Strength: first work to remove hand-crafted features and ILP constraints for end-to-end AM, addressing challenge of arbitrary "argument" definitions.
- Strength: thorough, well-designed experiments with careful comparisons and analyses explaining model behaviors (e.g., parser deterioration on long sequences).
- Strength: methodological insight that sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagT) captures long-range dependencies in AM.
- Weakness: omission of Potash et al. (2017) from related work despite its relevance to neural AM and pointer networks.
- Weakness: claim that dependency parsing is ineffective is based mainly on paragraph-level results and should be qualified given essay-level challenges.
- Weakness: multi-task learning (STagBL) underperforms single-task STagBLCC on essay level, contradicting the claimed MTL benefit and lacking sufficient explanation.
- Weakness: lack of comparison to more recent neural dependency parsers beyond the selected baselines weakens the claim about parsing's ineffectiveness.
- Question: why was Potash et al. (2017) not cited, and how does the current approach differ from theirs?
- Question: how would dependency parser performance change when evaluated at the essay level instead of paragraph level?
- Question: why does the MTL setup perform worse than single-task on essays, and how is MTL still beneficial despite these results?
