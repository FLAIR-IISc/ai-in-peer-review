- Summary: Paper studies end-to-end neural argumentation mining, framing it as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning, and proposes a novel distance-encoding sequence tagger.

- Contribution 1: First neural end-to-end AM solutions removing hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.

- Contribution 2: Comprehensive evaluation showing neural models outperform prior feature-based ILP baselines.

- Contribution 3: Key finding that token-based dependency parsing fails for end-to-end AM while distance-encoded sequence tagging is robust.

- Strength 1: Advances the field by eliminating manual feature engineering and complex ILP design for AM.

- Strength 2: Thorough experimental comparison across multiple neural approaches and evaluation levels with detailed analysis.

- Strength 3: Demonstrates the effectiveness and practical value of a simple sequence tagging model with distance encoding.

- Strength 4: Multi-task learning with natural AM subtasks improves performance, especially when component detection is used as an auxiliary task.

- Weakness 1: Evaluation restricted to one dataset (Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays), limiting generalizability.

- Weakness 2: Lacks ablation studies to isolate the effect of distance encoding versus model architecture.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient quantitative analysis of why LSTM-ER degrades at essay level as relation counts grow.

- Weakness 4: Provides limited practical guidance on when to choose more restrained modeling for long documents.

- Question 1: How would the models generalize to other AM datasets with different structures (e.g., Peldszus & Stede)?

- Question 2: Which specific architectural aspects of the LSTM-Parser (beyond stack history) cause sensitivity to longer sequences?

- Question 3: How exactly does the ILP model use paragraph/essay-level information and paragraph boundaries for predictions (e.g., major claims)?
