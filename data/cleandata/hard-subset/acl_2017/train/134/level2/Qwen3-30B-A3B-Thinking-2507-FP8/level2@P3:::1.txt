

Summary: This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER frameworks. The authors claim to present the first neural end-to-end solutions for AM, showing that sequence tagging models outperform dependency parsing for this task and that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks improves performance.

Main Contributions: (1) First neural end-to-end approaches for computational AM; (2) Sequence tagging models with distance encoding outperform dependency parsing for token-level AM; (3) Multi-task learning with component detection and relation detection as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural approaches for end-to-end AM, addressing a significant gap in the literature. Previous work relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline approaches, while this work demonstrates that neural end-to-end models can achieve better or comparable performance without feature engineering.
2. The analysis of paragraph vs. essay level performance reveals important insights about model behavior, particularly regarding the relationship between sequence length and model performance. This is valuable for understanding the practical limitations of different approaches.
3. The multi-task learning experiments demonstrate that auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation detection) consistently improve performance, with component detection being particularly beneficial. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks where multiple related subtasks exist.
4. The paper provides detailed analysis of why dependency parsing performs poorly for token-level AM, identifying the complexity of the search space for long sequences as a key factor, which is a useful insight for future work.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim requires clarification. While the authors present a new application of neural models to end-to-end AM, Miwa and Bansal (2016) already proposed a similar end-to-end neural approach for entity and relation extraction. The paper should more clearly distinguish their work from this prior work and explain why their specific framing for AM is novel.
2. The comparison with the ILP model is not entirely fair. The ILP model has access to paragraph-level information (knowing which paragraph in an essay it's in), which provides advantages such as knowing major claims typically occur in first/last paragraphs. The neural models are not evaluated on the same information level, which may disadvantage them.
3. The paper claims sequence tagging models "perform robustly across classification scenarios," but evaluation is limited to a single dataset (PE from Stab and Gurevych 2016). Without evaluation on additional datasets with different argumentation structures, this claim of robustness is overstated.
4. The paper identifies that dependency parsing is ineffective for token-level AM but doesn't provide sufficient evidence about why this is the case. The authors suggest it's due to the "global view" being too complex for long sequences, but don't provide a thorough analysis of the underlying reasons or alternative parsing approaches that might work better.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the proposed sequence tagging approach differ from Miwa and Bansal (2016) in terms of model architecture and evaluation, given that they also proposed an end-to-end neural approach for entity and relation extraction?
2. How would the neural models perform if evaluated on the same information level as the ILP model (i.e., with paragraph-level information available)?
3. The paper states that STagBLCC doesn't suffer from the same degradation as LSTM-ER on essay level despite handling the same number of potential relations. What specific aspects of the tagging model's architecture prevent this degradation?

Additional Comments:
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the PE dataset and how the proposed models might generalize to other argumentation structures. The authors should also clarify the novelty claim by more thoroughly distinguishing their work from prior neural approaches to AM. While the multi-task learning results are promising, the paper could provide more insight into why component detection as an auxiliary task is more beneficial than relation detection. The evaluation metrics (C-F1 and R-F1) are well-defined, but the paper should clarify how the exact match (100%) and approximate match (50%) metrics were calculated for components.
