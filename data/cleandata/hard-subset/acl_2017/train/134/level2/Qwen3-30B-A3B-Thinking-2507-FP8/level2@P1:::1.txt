

Summary: This paper investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as token-based dependency parsing, token-based sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The authors present several neural approaches to AM, including a novel sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components and a multi-task learning setup where natural subtasks of AM serve as auxiliary tasks.

Main Contributions (in order of strength):
1. First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM that eliminate the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.
2. Comprehensive evaluation showing neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model.
3. Key insight that token-based dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM (contrary to previous component-level approaches), while sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important gap in the field by providing the first neural end-to-end AM solutions, which eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint design. This is a significant contribution that advances the field toward more automated argumentation mining systems.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured, with careful comparison of multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) across different evaluation levels (paragraph vs. essay). The authors provide detailed analysis of why certain approaches work better than others, particularly the insight about dependency parsing being ineffective at the token level.

3. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that a standard neural sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) with distance encoding performs robustly across different environments. This finding has clear implications for future work in the field and could guide practitioners toward simpler, more effective approaches.

4. The multi-task learning results are insightful, showing that including natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks improves performance. The authors provide a useful analysis of why this works, particularly noting that component detection is more helpful as an auxiliary task than relation identification.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych, 2016). While this is a well-established dataset in the field, the lack of evaluation on multiple datasets with varying argumentation structures limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors should discuss whether their insights would hold on other datasets.

2. The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components. For example, it's unclear how much the distance encoding contributes to the performance versus the neural architecture itself. A more detailed ablation study would strengthen the paper's claims about why their sequence tagging approach works so well.

3. The paper mentions that LSTM-ER's performance degrades significantly on essay level due to "many more possible relations," but doesn't provide a clear quantitative analysis of this phenomenon. It would be helpful to see a more detailed breakdown of how relation count increases with document length and how this affects performance.

4. The paper claims that "when there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable," but doesn't provide clear guidance for practitioners on when to choose which approach. The practical implications for real-world applications could be better articulated.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper only evaluates on one dataset. How would you expect your models to perform on other argumentation datasets with different structures (e.g., the Peldszus and Stede corpus, which has more complex argument structures)?

2. The paper states that "the LSTM-Parser's deterioration [from paragraph to essay level] is much more severe than the other models' surveyed below." What specific aspects of the LSTM-Parser's architecture (beyond the stack history) contribute to this sensitivity to sequence length?

3. The paper mentions that the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information" but doesn't clarify how this information is used. Could you elaborate on how the ILP model leverages paragraph boundaries in its predictions, particularly for major claims that appear in first or last paragraphs?
