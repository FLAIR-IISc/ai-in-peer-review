**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem in several ways: as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. These neural approaches are systematically evaluated against each other and a feature-based ILP baseline on the persuasive essays dataset, comparing performance on both paragraph- and full essay-level inputs.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A thorough comparative analysis of different neural frameworks for end-to-end AM. The paper provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between modeling AM as parsing versus tagging, and using local versus global models.
2.  An important empirical finding that model performance is highly dependent on document length and the constraints imposed by the model. The analysis clearly shows how complex models like dependency parsers and the relation component of LSTM-ER struggle with longer sequences, while a more constrained sequence tagging model proves more robust.
3.  The demonstration that relatively simple neural models, without manual feature engineering, can achieve new state-of-the-art results on this task, significantly outperforming a strong ILP-based system.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper's core strength is its rigorous and insightful experimental design. Rather than proposing a single model, it explores a variety of well-motivated neural architectures, providing a clear picture of what works and why for this particular task.
2.  The analysis of model failures is particularly strong. The discussion on why dependency parsing is ineffective for long documents and why the LSTM-ER model's relation performance degrades on the essay level provides valuable lessons for the community about the scalability of different relation extraction approaches.
3.  The results are convincing and well-supported. The paper clearly demonstrates the superiority of neural end-to-end methods over a strong, feature-engineered pipeline system, marking a significant advance for the field of argumentation mining.
4.  The exploration of multi-task learning (MTL) adds another dimension to the analysis, showing that jointly learning sub-problems like component identification can improve performance on the full, complex task.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness is the limited architectural novelty. The paper excels in its application and analysis of existing models (BiLSTM-CRFs, neural parsers, LSTM-ER) to the AM task, but it does not propose a fundamentally new model architecture. The main innovation lies in the problem framing, particularly the label encoding for the sequence tagger.
2.  The conclusions are drawn from experiments on a single dataset (PE essays), which has a particular, near-tree-like argumentation structure. The finding that more constrained models perform better on longer texts might not generalize to datasets with more complex, graph-structured arguments. This limitation should be more explicitly discussed.
3.  While the MTL experiments show a performance gain, the analysis could be deeper. For instance, a more detailed explanation of why the component-level auxiliary task (C) is consistently more beneficial than the relation-level task (R) would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
