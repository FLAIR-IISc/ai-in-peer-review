1. Comprehensive empirical study comparing neural end-to-end AM approaches (parsing, sequence tagging, MTL) against an ILP baseline on the persuasive essays dataset.  
2. Thorough comparative analysis showing trade-offs between parsing vs. tagging and local vs. global models.  
3. Key finding that performance depends heavily on document length and model constraints, with complex models struggling on longer inputs.  
4. Demonstration that simple neural models without manual features can achieve new state-of-the-art results, outperforming a strong feature-engineered system.  
5. Rigorous experimental design exploring multiple well-motivated architectures rather than proposing a single model.  
6. Strong analysis of model failures explaining scalability issues of dependency parsing and LSTM-ER on long documents.  
7. Convincing, well-supported results showing superiority of neural end-to-end methods for the task.  
8. Inclusion of multi-task learning experiments showing benefits of jointly learning sub-problems like component identification.  
9. Limited architectural novelty—the main innovation is problem framing and label encoding rather than a new model.  
10. Experiments limited to a single near-tree-structured dataset (PE essays), raising generalization concerns; MTL analysis could be deeper (why component-level auxiliary task helps more than relation-level).
