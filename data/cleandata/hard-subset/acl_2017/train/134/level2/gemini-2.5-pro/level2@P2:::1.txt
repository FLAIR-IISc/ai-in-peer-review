### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the task in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a complex sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning setup, and using a state-of-the-art joint entity and relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compared against a strong feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A thorough comparative analysis of different neural framings for end-to-end AM.** The paper's primary strength is its systematic investigation into how this complex structured prediction task can be modeled using different neural paradigms. This provides a valuable survey and strong set of baselines for future research.
2.  **Establishing a new state-of-the-art on a key AM benchmark.** The paper demonstrates that relatively standard neural sequence tagging models can significantly outperform a complex, feature-engineered ILP model, advancing the state-of-the-art for end-to-end AM on the PE dataset without requiring domain-specific feature engineering or constraint design.
3.  **Valuable insights into the trade-offs of different modeling choices.** The paper provides a well-supported analysis of why certain models succeed or fail. Key insights include the sub-par performance of token-level dependency parsing, the robustness of local sequence tagging models, and the breakdown of general-purpose relation extraction models on longer documents with many candidate entities.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Timely and Significant Topic.** The paper addresses a crucial direction for the field of argumentation mining: moving from multi-stage, feature-heavy pipelines to integrated, end-to-end neural systems. This work is one of the first to do so comprehensively and successfully, making it a timely and important contribution.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation.** The authors should be commended for the breadth of their experiments. They do not merely propose one new model but instead implement and compare four distinct and plausible neural approaches, including state-of-the-art architectures for parsing (Dyer et al., 2015), tagging (Ma and Hovy, 2016), and joint extraction (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). This makes the paper’s conclusions about the relative merits of each approach well-substantiated and highly informative.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis.** The paper reports state-of-the-art results, clearly outperforming the strong ILP baseline from Stab and Gurevych (2016). The analysis that accompanies these results is a major strength. For instance, the discussion of why the LSTM-ER model's relation F1 score degrades so severely from the paragraph to the essay level (due to the quadratic explosion of possible relations) is very insightful and supported by clear evidence (Figure 4). Similarly, the detailed breakdown of the performance of different parsers provides a clear picture of why this framing struggles.
4.  **Novel and Effective Problem Formulation.** The formulation of the end-to-end AM task as a sequence tagging problem with a single, structured label (STag) is clever. Encoding the relational information via component distance (`d`) directly into the tag is an effective way to handle links within a sequential framework and appears to work very well in practice.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Generalizability.** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset from a single domain (persuasive student essays). This dataset has very specific structural properties (e.g., arguments are almost always trees, relations are largely contained within paragraphs). The paper's main conclusion—that a local sequence tagging model is superior to a global parsing model or a more general relation extraction model—may be highly contingent on these properties. It is unclear if these findings would generalize to other domains like legal text or online debates, where argumentation structures can be more graph-like and less localized.
2.  **Potentially Unfair Framing of Dependency Parsing.** The conclusion that dependency parsing is "ineffective" for AM seems too strong. The chosen token-level representation makes parsing an extremely difficult task, as the parser must build a tree over hundreds of tokens. A more standard approach for discourse-level tasks is to first identify the argumentative components (as spans) and then parse the relations between them. While this would be a two-stage process, it might be a more effective use of parsing technology. The poor performance reported here may be more an indictment of the specific token-level framing than of parsing models in general for this task.
3.  **The Multi-Task Learning (MTL) experiments are based on a weaker baseline.** The MTL experiments are conducted with a simple BiLSTM tagger (STagBL), whereas the main sequence tagging results use a much stronger BiLSTM-CRF-CNN model (STagBLCC). While the results show that MTL helps the weaker model, it is difficult to conclude how MTL would interact with the stronger model. The performance gains attributed to MTL might look different if applied to the state-of-the-art STagBLCC baseline.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on the generalizability of your findings? Given that the PE dataset has a fairly regular, tree-like structure, how do you expect your best-performing sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) to perform on datasets with more complex, non-tree, or non-projective argumentation structures? Would the constraints implicitly learned by the tagger (e.g., each component links to one head) become a limitation?
2.  The performance of the LSTM-Parser drops dramatically on the essay level. You attribute this to long sequences and the complexity of a global model. Do you think a hybrid approach—using a tagger like STagBLCC to identify component spans first, and then applying a parser over these identified spans—could be a viable strategy to leverage the strengths of both models? How does this align with your philosophy of "end-to-end" learning?
