**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a systematic investigation into neural network architectures for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The work frames the AM task in several distinct ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a complex sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning setup, and using an established joint entity-relation extraction model. These approaches are empirically evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset, and their performance is compared against a state-of-the-art feature-based system that uses Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The primary finding is that sequence tagging models, which are conceptually simpler and more local, are more robust and effective for this task than global dependency parsing models, particularly when dealing with longer documents.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A comprehensive empirical study of different neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining.** The work systematically evaluates four different ways to model the AM task with neural networks, providing a valuable comparative analysis. This is the first such study for end-to-end AM and offers important insights into which architectural choices are most effective.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for joint component and relation extraction.** The proposed tagging scheme, which encodes component type, relation type, and the relative distance between components into a single label, is an innovative method for linearizing a structured prediction task. This allows powerful, off-the-shelf sequence labeling models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) to be applied directly to the problem.
3.  **Insightful analysis showing that simpler, local models can outperform more complex, global models on this task.** The finding that sequence tagging surpasses token-level dependency parsing is significant, as argument structures are inherently tree-like. The paper provides a convincing explanation, linking the subpar performance of parsers to the challenges of modeling long-range dependencies and a vast search space in longer texts.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** This work represents a significant step forward for argumentation mining. By being the first to systematically apply neural methods to the end-to-end task, it demonstrates that these models can achieve state-of-the-art results without the need for extensive feature engineering or complex, hand-designed constraints, which were characteristic of previous approaches.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides a strong analysis of the results that goes beyond simply reporting scores. The discussion on the performance trade-offs between paragraph-level and essay-level processing is illuminating. The comparison of how the sequence tagging model (STag) and the LSTM-ER model handle long-range dependencies (Figure 4) provides a clear explanation for their differing performance on longer documents. This level of analysis greatly enhances the paper's contribution.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is robust, comparing a wide array of models from traditional feature-based parsers to several modern neural architectures. The inclusion of the strong LSTM-ER model from Miwa and Bansal (2016) as a point of comparison is particularly valuable, as it grounds the work within the broader context of joint entity and relation extraction.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The different framings of the AM problem are explained clearly with helpful examples and figures, making the proposed methods accessible.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Generalizability of a Single-Corpus Study:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (the PE corpus), which has a particular, relatively constrained argumentation structure (e.g., premises have a single parent, creating a near-tree structure). The paper’s strong conclusions about the general superiority of certain models for AM might be specific to this dataset's characteristics. The work would be strengthened by a discussion of how these findings might generalize (or not) to other AM corpora with different annotation schemes or more complex, graph-like structures.
2.  **Conflated Variables in the Multi-Task Learning (MTL) Experiment:** The MTL experiments are performed using a simpler base tagger (STagBL) than the one used for the main sequence tagging results (STagBLCC). This makes it difficult to isolate the benefit of the MTL framework itself, as the baseline is weaker. To make a clearer case for MTL, it would be beneficial to either apply it to the stronger STagBLCC model or provide a more detailed justification for this experimental design choice. As it stands, the reported gains might stem from both the MTL setup and other architectural differences.
3.  **Ambiguity in Dependency Parsing Preprocessing:** The dependency parsing framing requires a strict tree structure. The paper mentions that to handle multiple Major Claims, claims are re-linked "to one of the major claims." This preprocessing step is crucial for defining the task for the parsers, but the details are sparse. A more thorough description of this tree-creation process would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the parsing experiments, as the specific choices made could influence the difficulty of the task.
