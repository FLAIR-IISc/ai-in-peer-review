**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM). The authors frame the end-to-end task, which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification, in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a complex sequence tagging problem, and a multi-task learning problem. These different framings are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline and the joint entity-relation extraction model of Miwa and Bansal (2016). The experiments show that framing AM as token-level dependency parsing yields poor results, especially for longer documents. In contrast, a sequence tagging approach using a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN architecture proves to be robust and effective. This model uses a novel, complex tagging scheme that encodes component boundaries, types, and relational information (distance and type) into a single label. The results demonstrate that the proposed neural models, particularly the sequence tagger and the LSTM-ER model on shorter texts, outperform the feature-based ILP baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art for this task.

**Contributions**

1.  A thorough and insightful empirical comparison of multiple neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining. Rather than proposing a single model, the paper systematically investigates different ways to frame the problem (parsing vs. tagging, local vs. global), providing valuable analysis on their respective trade-offs, particularly concerning document length.
2.  A novel and effective formulation of end-to-end AM as a sequence tagging problem. The proposed 4-tuple tag `(b, t, d, s)` cleverly encodes all necessary structural and relational information into a flat label set, allowing a standard (though powerful) sequence tagger to tackle this complex structured prediction task.
3.  The paper provides a clear demonstration that neural, feature-free methods can surpass heavily engineered pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and complex constraint modeling (ILP). This is an important result for the field of argumentation mining.

**Strengths**

1.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper's main strength lies in its breadth. The authors evaluate four distinct neural modeling paradigms (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and joint entity-relation extraction) as well as four different dependency parsers. This rigorous comparison provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.
2.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of the results is excellent. The authors provide a compelling explanation for why dependency parsing fails on long sequences (combinatorial complexity) and why the LSTM-ER model's performance on relations degrades on the essay-level (quadratic growth of candidate pairs). This is well-supported by the distinction between paragraph- and essay-level experiments and the analysis in Figure 4, which clearly shows the different models' sensitivity to relation distance.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed models achieve new state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark dataset, significantly outperforming a strong ILP-based system. The results convincingly show the power of end-to-end neural models for this task, obviating the need for feature engineering and manual constraint design.
4.  **Novelty in Problem Formulation:** The sequence tagging formulation (`STagT`) is a creative and effective way to linearize a structured prediction problem. Encoding the relative distance to a related component directly into the tag is an elegant solution for capturing relations within a tagging framework and appears key to its strong performance on long documents.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Tag Set Complexity and Scalability:** The `STagT` formulation results in a very large and complex label set, as it is the Cartesian product of four different elements, one of which (distance `d`) is unbounded in principle. The paper notes that the distances observed in the data are limited, but it does not discuss the total size of the resulting label vocabulary. This could pose a data sparsity problem and might not scale well to datasets with different structural properties or longer-range dependencies. A brief discussion of the label set size and any potential limitations would be beneficial.
2.  **"Loss-less" Assumption in Data Preprocessing:** The paper states that the argument graph structure, which is not strictly a tree, can be converted to a tree in a "loss-less" manner by re-linking claims to a single major claim. This simplification is necessary for the dependency parsing formulation, but calling it "loss-less" seems inaccurate, as it alters the annotated semantic dependencies. It would be helpful to clarify the impact of this modification on the task and evaluation, especially for relations involving claims and major claims.
3.  **Inconsistent Models for MTL Experiments:** The multi-task learning (MTL) experiments are conducted using a `STagBL` model (a BiLSTM without a CRF or CNN layer), which is weaker than the main `STagBLCC` model used for the primary sequence tagging results. While the results show that MTL helps the weaker model, it is unclear if the same gains would apply to the stronger, state-of-the-art `STagBLCC` model. This makes it difficult to disentangle the benefits of MTL from the benefits of using a more powerful base architecture. Applying MTL to the `STagBLCC` model would have provided a more conclusive result.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you report the total size of the label vocabulary for the `STagT` model on the PE dataset? How are the distance values `d` handled in the label set? For instance, are all unique distances mapped to distinct labels, or is there any binning or thresholding for larger distances to keep the label set manageable?
2.  Regarding the conversion of the argument graph to a tree: If a claim C was originally linked to both Major Claim MC1 and Major Claim MC2, and you re-link it to only MC2, how is the original C->MC1 relation handled during training and evaluation? Does this simplification mean that some gold-standard relations are ignored, potentially affecting the reported R-F1 scores?
3.  Your analysis suggests that LSTM-ER struggles with relation identification on the essay level due to the large number of candidate entity pairs. Did you consider experimenting with a constrained version of LSTM-ER, for example by limiting relation prediction to entity pairs within a certain distance window? This could provide a more direct comparison to the implicit distance-awareness of your `STagT` model.
