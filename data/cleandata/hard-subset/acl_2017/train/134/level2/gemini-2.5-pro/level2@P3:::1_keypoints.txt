- Investigates neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining, comparing multiple framings.
- Frames the task as token-level dependency parsing, complex sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a joint entity–relation model.
- Evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset and compared to an ILP-based feature system; sequence tagging models perform best, especially on longer documents.
- Contribution: first comprehensive empirical comparison of four neural framings for end-to-end AM, guiding architectural choices.
- Contribution: introduces a novel sequence tagging label that encodes component type, relation type, and relative distance for joint extraction.
- Contribution: provides evidence that simpler, local sequence tagging models can outperform global dependency parsers due to long-range dependency and search-space issues.
- Strength: high novelty and impact by showing neural models can reach state-of-the-art without heavy feature engineering or hand-designed constraints.
- Strength: thorough analysis, including paragraph vs essay-level trade-offs and clear comparisons (e.g., Figure 4) explaining model behaviors.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental setup including traditional parsers and strong neural baselines like LSTM-ER.
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation with helpful examples and figures.
- Weakness: limited generalizability from a single PE corpus that has near-tree argument structures, possibly limiting applicability to other corpora.
- Weakness: MTL experiments use a weaker base tagger (STagBL) than the main model (STagBLCC), conflating MTL effects with architectural differences.
- Weakness: insufficient detail on dependency parsing preprocessing (relinking multiple Major Claims), reducing clarity and reproducibility.
