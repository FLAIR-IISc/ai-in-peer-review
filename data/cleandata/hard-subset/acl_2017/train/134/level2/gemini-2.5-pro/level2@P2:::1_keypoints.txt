- Comprehensive empirical study comparing neural end-to-end framings for argumentation mining (parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, joint extraction) on the PE dataset.  
- Establishes new state-of-the-art on the PE benchmark using standard neural sequence tagging models without domain-specific features.  
- Provides insights into modeling trade-offs: token-level parsing underperforms, local sequence tagging is robust, and general relation-extraction models struggle on long documents.  
- Timely and significant shift toward integrated, end-to-end neural AM systems instead of multi-stage feature-heavy pipelines.  
- Broad, rigorous evaluation implementing four distinct neural approaches and state-of-the-art architectures, making conclusions well-substantiated.  
- Strong empirical results with detailed analysis (e.g., relation F1 degradation for LSTM-ER at essay level due to quadratic relation explosion).  
- Novel formulation: single structured sequence tag (STag) encoding relational distance proves effective.  
- Limited generalizability: experiments use only the PE dataset with tree-like, paragraph-local relations, so findings may not hold for other domains with graph-like structures.  
- Potentially unfair framing of dependency parsing: token-level parsing over long sequences may be an inappropriate setup, biasing against parsing approaches.  
- MTL experiments use a weaker BiLSTM baseline, so reported MTL gains may not reflect interaction with the stronger BiLSTM-CRF-CNN model.  
- Question: How would STagBLCC generalize to datasets with non-tree or non-projective argument structures, and could its implicit constraints be limiting?  
- Question: Could a hybrid approach (tagger to identify spans, then parser over spans) combine strengths of both models, and how does that fit the authors' end-to-end philosophy?
