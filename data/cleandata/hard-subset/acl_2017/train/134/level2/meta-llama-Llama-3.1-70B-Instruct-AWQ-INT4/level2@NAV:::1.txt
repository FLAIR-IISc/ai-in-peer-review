**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining" presents a study on neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate different framings of the AM problem, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a combination of sequential and tree structure information. They evaluate the performance of these framings on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compare them to a feature-based ILP model.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, which eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. They show that BiLSTM taggers perform very well for component detection and that relation detection is more difficult, especially when there are many entities in a text.
3. The authors demonstrate that treating component and relation detection separately but modeling them jointly is not optimal, and that a more restrained modeling is preferable when there are many entities.
4. They present a new state-of-the-art result in end-to-end AM on the PE dataset.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a comprehensive study of neural end-to-end solutions for AM, including different framings and evaluations.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including comparisons to a feature-based ILP model and discussions of the strengths and weaknesses of each framing.
3. The paper contributes to the development of neural end-to-end solutions for AM, which is an important task in natural language processing.
4. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work, including a detailed description of the methods and results.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper focuses on a specific dataset (PE) and may not generalize to other datasets or domains.
2. Some of the framings presented in the paper, such as dependency parsing, may not be suitable for all types of AM problems.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for each framing, which could be an important consideration for practical applications.
4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results for the development of AM systems.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the results of this study generalize to other datasets or domains?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for each framing?
3. How do the results of this study inform the development of AM systems, and what are the implications for practical applications?
4. Are there any plans to extend this work to other types of AM problems or to explore other neural end-to-end solutions?
