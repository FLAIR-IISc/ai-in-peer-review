**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a study on neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate various neural techniques for AM, including framing the problem as a token-based dependency parsing problem, a token-based sequence tagging model, and a multi-task learning setup. They evaluate the performance of these models on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compare them with a feature-based ILP model. The results show that neural computational AM can achieve state-of-the-art performance without requiring manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.

**Main Contributions**

1. The first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2. A comparison of different neural framings for AM, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.
3. An evaluation of the performance of these models on the PE dataset and a comparison with a feature-based ILP model.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end AM, exploring various framings and evaluating their performance on a benchmark dataset.
2. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each model and discussing the implications of their findings.
3. The paper demonstrates the potential of neural computational AM to achieve state-of-the-art performance without requiring manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper focuses primarily on the PE dataset, which may not be representative of all AM tasks. Further evaluation on other datasets is needed to confirm the generalizability of the results.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for each model, which may be an important consideration for practical applications.
3. Some of the models, such as the dependency parsing model, may not be directly applicable to other AM tasks due to their specific design choices.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend this work to other AM tasks and datasets?
2. Can you provide more details on the computational resources required for each model and how they compare to the feature-based ILP model?
3. How do you think the results would change if you used a different evaluation metric, such as the mean average precision (MAP) or the area under the ROC curve (AUC)?
