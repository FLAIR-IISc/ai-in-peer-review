**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a study on neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate several neural techniques for AM, including framing the problem as a token-based dependency parsing, a token-based sequence tagging model, and a multi-task learning setup. They evaluate the performance of these models on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compare them with a feature-based ILP model. The results show that neural computational AM can perform as well as or better than the competing feature-based ILP formulation, while eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.

**Contributions**

1. **Neural End-to-End Solutions**: The authors present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, which do not require the hand-crafting of features or constraints.
2. **Performance Comparison**: They compare the performance of their neural models with a feature-based ILP model, showing that neural computational AM can perform as well as or better than the competing feature-based ILP formulation.
3. **Multi-Task Learning**: The authors investigate the effectiveness of multi-task learning for AM, showing that jointly learning natural subtasks can improve performance.
4. **Sequence Tagging**: They demonstrate that a standard neural sequence tagging model can perform robustly in different environments, especially for component detection.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to AM by framing the problem as a token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging model, which is different from traditional feature-based approaches.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The paper presents state-of-the-art results in end-to-end AM on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).
3. **Thorough Evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their models, including a comparison with a feature-based ILP model and an analysis of the performance of different models on paragraph and essay levels.
4. **Insights into AM**: The paper provides insights into the characteristics of AM, including the importance of considering long-range dependencies and the effectiveness of multi-task learning.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Dataset**: The paper only evaluates the models on a single dataset, which may not be representative of all types of text.
2. **Complexity of Models**: Some of the models proposed in the paper, such as the multi-task learning setup, may be complex and difficult to implement.
3. **Overfitting**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of overfitting, which may be a problem in some of the models.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper does not provide a detailed error analysis, which may be helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the models.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other types of text, such as news articles or social media posts?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the multi-task learning setup, including the choice of hyperparameters and the architecture of the model?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting in their models, especially in the case of the multi-task learning setup?
4. Can the authors provide a more detailed error analysis, including an analysis of the types of errors made by each model and the characteristics of the text that may be contributing to these errors?
