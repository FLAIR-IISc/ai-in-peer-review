**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes neural end-to-end learning techniques for computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative structures in text. The authors frame the problem as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning, and evaluate the performance of various models on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. The main contributions of the paper are the introduction of neural end-to-end solutions to AM, the demonstration that these solutions can outperform state-of-the-art joint ILP models, and the finding that a standard neural sequence tagging model can perform robustly in different environments.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, which eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. The authors show that several neural models can outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model on the PE dataset.
3. The paper demonstrates that a standard neural sequence tagging model can perform robustly in different environments, including component detection and relation identification.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of various neural models for AM, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.
2. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including an examination of the performance of each model on different tasks and datasets.
3. The paper introduces a new evaluation metric, which takes into account the complexity of the argumentative structures in the text.
4. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the models and their architectures, making it easy to follow and understand the paper.
5. The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work in AM, including the limitations of previous approaches and the benefits of the proposed neural end-to-end solutions.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes a high level of background knowledge in natural language processing and machine learning, which may make it difficult for non-experts to follow.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the models are trained and optimized, which may make it difficult to replicate the results.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by each model, which may provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
4. The authors do not compare the performance of the neural models to other machine learning approaches, such as support vector machines or random forests.
5. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the models can be applied to real-world applications, such as document summarization or question answering.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the models are trained and optimized, including the hyperparameters used and the optimization algorithms employed?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by each model, including the types of errors and the frequency of each error?
3. Can you compare the performance of the neural models to other machine learning approaches, such as support vector machines or random forests?
4. Can you provide more details on how the models can be applied to real-world applications, such as document summarization or question answering?
5. Can you provide more insights into the limitations of the proposed neural end-to-end solutions and how they can be improved in future work?
