- Paper studies neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining, comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task, and hybrid framings on the PE dataset vs. a feature-based ILP model.
- Contribution: First neural end-to-end AM solutions removing need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.
- Contribution: BiLSTM taggers perform very well for component detection; relation detection is harder, especially with many entities.
- Contribution: Modeling components and relations jointly but separately is suboptimal; more restrained modeling works better with many entities.
- Contribution: Reports a new state-of-the-art in end-to-end AM on the PE dataset.
- Strength: Comprehensive study covering multiple neural framings and evaluations.
- Strength: Thorough analysis and comparison to a feature-based ILP model with clear discussion of strengths and weaknesses.
- Strength: Advances neural end-to-end approaches for an important NLP task (AM).
- Strength: Clear, well-organized presentation with detailed methods and results.
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to the PE dataset, so generalizability is uncertain.
- Weakness: Some framings (e.g., dependency parsing) may not suit all AM problem types.
- Weakness: Lacks detailed analysis of computational resources required per framing.
- Weakness: Needs a deeper discussion of implications for AM system development.
- Question: How do the results generalize to other datasets or domains?
- Question: Can the authors provide detailed analysis of computational resource requirements for each framing?
- Question: What are the practical implications of these results for developing AM systems?
- Question: Are there plans to extend this work to other AM types or explore additional neural end-to-end solutions?
