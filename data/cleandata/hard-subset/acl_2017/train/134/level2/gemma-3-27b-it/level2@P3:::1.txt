This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The work frames the AM problem as a token-based dependency parsing or sequence tagging task, exploring multi-task learning to improve performance. A key finding is that framing AM as dependency parsing is ineffective, while sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly. The primary contributions are the exploration of neural end-to-end AM approaches, demonstrating the ineffectiveness of token-based dependency parsing for this task, and showing that a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding and multi-task learning yields strong results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Exploration of Neural End-to-End AM:** The paper presents a valuable initial investigation into applying neural network architectures to the challenging task of end-to-end argumentation mining.  Prior work relied heavily on hand-crafted features and pipeline approaches, and this paper offers a compelling alternative. The exploration of different neural framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning) provides a useful comparative analysis.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate that the proposed sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) outperforms the feature-based ILP baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset. The performance gains are particularly noteworthy given the complexity of the task and the limited amount of annotated data.
3. **Insightful Negative Result Regarding Dependency Parsing:** The finding that token-based dependency parsing performs poorly for AM is a significant contribution. This counterintuitive result challenges a natural intuition that argument structures are inherently tree-like and provides valuable guidance for future research. The paper offers plausible explanations for this finding, relating it to the difficulty of long-range dependencies and the complexities of the task.
4. **Effective Use of Distance Encoding:** The incorporation of distance information into the sequence tagging framework is a well-motivated and effective technique.  The paper convincingly demonstrates the importance of modeling relationships between argument components, even when they are far apart in the text.
5. **Multi-Task Learning Improves Performance:** Demonstrating that incorporating auxiliary tasks like component detection into the learning process improves performance provides a useful avenue for future exploration. This suggests that joint learning can effectively leverage relationships between subtasks within AM.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset and Generalizability:** The experiments are conducted solely on the PE dataset. While this allows for a focused comparison, it raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other domains and argumentation structures.  A more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper provides some discussion of the results, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial.  Specifically, identifying the types of errors made by each model (e.g., incorrect component type, incorrect relation type, missed components) could provide valuable insights for future improvement.  The discussion of LSTM-ER’s performance degradation on longer documents hints at a need for this kind of analysis.
3. **Complexity of Sequence Tagging Label Set:** The tag set used in the sequence tagging approach is quite large and complex (incorporating BIO tags, component types, distances, and relation types).  This complexity may introduce challenges in training and could potentially limit the model's ability to generalize. It is unclear whether a simpler tagging scheme might achieve comparable results.
4. **Limited Exploration of LSTM-ER Hyperparameters:** While LSTM-ER shows promise, the paper acknowledges that its performance is sensitive to parameter settings and does not provide details on the hyperparameter tuning process. A more thorough exploration of LSTM-ER's configuration would be helpful.
5. **Insufficient Justification for MTL Layer Selection:** The rationale for choosing specific layers for the MTL tasks (feeding from certain layers in the BiLSTM) is not clearly explained. A more detailed explanation or ablation study would strengthen the justification for this design choice.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could the authors elaborate on the computational cost of training the different models, particularly the dependency parsers and LSTM-ER?  This would provide a better understanding of the practical feasibility of these approaches.
2.  What specific error types are most common for the sequence tagging model (STagBLCC)? Are there systematic errors related to specific relation types or component types?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of distance encoding scheme?  Did the authors explore alternative ways of encoding distance information?
4. The paper mentions that the LSTM-ER performance degrades on longer sequences. Is this degradation related to memory constraints or to the model’s ability to capture long-range dependencies?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more visually compelling illustration of the tagging scheme. An example showing the tag sequence for a short argumentative sentence would be helpful.
*   The citation style is inconsistent in a few places (e.g., missing punctuation or incorrect formatting).
*   Consider providing links to the code and data used in the experiments to promote reproducibility.
