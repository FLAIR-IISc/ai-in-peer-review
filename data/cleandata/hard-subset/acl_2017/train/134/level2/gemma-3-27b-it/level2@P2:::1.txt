This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem as both a token-based dependency parsing task and a token-based sequence tagging task, including a multi-task learning setup. The key finding is that while dependency parsing performs poorly, a BiLSTM-based sequence tagging model with distance encoding is robust and improved further via multi-task learning.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of End-to-End Neural AM:** The paper provides a thorough empirical investigation of several neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, establishing a baseline for future work in this direction. This is a valuable contribution as prior work largely relied on pipeline approaches.
2. **Negative Result for Dependency Parsing:** The finding that framing AM as a dependency parsing problem is ineffective is a significant and surprising result. This challenges a natural intuition and provides valuable guidance for future research, demonstrating that complex, global models aren’t necessarily superior for this task.
3. **Effectiveness of Sequence Tagging and Multi-Task Learning:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of a relatively simple BiLSTM sequence tagging model, enhanced with distance encoding and multi-task learning. This provides a strong and practical approach for AM, showing that sophisticated architectures are not always necessary to achieve good performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of several models, including dependency parsers, sequence taggers, and multi-task learning setups, across different evaluation metrics. The inclusion of a strong baseline (ILP model) further strengthens the results.
2. **Clear Problem Formulation & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional pipeline approaches to AM and motivates the exploration of end-to-end neural solutions. The explanation of why feature engineering is problematic is well-reasoned.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper includes insightful discussion of why certain models perform better than others, particularly regarding the challenges of long-range dependencies and the impact of data characteristics (essay vs. paragraph level).  The analysis of the LSTM-ER’s performance degradation is particularly strong.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The proposed approach – a BiLSTM sequence tagger with distance encoding and multi-task learning – is relatively simple to implement and potentially scalable to larger datasets.
5. **Thorough Comparison to Related Work:** The related work section is well-organized and provides a good overview of existing approaches to AM, highlighting the novelty of the proposed work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The experiments are performed on a single, relatively small dataset (PE). While this dataset is well-annotated, the results may not generalize to other types of argumentative text. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but the limitations should be more explicitly stated.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, but lacks specific details about the tuning process. A more detailed description of the hyperparameter search space and selection criteria would increase the reproducibility of the results.
3. **Distance Encoding Implementation:** The description of how distance information is encoded in the sequence tagging model is slightly vague. More details on the implementation of the distance encoding scheme would be helpful. For example, how are large distances handled? Is there a maximum distance considered?
4. **Overly Dense Writing Style:** Some sections of the paper are quite dense and could benefit from clearer organization and more concise language. This makes it slightly difficult to follow the technical details.
5. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While multi-task learning is shown to improve performance, the ablation studies could be more comprehensive. Specifically, it would be beneficial to systematically evaluate the contribution of each auxiliary task individually.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the distance encoding: What is the maximum distance considered in the tag label? How are distances beyond that threshold handled?
2.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter optimization process? What search space was explored, and what criteria were used to select the best hyperparameters?
3.  Given the limitations of the PE dataset, do you have plans to evaluate your approach on other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different domains, argument types)?
4.  In the MTL experiments, were the auxiliary tasks selected based on any specific criteria (e.g., correlation with the main task)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more visually appealing presentation of the experimental results, perhaps including error analysis examples.
*   Consider discussing the potential ethical implications of automated argumentation mining, such as the risk of bias in the models.
*   The formatting of equation (1) is slightly awkward; consider reformatting for clarity.
