This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the task as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning, and compare these approaches against a strong ILP baseline. A key finding is that the dependency parsing framing performs poorly, while a simpler BiLSTM-based sequence tagging model with distance encoding is robust and effective. Furthermore, they demonstrate that multi-task learning improves performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Neural End-to-End AM:** The most significant contribution is a thorough empirical exploration of various neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, providing a valuable baseline for future research.  This includes comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning approaches.
2. **Demonstration of Sequence Tagging Effectiveness:** The paper convincingly shows that a relatively simple BiLSTM sequence tagging model outperforms more complex approaches like dependency parsing for this task, challenging a previously held intuition. This is a strong practical result.
3. **Evidence for Multi-Task Learning Benefits:** The results support the effectiveness of incorporating auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) in a multi-task learning setup to improve overall performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Setup:** The authors use a well-established dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and compare their models to a strong, existing baseline (the ILP model).  The clear presentation of results across different evaluation metrics (C-F1, R-F1) is helpful.
2. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.
3. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The investigation of different tagging models (BLCC, BL) and multi-task learning configurations provides valuable insights into the key components contributing to performance. The ablation studies are a strong aspect of the work.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion surrounding the limitations of LSTM-ER on longer sequences and the benefits of distance encoding provides valuable insights into the challenges of AM.  The observation that the ILP model, while having access to more information, does not necessarily outperform the neural models is also noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Dependency Parsing:** While the paper demonstrates that dependency parsing is ineffective, the exploration of different parsing architectures and hyperparameters feels somewhat limited. It's unclear whether the negative result is inherent to the task or due to specific implementation choices.  More investigation into different neural parsing models might be warranted.
2. **Lack of Detailed Analysis of Tagging Model Predictions:** The paper focuses on overall F1 scores but lacks a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the sequence tagging models. For example, it would be interesting to see a confusion matrix for component types or relation types to identify specific areas for improvement.
3. **Over-reliance on a Single Dataset:** The experiments are conducted solely on the PE dataset.  While this dataset is well-established, the results might not generalize to other domains or argumentation styles. Evaluating the models on additional datasets would strengthen the findings.
4. **Limited Discussion of Generalization:** The paper does not explicitly address the potential for generalization to unseen argument structures or domains. The impact of dataset-specific characteristics on the observed performance is not fully explored.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process for the dependency parsing models? Were different architectures and configurations explored beyond the ones presented?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the sequence tagging models?  For example, are there specific component types or relations that are consistently misclassified?
3.  Do you have any preliminary results on the performance of the models on other argumentation datasets? If not, what steps would be necessary to adapt the models to new domains?
4. The discussion of LSTM-ER's performance drop with longer sequences is insightful.  Would incorporating attention mechanisms or hierarchical models potentially mitigate this issue?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The use of a clear notation for the tagging scheme is appreciated.
*   The supplementary material mentioned (hyperparameter details, software links, error analysis) would be valuable to review.
