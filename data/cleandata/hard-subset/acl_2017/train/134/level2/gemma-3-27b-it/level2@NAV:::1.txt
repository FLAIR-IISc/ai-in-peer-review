## Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

This paper investigates neural network approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging. The authors explore multi-task learning to further improve performance. A key finding is that while dependency parsing performs poorly for this task, a simpler BiLSTM-based sequence tagging model with distance encoding proves surprisingly robust.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the thorough empirical evaluation of several neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. The authors demonstrate that, contrary to intuition, a simpler sequence tagging approach outperforms more complex dependency parsing models, providing valuable insights into the optimal framing of the problem. The observation that multi-task learning improves performance is also a noteworthy contribution, suggesting a promising direction for future research. Finally, the comprehensive experimental results on the PE dataset provide a strong baseline for future work in this area.

A key strength of the paper is the extensive experimental comparison. The authors rigorously evaluate a range of models, including established parsers, their proposed sequence tagging approach, and LSTM-ER, a previously published end-to-end system. The detailed analysis of results, including ablation studies and error analysis (referenced to supplementary material), strengthens the conclusions. The authors also clearly articulate the limitations of each approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The explanation for the poor performance of the dependency parsing approach feels somewhat incomplete. While the authors mention the challenge of long sequences, a deeper investigation into *why* the parser fails to capture argumentation structure would be beneficial. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the chosen dataset (PE essays), which may introduce biases that affect the generalizability of the findings. While the authors acknowledge the dataset's peculiarities, a more in-depth analysis of how these impact the results would be valuable.  Furthermore, the connection between the observation about distance encoding being important and the LSTM-ER's performance drop on longer texts could be more clearly articulated. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the dependency parsing models? Specifically, what aspects of argumentation structure are they failing to capture?
2.  How might the specific characteristics of the PE essay dataset (e.g., argumentative style, topic domain) influence the performance of the different models?
3.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind why distance encoding is particularly important for sequence tagging models in the context of AM, and how this relates to the LSTM-ER results on longer texts?

**Additional Comments:**

The formatting and readability of the paper are generally good. Providing links to the code and datasets would be greatly appreciated.
