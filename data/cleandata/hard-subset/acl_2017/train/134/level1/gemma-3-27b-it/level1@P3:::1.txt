This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The central finding is that while dependency parsing performs poorly, sequence tagging with BiLSTMs and multi-task learning improve performance over a strong ILP baseline.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Effective Neural Architectures for End-to-End AM:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of different neural network architectures for tackling the complex end-to-end AM task, demonstrating the viability of neural approaches and identifying BiLSTM-based sequence tagging as a particularly effective strategy. This is a significant contribution, given the prior reliance on feature engineering and ILP solvers.
2. **Negative Result for Token-Level Dependency Parsing in AM:** The finding that framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem is ineffective is a valuable insight. While discourse parsing has been explored, this work specifically demonstrates the limitations of this approach in an end-to-end setting, guiding future research away from potentially unproductive avenues.
3. **Demonstration of Multi-Task Learning Benefits:** The paper convincingly shows that incorporating auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) via multi-task learning improves overall performance. This suggests a fruitful direction for further optimization and generalization of AM models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a rigorous and comprehensive evaluation, comparing multiple model architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, LSTM-ER, multi-task learning) against a strong baseline (ILP model). They experiment with different tagging models (BLCC, BL) and explore various auxiliary tasks in the MTL setup.
2. **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional AM approaches, motivating the need for end-to-end neural solutions that avoid manual feature engineering and error propagation. The motivation for each modeling choice (dependency parsing, sequence tagging) is well-explained.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Discussion:** The results are not simply presented but are carefully analyzed and discussed, providing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The authors delve into why dependency parsing fails and why sequence tagging succeeds, offering plausible explanations. The discussion of the long-range dependencies and limitations of LSTM-ER is particularly insightful.
4. **Well-Established Dataset:** The use of the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset, a high-quality and widely recognized resource for AM, strengthens the validity and reproducibility of the results.
5. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of relevant work in AM, discourse parsing, and neural sequence modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Dependency Parsing Architectures:** The dependency parsing experiments primarily focus on established parsers (MST, Mate, Dyer et al., Kiperwasser & Goldberg). A more thorough exploration of specialized neural dependency parsers potentially more suited to argumentation structures could be beneficial. While the paper acknowledges that “dependency parsers’ global view upon the data may be too complex,” more investigation could have been performed.
2. **Distance Encoding in Sequence Tagging:** While the distance encoding in the sequence tagging approach is a clever idea, the paper doesn’t fully explore the implications of using a limited distance range (as hinted in Figure 2). Is the performance sensitive to the maximum distance considered? A more detailed ablation study on the distance encoding scheme would be valuable.  The rationale for a fixed maximum distance is unclear.
3. **Hyperparameter Optimization Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks specifics. Providing more details about the optimization process (e.g., search space, optimization algorithm) would enhance reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of the model's sensitivity to hyperparameters.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the authors offer some discussion of the errors, a more detailed error analysis—categorizing common error types and providing illustrative examples—would be helpful for identifying areas for improvement. For example, what kinds of relation errors are most frequent?
5. **Limited Comparison to Other Neural AM Models:** The comparison is mostly limited to the ILP baseline. Adding comparisons to other recent neural AM models (if available) would further strengthen the contribution of the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter optimization process used for each model, including the search space and optimization algorithm?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the sequence tagging model to the maximum distance considered in the distance encoding scheme? Did you experiment with different maximum distance values?
3.  Can you provide more specific examples of the types of errors made by the different models, particularly the dependency parser and the sequence tagger? What are the most frequent error patterns?
4.  The paper mentions a potential trade-off between modularity and constraints. Could you discuss this trade-off further, perhaps with examples of how the constraints in the dependency parsing approach might hinder performance in specific cases?
