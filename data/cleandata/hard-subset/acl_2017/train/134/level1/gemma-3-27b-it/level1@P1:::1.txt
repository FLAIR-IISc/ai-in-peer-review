This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, and exploring multi-task learning. The authors demonstrate that while dependency parsing performs poorly, sequence tagging (specifically BiLSTM-CRF) achieves strong results. Furthermore, incorporating auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup improves performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Neural End-to-End AM:** The paper successfully demonstrates that neural network-based approaches can achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance to existing feature-engineered ILP models for end-to-end AM. This is a significant step towards a more streamlined and adaptable approach to this complex task.
2. **Identification of Sequence Tagging as a Strong Baseline:** The finding that a relatively simple BiLSTM-CRF sequence tagging model performs robustly across different classification scenarios is a key contribution. This provides a strong baseline for future work in AM, suggesting that complex parsing structures are not necessarily required for good performance.  The encoding of distance information into the tag labels is also a useful insight.
3. **Evidence for the Benefits of Multi-Task Learning:** The paper provides empirical evidence that incorporating ‘natural’ subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup leads to performance improvements. This suggests a valuable direction for further research in improving AM models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conducted a thorough empirical evaluation of different approaches, comparing them against a strong baseline (ILP model) on a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). The clear presentation of results in tables allows for easy comparison.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion of why dependency parsing fails and why LSTM-ER performs better on paragraph vs. essay level provides valuable insights into the challenges of AM and the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling approaches. The analysis regarding long-range dependencies and the benefit of constraining relation search space is particularly strong.
3. **Clear Problem Framing and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional pipeline approaches to AM and motivates the need for end-to-end neural learning. The connection to argumentation theory and the discussion of the ‘arbitrary’ nature of argument definitions is well-articulated.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The findings have practical implications for the development of automated argumentation analysis tools, which could be used in a variety of applications, such as legal reasoning, scientific discourse analysis, and educational writing assistance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Tagging Model Variations:** While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of BiLSTM-CRF, the exploration of different tagging model architectures (e.g., incorporating character-level embeddings more thoroughly, experimenting with different LSTM variants) seems limited. A more systematic evaluation of different tagging architectures could potentially yield further performance gains.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks specific details about the search space and methods used. Providing more information about the hyperparameter tuning process would increase the reproducibility of the results.
3. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** While the authors discuss the general reasons for performance differences, a more detailed qualitative error analysis, including examples of misclassified instances and their corresponding errors, would be helpful for understanding the limitations of the proposed models and identifying areas for improvement.
4. **Focus on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). While this is a widely used dataset, the generalizability of the findings to other types of argumentative text could be questioned. Evaluating the models on additional datasets would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the optimization algorithm used?
2.  You mention that LSTM-ER degrades significantly on essay level for relation identification due to the increased number of possible relations. Have you experimented with techniques to mitigate this issue, such as limiting the search space of potential relations based on proximity or syntactic constraints?
3.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, why did including both component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks *not* always lead to improvements? Were there specific combinations of tasks or architectures that performed better than others?
4.  Could you elaborate on the specific “natural subtasks” you considered beyond component detection and relation identification? Were there any other subtasks that you explored but found to be less effective?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the label set Y could be made clearer.
*   The references are somewhat verbose (e.g., including author names after each citation). A more standard citation style would be preferred.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key differences between the different models and their respective strengths and weaknesses.
