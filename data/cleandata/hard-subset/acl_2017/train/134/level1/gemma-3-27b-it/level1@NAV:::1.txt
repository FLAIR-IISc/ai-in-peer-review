## Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

This paper investigates neural network approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, with a multi-task learning extension. The core finding is that while dependency parsing struggles in this context, a simpler BiLSTM-based sequence tagging approach, encoding distance information between argumentative components, performs robustly. Furthermore, incorporating natural subtasks via multi-task learning improves performance.

The most significant contribution of this work, in my view, is the comprehensive empirical evaluation of different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, particularly the demonstration of the ineffectiveness of a token-based dependency parsing approach. This is a surprising and important negative result, challenging a potentially intuitive framing of the problem. The strong performance of the sequence tagging model, combined with the benefits of multi-task learning, also provides a solid baseline for future research. A second key contribution is the release of results on a standard dataset (Stab and Gurevych, 2016), providing valuable benchmarks for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation of multiple approaches, including comparisons to a strong feature-based ILP baseline. The use of both paragraph and essay-level evaluation provides valuable insights into the challenges of long-range dependencies.
2. **Important Negative Result:** Demonstrating the limitations of token-based dependency parsing for end-to-end AM is a valuable contribution. This saves researchers from pursuing potentially fruitless avenues of investigation.
3. **Robust Baseline:** The sequence tagging model achieves competitive performance, establishing a strong baseline for future research in this area. The inclusion of distance encoding is a sensible and effective design choice.
4. **Multi-Task Learning Benefits:** The observation that incorporating natural subtasks as auxiliary tasks improves performance highlights the value of leveraging task relationships in AM.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is valuable, the individual components (BiLSTM-CRF, multi-task learning) are well-established in the NLP literature. The novelty lies more in the application to the AM domain and the empirical findings.
2. **Distance Encoding Simplification:** The distance encoding scheme (coding distances into the tag label) seems somewhat arbitrary and potentially limiting. A more nuanced representation of relationships might be beneficial.
3. **Analysis of Errors:** While the paper presents results, a more in-depth error analysis would be valuable for understanding the limitations of the proposed models and guiding future work. For instance, a closer look at errors related to long-range dependencies would be insightful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific auxiliary tasks for multi-task learning? Were other potential tasks considered and why were they excluded?
2.  Regarding the distance encoding, did you experiment with other representations of relational information (e.g., learned embeddings of relationships)? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide more details about the types of errors made by the models, particularly concerning long-range dependencies? Were there specific patterns or challenges encountered?
