This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The authors demonstrate that dependency parsing performs poorly, while sequence tagging (specifically BiLSTM-CRF) is robust. They also show that multi-task learning improves performance. The primary contribution is the exploration of these neural framings for end-to-end AM and the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of sequence tagging, a surprisingly simple approach. A secondary contribution lies in the negative result regarding dependency parsing; challenging a potentially intuitive framing. Finally, the investigation into multi-task learning as a means to enhance performance is valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Performance:** The paper presents strong empirical results, consistently outperforming a feature-based ILP baseline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) on the persuasive essay dataset. The performance gains, particularly with the BiLSTM-CRF tagger, are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed neural approach.  The clear presentation of results in Table 1 allows for direct comparison.
2. **Insightful Negative Result:** The finding that dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, despite its intuitive appeal given the tree-like structure of arguments, is a valuable contribution. This directs future research away from potentially fruitless avenues and highlights the importance of empirical validation. The authors thoughtfully discuss potential reasons for this failure, linking it to the complexity of modeling long sequences and global dependencies with parsers.
3. **Robust Sequence Tagging:** The demonstration that a relatively straightforward BiLSTM-CRF sequence tagging model achieves strong performance is a significant finding. This suggests that, at least for this dataset, complex models are not necessarily required and that leveraging local dependencies is crucial. The explanation relating to simpler models being less prone to overfitting and better handling long sequences is compelling.
4. **Multi-task Learning Benefits:** The positive results from multi-task learning – incorporating component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks – highlight the benefits of exploiting relationships between subtasks in AM. This opens avenues for further exploration of MTL strategies to improve performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The experiments are conducted solely on the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset. While this dataset is a valuable resource, its limited size and specific characteristics (e.g., student essays) raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The performance drop observed when scaling to the essay level, specifically for LSTM-ER, reinforces this concern. Evaluating on more diverse datasets is crucial.
2. **Distance Encoding in Tagging:** The method of encoding distance between related components directly into the tag labels (STagT) is a potentially limiting factor. While it acknowledges the importance of long-range dependencies, it discretizes the distance information and might struggle with very large distances or complex relationships. Further investigation into alternative methods for capturing these dependencies, such as attention mechanisms, might be beneficial.
3. **LSTM-ER Complexity and Performance Discrepancy:** Although LSTM-ER shows promise on the paragraph level, its significantly degraded performance on the essay level warrants further investigation. The authors attribute this to the increased number of potential relations, but a more detailed analysis of the model’s behavior and potential bottlenecks would be helpful. The discrepancy between paragraph and essay level performance should be more thoroughly addressed.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** While the paper explores different framings and MTL configurations, a more systematic ablation study would strengthen the analysis. For example, systematically removing layers or components from the BiLSTM-CRF model to assess their individual contributions would provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the size of the label set Y (in STagT) is potentially infinite but expect it to be finite in practice. Could you provide more detail on how you handled the potentially unbounded distance values in practice? Was there a truncation or any other form of regularization applied?
2.  Given the poor performance of dependency parsing, did you experiment with different dependency parsing architectures or pre-training strategies that might be more suitable for this task?
3.  Regarding LSTM-ER's performance drop on the essay level, could you provide an analysis of the types of errors it makes in relation identification compared to STagBLCC? Is it primarily misclassifying relations, or is it failing to identify relations altogether?
4.  How sensitive are the multi-task learning results to the choice of auxiliary tasks? Did you explore other potential auxiliary tasks besides component detection and relation identification?
