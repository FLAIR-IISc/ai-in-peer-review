This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The authors demonstrate that a sequence tagging approach performs robustly, while dependency parsing is ineffective. They also show that multi-task learning improves performance.

The primary contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration of effective neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, specifically showcasing the strength of sequence tagging models. While end-to-end neural AM has been proposed, this paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation of different neural framings, identifying a robust baseline (BiLSTM tagging) and demonstrating improvements through multi-task learning. A secondary contribution is the negative result regarding dependency parsing – contrary to intuition, framing AM as dependency parsing does not yield strong performance. This finding is valuable for guiding future research. Finally, the dataset used (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) is a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Validation of Sequence Tagging:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that a relatively simple BiLSTM-CRF sequence tagging approach is a strong baseline for end-to-end AM. This is significant because it provides a practical and effective starting point for future research, avoiding the complexity of more elaborate models. The thorough experimentation across different tagging configurations (BLCC, MTL) strengthens this argument.
2. **Negative Result on Dependency Parsing:** The finding that dependency parsing performs poorly is a key insight. It challenges the intuition that argument structures naturally map to tree-like dependencies and suggests that alternative representations may be more suitable. This is a valuable contribution as it guides the community away from a potentially unproductive research direction.
3. **Demonstration of Multi-Task Learning Benefit:** The authors show that incorporating auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) via multi-task learning improves performance. This highlights the potential for leveraging relationships between subtasks within AM and suggests a promising avenue for further investigation.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across different models, datasets (paragraph vs. essay level), and evaluation metrics. This thoroughness strengthens the validity of the findings and allows for a clear comparison between the different approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of these components—neural end-to-end AM with BiLSTM-CRF, demonstration of the ineffectiveness of dependency parsing, and multi-task learning—is valuable, each individual component isn't inherently novel. BiLSTM-CRF is a standard sequence tagging technique, and the application of multi-task learning is common in NLP. The core novelty lies in the application and evaluation within the specific domain of computational argumentation mining.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions some observations about the errors made by the dependency parser, a more detailed error analysis across all models would be beneficial. Understanding *why* certain models fail in specific cases could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, a discussion of common errors in relation classification would be insightful.
3. **Suboptimal Tuning of LSTM-ER:** The authors acknowledge that LSTM-ER's performance degrades significantly at the essay level. It's unclear if the parameters were sufficiently tuned for this level, or if the model inherently struggles with longer sequences. Further investigation and optimization of LSTM-ER could potentially yield better results.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the different models. This information would be valuable for assessing the practicality of the approaches, particularly for large-scale applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis for the sequence tagging models, specifically focusing on the types of errors made in relation classification?
2.  Did you experiment with different parameter settings or optimization strategies for LSTM-ER when applied to the essay level data? If so, could you share those results?
3.  What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of the best-performing models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) on the essay-level data?
4. How sensitive are the MTL results to the choice of auxiliary tasks? Were other auxiliary tasks considered, and if so, what was their impact on performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used in Equation (1) for the tag set Y is somewhat dense and could be clarified.
*   The phrase "natural subtasks" used to describe the auxiliary tasks in MTL is subjective. It would be helpful to provide a more concrete justification for why these tasks are considered "natural."
*   Figures could be improved by providing a more detailed explanation of each component.
