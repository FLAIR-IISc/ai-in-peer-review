

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as token-based dependency parsing, token-based sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The authors' key empirical finding is that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to previous work that assumed argument structures naturally form trees. Their main contributions as I see them are:

1. Demonstrating that a neural sequence tagging model with distance encoding (STagBLCC) outperforms state-of-the-art ILP-based approaches by 10-15% F1 on component identification, establishing a new SOTA for end-to-end AM.

2. Providing the first thorough empirical analysis showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (despite argument structures often forming trees), which challenges assumptions in previous work.

3. Showing that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) significantly improves performance (up to 5% F1 increase), offering a practical framework for future AM systems.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application and Strong Empirical Results**: This is the first study applying neural end-to-end learning to computational argumentation mining. The authors demonstrate that their neural approach outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP-based method (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) by 10-15% F1 on component identification, while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and complex ILP constraint design. The results are robust across both exact and approximate matching metrics, with STagBLCC achieving 66.69% C-F1 (50%) and 39.83% R-F1 (50%) on the paragraph level.

2. **Important Insight on Dependency Parsing Failure**: The paper provides a compelling empirical case that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to intuition. The authors show that dependency parsers (including neural ones) fail to capture the complex argumentation structures, with the best parser (LSTM-Parser) performing 100% better on exact spans than the next best (Kiperwasser) but still significantly worse than the tagging approach. The explanation that dependency parsers' global view becomes "too complex" for longer sequences is well-supported by the dramatic performance drop from paragraph to essay level (from 63.23% to 44.38% F1).

3. **Effective Multi-Task Learning Framework**: The paper demonstrates that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) significantly improves performance, with the best configuration (Y-3:YC-3:YR-3) achieving 50.51% F1 (50%) compared to 47.25% for the base model. This finding is particularly valuable because it shows that treating component and relation identification as separate but jointly modeled tasks outperforms naively coupling them, providing a clear framework for future AM systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Explanation of Dependency Parsing Failure**: While the paper demonstrates that dependency parsing fails, it doesn't adequately explain *why* this is the case beyond noting that the global view becomes too complex for longer sequences. A deeper analysis of specific failure modes (e.g., how parsers handle long-range dependencies or multiple major claims) would strengthen this key insight.

2. **Limited Dataset Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on the Stab and Gurevych (2016) persuasive essays dataset. Given that argumentation structures can vary significantly across domains (e.g., legal documents vs. social media), this limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors should discuss how their approach might perform on other datasets with different argumentation structures.

3. **Incomplete Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper mentions LSTM-ER but doesn't sufficiently compare it to more recent neural relation extraction approaches specifically designed for complex relations. The paper claims LSTM-ER's general framework is too broad for long documents, but doesn't demonstrate how their distance-encoding tagging approach specifically addresses this limitation compared to other neural relation extraction methods.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Long-Document Limitations**: The paper shows a significant performance drop from paragraph to essay level (from 63.23% to 44.38% F1), but doesn't provide a clear path forward for handling longer documents. This is a critical limitation for real-world AM applications where argumentation structures often span entire documents.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the LSTM-Parser's good performance, relative to the other parsers, is due to its encoding of the whole stack history," but doesn't provide specific evidence for this claim. Could you elaborate on how the LSTM-Parser's architectural differences specifically address the long-range dependencies in AM?

2. The paper mentions that "92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences" in their dataset. Given this, why does the distance encoding in the tagging model work better than dependency parsing, which is designed for capturing such relationships?

3. The paper states that "component and relation identification should be treated separately, but modeled jointly." How does this approach differ from the LSTM-ER model, which also decouples these tasks but uses a TreeLSTM that incorporates dependency structure?
