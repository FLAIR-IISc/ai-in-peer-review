

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the problem of identifying argumentative structures in text without relying on hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures. The authors present three main contributions: (1) the first neural end-to-end solutions for AM that eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design; (2) empirical demonstration that sequence tagging models based on BiLSTMs outperform dependency parsing approaches and achieve better results than the state-of-the-art ILP model; and (3) evidence that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel neural framing of end-to-end argumentation mining:** The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to explore neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining. Prior work relied on pipeline architectures with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which the authors correctly identify as problematic due to error propagation and the need for dataset-specific adaptation. The neural approach eliminates these limitations, representing a methodological advance that opens new research directions in argumentation mining.

2. **Robust performance of sequence tagging models:** The paper provides compelling evidence that sequence tagging models (particularly STagBLCC) outperform dependency parsing approaches and the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple evaluation metrics. The results are consistent across both exact (100%) and approximate (50%) match levels, with STagBLCC achieving 59.34%/66.69% C-F1 (100%/50%) and 74.08%/39.83% R-F1 (100%/50%) on the paragraph level. This finding is particularly valuable as it contradicts the intuitive expectation that dependency parsing (given the tree-like nature of argument structures) would perform well.

3. **Effective multi-task learning setup:** The paper demonstrates that adding natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning framework improves performance. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks with inherent substructures. The authors show that including the component detection task as an auxiliary task consistently yields better results than adding the relation identification task, providing useful insights for future work in multi-task learning for structured prediction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The paper evaluates exclusively on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), a single dataset of persuasive student essays. While this is a well-established dataset in argumentation mining, the lack of evaluation on multiple datasets (e.g., the corpus from Peldszus and Stede, 2016) limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors should have tested their models on multiple datasets to validate their claims across different argumentation structures and domains.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies on key design choices:** The paper compares different neural approaches but lacks thorough ablation studies on key design decisions. For example, the paper doesn't explore the impact of different embedding sizes, LSTM layer counts, or word embedding types (e.g., GloVe vs. fastText). This omission makes it difficult to assess the sensitivity of their approach to hyperparameters and to determine which design choices were most critical to their performance gains.

3. **Inadequate comparison to recent neural approaches:** The paper compares against the ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and mentions the LSTM-ER model from Miwa and Bansal (2016), but doesn't thoroughly compare to more recent neural approaches published around ACL 2017. The paper references Potash et al. (2017) but doesn't evaluate against this approach, which presents a more general encoder-decoder framework for argumentation mining. A comprehensive comparison with contemporary neural methods would strengthen the paper's claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that sequence tagging outperforms dependency parsing, despite the tree-like nature of argument structures. Could the authors elaborate on why dependency parsing performs poorly compared to sequence tagging, particularly given their token-level framing (as opposed to component-level framing used in previous work)?

2. The paper shows that LSTM-ER performs better on paragraph-level than essay-level prediction. How do the authors handle paragraph boundaries in their models, and would explicitly modeling paragraph structure (e.g., as a separate input layer) further improve performance?

3. The paper claims that sequence tagging models can handle long-range dependencies (with 30% of relations having distance -1 and 2/3 of distances in {-2,-1,1}). Could the authors provide specific examples from their model's predictions that demonstrate successful handling of long-range dependencies (e.g., relations with distance > 5)?
