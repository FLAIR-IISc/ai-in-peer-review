

This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The primary contribution is the demonstration that neural sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagBLCC) significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art ILP-based approaches (by ~13% C-F1 on paragraph level) while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The second key contribution is the insightful empirical analysis showing dependency parsing approaches fail for end-to-end AM due to complex search spaces with long sequences, whereas local sequence tagging models better capture long-range dependencies. The third contribution is the demonstration that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task improves performance by ~5% F1.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant methodological contribution by being the first to explore neural end-to-end AM without feature engineering. The empirical results are robust and consistent across multiple metrics, with STagBLCC outperforming the ILP baseline on both component and relation identification. The analysis explaining why dependency parsing fails (due to complex search spaces with long sequences) provides valuable theoretical insight beyond the empirical results. The multi-task learning approach with component detection as an auxiliary task is a meaningful contribution that could benefit other sequence labeling tasks.

Weaknesses: The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Stab and Gurevych's persuasive essays), which weakens claims about generalizability. The "first neural end-to-end" claim is overstated as the authors adapt LSTM-ER (Miwa and Bansal, 2016), which was designed for relation extraction. The paper fails to address why dependency parsing performs poorly on essay level (with 100%+ performance drop) compared to paragraph level, which is critical for real-world applications. The comparison with more recent neural architectures (e.g., transformers) is missing, especially given the paper's 2016 timeframe.

Questions to Authors: How would your approach perform on other AM datasets with different argumentation theories? Could the multi-task learning framework be adapted to handle more complex argumentation structures beyond the tree-like hierarchy observed in the PE dataset?
