

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and using an LSTM-ER model. The authors demonstrate that token-level dependency parsing performs poorly compared to sequence tagging models, which can effectively handle long-range dependencies in argumentation structures. They also show that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) improves performance.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and constraints.
2. Demonstration that neural sequence tagging models with distance encoding outperform state-of-the-art feature-based ILP models.
3. Insightful analysis showing dependency parsing is ineffective at the token level (contrary to component-level approaches), while simple sequence tagging models robustly handle the problem.

## Strengths
1. **First comprehensive neural approach to end-to-end AM**: This is a significant contribution as the authors present the first neural end-to-end framework for AM, addressing a problem previously tackled only with pipeline approaches requiring manual feature engineering. The paper convincingly demonstrates that neural models can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) on the PE dataset, with improvements of up to 13% in component F1 scores. This represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

2. **Insightful comparison of different neural framings**: The authors provide a thorough empirical comparison of different neural approaches, revealing important insights about why certain methods work better than others. Their finding that token-level dependency parsing performs poorly (while component-level approaches might work) is particularly valuable, as it corrects a common misconception about the natural fit of dependency parsing for argumentation structures. The paper effectively explains this through the distribution of relation distances (Figure 2) and the nature of argument structures.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness of multi-task learning**: The paper shows that incorporating natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup improves performance. This finding has broader implications beyond AM, as it suggests that similar approaches could benefit other sequence labeling tasks with complex interdependencies. The authors provide clear evidence that the component detection task is particularly helpful as an auxiliary task.

4. **Practical implementation guidance**: The paper offers concrete recommendations for practitioners, such as using more restrained modeling for long documents with many entities (where STagBLCC excels) versus more general modeling for shorter texts (where LSTM-ER performs better). This practical guidance is valuable for real-world applications of argumentation mining.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited dataset scope**: The paper evaluates only on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which has specific structural properties (e.g., paragraphs containing complete argument structures). Without evaluation on multiple datasets with varying argumentation structures, the generalizability of the findings is questionable. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't address how their approach might perform on datasets with different argumentation patterns.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: While the paper compares different model architectures, it lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific design choices. For example, the paper doesn't provide evidence showing how much of the performance improvement comes from the distance encoding in the sequence tagging model versus the neural architecture itself. Without this, it's difficult to determine which components of their approach are truly essential.

3. **Limited discussion of failure modes**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze where their models fail, which would help understand the limitations of the approach. For instance, the paper notes that LSTM-ER performs poorly on essay-level relation identification but doesn't explore why (e.g., whether it's due to the longer sequences, the specific argumentation structures in the dataset, or other factors). Understanding failure modes is critical for advancing the field.

4. **Inadequate comparison to other neural approaches**: The paper mentions the encoder-decoder approach as a possible future direction but doesn't compare with other recent neural approaches for AM that might have been published around the same time (e.g., Potash et al., 2017). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's claims about the superiority of their approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper demonstrates that paragraph-level processing outperforms essay-level processing. Could the authors provide more insight into how the paragraph structure of the PE dataset (where each paragraph contains a complete argument structure) influences this result, and how the approach would generalize to datasets without this paragraph structure?

2. The authors state that their sequence tagging model with distance encoding is effective for long-range dependencies. Could they provide more evidence about how the distance encoding specifically contributes to performance, perhaps through an analysis of how often the model correctly captures long-distance relationships?

3. The paper mentions that LSTM-ER performs better than STagBLCC on components but worse on relations. Given that the relation performance is heavily dependent on component accuracy, could the authors clarify why LSTM-ER's relation performance degrades so significantly on essay level, and whether this is due to the model architecture or the specific dataset properties?
