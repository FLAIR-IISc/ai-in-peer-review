

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem through dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a combined sequence-tree model. The authors evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). 

The main contributions as I see them are:
1. The first comprehensive comparison of neural end-to-end approaches for AM, demonstrating that neural methods eliminate the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.
2. The demonstration that sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagT) significantly outperforms dependency parsing for AM, contrary to previous assumptions.
3. The finding that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel framing of AM as sequence tagging with distance encoding**: The authors make a compelling case for why sequence tagging with distance encoding is particularly suitable for AM, given the long-range dependencies between argument components. Their label encoding (b, t, d, s) effectively captures the argument structure in a way that is both interpretable and practical. The empirical evidence showing that STagT outperforms dependency parsing by 10-20% on key metrics is strong and well-supported.

2. **Thorough empirical comparison of different neural architectures**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER) across different evaluation levels (paragraph vs. essay). The analysis of why certain models work better on certain levels (e.g., LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level due to combinatorial explosion of relations) is insightful and adds significant value beyond just reporting results.

3. **Meaningful multi-task learning results**: The paper demonstrates that adding natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks improves performance, with the component detection task being particularly beneficial. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks beyond AM and provides a practical approach for improving model performance in similar domains.

4. **Clear explanation of limitations**: The authors provide a nuanced analysis of why dependency parsing fails for AM (despite being a "natural" choice), noting that the long sequences and complex argument structures make the global tree constraint too restrictive. Their explanation of why LSTM-ER degrades on essay level (due to combinatorial explosion of relations) is particularly valuable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but Miwa and Bansal (2016) already proposed a neural end-to-end model for entity and relation extraction that could be applied to AM. The authors do not adequately compare their work to this prior model, which weakens their novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient analysis of model limitations**: While the authors identify some limitations (e.g., LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level), they don't thoroughly explore the constraints of their own STagT approach. For instance, how does the distance encoding handle cases where components are extremely far apart (beyond the maximum distance observed in training)? The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the model's limitations.

3. **Inadequate discussion of evaluation metrics**: The paper uses "level α matching" (100% vs. 50% match) but doesn't provide sufficient justification for why these specific thresholds are appropriate for AM. The authors should have discussed how these metrics relate to the practical needs of AM applications.

4. **Lack of comparison to recent sequence labeling advances**: The authors reference Ma and Hovy (2016) and Huang et al. (2015) but don't situate their work within the rapidly evolving field of neural sequence labeling. They could have compared their approach to more recent architectures like BERT-based models, which have shown significant improvements in similar tasks.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first to investigate neural end-to-end AM, but Miwa and Bansal (2016) proposed a neural end-to-end model for entity and relation extraction that could be directly applied to AM. Why wasn't this model included in your comparison? How would your results change if you compared against it?

2. Your STagT approach encodes distance between components as a feature. Given that the maximum observed distance in your dataset is 10 (as shown in Figure 2), how would you handle argument structures where components are more than 10 components apart? Would you need to modify your model for such cases?

3. The paper notes that "the LSTM-ER model has access to both paragraph and essay level information" while neural models only use paragraph-level information for training. How would you address this evaluation discrepancy in future work to ensure fair comparison?

4. The authors mention that "text sequences are much longer on essay level than on paragraph level" and that this causes degradation for LSTM-ER. How would you extend your approach to handle extremely long documents without sacrificing performance?
