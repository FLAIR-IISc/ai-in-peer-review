Contribution — First neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining, removing the need for hand-crafted features and pipeline constraints.
Contribution — Neural sequence tagging models with distance encoding outperform prior feature-based ILP models on the PE dataset.
Contribution — Token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM while simple sequence tagging models robustly handle long-range argumentation structures.

Strength — Presents the first comprehensive neural end-to-end framework for AM with up to ~13% component F1 gain over prior work.
Strength — Thorough empirical comparison of neural framings, showing why some methods (e.g., token-level parsing) fail and others succeed.
Strength — Demonstrates multi-task learning (component detection + relation ID) improves performance, with component detection being a particularly helpful auxiliary task.
Strength — Provides practical guidance on model choices for different document types (e.g., STagBLCC for long documents, LSTM-ER for shorter texts).

Weakness — Evaluation is limited to a single dataset (PE), raising questions about generalizability to other argumentation structures.
Weakness — Lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate contributions of components like distance encoding versus architecture.
Weakness — Limited analysis of failure modes (e.g., why LSTM-ER fails at essay-level relation identification).
Weakness — Inadequate comparison to other contemporary neural AM approaches (e.g., encoder-decoder or Potash et al., 2017).

Question — How does the PE dataset's paragraph-level structure (paragraphs as complete arguments) influence paragraph-vs-essay performance and generalization to other datasets?
Question — Can the authors provide evidence isolating the contribution of distance encoding to capturing long-range relations?
Question — Why does LSTM-ER degrade on essay-level relations — is it the architecture, dataset properties, or longer sequences?
