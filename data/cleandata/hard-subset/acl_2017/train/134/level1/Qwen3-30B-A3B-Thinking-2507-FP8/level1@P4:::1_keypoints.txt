- Summary: paper presents neural end-to-end methods for computational argumentation mining, comparing sequence tagging, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning to prior ILP/pipeline approaches.

- Contribution 1: first neural end-to-end solutions for argumentation mining removing hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.

- Contribution 2: empirical finding that BiLSTM-based sequence tagging outperforms dependency parsing and the state-of-the-art ILP model.

- Contribution 3: multi-task learning with component detection and relation identification improves performance, with component detection as the most helpful auxiliary task.

- Strength 1: novel neural framing that avoids pipeline error propagation and dataset-specific ILP tuning, opening new directions for AM.

- Strength 2: strong, consistent empirical results showing sequence tagging (STagBLCC) achieves higher C-F1 and R-F1 on paragraph-level evaluation.

- Strength 3: effective multi-task setup demonstrating benefits of natural subtasks for structured sequence labeling.

- Weakness 1: evaluation limited to a single dataset (PE dataset), restricting claims about generalizability across domains and corpora.

- Weakness 2: insufficient ablation studies on hyperparameters and architecture choices (embedding sizes, LSTM layers, embedding types).

- Weakness 3: inadequate comparison to contemporaneous neural approaches (e.g., Potash et al. 2017 and other ACL 2017 methods).

- Question 1: why does dependency parsing perform poorly compared to sequence tagging given token-level framing?

- Question 2: how are paragraph boundaries handled, and would explicit paragraph-structure modeling improve paragraph vs. essay-level performance?

- Question 3: can the authors provide concrete examples showing their model correctly captures long-range relations (e.g., distance > 5)?
