- Summary: Comprehensive study of neural end-to-end argumentation mining using dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning on the Persuasive Essays dataset; neural models outperform a feature-based ILP and show benefits of treating components and relations separately but jointly.

- Contribution 1: Proposes the first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning).

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates improved performance of proposed neural approaches over a feature-based ILP model on the PE dataset.

- Contribution 3: Shows that modeling component detection and relation detection separately but jointly yields better results.

- Strength 1: Provides a comprehensive empirical study covering multiple neural techniques on a benchmark dataset.

- Strength 2: Presents clear performance gains of neural methods over a traditional ILP baseline.

- Strength 3: Offers useful insights about the joint modeling of components and relations that can guide future work.

- Weakness 1: Evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Persuasive Essays), raising concerns about generalizability.

- Weakness 2: Lacks comparison with other neural architectures (e.g., encoder–decoder models).

- Weakness 3: Overemphasizes sequence tagging and underexplores other paradigms like graph-based methods.

- Question 1: How do the approaches generalize to other AM domains (e.g., scientific papers, legal texts)?

- Question 2: Can you compare the proposed methods with other neural models such as encoder–decoder architectures?

- Question 3: How are out-of-vocabulary words and unseen entities handled at test time?
