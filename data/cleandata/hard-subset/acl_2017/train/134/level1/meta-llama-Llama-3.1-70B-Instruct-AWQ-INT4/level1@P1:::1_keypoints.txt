- Proposes neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining framed as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.  
- Evaluates multiple architectures including dependency parsing, a specific sequence tagging label set, multi-task sequence tagging, and the Miwa & Bansal (2016) model.  
- Claims to present the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM and reports several models outperforming the joint ILP baseline.  
- Contribution: first neural end-to-end AM methods that avoid manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.  
- Contribution: shows sequence tagging and multi-task tagging approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.  
- Contribution: empirically compares different neural architectures for AM (parsing, tagging, multi-task).  
- Strength: novel neural end-to-end approach removes need for manual feature/constraint engineering.  
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across several neural architectures.  
- Strength: reports new state-of-the-art end-to-end results on the PE dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016).  
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of limitations and potential drawbacks of neural end-to-end approaches for AM.  
- Weakness: does not clearly explain why dependency parsing framing underperforms sequence tagging.  
- Weakness: does not explore other neural architectures such as encoder–decoder models.  
- Question: please detail limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed approaches.  
- Question: please explain why dependency parsing performs poorly versus sequence tagging.  
- Question: have you considered other architectures (e.g., encoder–decoder) for AM?  
- Question: provide more details on hyperparameter optimization and the pre-trained embeddings used.  
- Question: provide more detailed error analysis and the types of errors made by different models.
