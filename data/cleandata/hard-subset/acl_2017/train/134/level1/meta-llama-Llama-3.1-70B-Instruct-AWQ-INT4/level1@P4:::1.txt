**Summary and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes neural end-to-end learning techniques for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem as a token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning setup. They evaluate the performance of these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset and compare them with a feature-based ILP model. The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2. A comparison of different neural approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.
3. An evaluation of the performance of these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a thorough evaluation of different neural approaches to computational AM, providing insights into their strengths and weaknesses.
2. The authors propose a novel framing of AM as a multi-task learning problem, which shows promising results.
3. The paper provides a detailed comparison with a feature-based ILP model, highlighting the advantages of neural approaches.
4. The authors use a robust evaluation metric, which takes into account both component and relation identification.
5. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on existing neural architectures, such as BiLSTM-CRF and LSTM-ER, without proposing significant modifications or improvements.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by the different approaches, which could provide valuable insights into their limitations.
3. The paper does not discuss the interpretability of the neural models, which is an important aspect of AM.
4. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why the LSTM-ER approach performs poorly on relation identification on the essay level.
5. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results and their potential applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the different approaches, including the types of errors and their frequencies?
2. How do you plan to improve the interpretability of the neural models, and what techniques do you think could be used to achieve this?
3. Can you provide a clear explanation of why the LSTM-ER approach performs poorly on relation identification on the essay level, and how this could be addressed?
4. How do you think the results of this paper could be applied in real-world scenarios, such as argumentation mining in educational settings?
5. Are there any plans to extend this work to other datasets or domains, and how do you think the approaches proposed in this paper could be adapted to these new settings?
