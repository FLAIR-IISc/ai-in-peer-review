**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate several neural network architectures, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a model that combines sequential and tree structure information. The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2. A thorough evaluation of different neural network architectures for AM, including a comparison with a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model.
3. The demonstration that a standard neural sequence tagging model can perform robustly in different environments.
4. The finding that a multi-task learning setup can improve performance by jointly learning natural subtasks of the full AM problem.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of different neural network architectures for AM, including a comparison with a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model. This provides a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
2. **Novel application of neural networks**: The authors apply neural networks to AM, a task that has traditionally been addressed using feature-based approaches. This demonstrates the potential of neural networks for this task.
3. **Robust performance**: The authors show that a standard neural sequence tagging model can perform robustly in different environments, including paragraph and essay levels.
4. **Improved performance with multi-task learning**: The authors demonstrate that a multi-task learning setup can improve performance by jointly learning natural subtasks of the full AM problem.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a single dataset (PE), which may limit the generalizability of their findings to other datasets or domains.
2. **Overfitting**: The authors note that some of their models may be prone to overfitting, particularly on the essay level. This could be addressed by using regularization techniques or collecting more data.
3. **Lack of interpretability**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the learned representations or the decision-making process of their models. This could be addressed by using techniques such as feature importance or attention visualization.
4. **Comparison to other neural architectures**: The authors only compare their approach to a single feature-based ILP model. It would be interesting to compare their approach to other neural architectures, such as encoder-decoder models or graph-based models.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in your models, particularly on the essay level?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations or the decision-making process of your models?
3. How do you think your approach could be extended to other domains or datasets?
4. Have you considered using other neural architectures, such as encoder-decoder models or graph-based models, for AM?
