**Summary**

This paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate various neural techniques, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning, to tackle the AM problem. They evaluate their approaches on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset and compare the results with a feature-based ILP model. The experimental results show that the proposed neural approaches outperform the ILP model, and the authors highlight the importance of treating component and relation detection separately but jointly.

**Main Contributions**

1. **First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM**: The authors propose several neural approaches to tackle the AM problem, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.
2. **Improved performance over feature-based ILP model**: The experimental results show that the proposed neural approaches outperform the feature-based ILP model on the PE dataset.
3. **Importance of treating component and relation detection separately but jointly**: The authors highlight the importance of treating component and relation detection as separate tasks but modeling them jointly to achieve better performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive study**: The paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end computational AM, covering various techniques and evaluating their performance on a benchmark dataset.
2. **Improved performance**: The proposed neural approaches outperform the feature-based ILP model, demonstrating the effectiveness of neural methods for AM.
3. **Insights into component and relation detection**: The authors provide valuable insights into the importance of treating component and relation detection separately but jointly, which can inform future research in AM.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed approaches on a single dataset (PE), which may not be representative of other AM datasets or tasks.
2. **Lack of comparison with other neural approaches**: The paper does not compare the proposed approaches with other neural methods, such as encoder-decoder models, which may be relevant for AM.
3. **Overemphasis on sequence tagging**: The paper focuses primarily on sequence tagging approaches, which may not be the most effective method for AM, and neglects other potential approaches, such as graph-based methods.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the proposed approaches perform on other AM datasets or tasks, such as argumentation mining in scientific papers or legal texts?
2. Can you compare the proposed approaches with other neural methods, such as encoder-decoder models, to determine their relative strengths and weaknesses?
3. How do the proposed approaches handle out-of-vocabulary words or unseen entities in the test data?
