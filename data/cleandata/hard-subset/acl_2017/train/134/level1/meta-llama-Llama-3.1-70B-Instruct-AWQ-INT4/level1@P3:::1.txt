**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a study on neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem as a token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning setup. They investigate the effectiveness of these approaches on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). The results show that a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly, and a multi-task learning setup improves performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM**: The authors present the first study on neural end-to-end AM, providing a new direction for the field.
2. **Effective sequence tagging model**: The authors show that a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly in different environments.
3. **Improved performance with multi-task learning**: The authors demonstrate that a multi-task learning setup where natural subtasks of the full AM problem are added as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to AM**: The authors' neural end-to-end approach eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing, providing a more efficient and effective solution.
2. **Robust sequence tagging model**: The authors' sequence tagging model performs well in different environments, demonstrating its robustness and generalizability.
3. **Improved performance with multi-task learning**: The authors' multi-task learning setup improves performance, demonstrating the effectiveness of this approach in AM.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on the PE dataset, which may not be representative of all AM tasks.
2. **Overfitting**: The authors' models may be overfitting to the training data, as they do not provide any regularization techniques or early stopping methods.
3. **Lack of comparison to other neural models**: The authors only compare their approach to a feature-based ILP model and do not provide a comparison to other neural models, such as those using attention mechanisms or graph convolutional networks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting in their models?
2. Can the authors provide a comparison to other neural models, such as those using attention mechanisms or graph convolutional networks?
3. How do the authors plan to evaluate their approach on other AM datasets, such as those with different argumentation structures or domains?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' approach is a significant contribution to the field of AM, providing a new direction for research. However, the evaluation is limited, and the authors should consider providing more robust evaluation methods, such as cross-validation or evaluation on multiple datasets. Additionally, the authors should consider providing more details on their implementation, such as the hyperparameter settings and the architecture of their models.
