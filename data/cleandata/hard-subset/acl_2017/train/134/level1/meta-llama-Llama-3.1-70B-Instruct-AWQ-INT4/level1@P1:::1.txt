**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes neural end-to-end learning techniques for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem as a token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning setup. They investigate several approaches, including a dependency parsing framing, a sequence tagging framing with a specific label set, and a multi-task sequence tagging problem. The authors also evaluate the model of Miwa and Bansal (2016) that combines sequential and tree structure information. The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM and shows that several of them perform better than the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining (AM), which do not require manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. The authors show that several neural approaches perform better than the state-of-the-art joint ILP model, including a sequence tagging framing with a specific label set and a multi-task sequence tagging problem.
3. The paper investigates the effectiveness of different neural architectures for AM, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to AM using neural end-to-end learning techniques, which eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of different neural architectures for AM, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.
3. The paper presents new state-of-the-art results in end-to-end AM on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed approaches, including the potential drawbacks of using neural end-to-end learning techniques for AM.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why the dependency parsing framing performs poorly compared to the sequence tagging framing.
3. The paper does not investigate the effectiveness of other neural architectures, such as encoder-decoder models, for AM.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the limitations of the proposed approaches, including the potential drawbacks of using neural end-to-end learning techniques for AM?
2. Can you explain why the dependency parsing framing performs poorly compared to the sequence tagging framing?
3. Have you considered investigating the effectiveness of other neural architectures, such as encoder-decoder models, for AM?
4. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter optimization and pre-trained word embeddings used in the experiments?
5. Can you provide more details on the error analysis and the types of errors made by the different models?
