Summary
The paper studies neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset. It compares four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) token-level sequence tagging with a composite label encoding component boundaries, types, distances, and stance, (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) variants of the tagger using “natural” subtasks, and (iv) an entity–relation model (LSTM-ER) that jointly models entity tagging and relation classification. Results show dependency parsing to be ineffective for token-level end-to-end AM, while BiLSTM-based tagging is robust and benefits from MTL; LSTM-ER is strongest on paragraphs but degrades on long essays for relations. Neural approaches match or surpass an ILP-based feature-rich joint system without hand-engineered features/constraints.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding that simple neural sequence tagging with a distance-augmented label space outperforms token-level dependency parsing and a strong ILP pipeline for end-to-end AM, with robust gains across paragraph/essay settings.
2) Evidence that MTL with “natural” subtasks (component-only and relation-only projections) improves end-to-end tagging, and that decoupling entities and relations (as in LSTM-ER) is beneficial especially when the number of entities is small.
3) A careful, negative result on token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM, clarifying when global tree-structured modeling is counterproductive compared to local tagging.

Strengths
- Thorough comparative study across modeling paradigms with clear takeaways. The paper contrasts transition- and graph-based dependency parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser & Goldberg, Dyer LSTM-Parser), BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging (STagBLCC), an MTL tagger (STagBL), LSTM-ER, and a strong ILP baseline. On paragraph level, STagBLCC improves over the best parser by ~8 points C-F1 (100%: 66.69 vs 58.86) and ~4 points R-F1 (100%: 39.83 vs 35.63), and LSTM-ER is best overall (C-F1 70.83, R-F1 45.52). On essay level, STagBLCC remains robust (C-F1 63.23, R-F1 34.82) while LSTM-ER degrades on relations (R-F1 29.56), supporting the central claims.
- Clear analysis of document-length effects and search-space constraints. The paragraph vs. essay analysis, coupled with the observation that relation candidates grow quadratically with entities for LSTM-ER, convincingly explains why constrained tagging (with one outgoing link and distance bias) fares better on long essays.
- Practical end-to-end modeling without hand-crafted features/constraints. The neural systems eliminate manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design, yet match or exceed the ILP baseline (e.g., STagBLCC paragraph-level C-F1 66.69 vs ILP 62.61; R-F1 39.83 vs 34.74).
- Sensible exploitation of structure via labels and tasks. Encoding distance in the tag and using component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks yield consistent gains in MTL (e.g., Y-3:YC-3 improves essay-level C-F1 100% from 49.59 to 54.58; global F1 100% from 34.35 to 38.90), and the paper shows auxiliary “C” is more helpful than “R”.
- Negative result and diagnostic insights on dependency parsing. The token-level parsing framing underperforms markedly (e.g., LSTM-Parser 58.86 C-F1 100% on paragraphs, far lower on essays), and the analysis attributes this to tree search complexity and span boundary issues, informing future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited generality and dataset dependence. All results are on a single domain-specific corpus with particular structural idiosyncrasies (paragraph-contained structures; almost-trees; claims connect to all major claims). The proposed tag set explicitly encodes stance types tied to this dataset (Supp/Att/For/Ag) and relies on single-head constraints and finite distance distributions observed here (−11..+10). It is unclear how well these choices carry over to other AM corpora with different schemata or denser cross-paragraph links.
- Incomplete and potentially uneven comparisons. Dependency parsers are trained on paragraphs due to memory limits, with essay-level reported only for the LSTM-Parser; other parsers’ essay-level results are omitted, making the cross-paradigm comparison less balanced. For MTL, the base tagger is weakened (no CRF, no char-CNN), so the reported MTL gains are not directly comparable to STagBLCC; an MTL variant atop BLCC would strengthen the claim. The ILP results per level are not clearly separated in Table 1 (only one row shown), and it is unclear whether essay-level ILP numbers are directly comparable.
- Decoding and constraint satisfaction are under-specified. The paper does not detail how token-level tags are decoded into coherent component spans and single-head relations while enforcing global validity (e.g., consistency of distance d and stance s across tokens within a component, resolving conflicts, handling invalid tags). For example, ensuring “each premise has exactly one parent” under tag noise may need post-processing, but the method is not fully described.
- Tagset explosion and long-range handling not fully analyzed. The composite label space Y grows with distance values and stance types, which can exacerbate data sparsity; while Figure 2 shows most distances are short, the model still needs to cover rare long-range tags. There is no ablation on capping/pruning distance values, nor comparison to alternative relation encodings (e.g., pointer networks) within the same framework.
- Limited error analysis and diagnostics on boundary/type errors. The paper notes span boundary issues for parsers and provides some distance-based analyses (Figure 4), but a deeper breakdown (precision/recall per component type, cross-sentence relation behaviors, and typical failure modes for long components) would strengthen the conclusions and guide future improvements.

Questions to Authors
- How exactly do you decode sequence tags into components and relations? Please detail the post-processing to enforce coherent spans, single outgoing links, and consistent (d, s) across tokens, and how conflicts are resolved.
- Did you cap the set of distance values in Y during training/inference? If so, at what threshold, and how sensitive are results to this cap? If not, how many distinct labels were observed in practice?
- Can you report essay-level results for all dependency parsers and paragraph/essay-level results for the ILP baseline separately, to ensure balanced comparisons?
- Have you tried MTL using the stronger BLCC backbone (with CRF and char-CNN) rather than BL? If not, do you expect similar gains?
- How sensitive are the models to violations of the PE corpus assumptions (e.g., cross-paragraph relations, multiple heads)? Could your tagging formulation handle many-to-many relations if needed?
- For LSTM-ER, did scheduled sampling and entity pretraining follow Miwa & Bansal exactly? Could removing the sentence-level TreeLSTM (which you argue is ineffective across sentences) improve essay-level relation performance?
