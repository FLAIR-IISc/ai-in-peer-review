Summary and contributions
This paper explores neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), targeting simultaneous component segmentation/typing and relation identification/typing from raw text. The authors evaluate four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding component BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance (STagT), (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary subtasks (component-only and relation-only), and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa and Bansal’s LSTM-ER model that jointly extracts entities and relations. Experiments are conducted on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus, at both paragraph and essay levels, and compared against a feature-based ILP pipeline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A strong empirical study demonstrating that simple neural sequence taggers (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) with a distance-informed label space set new SOTA on end-to-end AM on PE, outperforming a prior ILP joint model, especially on relations in the more realistic essay-level setting.
2) A careful negative result: token-level dependency parsing (even with neural parsers) performs substantially worse for end-to-end AM, especially on longer sequences, illuminating the limits of global token-level tree enforcement for this task.
3) Evidence that multi-task learning with “natural” auxiliary tasks (component-only, relation-only) improves performance of a weaker tagger, and that decoupling component and relation modeling (as in LSTM-ER or via MTL) is beneficial.

Strengths
- Clear, well-justified modeling comparisons and insightful takeaways:
  The paper considers multiple plausible framings with different inductive biases (global parsing vs local tagging; constrained vs unconstrained relation structures) and ties observed performance patterns to data properties (e.g., long cross-sentence links, number of entities). The analysis that LSTM-ER excels on components but suffers on relations at essay level due to O(m^2) pairwise decisions, while STagT implicitly constrains the search via distance coding, is compelling (Table 1; Figure 4).

- Strong empirical gains and breadth of evaluation:
  The BiLSTM-CRF-CNN (STagBLCC) and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baseline on paragraph-level; STagBLCC is best overall on essay-level relations. The paper reports exact and approximate F1 for both components and relations and a composite F1, using accepted metrics (Persing & Ng). The paragraph vs essay analysis is informative and relevant given practical deployment.

- Negative result with diagnostic value:
  The failure of token-level dependency parsing—even with strong neural parsers—on long documents is important given a body of work proposing discourse/argument parsing. The authors provide plausible explanations (global search over trees on long sequences; token-level head choices; stack-history access helping but still insufficient), and the drastic paragraph→essay degradation is a useful cautionary result.

- Multi-task learning insights:
  The study shows consistent gains from auxiliary component/relations tasks (Table 2), and that “lower task to lower layer” is not necessarily optimal here. This is a concrete, transferable insight for multi-attribute sequence tagging beyond AM (e.g., morphosyntactic tagging).

- Thoughtful design of tagging labels and analysis of distance priors:
  Encoding the inter-component distance in labels operationalizes a key prior (short-range preference) while remaining end-to-end. The empirical distance distribution (Figure 2) and the effect on relation correctness by distance (Figure 4) provide valuable intuition.

Weaknesses
- Comparability and fairness of settings not fully controlled:
  The dependency parsers are trained/tested only at the paragraph level due to memory limits, while other models are evaluated at both levels. The ILP baseline is reported as operating at both levels but appears only as one line in Table 1; it is unclear whether those numbers correspond to paragraph or essay settings, and whether its access to paragraph-in-essay position (which helps major claims) creates an information advantage. Please clarify training/evaluation regimes per model per level to ensure like-for-like comparisons.

- Label space explosion and dataset-specific assumptions:
  The STagT design conflates multiple variables into one label, which can lead to sparsity as distances increase and as stance types differ by component type (Support/Attack vs For/Against). While Figure 2 suggests most relations are local on PE, it is unclear how robust this label space is on corpora with more variable structures or longer-range discourse dependencies. No experiments beyond PE are provided, and the approach implicitly assumes near-tree structures (each component links to one other). This limits generalizability.

- Token-level dependency parsing framing seems misaligned:
  The chosen token-level head assignments (e.g., all non-argumentative tokens pointing to END; premises pointing to the first token of the target component) produce trees that are only weakly aligned with argument structure and are prone to span errors (as observed). It is not surprising this underperforms; more faithful component-level parsing (predicting component heads with a component segmenter upstream or jointly) would be a fairer test of the “dependency parsing for AM” hypothesis. As is, the negative result may partly reflect the chosen token-level encoding rather than inherent unsuitability of parsing.

- Limited structural validity analysis:
  The paper argues that neural models often learn structural constraints implicitly, but does not quantify how often the outputs violate basic AM constraints (e.g., multiple heads per premise; cycles; invalid stance-type/component-type combinations). A summary of constraint violations per model would strengthen the claim and help explain performance differences, especially for LSTM-ER at essay level.

- LSTM-ER setup could be strengthened:
  Given that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, the TreeLSTM over syntactic dependencies is indeed suboptimal. However, simple and standard pruning or candidate selection (e.g., same-paragraph restriction; distance-based pruning; heuristic top-K by a biaffine scorer) could reduce the O(m^2) relation space and likely improve essay-level R-F1. The lack of such adaptations weakens the comparison.

Questions to authors
1) Clarify exactly which numbers in Table 1 for ILP correspond to paragraph vs essay level, and whether training/evaluation were done separately on both levels. Did ILP use paragraph ID or essay position as features? Were its constraints adapted between levels?

2) For STagT, what is the effective size of the tag set Y in train and test? How many distance values are present in training, and how often do unseen distances occur at test time? How do you handle unseen labels (e.g., via collapsing or UNK distance)?

3) Structural validity: What fraction of predicted structures violate key AM constraints for each model (e.g., multiple heads for premises, cycles, invalid stance-component type pairs)? Does MTL reduce such violations?

4) For dependency parsing, did you try component-level parsing with a preliminary or jointly learned segmenter (e.g., predict component spans, then parse components)? This could address span boundary issues and test the hypothesis more directly.

5) For LSTM-ER, did you try simple pruning of candidate relation pairs (distance or paragraph-based) or a constrained decoder (e.g., select one head per premise) to make essay-level inference more tractable?

6) Reproducibility details: Please provide the exact hyperparameters, pretrained embeddings, dev split, and code pointers (mentioned as supplementary) to facilitate replication. How were early stopping and model selection performed?

Overall, this is a timely, empirically solid paper with clear takeaways for end-to-end neural AM. Addressing the comparability concerns and adding structural validity analyses would further strengthen the work.
