Summary
The paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. It compares four framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing (both feature-based and neural parsers), (2) token-level sequence tagging with a joint label encoding component boundaries/types, inter-component distance, and stance (BIO × {P,C,MC,⊥} × distance × stance), (3) multi-task tagging where natural subtasks (component-only, relation-only) are auxiliary objectives, and (4) an entity–relation model (LSTM-ER) combining BiLSTM-CRF entity tagging with TreeLSTM relation classification. Against a feature-based joint ILP baseline, the authors report that: token-level dependency parsing underperforms; BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging (STagBLCC) is robust, particularly on essay-level data; MTL improves tagging; and LSTM-ER excels on shorter (paragraph) inputs but degrades on longer (essay) inputs due to relation combinatorics. Results set new state-of-the-art F1 on the PE dataset and reduce reliance on hand-crafted features/constraints.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A comprehensive, controlled empirical study that establishes simple neural sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels as a strong end-to-end AM solution that outperforms a prior ILP-based joint system; and provides evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ill-suited for end-to-end AM on this dataset.
2) An analysis of model behavior across input granularity (paragraph vs. essay), showing the trade-off between flexible entity–relation modeling (LSTM-ER) and constrained tagging, with MTL yielding consistent gains by leveraging natural subtasks.
3) A practical labeling scheme that encodes long-range dependencies via component-distance within token tags, demonstrating that local models can capture key AM relations without explicit global constraints.

Strengths
- Strong, well-controlled comparison across modeling paradigms: The paper evaluates multiple parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser & Goldberg, Stack-LSTM), a state-of-the-art tagger (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), an MTL variant, and LSTM-ER, on the same data, with both paragraph- and essay-level settings and consistent metrics (Persing & Ng F1 at 100%/50%). Statistical significance testing (Wilcoxon on document-level F1s) is reported.
- Clear, technically motivated task formulations: The distance-augmented tagging label is justified by empirical distance distributions; the analysis of why token-level dependency parsing struggles (very large search space for long sequences) and why LSTM-ER degrades on essays (O(m^2) relation candidates) is clear and supported (e.g., relation accuracy vs. |d| plots).
- Robust performance with reduced engineering: STagBLCC and LSTM-ER attain or exceed the ILP system while removing hand-crafted features and manually designed ILP constraints, improving portability to other datasets.
- Insightful multi-task findings: Adding component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks improves performance of the simpler BiLSTM tagger; the component task is more beneficial than the relation task, and the best configuration does not strictly follow a “lower task at lower layer” hierarchy, contributing practical guidance for MTL design in AM-like tagging.
- Granularity-aware evaluation: The paper explicitly contrasts paragraph vs. essay settings, which is important given structure locality in PE; the analysis quantifies the benefits and limitations of each approach under different input lengths.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset diversity and structural assumptions: All conclusions are drawn from a single domain/dataset with (near-)tree discourse structure and paragraph-contained relations. Generalization to other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts, legal, scientific) with different structures (e.g., non-trees, cross-paragraph relations) is not demonstrated.
- Tag label space and sparsity concerns: Encoding distances directly into labels inflates the tag space and may cause sparsity, especially for long-distance relations. The paper does not report the effective label cardinality, distance capping/normalization strategies, or ablations on distance bucketing; it is unclear how robust the method is when longer distances occur more frequently.
- Dependency parsing framing may be suboptimal as instantiated: Only token-level parsing was attempted; a more natural two-stage neural approach (component detection followed by component-level dependency/graph parsing) was not evaluated, leaving open whether “dependency parsing is ineffective” or whether the chosen granularity is the main issue. Moreover, parsers could not be uniformly trained on essay-level due to memory, complicating fairness.
- Incomplete methodological details in the main text: Key implementation aspects (pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, optimization, scheduled sampling for LSTM-ER, MTL layer selection protocol) are deferred to supplementary material. This limits reproducibility and makes it harder to attribute gains to specific design choices.
- Missing structural constraints beyond implicit learning: While the paper argues that models can learn structural constraints, it does not explore light-weight constrained decoding (e.g., ILP/DP postprocessing or soft constraints) for tagging to further improve relation consistency, nor ablations measuring the benefit of the one-link-per-component constraint in STag relative to unconstrained LSTM-ER on essays.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Tagging label space: What is the maximum observed |d|, and how many distinct labels Y were used at train/test? Did you cap or bucket distances, and how does performance change with coarser distance bins?
- Stance encoding: You use Supp/Att and For/Ag. How are these mapped across component types (e.g., claim–major claim vs. premise–claim), and are there cases where both labels can apply? Any confusion analysis?
- MTL architecture: How were task-to-layer assignments selected (grid search? dev set)? Did you try adding a CRF/top-layer dependency structure to the MTL tagger to close the gap with BLCC?
- Component-level parsing: Did you experiment with detecting components first (e.g., BLCC) and then applying a component-level neural parser/graph model (e.g., biaffine or MST) for relations? If so, how does it compare to STag and LSTM-ER?
- Efficiency and scalability: What are training/inference times and memory footprints for essay-level models across approaches? How do models scale with longer documents or more entities per document?
- Statistical testing: Please detail the exact pairing and multiple-comparison handling for Wilcoxon tests across many systems/metrics.

Overall, the study is technically sound within its scope, the analyses are coherent, and the empirical results are strong. The main novelty is empirical and framing-oriented rather than algorithmic, but the conclusions are useful to the AM community. Further experiments on broader datasets and component-level relation modeling would strengthen the claims.
