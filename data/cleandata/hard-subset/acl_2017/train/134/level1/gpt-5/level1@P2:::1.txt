Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural models, comparing several framings: token-level dependency parsing, token-level sequence tagging that encodes component type, distance, and stance in a composite label; multi-task learning (MTL) variants; and an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal (2016)’s LSTM-ER joint entity-relation model. Experiments on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset evaluate at paragraph and essay level against the feature-based ILP joint model of Stab & Gurevych (2016).

Contributions (ordered by strength):
1) Empirical finding that simple neural sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF + char-CNN) with a distance-augmented label space is a strong, robust end-to-end baseline that matches or exceeds the prior ILP SOTA and outperforms token-level dependency parsing; MTL further improves results. Evidence: On paragraph level, STagBLCC achieves C-F1 66.69/74.08 (100/50) and R-F1 39.83/44.02, surpassing ILP in R-F1 and overall F1 (49.87/55.22 vs 44.68/55.23).
2) Careful comparative analysis across modeling paradigms and document granularity, revealing when more general relation architectures help or hurt. LSTM-ER is best on paragraphs (C-F1 70.83/77.19; R-F1 45.52/50.05; F1 55.42/60.72) but degrades on essays for relations (R-F1 29.56/32.72), while STagBLCC remains comparatively robust (essay R-F1 34.82/39.68).
3) Negative result on token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM, with plausible analysis (global tree search over long sequences is brittle; token-level head design is unnatural). Even the best parser (LSTM-Parser) trails the tagger (paragraph C-F1 58.86/68.20; R-F1 35.63/40.87).
4) Demonstration that adding “natural” subtasks via MTL (component-only or relation-only projections) provides consistent gains over the single-task tagger with the same backbone (e.g., essay F1 improves from 34.35/47.25 to 38.90/50.51).

Strengths
1) Solid, comprehensive empirical comparison with strong baselines and fair setups. The paper evaluates multiple families (parsing, tagging, joint entity-relation) at both paragraph and essay levels, and provides statistical testing (Wilcoxon over documents). The cross-paradigm findings are actionable: simple local tagging with an inductive bias (distance-coded labels) beats global token-level parsing and scales better to long sequences.
2) Clear, insightful analysis that explains observed trends. The paper articulates why LSTM-ER excels on paragraphs yet struggles on essays: the O(m^2) relation candidate space with many entities, and the lack of hard constraints like “each premise links to exactly one target,” which STag encodes implicitly via labels. Figure 4 supports the argument that explicit distance coding biases toward realistic short-range links.
3) Practical end-to-end modeling without hand-crafted features or ILP constraints, yet competitive or superior to ILP. This lowers the barrier for adapting AM to new annotation schemes, addressing a real pain point in AM where theory-dependent labels vary across corpora.
4) Useful negative result and design lessons on token-level dependency parsing for AM. The head/label design is carefully specified, training on paragraphs vs essays is explored, and the analysis (stack-history advantages of LSTM-Parser; long sequence brittleness; “too large” spans) provides guidance for future work.
5) Evidence that MTL with natural subtasks helps. Although the MTL backbone (BL) is weaker than BLCC, the consistent gains from adding component-only and relation-only tasks (e.g., essay F1 from 34.35/47.25 to 38.90/50.51) suggest a generally useful recipe for complex multi-attribute tagging (relevant beyond AM, e.g., morphological tagging).

Weaknesses
1) Generalization beyond a single dataset is unclear. All results are on the PE essays with specific structural properties (e.g., paragraph-contained structures, special claim–major-claim links). The label design and some modeling choices (e.g., re-linking claims to one major claim to maintain tree structure; paragraph-level containment) may overfit data idiosyncrasies. Without a second corpus (e.g., microtexts, web discourse) or cross-domain tests, claims of generality are limited.
2) Tag space scalability and sparsity concerns are underexplored. Encoding distance and stance into the tag label expands Y substantially. While the authors note observed distance ranges are finite, the explosion can hurt learning, especially for rare long distances. There is no analysis of label frequency, distance clipping, or smoothing strategies; nor comparisons to alternative encodings (e.g., predicting distance separately, pointer-style linking).
3) Fairness and isolation of factors in MTL vs BLCC comparisons. The MTL gains are shown on a weaker BL backbone (no char CNN, no CRF). It is thus hard to quantify the incremental value of MTL vs architectural capacity. A BLCC+MTL variant (or at least BL+char CNN and BL+CRF ablations) would better isolate the contribution of MTL.
4) Limited robustness reporting (variance across random seeds/hyperparameters) and limited per-condition breakdowns. While statistical tests are reported, run-to-run variance is not discussed, and some models (e.g., LSTM-Parser on essays) are said to vary “drastically.” More systematic sensitivity analyses and breakdowns (by distance bins, component types, stance) would strengthen the conclusions about long-range handling and model inductive biases.
5) Token-level dependency parsing might be a strawman. The chosen head design (token-level, head at first token of target span) is arguably unnatural; component-level parsing with learned segmentation or joint span-based parsing might fare better. The negative conclusion about dependency parsing may therefore be too broad without alternative span-aware parsing baselines.

Questions to Authors
1) Tag set design: Did you cap the distance d or backoff rare distances? What is the distribution of composite labels, and how sparse is Y in practice? Could you report performance by |d| bins and the effect of distance clipping on accuracy and label entropy?
2) MTL architecture: Did you try MTL with a BLCC backbone (char CNN + CRF) to separate MTL gains from backbone capacity? If not, can you comment on expected gains or provide a small-scale experiment?
3) Generalization: Have you attempted transfer or zero-shot adaptation to another AM corpus (e.g., microtexts) to test whether the distance-augmented labeling and MTL carry over?
4) LSTM-ER constraints: If you enforce “each premise links to exactly one target” at inference (e.g., via simple post-processing or constrained decoding), does LSTM-ER’s essay-level R-F1 improve substantially?
5) Dependency parsing: Did you experiment with span-based parsing or component-level parsing (predict spans first, then parse), or pointer-network variants that directly predict heads at the component level? This could clarify whether the negative result is specific to token-level trees.
