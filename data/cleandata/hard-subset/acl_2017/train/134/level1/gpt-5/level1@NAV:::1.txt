Summary: The paper studies neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the PE corpus, contrasting four framings: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a composite BIO+type+distance+stance label, multi-task tagging with auxiliary component/relation tasks, and an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal). The central empirical finding is that simple local tagging models (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) robustly outperform token-based dependency parsers; MTL yields further gains; LSTM-ER excels on paragraphs but degrades on essays for relations; and several neural settings rival or surpass a feature-rich ILP pipeline without handcrafted features/constraints.

Contributions (as I see them):
- Empirical demonstration that local sequence tagging with distance-coded labels provides strong, robust end-to-end AM performance and outperforms token-level dependency parsing and a joint ILP baseline on key metrics.
- Careful analysis of modeling choices across document granularity (paragraph vs essay), revealing when constrained local models vs more general entity–relation architectures are preferable.
- Evidence that multi-task learning with “natural” subtasks (component detection, relation cues) yields consistent improvements.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated comparison across diverse neural framings with consistent evaluation (C-F1 and R-F1 at 100%/50%), including significance testing; STagBLCC improves ~13% C-F1 and ~11% R-F1 over the best parser at paragraph level, and matches/exceeds ILP.
- Insightful diagnosis of long-range dependencies via explicit distance encoding; the analysis of distance distributions and success probabilities (Fig. 2, Fig. 4) convincingly explains when models succeed or fail.
- Practical, reproducible message: eliminating handcrafted features and ILP constraints while retaining or improving performance is impactful for AM datasets with divergent annotation schemes.
- Useful granularity study: the essay vs paragraph results tease apart sequence-length effects and combinatorial relation explosion, clarifying LSTM-ER’s strengths/limits.
- MTL ablations are informative: auxiliary component labeling consistently helps, and architectural placement findings (not strictly “lower task → lower layer”) are valuable to practitioners.

Weaknesses:
- Methodological novelty is moderate: the core techniques (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, MTL, LSTM-ER) are adaptations; the key novelty lies in task framing and labeling rather than new architectures.
- Single-domain evaluation (PE essays) limits external validity; no cross-dataset transfer or domain shift test.
- Fairness and scope of baselines: ILP uses essay/paragraph info and constraints, but some neural models are paragraph-only; conversely, newer seq2seq/pointer baselines are cited but not compared.
- Tag-set explosion and sparsity from distance×stance labeling are under-analyzed (e.g., OOV distances, rare labels); decoding guarantees for globally consistent structures are not discussed.
- Limited reporting on stability: variance across random seeds and sensitivity to hyperparameters are not quantified, and some models show large parametrization variance.

Questions to Authors (optional):
- How large is the effective tag inventory (distance cutoffs), and how are unseen distances handled at test time?
- What is performance variance across seeds for STagBLCC and MTL setups?
- Can the distance-coded tagger generalize to corpora with non-tree, many-to-many links?
- Could lightweight global consistency (e.g., constrained decoding or post-hoc ILP) further improve R-F1 without heavy engineering?
