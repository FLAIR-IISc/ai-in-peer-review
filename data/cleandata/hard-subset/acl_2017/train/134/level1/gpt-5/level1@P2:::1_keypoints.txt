- Paper compares end-to-end neural AM framings (token-level dependency parsing, composite-label sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset against an ILP baseline.

Contributions:
- Simple BiLSTM-CRF+char-CNN tagger with distance-augmented labels is a strong, robust end-to-end baseline that matches/exceeds ILP SOTA; MTL yields further gains.
- Comparative analysis shows model performance depends on paradigm and document granularity: LSTM-ER excels on paragraphs but degrades on essays, while the tagger remains robust.
- Token-level dependency parsing is a negative result for end-to-end AM: global tree search over long sequences is brittle and unnatural for component linking.
- Adding natural subtasks via MTL (component-only or relation-only) consistently improves performance over the single-task tagger.

Strengths:
- Solid, comprehensive empirical comparison with fair setups and statistical testing across multiple model families and granularities.
- Clear, insightful analysis explaining why certain architectures (e.g., LSTM-ER) work on short texts but struggle on longer ones.
- Practical end-to-end approach without hand-crafted features or ILP constraints, lowering adaptation barriers to new annotation schemes.
- Useful negative result and detailed design lessons regarding token-level dependency parsing for AM.
- Evidence that MTL with natural subtasks helps learning, suggesting applicability beyond AM.

Weaknesses:
- Generalization is unclear due to evaluation on a single dataset with dataset-specific structural assumptions.
- Tag space scalability and sparsity (distance-augmented composite labels) are underexplored; no label-frequency or clipping analysis provided.
- MTL vs BLCC comparisons may be confounded by backbone capacity differences; ablations with BLCC+MTL are missing.
- Limited reporting of robustness (seed/hyperparameter variance) and per-condition breakdowns like distance bins or component types.
- The dependency parsing negative result may be a strawman since alternative span-aware or component-level parsing variants were not evaluated.

Questions to authors:
- Did you cap/backoff distances in the tag set, and what is the composite label distribution and sparsity by distance bins?
- Did you try MTL with the stronger BLCC backbone (char CNN + CRF) to isolate MTL benefits from model capacity?
- Have you attempted transfer or zero-shot evaluation on another AM corpus to test generality of distance-augmented labeling and MTL?
- If you enforce “each premise links to exactly one target” at inference for LSTM-ER, does essay-level relation performance improve?
- Did you experiment with span-based/component-level parsing or pointer-network variants to determine if the parsing negative result is specific to token-level trees?
