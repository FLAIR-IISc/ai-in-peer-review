- Investigates neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL) on the Persuasive Essays dataset vs an ILP baseline.
- Sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) outperforms dependency parsing and the ILP baseline for component and relation identification.
- Multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks (e.g., component detection) improves performance by leveraging task interdependencies.
- Dependency parsing is suboptimal for AM due to global complexity and sensitivity to data sparsity, especially on long sequences.
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple neural framings and paragraph- and essay-level experiments.
- Proposed neural approaches achieve state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset.
- Provides detailed error analyses and discusses trade-offs between modularity and constraints across framings.
- Eliminates need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints by using neural methods.
- Limited methodological novelty: established models (BiLSTM-CRF, MTL) are adapted rather than new techniques introduced.
- Inclusion of dependency parsing is of limited impact and could be replaced by more promising alternatives (e.g., encoder-decoder models).
- Findings may be dataset-specific to PE, leaving generalizability to other AM datasets unclear.
- Relation identification remains a bottleneck for long documents, with no concrete solutions proposed.
- Question: How do the models generalize to other AM datasets and were additional datasets tested?
- Question: Why were encoder-decoder models not explored for capturing complex AM relationships?
- Question: Were other auxiliary tasks (e.g., stance detection) tried in the multi-task learning setup?
- Overall: Worth accepting for strong evaluation and SOTA results despite limited novelty; addressing generalizability and relation-ID would strengthen the work.
