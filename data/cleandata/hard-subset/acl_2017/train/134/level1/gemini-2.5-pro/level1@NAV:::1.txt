**Summary and Contributions:**
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural, end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the task in several ways, including as token-level dependency parsing and sequence tagging, and compare these approaches against an established joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER) and a feature-based ILP baseline. The primary contribution is a thorough comparative analysis of these different neural framings, offering valuable insights into how model choices (e.g., local tagging vs. global parsing) perform on a complex structured prediction task, particularly in relation to text length. A key secondary contribution is the successful design and application of a sequence tagging model that creatively encodes component boundaries, types, and relational information (distance and stance) into a single label, proving to be a robust and scalable solution.

**Strengths:**
1. The experimental design is a major strength. By systematically evaluating four distinct neural paradigms on both paragraph- and essay-level data, the paper provides a clear and well-supported analysis of their relative merits. The finding that simpler, local sequence tagging models are more robust on longer texts than global parsers or all-pairs relation models is a particularly important and well-evidenced takeaway for the field.
2. The formulation of AM as a sequence tagging problem is innovative and effective. Encoding relational distance directly into the tag allows a BiLSTM to capture long-range dependencies without the computational complexity of parsing or considering all entity pairs, which is a key reason for its strong performance on the challenging essay-level task.
3. The work achieves new state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset, surpassing a strong, feature-engineered ILP-based system. This clearly demonstrates the viability of feature-free, end-to-end neural methods for this task.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The architectural novelty of the work is limited. The paper’s main contribution lies in its novel problem framings and comparative analysis, rather than in the development of new neural architectures. The core models used (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-Parser, LSTM-ER) are established methods applied to a new domain.
2. The explanation for the performance degradation of the LSTM-ER model on essay-level relation extraction is plausible but could be better supported. While the O(m^2) complexity is a likely culprit, a more detailed error analysis showing the types of mistakes made (e.g., long-distance vs. short-distance errors, confusion between many candidates) would strengthen this claim.
3. The token-level dependency parsing framing feels somewhat brittle and potentially suboptimal. The negative results might be more of an indictment of this specific, fine-grained framing than of parsing-based approaches to AM in general.

**Questions to Authors:**
The STag model's label set appears to enforce a constraint that each argumentative component has at most one outgoing relation. How would this approach need to be modified to handle more complex, non-tree-like argumentation structures where a single premise might support multiple claims?
