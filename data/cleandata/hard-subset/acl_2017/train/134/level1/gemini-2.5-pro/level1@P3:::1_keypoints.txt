- Paper explores four neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining (token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging STag, multi-task tagging, and joint entity-relation LSTM-ER) and evaluates them on the persuasive essays dataset.  
- Key finding: token-level dependency parsing performs poorly; simple local sequence tagging (STag) is robust and effective; LSTM-ER is strong at component ID but weak at relation detection on long essays; neural models beat the ILP feature-baseline.  

Contributions:
- Systematic empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for AM, mapping the design space rather than proposing just one model.  
- Demonstrates that constrained local models (STag) can outperform more general or global models, highlighting the value of task-specific inductive bias.  
- Reports a strong negative result for token-level dependency parsing as encoded in this work, guiding future research away from this specific framing.

Strengths:
- Comprehensive and methodologically sound evaluation, including paragraph- vs essay-level analyses and comparison to a strong ILP baseline.  
- Insightful analysis of why models succeed or fail (e.g., overfitting/global constraints for parser, O(m^2) relation explosion for LSTM-ER).  
- Novel and creative problem formulations (e.g., the STag label encoding and the token-level parsing attempt).  
- Clear practical implications and actionable recommendations for future AM systems.

Weaknesses:
- STag label space is under-specified: unclear total label vocabulary, how distance d is handled, capped, or generalized at test time.  
- Conclusion that dependency parsing is ineffective is premature—based on one unconventional encoding; alternative tree representations might work better.  
- Paragraph vs essay treatment ignores natural hierarchical structure of essays; a hierarchical paragraph-then-essay model is not explored.  
- Ambiguity in the “joint vs decoupled” claim: results suggest modularity/auxiliary tasks help, but the paper does not clearly distinguish joint training from joint inference or optimal modularity levels.

Questions to authors:
- Please specify the STag label vocabulary size, whether distance d was capped, and how out-of-range/unseen distances were handled at test time.  
- Did you try heuristics to prune LSTM-ER’s O(m^2) relation candidates (e.g., max token/sentence distance) to improve essay-level relation detection?  
- Were absolute/relative positional features or positional embeddings used to help detect Major Claims (which often appear in intro/conclusion)?
