- Rigorous comparative analysis of multiple neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining, with insights on paragraph- vs essay-level performance.
- Novel STag sequence-tagging formulation that encodes BIO status, component type, relation distance, and relation type into a single token label.
- Demonstration that feature-free neural models can outperform a strong ILP-based, feature-engineered baseline on the benchmark dataset.
- Thorough experimental design comparing dependency parsers, sequence taggers, MTL, and LSTM-ER, evaluated at both paragraph and essay levels.
- Clear, convincing analysis of why specific models succeed or fail (e.g., LSTM-ER’s combinatorial negative pairs vs. STag’s implicit locality).
- STag’s distance-encoding is a creative, effective way to capture non-local relations and yields strong essay-level results (STagBLCC).
- Dependency parsing formulation is under-described and contains arbitrary head-selection choices (e.g., doc-final head, first-token heads) that need justification.
- STag creates a large, potentially sparse label space (distance range) without discussion of sparsity mitigation or generalization to rare/unseen distances.
- MTL experiments are inconclusive: improvements are modest, run on a weaker base tagger, and contradict prior layer-sharing findings without sufficient analysis.
- Question: Please justify the specific head-selection rules for the dependency parsing formulation and whether alternatives were tried.
- Question: How does STag handle rare or unseen relation distances at test time, and did you consider distance bucketing to reduce sparsity?
- Question: Could LSTM-ER be improved by pruning candidate relations (e.g., by sentence/component distance) to create a fairer comparison with STag’s locality bias?
