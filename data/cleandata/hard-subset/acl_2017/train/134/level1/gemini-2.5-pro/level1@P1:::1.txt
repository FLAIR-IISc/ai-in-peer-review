**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural, end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors' goal is to move beyond traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for imposing global constraints. They systematically explore four different neural framings of the end-to-end AM task: (1) as token-level dependency parsing, (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem, (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem, and (4) using a state-of-the-art joint entity and relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). The experiments are conducted on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). The key findings are that while dependency parsing is not effective, a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger performs robustly, outperforming a strong ILP baseline. The paper also shows that performance is highly dependent on document length, with different models excelling at paragraph-level versus essay-level prediction.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A rigorous comparative analysis of different neural framings for end-to-end AM.** This is the primary contribution. The paper doesn't just propose a single new model but instead provides a valuable service to the community by systematically investigating how different architectural assumptions (global parsing vs. local tagging, constrained vs. unconstrained relation prediction) impact performance on this complex structured prediction task. The analysis of performance differences between paragraph- and essay-level inputs provides crucial insights into model scalability and robustness.
2.  **A novel and effective formulation of joint component and relation extraction as a sequence tagging problem (STag).** The proposed tagging scheme, which encodes BIO status, component type, relation distance, and relation type into a single token-level label, is a clever and powerful way to collapse a complex graph-prediction problem into a format solvable by standard sequence labeling models. This is a significant methodological contribution.
3.  **Demonstration that feature-free neural models can achieve new state-of-the-art results.** The paper shows that carefully chosen neural architectures can outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP-based system on a standard benchmark. This provides compelling evidence for the viability of end-to-end neural learning for argumentation mining, reducing the need for domain-specific feature engineering and complex constraint design.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Design:** The paper's greatest strength is its comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors compare multiple classes of models, from different types of dependency parsers (feature-based and neural) to various sequence taggers and a sophisticated joint model (LSTM-ER). The direct comparison against the previous state-of-the-art ILP model on the same dataset provides a clear and convincing benchmark. The decision to evaluate on both paragraph and full essay levels is particularly insightful, as it reveals critical differences in how models handle short vs. long contexts and sparse vs. dense relation spaces.
2.  **Clear and Well-Motivated Problem Analysis:** The paper provides excellent analysis of *why* certain models succeed or fail. The hypothesis for the LSTM-Parser's relative success (encoding the whole stack history) is plausible. More importantly, the explanation for LSTM-ER's performance degradation on the essay level—that its unconstrained, pairwise relation classifier suffers from a combinatorial explosion of negative candidates in longer texts—is extremely convincing and well-supported by the data in Figure 4. This contrasts nicely with their own STag model, which implicitly constrains the search space. This level of analysis elevates the paper from a simple leaderboard report to a valuable scientific contribution.
3.  **Novelty and Efficacy of the STag Formulation:** The proposed STag model is an elegant solution to the joint task. By encoding the relative distance to the relation head directly into the tag, the authors create a local tagging model that can capture non-local structural information. This is a creative way to leverage the power of LSTMs to handle long-range dependencies within a comparatively simple framework. The strong performance of STagBLCC, especially on the challenging essay-level task, validates this approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Ambiguity in the Dependency Parsing Formulation:** While framing AM as dependency parsing is an interesting idea, its implementation is not described with sufficient clarity or motivation. Figure 3 illustrates the output structure, but the underlying linguistic or structural justification for the specific head-attachment rules is missing. For example, the decision to make the document-final token the head of all non-argumentative words seems arbitrary and could introduce a strange bias. Similarly, choosing the *first* token of a component as its head is a design choice that is presented without justification. Because the dependency parsing models performed poorly, a more detailed discussion of these choices and their potential impact is necessary to make the negative result fully convincing.
2.  **Lack of Discussion on Label Sparsity and Complexity:** The STag approach creates a very large and potentially sparse label set. The label is a 4-tuple, and the distance component `d` alone ranges from -11 to +10, creating a large number of unique labels. Many of these complex labels will appear only a few times in the training data. The paper does not address the challenges this poses, such as data sparsity for rare `(type, distance, stance)` combinations or how the model generalizes to unseen distances at test time. A brief discussion of the label space size and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., distance binning) would strengthen the paper.
3.  **The Multi-Task Learning (MTL) experiments feel somewhat inconclusive.** While the MTL results show a positive effect, the improvements are modest. Furthermore, the experiments are conducted with a weaker base tagger (STagBL) than the best-performing single-task model (STagBLCC). This makes it difficult to assess the true potential of MTL. Would adding these auxiliary tasks also improve the stronger BiLSTM-CRF-CNN model? The paper also notes that its findings on layer-sharing contradict previous work (Søgaard and Goldberg, 2016) but offers little analysis as to why this might be the case for the AM task. This section feels less developed than the others.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for the specific head-selection rules used in your dependency parsing formulation? For example, have you experimented with alternative attachment points for non-argumentative tokens or different head-selection rules within components (e.g., a semantic head)?
2.  Your `STag` model's performance on relations depends on predicting the correct distance `d`. How does the model handle relations at test time that have a distance unseen (or very rare) in the training data? Did you consider bucketing large distances to reduce label sparsity?
3.  The analysis of LSTM-ER's failure on essays is very insightful. Do you think its performance could be improved by adding simple heuristics to prune the candidate relation space, for instance, by only considering pairs of components within a certain sentence or component distance? This would seem to create a fairer comparison with your `STag` model, which has a strong locality bias implicitly built into its design.
