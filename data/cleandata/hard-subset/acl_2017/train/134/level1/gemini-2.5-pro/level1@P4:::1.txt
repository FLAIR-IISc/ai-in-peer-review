### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural architectures for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to move from unstructured text to a full argumentation graph (components and relations) in a single step, avoiding traditional pipeline approaches that rely on hand-crafted features and are prone to error propagation. The authors investigate four distinct framings of the problem: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing task; (2) as a sequence tagging task where labels encode component type, boundaries, and relational information (distance to parent); (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of sequence tagging; and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) model. The main findings are that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for this task, while simpler sequence tagging models are robust and outperform the ILP baseline, with an MTL setup providing further gains.

The main contributions of this work are assessed as follows:
1.  A thorough comparative analysis of different neural framings for end-to-end AM. The paper provides valuable insights by systematically evaluating the trade-offs between global, structured models (dependency parsing) and more local, flexible models (sequence tagging) for this complex structured prediction task. The finding that simpler models are more effective, particularly on longer documents, is a significant result.
2.  The proposal of a novel sequence tagging scheme that encodes relational structure (specifically, the distance to a related component) directly into a flat label set. This is a creative method for solving a joint entity and relation extraction task using a standard tagging architecture, and it proves to be highly effective on the target dataset.
3.  A demonstration that feature-free neural models can achieve new state-of-the-art results on this task, outperforming a complex, feature-engineered ILP-based system. This underscores the power of representation learning for AM and reduces the need for domain-specific feature engineering and constraint design.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The primary strength of this paper is its comprehensive experimental design. Rather than proposing a single new architecture, the authors conduct a systematic exploration of multiple, conceptually different ways to frame the AM task for neural networks. The comparison between dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and a state-of-the-art joint model (LSTM-ER) on the same data is highly informative. The further breakdown of results on paragraph-level versus essay-level inputs provides a nuanced understanding of how model complexity interacts with input length, which strengthens the conclusions.
2.  **Novelty in Problem Formulation:** The paper introduces inventive ways to model the AM task. While the token-level dependency parsing approach was ultimately not successful, it represents a non-trivial and interesting hypothesis to test. The sequence tagging formulation, which collapses component and relation information into a single complex label `(b, t, d, s)`, is a particularly novel contribution. This method of encoding structural dependencies in a flat tagging scheme is clever and proves to be robust.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The authors provide a clear and well-reasoned analysis of their results. They offer plausible explanations for the performance differences observed, such as attributing the failure of dependency parsers on long texts to the vast search space of possible trees, and the failure of LSTM-ER on relations to the quadratic number of candidate pairs it must consider. The analysis of how relation accuracy degrades with distance (Figure 4) provides concrete evidence for the different behaviors of the STagBLCC and LSTM-ER models. This level of analysis elevates the paper from a simple report of results to a valuable guide for future work in this area.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The different model framings are explained with sufficient detail and helpful illustrations (e.g., Figures 1 and 3). The experimental setup and evaluation metrics are clearly defined, contributing to the work's potential for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Generality of Findings due to Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (persuasive essays) which has a highly constrained, near-tree-like argumentation structure (e.g., each premise links to exactly one other component). The success of the proposed sequence tagging model, which encodes a single parent distance `d`, is likely highly dependent on this structural property. The paper does not discuss how this approach would generalize to datasets with more complex, graph-like structures where components may have multiple parents or relations. This makes it difficult to assess whether the conclusions hold for AM in general or are specific to this dataset.
2.  **Potential Scalability Issue of the Sequence Tagging Scheme:** The proposed sequence tagging label `(b, t, d, s)` has a vocabulary size that grows with the maximum observed component distance `|d|`. While this distance is bounded in the PE dataset, this approach may not scale to other domains or genres where argumentative relations can span much larger sections of text. A very large label set could make the model's output layer unwieldy and the model itself difficult to train effectively. This potential limitation of an otherwise elegant approach is not addressed.
3.  **Analysis of Multi-Task Learning is Preliminary:** The MTL experiments are a valuable addition, but the analysis feels underdeveloped compared to the rest of the paper. The performance gains reported in Table 2 are relatively modest, and the discussion on the optimal layer for each auxiliary task is brief. The conclusion that component detection ("C") is a more helpful auxiliary task than relation detection ("R") is interesting but would benefit from a more thorough analysis or intuition as to why this might be the case.
4.  **Ambiguity in the Use of "End-to-End":** The paper uses the term "end-to-end" to describe its approach. However, the analysis suggests that the most successful models (LSTM-ER on paragraphs, and the MTL setup) benefit from a degree of modularity or de-coupling between the component and relation identification tasks. The STag formulation represents a more monolithic end-to-end approach, while LSTM-ER is better described as a joint model with distinct modules. A more precise discussion of this distinction and where each model falls on the spectrum from monolithic to modular would clarify the paper's conceptual claims.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The sequence tagging model relies on encoding a single distance `d` for each component, which is well-suited for the PE dataset where premises have one parent. How would you adapt this `STag` framing to handle argumentation structures where a single component can support or attack multiple other components, which would necessitate encoding multiple relations and distances?
2.  The analysis suggests that LSTM-ER's performance degrades on relation identification for longer documents due to the quadratic complexity of evaluating all pairs of identified components. Did you consider applying a simple heuristic or pruning strategy (e.g., only considering component pairs within a maximum token or component distance) to make the LSTM-ER baseline more competitive on the essay-level task?
3.  The token-level dependency parsing framing performed poorly, which you attribute to the global nature of the model and the length of the sequences. Do you believe this framing could be more successful if applied at the component level (using pre-identified component spans) rather than the token level, thereby reducing sequence length and simplifying the task to relation prediction? This would help clarify whether the "parsing as AM" approach is fundamentally flawed or if the token-level application was the primary issue.
