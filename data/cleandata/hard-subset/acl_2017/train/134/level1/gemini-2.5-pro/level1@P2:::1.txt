**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper presents a thorough empirical investigation into neural end-to-end models for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM). The work frames the complex task of identifying argument components and their relationships in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as a complex sequence tagging task, as a multi-task learning problem, and by adapting a general-purpose joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). The experiments are conducted on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing the neural approaches against each other and a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The key findings are that simple but powerful sequence tagging models outperform more complex, globally constrained models like dependency parsers, and that these neural approaches can achieve new state-of-the-art results without manual feature engineering.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A comprehensive benchmark and new state-of-the-art for neural end-to-end AM:** This is the first work to systematically compare a range of neural architectures for the full end-to-end AM task. It demonstrates that neural models, particularly BiLSTM-based taggers, significantly outperform a strong feature-based ILP system, establishing a new and compelling direction for research in this area.
2.  **An insightful negative result on the viability of dependency parsing for AM:** The paper clearly shows that framing AM as token-level dependency parsing, while theoretically appealing, is empirically ineffective. This is a valuable finding that will help guide the community away from potentially unfruitful research avenues.
3.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for structured prediction:** The authors propose an elegant method to linearize the argument structure prediction problem by encoding component boundaries, types, and relational information (distance and type) into a single, complex tag. This formulation proves to be robust, particularly for handling relations in long documents where other models falter.

**Strengths**

1.  **Systematic and Rigorous Investigation:** The paper’s greatest strength is its thorough and methodical comparison of multiple, well-motivated neural framings for the end-to-end AM task. Rather than proposing a single model, the authors explore a diverse set of approaches, from parsing to sequence tagging, and provide a clear comparison of their relative merits. This comprehensive study provides a much-needed map of the neural solution space for this challenging problem.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Clear SOTA Improvement:** The proposed models achieve excellent results, significantly outperforming the published feature-based ILP baseline. For instance, on the paragraph-level task, the LSTM-ER model improves the overall F1 score (50% match) from 55.23% to 60.72%. This demonstrates the power of end-to-end neural learning and moves the state-of-the-art in AM forward substantially, all while obviating the need for painstaking feature engineering.
3.  **Valuable Analysis and Actionable Insights:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting scores and offers insightful analysis into why certain models perform better than others. The explanation for the performance drop of the LSTM-ER model on essay-level relation detection (due to the quadratic explosion of candidate entity pairs) versus the more constrained sequence tagging model is particularly compelling. This analysis provides clear, actionable takeaways for researchers designing models for similar structured prediction tasks on long documents.
4.  **Novelty in Problem Formulation:** The proposed sequence tagging scheme, which encodes structural relations via a component-based distance metric `d` in the tag label, is a novel and clever contribution. This method effectively transforms a graph prediction problem into a sequential one that can be readily handled by powerful models like BiLSTMs, while implicitly encoding a strong inductive bias that related components are often textually proximate.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Generality of the Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The proposed STagT formulation implicitly assumes that each premise has exactly one outgoing link, which aligns with the tree-like structure of the PE dataset. However, this is a strong constraint that does not hold for many other argumentation corpora or theories, which allow for more complex graph structures like linked support (multiple premises for one claim) or serial arguments where one component links to multiple others. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss the challenges of applying this otherwise effective model to more general argumentation structures.
2.  **Ambiguity in the "Distance" Encoding:** The definition of the distance `d` as the "number of components" between a source and its target is underspecified. It is not clear how this count is performed. Is it based on the linear order of component start tokens in the text? If so, an example illustrating the calculation in a complex sentence with interleaved components would greatly improve clarity. This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully understand the mechanics of the model and to reproduce the tagging scheme.
3.  **Potential Scalability Issues of the Tag Set:** The size of the tag set in the STagT model grows linearly with the maximum distance `d` observed in the data. While distances are relatively small in the PE corpus, other domains might feature much longer-range dependencies. This could lead to an extremely large and sparse label space, potentially making the model difficult to train. The authors should discuss the practical limits of this approach and clarify how they handled the range of `d` in their experiments (e.g., was it clipped at a certain value?).
4.  **Lack of Deeper Error Analysis:** The analysis is strong at the model level but could be improved with a more detailed, qualitative error analysis of the best-performing models. For example, what are the most common error types made by the STagBLCC model? Does it confuse component boundaries, misclassify component types (e.g., Claim vs. Premise), or fail on specific relation types (Attack vs. Support) or distances? Such an analysis would provide a clearer roadmap for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more precise definition of how the component distance `d` is calculated? Specifically, is it based on the linear ordering of components in the text, and how does the model handle prediction of a relation to a component that appears later in the sequence (i.e., a positive distance)?
2.  The STagT model is well-suited for the tree-like structures in the PE dataset. How might you adapt this formulation for datasets that permit non-tree structures, such as a single premise supporting multiple claims, or multiple premises jointly supporting a single claim?
3.  Regarding the label space for the STagT model: did you cap the maximum value for the distance `d` during experiments? If so, what was the cap, and how did the model handle true relations that exceeded this distance?
