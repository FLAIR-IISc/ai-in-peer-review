### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical investigation into applying neural, end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). Moving away from traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for global constraints, the authors explore four distinct neural framings:
1.  **Token-level Dependency Parsing:** Modeling the argument structure as a dependency tree where each token has a head and a labeled relation.
2.  **Sequence Tagging (STag):** Casting the entire problem as a sequence tagging task using a complex BIO-style label that encodes component type, relation distance, and relation type simultaneously for each token.
3.  **Multi-Task Learning (MTL):** Using the sequence tagging framework but adding simpler, auxiliary tasks (like component identification) to be learned jointly to improve the main task performance.
4.  **Joint Entity-Relation Extraction:** Applying the existing model of Miwa and Bansal (2016), which uses a BiLSTM-CRF for entity detection and a TreeLSTM for relation classification.

The models are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. The key findings are that token-level dependency parsing performs poorly, while the simpler, local sequence tagging models are surprisingly robust and effective. The model by Miwa and Bansal (LSTM-ER) excels at component identification but struggles with relation detection on long documents (essays). Overall, the proposed neural models outperform the feature-based ILP state-of-the-art on this dataset.

### Contributions
1.  **A Systematic Empirical Comparison of Neural Framings for End-to-End AM:** This is the primary contribution. The paper does not just propose a single new model but provides a valuable comparative analysis of several plausible ways to frame the complex, structured task of AM for modern neural architectures. This exploration of the design space is a significant service to the community.
2.  **Demonstration of the Effectiveness of Constrained Local Models:** The paper shows that a relatively simple sequence tagging model (STag), which imposes strong local constraints (e.g., each component links to exactly one other component), can outperform more general (LSTM-ER) and more global (dependency parsing) models, especially on long documents. This provides a key insight into the trade-off between model complexity and task-specific inductive bias.
3.  **A Strong Negative Result for Token-Level Dependency Parsing:** The finding that framing AM as token-level dependency parsing leads to subpar performance is an important contribution. While dependency parsing seems like a natural fit for tree-like argument structures, this work shows its practical limitations in a noisy, end-to-end setting, guiding future research away from this specific approach.

### Strengths
1.  **Comprehensive and Methodologically Sound Evaluation:** The authors thoroughly evaluate four distinct classes of models, including multiple variants within the dependency parsing and sequence tagging paradigms. The comparison against a strong, contemporary feature-based ILP system (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) on the same data establishes the significance of the results. The separate evaluation on paragraph-level and essay-level data provides crucial insights into how these models handle sequences of varying lengths and complexity.
2.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Failures and Successes:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting F1 scores. The analysis of *why* certain models perform better than others is a major strength. For instance, the hypothesis that the LSTM-Parser's global tree constraint overfits on long sequences is convincing. The analysis of LSTM-ER's performance degradation on essays due to the quadratic explosion of candidate relations is particularly insightful and well-supported by the data in Figure 4, which clearly contrasts its weakness on long-distance relations with STag's robustness.
3.  **Novelty in Problem Formulation:** While the underlying neural components (BiLSTMs, CRFs) are standard, the formulations of the AM task are novel and creative. The STag label, `(b, t, d, s)`, which encodes component boundaries, type, relational distance, and stance into a single tag, is a clever way to force a standard sequence tagger to solve a structured prediction problem. Similarly, the token-level dependency parsing framing is an original, albeit ultimately unsuccessful, attempt to tackle the problem globally.
4.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The paper concludes with clear "policy recommendations" based on the findings: BiLSTM taggers are excellent for component detection, component and relation detection should be modeled jointly but perhaps de-coupled, and more constrained models are preferable for relation detection in long documents. These are actionable takeaways for future work in this area.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Detail on the STag Label Space:** The complex label `(b, t, d, s)` is central to the most successful proprietary model, yet its practical implementation is under-specified. The set of possible distances `d` could make the label space enormous. The paper does not clarify how this is handled. Is there a maximum distance threshold? What happens for unseen distances at test time? Does the model generalize to distances not seen in training, or does it treat them as distinct, unrelated class labels? This missing detail hinders the reproducibility and full understanding of the model's mechanics.
2.  **Potentially Premature Dismissal of Dependency Parsing:** The paper concludes that dependency parsing is "ineffective". However, this conclusion is based on a single, specific way of encoding the problem as a dependency tree (Figure 3). The chosen structure, where all non-argumentative tokens point to an `END` token, is unconventional and may not be optimal. It is possible that an alternative tree representation (e.g., using a virtual ROOT, chaining non-argumentative tokens, etc.) could yield better results. The conclusion should be softened to reflect that this *specific framing* of parsing was ineffective, rather than parsing as a general approach.
3.  **The Paragraph vs. Essay Dichotomy:** The analysis highlights the performance gap between paragraph-level and essay-level processing, but it treats them as two independent settings. An essay has an inherent structure of paragraphs. This structure is ignored by the models, which either process paragraphs in isolation or the entire essay as a flat sequence of tokens. A promising direction, which is not discussed, would be a hierarchical model that first identifies components and relations within paragraphs and then resolves inter-paragraph relations (especially for claims linking to major claims). This seems like a natural extension that is overlooked.
4.  **Ambiguity in the "Joint vs. Decoupled" Argument:** The paper critiques pipeline approaches but finds that the LSTM-ER model, which decouples entity and relation prediction into separate modules (though trained jointly), performs best on component identification. Furthermore, the MTL experiments show that adding auxiliary tasks for sub-problems (a form of decoupling) improves performance. This suggests that a fully monolithic prediction (as in STag) might not be strictly optimal. The paper's conclusion could be sharpened to better delineate between joint *training* and joint *inference*, and to discuss what level of modularity within a joint model appears to be most effective.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the STag model: Could you please elaborate on the practical implementation of the label set? What was the total size of the label vocabulary? Was the distance `d` capped at a maximum value, and how were out-of-range or unseen distances handled during testing?
2.  Your analysis suggests that LSTM-ER's R-F1 score suffers on the essay level due to the O(m^2) complexity of considering all pairs of `m` components. Did you consider or experiment with any simple heuristics to prune this search space, such as imposing a maximum sentence or token distance between candidate components? It seems this might be a way to combine the strengths of LSTM-ER's component tagger with a more constrained relation finder.
3.  Given that Major Claims often appear in introductory or concluding paragraphs, did any of your models learn to use absolute or relative positional features? For the neural models, were positional embeddings used? This seems like a simple feature that could significantly help in the essay-level setting, which is currently not discussed.
