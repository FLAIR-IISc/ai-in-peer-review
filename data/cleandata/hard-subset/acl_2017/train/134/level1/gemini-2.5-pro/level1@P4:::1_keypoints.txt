- Empirical study comparing four neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining (token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL sequence tagging, and LSTM-ER) on the PE dataset.  
- Primary finding: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, while sequence tagging models outperform a strong feature-based ILP baseline.  
- Novel sequence tagging scheme encodes component type, boundaries, and parent distance into a flat label set (b,t,d,s), enabling joint entity-relation extraction with a standard tagger.  
- MTL extension of sequence tagging yields further gains over single-task tagging.  
- Feature-free neural models achieve new state-of-the-art results, reducing reliance on hand-crafted features and constraints.  
- Strong and rigorous empirical evaluation comparing conceptually different model framings and analyzing paragraph vs. essay length effects.  
- Creative problem formulations, including the token-level parser hypothesis and the novel flat-tag encoding of relations.  
- Insightful analysis explaining model behaviors (e.g., parsing fails on long texts; LSTM-ER struggles with quadratic candidate pairs; relation accuracy degrades with distance).  
- Paper is clear, well-organized, and reproducible with helpful figures and detailed experimental setup.  
- Limited generality: experiments use a single PE dataset with near-tree structures, so results may not generalize to graph-like argumentation with multiple parents.  
- Scalability concern: the tagging label vocabulary grows with maximum relation distance |d|, which may not scale to domains with long-range relations.  
- MTL analysis is preliminary; reported gains are modest and the role of different auxiliary tasks (C vs. R) needs deeper exploration.  
- Ambiguity in the term "end-to-end": successful models show modularity, so clearer discussion of monolithic vs. modular approaches is needed.  
- Question: How would STag handle components with multiple parents or multiple relations (i.e., encode multiple distances/relations)?  
- Question: Did you try heuristics/pruning (e.g., max distance) to reduce LSTM-ER’s quadratic relation search on essays?  
- Question: Could dependency parsing work better at the component-span level (pre-identified components) rather than token-level, to reduce sequence length and simplify relation prediction?
