- Paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of end-to-end neural models for argumentation mining, framing the task as token-level dependency parsing and sequence tagging and comparing to LSTM-ER and an ILP baseline.
- Primary contribution: thorough comparative analysis of different neural framings, showing how model choices (local tagging vs. global parsing) perform, especially with varying text length.
- Secondary contribution: a sequence-tagging (STag) model that encodes component boundaries, types, distance, and stance into a single label, proving robust and scalable.
- Strength: strong experimental design systematically evaluates four neural paradigms on paragraph- and essay-level data, yielding clear, well-supported comparisons.
- Strength: the sequence-tagging formulation that encodes relational distance is innovative and lets a BiLSTM capture long-range dependencies without parsing or all-pairs complexity.
- Strength: achieves new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset, outperforming a strong feature-engineered ILP system and demonstrating end-to-end neural viability.
- Weakness: limited architectural novelty — core models (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-Parser, LSTM-ER) are established methods applied to a new domain.
- Weakness: attribution of LSTM-ER’s essay-level degradation to O(m^2) complexity is plausible but lacks detailed error analysis (e.g., long- vs short-distance errors, candidate confusion).
- Weakness: token-level dependency parsing framing may be brittle or suboptimal; negative results could reflect this specific fine-grained framing rather than parsing-based approaches in general.
- Question: STag appears to enforce at most one outgoing relation per component — how would it be modified to handle non-tree-like structures where a premise supports multiple claims?
