## Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

This paper investigates neural network approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging. The authors explore multi-task learning to further improve performance. A key finding is that dependency parsing performs poorly for this task, while simpler BiLSTM-based sequence tagging models are surprisingly robust.

The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, demonstrating the effectiveness of sequence tagging approaches. While end-to-end neural AM has been proposed, this paper provides a thorough investigation and comparison of several models, including a strong baseline using an existing ILP approach. The negative result regarding dependency parsing is also a significant contribution, challenging a previously suggested approach for AM. Finally, the demonstration that multi-task learning with related subtasks improves performance is a valuable finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a well-designed experimental setup, comparing multiple neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, LSTM-ER, and multi-task learning) against a strong feature-based baseline (ILP). The use of a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) allows for direct comparison with existing work.
2. **Counterintuitive Finding on Dependency Parsing:** The finding that dependency parsing performs poorly for end-to-end AM is a significant result. This challenges the intuition that the tree-like structure of arguments lends itself naturally to dependency parsing and suggests that simpler, local models may be more effective.
3. **Demonstration of Multi-Task Learning Benefits:** The authors show that incorporating auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) through multi-task learning improves performance, highlighting the potential for leveraging relationships between subtasks in AM.
4. **Thorough Analysis:** The paper provides detailed analysis of the results, discussing the strengths and weaknesses of each approach and offering plausible explanations for the observed performance differences. The discussion of the impact of sequence length on LSTM-ER is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of these techniques for end-to-end AM is valuable, the individual components (BiLSTM-CRF, dependency parsing) are well-established in the NLP literature. The novelty lies primarily in the application and comparative evaluation within the AM domain.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study for MTL:** While the authors demonstrate the benefits of multi-task learning, a more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of each auxiliary task would strengthen the findings. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand which auxiliary task contributes the most to performance gains.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While some error analysis is mentioned, a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the errors made by each model could provide valuable insights into their limitations and guide future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the dependency parsing model? Specifically, what kinds of relationships are most often misidentified or missed?
2.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, did you explore different weighting schemes for the loss functions of the auxiliary tasks? If so, what were the results?
3.  The paper mentions that LSTM-ER’s performance degrades significantly on longer sequences. Have you explored any techniques to mitigate this issue, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical models?
