- Study of neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) comparing framings like dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and combined sequential/tree models on persuasive essays.
- First neural end-to-end AM solutions removing manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.
- BiLSTM sequence taggers are highly effective for component detection and robust across environments.
- Joint modeling of components and relations is important, but naive coupling of tasks is suboptimal.
- Achieves new state-of-the-art end-to-end AM results on the PE dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016).
- Provides a comprehensive evaluation of multiple neural framings versus a feature-based ILP baseline.
- Offers insights into the difficulty of relation detection in long documents and the need for restrained modeling.
- Limited hyperparameter exploration for neural models, which may affect reported performance.
- No comparison to other neural architectures (e.g., encoder-decoder models) that might be suitable for AM.
- Results are reported on a single dataset, raising questions about cross-dataset and cross-domain generalization.
- Question: How will hyperparameter tuning be addressed for the neural models?
- Question: Can the authors explain why the LSTM-ER model struggles with relation identification in long documents?
- Question: How do the authors plan to extend and adapt their approach to other datasets or domains, and what challenges are expected?
