### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural network models for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to identify argumentative components (claims, premises) and the relations between them directly from raw text, without relying on handcrafted features or complex constraint-based systems like Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors explore and compare several distinct neural framings of the AM task on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. These include: (1) framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem, evaluated with several traditional and neural parsers; (2) framing it as a sequence tagging problem using a complex BIO-style tagging scheme that encodes component type, relational distance, and relation type into a single label; (3) extending the sequence tagging model to a multi-task learning (MTL) setup where sub-problems like component identification are learned as auxiliary tasks; and (4) applying an existing joint entity and relation extraction model (LSTM-ER by Miwa and Bansal, 2016).

The main findings are that framing the task as token-level dependency parsing performs poorly. In contrast, sequence tagging models, particularly those based on BiLSTM-CRF architectures, are highly effective. The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves the best performance on paragraph-level prediction, while the authors' proposed sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) performs best on the more challenging full-essay level. Both approaches significantly outperform the feature-based ILP state-of-the-art model, demonstrating the promise of end-to-end neural methods for this task. Furthermore, the multi-task learning experiments show that jointly training on simpler subtasks improves performance on the full, complex task.

### Main Contributions

1.  **First Comprehensive Evaluation of Neural End-to-End Argumentation Mining:** The primary contribution of this work is that it is the first to systematically investigate and compare multiple neural architectures for the full, end-to-end AM task. By demonstrating that feature-free neural models can outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP-based system, the paper marks a significant advancement for the field and establishes a new, strong baseline for future research.

2.  **Valuable Architectural Comparison and Analysis:** The paper provides an insightful comparison between framing AM as a global, structured prediction problem (dependency parsing) versus a more local one (sequence tagging). The finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, while simpler sequence tagging models excel, is a key result. The analysis provides plausible explanations for this phenomenon (e.g., the complexity of parsing very long sequences), which is a valuable lesson for future work on AM and other discourse-level tasks.

3.  **Demonstration of Effective Modeling Techniques for Complex Structured Prediction:** The paper successfully shows that complex, multi-faceted labels (encoding component type, span, relation target, and relation type) can be effectively modeled with modern sequence taggers. Furthermore, it demonstrates that a multi-task learning setup, which decomposes this complex label into "natural" subtasks, provides a clear performance benefit. This serves as a useful case study for applying MTL to similar NLP tasks with structured, multi-part outputs.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Systematic Investigation:** The authors do not merely propose a single model but instead conduct a broad and rigorous comparison across fundamentally different approaches. The inclusion of four dependency parsers, multiple sequence tagging architectures (STagBLCC, STagBL), a multi-task variant, and a state-of-the-art joint entity-relation model (LSTM-ER) makes the experimental setup very strong. This comparative approach provides a much clearer and more convincing picture of the problem landscape than a paper focused on a single architecture.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper achieves new state-of-the-art results on the standard PE dataset. The best-performing models (LSTM-ER on the paragraph level and STagBLCC on the essay level) show substantial improvements over the previous ILP-based method. For example, on the paragraph level, LSTM-ER achieves a global F1 of 55.42%/60.72% (exact/approximate), compared to the ILP's 44.68%/55.23%. This is a clear and impactful empirical contribution.

3.  **Insightful Analysis and Negative Results:** The paper's conclusion that token-level dependency parsing is not well-suited for this task is an important and well-supported "negative" result. The analysis explaining the performance differences between models is also a major strength. For instance, the hypothesis that LSTM-ER's performance degrades on the essay level due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations in longer texts, whereas the STagT model's constrained labeling scheme is more robust, is insightful and provides a clear direction for future model design.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The paper provides a clear description of the models and experimental setup, and the mention of a comprehensive supplementary material detailing hyperparameters and software is commendable. This commitment to transparency and reproducibility increases the value and impact of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Best-Performing Model:** One of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of the model from Miwa and Bansal (2016) with minimal adaptation. While its application to AM is new and the analysis is valuable, the core technical contribution in this part of the paper is limited. The paper's strength lies more in its comprehensive evaluation and analysis than in proposing a novel neural architecture that fundamentally outperforms existing ones.

2.  **Potential for Data Leakage in Evaluation:** The paper uses the standard train/test split of the PE dataset. However, it is not clear whether the controversial topics of the essays (e.g., "competition or cooperation") are disjoint between the training and test sets. If essays on the same topic appear in both splits, the models might learn topic-specific lexical cues rather than generalizable argumentative patterns, which could lead to an overestimation of the models' true performance on unseen topics.

3.  **Clarity of Model Descriptions and Constraints:** At times, the descriptions could be clearer. For instance, the paper claims LSTM-ER "de-couples relation information from entity information" but models them "jointly," which is somewhat confusing terminology. A more precise explanation of how its architecture differs from the MTL setup, which also separates these tasks to some degree, would be beneficial. Similarly, the process of converting the argument graph into a single-head tree for the dependency parsers and then reconstructing the graph from the parser's output is not fully detailed, particularly for the case where claims relate to multiple major claims.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the train/test split protocol for the PE dataset? Specifically, are the essay topics disjoint between the training and test sets? This is crucial for understanding whether the models are learning general argumentation structures or topic-specific artifacts.

2.  The evaluation is performed on both paragraph and essay levels. Are the reported F1 scores directly comparable? The paragraph-level evaluation seems inherently easier as it restricts the search space for relations. Does the paragraph-level model setup preclude the identification of inter-paragraph relations, and if so, what percentage of relations in the gold data does this miss?

3.  The paper states that the argument graph can be "loss-lessly" converted to a tree for the dependency parsing framework by re-linking claims to a single major claim. Could you please elaborate on the reverse process? How is the full argumentative graph, including the one-to-many links from a claim to all major claims, reconstructed from the single-head-per-token tree structure predicted by the parser?

4.  The description of LSTM-ER states it "de-couples entity and relation detection but jointly model[s] them." Could you please clarify this distinction? How does this "de-coupling" in a joint model differ conceptually from the multi-task learning framework (e.g., Y-3:YC-1:YR-2) where component and relation tasks are also trained jointly but as separate prediction heads?
