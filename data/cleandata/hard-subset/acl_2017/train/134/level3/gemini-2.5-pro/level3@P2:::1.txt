### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the AM task in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a sequence tagging problem, and a multi-task learning problem. The core motivation is to move beyond traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for joint inference, thereby avoiding manual feature engineering and error propagation. The study compares multiple neural architectures, including various dependency parsers and BiLSTM-based sequence taggers, against a strong feature-based ILP baseline on the persuasive essays dataset. The key findings are that token-based dependency parsing is ill-suited for this task, while a simpler sequence tagging model that encodes relational distance in its labels performs robustly and achieves state-of-the-art results.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A pioneering and thorough empirical investigation of end-to-end neural models for argumentation mining.** This is the first work to systematically compare different neural paradigms (parsing, tagging, multi-task learning) for the full AM task, providing a valuable benchmark and analysis for future research.
2.  **An insightful finding that simpler, local sequence tagging models outperform more complex, global dependency parsing models for this task.** The paper convincingly demonstrates the limitations of framing token-level AM as dependency parsing and highlights the effectiveness of a BiLSTM-based tagging approach, which offers a strong and practical solution.
3.  **A demonstration that multi-task learning (MTL) improves performance by jointly training on the full AM task and its natural subtasks.** This shows that even within a joint model, explicitly guiding the network to learn component and relation identification as auxiliary tasks is beneficial.

### Strengths

1.  **Clarity and High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The motivation for moving to end-to-end neural models is clearly articulated. The different framings of the AM problem are explained intuitively, with helpful examples and figures (e.g., Figure 3). This clarity makes the paper's novel approaches and complex experimental results highly accessible.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a rigorous and extensive set of experiments. They compare four different dependency parsers (both feature-based and neural), several advanced sequence tagging models (STagBLCC, MTL), and a state-of-the-art joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). Crucially, all models are compared against a strong, feature-based ILP system from prior work. The evaluation on both paragraph and essay levels provides further insights into the models' behavior with varying sequence lengths and complexity.

3.  **Novel and Impactful Findings:** The paper offers several valuable insights for the AM community. The negative result for the dependency parsing approach is particularly important, as it challenges the intuitive assumption that tree structures are a natural fit for modeling argumentation. The success of the STagBLCC model, which cleverly encodes relation distance into the label set, provides a strong and effective baseline for future end-to-end AM research. The analysis comparing LSTM-ER and STagBLCC (Figure 4) provides a convincing explanation for why a more constrained model excels on longer documents.

### Weaknesses

1.  **High Risk of Overfitting on a Small Dataset:** My main concern is the use of large-capacity neural models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) on the very small PE dataset, which contains only 402 essays (presumably around 320 for training). Such models have millions of parameters and are prone to overfitting on limited data. The paper provides no analysis to allay this concern, such as plots of training and development set loss/F1-score over epochs. Without this, it is hard to be confident that the models have learned generalizable representations of argumentation rather than simply memorizing the training set.

2.  **Lack of Robustness Analysis:** The results appear to be based on single experimental runs. Neural network performance can be highly sensitive to weight initialization and other sources of randomness. The authors themselves note that the LSTM-Parser's performance "varied drastically between different parametrizations." This instability may affect other models as well. To ensure the reliability and reproducibility of the findings, the mean and standard deviation of key metrics should be reported over multiple runs with different random seeds. While a significance test is provided, it does not address the core issue of model stability.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on Regularization:** The paper omits a discussion of the regularization techniques used to combat the aforementioned overfitting risk. Methods like dropout, L2 regularization, and early stopping are standard and essential when training deep models on small datasets. The main text should explicitly state which techniques were used for each model, along with the corresponding hyperparameter values (e.g., dropout rate). Deferring this information to supplementary material (and only for one model) is not sufficient for such a critical aspect of the methodology.

4.  **Missing Context for Evaluation:** The performance of any system is best understood in the context of the task's difficulty. The paper would benefit from reporting the inter-annotator agreement (IAA) scores from the original dataset paper (Stab and Gurevych, 2016). This would provide a soft upper bound and help readers contextualize the reported F1-scores.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning?
2.  Given the small training set size, could you provide evidence that the models are not overfitting? For instance, by showing learning curves for the training and development sets for your best-performing models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER).
3.  To address concerns about the stability and reliability of the results, could you please report the mean and standard deviation of the F1 scores for your main models over at least 3-5 runs with different random seeds?
4.  Could you please elaborate on the specific regularization techniques and their associated hyperparameters (e.g., dropout rates, L2 penalty) used for the STagBLCC and MTL models in the main paper?
