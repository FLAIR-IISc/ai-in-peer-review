- Paper presents the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end models for argumentation mining, comparing token-level sequence tagging and dependency parsing to identify components and relations.

- Contribution: thorough empirical comparison of framing AM as sequence tagging vs dependency parsing, providing guidance on suitable architectures.

- Contribution: finding that simpler, local BiLSTM-based sequence tagging models outperform global dependency parsing models.

- Contribution: showing multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary subtasks improves performance and can beat a strong ILP feature-based baseline.

- Strength: paper is very clear and well-motivated, with accessible explanations for each proposed framing.

- Strength: comprehensive, rigorous experiments comparing multiple neural and feature-based baselines, including paragraph vs essay analyses.

- Strength: delivers actionable findings (e.g., parsing framing fails, trade-offs between models) that guide future AM research.

- Weakness: very small dataset (~402 essays; ~320 train) risks overfitting high-capacity models, with no learning curves to show generalization.

- Weakness: no stability analysis—results appear from a single run without mean/std over multiple random initializations.

- Weakness: insufficient detail on regularization in the main text (dropout, weight decay), limiting reproducibility and assessment.

- Question: provide learning curves and state development set size to show models did not overfit on the small dataset.

- Question: report mean and standard deviation of main F1 scores over multiple runs to demonstrate result stability and significance.

- Question: detail specific regularization techniques and hyperparameter selection for all neural models, and report inter-annotator agreement from the original dataset for context.
