**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper presents the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors' primary motivation is to move beyond traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for constraint satisfaction, thereby avoiding manual engineering effort and error propagation. The work explores several neural framings for the end-to-end task, which includes identifying argument components (claims, premises), their types, and the relations between them.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A thorough empirical comparison of different neural architectures for end-to-end AM. The paper systematically investigates framing the problem as a token-level sequence tagging task versus a token-level dependency parsing task. This comparative study provides valuable insights into what architectural choices are suitable for this complex structured prediction problem.
2.  Demonstrating that simpler, local sequence tagging models (specifically BiLSTM-based variants) are more effective and robust for this task than global, more complex dependency parsing models, which perform poorly.
3.  Showing that a multi-task learning (MTL) setup, where the main sequence tagging task is jointly trained with auxiliary subtasks like component identification, can further improve performance. The best-performing neural models are shown to outperform a strong, feature-based ILP baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Clarity and Strong Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for pursuing end-to-end neural models is clearly articulated, highlighting the well-known drawbacks of feature engineering, pipeline architectures, and complex constraint design in traditional AM systems. The authors do a great job of explaining the intuition behind each of their proposed framings (sequence tagging, dependency parsing, MTL), making the work highly accessible.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a substantial set of experiments, evaluating a wide range of models. They compare two feature-based and two neural dependency parsers, a state-of-the-art sequence tagger (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), an MTL variant, and a sophisticated joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). The inclusion of a strong ILP-based system from prior work as a baseline provides a solid point of comparison and strengthens the claim that neural methods are a viable and superior alternative. The analysis of performance on both paragraph and essay levels is also insightful.

3.  **Interesting and Actionable Findings:** The results presented are not only strong but also offer valuable lessons for the field. The clear failure of the dependency parsing framing is an important negative result. The analysis revealing the trade-offs of different models is particularly compelling—for instance, how the LSTM-ER model excels at component identification but struggles with relation identification on longer texts (essays), while the authors' more constrained sequence tagging model is more robust. These findings provide practical guidance for future research in AM and related structured prediction tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Dataset Size and Risk of Overfitting:** My primary concern is the very small dataset used for evaluation (402 essays total, implying ~320 for training and 80 for testing). High-capacity models like BiLSTMs with CNNs and CRFs are known to require substantial amounts of data to train effectively and avoid overfitting. The paper presents results on this small dataset without a thorough analysis of this potential issue. For instance, there are no learning curves (training vs. development loss/F1) provided to demonstrate that the models are not simply memorizing the training set. The generalizability of the findings to other datasets or domains is therefore questionable.

2.  **Lack of Stability Analysis:** The paper reports performance metrics from what appears to be a single training run for each model. Given the small dataset and the stochastic nature of neural network training (e.g., random weight initialization), results can vary significantly across different runs. Standard practice requires reporting the mean and standard deviation of key metrics over multiple runs to demonstrate the stability and statistical significance of the results. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the reported performance differences between models are consistent or due to chance.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on Regularization:** Directly related to the risk of overfitting, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the regularization techniques employed. Regularization (e.g., dropout, L2 weight decay) is critical when training large models on limited data. The main text omits this entirely, and the authors state that such details are in the supplementary material. However, this information is crucial for reproducibility and for assessing the validity of the training procedure, and it should be clearly described in the main paper for all neural models tested.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the small size of the PE dataset (~320 training essays), how did you ensure that the high-capacity neural models (e.g., STagBLCC) were not overfitting? Could you please provide learning curves showing training and development set performance over epochs? What was the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning?

2.  The reported results seem to be from a single experimental run. Could you report the mean and standard deviation of the main F1 scores (Component and Relation, at 100% and 50% match) over multiple runs with different random initializations? This is crucial for assessing the stability of the models and the statistical significance of the performance gains.

3.  Could you elaborate on the specific regularization techniques (e.g., dropout rates, weight decay parameters) used for all the neural models (STagT, parsers, etc.), not just the LSTM-ER model mentioned in the supplement? How were these hyperparameters selected?

4.  To help contextualize the model performance, could you state the inter-annotator agreement scores for this task as reported in the original dataset paper (Stab and Gurevych, 2016)? This would provide a clearer sense of the task's difficulty and the upper bound for performance.
