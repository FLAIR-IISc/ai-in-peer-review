**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents an investigation into neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining. The authors frame the task using two main approaches: token-based sequence labeling and dependency parsing. They also explore a multi-task learning (MTL) variant of the sequence labeling model. The central findings are that simpler BiLSTM-based sequence tagging models perform more robustly and achieve better results than the more complex dependency parsing framing, and that the MTL setup provides additional performance gains. The proposed neural models outperform a feature-based ILP state-of-the-art baseline.

**Main Contributions**
1.  A systematic comparison of different neural end-to-end frameworks (sequence tagging, dependency parsing) for argumentation mining on a standard dataset, providing valuable insights into which architectural assumptions are most effective for the task.
2.  An empirical demonstration that a relatively simple sequence tagging model, which encodes relation information as distance-based tags, can outperform more structurally complex models like dependency parsers, particularly on a small dataset.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly motivated. The rationale for moving away from pipeline approaches and manual feature engineering toward end-to-end joint learning is well-articulated and compelling.
2.  The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors test multiple architectures within each framework (e.g., several parsers, different tagging models) and compare them against a strong ILP baseline, providing a solid empirical grounding for their conclusions.
3.  The methods and results are interesting and insightful. The failure of the dependency parsing approach, despite its intuitive appeal, and the success of the simpler tagging model offer an important lesson for structuring similar NLP tasks.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The primary and most significant weakness is the use of a very small dataset (approx. 320 training essays). This severely limits the reliability of the conclusions. Training large-capacity models like BiLSTMs on such limited data introduces a high risk of overfitting and poor generalization, a concern the paper fails to address.
2.  The evaluation lacks statistical rigor and analysis of model stability. The paper does not report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs with different initializations, making it impossible to assess the stability of the models or the statistical significance of the reported gains. No analysis of overfitting (e.g., training/development loss curves) is provided.
3.  Important experimental details are either missing or relegated to supplementary material. The main paper lacks a discussion of regularization techniques, omits details on pre-trained word embeddings, and fails to report inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, which are crucial for contextualizing performance and ensuring reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Given the small dataset and large models, can you provide an analysis of overfitting by reporting training and development set loss/F1 curves?
2.  To address model stability, could you please report the mean and variance of F1 scores over multiple runs (e.g., 5 with different random seeds) for your main STagBLCC and LSTM-ER models? This is critical for interpreting the significance of your results.
