### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for argumentation mining on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus. The authors frame the task in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of sequence tagging, and as a joint entity-relation extraction problem using an existing neural model (LSTM-ER). These neural approaches are compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The key findings are that token-level dependency parsing performs poorly, while sequence tagging models are highly effective. The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves the best performance at the paragraph level, while the authors' sequence tagging model is superior at the full essay level. Both approaches outperform the previous state-of-the-art ILP model, demonstrating the promise of end-to-end neural methods for this task. The paper also shows that an MTL setup substantially improves the sequence tagging model's performance.

### Contributions
1.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of diverse neural paradigms for end-to-end argumentation mining.** This is the paper's primary contribution. Rather than proposing a single new architecture, the authors systematically evaluate a range of plausible neural frameworks (parsing, tagging, MTL, joint entity-relation) on a standard benchmark. This provides the community with valuable insights into which architectural choices are effective for this complex structured prediction task.

2.  **Demonstration that simpler neural models outperform a complex, feature-engineered SOTA system.** The paper shows that both a sequence tagging model and an off-the-shelf entity-relation model can surpass a sophisticated ILP-based system. This result strongly motivates the move away from hand-crafted features and constraints toward end-to-end learning for argumentation mining.

3.  **A valuable negative result and analysis regarding the dependency parsing framework.** The authors show that framing token-level argumentation mining as a dependency parsing problem is surprisingly ineffective, despite the tree-like nature of argument structures. Their analysis correctly hypothesizes that the global constraints of parsing may be too complex for the long sequences found in this task, providing a useful cautionary result for future work.

### Strengths
1.  **Thorough and Systematic Evaluation:** The paper's main strength lies in its breadth of experimentation. The authors explore four distinct and well-motivated neural frameworks and compare them against four different parsers and a strong non-neural baseline. The evaluation is conducted at both the paragraph and essay level, providing a nuanced understanding of how sequence length impacts model performance. This rigorous comparison robustly establishes a new state-of-the-art for the task.

2.  **Strong Results and Insightful Analysis:** The proposed sequence tagging models and the adapted LSTM-ER model achieve significant gains over the prior state-of-the-art. The analysis goes beyond simply reporting scores; for instance, the comparison of model performance degradation from paragraph to essay level (Table 1) and the analysis of relation accuracy versus distance (Figure 4) provide clear insights into the models' respective strengths and weaknesses. The finding that MTL improves performance, particularly with the component identification subtask, is a useful practical contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The different model framings are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The authors' commitment to reproducibility is commendable, as evidenced by the comprehensive supplementary material detailing hyperparameter optimization and training procedures.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Potential for Data Leakage in the Experimental Setup:** The paper uses the standard train/test split of the PE corpus. However, it is not stated whether essays on the same topic (e.g., "competition or cooperation") are present in both the training and test sets. If so, models may achieve high performance by learning topic-specific lexical cues and argument patterns rather than generalizable argumentation structures. This potential for "data leakage" across the split could lead to an overestimation of the models' true performance on unseen topics.

2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** A key weakness is that one of the best-performing models (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of a previously published model by Miwa and Bansal (2016). While its application to argumentation mining and the accompanying analysis are valuable, this reduces the paper's methodological novelty. The primary contribution is therefore more of an empirical survey and strong application paper than one that introduces a new technique.

3.  **Minor Issues with Clarity and Organization:** The paper could be slightly clearer in a few places. The description of LSTM-ER as a model that "de-couples entity and relation detection but jointly models them" (line 455) is abstract and could benefit from a more concrete explanation of the information flow between modules during training. Furthermore, as the authors themselves note (lines 443-444), the paper's narrative might be improved by presenting the successful sequence tagging section before the less successful dependency parsing section.

### Questions to Authors
1.  **Regarding Data Leakage:** Could you clarify the distribution of essay topics across the training and test splits of the PE corpus? If topics do overlap, have you considered performing a leave-one-topic-out cross-validation to more rigorously assess how the models generalize to unseen topics?

2.  **Regarding Evaluation Comparability:** The comparison between paragraph-level and essay-level models is very interesting. However, are the F1 scores directly comparable? The PE annotation scheme allows for relations between claims and major claims to cross paragraph boundaries. Does your paragraph-level evaluation setup ignore these potential inter-paragraph links? If so, paragraph-level models are being tested on a simplified version of the task, which could partly explain their higher scores compared to essay-level models that must handle a larger and more complex search space.

3.  **Regarding Dependency Tree Reconstruction:** In Section 3, you state that the argument graph can be "loss-lessly" reconstructed from the tree structure created for the dependency parsers. Could you please elaborate on the exact procedure for this reconstruction, particularly for handling claims that link to multiple major claims, and confirm that this step is factored into the final evaluation of the parsing models?

4.  **Regarding Model "Decoupling":** Could you provide a more detailed explanation for the phrase "de-couples them but jointly models them" (line 455) in the context of the LSTM-ER model? Specifically, which architectural or training aspects constitute the "decoupling" of entity and relation tasks, and how is the "joint modeling" then achieved?
