**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining. The authors frame the task in two primary ways: as a token-based sequence tagging problem and as a dependency parsing problem. They propose a novel sequence tagging scheme that encodes component types and their relations (via distance) into a single tag. They also explore a multi-task learning (MTL) setup where subtasks like component identification are trained jointly. The proposed models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger, are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compared against several dependency parsers, another neural end-to-end model (LSTM-ER), and the previous feature-based state-of-the-art ILP model, achieving superior results.

**Contributions**

1.  **First Comprehensive Study of Neural End-to-End Argumentation Mining:** The primary contribution is the novel application and thorough investigation of end-to-end neural architectures for this task. This marks a significant shift away from traditional pipeline systems that rely on heavy feature engineering and complex constraints (e.g., ILP), thereby avoiding issues like manual effort and error propagation.
2.  **Novel Task Formulations and Empirical Comparison:** The paper introduces creative framings of argumentation mining as token-level dependency parsing and, more successfully, as a complex sequence tagging problem. The empirical results provide valuable insights, demonstrating that a simpler, local sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) is more robust and effective than global dependency parsing models, especially on long documents (essays).
3.  **State-of-the-Art Results and Analysis:** The proposed models establish a new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset. The paper provides a strong analysis comparing different model constraints and behaviors, such as why LSTM-ER excels at component identification but fails at relation detection on the essay level, offering useful "policy recommendations" for future work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for moving to an end-to-end neural approach is clearly articulated, highlighting the limitations of feature engineering and pipeline architectures. The different model framings are explained with clarity and supported by illustrative examples.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experiments:** The authors conduct a substantial set of experiments, comparing their methods against a wide range of strong baselines from different paradigms (feature-based, neural parsing, joint entity-relation extraction). The analysis is insightful, particularly the comparison between paragraph-level and essay-level performance, which effectively diagnoses the strengths and weaknesses of different architectures (e.g., the breakdown of parsers on long sequences).
3.  **Novel and Effective Methodologies:** The proposed sequence tagging formulation, which integrates component boundaries, types, and relational links (via distance) into a single label, is an elegant and effective way to tackle the end-to-end task. The exploration of multi-task learning, which shows that jointly learning subtasks improves performance, is also a valuable finding for this and potentially other complex information extraction tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Extremely Small Dataset and Risk of Overfitting:** The most significant weakness is the reliance on a very small dataset (approx. 320 training essays, 80 test essays). This is a tiny amount of data for training high-capacity models like BiLSTMs (BLC/BLCC), creating a high risk of the models simply memorizing the training set rather than learning generalizable patterns. The paper completely lacks any analysis of overfitting (e.g., training/development loss curves), which is a critical omission given the experimental setup. The size of the development set is also not reported.
2.  **Lack of Stability Analysis and Statistical Rigor:** The results are reported as single F1 scores. Neural models trained on small datasets can exhibit high variance depending on random initialization. It is crucial to report the mean and standard deviation over multiple runs to assess the stability and reliability of the results. Without this, it is difficult to know if the reported gains are consistent or the result of a fortunate random seed. The paper's claim of observing drastic performance variation for the LSTM-Parser underscores the importance of this analysis for all models.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on Regularization and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper omits any discussion of regularization techniques (e.g., dropout, L2) in the main text, which are standard and essential for preventing overfitting in neural models, especially on small data. The supplementary material only briefly mentions LSTM-ER's methods. Furthermore, the hyperparameter tuning process described in the supplement could be more principled; methods like Bayesian optimization are better suited for exploring the parameter space efficiently.
4.  **Important Details Relegated to Supplementary Material:** Crucial information for reproducibility and full understanding of the work, such as details on the pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis, are placed in the supplementary material. These elements are central to the experimental setup and findings and should be summarized in the main paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the small dataset and large model capacity, overfitting is a major concern. Could you please provide training and development loss/F1 curves to demonstrate that your models are not simply overfitting to the training data?
2.  Could you report the mean and standard deviation of F1 scores for your main models (STagBLCC, LSTM-ER) over 5-10 runs with different random initializations? This is critical for assessing model stability and the robustness of your claims. For instance, high variance in other settings (e.g., Kiperwasser F1 reportedly varying between 0.56 and 0.61) suggests this is a vital analysis.
3.  What was the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning? And can you elaborate on the specific regularization techniques (e.g., dropout rates, weight decay) applied to your STag models to mitigate overfitting?
4.  The inter-annotator agreement (IAA) scores from the original dataset paper would provide valuable context for your results, serving as a practical upper bound. Could you include these scores and discuss your performance in light of them?
