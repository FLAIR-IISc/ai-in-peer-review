- Paper conducts a thorough empirical comparison of end-to-end neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL, LSTM-ER) for argumentation mining against an ILP feature-based baseline.
- Main finding: sequence-based models—especially off-the-shelf LSTM-ER and a multi-task sequence tagger—substantially outperform dependency-based methods and prior SOTA.
- Contribution: provides a comprehensive analysis showing simpler, local sequence labeling models work better than complex global dependency parsing for this task.
- Contribution: establishes new strong SOTA using end-to-end neural models without hand-crafted features or ILP-style constraint engineering.
- Strength: offers a broad, clear survey of modern neural architectures applied to a complex structured prediction problem.
- Strength: delivers strong, convincing empirical results with neural models beating prior state-of-the-art.
- Strength: insightful analysis on multi-task learning, showing MTL helps and component identification is more beneficial than relation modeling; supplement aids reproducibility.
- Weakness: potential data leakage—unclear if train/test splits are disjoint by topic, risking inflated performance from topic-specific cues.
- Weakness: limited novelty—best-performing LSTM-ER is largely an off-the-shelf application of prior work.
- Weakness: some methodological descriptions are unclear (e.g., “de-couples them but jointly models them”) and how tree-based dependency outputs reconstruct non-tree argument graphs is unexplained.
- Question: please clarify whether essays on the same prompt/topic were kept entirely within either training or testing splits to avoid topic leakage.
- Question: are paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores comparable given paragraph-level evaluation ignores cross-paragraph relations and may favor models that miss long-range dependencies?
