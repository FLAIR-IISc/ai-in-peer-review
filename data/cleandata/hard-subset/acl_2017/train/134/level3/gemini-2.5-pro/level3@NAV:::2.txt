**Summary & Contributions**
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical investigation into neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining. The authors frame the task using several distinct architectures: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task sequence labeling, and a joint entity-relation model (LSTM-ER). They compare these against a feature-based ILP state-of-the-art baseline. The key finding is that sequence-based models, particularly the out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model and a multi-task sequence tagger, significantly outperform both dependency-based approaches and the prior SOTA.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A thorough comparative analysis of different neural paradigms for end-to-end argumentation mining on a standard dataset. This study provides valuable insights, demonstrating that simpler, local sequence labeling models are more effective than complex, global dependency parsing frameworks for this task.
2.  The establishment of new, strong state-of-the-art results using end-to-end neural models, which notably do not require hand-crafted features or complex constraint engineering, unlike previous ILP-based methods.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper provides an excellent, broad survey of modern neural techniques applied to a complex structured prediction task. By testing and analyzing fundamentally different approaches (parsing vs. tagging), the authors offer a clear picture of what architectures are best suited for argumentation mining, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2.  The results are strong and convincing. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models substantially outperform the prior state-of-the-art, demonstrating the clear superiority of neural end-to-end learning for this problem.
3.  The analysis is insightful, particularly the multi-task learning experiments. The finding that MTL boosts performance—and that the component identification sub-task is more beneficial than the relation sub-task—is a useful result for future work in this and related domains. The inclusion of a detailed supplement further enhances the work's reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**
1.  A primary concern is the potential for data leakage. The authors do not specify whether the train and test splits of the persuasive essay dataset are disjoint with respect to topics. If essays on the same topics appear in both splits, the models may be learning topic-specific cues rather than generalizable argument structures, potentially inflating performance metrics.
2.  The novelty of the best-performing models is limited. The LSTM-ER model, which achieves the highest scores at the paragraph level, is an off-the-shelf application of prior work. While its successful application is valuable, it tempers the paper's contribution in terms of novel model architecture.
3.  Certain methodological descriptions lack clarity. For instance, the phrase "de-couples them but jointly models them" (line 455) regarding the LSTM-ER model is confusing and requires further explanation. Similarly, it is unclear how a tree-based dependency output can reconstruct the full argument graph, which is not strictly a tree.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you clarify the train/test split methodology? Specifically, were essays on the same prompt/topic kept entirely within either the training or testing set to prevent the model from learning topic-specific argumentative patterns?
2.  Are the paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores truly comparable? It seems the paragraph-level evaluation inherently ignores any potential cross-paragraph relations. How does this simplification affect the interpretation of the results, especially since it may artificially favor models that struggle with long-range dependencies?
