### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The work frames the full AM task—including component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—as a single joint learning problem, moving away from traditional pipeline approaches that rely on heavy feature engineering and complex constraints (e.g., ILP). The authors systematically explore and compare several neural architectures: (1) framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem, tested with multiple parsers; (2) framing it as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, rich label set that encodes component, type, and relational information; (3) enhancing the sequence tagging model with a multi-task learning setup; and (4) applying a state-of-the-art joint entity and relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). The experiments, conducted on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), show that token-based dependency parsing performs poorly. In contrast, sequence tagging approaches are highly effective, with both the authors' proposed model (STag) and the adapted LSTM-ER model outperforming the previous state-of-the-art ILP model. The results demonstrate the viability and advantages of neural end-to-end learning for this task.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Broad and Systematic Empirical Comparison of Neural Architectures for End-to-End AM:** This work is the first to systematically investigate and compare multiple distinct neural paradigms (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, joint entity-relation extraction) for the full end-to-end argumentation mining task. The paper provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, establishing a strong empirical baseline for future research. A key finding is that simpler, local sequence tagging models are more effective than global, token-level dependency parsing models for this task.
2.  **A Robust Sequence Tagging Formulation for AM:** The paper introduces a novel and effective sequence tagging scheme (STag) that encodes component boundaries, component types, relation targets (via relative distance), and relation types into a single, unified label. This formulation, especially when enhanced using a multi-task learning framework, proves to be a powerful method that achieves state-of-the-art performance.
3.  **Demonstration of the Superiority of Neural Models over Feature-Engineered Approaches:** By showing that multiple neural models outperform a strong, feature-based ILP system, the paper makes a convincing case for abandoning hand-crafted features and manually designed constraints in favor of end-to-end representation learning for argumentation mining.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Thoroughness of the Investigation:** The paper's primary strength is its breadth. It does not merely propose a single model but instead evaluates a wide range of plausible and representative neural architectures. The comparison includes four different dependency parsers (both classic and neural), a state-of-the-art BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger, a multi-task variant, and a sophisticated joint entity-relation model. This comprehensive comparison provides the community with a clear picture of the current landscape of neural approaches for AM.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The proposed and adapted models achieve new state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark dataset. The paper's analysis of these results is particularly insightful. For example, the discussion of why LSTM-ER's relation F1 score degrades significantly on the essay-level task (due to the quadratic explosion of candidate pairs) compared to the more constrained STag model is well-reasoned and provides a valuable lesson for applying such models to long documents.
3.  **Effective Use of Multi-Task Learning (MTL):** The paper clearly demonstrates that MTL provides a substantial performance boost for the complex sequence tagging task. The experiments in Table 2 successfully show that adding "natural subtasks" (component identification and relation identification) as auxiliary training objectives helps the model learn better representations and improves overall performance. This is a practical and well-supported finding.
4.  **High Potential for Reproducibility:** The authors provide detailed information regarding experimental setup, including hyperparameter optimization and software used, in the supplementary material. This commitment to transparency is commendable and greatly facilitates the reproduction of their results.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty of the Best-Performing Model:** While the comparative study is a significant contribution, one of the best-performing models (LSTM-ER, particularly at the paragraph level) is a direct application of a pre-existing model from Miwa and Bansal (2016). Although the authors' own STag formulation is more robust at the essay level, the strong performance of an off-the-shelf model somewhat reduces the perceived novelty of the proposed methods. The core contribution leans more towards a strong analysis paper than a paper introducing a novel state-of-the-art technique.
2.  **Potential for Data Leakage in Evaluation:** The paper evaluates on the standard train/test split of the persuasive essays dataset but does not discuss the distribution of essay topics across these splits. Since these essays are written on a fixed set of controversial topics, there is a non-trivial risk that essays on the same topic appear in both training and test sets. This could allow models to overfit to topic-specific lexical cues rather than learning generalizable argumentation structures, potentially inflating the reported scores.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** Certain conceptual descriptions could be improved. For instance, the statement that LSTM-ER "de-couples entity and relation detection but jointly models them" (line 455) is confusing and could be explained more clearly. Furthermore, the paper's structure could be more intuitive; presenting the highly successful sequence tagging approaches (Section 4.1) before the less successful dependency parsing approaches (Section 4.2) might provide a smoother narrative for the reader.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the distribution of essay topics across the train and test splits of the dataset? Is it ensured that topics seen in the test set are not present in the training set? If there is overlap, have you considered its potential effect on the reported performance?
2.  The dependency parsing approach requires converting the argumentation graph into a tree by, for instance, re-linking claims to a single major claim (lines 222-226). How is the original graph structure (with claims linking to multiple major claims) reconstructed from the parser's single-head-per-token tree output during evaluation to calculate the final F1 scores?
3.  The description of LSTM-ER as a model that "de-couples" but "jointly models" entity and relation detection is ambiguous. Could you please elaborate on the specific architecture and training procedure that this phrasing refers to? For example, are there separate modules with distinct parameters that are optimized via a joint loss function?
4.  You report results on both paragraph-level and essay-level settings. Paragraph-level models are presumably incapable of capturing relations that span across paragraphs. Does the dataset contain such relations, and if so, does this evaluation setup disadvantage the essay-level models or mean that the F1 scores are not directly comparable across settings?
