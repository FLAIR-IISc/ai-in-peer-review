- Sequence labeling (BiLSTM-CRF with distance encoding) outperforms dependency parsing for end-to-end argumentation mining.  
- Multi-task learning improves AM performance, with component (claim) detection contributing more than relation detection.  
- Neural approaches can beat the ILP-based state-of-the-art without manual features or constraints.  
- Thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural architectures fills a gap in AM research.  
- The finding that dependency parsing performs poorly despite tree-like argument structure challenges common assumptions.  
- Multi-task results suggest broader applicability to related sequence labeling problems.  
- Neural models outperform ILP baseline on component and relation identification (best global F1 55.22% at essay level).  
- Analysis explains why some models (e.g., LSTM-ER) drop in essay-level performance due to larger relation search space.  
- Supplementary materials provide detailed hyperparameter and training information, aiding reproducibility.  
- LSTM-ER is used with minimal adaptation from prior work, limiting methodological novelty for AM.  
- Potential data leakage: unclear whether train/test sets are topic-diverse or share topics, which could inflate results.  
- Unclear how dependency parser tree outputs are converted into full argumentation graphs that may have multiple roots.  
- Paragraph- and essay-level comparisons are problematic because different text lengths make F1 scores not directly comparable.  
- Paper structure misrepresents findings by presenting dependency parsing before the more successful sequence tagging approach.  
- Lack of clarity on how the model "de-couples but jointly models" entities and relations, especially for LSTM-ER.  
- Clarify whether train/test sets are topic-diverse to rule out data leakage.  
- Explain how dependency parsing output can reconstruct multi-root argumentation graphs.  
- Clarify the meaning of "de-couples them but jointly models them" regarding entity vs relation modeling.  
- Discuss whether paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores are directly comparable given differing lengths and complexities.
