

# Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining, framing the problem as token-based dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The authors claim to present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining. Their key contributions include: (1) demonstrating that sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing for this task; (2) showing that multi-task learning improves performance; and (3) establishing neural models as competitive with (or better than) pipeline ILP approaches without requiring manual feature engineering.

## Strengths

1. **Well-written and clear presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex neural network approaches accessible. The motivation for avoiding ILP and manual feature engineering is compelling and clearly articulated. The explanation of the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is particularly elegant and well-justified.

2. **Substantial empirical evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across multiple architectures (dependency parsers, sequence taggers, multi-task learning) and evaluation metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at 100% and 50% matching levels). The comparison with the state-of-the-art ILP approach provides strong evidence for their claims.

3. **Novel framing of argumentation mining**: The sequence tagging approach that encodes distance information between components is a clever solution to the long-range dependency problem inherent in argumentation mining. The paper provides convincing evidence that this approach outperforms the more intuitive dependency parsing approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Severe data limitations**: The paper uses a dataset with only ≈320 training essays and ≈80 test essays, which is extremely small for neural network training. The training set contains only a few thousand annotated components (vs. hundreds of thousands/millions in standard sequence tasks), creating a high risk of overfitting. The authors' use of large-capacity BiLSTM models (BLCC) on such limited data is problematic.

2. **Lack of proper overfitting analysis**: The paper omits critical analyses including training/development loss curves, which would reveal whether the models are overfitting to the small training set. Without this, it's impossible to assess the reliability of the reported results.

3. **Insufficient model stability analysis**: The paper reports single-point results without providing mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different initializations. The authors note significant variance in results (e.g., Kiperwasser setting F1 in [0.56, 0.61]), yet fail to address this critical issue. Statistical significance tests are also missing.

4. **Insufficient methodological details**: The paper omits crucial details about regularization techniques (beyond the brief mention of LSTM-ER in supplementary material) and hyperparameter tuning (using a non-optimal method described in the supplement). The details about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis should be in the main paper rather than supplementary.

## Questions to Authors

1. Given the extremely small dataset (≈320 training essays), how did you prevent severe overfitting with large-capacity BiLSTM models? Please provide training/development loss curves and evidence of model generalization.

2. The results show significant variance across different model runs (e.g., Kiperwasser F1 in [0.56, 0.61]). What is the mean and standard deviation for all reported results? Please provide statistical significance tests.

3. Why did you use a non-optimized hyperparameter tuning method (described in supplement) rather than Bayesian optimization, which is standard for neural network hyperparameter tuning?

4. The paper mentions "LSTM-ER" in the supplement but provides no details about its implementation. Please include a proper description of this regularization technique in the main paper.

5. The annotation quality of the dataset is crucial for interpreting results. Please include inter-annotator agreement scores from the original dataset paper to contextualize your system performance.

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting approach to argumentation mining with clear methodological contributions. However, the severe data limitations and lack of proper overfitting/stability analysis undermine the reliability of the reported results. The authors should address the concerns about data size, overfitting, and model stability before the paper can be considered for acceptance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by moving critical details from the supplement to the main text and by providing proper statistical analysis of results.

The main contribution of demonstrating that sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing is valuable, but the paper's claims about neural end-to-end learning being superior to pipeline approaches are not sufficiently supported by the current experimental setup due to the data limitations. With the requested revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
