

# Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary
This paper investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and evaluating an out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model. The authors demonstrate that dependency-based approaches perform poorly, while sequence labeling models (particularly with multi-task learning) achieve robust performance. The LSTM-ER model performs exceptionally well at the paragraph level, outperforming the ILP state-of-the-art. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison across multiple approaches and levels of granularity (paragraph vs. essay).

## Main Contributions
1. **Demonstrating the effectiveness of sequence labeling with distance encoding for end-to-end AM**, establishing it as a superior approach to dependency parsing and outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods.
2. **Showing that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task substantially improves performance**, with component detection being more beneficial than relation detection.
3. **Providing the first comprehensive empirical comparison of neural approaches for end-to-end AM**, establishing new state-of-the-art results and offering valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of different modeling approaches.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical evidence for sequence labeling approaches**: The paper provides compelling evidence that sequence labeling with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing (by 13-11% in C-F1/R-F1 on paragraph level) and the previous ILP state-of-the-art. This is significant because it demonstrates a simpler, more robust approach that doesn't require hand-crafted features or complex constraints.

2. **Compelling multi-task learning results**: The paper shows that adding component detection as an auxiliary task substantially improves performance (up to 5.5% in C-F1), with the component task being more beneficial than relation task. This provides valuable insights into how to structure neural models for AM tasks.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper evaluates multiple approaches across different levels (paragraph vs. essay), with both exact and approximate matching metrics. This thorough evaluation provides a clear understanding of when and why different approaches work well.

## Weaknesses
1. **LSTM-ER as an out-of-the-box application**: The paper's strongest model (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work, which significantly weakens the novelty claim. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently address why this constitutes a meaningful contribution to argumentation mining rather than just a straightforward application of existing technology.

2. **Potential data leakage concern**: The paper doesn't explicitly state whether the test essays are from different topics than the training essays. If the same topics appear in both sets, the model could be leveraging topic-specific argument patterns rather than learning general argumentation structures, potentially inflating performance.

3. **Lack of clarity on paragraph vs. essay comparison**: The paper shows paragraph-level models significantly outperform essay-level models, but it doesn't adequately address whether paragraph-level models miss important cross-paragraph relations (e.g., relations between claims and major claims that span paragraphs). This is a fundamental limitation for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors
1. The LSTM-ER model is described as an "out-of-the-box application" of prior work. How does this constitute a meaningful contribution to argumentation mining, and why should the field consider this a novel approach rather than just an application of existing technology?

2. The paper states that paragraph-level models miss cross-paragraph relations (e.g., claims to major claims). Can you clarify how the tree output of the dependency parsing approach can be used to reconstruct the full argumentation graph, especially given the paper's observation that argument structures are "almost trees" with multiple major claims potentially creating non-tree structures?

3. The paper compares paragraph and essay-level results but doesn't address whether F1 scores across these settings are comparable. Given that paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations, how should we interpret the performance differences between these two settings? Does the paragraph-level performance represent a true improvement or simply a restricted task?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating the effectiveness of sequence labeling approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. However, the lack of novelty in the LSTM-ER approach and the potential data leakage concern need to be adequately addressed to fully support the paper's claims. The multi-task learning results are particularly strong and provide useful insights for future work.
