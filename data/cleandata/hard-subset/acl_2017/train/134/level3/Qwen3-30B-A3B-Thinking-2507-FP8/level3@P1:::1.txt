

This paper presents a novel application of neural networks to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing a significant limitation in current approaches that rely on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that sequence tagging approaches (specifically BiLSTM-CRF variants) outperform dependency parsing for AM, (2) showing that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) improves performance, and (3) providing the first neural end-to-end AM solution that eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for neural end-to-end AM, effectively demonstrating that joint learning avoids pipeline error propagation and eliminates the need for costly feature engineering. The experimental design is comprehensive, with thorough comparisons across multiple neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning) against the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based approach. The authors provide insightful analysis explaining why dependency parsing fails for token-level AM (due to the long-range dependencies and tree structure mismatch) while sequence tagging succeeds (by encoding distance information between components). The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the technical contributions accessible.

The most significant strength is the demonstration that sequence tagging with BiLSTMs outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP approach by 10-15% F1 points on the PE dataset, while eliminating the need for manual feature engineering. The multi-task learning results are particularly compelling, showing consistent improvements (up to 5% F1) when including component detection as an auxiliary task, which aligns with recent findings in multi-task learning.

Weaknesses: The most critical weakness is the extremely small dataset size (320 training essays, 80 test essays), which is insufficient for training large neural models like BiLSTM-CRF-CNN. This raises serious concerns about overfitting, as the authors' models have high capacity (with hundreds of thousands of parameters) while the training data consists of only a few thousand annotated components. The paper lacks any analysis of model stability or overfitting, with no training/development loss curves, statistical significance tests, or mean/standard deviation across multiple runs. The reported results appear to be single runs, making it difficult to assess whether performance differences are meaningful or due to random initialization.

The paper also fails to adequately address the model-data mismatch. Standard sequence labeling tasks typically require hundreds of thousands of training examples, yet this paper uses approximately 320 essays with about 5,000 annotated components. The authors' claim that "neural sequence tagging models are in principle ideal candidates" for AM seems questionable given the data scarcity. The lack of regularization discussion (beyond brief mentions in supplementary material) and insufficient hyperparameter tuning details further compound these concerns.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by including inter-annotator agreement scores from the dataset to contextualize the achievable performance. The small dataset size also makes it difficult to assess whether the reported improvements over the ILP baseline are truly meaningful or artifacts of overfitting.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Given the small dataset size (only ~320 training essays), how did you ensure your models did not overfit? Could you provide training and validation loss curves to demonstrate this?
2. The paper reports only single runs of experiments. Could you provide mean and standard deviation across multiple model initializations to assess stability and report statistical significance (e.g., paired t-tests)?
3. The supplementary material mentions Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning. Could you move this discussion to the main paper and provide more details on how you applied it to avoid potential overfitting during tuning?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining, but the small dataset size and lack of model stability analysis significantly undermine the confidence in the reported results. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and impact.
