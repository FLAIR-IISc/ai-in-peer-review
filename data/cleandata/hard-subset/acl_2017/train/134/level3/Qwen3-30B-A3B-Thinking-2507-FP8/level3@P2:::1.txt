

# Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as token-based dependency parsing, token-based sequence tagging, and multi-task learning. The authors demonstrate that dependency parsing approaches perform poorly for AM, while sequence tagging models with distance encoding achieve robust results. The main contributions are: (1) The first neural end-to-end solutions for computational AM; (2) Demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model; and (3) Identification of sequence tagging with distance encoding as the most effective approach for AM, with multi-task learning providing additional improvements.

## Strengths
1. **Strong motivation and clear problem statement**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of current pipeline approaches (hand-crafted features, error propagation) and clearly justifies the need for end-to-end neural solutions. The explanation of why dependency parsing is ineffective for token-level AM (as opposed to component-level) is particularly insightful.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper thoroughly compares multiple neural approaches against a strong ILP baseline, covering component segmentation, typing, and relation identification. The analysis of why certain approaches work better than others (e.g., why sequence tagging outperforms dependency parsing) provides valuable theoretical insights.

3. **Well-written and accessible**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of complex concepts. The figures (despite needing improvement for black-and-white printing) effectively illustrate the argumentation structures and model behaviors.

4. **Practical contributions**: The finding that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) improves performance offers a practical framework for future AM work.

## Weaknesses
1. **Severe data limitations**: The dataset size (320 training essays, 80 test essays) is extremely small for the complexity of the models used (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN). This raises serious concerns about overfitting and model generalization. The paper lacks analysis of model stability (e.g., mean and standard deviation across multiple runs) and statistical significance testing.

2. **Insufficient model evaluation**: The paper does not provide training/development loss curves or analysis of overfitting, which is critical given the small dataset. The results show high variance (e.g., Kiperwasser's F1 ranges from 0.56 to 0.61), yet the paper fails to address this instability.

3. **Lack of regularization and hyperparameter discussion**: The paper omits crucial details about regularization methods (despite mentioning them briefly in the supplement) and uses an ad-hoc hyperparameter tuning approach. Given the small dataset, proper regularization techniques and Bayesian optimization would be essential to justify the results.

4. **Missing contextualization of results**: The paper does not report inter-annotator agreement scores from the dataset (Stab and Gurevych, 2016), making it difficult to assess whether the reported performance is near the human performance ceiling.

5. **Critical information in supplement**: Important details about pre-trained word embeddings, error analysis, and hyperparameter tuning belong in the main paper rather than supplementary material, reducing the paper's accessibility and reproducibility.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide mean and standard deviation of F1 scores across multiple random initializations to demonstrate model stability, especially given the high variance observed in some settings?

2. Please elaborate on the specific regularization techniques used and how you determined their optimal settings given the limited training data.

3. Could you include inter-annotator agreement scores from the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset to help contextualize your results?

4. Would you be able to share the training and development loss curves to better understand model convergence and potential overfitting?

This is an interesting and well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the argumentation mining field. However, the severe data limitations and lack of proper model evaluation procedures significantly undermine the confidence in the reported results. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.
