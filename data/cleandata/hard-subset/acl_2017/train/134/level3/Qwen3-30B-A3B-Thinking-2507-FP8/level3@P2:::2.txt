

# Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem through multiple neural architectures including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER. The authors present a comprehensive empirical comparison of these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. Demonstrating that sequence labeling approaches (specifically BiLSTM-CRF with distance encoding) significantly outperform dependency parsing approaches for end-to-end AM, challenging the intuition that argumentation structures (which resemble trees) would naturally suit dependency parsing.

2. Showing that multi-task learning substantially improves performance on argumentation mining tasks, with the claim detection task contributing more significantly than relation detection as an auxiliary task.

3. Providing strong empirical evidence that neural approaches can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach without requiring manual feature engineering or constraint design.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural architectures for end-to-end AM, which is valuable given the lack of prior neural approaches to this specific problem. The authors' finding that dependency parsing approaches perform poorly despite the tree-like nature of argument structures is particularly insightful and challenges common assumptions in the field.

2. The multi-task learning results are compelling, showing that incorporating natural subtasks (especially component detection) as auxiliary tasks significantly improves performance. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks where multiple related subtasks exist, potentially extending beyond argumentation mining.

3. The empirical results clearly demonstrate that neural approaches outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art system on both component and relation identification, with the best sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) achieving up to 55.22% global F1 on the essay level. This represents a meaningful advance in the field.

4. The paper's thorough analysis of why certain approaches work better than others (e.g., the explanation for LSTM-ER's performance drop on essay level due to increased search space for relations) provides valuable theoretical insights beyond just empirical results.

5. The authors provide comprehensive supplementary materials detailing hyperparameter optimization and training procedures, which greatly enhances the reproducibility of their results.

## Weaknesses

1. The LSTM-ER model used is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work (Miwa and Bansal, 2016), with minimal adaptation to the argumentation mining task. This significantly limits the novelty of the approach, as the authors don't propose new architectural innovations specific to AM.

2. There's a serious concern about possible data leakage between train and test sets. The authors don't explicitly address whether the train and test essays share topics or argument structures, which could inflate performance metrics. The paper states "80 of which are reserved for testing" but doesn't clarify if these are randomly selected or topic-diverse.

3. The paper doesn't adequately explain how the dependency parsing approach (which produces a tree structure) can be used to reconstruct the full argumentation graph, as claimed in the abstract. The authors state "we find that framing the problem as dependency parsing leads to subpar performance," yet don't clarify how the tree output relates to the more complex argumentation structures (which may contain multiple roots).

4. The comparison between paragraph-level and essay-level results is problematic because the two settings aren't directly comparable. The paper states "paragraphs have an average length of 66 tokens" while essays have "an average length of 368 tokens," but doesn't explain how the F1 scores across these different settings can be meaningfully compared.

5. The paper's structure could be improved by reordering sections to present the sequence tagging approach before dependency parsing, as the former is the more successful approach. The current ordering (dependency parsing first, then sequence tagging) misrepresents the paper's main findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. Please clarify whether the train and test sets are topic-diverse, and if not, whether this might lead to data leakage that inflates performance metrics.

2. Please explain how the dependency parsing approach (which produces a tree structure) can be used to reconstruct the full argumentation graph, which may contain multiple roots (as mentioned in the data section).

3. Please clarify the meaning of "de-couples them but jointly models them" and the claimed decoupling of relation vs entity information, especially in light of the fact that the LSTM-ER model is described as "de-coupling entity and relation detection."

4. Please discuss whether F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings are directly comparable, given the substantial difference in text length and complexity between the two settings.
