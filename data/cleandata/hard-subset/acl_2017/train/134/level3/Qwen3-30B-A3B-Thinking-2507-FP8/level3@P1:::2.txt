

This paper presents a comprehensive investigation of neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the limitations of previous pipeline methods that rely on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming. The primary contribution is establishing the first neural end-to-end framework for AM, which outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach (Stab and Gurevych, 2016). The authors demonstrate that token-based dependency parsing approaches are ineffective for AM, contrary to previous assumptions, while sequence tagging models with distance encoding perform robustly across different evaluation scenarios. A secondary contribution is showing that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (particularly component detection) significantly improves performance.

Strengths: First, the paper makes a compelling case for neural end-to-end AM as a superior alternative to feature-based pipeline approaches. The authors systematically compare multiple neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) and demonstrate consistent outperformance of the ILP baseline across both component and relation identification tasks. On the PE dataset, the best sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) achieves 63.23% C-F1 (50%) and 73.02% R-F1 (50%) at the essay level, outperforming the ILP baseline by 3.5% and 4.1% respectively. Second, the paper provides valuable empirical insights about model architecture choices: the authors correctly identify that token-based dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, while sequence tagging models with distance encoding effectively capture long-range dependencies inherent in argumentation structures. Third, the multi-task learning results are particularly strong, showing that adding component detection as an auxiliary task consistently improves performance (up to 5.5% absolute gain in C-F1 (100%)), with component detection being more valuable than relation detection as an auxiliary task. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks with hierarchical structure.

Weaknesses: First, there's a significant concern about potential data leakage in the experimental setup. The PE dataset contains 402 essays with 80 reserved for testing, but the authors don't clarify whether train and test essays share topics or argument structures, which could artificially inflate performance metrics. This is particularly problematic given that argumentation structures are highly topic-dependent. Second, the best-performing model (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work (Miwa and Bansal, 2016) without significant adaptation to the AM task, limiting the novelty of the contribution. Third, the paper fails to adequately address how the dependency parsing approach (which produces tree structures) can be used to reconstruct the full argumentation graph, given that the PE dataset contains non-tree structures (multiple major claims per essay). The explanation on lines 222-226 is insufficient. Fourth, the comparison between paragraph-level and essay-level performance is problematic since paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations, yet the paper treats these as comparable evaluations. The essay-level results for paragraph-trained models (e.g., 54.17% C-F1 (100%) vs. 60.46% when trained on essay level) suggest a significant performance drop, but the paper doesn't adequately address why this happens or how it impacts the practical utility of the models.

Questions to Authors: 1) Please clarify whether the train and test sets contain essays on overlapping topics or with similar argument structures, which could lead to data leakage. 2) How exactly does the multi-task learning framework improve performance beyond standard sequence tagging, particularly with respect to the component detection task? 3) The paper claims that LSTM-ER "de-couples" entity and relation detection but "jointly models" them—please clarify this relationship and the claimed decoupling of relation vs. entity information. 4) Given that paragraph-level models miss cross-paragraph relations, how should these findings be interpreted when evaluating end-to-end AM systems for real-world applications? 5) The paper states that dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, but the best neural parser (LSTM-Parser) still outperforms the ILP baseline on paragraph level. Please clarify the relationship between this finding and the paper's main claim about dependency parsing being ineffective.
