- Primary contribution: neural sequence labeling models, especially out-of-the-box LSTM-ER, outperform traditional ILP-based pipeline and dependency parsing for end-to-end argumentation mining, achieving paragraph-level SOTA.  
- Strength: sequence labeling captures long-range dependencies and substantially outperforms dependency parsing, which performed poorly.  
- Strength: multi-task learning improves performance, with component detection contributing more than relation detection.  
- Strength: neural end-to-end methods remove need for hand-crafted features and complex ILP constraints.  
- Weakness: potential data leakage between training and test sets (shared essay topics/arguments) could inflate performance.  
- Weakness: novelty concern since best-performing LSTM-ER is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work.  
- Weakness: insufficient explanation of how dependency parse tree outputs are used to reconstruct full argumentation graphs (lines 222–226).  
- Weakness: unclear claim about “decoupling but jointly modeling” entity and relation information (lines 455, 477–479) needs clarification.  
- Methodological issue: need to clarify whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores are truly comparable given possible missing cross-paragraph relations.  
- Presentation suggestion: reorder sections to present sequence labeling before dependency parsing for better logical flow.  
- Overall: valuable empirical contribution demonstrating sequence labeling’s superiority, but requires methodological clarifications and addressing data leakage before acceptance.
