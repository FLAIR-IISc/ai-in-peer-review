

This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, presenting a comprehensive evaluation of multiple techniques including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER. The primary contribution is establishing that neural sequence labeling models outperform traditional pipeline approaches (ILP-based) for argumentation mining, with the out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model achieving state-of-the-art results at paragraph level.

The strengths of this work are significant. First, it demonstrates that neural sequence labeling approaches, which capture long-range dependencies, substantially outperform dependency parsing methods (which performed poorly) and the ILP-based state-of-the-art. Second, the multi-task learning setup significantly improves performance, with component detection contributing more than relation detection. Third, the paper provides strong empirical evidence that neural end-to-end approaches eliminate the need for hand-crafted features and complex ILP constraints.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The most concerning is the potential data leakage between training and test sets, as the authors acknowledge essays may share topics/arguments. This could artificially inflate performance metrics. Second, the LSTM-ER model, which achieves the best results, is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work, raising questions about novelty. Third, the paper lacks sufficient explanation for how the tree output from dependency parsing can reconstruct the full argumentation graph, as requested in lines 222-226. The claim about "decoupling but jointly modeling" entity and relation information (lines 455, 477-479) also needs clarification.

The authors should address whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores are truly comparable, especially since paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations. Additionally, the paper would benefit from reordering sections to present sequence labeling before dependency parsing (lines 443-444) for better logical flow.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to argumentation mining with strong empirical results, but requires clarification on methodological details and potential data leakage concerns before acceptance. The core finding that sequence labeling outperforms dependency parsing for this task is well-supported and represents a meaningful advance.
