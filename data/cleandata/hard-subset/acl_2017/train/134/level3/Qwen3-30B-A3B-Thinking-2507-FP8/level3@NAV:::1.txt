

**Review**

This paper presents the first neural end-to-end approach to computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as sequence tagging with distance encoding, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning. The main contributions are: (1) introducing neural end-to-end AM without ILP or manual feature engineering; (2) demonstrating that sequence tagging with BiLSTMs outperforms dependency parsing for this task; and (3) showing that multi-task learning with natural subtasks improves performance.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear motivation for avoiding pipeline approaches and ILP constraints. The sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is novel and well-justified, particularly the insight that long-range dependencies in AM can be captured by neural tagging models. The experiments are substantial, with thorough comparisons across multiple neural architectures, and results consistently show that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP baseline.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant concern is the extremely small dataset (≈320 training essays, ≈80 test essays), which is orders of magnitude smaller than standard sequence labeling tasks. With training tags likely numbering only in the thousands (vs. hundreds of thousands/millions in standard NLP tasks), the large-capacity BiLSTM models risk severe overfitting. The paper lacks overfitting analysis (no training/development loss curves), model stability assessment (no mean/std across multiple runs), and statistical significance testing. Additionally, regularization methods are only briefly mentioned in supplementary material, and the authors should have used Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning. The paper also omits inter-annotator agreement scores to contextualize performance, and error analysis and pre-trained embeddings details should be moved from supplementary to main text.

**Questions to Authors:** Please provide F1-score ranges across multiple runs for different settings and address the high variance observed in results (e.g., Kiperwasser setting F1 in [0.56, 0.61]).
