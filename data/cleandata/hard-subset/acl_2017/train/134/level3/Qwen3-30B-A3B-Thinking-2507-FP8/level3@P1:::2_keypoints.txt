- Introduces the first neural end-to-end framework for argumentation mining (AM) that outperforms the ILP-based baseline (e.g., STagBLCC: 63.23% C-F1 and 73.02% R-F1 at essay level, beating ILP by ~3.5% and ~4.1%).  
- Demonstrates token-based dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contradicting prior assumptions.  
- Finds sequence tagging models with distance encoding robustly capture long-range dependencies in argumentation structures.  
- Shows multi-task learning—especially adding component detection as an auxiliary task—significantly improves performance (up to 5.5% absolute C-F1 gain), with component detection more valuable than relation detection.  
- Provides a systematic empirical comparison of multiple neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task, LSTM-ER) consistently outperforming the ILP baseline on component and relation identification.  
- Raises potential data leakage concerns: unclear whether train/test essays share topics or similar argument structures, which could inflate results.  
- Notes limited novelty: the best-performing LSTM-ER is largely an out-of-the-box application of prior work (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) with little AM-specific adaptation.  
- Criticizes insufficient explanation of how dependency parsing (tree outputs) can reconstruct full argumentation graphs given non-tree structures and multiple major claims in the PE dataset.  
- Flags problematic paragraph vs. essay-level comparisons: paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations, and training granularity causes notable performance drops (e.g., 54.17% vs. 60.46% C-F1).  
- Requests clarification whether train/test sets contain overlapping topics or argument structures to address data leakage concerns.  
- Asks for a precise explanation of how multi-task learning improves beyond standard sequence tagging, particularly via the component detection auxiliary task.  
- Seeks clarification on LSTM-ER’s claimed “de-coupling” of entities and relations versus its simultaneous/joint modeling.  
- Asks how paragraph-level findings should be interpreted for real-world end-to-end AM given missed cross-paragraph relations.  
- Requests reconciliation of the claim that dependency parsing is ineffective with the observation that the best neural parser (LSTM-Parser) still outperforms the ILP baseline at paragraph level.
