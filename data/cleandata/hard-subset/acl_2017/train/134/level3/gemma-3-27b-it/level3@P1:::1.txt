This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, including a multi-task learning setup. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of traditional pipeline approaches relying on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP), arguing that neural networks offer a path towards more robust and generalizable solutions. Their empirical analysis reveals that framing AM as dependency parsing yields suboptimal performance, while sequence tagging models – particularly BiLSTM-based architectures – perform robustly. Furthermore, incorporating auxiliary tasks via multi-task learning improves performance.

The primary contribution of this work lies in providing a comprehensive empirical investigation of neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. While prior work has explored components of this problem, this paper presents a systematic evaluation of several different framings and demonstrates the effectiveness of sequence tagging and multi-task learning. A secondary contribution is the negative result regarding dependency parsing as a suitable framing for end-to-end AM, challenging a previously proposed approach. Finally, the experiments and resulting dataset usage provide a strong baseline for future research in this area.

Several strengths support the acceptance of this submission. First, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the motivations, methods, and results. The authors effectively articulate the limitations of existing approaches and the potential benefits of their proposed neural network models. Second, the experimental setup is thorough, comparing multiple models and ablations. The inclusion of both paragraph-level and essay-level evaluations provides valuable insights into the challenges of long-range dependencies in argumentation mining. Third, the findings are interesting and potentially impactful. The demonstration that sequence tagging, specifically BiLSTM-based architectures, perform well on this task, coupled with the benefits of multi-task learning, offers a promising direction for future research. The observed limitations of dependency parsing are also noteworthy and redirect efforts away from a potentially fruitless path. Fourth, the paper addresses a timely and important problem. Computational argumentation mining has significant applications in areas such as legal reasoning, document summarization, and educational technology.

However, several weaknesses must be addressed. The most significant concern is the limited size of the dataset (approximately 320 training essays and 80 test essays). This small dataset raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the potential for overfitting, particularly given the complexity of the BiLSTM models employed. The paper does not report the size of a development set used for hyperparameter tuning, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the model selection process. Furthermore, there is a lack of analysis regarding overfitting; the inclusion of training and development loss curves would provide valuable insight into the model’s learning behavior. The paper reports single F1-score results without reporting mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different random initializations. The absence of statistical significance testing further weakens the conclusions drawn from the experiments. The discussion of regularization methods is inadequate; details regarding regularization should be included in the main paper, and the LSTM-ER regularization strategies outlined in the supplementary material should be properly described. Finally, while Bayesian optimization is suggested in the supplementary material, a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning strategies would be beneficial. 

Several details currently residing in the supplementary material should be moved to the main paper. These include specifics regarding pre-trained word embeddings and a more comprehensive error analysis. Additionally, including inter-annotator agreement scores from the original dataset paper would provide important context for interpreting the system performance. Figure 1’s color/contrast should be improved to ensure readability in black-and-white printing.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the small dataset size, what regularization techniques were employed to mitigate overfitting, and can you provide learning curves (training and development loss) to demonstrate the effectiveness of these techniques?
2.  Please report the mean and standard deviation of the results across multiple runs with different random initializations and provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to support your claims.
3.  For the settings where results exhibit high variance (e.g., Kiperwasser’s F1 score range of [0.56, 0.61]), can you provide more detail about the factors contributing to this variability and potential strategies for improving stability?
4.  Can you elaborate on the choices made during hyperparameter tuning, including the search space and optimization strategy?



**Additional Comments:**

The framing of AM as dependency parsing, while yielding poorer results, is a valuable exploration that provides insights into the task’s characteristics. The authors should acknowledge the potential for future work to explore more sophisticated parsing architectures or alternative dependency representations. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of each approach and the potential challenges in applying these models to real-world scenarios with more complex and noisy data.
