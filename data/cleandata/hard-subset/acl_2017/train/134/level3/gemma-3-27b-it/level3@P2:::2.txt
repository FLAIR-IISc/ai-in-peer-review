This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, a multi-task learning problem, and adapting the LSTM-ER model. The authors demonstrate that while dependency parsing proves ineffective, sequence tagging and LSTM-ER achieve strong performance, outperforming a feature-based ILP baseline. Multi-task learning further enhances sequence tagging performance.

The primary contribution of this work lies in providing a comprehensive empirical evaluation of several neural network architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining. While individual components of the presented approaches may not be entirely novel, the systematic comparison across different framing choices – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a pre-existing entity-relation model – offers valuable insights into the suitability of these techniques for the AM task. The clear demonstration that neural methods can surpass prior state-of-the-art ILP-based systems is a significant result. A second, related, contribution is the finding that simple sequence tagging models (BiLSTM-CRF variants) perform remarkably well on this task, particularly when combined with multi-task learning. This suggests that complex architectural choices are not necessarily required for strong performance in AM. Finally, the thorough experimental setup, including ablation studies with multi-task learning, provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates that neural methods, particularly sequence tagging with BiLSTM-CRFs, achieve competitive or superior performance to the established ILP baseline. This is a significant result, demonstrating the viability of end-to-end neural AM. The results across paragraph and essay levels provide useful granularity.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors explore a broad range of architectures and configurations, providing a thorough assessment of different modeling choices. This comparative analysis is valuable for researchers seeking to apply neural methods to AM. The inclusion of LSTM-ER as a strong comparator, and the comparison to existing dependency parsing methods, further strengthen the evaluation.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides insightful analysis of the results, particularly regarding the failure of dependency parsing and the benefits of multi-task learning. The discussion regarding the trade-offs between different modeling approaches (e.g., modularity vs. constraints) is well-reasoned and helpful. The observation about the difficulty of relation identification with increasing sequence length is a valuable observation.
4. **Well-Documented Experimentation:** The paper references a comprehensive supplementary material detailing implementation details, hyperparameter optimization, and further error analysis. This transparency enhances the reproducibility and credibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of LSTM-ER:** While the results with LSTM-ER are strong, the primary contribution appears to be applying an existing, off-the-shelf model to a new dataset rather than introducing significant architectural innovations. The paper acknowledges this but could more explicitly address how this application contributes to the field beyond simply demonstrating its effectiveness.
2. **Potential Data Leakage:** The authors use persuasive essays as their dataset. It is possible that multiple essays address similar arguments or topics, potentially leading to data leakage between the training and test sets and inflating performance estimates. The paper does not discuss this potential issue.
3. **Paragraph vs. Essay Level Discrepancy:** The significant performance drop when moving from paragraph-level to essay-level evaluation for dependency parsing and LSTM-ER requires further investigation. The authors hypothesize issues with long sequences but a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made at the essay level could provide valuable insights.
4. **Conceptual Clarity:** The phrasing "de-couples them but jointly models them" (lines 455, 477-479) is somewhat ambiguous and could benefit from clearer explanation. A more precise description of how the relation and entity information are treated independently and jointly would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on potential data leakage concerns related to the essay dataset? Did you take any steps to mitigate this issue during data splitting or evaluation?
2.  For the LSTM-ER model, what specific architectural choices or hyperparameters were found to be most critical for achieving good performance on this dataset?
3.  The paper mentions that the global F1 score combines component and relation F1 scores. Is there a weighting applied in this combination, or is it a simple average? If weighted, what is the rationale behind the chosen weights?
4.  Are the F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings directly comparable? Specifically, do the paragraph-level models adequately capture cross-paragraph argumentative relations, or are they limited to within-paragraph structure?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider reordering sections 3 and 4 for improved flow. Presenting the sequence tagging approach before the dependency parsing approach would likely enhance readability.
*   Lines 443-444: Clarify how the tree output of the dependency parsing model can be used to reconstruct the full graph structure.
* The discussion in lines 222-226 regarding losslessly relinking claims to major claims could be expanded with a small example.
