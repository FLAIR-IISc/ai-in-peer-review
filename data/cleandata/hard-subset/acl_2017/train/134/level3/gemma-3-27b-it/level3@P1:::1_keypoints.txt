- Paper studies end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) using neural nets framed as token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, including multi-task learning.  
- Authors argue neural approaches overcome pipeline methods that rely on hand-crafted features and ILP.  
- Empirically, dependency-parsing framing performs poorly compared to sequence tagging.  
- BiLSTM-based sequence tagging models are robust and perform best.  
- Multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks improves performance.  
- Primary contribution: systematic empirical evaluation of neural architectures and task framings for end-to-end AM.  
- Secondary contribution: negative result showing dependency parsing is a suboptimal framing for end-to-end AM.  
- Experiments and dataset usage provide strong baselines for future work.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly motivates methods and results.  
- Experimental setup is thorough, including multiple models, ablations, and paragraph- vs essay-level evaluations for long-range dependency insights.  
- Findings are interesting and impactful for future research directions in AM.  
- Problem addressed is timely with important applications (legal, summarization, education).  
- Major weakness: small dataset (~320 train, ~80 test) raises generalization and overfitting concerns.  
- Missing details: development set size and absence of training/development learning curves to assess overfitting.  
- Results reported as single F1 scores without mean/standard deviation across runs and no statistical significance testing.  
- Regularization methodology is inadequately described and some details are buried in supplementary material.  
- Hyperparameter tuning strategy (search space and optimization) is insufficiently detailed; Bayesian optimization mention needs elaboration.  
- Several important details (pre-trained embeddings, comprehensive error analysis, inter-annotator agreement) should be moved from supplementary to main paper.  
- Presentation issue: Figure 1 color/contrast needs improvement for black-and-white readability.  
- Questions for authors: what regularization was used and can you provide learning curves?  
- Questions for authors: report mean/std over multiple runs and provide statistical significance tests.  
- Questions for authors: explain sources of high variance in some settings and strategies to improve stability.  
- Questions for authors: detail hyperparameter tuning choices, search space, and optimization strategy.  
- Additional note: dependency-parsing exploration is valuable but authors should discuss potential for more sophisticated parsing architectures and limitations for real-world noisy data.
