This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore a range of models – dependency parsing, sequence tagging (including multi-task learning), and an existing LSTM-based entity/relation model – and rigorously evaluate them on a persuasive essay dataset. A key finding is that while dependency parsing struggles, sequence tagging and the LSTM-ER model achieve strong performance, surpassing previous ILP-based state-of-the-art results. The multi-task learning setup further boosts sequence tagging performance.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the viability of neural approaches for end-to-end AM, establishing a new strong baseline and showcasing the potential of sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models. This is the primary contribution, providing practical and competitive models for the field.
2. **Comprehensive Comparison of Modeling Choices:** The thorough evaluation of different neural architectures (dependency parsing vs. sequence tagging) and training strategies (multi-task learning) provides valuable insights into which approaches are most effective for AM, and why. The negative result for dependency parsing is particularly noteworthy, challenging prior assumptions.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Dataset Exploration:** The paper highlights important characteristics of the dataset, such as the distribution of relation distances, and how these characteristics influence model performance. The observation about paragraph vs. essay level difficulty is also insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance and State-of-the-Art Results:** The reported F1 scores for sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models, exceeding the ILP baseline, are a significant achievement. This immediately makes the work relevant and impactful to the AM community.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, varying model architectures, training strategies, and evaluation levels (paragraph vs. essay). This demonstrates a rigorous and thoughtful approach to the research problem.
3. **Comprehensive Supplementary Material:** The detailed description of training procedures and hyperparameter optimization in the supplementary material enhances the reproducibility and credibility of the results. This level of detail is highly appreciated.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion regarding the LSTM-ER model's degradation on essay level, linked to the increased number of potential relations, reveals a nuanced understanding of the model’s limitations and provides valuable direction for future work.
5. **Clear Identification of Challenges:** The authors openly acknowledge the potential for data leakage due to topic overlap between training and testing essays, which demonstrates a critical self-awareness and encourages further investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of LSTM-ER:** While achieving strong performance, the LSTM-ER model is largely an out-of-the-box application of existing work. The primary novelty lies in adapting and evaluating it for the AM task rather than developing a fundamentally new model. This reduces the overall impact of the contribution.
2. **Lack of Clarity in “Decoupling” Explanation:** The claim that the LSTM-ER model “decouples” entity and relation information, yet “jointly models them” (lines 455, 477–479) is vague and requires further clarification. It's unclear *how* the decoupling is achieved and what benefits it provides in the context of AM.
3. **Ambiguous Evaluation Comparison:** It's unclear whether F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings are directly comparable, given the differing complexities of the two levels. The paper states paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations, but doesn’t explicitly address how this impacts the F1 score comparison.
4. **Presentation Flow Could Be Improved:** The paper's structure could benefit from reorganization. Swapping the descriptions of sequence tagging and dependency-based approaches (as suggested in lines 443-444) would create a more logical flow, moving from simpler to more complex models.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Data Leakage Mitigation:** Given the acknowledged concern about potential data leakage, what steps were taken to mitigate this issue, or what future analyses could be conducted to confirm or refute its presence?
2. **Clarify Decoupling Mechanism:** Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the LSTM-ER model achieves decoupling of entity and relation information, and how this contributes to its performance? Specifically, what architectural choices support this decoupling?
3. **Paragraph vs. Essay F1 Comparability:** Are the paragraph and essay level F1 scores directly comparable? If not, how should we interpret the performance differences? Could future work explore models specifically designed to capture cross-paragraph relationships?
4. **Tree Reconstruction from Dependency Parsing:** Can you elaborate on how the tree output from the dependency parsing approach (lines 222–226) can be used to reconstruct the full argumentation graph, including handling the special root node for major claims? A more concrete example would be helpful.



**Additional Comments:**

* The notation used for the tagging scheme (Y) is dense and could benefit from a simpler representation in the main text.
* Exploring the impact of different pre-trained word embeddings (beyond those mentioned in the supplementary material) might provide further insights.
* It would be useful to include an ablation study to assess the contribution of individual components of the multi-task learning setup (e.g., the claim vs. relation tasks).
