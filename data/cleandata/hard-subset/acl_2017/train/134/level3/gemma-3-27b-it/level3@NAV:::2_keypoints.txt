- Paper presents a comprehensive study applying neural methods (dependency parsing, sequence tagging with MTL, and LSTM-ER) to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM).  
- Thorough experimental setup with ablation studies and detailed hyperparameter optimization in the supplementary material.  
- Main empirical finding: sequence labeling, especially with multi-task learning, significantly outperforms prior ILP-based SOTA for end-to-end AM.  
- Auxiliary claim detection task provides particularly large benefits when added to sequence labeling.  
- Negative result: framing AM as a dependency parsing problem is ineffective despite intuitive appeal.  
- Adapted LSTM-ER yields a strong baseline, especially at paragraph level, establishing a new benchmark.  
- Strength: systematic comparison of multiple architectures enables nuanced understanding of each approach’s benefits.  
- Strength: comprehensive ablation studies clarify the contributions of components like multi-task learning.  
- Strength: direct comparison to ILP baseline demonstrates clear performance improvements.  
- Evaluation at both paragraph and essay levels highlights challenges in scaling models to longer texts.  
- Weakness: reliance on out-of-the-box LSTM-ER is less novel compared to the sequence-labeling contributions.  
- Major concern: potential data leakage from shared topics/arguments across train and test essays, which may inflate performance.  
- Unclear how dependency parsing tree outputs are used to reconstruct the full argument graph (lines 222–226) — needs clarification.  
- Unclear phrasing and explanation of “de-couples them but jointly models them” regarding relation vs. entity information (lines 455, 477–479) — needs precision.  
- Suggestion: provide F1 comparisons across paragraph and essay settings and discuss whether paragraph-level models are inherently limited in capturing cross-paragraph relations.  
- Questions for authors: clarify data-leakage mitigation, tree-to-graph reconstruction, precise meaning of “de-couples but jointly models,” and statistical significance/implications of paragraph vs essay F1 differences.
