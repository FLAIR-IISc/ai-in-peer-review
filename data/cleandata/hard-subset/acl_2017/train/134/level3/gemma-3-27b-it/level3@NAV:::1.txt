## Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

This paper investigates neural network approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, with a multi-task learning extension. The core motivation – avoiding the pitfalls of pipeline approaches and manual feature engineering – is clearly articulated and compelling. The methods are generally well-explained, though a few details are lacking (see below). The experimental setup is substantial, including comparisons to a strong ILP baseline, and the initial results are intriguing, particularly the finding that dependency parsing performs surprisingly poorly compared to sequence tagging.

The most significant contribution of this work lies in its comprehensive exploration of neural network architectures for end-to-end AM and the insightful observation regarding the ineffectiveness of the dependency parsing approach in this context. While end-to-end AM has been explored previously, this work provides a thorough empirical comparison of different neural framings, moving beyond prior pipeline-based methods. The inclusion of multi-task learning is also a valuable contribution, demonstrating the potential benefits of leveraging auxiliary tasks.

However, the paper suffers from a critical weakness: the extremely small dataset size (approximately 320 training essays and 80 test essays). This raises serious concerns about the generalizability of the results and the potential for overfitting, especially given the large capacity of the BiLSTM models employed. The paper lacks any analysis of overfitting, such as training/development loss curves, and reports only single run results without mean and standard deviation across multiple initializations or statistical significance testing. The omission of regularization details (beyond a brief mention of LSTM-ER in the supplementary material) exacerbates this concern. Furthermore, the hyperparameter tuning method is not thoroughly described.

Several improvements would strengthen this work. The authors should conduct a more thorough overfitting analysis, provide statistics on model stability (mean and standard deviation across runs), and justify their choice of regularization methods (or lack thereof). Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning is recommended. Details of pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis should be moved from the supplementary material to the main paper. Including inter-annotator agreement scores would provide valuable context for interpreting the system’s performance. Finally, the contrast in Figure 1 could be improved for better readability in black and white.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the small dataset size and high model capacity, what steps were taken to mitigate overfitting? Please provide training and development loss curves.
2.  Please report the mean and standard deviation of the results across multiple runs with different random initializations. Include statistical significance tests.
3.  Please provide a detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process. Why was the chosen method selected over alternatives like Bayesian optimization?
4.  Can you elaborate on the choices of regularization techniques employed (or the reasoning behind not employing them)?
