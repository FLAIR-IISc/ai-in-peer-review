This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as both a token-based dependency parsing problem and a sequence tagging problem, with a multitask learning variant. The motivation is clear: to avoid the limitations of pipeline approaches and manual feature engineering inherent in traditional AM systems. The paper’s primary contribution lies in its exploration of end-to-end neural models for AM, demonstrating that sequence tagging, particularly when augmented with multitask learning, outperforms dependency parsing in this context. A secondary contribution is the identification of the ineffectiveness of a token-based dependency parsing framing for end-to-end AM, a counterpoint to previous suggestions. Finally, the study provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of several models on a challenging dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The problem definition, proposed methods, and experimental setup are clearly explained. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the existing AM literature and clearly articulate their motivations.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct substantial experiments, comparing multiple models – dependency parsers, sequence taggers (BLCC), LSTM-ER, and an ILP baseline. The range of models investigated and the thoroughness of the experiments are commendable.
3. **Interesting Results:** The findings—that sequence tagging outperforms dependency parsing and that multitask learning improves performance—are insightful and contribute to a better understanding of how to approach end-to-end AM. The observation regarding the limitations of dependency parsing is particularly valuable.
4. **Well-Motivated Work:** The paper clearly articulates the problems with existing approaches (pipeline errors, feature engineering) and convincingly argues for the potential benefits of end-to-end neural learning. The focus on joint learning and avoiding error propagation is a strong motivation.
5. **Exploration of Multitask Learning:** The investigation into multitask learning, specifically incorporating component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks, is a valuable contribution and reveals potential strategies for improving AM performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Set Size:** The most significant weakness is the extremely small size of the dataset (≈320 training essays, ≈80 test essays). This severely limits the generalizability of the results and raises concerns about overfitting, especially given the capacity of the BiLSTM models employed. The lack of reporting for the development set size exacerbates this concern.
2. **Model Capacity vs. Data Size:** The large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (BLC/BLCC) may be overly complex for the limited training data. This mismatch significantly increases the risk of overfitting and unreliable performance estimates. While the authors demonstrate good performance, the small dataset makes it difficult to confidently attribute this to model effectiveness rather than memorization.
3. **Lack of Overfitting Analysis:** The paper does not provide any analysis of overfitting. Crucially, training and development loss curves are missing. These curves would be essential for assessing whether the models are genuinely learning or simply memorizing the training data.
4. **Insufficient Reporting of Variance:** The results are presented as single point estimates without any indication of variance. Providing mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (with different initializations) is crucial for assessing model stability and the statistical significance of the observed differences. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the results are reproducible or simply due to random chance.
5. **Limited Discussion of Regularization:** The paper omits a detailed discussion of regularization techniques used to prevent overfitting. The supplementary material briefly mentions LSTM-ER, but a proper treatment of regularization methods (e.g., dropout, L1/L2 regularization) is needed.
6. **Suboptimal Hyperparameter Tuning:** The hyperparameter tuning procedure, as described in the supplement, appears inadequate. Recommending Bayesian optimization would be a significant improvement over the current approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Overfitting Analysis:** Please provide training and development loss curves for all models, demonstrating whether overfitting is occurring.
2. **Statistical Significance:**  Report mean and standard deviation of the F1 scores across multiple runs with different random initializations.  Conduct statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) to determine if the observed differences between models are statistically significant.
3. **Regularization Details:** Please provide a comprehensive description of all regularization techniques employed, including specific hyperparameters.
4. **F1 Score Ranges:**  Provide the full range of F1 scores observed across different runs, particularly for the Kiperwasser setting, which exhibits a large range ([0.56, 0.61]). This information is critical for assessing the stability and reproducibility of the results.
5. **Development Set Size:**  What is the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning and model selection?

**Additional Comments:**

* The details of the pre-trained word embeddings should be moved from the supplementary material to the main paper.
* Including inter-annotator agreement scores from the original dataset paper would provide valuable context for interpreting the system performance.
* Figure 1 could be improved by adjusting the color/contrast to ensure better visibility in black-and-white printing.
* A more detailed error analysis, perhaps with specific examples of misclassifications, would be beneficial.
