## Review for ACL 2017 - Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging. The authors explore several model architectures, including variations on BiLSTMs, multi-task learning, and adapt an existing entity-relation model (LSTM-ER). They demonstrate that framing AM as dependency parsing is ineffective, while sequence tagging models perform robustly. Furthermore, multi-task learning improves performance, and their neural models surpass a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline. 

The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, highlighting the effectiveness of sequence tagging approaches. While neural end-to-end AM has been proposed, this work provides a substantial set of experiments comparing various models and demonstrating a significant step forward from prior, feature-based methods. A second contribution lies in the negative result: the finding that framing the problem as dependency parsing, despite its intuitive appeal, does *not* yield strong performance. Finally, the demonstration of performance gains through multi-task learning, particularly with component detection as an auxiliary task, represents a valuable insight into improving AM systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough and systematic evaluation of multiple approaches to end-to-end AM.  The exploration of dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER offers valuable insights into the suitability of different neural architectures for this task. The consistent use of a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and evaluation metric allows for clear comparisons.
2. **Strong Performance:** The reported results demonstrate that the proposed neural models, particularly the sequence tagging approaches and LSTM-ER, outperform the strong ILP baseline. This is a significant advancement, as it eliminates the need for laborious feature engineering and ILP constraint design.  The performance on the paragraph level, in particular, is impressive.
3. **Insightful Negative Result:** The finding that dependency parsing performs poorly is a crucial contribution.  This challenges a potentially natural approach to modeling argumentation structure and directs future research toward more effective architectures. This result is well-supported by the experimental results and a plausible explanation regarding the complexity of long-range dependencies in essay-level text.
4. **Effective Multi-Task Learning:** The successful incorporation of auxiliary tasks (component detection) in a multi-task learning setup provides a practical method for improving performance. The observation that component detection is more beneficial than relation detection as an auxiliary task is a valuable observation.
5. **Thorough Experimental Setup & Reporting:** The paper includes details regarding hyperparameter optimization and provides links to the software used, enhancing reproducibility. The inclusion of both paragraph and essay-level results provides a nuanced understanding of model performance under different conditions.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in LSTM-ER:** While LSTM-ER achieves strong results, its primary contribution appears to be adapting a pre-existing model to the AM task rather than developing a novel architecture. The paper acknowledges this, but it is a significant point to consider when assessing the overall novelty of the work. The gains are largely due to the effectiveness of the existing LSTM-ER, rather than novel modifications specifically for argumentation mining.
2. **Potential Data Leakage:** The essay dataset used (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) consists of student essays responding to controversial topics. There is a concern that arguments and counter-arguments related to specific topics may be present in both the training and test sets, leading to an overestimation of performance. A clearer discussion of this potential issue and any steps taken to mitigate it would be valuable.
3. **Evaluation Metric Sensitivity:** While the F1 score is a standard metric, it may not fully capture the nuances of argumentation structure. Specifically, incorrectly identifying the *type* of a component (premise vs. claim) can have a significant impact on downstream reasoning, which isn't necessarily reflected in the F1 score. Considering additional metrics that assess the structural correctness of identified arguments could provide a more complete evaluation.
4. **Complexity of LSTM-ER & Analysis:** While performing well, the complexity of the LSTM-ER model makes understanding *why* it works well challenging. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of specific error cases made by LSTM-ER, which would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Data Leakage:** Could you elaborate on the potential for topic overlap between essays in the training and test sets and discuss any steps taken to address this issue?
2.  **Paragraph vs. Essay Evaluation:** Are the F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings directly comparable? If paragraph models are trained and evaluated only on paragraph-level data, do they completely miss cross-paragraph relations which are common in essays?
3.  **Clarification of “De-coupling”:** Could you elaborate on what is meant by “de-couples them but jointly models them” (lines 455, 477-479) regarding the relation versus entity information? Is this related to the architecture of LSTM-ER, and how does that architecture achieve the purported de-coupling?
4.  **Reconstructing the Graph:**  Lines 222-226 mention a loss-less re-linking of claims to major claims to create a tree. Can you clarify how this is achieved in practice and provide an example?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider reordering sections 3 and 4 for improved readability. Presenting the sequence tagging approach before dependency parsing would provide a more logical flow.
*   The phrase "we highlight two design decisions" in the dependency parsing section (lines 443-444) feels abrupt. A more transitional sentence would improve clarity.




Overall, this is a strong paper with valuable contributions to the field of computational argumentation mining. The thorough empirical evaluation, insightful negative result regarding dependency parsing, and successful application of multi-task learning make this work a significant step forward. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
