## Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into applying neural network approaches to the end-to-end task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore several modeling choices – dependency parsing, sequence tagging (including multi-task learning), and adaptation of an existing LSTM-ER model – and systematically evaluate their performance. A significant strength is the thoroughness of the experimental setup, including ablation studies and detailed hyperparameter optimization reported in the supplementary material.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a strong empirical demonstration that sequence labeling approaches, particularly when enhanced with multi-task learning, significantly outperform previous state-of-the-art ILP-based methods for end-to-end AM. The finding that incorporating claim detection as an auxiliary task is particularly beneficial is a valuable insight. Secondly, the paper provides a clear negative result: framing AM as a dependency parsing problem is ineffective, despite its intuitive appeal. This is a crucial finding that redirects future research efforts. Finally, the strong baseline performance achieved by the LSTM-ER model, especially at the paragraph level, establishes a new benchmark for the field.

Several strengths support these contributions. The authors evaluate multiple architectures and systematically compare them. The comprehensive ablation studies allow for a nuanced understanding of the benefits of each component (e.g., multi-task learning). The comparison to the established ILP baseline provides a clear performance improvement. The inclusion of both paragraph and essay level evaluation highlights the challenges of scaling to longer texts.

However, the paper has weaknesses. The reliance on the out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model, while achieving impressive results, feels less novel than the contributions of the sequence labeling experiments.  A significant concern is the potential for data leakage. The authors acknowledge the possibility that train and test essays share topics/arguments, potentially inflating performance. This needs to be addressed.  Furthermore, the explanation of how the tree output of dependency parsing can be used to reconstruct the full graph (lines 222-226) is unclear and requires clarification. There's also a need for clarity regarding the meaning of “de-couples them but jointly models them” (lines 455, 477–479) and how the separation of relation versus entity information is achieved. Finally, a comparison of F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings would be beneficial, alongside a discussion of whether paragraph-level models are inherently limited in capturing cross-paragraph relations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential for data leakage between train and test essays and describe any steps taken to mitigate this issue?
2.  Please clarify how the tree structure from the dependency parsing output can be used to reconstruct the full argument graph.
3.  Can you provide a more precise explanation of what is meant by “de-couples them but jointly models them” in the context of relation and entity information?
4.  Is the difference in F1 scores between paragraph and essay levels statistically significant? Do you believe that paragraph models are inherently limited in capturing cross-paragraph relations, and if so, how could this limitation be addressed?
