- Comprehensive evaluation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining, testing dependency parsing, sequence tagging (including MTL), and LSTM-ER on a persuasive essay dataset.  
- Key finding: dependency parsing performs poorly, while sequence tagging and LSTM-ER outperform prior ILP-based state of the art; multi-task learning further improves sequence tagging.  

Main contributions:
- Establishes strong neural baselines for end-to-end AM, showing sequence tagging and LSTM-ER are viable and competitive.  
- Provides a thorough comparison of modeling choices and training strategies, highlighting why certain approaches (e.g., dependency parsing) fail.  
- Offers detailed dataset analysis (e.g., relation distance distributions) and shows paragraph vs. essay level differences affecting performance.  

Strengths:
- Achieves state-of-the-art F1 scores for sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models.  
- Rigorous experimental setup covering architectures, training strategies, and evaluation granularities.  
- Comprehensive supplementary material detailing training and hyperparameter tuning for reproducibility.  
- Insightful error analysis linking LSTM-ER degradation at essay level to increased potential relations.  
- Transparently identifies potential data leakage due to topic overlap between train and test sets.  

Weaknesses:
- LSTM-ER application is largely an adaptation of existing models with limited methodological novelty.  
- The paper’s claim about “decoupling” entity and relation information in LSTM-ER is vague and needs clarification.  
- Unclear whether paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores are directly comparable given differing complexities.  
- Paper structure and presentation flow could be improved (e.g., reorder model descriptions).  

Questions to authors:
- What steps were taken to mitigate potential data leakage, or what future analyses could confirm/refute its presence?  
- Can you clarify how LSTM-ER decouples entity and relation information and which architectural choices enable this?  
- Are paragraph and essay F1 scores directly comparable, and how should differences be interpreted or addressed in future work?  
- How can the dependency parser’s tree outputs be concretely used to reconstruct full argumentation graphs (including special root handling)?  

Additional comments:
- The tagging scheme notation (Y) is dense and would benefit from simplification in the main text.  
- Exploring additional pre-trained embeddings could yield further insights.  
- An ablation study of multi-task learning components (e.g., claim vs. relation tasks) would be useful.
