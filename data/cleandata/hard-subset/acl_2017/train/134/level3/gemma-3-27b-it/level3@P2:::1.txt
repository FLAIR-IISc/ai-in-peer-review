This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, with a multi-task learning setup. The authors demonstrate that framing AM as dependency parsing performs sub-optimally, while simpler tagging models achieve robust performance, and multi-task learning improves results.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of neural network architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining, moving beyond traditional feature-engineered and pipeline approaches. Specifically, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sequence tagging models for this task, outperforming dependency parsing approaches. A secondary contribution is the finding that multi-task learning, incorporating auxiliary tasks related to component detection, boosts performance. While not a novel architectural contribution, applying these techniques specifically to the end-to-end AM problem and providing a comparative analysis is valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of prior work in AM, specifically the reliance on manual feature engineering and the error propagation inherent in pipeline architectures. The motivation for exploring end-to-end neural approaches is well-justified.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform a substantial experimental evaluation, comparing several different neural network architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning) and a strong baseline (ILP model). This thoroughness provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. **Interesting and Counterintuitive Findings:** The finding that dependency parsing performs poorly for end-to-end AM, despite its theoretical appeal, is a significant and surprising result. This highlights the challenges of applying traditional parsing techniques to this complex task. The success of the simpler tagging models is also noteworthy.
4. **Well-Written and Accessible:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand, even for readers unfamiliar with the specifics of argumentation mining. The explanations of the different models are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Size:** The most significant weakness is the small size of the dataset (approximately 320 training essays and 80 test essays). This severely limits the ability to train and evaluate large-capacity models like BiLSTMs effectively, raising concerns about overfitting and generalization. The lack of a dedicated development set further exacerbates this issue.
2. **Lack of Overfitting Analysis:** The paper does not provide any analysis of overfitting, such as training and development loss curves. Without this information, it's difficult to assess whether the reported results are truly representative of the models' performance or simply due to memorization of the training data.
3. **Insufficient Reporting of Statistical Significance:** The results are presented without sufficient statistical analysis. Reporting mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different initializations is crucial to assess the stability of the models and the statistical significance of the observed differences. The lack of significance testing makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the relative performance of the different approaches.
4. **Limited Discussion of Regularization:** The paper mentions regularization briefly in the supplementary material, but it lacks a detailed discussion of the regularization techniques used and their impact on performance. Given the small dataset size, appropriate regularization is critical to prevent overfitting.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The description of hyperparameter tuning in the supplementary material is insufficient. Recommending Bayesian optimization is a good suggestion, but further details on the search space and optimization process would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide training and development loss curves for the different models to demonstrate the absence of overfitting?
2.  Please report the mean and standard deviation of the results across multiple runs with different random initializations, along with statistical significance tests.
3.  What specific regularization techniques were used, and how were the regularization hyperparameters tuned?
4.  Could you elaborate on the dataset's inter-annotator agreement scores to contextualize the observed performance levels?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the error types made by the different models, beyond what is presented in the supplementary material?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Improve the color/contrast of Figure 1 for better readability in black-and-white printing.
*   Move the details on pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary material to the main paper.
*   Please report F1-score ranges and means with variance, especially given the high variance observed in some results (e.g., Kiperwasser’s setting of F1 in [0.56, 0.61]).



Overall, this paper presents a valuable exploration of neural end-to-end argumentation mining. However, the small dataset size and the lack of rigorous analysis raise concerns about the generalizability and reliability of the results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions contingent upon addressing the questions and concerns raised.
