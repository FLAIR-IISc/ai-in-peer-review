Summary
This paper studies end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods on the token level. It compares: (i) a token-based dependency parsing framing; (ii) a token-based sequence-tagging framing that jointly encodes component BIO/type, relation distance, and stance; and (iii) a multi-task learning (MTL) variant that adds “natural subtasks” (component-only and relation-only). The paper also evaluates LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) and a strong ILP baseline (Stab & Gurevych). On the persuasive essays corpus, token-level dependency parsing underperforms, while BiLSTM taggers are robust and MTL gives consistent gains. LSTM-ER excels on component detection but degrades on relation identification for longer (essay-level) sequences. Overall, the neural taggers set new SOTA and avoid hand-engineered features/ILP constraints.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by strength)
1) A strong empirical demonstration that end-to-end neural sequence tagging with a compact joint label (BIO, component type, distance, stance) is effective for AM, and that MTL with component-only and relation-only auxiliaries improves stability and accuracy.
2) A thorough comparative study across modeling choices (sequence tagging vs token-level dependency parsing vs LSTM-ER vs ILP) and document granularity (paragraph vs essay), yielding actionable guidance: local taggers are robust and scalable; general relation models need stronger inductive bias for long documents.
3) A negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, especially on essay-level sequences, despite AM’s tree-like structure.

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation: The paper clearly argues for avoiding hand-crafted features and ILP constraints and for reducing pipeline error propagation via joint learning. The token-level perspective is well-justified for end-to-end AM.
- Well-written and mostly clear: Model framings, tagging scheme (including distance encoding), and task variants are explained accessibly; results are easy to follow.
- Substantive experimental comparison: Multiple families of models, both paragraph- and essay-level settings, strong baselines, and standardized metrics (component and relation F1 at 100%/50%). The analyses around sequence length, distance distributions, and the constrained search space are insightful.
- Interesting and useful findings: (a) BLCC taggers handle long-range dependencies and outperform token-level parsers; (b) MTL with component/relationship auxiliaries improves the joint task; (c) LSTM-ER is strong on entities but needs stronger bias for relations in long documents; (d) the distance-encoded label is an effective inductive bias.
- Practical implications: The paper offers concrete guidance on when to prefer local tagging models versus more general entity–relation architectures.

Weaknesses
- Data scale and splits (primary concern): The dataset is very small (≈322 training essays; 80 test), with severe label sparsity for relation tags. The development set size/split is not reported. This raises concerns about robustness, tuning bias, and generalization.
- Overfitting and stability not analyzed: The work reports single-run numbers (with one per-document Wilcoxon test) but does not show training/dev curves, early-stopping behavior, or mean/std across seeds. Given small data and large-capacity models (BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN)), high variance is likely (e.g., Kiperwasser setting cited F1 in [0.56, 0.61]); without ranges, it’s hard to assess stability and significance.
- Model–data capacity mismatch and regularization: The paper deploys high-capacity (B)LSTM taggers on few thousand training instances without a clear regularization story. Dropout, weight decay, gradient clipping, early stopping, and data augmentation are not discussed in the main paper; the supplement only briefly mentions LSTM-ER. This omission undermines reproducibility and interpretation of gains.
- Hyperparameter tuning methodology: The supplement’s tuning approach is underspecified and likely suboptimal for the small-data/high-variance regime. A principled method (e.g., Bayesian optimization) with fixed budgets and nested validation would be preferable; report search space, trials, and selection criteria.
- Reporting and context gaps: Key details (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis) are relegated to the supplement and should be in the main paper. The paper should also report inter-annotator agreement (from the dataset paper) to contextualize ceilings for component and relation F1, and provide parameter counts to help judge capacity relative to data.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Data and splits: What is the exact development set size and selection procedure? Are any essays reused across tuning and testing? Please clarify paragraph vs essay-level splitting and whether paragraph experiments use only train paragraphs from train essays.
- Regularization and training: What dropout rates, weight decay, gradient clipping, early-stopping criteria, and patience were used for each model? Please include training and dev loss/F1 curves to assess overfitting.
- Stability and significance: For each setting, report results over at least 5–10 random seeds (different initializations and data shuffles), with mean, standard deviation, and 95% CIs. Provide F1 ranges; for example, you mention high variance for the Kiperwasser setting (F1 in [0.56, 0.61])—please systematically report such ranges for all major models (BLCC/BL, LSTM-ER, LSTM-Parser) on both C-F1 and R-F1 at 100%/50%. Re-run significance tests using mean-over-seeds or paired bootstrap across instances seeded runs.
- Hyperparameter tuning: Detail the search spaces, budgets, stopping criteria, and selection metrics. Would you consider a Bayesian optimization baseline and report its outcomes? How sensitive are results to embedding dimension, LSTM width/depth, CRF transitions, and CNN filter choices?
- Model capacity: Please report parameter counts for BL/BLCC/BLC, LSTM-ER, and LSTM-Parser. Did you try smaller models (e.g., fewer layers, narrower LSTMs) and do they close the gap given the small data?
- Embeddings and preprocessing: Move pre-trained embedding details (source, domain match, fine-tuning policy, OOV handling) to the main paper. How much do gains depend on pre-training? Provide ablations without pre-trained embeddings.
- Annotation quality: Include inter-annotator agreement from Stab & Gurevych to contextualize attainable ceilings for component and relation F1, and discuss where system performance sits relative to IAA.
- Presentation: Please improve Figure 1 color/contrast for black-and-white printing and color-vision accessibility. Consider moving the most informative error analysis examples from the supplement into the main paper.

Additional comments
- Consider reporting per-document-length performance and per-distance breakdowns to substantiate the claimed inductive bias benefits.
- Discuss to what extent a component-level parsing framing (rather than token-level) could alleviate search space and data sparsity.
