- Paper compares four end-to-end neural AM framings (token-level dependency parsing, composite token tagging, multi-task tagging, LSTM-ER) on the PE corpus.  
- Main empirical finding: distance-aware BiLSTM token tagging consistently outperforms token-level dependency parsing and matches/exceeds a strong ILP pipeline across paragraph and essay levels.  
- Multi-task learning that jointly predicts natural subtasks (components and relation variables) yields consistent improvements over single-task tagging.  
- LSTM-ER is competitive on short texts (paragraphs) but degrades on longer texts (essays) due to many candidate entity pairs and long-range dependencies.  
- Several neural models beat a feature-based ILP pipeline while removing the need for hand-crafted features/constraints.  
- Contribution: careful comparative study showing constrained local tagging with distance encoding is robust for end-to-end AM.  
- Contribution: actionable evidence that multi-task setups help practical neural AM without manual constraints.  
- Contribution: empirical guidance that general relation models suit short texts, constrained distance-aware tagging suits long texts.  
- Strength: clear framing and motivation linking modeling choices to AM data properties (tree-like structures, distance distributions).  
- Strength: substantive and relevant comparisons across model families and granularity levels.  
- Strength: strong results and analyses explaining why models perform differently (distance distributions, candidate-pair growth).  
- Strength: good writing and clear explanation of models, label encodings, and evaluation metrics.  
- Strength: practical impact by reducing reliance on hand-engineering and providing guidance for practitioners.  
- Weakness: limited data size (~402 essays, ~1.8k training paragraphs) and missing clear development split/protocol.  
- Weakness: stability and variance underreported—results appear single-run; need mean±std over multiple seeds.  
- Weakness: insufficient regularization/optimization details (dropout, L2, gradient clipping, early stopping, embedding tuning).  
- Weakness: MTL backbone is weaker (no CRF, no char-CNN), making MTL gains hard to compare to stronger BLCC baseline.  
- Weakness: evaluation only on PE limits generalization; should test at least one additional AM dataset or controlled analyses.  
- Weakness: composite distance-aware label space may be sparse/large; missing counts of label vocabulary, rare labels, and bucketing decisions.  
- Question: what dev split was used (essays vs paragraphs), was it fixed across models, and what metric drove early stopping?  
- Question: how many random seeds per configuration—please report mean±std for C-F1 and R-F1 and the seeds used.  
- Question: what regularization was used per model, and were embeddings fine-tuned or frozen?  
- Question: for STagT, what is the effective label vocabulary size, did you cap/bucket distances, and how do results change with bucketing?  
- Question: can you run MTL with a BLCC backbone (CRF+char-CNN) to fairly compare against single-task BLCC?  
- Question: please include inter-annotator agreement from the dataset to contextualize absolute F1.  
- Request: add learning curves (varying training size) and a concise error analysis in the main paper.  
- Additional suggestion: consider Bayesian hyperparameter search and report search ranges and final hyperparameters in the main text.  
- Additional suggestion: unify paragraph and essay comparisons or justify omissions due to resource limits.  
- Additional suggestion: improve Figure 1 for grayscale printing and move key reproducibility details (preprocessing, embeddings, training schedule, code URL) from supplement to main text.
