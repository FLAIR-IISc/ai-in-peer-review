Summary
This paper studies neural end-to-end argumentation mining at token level, comparing (i) several dependency parsers, (ii) BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagging with distance/stance encoding, (iii) multi-task variants on tagging, (iv) an out-of-the-box LSTM-ER entity–relation model, against ILP-based state-of-the-art. Evaluation uses component and relation F1 (exact/approximate), at paragraph and essay levels. Main findings: dependency parsing performs poorly for token-level AM; sequence tagging is robust and strong; LSTM-ER is very strong at paragraph level; both tagging and LSTM-ER outperform ILP; multi-task learning improves tagging, with the claim task more helpful than the relation task. The supplement thoroughly documents training and hyperparameters.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A rigorous empirical comparison that establishes strong neural baselines for end-to-end AM and yields actionable insights about modeling choices across document granularities.
2) Demonstration that multi-task supervision with component (claim/premise) tagging substantially boosts sequence tagging, clarifying the value of decoupled-yet-joint training.
3) A clear negative result showing token-level dependency parsing is an ill-suited framing for end-to-end AM under current data sizes.

Strengths
- Comprehensive experimental design: multiple architectures, two evaluation granularities (paragraph vs essay), and exact/approx F1 for both entities and relations, enabling nuanced conclusions.
- Strong, consistent gains over the ILP state-of-the-art for both tagging and LSTM-ER, especially at paragraph level, indicating practical impact.
- Careful analysis of why models behave differently with long sequences (e.g., combinatorics of relation candidates for LSTM-ER; distance encoding benefits for taggers).
- Multi-task learning results are informative: auxiliary claim/entity supervision helps more than relation supervision, guiding future modeling.
- Excellent supplement detailing optimization and training, supporting reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: one of the best-performing models (LSTM-ER) is an out-of-the-box application; the core advances are empirical rather than methodological.
- Potential data leakage: if train/test essays share topics or argument templates, reported F1 may be inflated; the split protocol and topic overlap are not clearly controlled.
- Comparability of paragraph vs essay F1 is unclear; paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations, complicating conclusions about overall end-to-end capability.
- Conceptual clarity: “de-couples them but jointly models them” and the asserted decoupling of relation vs entity information (lines 455, 477–479) need precise definitions and empirical backing.
- Presentation: the dependency-based section precedes sequence tagging; swapping them would improve narrative flow. Also, clarify how a tree output can reconstruct the full graph (lines 222–226).

Questions to Authors
- How is topic/prompt overlap handled between train and test? Any measures to assess/cap leakage?
- Are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores comparable in any controlled way? Do paragraph models ignore cross-paragraph relations, and how prevalent are these?
- Please detail the procedure that converts tree outputs to full argument graphs; what guarantees “loss-less” reconstruction?
- What exactly is meant by “de-couples them but jointly models them”? Can you provide ablations showing gains from decoupling vs fully joint tagging?
- How sensitive are tagging results to the distance-encoding design (e.g., clipping, bucketing)?
