Summary
The paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus. It evaluates several framings: token-level dependency parsing; token-level sequence tagging (with a novel label space that jointly encodes component boundaries/types, relation distance, and stance); multi-task learning (MTL) variants of sequence tagging; an entity–relation neural model (LSTM-ER); and prior ILP-based joint inference. Evaluation is by F1 for components (C-F1) and relations (R-F1), at exact and 50% overlap. Results show: dependency parsing performs poorly; sequence labeling is strong; out-of-the-box LSTM-ER is particularly strong at the paragraph level; both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baseline; and MTL brings substantial gains, with the component (claim/premise/MC) task contributing more than the relation task. The supplement is detailed and helpful.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM, establishing that simple sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform prior ILP and that dependency parsing is ineffective at the token level.
2) A practical, effective sequence tagging formulation that directly encodes relation distance and stance in the label set, and an MTL setup demonstrating consistent gains (especially from the component-typing auxiliary task).
3) Clear analysis of document-length effects (paragraph vs essay), highlighting when more general ER modeling helps (shorter texts) vs when stricter sequence constraints help (longer texts).

Strengths
- Strong empirical study with clear takeaways. The paper evaluates diverse modeling paradigms under a consistent metric, yielding robust conclusions: token-level dependency parsing underperforms; sequence tagging is effective; LSTM-ER excels for paragraphs. These are actionable insights for the community, beyond any single model’s performance.
- Simple methods work well and beat a competitive ILP baseline. BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging and LSTM-ER both outperform the state-of-the-art ILP; MTL yields further gains. The evidence includes significant improvements in both C-F1 and R-F1, with careful attention to exact vs approximate matching.
- Useful negative result on dependency parsing. The study convincingly shows that token-level transition/graph-based parsers—even strong neural ones—struggle, especially at essay level. This clarifies a common assumption that “argument = tree ⇒ parsing,” and will likely save future effort.
- Insightful analysis of length and search space. The comparison of paragraph vs essay behavior (including the O(m^2) relation candidate growth for LSTM-ER) provides a cogent rationale for when to prefer constrained local models over more general ER models.
- Comprehensive supplemental material. Details on training, embeddings, and hyperparameter optimization increase reproducibility and credibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. Most architectures are adaptations of prior work (BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN), MTL protocol, LSTM-ER). The primary novelty lies in the systematic comparison and the sequence label design (distance/stance encoding), rather than in new algorithms.
- Potential data leakage and topic effects. The dataset consists of essays written to prompts; if train/test share topics or similar arguments, performance may be inflated. The paper does not quantify topic overlap or control for prompt leakage. Given paragraph-level training/testing, leakage at the topic level could particularly benefit local models.
- Comparability across paragraph vs essay settings. Scores are presented for both levels, but it is unclear whether they are directly comparable; paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations (except claim–MC), and dependency parsers were trained only at paragraph level due to memory. This complicates fairness in cross-model comparisons.
- Clarity on graph reconstruction from trees. The text (lines 222–226) states the output can be “loss-lessly” transformed by re-linking claims to one major claim and adding a root. It is unclear how this preserves all original claim–MC relations and whether the evaluation reconstructs the full graph consistently across models.
- Ambiguity in “de-couples them but jointly models them.” The discussion (lines 455, 477–479) claims de-coupling of entity and relation information while “jointly modeling” them. The intended meaning (e.g., separate modules with shared parameters and joint loss) should be made explicit, and the extent to which relation modeling is decoupled from entity features clarified.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Data split and topic leakage: Are essay prompts/topics disjoint between train/dev/test? If not, what proportion overlaps? Did you control for topic during splitting or report topic-stratified performance? Please also confirm that no paragraphs from the same essay appear across splits.
- Paragraph vs essay comparability: Are C-F1/R-F1 across paragraph and essay settings intended to be compared directly? Do paragraph-level models miss any cross-paragraph relations (beyond claims linking to all MCs)? If so, how is this accounted for in evaluation?
- Tree-to-graph reconstruction (lines 222–226): Please detail the exact reconstruction procedure from a tree to the full AM graph. How are multiple claim–MC links recovered from a single re-linked edge? Is evaluation performed on the reconstructed full graph, and is this reconstruction applied consistently to all models?
- “De-couples them but jointly models them” (lines 455, 477–479): Concretely, which parameters are shared between entity and relation components, what is the training schedule (e.g., pretrain entities, then joint finetune), and how does this constitute “decoupling” in your taxonomy relative to STagT with MTL?
- Distance label space: What is the maximum |d| allowed in training/inference? If capped, how are out-of-range distances handled? Can you report performance sensitivity to the distance cap?
- Fairness across settings: Some parsers were only trained at paragraph level due to memory limits. Could you provide essay-level results for at least one graph-based and one transition-based parser with reasonable memory optimizations, or clarify why this comparison is infeasible?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Reorder for clarity: Consider presenting sequence tagging before dependency-based approaches (lines 443–444), since tagging is ultimately more successful and conceptually simpler; this would improve narrative flow.
- Clarify evaluation: Explicitly state whether “global F1” is the harmonic mean of C-F1 and R-F1 or computed at the instance level. Include per-class breakdowns (MC/Claim/Premise) in the supplement if available.
- Context on LSTM-ER usage: Acknowledge that the strongest paragraph-level model is largely an out-of-the-box application; emphasize your empirical insights and error analyses as the core contribution.
- Commendation: The supplementary material is exemplary; retaining those training and hyperparameter details is important for reproducibility.
