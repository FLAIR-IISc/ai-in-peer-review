Summary
The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural models. It compares four framings on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) token-level sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO, component type, link distance, and stance; (iii) a multi-task tagging variant where “natural” subtasks (component segmentation/typing and relation variables) are auxiliary objectives; and (iv) LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) that decouples entity and relation prediction. Experiments at both paragraph and essay granularity show that simple BiLSTM tagging is consistently strong, multi-tasking helps, dependency parsing underperforms in this end-to-end token setting, and LSTM-ER is competitive at paragraph level but degrades at essay level. Several models outperform a strong feature-based ILP pipeline while avoiding manual features/constraints.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by strength)
1) A careful comparative study establishing that a constrained, local BiLSTM tagging formulation with explicit distance encoding robustly outperforms token-level dependency parsing and matches/exceeds an ILP pipeline for end-to-end AM; and that gains persist across paragraph/essay settings.
2) Evidence that jointly learning “natural” subtasks in a multi-task setup improves performance over single-task tagging, offering practical guidance for neural AM without hand-crafted constraints.
3) Actionable empirical insight on modeling choices: more general relation frameworks (e.g., LSTM-ER) are better on short texts (paragraphs), while more constrained formulations (distance-aware tagging) fare better on long texts (essays) with many candidate entity pairs.

Strengths
- Clear framing and motivation: The paper articulates why neural, joint learning is preferable to feature-engineered pipelines with ILP constraints, and connects modeling choices to properties of AM data (tree-like structures, long-range dependencies, distance distributions).
- Substantive and relevant comparisons: Multiple families are compared (parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER, and ILP) on the same corpus, and at both paragraph and essay levels. The finding that token-level dependency parsing degrades, while tagging remains robust, is both non-obvious and useful to the community.
- Strong results with analyses: The distance-augmented tagging scheme is simple and effective; the multi-task setup leveraging component and relation projections yields consistent gains; and the analysis relating performance to distance distributions and candidate-pair growth convincingly explains why LSTM-ER struggles on essays.
- Writing and clarity: The paper is well-structured; model choices, label encodings, and evaluation metrics (exact vs approximate matching) are explained cleanly. Significance testing across documents is reported for table highlights.
- Practical impact: The work reduces reliance on hand-engineered features/constraints, which is important given theory- and dataset-dependent variation in AM. The guidance on when to prefer constrained vs general neural framings is actionable for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited data and missing development protocol: The dataset is small (≈402 essays; ≈1.8k training paragraphs). The paper does not clearly state the development split (size, essays vs paragraphs, whether fixed across models). Without a documented dev protocol, it is hard to assess the risk of overfitting and hyperparameter selection bias.
- Stability and variance underreported: Results appear to be from single runs per configuration. Given known sensitivity of (Bi)LSTM-CRF/CNN models to initialization and data order, mean±std across multiple seeds (e.g., 5–10) should be reported for C-F1 and R-F1 (100/50). Some settings already show large variability (e.g., essay-level parsing); without variance, robustness is unclear.
- Regularization and optimization details insufficient: The paper lacks a systematic treatment of regularization (dropout rates per layer, L2/weight decay, gradient clipping), early stopping criteria, embedding fine-tuning/freezing, and learning-rate schedules. These choices critically affect generalization on small data and should be in the main paper (not only partially in the supplement).
- Comparability of MTL results: The MTL backbone is a weaker BL tagger (no CRF, no char-CNN), making it hard to assess whether multi-tasking would also improve the stronger BLCC baseline. As-is, the MTL gains are relative to a less competitive single-task baseline, which blurs the take-away.
- Generalization beyond PE: All results are on a single corpus with special properties (paragraph-contained structures, tree-like constraints). The claims about modeling choices could be further strengthened by evaluating on at least one additional AM dataset (e.g., microtexts), or by providing leave-one-topic/paragraph-length-controlled analyses.
- Label sparsity in distance-aware tagging: The composite label space can be large due to distances; while the paper argues most distances are small, an explicit accounting of label vocabulary size, rare/zero-shot labels, and the impact of distance bucketing/cutoffs is missing.

Questions to authors
- What development split did you use (essays and paragraphs)? Was it fixed across all models? What metric drove early stopping?
- How many random seeds did you run per configuration? Please provide mean±std for C-F1 and R-F1 (100/50) and report seeds to aid replication.
- What regularization was used per model (dropout per layer, L2, gradient clipping)? Were word embeddings (type, dimension) fine-tuned or frozen?
- For STagT, what is the effective label vocabulary size in train/test? Did you cap or bucket distances d, and how do results change with bucketing?
- Can you run MTL with a BLCC backbone (CRF+char-CNN) to make the comparison to single-task BLCC fair?
- Please include inter-annotator agreement numbers from the dataset paper to contextualize absolute F1.
- Can you add learning curves (varying training size) and a concise error analysis in the main paper?

Additional comments
- Consider Bayesian hyperparameter optimization and report search ranges; explicitly list the final hyperparameters per model in the main paper.
- Unify comparisons where possible (report both paragraph and essay results for each model, or clearly justify omissions due to memory).
- Improve Figure 1 for grayscale printing (contrast/line styles).
- Move key reproducibility details (preprocessing, embeddings, training schedule, code URL) from the supplement into the main text.
