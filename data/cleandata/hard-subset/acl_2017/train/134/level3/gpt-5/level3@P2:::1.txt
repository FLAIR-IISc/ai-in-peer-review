Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM) from raw text, contrasting token-level dependency parsing with token-level sequence tagging and a multi-task learning (MTL) variant, and also adapting a joint entity–relation model (LSTM-ER). The work targets eliminating hand-crafted features and pipeline error propagation common in prior ILP-based systems, and studies performance at both paragraph and essay levels on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays dataset.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding that simple, local BiLSTM-based sequence tagging with an augmented label space (encoding component boundaries, types, and relation distances/stances) is robust and outperforms token-level dependency parsing and a strong ILP baseline on several settings; and that MTL with “natural” auxiliary tasks further improves performance.
2) A careful comparative study that clarifies when more general models help: LSTM-ER excels for components (and on shorter texts) but degrades on relation F1 for essays; token-level dependency parsing is subpar for end-to-end AM relative to tagging.
3) An end-to-end neural framing for AM that avoids hand-engineered features/constraints and provides state-of-the-art or competitive results on the PE dataset.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation. The paper convincingly motivates neural end-to-end AM as an alternative to feature-heavy pipelines with ILP post-processing, emphasizing joint learning and reduced manual effort.
- Thorough, relevant comparisons. The study spans multiple modeling paradigms (token parsing, tagging with distance-encoding, MTL tagging, joint entity–relation) and compares paragraph vs. essay settings, illuminating where each approach helps or fails.
- Interesting empirical insights. Key findings—tagging > token-level parsing; joint modeling without hard global constraints can still reflect structural constraints; MTL with component/relational subtasks helps—are useful to the community and likely generalize to related structured IE tasks.
- Generally strong clarity and presentation. The paper is well written, methods are mostly well specified, and the qualitative interpretation of results (e.g., distance distributions, long-range dependencies) is thoughtful.
- Proper baseline and metrics. The ILP baseline is appropriate and strong. The evaluation metric (component/relation F1 at 100%/50%) follows prior work, enabling comparability.

Weaknesses
- Small data and stability concerns. With ~322 train essays and a large tag space (BIO × component type × distance × stance), data sparsity is acute. Large-capacity (Bi)LSTM(CR F/CNN) models may overfit. The paper does not report development split size, learning curves, or multi-seed variability. Given known variance in neural sequence models, results should include mean±std over multiple random seeds and ranges across hyperparameter settings; otherwise, robustness is unclear.
- Incomplete regularization and tuning details in main text. Regularization (dropout rates/locations, weight decay, gradient clipping, early stopping criteria), hyperparameter search budget/strategy, and embedding choices are relegated to the supplement and are too brief (especially for LSTM-ER). Given the small dataset, these choices critically affect outcomes. Bayesian or at least structured tuning, with reporting of budgets and candidate ranges, would strengthen claims.
- Token-level dependency parsing may be a weak strawman. The negative result on token-level parsing is valuable but may not generalize to component-level parsing, which is the granularity most discourse/AM parsers assume. Without a component-level parsing comparison (or a discussion/experiment showing why token vs. component granularity matters), the conclusion “parsing is subpar” should be tempered.
- Limited external validation. Results are confined to one dataset/domain. Given dataset-specific structure (e.g., paragraph-internal relations, major claims at boundaries), it is hard to assess generalization. Even a small-scale transfer or cross-validation would help.
- Reporting gaps and reproducibility. Key experimental details should be in the main paper: precise development split, pre-trained embeddings (type, vocabulary coverage, fine-tuning), number of training epochs/early stopping rules, computational budget, and error analysis. Also, include inter-annotator agreement from the dataset paper to contextualize achievable F1.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in the response)
- Data splits and tuning:
  - What is the development set size and selection protocol? Did you use any cross-validation?
  - How many epochs were trained, what early stopping criterion was used, and was it based on dev C-F1, R-F1, or the global F1?
- Stability and significance:
  - For each model (especially BLCC, BL, LSTM-ER, and Kiperwasser), please report mean±std over at least 5 random seeds and the range of F1 scores (component and relation, 100/50). If there are settings where F1 varies widely (e.g., 0.56–0.61), please elaborate.
  - Are the significance tests in Table 1 computed over documents for a single seed, or averaged across seeds?
- Regularization and hyperparameters:
  - Specify dropout placement and rates (embedding, LSTM inputs/outputs, recurrent dropout?), L2 weight decay, gradient clipping thresholds, learning rate schedule, batch sizes, and weight initialization for all models. For LSTM-ER, please provide the same level of detail.
  - How large is the effective tag set Y in training (unique (b,t,d,s)), and do you clip distance d (e.g., to ±K with an “other” bucket)? Any class-imbalance handling?
- Modeling choices:
  - How is the “each premise links to exactly one target” constraint enforced at inference in the tagging model? Does decoding ever produce invalid structures, and if so, how are they resolved?
  - Did you try component-level parsing/tagging pipelines (detect components, then parse relations) to disentangle token-level errors from parsing ability?
  - For MTL, how sensitive are gains to which layers the auxiliary tasks feed from? Please include an ablation of YC vs. YR and their combination across layers.
- Generalization:
  - Have you tried your best model on the microtexts corpus or any other AM dataset (even if partially mismatched) to assess portability?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Move essential experimental details (pre-trained embeddings and their handling, dev split, regularization, main error typology) from the supplement into the main paper.
- Add inter-annotator agreement numbers from Stab & Gurevych (2016) to contextualize system performance and headroom.
- Provide learning curves (train vs. dev) for main models to assess overfitting.
- Consider more systematic hyperparameter search (e.g., Bayesian optimization) and report search budget.
- Include ablations on the tagging label space: distance bucketing (±1/±2/other), removing stance from tags, and analyzing their effects on C-F1/R-F1.
- For fairness, report compute budgets and memory constraints that motivated paragraph-level training for parsers, and consider a component-level parsing baseline.
- Improve Figure 1 for grayscale/print (contrast, line styles) to ensure clarity.
- Post-response request: please include mean, std, and ranges of F1 across seeds and settings to facilitate replication and to assess stability of the findings. Code, configs, and random seeds would be valuable for reproducibility.
