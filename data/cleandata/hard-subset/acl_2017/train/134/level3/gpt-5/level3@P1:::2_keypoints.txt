Summary:
- Evaluates end-to-end neural approaches to argument mining via token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task tagging, and an adapted LSTM-ER on the Persuasive Essays corpus.  
- Finds token-level parsing underperforms, BiLSTM tagging is robust, LSTM-ER best on components/paragraph-level, multi-tasking helps, and neural systems rival or beat ILP baselines.

Main contributions:
- Thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM across paragraph and essay granularities, highlighting what works and what fails.  
- Proposes a simple sequence-tagging formulation that encodes relation distance in labels to capture long-range relations effectively.  
- Shows multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection/typing and relation distance/stance) consistently improves end-to-end tagging.

Strengths:
- Strong, consistent empirical results with clear quantitative comparisons across models and granularities.  
- Convincing negative finding that token-level dependency parsing is ill-suited for end-to-end AM with explanations for why.  
- Effective, simple tagging design that leverages distance-labeled tags to model long-range dependencies.  
- Multi-task learning yields consistent gains, especially from the component subtask.  
- Reproducible setup with standard metrics, detailed experimental details, and code/hyperparameter links.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty; main advances are comparative study and task-specific label design rather than new algorithms.  
- Evaluation restricted to a single dataset/domain (PE corpus), leaving generalization to other AM corpora untested.  
- Possible topic/prompt leakage or stratification concerns not addressed, risking inflated performance.  
- Unclear comparability between paragraph- and essay-level evaluations and treatment of cross-paragraph relations.  
- Ambiguity in descriptions of “decoupling but joint modeling” and details of tree-to-graph reconstruction that could affect interpretation.

Questions to authors:
- Are prompts/topics disjoint across train/dev/test, and can you report topic-held-out performance to rule out leakage?  
- How are cross-paragraph relations handled in paragraph-level evaluation, how often do they occur, and how do they affect F1?  
- What maximum relation distance |d| is used, and how sensitive are results to capping, rebinning, or class imbalance over distances?  
- Did you try negatives sampling, distance-windowing, or a learned distance prior to reduce LSTM-ER’s O(m^2) candidate space, and with what effect on R-F1?  
- Did you try component-level parsing (predict spans first, then parse components) to test whether token-level parsing’s failure is due to tree complexity?  
- Can you provide a clear ablation of MTL layer-sharing choices and whether combining CRF label dependency modeling with MTL closes the gap to BLCC?

Additional comments:
- Clarify the operational meaning of “de-couples but jointly models” with concrete architectural details and training schedules.  
- Spell out the exact tree-to-graph relinking procedure (relinking claims to a single major claim and adding a root) and justify no evaluation bias.  
- Consider reordering the paper to present sequence-tagging approaches before dependency parsing to match the empirical narrative.
