- Compares token-level dependency parsing, token-based sequence tagging with a compact joint label, and an MTL variant, plus LSTM-ER and an ILP baseline for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM).  
- Finds token-level dependency parsing underperforms, BiLSTM taggers are robust, MTL yields consistent gains, and neural taggers set new SOTA on persuasive essays.  
- Contribution: Shows end-to-end neural sequence tagging with joint BIO/type/distance/stance labels is effective and improved by MTL with component-only and relation-only auxiliaries.  
- Contribution: Provides a thorough comparative study across modeling choices and document granularities, offering actionable guidance on model selection (local taggers vs general relation models).  
- Contribution (negative): Demonstrates token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, especially at essay scale.  
- Strength: Clear motivation to avoid hand-crafted features/ILP and to adopt joint learning from a token-level perspective.  
- Strength: Well-written and accessible explanation of model framings, tagging scheme, distance encoding, and task variants.  
- Strength: Substantive experiments with multiple model families, paragraph- and essay-level settings, strong baselines, and standardized metrics.  
- Strength: Insightful analyses on sequence length, distance distributions, and constrained search space.  
- Strength: Useful findings that BLCC taggers handle long-range dependencies, MTL improves joint tasks, LSTM-ER is strong on components but weaker on relations in long docs, and distance encoding is an effective bias.  
- Strength: Practical guidance on when to prefer local tagging models over entity–relation architectures.  
- Weakness: Dataset is very small with severe relation label sparsity; development set size/split not reported, raising robustness and generalization concerns.  
- Weakness: No analysis of overfitting or training stability — single-run numbers reported without training/dev curves or mean/std across seeds.  
- Weakness: Possible model–data capacity mismatch and insufficient regularization details (dropout, weight decay, clipping, early stopping, augmentation) undermining reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Underspecified hyperparameter tuning methodology; lacks principled search details, budgets, and selection criteria.  
- Weakness: Important details (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis) relegated to supplement; inter-annotator agreement and parameter counts missing.  
- Question: Clarify exact dev set size and selection procedure, and ensure no essays were reused across tuning and testing; clarify paragraph vs essay-level splitting.  
- Question: Provide dropout rates, weight decay, gradient clipping, early-stopping criteria, patience, and training/dev loss or F1 curves.  
- Question: Report stability by running 5–10 seeds per setting with mean, std, and 95% CIs and re-run significance tests using mean-over-seeds or paired bootstrap.  
- Question: Detail hyperparameter search spaces, budgets, stopping criteria, selection metrics, and consider Bayesian optimization baselines.  
- Question: Report parameter counts for BL/BLCC/BLC, LSTM-ER, LSTM-Parser and experiments with smaller models to assess capacity effects.  
- Question: Move pre-trained embedding details to main paper, state fine-tuning/OOV policy, and provide ablations without pre-trained embeddings.  
- Question: Include dataset inter-annotator agreement to contextualize attainable ceilings for component and relation F1.  
- Presentation request: Improve Figure 1 color/contrast for black-and-white printing and color-vision accessibility and move key error analysis examples into the main paper.  
- Additional: Report per-document-length and per-distance performance breakdowns to support claims about inductive bias benefits.  
- Additional: Discuss whether a component-level parsing framing (instead of token-level) could reduce search space and data sparsity.
