- Paper compares multiple token-level end-to-end AM architectures (dependency parsers, BiLSTM-CRF-CNN taggers with distance/stance encodings, multi-task tagging, off-the-shelf LSTM-ER) against ILP-based SOTA, evaluated by exact/approx component and relation F1 at paragraph and essay levels.  
- Main empirical findings: dependency parsing performs poorly for token-level AM; sequence tagging is robust; LSTM-ER is very strong at paragraph level; both tagging and LSTM-ER outperform ILP; multi-task learning improves tagging, with claim supervision more helpful than relation supervision.  
- Contribution 1: Rigorous empirical comparison establishing strong neural baselines and actionable modeling insights across document granularities.  
- Contribution 2: Shows multi-task supervision with component (claim/premise) tagging substantially boosts sequence tagging, supporting decoupled-yet-joint training.  
- Contribution 3: Clear negative result that token-level dependency parsing is ill-suited for end-to-end AM at current data sizes.  
- Strength: Comprehensive experimental design with multiple architectures, two granularities, and exact/approx F1 enabling nuanced conclusions.  
- Strength: Strong, consistent gains over ILP SOTA for tagging and LSTM-ER, especially at paragraph level, indicating practical impact.  
- Strength: Careful analysis of model behavior on long sequences (relation combinatorics for LSTM-ER; distance encoding benefits for taggers).  
- Strength: Informative multi-task learning results showing auxiliary claim/entity supervision helps more than relation supervision.  
- Strength: Excellent supplementary material documenting optimization and training for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty — top-performing LSTM-ER is an out-of-the-box application and core advances are empirical.  
- Weakness: Potential data leakage concern if train/test essays share topics or argument templates; split protocol and topic overlap not clearly controlled.  
- Weakness: Unclear comparability of paragraph vs essay F1 — paragraph models may ignore cross-paragraph relations, complicating end-to-end claims.  
- Weakness: Conceptual clarity issues around “de-couples them but jointly models them” and asserted decoupling of relation vs entity information lacking precise definitions and empirical support.  
- Weakness: Presentation/order could improve (put sequence tagging before dependency section) and clarify how a tree output reconstructs the full graph.  
- Question: How is topic/prompt overlap handled between train and test; any measures assessing/capping leakage?  
- Question: Are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores comparable in a controlled way; do paragraph models ignore cross-paragraph relations and how common are those?  
- Question: Please detail the procedure converting tree outputs to full argument graphs and what guarantees “loss-less” reconstruction.  
- Question: What exactly is meant by “de-couples them but jointly models them”; can you provide ablations showing gains from decoupling vs fully joint tagging?  
- Question: How sensitive are tagging results to distance-encoding design choices (clipping, bucketing, etc.)?
