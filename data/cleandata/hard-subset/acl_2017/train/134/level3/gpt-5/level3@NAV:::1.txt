Summary
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (AM) and compares token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging (with distance-encoded labels), a multi-task learning (MTL) variant, and LSTM-ER. The motivation—avoiding hand-engineered features and ILP constraints, and mitigating pipeline error propagation via joint learning—is clear and compelling. Empirically, token-level dependency parsing underperforms, while BiLSTM-based tagging (and MTL) performs robustly; results against prior ILP baselines are competitive or better.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
- A thorough empirical study showing that simple BiLSTM sequence tagging with distance-aware labels is a strong end-to-end AM baseline and that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for this task.
- Evidence that multi-task learning with “natural” subtasks (component vs. relation) confers measurable gains over single-task tagging.
- An informative comparison between local (tagging) and more general joint models (LSTM-ER), clarifying when each succeeds (paragraph vs. essay level).

Strengths
- Clear, well-written presentation; the motivation for neural, joint learning without ILP/hand features is well articulated.
- Substantial experimental comparisons across modeling families (parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) with informative analyses (e.g., distance distributions, paragraph vs. essay effects).
- Interesting and replicable modeling ideas (distance-encoded labels; modularity vs. constraints trade-offs).
- Proper baseline inclusion (feature-based ILP) and task-relevant metrics.

Weaknesses
- Data scale is very small (≈320 train essays, 80 test); development set size is not reported. This raises concerns about robustness and generalization.
- Potential model–data mismatch: large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (BLC/BLCC) on few thousand training tags risk overfitting; no training/dev curves, no discussion of regularization choices (dropout rates, weight decay, early stopping).
- Stability and significance: report only single-seed results; need mean±std over multiple runs and significance across runs. Some settings appear high-variance (e.g., Kiperwasser ≈0.56–0.61 F1).
- Important details (pre-trained embeddings, hyperparameter tuning, error analysis) are relegated to the supplement; recommend moving key parts to the main paper. Consider Bayesian optimization for tuning.
- Experimental comparability: ILP has paragraph/essay awareness; the paragraph-level focus may inflate scores relative to the more realistic essay setting. Include inter-annotator agreement from the dataset paper to contextualize system F1.

Questions to Authors
- Please report dev set construction, multiple-seed results (mean, std, ranges), and overfitting diagnostics (train/dev curves). What regularization proved most effective?
- Provide F1 ranges per setting (e.g., for Kiperwasser, BLCC, MTL) and statistical tests across seeds. Can you explain sources of variance?
- Detail pre-trained embeddings (source, OOV handling) in the main paper and expand the error analysis. Also, please improve Figure 1 color/contrast for B/W printing.
