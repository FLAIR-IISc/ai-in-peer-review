Summary
The paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) that avoid hand-crafted features and pipeline ILP constraints. It frames AM as (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging that jointly encodes component spans, types, relation distance, and stance, (iii) multi-task sequence tagging using natural subtasks, and (iv) a prior entity-relation model (LSTM-ER) adapted to AM. Experiments on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus compare paragraph- vs. essay-level settings with component and relation F1 metrics. Results show that token-level dependency parsing underperforms; BiLSTM-based tagging is robust; LSTM-ER excels on components and on paragraph-level overall; and multi-tasking improves tagging. Neural systems meet or surpass the ILP state of the art without manual feature engineering.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A thorough empirical study of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM, including negative evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ill-suited, and positive evidence for sequence tagging and MTL. This comparative analysis, spanning levels of granularity (paragraph vs. essay), is the primary contribution.
2) A simple but effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes long-range relation information via distance-labeled tags, yielding strong, robust results across settings and outperforming a strong ILP baseline on paragraph-level evaluation.
3) Evidence that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (especially component detection/typing) consistently improves end-to-end tagging, clarifying when and how to decouple component vs. relation modeling while training jointly.

Strengths
- Strong empirical comparison with clear takeaways. The study spans four neural framings plus an ILP baseline, and evaluates both paragraph and essay granularity. Results are consistent and insightful: STagBLCC outperforms parsers and ILP at paragraph-level (e.g., C-F1 74.08 vs. ILP 73.35; R-F1 44.02 vs. 44.29), while LSTM-ER is best on paragraph-level overall (C-F1 77.19, R-F1 50.05, F1 60.72). At essay-level, STagBLCC has better overall F1 than LSTM-ER due to more precise relation predictions (R-F1 39.68 vs. 32.72), despite LSTM-ER’s stronger C-F1 (73.02 vs. 69.49).
- Clear negative result with analysis on dependency parsing. The token-level parsing framings (feature-based and neural) lag markedly behind tagging (e.g., LSTM-Parser R-F1 40.87 paragraph-level; 14.45 essay-level), with convincing explanations: long sequences inflate the search space, and token-level tree constraints are a poor fit for AM structures. This saves future effort by steering the community away from this line for end-to-end AM.
- Effective, simple tagging design that captures long-range relations. Encoding component-relative distances in the label space leverages BiLSTM context to model long-range dependencies while constraining search to one head per component and biasing toward realistic distance distributions. This explains stronger relation identification on longer texts relative to pairwise relation models.
- Multi-task learning improves end-to-end tagging. Adding natural subtasks (YC for component span+type, YR for relation distance+stance) yields consistent gains over the single-task baseline (e.g., Y-3:YC-3 improves essay-level C-F1 by ~5 points and R-F1 by ~4 vs. Y-3), with the component task contributing more than the relation task. This offers actionable guidance for model design and training.
- Reproducibility and careful evaluation. The paper uses standard F1 metrics at exact and approximate match levels, provides detailed setup, and references supplementary materials with training/hyperparameters and code links.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The strongest systems are adaptations of known architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging; LSTM-ER). The main novelty lies in the comparative study and task-specific label design, not in algorithmic innovations. This is acceptable but should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Single dataset and domain. All conclusions are drawn from the PE corpus, which has specific structural properties (paragraph-contained structures, regular placement of major claims). Generalization to other AM corpora with different relation topology or longer-range cross-paragraph links remains untested.
- Potential topic leakage/data stratification concerns. If essays or prompts in train and test overlap in stance distributions or argument patterns, performance may be inflated, especially for tagging models with strong lexicalization. The paper does not detail topic splits or controls for prompt leakage.
- Comparability of paragraph vs. essay results and completeness of relations. It is unclear whether paragraph-level models are penalized for cross-paragraph relations (e.g., claim–major claim), and whether F1 across levels is intended to be compared directly. This affects interpretation of state-of-the-art claims.
- Clarity issues around “decoupling but joint modeling” and tree-to-graph reconstruction. The paper claims to decouple components and relations but also “jointly model” them (for LSTM-ER and MTL tagging); the precise operational meaning needs clarification. The description of converting near-tree essay structures (relinking claims to a single major claim and adding a root) needs more detail to ensure no evaluation bias.

Questions to authors
1) Data splits and topic leakage: Are prompts/topics disjoint between train/dev/test? If not, what controls ensure no leakage of topic-specific lexical cues? Can you report performance on topic-held-out splits?
2) Paragraph vs. essay evaluation: Are these settings directly comparable? How are cross-paragraph relations treated at paragraph level (ignored, approximated, or reconstructed)? Please quantify how often such relations occur and their impact on paragraph-level F1.
3) Distance-labeled tagging: What is the maximum |d| observed and used at training/inference? How sensitive are results to (i) capping |d|, (ii) rebinning distances, and (iii) skewed class distribution over d?
4) LSTM-ER relation bottleneck on essays: Did you try hard negatives sampling, restricting candidate pairs by distance windows, or learning a distance prior to reduce the O(m^2) search space? If so, how did R-F1 change?
5) Dependency parsing design: Did you try component-level parsing (predict spans first, then parse components) to reduce tree complexity? This could separate the negative result for token-level trees from the general “dependency parsing is ineffective” claim.
6) Multi-task architecture: You report the best configurations sometimes share layers between main and auxiliary tasks. Can you provide a clearer ablation on which layer-depth assignments consistently help and whether label-dependency modeling (CRF) could be combined with MTL to close the gap to BLCC?

Additional comments
- Clarify “de-couples them but jointly models them”: e.g., LSTM-ER decouples training objectives for entities vs. relations but trains a joint model with shared representations and scheduled sampling; in STagT+MTL, subtasks have separate heads but shared encoders. A concise, unambiguous statement would help.
- Tree-to-graph reconstruction: Please spell out the exact procedure for relinking claims to major claims and adding a root, and justify that this does not bias evaluation.
- Editorial: Consider presenting sequence tagging before dependency parsing to match the empirical narrative and emphasize the effective approaches early.
