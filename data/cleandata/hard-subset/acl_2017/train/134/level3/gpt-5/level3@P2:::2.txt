Summary and contributions
The paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the PE dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016), comparing four framings: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes component type, distance, and stance, multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary “component” and “relation” subtasks, and an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016). Results are reported at both paragraph and essay levels and compared to a strong ILP-based state-of-the-art baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A thorough empirical benchmark of neural end-to-end AM framings with clear, actionable insights and new state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset. The study establishes that local BiLSTM tagging models are strong and robust for components and relations, and that LSTM-ER excels on shorter inputs.
2) A simple but effective sequence-tagging formulation that encodes distance and stance in the tag, plus a multi-task setup where adding “natural” subtasks (especially the component subtask) consistently improves performance.
3) A well-substantiated negative result: token-based dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, particularly on long sequences, with analysis of why (search space, long-range dependencies, global constraints not helping).

Strengths
- Strong empirical results against a competitive baseline:
  - On paragraphs, STagBLCC outperforms the ILP baseline on exact component F1 (66.69 vs. 62.61), exact relation F1 (39.83 vs. 34.74), and overall F1 (49.87 vs. 44.68). LSTM-ER is even stronger on paragraphs (C-F1: 70.83, R-F1: 45.52, overall F1: 55.42), delivering new SOTA.
  - On essays, STagBLCC remains competitive (C-F1: 63.23, R-F1: 34.82, F1: 44.90), while LSTM-ER maintains excellent component performance (C-F1: 66.21).
  - Results are statistically validated (Wilcoxon signed rank, p<0.01).
- Insightful analysis that clarifies when each modeling choice works:
  - Local taggers are length-invariant and handle long-range dependencies robustly; dependency parsers degrade sharply from paragraph to essay level (e.g., LSTM-Parser R-F1 35.63→9.11), likely due to the combinatorial explosion of global structures.
  - LSTM-ER’s relation performance degrades on essays due to O(m^2) relation candidates, while the tagging formulation’s explicit single-link constraint and distance encoding bias toward short-range links are advantageous.
- Multi-task learning yields consistent gains:
  - Adding the component subtask raises exact C-F1 from 49.59 to 54.58 and exact overall F1 from 34.35 to 38.90; component supervision is more helpful than relation supervision. These are meaningful and reproducible improvements for a practical AM pipeline.
- Careful experimental design and transparency:
  - The paper evaluates at two granularities (paragraph, essay), contrasts multiple parsers and taggers, uses both exact and approximate matching for components and relations, and provides a comprehensive supplement with training details and error analysis.
- Practical takeaways for future system design:
  - Clear recommendations: joint but decoupled modeling of entities and relations; prefer local tagging for long documents; more general relation modeling can work well when the number of entities is small.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty:
  - Much of the lift comes from applying existing architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, LSTM-ER) with thoughtful task framing. The novelty lies mainly in the tag design, the comprehensive comparison, and the negative result for token-level dependency parsing.
- Token-level dependency framing feels misaligned:
  - While the analysis is useful, the token-based tree constraint appears unnatural for AM compared to component-level parsing. The negative result may partly reflect this mismatch rather than an indictment of “dependency parsing” per se.
- Comparability of paragraph vs. essay results:
  - It is unclear how to compare F1 across settings. Paragraph models may benefit from fewer entities and fewer plausible relations; they also may miss cross-paragraph links (claim–major-claim), potentially inflating relation F1 relative to essay-level models. The paper mentions structure is contained in paragraphs except possibly claim→major-claim; a clearer treatment would help.
- Potential data leakage and domain overlap:
  - If train/test essays share topics or characteristic arguments, lexical overlap could inflate scores, especially for local models. The paper should document the split protocol and topic distribution across splits.
- Clarity issues:
  - “De-couples them but jointly models them” (re LSTM-ER) is confusing; the paper should explicitly formalize what is trained jointly, what is conditioned on predicted entities, and where parameter sharing occurs.
  - The description of constructing a tree by re-linking claims to a single major claim and adding a root is concise; how is the original multi-link graph reconstructed for evaluation, and is there any loss or bias?

Questions to authors
- Data split and leakage: How are training/dev/test essays split across topics? Do topics overlap across splits? Did you inspect lexical overlap to rule out leakage or topic memorization?
- Paragraph vs. essay comparability: Are paragraph-level F1 scores meant to be compared to essay-level scores? What cross-paragraph relations exist (beyond claim→major-claim), and are they lost in paragraph experiments?
- Tree-to-graph conversion: When claims are re-linked to one major claim and a root is added to form a tree, how do you recover the original multiple links for evaluation? Please detail the exact conversion and its invertibility.
- LSTM-ER configuration: Did you use scheduled sampling and entity pretraining as in Miwa & Bansal? Given that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, what is the contribution of the TreeLSTM over sentence-level dependency trees? Can you report an ablation without TreeLSTM?
- Tag-set size and sparsity: Distances range from −11 to +10. Did you cap or bucket d to control label sparsity? How did you handle unseen distances or very rare d values at test time?
- MTL architecture: You note best results when component and full tasks feed from the same layer. Can you provide a brief rationale or additional analysis (e.g., probing) about what each layer learns?

Additional comments
- For readability, consider presenting sequence tagging before dependency parsing (the paper’s primary positive results), and moving the parsing negative result after.
- Please clarify the statements around “decoupling but joint modeling” and the separation of entity vs. relation modules (references around lines 455, 477–479).
- Minor: define “Acc.” in tables; fix typos (e.g., “seperately”→“separately”).
