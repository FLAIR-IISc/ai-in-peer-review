- Paper compares neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining on the Persuasive Essays corpus, evaluating dependency parsing, sequence tagging (novel label space), MTL, LSTM-ER, and ILP baseline using C-F1 and R-F1 (exact/50% overlap).
- Key empirical findings: dependency parsing performs poorly; sequence labeling is strong; LSTM-ER excels at paragraph level; both beat the ILP baseline; MTL yields substantial gains, mainly from the component-typing task.
- Contribution 1: Thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural framings, showing simple sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform prior ILP and parsing is ineffective at token level.
- Contribution 2: Practical sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance and stance, plus an MTL setup that consistently improves performance.
- Contribution 3: Analysis of document-length effects, clarifying when ER models help (short texts) versus when constrained sequence models help (long texts).
- Strength: Strong, diverse empirical study with consistent metrics and clear, actionable takeaways for the community.
- Strength: Simple methods (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging, LSTM-ER) outperform a competitive ILP baseline, with significant C-F1 and R-F1 gains.
- Strength: Valuable negative result showing token-level dependency parsers struggle, especially on essays.
- Strength: Insightful analysis of length and search-space tradeoffs (paragraph vs essay) guiding model choice.
- Strength: Comprehensive, detailed supplementary material that aids reproducibility.
- Weakness: Limited novelty in modeling—mostly adaptations of existing architectures; novelty mainly in comparison and label design.
- Weakness: Potential topic/data leakage—unclear if prompts/topics are disjoint across splits and whether that inflates performance.
- Weakness: Comparability issues between paragraph and essay settings, since some models (e.g., parsers) were only trained at paragraph level and cross-paragraph relations may be missed.
- Weakness: Unclear how tree outputs are losslessly reconstructed to full graphs and whether evaluation is consistent across models.
- Weakness: Ambiguity in the phrase “de-couples them but jointly models them”; unclear what parameter sharing/training schedule constitutes “decoupling.”
- Question: Are essay prompts/topics disjoint across train/dev/test, and were paragraphs from the same essay separated across splits?
- Question: Are paragraph- and essay-level C-F1/R-F1 intended to be directly comparable, and how are cross-paragraph relations handled in evaluation?
- Question: Please detail the tree-to-graph reconstruction procedure and confirm it is applied consistently across models during evaluation.
- Question: Which parameters are shared between entity and relation modules, what is the training schedule, and how is “de-coupling” defined operationally?
- Question: What is the maximum distance |d| in the distance-encoded label space, how are out-of-range distances handled, and how sensitive are results to this cap?
- Question: Can essay-level results be provided for at least one graph-based and one transition-based parser, or clarify why infeasible due to memory?
- Suggestion: Consider presenting sequence tagging before dependency approaches to improve narrative flow.
- Suggestion: Clarify whether “global F1” is harmonic mean of C-F1 and R-F1 or instance-level, and provide per-class breakdowns (MC/Claim/Premise).
- Suggestion: Acknowledge LSTM-ER as largely out-of-the-box while emphasizing empirical insights and error analyses as the main contribution.
- Commendation: Supplementary material is exemplary and should be retained for reproducibility.
