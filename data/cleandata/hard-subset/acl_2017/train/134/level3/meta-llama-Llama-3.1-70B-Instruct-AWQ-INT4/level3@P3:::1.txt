This paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), a complex task that involves identifying argumentative structures in text. The authors propose several neural network framings for AM, including sequence tagging, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning. The paper is well-written and easy to understand, making it accessible to a broad audience.

The authors' main contribution is the proposal of end-to-end AM using neural networks, which eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing. They also demonstrate that their neural models perform better than or comparable to a competing feature-based ILP formulation. The paper's strengths include its clear explanation of the motivation behind the proposed approach, its thorough evaluation of different models, and its discussion of the results.

One of the paper's main weaknesses is the limited size of the dataset used for evaluation. With only approximately 320 training essays and 80 test essays, the results may not be generalizable to larger datasets. Additionally, the development set size is not reported, which makes it difficult to assess the models' performance on unseen data. The authors also do not provide training and development loss curves, which would help to analyze overfitting.

The authors' choice of models, particularly the large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (BLC/BLCC), may be too big for the limited data, risking poor training. The paper also lacks a discussion of regularization methods, which is crucial for preventing overfitting. The authors mention LSTM-ER in the supplementary material, but it is not properly treated in the main paper.

The evaluation metric used in the paper is the F1-score, which is a common metric for evaluating sequence labeling tasks. However, the authors do not provide F1-score ranges and do not report the mean and variance for different settings, which would aid in replication and assessing stability.

The paper's results show that the sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) performs better than the dependency parsing model (LSTM-Parser) on both paragraph and essay levels. The multi-task learning model (STagBL) also performs well, particularly when including the "C" and "R" tasks as auxiliary tasks. The LSTM-ER model performs well on component identification but poorly on relation identification on the essay level.

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

* Provide more details on the dataset, including the development set size and the data preprocessing steps.
* Include training and development loss curves to analyze overfitting.
* Discuss regularization methods and their impact on the models' performance.
* Provide F1-score ranges and report the mean and variance for different settings.
* Consider using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning instead of the current method described in the supplement.
* Move the pre-trained word embeddings details and error analysis from the supplementary material to the main paper.
* Include inter-annotator agreement scores to contextualize system performance.
* Improve Figure 1's color and contrast for better black-and-white printing.

In terms of contributions, I identify the following:

1. Proposal of end-to-end AM using neural networks, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. Demonstration of the effectiveness of neural models in AM, performing better than or comparable to a competing feature-based ILP formulation.
3. Introduction of a multi-task learning framework for AM, which improves performance on the AM problem.

The authors' main arguments for the acceptance of this submission are:

* The paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end learning for AM, a complex task that has not been thoroughly explored before.
* The proposed models perform better than or comparable to a competing feature-based ILP formulation, demonstrating the effectiveness of neural approaches in AM.
* The paper provides a thorough evaluation of different models and discusses the results in detail, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

The main weaknesses of the paper are:

* Limited size of the dataset used for evaluation, which may not be generalizable to larger datasets.
* Lack of discussion of regularization methods, which is crucial for preventing overfitting.
* Limited analysis of overfitting, which may impact the models' performance.

Questions to authors:

* How do the authors plan to address the limited size of the dataset used for evaluation?
* Can the authors provide more details on the regularization methods used in their models?
* How do the authors plan to analyze overfitting in their models, and what measures can be taken to prevent it?
