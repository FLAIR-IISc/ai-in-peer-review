**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the AM problem as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence tagging problem, and a multi-task learning problem. They evaluate several neural network architectures, including BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, and LSTM-ER, and compare their performance to a feature-based ILP model. The results show that the neural models outperform the ILP model in some cases, and that the sequence tagging approach with a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN model achieves the best performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes the first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining.
2. The authors evaluate several neural network architectures and compare their performance to a feature-based ILP model.
3. The paper shows that the sequence tagging approach with a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN model achieves the best performance.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-written and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the methods and results.
2. The authors propose a novel approach to AM by framing it as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence tagging problem, and a multi-task learning problem.
3. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of several neural network architectures and compares their performance to a feature-based ILP model.
4. The results are interesting and provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches to AM.

**Weaknesses**

1. The evaluation dataset is small, with only 320 training essays and 80 test essays, which may not be representative of the entire population of essays.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of overfitting, and the paper does not include training and development loss curves.
3. The paper does not provide a thorough discussion of regularization methods, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions LSTM-ER.
4. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of model stability, and the paper does not include mean and standard deviation across multiple runs.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the evaluation dataset, including the distribution of essay topics and the annotation process?
2. How did you address overfitting in your models, and what regularization methods did you use?
3. Can you provide more details on the LSTM-ER model, including the architecture and training procedure?
4. How did you evaluate model stability, and what were the results?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, including error analysis and discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
2. The authors could consider using more advanced techniques for regularization, such as dropout or weight decay.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results for AM and related applications.
