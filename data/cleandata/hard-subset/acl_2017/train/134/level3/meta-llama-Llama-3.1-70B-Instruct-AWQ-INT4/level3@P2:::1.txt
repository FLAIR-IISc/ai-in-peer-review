This paper proposes end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks with two approaches (sequence labeling and dependency parsing) and a multitask learning variant. The authors' motivation for using joint learning is clearly explained, highlighting the drawbacks of traditional ILP/manual feature engineering and pipeline error propagation. The methods are well-explained, making the paper easy to understand, and the results are interesting.

The main contributions of this paper are: (1) proposing end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks, (2) presenting two approaches (sequence labeling and dependency parsing) and a multitask learning variant, and (3) demonstrating the effectiveness of these approaches through substantial experiments and proper comparisons.

The strengths of this paper are: (1) the paper is well-written and easy to understand, (2) the methods and results are interesting, and (3) the authors provide a clear motivation for using joint learning. The use of neural networks for end-to-end argumentation mining is a novel approach, and the authors' experiments demonstrate its potential.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The evaluation and obtained results may be problematic due to the small dataset size (≈320 training essays, ≈80 test essays) and the lack of development set size reporting. The large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (BLC/BLCC) may be too big for the limited data, risking poor training. Additionally, the training tags are likely only a few thousand versus hundreds of thousands/millions in standard sequence tasks, which may lead to data sparsity issues. The paper does not analyze overfitting or provide training/development loss curves, and the model stability is not assessed through mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests. Furthermore, the paper omits discussion of regularization methods, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions LSTM-ER without providing a proper treatment. The hyperparameter tuning method described in the supplement is also not optimal, and Bayesian optimization is recommended instead.

To improve the paper, the authors should: (1) provide more details on the experimental setup, including the development set size and the number of training tags, (2) analyze overfitting and provide training/development loss curves, (3) assess model stability through mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests, (4) discuss regularization methods and provide a proper treatment of LSTM-ER, (5) use Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, and (6) move the pre-trained word embeddings details and error analysis from the supplementary material to the main paper. Additionally, the authors should include inter-annotator agreement scores (from the dataset paper) to contextualize system performance and improve Figure 1 for better black-and-white printing.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the development set size and the number of training tags?
2. How do you plan to address the potential overfitting issue due to the large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models and the limited data?
3. Can you provide training/development loss curves to analyze overfitting?
4. How do you assess model stability, and can you provide mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests?
5. Can you discuss regularization methods and provide a proper treatment of LSTM-ER?
6. Why did you choose the current hyperparameter tuning method, and can you consider using Bayesian optimization instead?
7. Can you move the pre-trained word embeddings details and error analysis from the supplementary material to the main paper?
8. Can you include inter-annotator agreement scores (from the dataset paper) to contextualize system performance?
9. Can you improve Figure 1 for better black-and-white printing?

Post-response request:

1. Can you include F1-score ranges and report mean and variance for different settings?
2. How do you plan to address the high variance (e.g., Kiperwasser setting F1 in [0.56, 0.61]) to aid replication and assess stability?
