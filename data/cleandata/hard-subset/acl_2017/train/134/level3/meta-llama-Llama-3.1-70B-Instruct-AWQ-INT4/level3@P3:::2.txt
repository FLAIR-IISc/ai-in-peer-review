This paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), a task that involves identifying argumentative structures in text. The authors explore multiple approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER, a neural model that combines sequential and tree structure information.

The main contributions of this paper are the investigation of neural techniques for end-to-end AM, the demonstration that several of these techniques outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model, and the finding that a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective. Additionally, the authors show that a standard neural sequence tagging model performs robustly in different environments and that a multi-task learning setup improves performance.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the different approaches and their results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach and discuss the implications of their findings.

One of the strengths of the paper is its comprehensive evaluation of different approaches, which provides a clear understanding of what works and what doesn't in the context of AM. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including an examination of the effects of different hyperparameters and the impact of multi-task learning on performance.

Another strength of the paper is its use of a robust evaluation metric, which allows for a fair comparison of the different approaches. The authors use the F1 score, which is a widely used metric in natural language processing, to evaluate the performance of each approach.

One of the weaknesses of the paper is the lack of clarity on how the LSTM-ER model is adapted for the argumentative structure encoded in the PE dataset. The authors mention that they model three types of entities (premise, claim, major claim) and four types of relations (for, against, support, attack), but they do not provide further details on how this adaptation is done.

Another weakness of the paper is the concern about possible data leakage if train and test essays share topics or arguments, potentially inflating performance. The authors do not address this concern explicitly, and it is not clear how they ensure that the train and test sets are disjoint.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining, demonstrating the effectiveness of neural techniques for end-to-end AM and providing a comprehensive evaluation of different approaches.

**Contributions:**

1. Investigation of neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining.
2. Demonstration that several neural techniques outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3. Finding that a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective.

**Strengths:**

1. Comprehensive evaluation of different approaches.
2. Clear explanations of the different approaches and their results.
3. Robust evaluation metric.
4. Detailed analysis of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. Lack of clarity on how the LSTM-ER model is adapted for the argumentative structure encoded in the PE dataset.
2. Concern about possible data leakage if train and test essays share topics or arguments.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide further details on how the LSTM-ER model is adapted for the argumentative structure encoded in the PE dataset?
2. How do you ensure that the train and test sets are disjoint, and what measures do you take to prevent data leakage?
3. Can you clarify the meaning of "de-couples them but jointly models them" and the claimed decoupling of relation vs entity information?
4. Are F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings comparable, and do paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations?
