**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a comprehensive study on neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore various approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER, to identify argumentative structures in text. They evaluate the performance of these approaches using the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compare them to a feature-based ILP model. The results show that sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP model, and that multi-task learning can improve performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM**: The paper presents the first study on neural end-to-end learning for AM, providing a comprehensive evaluation of various approaches.
2. **Improved performance over feature-based ILP model**: The results show that sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the feature-based ILP model, demonstrating the effectiveness of neural approaches for AM.
3. **Multi-task learning improves performance**: The paper demonstrates that multi-task learning can improve performance by jointly learning natural subtasks of the full AM problem.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive evaluation of various approaches**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of different approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER.
2. **Improved performance over state-of-the-art ILP model**: The results show that sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the feature-based ILP model, demonstrating the effectiveness of neural approaches for AM.
3. **Insights into the importance of multi-task learning**: The paper demonstrates that multi-task learning can improve performance by jointly learning natural subtasks of the full AM problem.
4. **Well-written and easy to follow**: The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.
5. **Reproducibility**: The authors provide a comprehensive description of their experiments, including details on the dataset, models, and hyperparameter optimization, making it easy to reproduce their results.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependency-based solutions perform poorly**: The results show that dependency-based solutions perform poorly, which may be due to the complexity of the AM problem.
2. **LSTM-ER model is not novel**: The LSTM-ER model is not a novel contribution, as it is an out-of-the-box application of prior work.
3. **Data leakage concern**: There is a concern about data leakage, as the train and test essays may share topics/arguments, potentially inflating performance.
4. **Limited analysis of results**: The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the results, including error analysis and discussion of the implications of the findings.
5. **Lack of comparison to other neural models**: The paper could benefit from a comparison to other neural models, such as transformer-based models, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of neural approaches for AM.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the data leakage concern and how you addressed it in your experiments?
2. How did you select the hyperparameters for the LSTM-ER model, and did you perform any hyperparameter optimization?
3. Can you provide more insights into why the dependency-based solutions performed poorly?
4. How do you plan to extend this work to other datasets and domains?
5. Can you provide more details on the multi-task learning framework and how you selected the auxiliary tasks?
