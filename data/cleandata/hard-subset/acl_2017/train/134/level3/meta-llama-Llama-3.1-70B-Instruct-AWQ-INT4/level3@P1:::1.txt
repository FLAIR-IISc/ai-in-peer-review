This paper presents a novel approach to computational argumentation mining (AM) using neural networks. The authors propose two end-to-end methods, sequence labeling and dependency parsing, as well as a multi-task learning variant. The paper is well-written and easy to understand, and the methods and results are interesting.

The authors' motivation for using neural networks is clearly explained, as they aim to avoid the need for manual feature engineering and pipeline error propagation via joint learning. The methods are clearly explained, except for a few missing details.

The experiments are substantial and provide proper comparisons, and the results are interesting. However, the main concern is that the dataset is very small, with approximately 320 training essays and 80 test essays. The development set size is not reported, which raises concerns about model-data mismatch and overfitting.

The use of large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (BLC/BLCC) may be too big for the limited data, risking poor training. Additionally, the training tags are likely only a few thousand, whereas standard sequence tasks typically involve hundreds of thousands or millions of tags.

The paper does not analyze overfitting or provide training/development loss curves, which is a significant omission. Furthermore, the paper does not report mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests, which are essential for assessing model stability.

The paper also omits a discussion of regularization methods, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions LSTM-ER without providing a proper treatment. The hyperparameter tuning method described in the supplement could be improved by using Bayesian optimization.

Some minor suggestions include moving the pre-trained word embeddings details and error analysis from the supplementary material to the main paper, including inter-annotator agreement scores to contextualize system performance, and improving Figure 1 for better black-and-white printing.

In the post-response request, the authors should include F1-score ranges and report mean and variance for different settings, and address the high variance (e.g., Kiperwasser setting F1 in [0.56, 0.61]) to aid replication and assess stability.

The contributions of this paper are:

1. Proposes end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks with two approaches (sequence labeling and dependency parsing) and a multi-task learning variant.
2. Clearly explains the motivation for avoiding ILP/manual feature engineering and pipeline error propagation via joint learning.
3. Provides substantial experiments and proper comparisons, and the results are interesting.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. Evaluation and obtained results may be problematic due to the small dataset size and potential model-data mismatch.
2. Lack of analysis of overfitting and provision of training/development loss curves.
3. Omission of discussion of regularization methods and proper treatment of hyperparameter tuning.
4. Need for mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests.

Strengths:

1. Well-written and easy to understand paper.
2. Interesting methods and results.
3. Clearly explained motivation for using neural networks.

Weaknesses:

1. Small dataset size and potential model-data mismatch.
2. Lack of analysis of overfitting and provision of training/development loss curves.
3. Omission of discussion of regularization methods and proper treatment of hyperparameter tuning.
4. Need for mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the development set size and how it was used in the experiments?
2. How did you address the potential model-data mismatch and overfitting in your experiments?
3. Can you provide more information on the regularization methods used in your models?
4. How did you select the hyperparameters for your models, and can you provide more details on the tuning process?
5. Can you provide more information on the inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset and how they contextualize the system performance?
