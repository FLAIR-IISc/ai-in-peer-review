- Paper studies neural end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM), comparing dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER on the PE dataset.  
- Sequence labeling solutions (especially BiLSTM taggers) are effective; dependency-based solutions perform poorly.  
- LSTM-ER performs very well, particularly at paragraph level, and sequence labeling + LSTM-ER beat the ILP state-of-the-art.  
- Contribution: first neural end-to-end study for computational AM removing manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.  
- Contribution: demonstrates effective sequence labeling approaches for AM using BiLSTM taggers.  
- Contribution: shows multi-task learning substantially improves sequence labeling, with the claim task contributing more than relation task.  
- Strength: provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple modeling approaches.  
- Strength: reports strong empirical results outperforming prior ILP-based methods.  
- Strength: presents methods and results clearly and accessibly.  
- Weakness: potential data leakage if train/test essays share topics/arguments is not addressed.  
- Weakness: LSTM-ER may lack novelty, being an out-of-the-box application of prior work.  
- Weakness: unclear explanation of how a tree output is used to reconstruct the full graph (lines 222–226).  
- Weakness: unclear explanation of what "de-couples them but jointly models them" means regarding relation vs entity information (lines 455, 477–479).  
- Questions for authors: how is data leakage prevented; are paragraph and essay F1 scores comparable and do paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations; response to LSTM-ER novelty critique; clearer explanation of tree-to-graph reconstruction?
