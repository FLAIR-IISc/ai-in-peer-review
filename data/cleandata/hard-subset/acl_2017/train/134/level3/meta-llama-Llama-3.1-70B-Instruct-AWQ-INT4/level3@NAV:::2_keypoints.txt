- Study of neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining, evaluating dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER on the persuasive essays dataset.  
- Sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform a feature-based ILP baseline; multi-task learning can further improve performance.  

- Contribution: first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining.  
- Contribution: neural sequence labeling and LSTM-ER improve over the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model.  
- Contribution: multi-task learning shown to boost performance by jointly learning subtasks of AM.  

- Strength: thorough evaluation across multiple modeling approaches (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL, LSTM-ER).  
- Strength: empirical improvement over the ILP baseline demonstrates effectiveness of neural methods.  
- Strength: provides useful insights into the benefits of multi-task learning for AM.  
- Strength: well-written and easy to follow.  
- Strength: experiments described in detail, aiding reproducibility.  

- Weakness: dependency-based methods perform poorly on this task.  
- Weakness: LSTM-ER is an application of existing work rather than a novel model.  
- Weakness: potential data leakage — train and test essays may share topics/arguments, possibly inflating results.  
- Weakness: analysis is limited; lacks deeper error analysis and discussion of implications.  
- Weakness: missing comparison to other neural models (e.g., transformer-based approaches).  

- Question: provide details on the data leakage concern and how it was addressed.  
- Question: explain hyperparameter selection and optimization for the LSTM-ER model.  
- Question: offer insights into why dependency-based solutions performed poorly.  
- Question: outline plans for extending the approach to other datasets and domains.  
- Question: clarify the multi-task learning framework and criteria for choosing auxiliary tasks.
