**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a neural end-to-end approach to computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative structures in text. The authors explore multiple approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER, a neural model for entity and relation extraction. They evaluate their models using F1 scores for concepts and relations on a dataset of persuasive essays. The results show that sequence labeling solutions are effective, while dependency-based solutions perform poorly. The LSTM-ER model performs well, especially at the paragraph level, and both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP state-of-the-art. Multi-task learning is also shown to improve sequence labeling performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. They show that sequence labeling solutions are effective for AM, outperforming dependency-based solutions.
3. The LSTM-ER model is shown to perform well, especially at the paragraph level, and outperforms the ILP state-of-the-art.
4. Multi-task learning is demonstrated to improve sequence labeling performance.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of multiple approaches to AM, providing a thorough understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each method.
2. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion of the impact of paragraph-level vs. essay-level evaluation.
3. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of sequence labeling solutions for AM, which is a significant contribution to the field.
4. The use of multi-task learning is shown to improve sequence labeling performance, which is a valuable insight for future research.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on existing models and techniques, with the LSTM-ER model being essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how a tree output can be used to reconstruct the full graph, which may be a limitation of the approach.
3. The paper does not address the potential issue of data leakage, where train and test essays may share topics or arguments, potentially inflating performance.
4. The authors do not provide a clear comparison of F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings, which may make it difficult to interpret the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify how a tree output can be used to reconstruct the full graph, as mentioned in lines 222-226?
2. How do you address the potential issue of data leakage, where train and test essays may share topics or arguments?
3. Can you provide a clear comparison of F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings, and discuss the implications of these results?
4. How do you plan to extend this work to more complex argumentation structures, such as those found in real-world texts?
