**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a study on neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore multiple approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and an entity-relation neural model (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches using F1 scores for concepts and relations on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). The results show that sequence labeling solutions are effective, while dependency-based solutions perform poorly. The LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially at the paragraph level, and both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions**

1. **First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM**: The authors present the first study on neural end-to-end approaches to AM, which eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint designing.
2. **Effective sequence labeling solutions**: The authors show that sequence labeling solutions are effective for AM, especially when using BiLSTM taggers.
3. **Multi-task learning improves performance**: The authors demonstrate that multi-task learning substantially improves sequence labeling performance, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of multiple approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, and LSTM-ER.
2. **Strong results**: The authors report strong results, with sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperforming the ILP state-of-the-art.
3. **Clear presentation**: The authors present their results and methods clearly, making it easy to follow their arguments.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Possible data leakage**: The authors do not address the concern of possible data leakage if train and test essays share topics/arguments, potentially inflating performance.
2. **Lack of novelty in LSTM-ER**: One of the best models (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work, which may not be considered a significant contribution.
3. **Unclear explanation of tree output**: The authors do not clearly explain how a tree output can be used to reconstruct the full graph (lines 222-226).
4. **Unclear explanation of decoupling**: The authors do not clearly explain the meaning of "de-couples them but jointly models them" and the claimed decoupling of relation vs entity information (lines 455, 477-479).

**Questions to Authors**

1. **Data leakage**: How do you address the concern of possible data leakage if train and test essays share topics/arguments, potentially inflating performance?
2. **Comparability of F1 scores**: Are F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings comparable, and do paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations?
3. **Novelty of LSTM-ER**: How do you respond to the criticism that the LSTM-ER model is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work?
4. **Tree output explanation**: Can you provide a clearer explanation of how a tree output can be used to reconstruct the full graph?
