## Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, framing the task as both dependency parsing and sequence tagging. The authors explore several model variations, including multi-task learning and a pre-trained LSTM-ER model, evaluating performance using F1 scores for concepts and relations. A key finding is that dependency parsing performs poorly, while sequence labeling, particularly with LSTM-ER, achieves strong results, outperforming existing ILP-based state-of-the-art methods. The multi-task learning setup further improves sequence labeling performance, with the claim task proving more beneficial than the relation task.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining. While the individual models themselves aren't entirely novel, the systematic comparison across dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a pre-trained entity/relation model provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach within this specific domain. The demonstration that a relatively simple sequence tagging model can outperform more complex, feature-engineered ILP baselines is a significant result. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed analysis of the impact of multi-task learning, highlighting the importance of the claim detection task. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper thoroughly explores a wide range of neural architectures and configurations, offering a robust empirical comparison.
*   **Strong Performance:** The LSTM-ER model achieves state-of-the-art results, demonstrating the potential of neural approaches for argumentation mining.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The findings regarding the ineffectiveness of dependency parsing and the benefits of multi-task learning are valuable contributions to the field.
*   **Detailed Supplementary Material:** The comprehensive documentation of training details and hyperparameter optimization enhances reproducibility and transparency.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the different approaches and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Potential Data Leakage:** The concern raised by the supervisor regarding potential topic/argument overlap between training and test essays is valid and needs to be addressed. This could artificially inflate performance metrics.
*   **Limited Novelty of LSTM-ER:** While achieving strong results, the reliance on an out-of-the-box application of the LSTM-ER model diminishes the novelty of this aspect of the work. More adaptation or analysis of *why* it performs well would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity Issues:** The explanations of “de-coupling” and the joint modeling of relations and entities are somewhat unclear and require further elaboration.
*   **Paragraph vs. Essay Level Comparison:** The comparability of F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings is questionable, and the potential for paragraph models to miss cross-paragraph relations should be investigated.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more details on the data split and address the potential for topic/argument overlap between training and test essays?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific adaptations made to the LSTM-ER model for this task, and provide an analysis of which components contribute most to its performance?
3.  Please clarify the meaning of “de-couples them but jointly models them” and explain how the decoupling of relation vs. entity information is achieved in your models.
4.  Are the F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings directly comparable? If not, how do you account for the difference in complexity when interpreting the results? Do paragraph-level models struggle with cross-paragraph relations?
