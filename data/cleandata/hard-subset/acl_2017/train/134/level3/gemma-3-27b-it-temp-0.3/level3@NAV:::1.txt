## Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as both a dependency parsing and sequence tagging problem, with a multi-task learning variant. The motivation – avoiding the pitfalls of pipeline approaches and manual feature engineering – is clearly articulated and a significant contribution to the field. The methods are generally well-explained, though some details are relegated to the supplementary material. The authors conduct substantial experiments and provide comparisons to a strong baseline (ILP model), yielding interesting initial results.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies not in the novelty of applying neural networks to AM (which is incremental), but in the comprehensive *empirical* investigation of different neural framings for this task, specifically highlighting the limitations of a dependency parsing approach and the robustness of sequence tagging. The demonstration that a relatively simple BiLSTM-CRF model can achieve competitive performance, and the exploration of multi-task learning to further improve results, are valuable contributions. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Accessible Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for those not deeply familiar with argumentation mining. The problem is clearly defined, and the proposed methods are explained in a straightforward manner.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a thorough set of experiments, comparing multiple models and configurations. The inclusion of a strong baseline (the ILP model) provides a valuable point of comparison.
3. **Interesting Findings:** The observation that dependency parsing performs poorly for end-to-end AM, while sequence tagging is more robust, is a significant and counterintuitive finding that challenges existing assumptions.
4. **Strong Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for end-to-end learning approaches in AM, highlighting the limitations of traditional pipeline methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Sparsity & Overfitting Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the extremely small dataset (≈320 training essays, ≈80 test essays). The large-capacity BiLSTM models (BLC/BLCC) are likely prone to overfitting with such limited data. The paper lacks a proper analysis of overfitting, such as training/development loss curves.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Results are presented without measures of statistical significance (e.g., p-values). Reporting mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (with different initializations) is crucial for assessing the reliability and stability of the results.
3. **Insufficient Regularization Discussion:** The paper briefly mentions LSTM-ER in the supplementary material, but a thorough discussion of regularization techniques used (or not used) to mitigate overfitting is missing.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The hyperparameter tuning process, as described in the supplement, appears ad-hoc. Bayesian optimization would be a more systematic and effective approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide training and development loss curves for the different models to demonstrate the extent of overfitting.
2.  Report mean and standard deviation of F1 scores across multiple runs with different random initializations. Include statistical significance tests to support your claims.
3.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the regularization techniques employed (or the rationale for not using them).
4.  Can you elaborate on the rationale for choosing the specific MTL configurations explored? Were any ablation studies performed to assess the contribution of each auxiliary task?
5.  Please include inter-annotator agreement scores for the PE dataset in the main paper to provide context for the reported performance.
