- Paper compares five neural strategies for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task sequence labeling, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
- Evaluation uses F1 for concepts and relations; dependency methods perform poorly while sequence labeling is effective.  
- Off-the-shelf LSTM-ER performs very strongly, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Sequence labeling and LSTM-ER both outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art.  
- Supplementary material is comprehensive, including training details and hyperparameter optimization.  
- Multi-task learning improves sequence labeling substantially, with the claim task helping more than the relation task.  
- Overall the study is a thorough empirical investigation of neural approaches to argumentation mining.  
- Major concern: possible train–test leakage because essays in train and test may share topics and similar arguments, inflating performance estimates.  
- Major concern: strongest model (LSTM-ER) is largely an out-of-the-box application of prior work, raising novelty questions.  
- Minor: 222–226 — unclear claim that a full graph can be recovered once a tree is produced; needs clarification.  
- Minor: 443–444 — suggested reordering of presentation (seq. tagging and dependency-based) for clarity.  
- Minor: 455 — unclear phrasing “decouple them but jointly model them”; request clarification on what is meant.  
- Minor: Miwa and Bansal reference could not be found or verified by reviewer.  
- Minor: 477–479 — confusing description of decoupling relation information from entity information; needs clearer explanation.  
- Minor: Question whether F1 scores in paragraph vs. essay settings are comparable, since paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations.
