Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a thorough empirical study of neural, end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. The authors explore four neural framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing with both feature-based and neural parsers; (2) token-level sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes component BIO/type, relation distance, and stance; (3) multi-task sequence tagging where natural subtasks (component vs. relation) are auxiliary objectives; and (4) LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016), an off-the-shelf neural architecture jointly modeling entity and relation extraction. Results are compared against a strong ILP-based system using handcrafted features. Evaluation follows Persing & Ng’s component and relation F1 at exact and approximate overlap, reported at both paragraph and essay levels.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
- A systematic neural end-to-end evaluation for AM that establishes strong baselines surpassing a state-of-the-art ILP pipeline on most settings without manual features.
- An empirical finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, whereas BiLSTM-based sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels is robust across settings; multi-task learning further improves performance, with component tagging helping more than relation tagging.
- An analysis of document length and model bias showing that more general relation models (LSTM-ER) excel on paragraphs but degrade on essays due to combinatorial growth in candidate relations; conversely, the more constrained sequence taggers scale better.

Strengths
- Comprehensive, well-controlled comparison across modeling paradigms. The paper contrasts transition- and graph-based parsers, modern BiLSTM-CRF-CNN taggers, multi-task variants, and LSTM-ER against a strong ILP system, under a common evaluation protocol. This breadth makes the conclusions credible and useful to the community.
- Strong empirical gains with clear takeaways. Sequence tagging and LSTM-ER substantially outperform the ILP model on paragraph-level C-F1 and R-F1; sequence tagging also surpasses the best dependency parser by large margins. The analysis convincingly explains why token-level parsing underperforms (global tree constraints over very long sequences; mismatch to end-to-end span detection).
- Insightful modeling choices and analysis. Encoding inter-component distance directly in the tag space biases the model toward realistic, short-range relations and helps scale to essays. The study also clarifies when general joint models help (few entities/paragraphs) versus when constrained models are preferable (many entities/essays).
- Multi-task gains with practical guidance. Adding natural subtasks (especially component tagging) improves end-to-end tagging despite using a weaker base tagger (no CRF, no char CNN). The result is actionable for practitioners facing complex label spaces.
- Excellent reproducibility. The supplemental material documents embeddings, hyperparameters, and training details, facilitating future work to build upon these baselines.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage via topic overlap. If train and test essays address the same prompts, writers may produce similar structures and arguments, which can inflate performance estimates. Although this would affect all systems, discussing topic-level splits or reporting cross-topic performance would strengthen claims of generalization.
- Limited novelty in the strongest model. One of the best-performing systems (LSTM-ER) is largely an off-the-shelf application with minimal adaptation. The paper’s primary contributions are empirical rather than methodological. Given the depth of analysis and breadth of experiments, this is still valuable, but the novelty is more in findings than in new modeling.
- Clarity issues in the exposition. Several places are hard to follow:
  - Reconstructing full argument graphs from token-level trees (claims linked to multiple major claims) is described but not fully clear; an explicit procedure would help.
  - The text alternates between sequence-tagging-first vs. parsing-first framings; consistent ordering and acronym reminders would reduce cognitive load.
  - The discussion of “de-coupling but jointly modeling” entities and relations (LSTM-ER) is confusing without a concise summary of the architecture’s training/inference interplay.
- Comparability of paragraph vs. essay results. Paragraph-level evaluation is substantially easier (shorter sequences, fewer entities, relations largely intra-paragraph), raising questions about how to interpret cross-setting comparisons, especially for relations. Clear guidance on when to prefer paragraph-level training/deployment and whether any cross-paragraph links are systematically missed would be useful.
- External validity and ablations. Results are limited to one dataset with a specific structure (near-tree per paragraph). The paper would benefit from either a second dataset or additional ablations (e.g., the impact of the distance feature, or CRF/char-CNN components within the MTL tagger) to better isolate what drives gains.

Questions to Authors
- Data splits and topic overlap: Were train/dev/test splits controlled by prompt/topic? If not, can you quantify topic overlap and, if feasible, report cross-topic performance to assess generalization?
- Graph reconstruction from trees: Please detail how you convert token-level trees to component-level structures when claims connect to multiple major claims. Is this transformation fully lossless for evaluation, and how are stance labels preserved?
- “De-coupled but jointly modeled” in LSTM-ER: Could you clarify the training and inference pipeline? Specifically, how are predicted entities fed to relation classification (scheduled sampling vs. gold), and to what extent are parameters shared?
- Paragraph vs. essay comparability: Are paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores intended to be comparable? For relations, do paragraph models systematically miss cross-paragraph links (e.g., claim–major claim), and how is this handled in evaluation?
- Label space and distance encoding: What is the effective size of Y in practice? Did you experiment with capping/bucketing distances, and how sensitive are results to the distance range?
- MTL architecture: Did you try adding CRF and character CNNs to the MTL tagger, or sharing lower layers only? Any insights on why component tagging helps more than relation tagging as an auxiliary task?

Overall Assessment
This is a careful and valuable empirical study that advances end-to-end AM with strong neural baselines, clear insights about modeling choices, and thorough analysis. Despite limited methodological novelty in the top-performing model, the findings are important and actionable. I lean toward acceptance after clarifications, particularly around data splits, graph reconstruction, and cross-setting comparability.
