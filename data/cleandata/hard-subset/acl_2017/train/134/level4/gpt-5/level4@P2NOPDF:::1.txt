- Strengths:
The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The methods and results are engaging.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation and reported results may be problematic (see detailed comments below).

- General Discussion:
This paper presents an end-to-end argumentation mining system based on neural networks, framing the task in two ways: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. It also explores a multitask learning setup for sequence labeling. The motivation is well articulated: prior work relies on ILP with manual feature engineering and handcrafted constraints, whereas the proposed approach reduces manual effort. By jointly learning the subtasks, it also mitigates error propagation common in pipeline architectures. Aside from a few missing details (noted below), the methodology is described clearly.

The experiments are substantial, the comparisons are appropriate, and the results are interesting. My main concern is the small dataset relative to the capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The dataset contains roughly 320 training essays and 80 test essays; the development set size is not specified in the paper or supplement. This is worrisome because so few essays remain for training. The total number of tags is likely only a few thousand, far below typical sequence labeling datasets with hundreds of thousands (or more). As a result, it is unclear whether the models can be reliably trained. The paper does not analyze overfitting; please report training and development losses over time (per epoch or per update). The manuscript itself suggests possible overfitting (line 622: “taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting”).

Relatedly, model stability is uncertain. Please provide means and standard deviations over multiple runs with different random initializations, and include statistical significance tests. Without these, it is hard to tell whether observed gains reflect true improvements or randomness.

Regularization warrants more attention given the tiny dataset. The main text does not discuss it, and the supplement only briefly mentions LSTM-ER. Please detail the regularization techniques used (e.g., dropout, weight decay, early stopping) and the chosen hyperparameters.

For hyperparameter optimization, consider Bayesian optimization instead of (or in addition to) the current approach described in the supplement. Also, move key details—pretrained word embeddings and the error analysis—from the supplement into the main paper; an extra page should suffice.

Please report inter-annotator agreement from the dataset paper to contextualize system performance and indicate remaining headroom. Also, adjust Figure 1’s colors to ensure readability in grayscale for print.
