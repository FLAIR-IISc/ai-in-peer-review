Summary:
This paper studies neural, end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus. The authors compare four framings: (a) token-level dependency parsing; (b) token-level sequence tagging with a label scheme that encodes component spans, types, stance, and inter-component distance; (c) multi-task sequence tagging that jointly learns natural subtasks (component and relation labeling); and (d) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016), which decouples entity and relation extraction while modeling both jointly. Across paragraph- and essay-level settings, the paper finds that dependency parsing underperforms, BiLSTM-based taggers are robust, multi-task learning helps, and several neural models match or outperform a strong ILP baseline that relies on hand-crafted features and constraints.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A systematic empirical comparison of multiple neural end-to-end framings for AM, demonstrating that sequence tagging with distance-aware labels and an adapted LSTM-ER outperform a feature-engineered ILP baseline on several settings.
2) Evidence that simple, local BiLSTM tagging is more effective than token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM, and that adding natural subtasks in an MTL setup yields further gains.
3) An analysis of paragraph vs. essay granularity and relation distance, clarifying when more general relation modeling (LSTM-ER) helps and when restrained modeling (distance-aware tagging) is preferable.

Strengths:
- Clear problem framing and motivation: The paper convincingly argues for end-to-end neural approaches that avoid manual features and ILP constraints, mitigate error propagation, and allow joint learning. The rationale for token-level modeling (to avoid assuming component boundaries) is well articulated.

- Thorough, relevant comparisons: The study spans multiple modern dependency parsers (feature-based and neural), two sequence taggers (with and without character CNNs/CRF), an MTL variant, and LSTM-ER, against a strong ILP baseline. This breadth, combined with paragraph vs. essay evaluations and F1 at exact/approximate span levels, provides a comprehensive view.

- Insightful empirical findings: The paper shows that token-level dependency parsing performs poorly relative to tagging, particularly on long sequences (essay-level), while BiLSTM taggers handle long-range dependencies via distance-encoded labels. MTL with natural subtasks (component and relation) reliably improves performance. The analysis that LSTM-ER excels on components but struggles with relations on longer documents—due to a combinatorial increase in candidate pairs—is informative, as is the observation that distance coding implicitly regularizes relation scope.

- Practical significance: Several neural models outperform or match the ILP baseline while greatly reducing manual engineering. The work thus provides actionable guidance for practitioners on model choice under different document lengths and task couplings.

Weaknesses:
- Data scale vs. model capacity and stability: The training set (~320 essays) is small relative to the capacity of the BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN) models. The paper does not report development set size/split, number of tags/tokens used for training, or training curves. Overfitting and stability are legitimate concerns in this regime. While Table 1 notes significance for a subset of comparisons, the paper does not report mean and standard deviation across multiple random initializations for each model/setting. Without these, it is difficult to assess robustness; even the reported spread for the Kiperwasser setting suggests notable variance. Please include F1 ranges/mean±std across seeds for all main results.

- Limited discussion of regularization and optimization: The manuscript and supplement provide only brief details (e.g., for LSTM-ER). Given the small dataset, explicit reporting of regularization choices (dropout rates/locations, L2/weight decay, early stopping criteria, gradient clipping), training epochs, and parameter counts is important. The current hyperparameter search could be strengthened; consider Bayesian optimization or at least a clearer accounting of the search space and selection criteria.

- Reporting and contextualization gaps: Key practical details are relegated to the supplement (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis). Some of these (embeddings used, tokenization, handling of OOV, and a concise error taxonomy) should move to the main paper. In addition, inter-annotator agreement (IAA) from the dataset paper should be summarized to contextualize achievable performance and task ambiguity.

- Presentation details: Figure 1 would benefit from higher-contrast line styles and clearer labels to ensure legibility in grayscale printing. More explicit labeling of paragraph vs. essay settings alongside all tables/figures would improve readability.

Questions to Authors (for response/clarification):
- Data splits and stability: What is the development set size and selection protocol? Please report, for each main model and for both paragraph and essay settings, mean±std (or F1 ranges) across multiple seeds/initializations. How many runs per configuration? Did you observe hyperparameter sensitivity?

- Regularization and training: What dropout rates (embedding/LSTM/CRF/char-CNN), L2 penalties, gradient clipping, and early stopping criteria did you use? How many epochs and what parameter counts per model? Please include representative training vs. dev loss/F1 curves to assess overfitting.

- Significance testing: Beyond Table 1, were significance tests conducted for MTL variants and LSTM-ER comparisons? If so, specify the test, pairing protocol, and corrections for multiple comparisons.

- Hyperparameter search: What ranges and budgets were used? Would a simple Bayesian optimization or random search over a broader space change conclusions?

- LSTM-ER specifics: Please detail scheduled sampling settings, entity pretraining, and how sentence-level dependency information is used given that 92% of relations cross sentence boundaries. Any ablations quantifying the contribution of the TreeLSTM component on this corpus?

- Reproducibility: Will you release code, pre-processing scripts, and exact configurations/checkpoints used to produce the reported numbers?

Post-rebuttal update:
Thank you for addressing my questions. Including F1-score ranges and mean±variance across runs would substantially strengthen the empirical claims and replicability.
