- Strengths:
The paper is well written and easy to follow. The proposed methods and the reported results are interesting.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation methodology and the reliability of the reported results may be problematic (see detailed comments below).

- General discussion:
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system based on neural networks, modeling the task with two approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. It also explores a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The motivation is clearly explained: prior work relies on ILP with manual feature engineering and hand-designed constraints, whereas the proposed models avoid such manual effort and jointly learn the subtasks, thereby mitigating error propagation in pipeline methods. With a few missing details (noted below), the methods are clearly described. The experiments are substantial, comparisons are appropriate, and the results are interesting.

My primary concern is the small size of the dataset relative to the capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent models (BLC and BLCC). The dataset has roughly 320 essays for training and 80 for testing; the size of the development set is not stated in the paper or the supplementary materials. This is worrisome because it leaves very few essays for training, which is critical here. The total number of tags in training is likely only a few thousand, whereas standard sequence labeling tasks often have hundreds of thousands (sometimes millions) of tags. Under these conditions, it is unclear whether the model parameters can be trained reliably. The paper also does not analyze overfitting. It would be helpful to include training and development loss curves during training (e.g., per epoch or per update). The authors themselves provide indirect evidence suggestive of overfitting: Line 622—“Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting.”

For the same reason, I am not sure about model stability. Please report means and standard deviations over multiple runs with different random initializations. Statistical significance tests would further clarify stability and the reliability of improvements. Without these, it is difficult to determine whether gains reflect the superiority of the proposed method or chance variation.

Although the neural architectures include standard regularization techniques, the dataset is small enough that regularization deserves more explicit attention. The paper does not discuss regularization, and the supplementary material mentions it only briefly for LSTM-ER. This should be addressed thoroughly in the paper.

Regarding hyperparameter tuning, consider replacing the current procedure (as described in the supplementary materials) with Bayesian optimization methods.

Please also move key details currently in the supplementary material into the main paper—specifically, information about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis. The extra page should suffice.

Including inter-annotator agreement scores would provide useful context on dataset difficulty and headroom. The dataset paper contains relevant information you can cite and summarize.

For Figure 1, consider using distinct colors or patterns to ensure the figure remains clear in black-and-white prints.

Edit:
Thank you for addressing my questions. I have increased my recommendation score to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in the paper and report means and variances across different runs/settings. I remain concerned about model stability; for example, the large variance in the Kiperwasser setting should be analyzed carefully. Even an F1 range of [0.56, 0.61] is relatively large. Reporting these score ranges in the paper will also facilitate replication.
