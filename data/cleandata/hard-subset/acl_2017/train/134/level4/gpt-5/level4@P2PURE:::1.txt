- Strengths:
The paper is clearly written and accessible. The proposed methods and findings are interesting.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation and resulting performance may be problematic (see details below).

- General Discussion:
This work presents an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, formulated via two approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. It also reports a multitask learning setup for the sequence labeling approach. The paper provides a clear motivation: prior methods rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and hand-crafted ILP constraints, whereas the proposed model avoids such manual effort. Moreover, it jointly learns the subtasks of argumentation mining, thereby mitigating error propagation in pipeline systems. Aside from a few missing details (noted below), the methods are well explained.

The experiments are substantial, the comparisons are appropriate, and the results are compelling. My main concern is the small dataset size relative to the capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent models (BLC and BLCC). The dataset has only about 320 training essays and 80 test essays. However, the paper (and the supplementary material) does not state the size of the development set. This is worrisome because very few essays remain for training, which is critical. The total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand, whereas standard sequence labeling tasks often provide hundreds of thousands (or even millions) of tags. Given this disparity, I am not confident the model parameters are being properly estimated. The paper also does not investigate overfitting. It would be helpful to include training and development loss over time (e.g., per update or per epoch). The authors themselves provide a statement suggestive of overfitting: Line 622 “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting.”

For the same reason, I am unsure about the stability of the models. Please report the mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different random initializations. Statistical significance testing would also clarify model stability and the reliability of the reported improvements. Without such tests, it is difficult to determine whether gains are due to the proposed method or chance.

I recognize that the neural architectures employ regularization, but given the very small dataset, more attention to regularization is warranted. The paper does not discuss regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions it for LSTM-ER. This should be addressed thoroughly in the main paper.

Instead of the current hyperparameter optimization procedure (described in the supplementary material), consider using Bayesian optimization. Also, please move details on pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the paper; the additional page should suffice.

Please include inter-annotator agreement scores. The dataset paper contains relevant information, and including it here would contextualize system performance and indicate headroom for improvement.

Please also consider revising Figure 1 with distinct colors or styles to improve legibility in black-and-white prints.

Edit:
Thank you for addressing my questions. I have raised my recommendation to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in the paper and report the mean and variance across different settings. I remain concerned about model stability; for example, the large variance in the Kiperwasser setting should be analyzed in depth. Even an F1 range of [0.56, 0.61] is relatively large. Reporting these ranges will facilitate replication.
