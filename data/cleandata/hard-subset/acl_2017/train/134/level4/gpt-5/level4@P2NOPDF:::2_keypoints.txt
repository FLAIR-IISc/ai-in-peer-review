- Paper compares joint neural formulations for argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL (on sequence labeling), out-of-the-box LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.
- Evaluation uses F1 for components (“concepts”) and relations; dependency-based models underperform.
- Sequence labeling is effective; LSTM-ER performs particularly well at the paragraph level.
- Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baseline.
- Supplementary material is comprehensive and transparent about training and hyperparameter tuning.
- MTL improves sequence labeling models, with the claim prediction task helping more than the relation task.
- Major concern: possible train/test topic overlap may cause leakage and inflate performance; paper should discuss/quantify this (e.g., topic-disjoint splits or robustness checks).
- Use of an off-the-shelf LSTM-ER raises novelty questions, but the study’s breadth and practical insights justify its value and publishability.
- Clarify how the full argument graph is reconstructed from a predicted tree and how non-tree edges or cycles are handled.
- Reorder exposition (dependency-based → sequence tagging → MTL) to improve readability and acronym recall.
- Fix the contradictory phrasing “decouples them but jointly models them” by specifying what is decoupled (parameters/prediction stages) and what is modeled jointly (loss/decoding).
- Explain precisely how relation information is decoupled from entity information and how the signals interact (shared representations, pipelining, joint loss).
- Clarify comparability of F1 between paragraph- and essay-level settings and how cross-paragraph relations are treated or excluded.
- Strengths: clear systematic comparison, strong ILP baseline, thorough experimental details, and convincing results for sequence labeling, MTL, and paragraph-level LSTM-ER.
- Overall: a well-executed empirical study that would be stronger by addressing topic-overlap risk, clarifying decoupling/joint modeling claims, and tightening exposition.
