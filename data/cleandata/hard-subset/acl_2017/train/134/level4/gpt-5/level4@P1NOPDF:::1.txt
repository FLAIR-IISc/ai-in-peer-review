- Strengths:
The paper is well written and easy to follow. The methods and results are interesting.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation and reported results may be problematic (see below).

- General Discussion:
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the task via two approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. It also explores multitask learning for sequence labeling. The motivation is clearly articulated: prior work relies on ILP, manual feature engineering, and hand-crafted ILP constraints, while the proposed model avoids such manual effort. Moreover, the model jointly learns subtasks in argumentation mining, mitigating the error propagation problem common in pipelines. Aside from a few missing details (noted below), the methods are explained clearly.

The experiments are substantial, comparisons are appropriate, and the results are interesting. My main concern is the small dataset relative to the capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset has only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing; the size of the development set is not mentioned in either the paper or the supplementary material. This is worrisome because very few essays remain for training, which is critical. The total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand—far smaller than standard sequence labeling tasks, which often have hundreds of thousands or even millions of tags. Given this, I am not confident the model parameters are well estimated.

Relatedly, the paper does not analyze overfitting. It would be useful to see training and development loss curves during training (after each update or after each epoch). The authors themselves provide a hint of overfitting: Line 622—“Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting.”

For the same reason, I am unsure about model stability. Please report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs with different random initializations. Statistical significance tests would further clarify stability and the reliability of improvements. Without these, it is hard to tell whether gains are due to the proposed method or chance.

I recognize that the neural models include some regularization techniques. However, given the very small dataset, greater attention to regularization is necessary. The paper does not discuss regularization at all, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization for LSTM-ER. This needs to be addressed directly in the main text (e.g., types of regularization used, values, and their impact).

Regarding hyperparameter tuning, please consider Bayesian optimization instead of the current method described in the supplementary material.

Also, move details about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main paper; the extra page should suffice.

Please include inter-annotator agreement scores. The dataset paper contains relevant information, and including it here would contextualize system performance and the remaining headroom.

For Figure 1, please revise the illustration to use clearly distinguishable colors and styles so it remains legible in black-and-white prints.

Edit:
Thank you for answering my questions. I have increased my recommendation to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in the paper and report mean and variance across different settings. I remain concerned about stability. For example, the large variance for the Kiperwasser setting should be analyzed thoroughly. Even an F1 range of [0.56, 0.61] is relatively large. Including these ranges will help others replicate your work.
