Paper summary and contributions (as I see them)
- This paper presents a thorough empirical study of neural, end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays dataset. The authors explore several framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing; (2) token-level sequence tagging with labels that jointly encode component boundaries, types, relation distances, and stances; (3) multi-task learning (MTL) with “natural” auxiliary tasks; (4) an off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER); and (5) comparison to a feature-based ILP baseline.
- Main contributions:
  1) A comprehensive evaluation showing that neural models, especially sequence taggers, outperform the prior ILP-based joint approach on end-to-end AM.
  2) Evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for this end-to-end setting, while simpler BiLSTM taggers are robust and capture long-range dependencies via distance-encoded labels.
  3) Demonstration that MTL with natural subtasks improves performance, with component detection (claims/premises/major claims) helping more than relation prediction.

Strengths
- Breadth and depth of evaluation: The study compares multiple neural framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, and LSTM-ER) against a strong ILP baseline, across both paragraph and essay levels, and reports component and relation F1 under exact and approximate matching. The supplementary material is detailed and transparent about training and hyperparameters.
- Clear empirical findings with useful insights: Dependency parsing underperforms substantially, especially at essay level, while sequence tagging performs robustly. The paper offers plausible explanations (e.g., global search complexity for long sequences and the vast tree search space), which are valuable for the community.
- Strong results with practical takeaways: Both the BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP approach. LSTM-ER is particularly strong at component detection and on paragraph-level evaluation. The study also shows that explicit distance encoding biases the tagger toward realistic short-range relations, improving relation F1 compared to a fully general relation model on long documents.
- Multi-task learning benefits: Adding auxiliary tasks improves tagging performance, with component identification helping more consistently than relation identification. This is an actionable design insight for future AM systems.
- Reproducibility and analysis: The work is careful in reporting settings and ablations, and it discusses why models degrade from paragraph to essay level (e.g., O(m^2) relation candidates for LSTM-ER versus constrained search in the tagging scheme).

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage across topics: If training and test essays address overlapping topics, authors may reuse similar arguments, which could inflate performance estimates. While this affects ILP baselines as well, it would be useful to quantify topic overlap or report results under topic-based splits (if feasible).
- Novelty concerns around the strongest model: One of the top-performing systems (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work. The paper’s value lies in the comparative analysis and lessons learned across framings, but the novelty of the best-performing model itself is limited.
- Exposition clarity:
  - Lines 222–226: It is not fully clear how the full graph is reconstructed if the model outputs a tree. Please clarify the reconstruction process and any assumptions or constraints used.
  - Lines 443–444: The section order (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → MTL built on sequence tagging) is hard to follow. Reversing the first two would reduce cognitive load, since STag_T is defined earlier.
  - Line 455: “De-couples them but jointly models them” is confusing. Joint modeling typically implies coupling; please explain the precise sense in which the components and relations are de-coupled yet trained jointly.
  - Lines 477–479: The statement that the system “de-couples relation information from entity information” is unclear. If you mean that some modules learn structure (edges) and others learn labels on those edges, please state this explicitly.
- Comparability of paragraph vs. essay F1: It is unclear whether relation F1 is directly comparable across paragraph and essay settings, given that paragraph-based models may inherently miss cross-paragraph relations. Please clarify what relations are evaluated in each setting and whether any are excluded by design.

Questions to authors
- Topic overlap: Do train and test sets share topics? If so, can you estimate the degree of overlap and discuss its potential impact? Have you considered topic-held-out evaluation?
- Graph reconstruction (Lines 222–226): When modeling AM as dependency parsing with token-level trees, how exactly do you recover the full argument graph (especially with claims linking to multiple major claims)? What post-processing is required?
- Presentation order (Lines 443–444): Would you consider presenting dependency parsing before sequence tagging, or briefly reintroducing STag_T when you return to it? The current order makes it hard to track the notation.
- Joint vs. de-coupled modeling (Line 455): Please clarify how components and relations are “de-coupled but jointly modeled” in LSTM-ER. Is the idea that entity tagging and relation classification are separate modules trained together with shared parameters?
- Decoupling explanation (Lines 477–479): If the distinction you intend is between predicting tree edges versus labeling those edges, please make that explicit and describe how it manifests in the architectures you compare.
- Paragraph vs. essay evaluation: Are relation F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings directly comparable? Do paragraph-level systems inherently ignore cross-paragraph relations (e.g., claim-to-major-claim), and how is this reflected in the evaluation?

Overall recommendation
This is a thorough, well-executed study that advances the state of the art in end-to-end AM and provides clear, practical guidance on model choice. Despite the limited novelty of the strongest model, the empirical insights and careful comparisons make this paper a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance.
