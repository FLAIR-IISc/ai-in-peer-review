This paper presents a joint neural approach to end-to-end argumentation mining and evaluates several alternatives:
- Casting the task as dependency parsing (with multiple parsers)
- Casting the task as sequence labeling
- Multitask learning (MTL) built on top of a sequence-labeling model
- An out-of-the-box neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER)
- ILP-based state-of-the-art models

All approaches are evaluated using F1 on concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions do not perform well, whereas sequence-labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. The supplementary material is comprehensive and clearly details training procedures, hyperparameter optimization, and other technicalities. The paper also shows that sequence-labeling models can be substantially improved via multitask learning, with the claim task providing more benefit than the relation task. Overall, this is a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major remarks
- Data split and potential topic leakage: I am concerned that essays in the training and test sets may be on the same topics. Writers might use similar arguments across essays, potentially causing information leakage from train to test and inflating performance estimates. I believe the same issue would affect the ILP baselines, so your models do not have an unfair advantage, but this deserves explicit discussion.
- Out-of-the-box LSTM-ER: One of your strongest models (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. However, given the strong performance of sequence-based models, the breadth of experiments, and the useful lessons learned, I still believe the paper merits publication.

Minor remarks and questions
- Lines 222–226: I infer you are arguing that the full graph can be reconstructed once a tree is produced. This is not entirely clear; please elaborate on the reconstruction procedure and any assumptions or guarantees.
- Lines 443–444: The section order is sequence tagging → dependency-based → MTL using sequence tagging. It would be easier to follow if the first two were reversed, since by this point it is easy to forget what STag_T stands for.
- Line 455: The statement that the model “decouples them but jointly models them” is confusing. Joint modeling typically implies some coupling. I checked Miwa and Bansal but could not find a justification for this phrasing; please clarify what is decoupled, what is joint, and how.
- Lines 477–479: It is confusing to say the system “decouples relation information from entity information.” I suspect you mean it learns some tasks as “edges of the tree” and others as “labels on those edges,” thereby decoupling them. If so, please state this explicitly and explain how the decoupling is implemented and leveraged.
- Comparability of F1 scores across paragraph and essay settings: Are these F1 scores directly comparable, especially for relations? Paragraph-based models may by construction miss cross-paragraph relations, since they never consider them. Please clarify how this affects evaluation and interpretation across settings.
