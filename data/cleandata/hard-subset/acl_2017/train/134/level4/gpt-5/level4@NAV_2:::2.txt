The paper presents a neural, end-to-end study of argumentation mining. It explores multiple formulations and compares them empirically:
 1) recasting the task as dependency parsing (evaluating several parsers)
 2) recasting the task as sequence tagging
 3) multi-task learning built on the sequence tagging formulation
 4) an off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER)
 5) state-of-the-art ILP-based pipelines
All methods are evaluated with F1 on components and relations. Dependency-based formulations underperform, whereas sequence tagging is effective. The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model performs strongly, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER approaches outperform the ILP baseline. The supplementary material is extensive and clearly documents training details and hyperparameter optimization. The work also shows that multi-task learning substantially improves sequence tagging, with the “claim” component task being more beneficial than the relation task. Overall, this is a careful, thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

- Major remarks
  - I am concerned about potential topic overlap between training and test essays. If the same or closely related prompts appear in both splits, writers might reuse similar arguments, creating leakage that inflates test performance. That said, the ILP baselines would be affected similarly, so this does not give your models an undue advantage. Still, it would be helpful to discuss this and, if possible, quantify topic overlap or control for it.
  - A further concern is that one of your top-performing systems, LSTM-ER, is essentially a direct application of prior work. However, given the compelling evidence in favor of sequence-based models, the breadth of experiments, and the practical insights derived, I still believe this submission warrants publication.

- Minor remarks and questions:
  222–226: If I understand correctly, you argue that the full argument graph can be recovered from a tree output by the model. This section remains unclear—please spell out the reconstruction step explicitly.
  443–444: The section order is sequence tagging → dependency parsing → MTL over sequence tagging. It would read more naturally if dependency parsing preceded sequence tagging (I had to scroll back to recall what STag_T denotes).
  455: “De-couples them but jointly models them” is confusing. Doesn’t joint modeling imply some form of coupling? Please clarify the intended distinction. I checked Miwa and Bansal but could not find this phrasing there.
  477–479: The statement that your system de-couples relation information from entity information is confusing. My best guess is you mean some tasks are learned as “tree edges” and others as “edge labels,” thereby separating concerns. In any case, please make this more explicit.
  Are paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores directly comparable, particularly for relations? Paragraph-based models might miss cross-paragraph links by construction if they never consider them. Please clarify the comparability and any adjustments made.
