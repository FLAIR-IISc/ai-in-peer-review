The paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining. It explores several strategies, including:
 1) formulating the task as dependency parsing (testing multiple parsers)
 2) framing it as sequence labeling
 3) multi-task learning built on top of the sequence labeling model
 4) an off-the-shelf neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER)
 5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models
All approaches are evaluated using F1 on concepts and relations. Dependency-based methods perform poorly, whereas sequence labeling methods are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs very strongly, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. The supplementary material is very comprehensive, covering training details, hyperparameter optimization, etc. It is also shown that sequence labeling models can be substantially improved via multi-task learning (with the claim task helping more than the relation task). Overall, this is a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

- Major remarks
  - I have a concern about the dataset: essays in the training and test splits may address the same topics, so authors might reuse similar arguments across essays, leading to train–test leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates. That said, the ILP models would be subject to the same issue, so your model likely does not have an unfair advantage; still, this is worth discussing.
  - I am also concerned that one of your best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work. However, given the success of sequence-based models and the breadth of experiments and takeaways, I believe this work deserves publication.
