- Paper evaluates joint neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, comparing dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
- Evaluation uses F1 on concepts and relations; sequence-labeling models perform well while dependency-based solutions perform poorly.  
- Out-of-the-box LSTM-ER performs very well, especially at paragraph level.  
- Sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER models both outperform ILP-based state-of-the-art.  
- Multitask learning substantially improves sequence-labeling models, with the claim task providing more benefit than the relation task.  
- Supplementary material is comprehensive and clearly documents training procedures and hyperparameter optimization.  
- Major concern: potential topic leakage from train to test due to essays sharing topics, which could inflate performance and needs explicit discussion.  
- Major note: one of the strongest models (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application of prior work, though the experimental breadth still supports publication.  
- Request clarification on how a full graph is reconstructed from a produced tree (Lines 222–226), including assumptions and guarantees.  
- Suggest reordering sections to present dependency-based before sequence tagging for readability (Lines 443–444).  
- Ask to clarify the phrasing about “decouples them but jointly models them” and what is decoupled versus joint (Line 455).  
- Ask to clarify what is meant by “decouples relation information from entity information,” how that decoupling is implemented, and how it’s leveraged (Lines 477–479).  
- Clarify whether F1 scores are comparable across paragraph and essay settings, since paragraph models cannot capture cross-paragraph relations and this affects evaluation/interpretation.
