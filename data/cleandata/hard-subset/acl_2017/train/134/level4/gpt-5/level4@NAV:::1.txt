Review-
- Strengths:
The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow. The proposed methods and the findings are compelling.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation setup and reported results may be questionable (see detailed comments below).

- General Discussion:
This paper presents an end-to-end argumentation mining system built with neural networks. The task is formulated via two strategies: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. The work also explores a multitask learning configuration for the sequence labeling approach. The paper articulates the motivation for the model well. Prior approaches rely on ILP, handcrafted features, and manually designed ILP constraints, whereas the proposed model avoids such manual effort. Additionally, the model jointly learns argumentation subtasks, thereby mitigating the error propagation typical of pipeline systems. With the exception of a few missing details (listed below), the methods are described clearly.

The experiments are substantial, the comparisons are appropriate, and the results are interesting. My primary concern is the tension between the small dataset and the high capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent architectures (BLC and BLCC). The dataset contains roughly 320 essays for training and 80 for testing; however, the development set size is not stated in the paper or the supplementary material. This is problematic because it leaves very few essays for training, which is critical. The total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand, in contrast to standard sequence labeling tasks that often involve hundreds of thousands or even millions of tags. Consequently, I am uncertain whether the model parameters are adequately trained. The paper also lacks an analysis of overfitting. It would be useful to show training and development loss curves during training (either after each update or per epoch). The authors themselves provide a hint suggestive of overfitting: at Line 622, “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting.”

For the same reason, I am unsure about model stability. Please report the mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different random initializations. Statistical significance testing would further clarify the stability of the models and the reliability of the improvements. Without such tests, it is difficult to determine whether gains reflect true model superiority or randomness.

While I understand the neural architectures employ regularization, the small dataset necessitates extra attention to regularization choices. The paper does not discuss regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions it for LSTM-ER. This should be addressed thoroughly in the main text.

Instead of the current hyperparameter tuning procedure (as described in the supplement), consider Bayesian optimization methods.

Also, move the details about the pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplement into the main paper. The extra page should suffice.

Please include inter-annotator agreement statistics. The dataset paper provides relevant figures. This would contextualize system performance and indicate headroom for improvement.

Please adjust Figure 1’s color scheme to ensure better readability in black-and-white printouts.

Edit:
Thank you for addressing my questions. I have raised my recommendation to 4. Please include the F1-score ranges in the paper and report the mean and variance across different configurations. I remain concerned about model stability. For instance, the large variance in the Kiperwasser setting warrants careful analysis. Even an F1 fluctuation within [0.56, 0.61] is relatively large. Reporting these ranges will aid reproducibility.
