Summary
This paper proposes an end-to-end neural approach to argumentation mining, instantiated via two formulations: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. The authors also explore a multitask learning setup for sequence labeling. The motivation is well argued: compared to prior ILP-based systems with hand-engineered features and constraints, the proposed neural models reduce manual effort and enable joint learning of subtasks, potentially mitigating error propagation in pipelines. With a few exceptions noted below, the methods are clearly described, and the empirical comparisons are substantial and thoughtfully designed.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and well-written presentation.
- Interesting modeling choices (sequence labeling vs. dependency parsing) and inclusion of a multitask variant.
- Thorough baselines and generally careful empirical comparisons.
- Potentially impactful shift away from ILP/feature engineering toward end-to-end learning.

Weaknesses
- Risk of overfitting and instability due to very limited data relative to model capacity; insufficient analysis of this issue.
- Missing details about the development set, regularization, and training diagnostics.
- Lack of robustness reporting (e.g., multiple random seeds) and significance testing.
- Some crucial experimental details (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis) are relegated to the supplement.
- No inter-annotator agreement (IAA) discussion to contextualize upper bounds and error sources.
- Minor presentation issues (e.g., figure readability in grayscale).

Detailed comments
1) Data scale vs. model capacity and overfitting
- The dataset reportedly comprises ~320 training essays and 80 test essays; the development split is not stated. Given the likely small number of labeled tokens/spans/relations (on the order of only a few thousand tags), there is a substantial risk that large BiLSTM-based models will overfit.
- The paper does not analyze overfitting. Please report training and development losses (or F1) across epochs for key settings, and describe early stopping criteria.
- The paper itself notes that simpler taggers may be less prone to overfitting. This underscores the need to quantify and diagnose overfitting in the more complex models.

2) Model stability and statistical validity
- Report mean and standard deviation (or 95% confidence intervals) over multiple runs with different random initializations for all main results. Given the data size, sensitivity to initialization is expected and must be characterized.
- Include statistical significance testing of key comparisons (e.g., paired bootstrap or approximate randomization over document-level outputs). Without this, it is hard to attribute gains to the method rather than chance.

3) Development set and evaluation protocol
- Specify the development set size and how it was constructed. Ensure that all hyperparameter tuning and early stopping use only the development set.
- Given the small dataset, consider k-fold cross-validation or repeated hold-out to provide more reliable performance estimates, or at least report robustness across multiple train/dev/test splits if available.

4) Regularization and training details
- Regularization is under-specified in the main paper. Please detail all techniques used (dropout rates and where applied, recurrent dropout/variational dropout, L2 weight decay, word/embedding dropout, gradient clipping, label smoothing, early stopping criteria).
- Provide ablations showing the impact of key regularizers on development performance and stability. This is especially important with limited data.
- If using pre-trained embeddings, clarify whether they are frozen or fine-tuned and whether any additional regularization is applied to them.

5) Hyperparameter optimization
- The current search procedure (as described in the supplement) seems limited for the model complexity. Consider Bayesian optimization (e.g., Gaussian process or TPE-based) to explore learning rates, hidden sizes, dropout rates, and layer counts more efficiently and reduce overfitting risk.

6) Reporting and reproducibility
- Move critical details from the supplement into the main paper, space permitting: sources and dimensions of pre-trained embeddings, OOV handling, tokenization/casing, and error analysis. One additional page should suffice.
- Include inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen’s κ or Krippendorff’s α) from the dataset paper. These figures contextualize the achievable performance and help interpret system errors.
- Report F1 score ranges across seeds for core settings, as well as mean and variance, to aid replication.
- If possible, discuss the label distribution (e.g., class imbalance) and how it was handled (loss weighting, sampling).

7) Presentation
- Improve the readability of Figure 1 for grayscale printing—use distinct colors with patterns or line styles to ensure adequate contrast.
- Ensure all acronyms (e.g., BLC, BLCC) are defined upon first use.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and well-motivated contribution to end-to-end argumentation mining, with clear exposition and relevant baselines. However, the reliability of the reported gains is difficult to assess without stronger evidence of stability and generalization. The combination of a small dataset and high-capacity models necessitates more rigorous regularization, training diagnostics, and robustness reporting. Addressing the points above would substantially strengthen the paper and its reproducibility.
