Summary and main contributions
This paper studies neural, end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) at the token level. The authors compare several framings: (a) sequence tagging with BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tags that jointly encode component boundaries, types, relation distances, and stance; (b) token-level dependency parsing; (c) multi-task learning (MTL) variants of the tagging approach; and (d) an adaptation of LSTM-ER that decouples entity and relation prediction. On a standard persuasive essay dataset, they report that:
1) Token-level dependency parsing performs poorly relative to sequence tagging, a useful negative result for the community.
2) Neural end-to-end models, especially BiLSTM-based taggers, achieve strong results and can surpass a feature-rich ILP baseline without hand-crafted features or constraints.
3) Joint learning via MTL of “natural” subtasks (component and relation labeling) provides further gains.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation. The paper is well written and motivates why end-to-end neural models are appealing for AM: they avoid manual feature engineering and hand-designed ILP constraints, and can exploit joint learning instead of pipelines with error propagation. The token-level framing and the design of the tagging label space (including distance and stance) are clearly explained.

- Comprehensive empirical comparison. The work evaluates multiple neural paradigms (sequence tagging, dependency parsing, MTL, and LSTM-ER), across paragraph and essay levels, with component- and relation-level metrics under exact and approximate matching. The breadth of comparisons makes the findings informative beyond a single model.

- Useful methodological insights. The results show: (i) token-level dependency parsing is a poor fit in this end-to-end setting; (ii) relatively “local” BiLSTM-based taggers are robust and handle long-range dependencies via distance encoding; (iii) MTL on natural subtasks (especially component detection) helps; and (iv) model choice should depend on document length (e.g., LSTM-ER excels at paragraph level).

- Competitive performance without hand-crafted features. The neural models match or outperform the ILP baseline, removing the need for manually engineered features and constraints—an important practical contribution given dataset variability in AM.

Weaknesses
- Small data vs. model capacity; missing development split. The training set (∼320 essays) is small for high-capacity BiLSTM(-CRF-CNN) models. The development set size and selection protocol are not reported (in the paper or supplement). This raises concerns about proper hyperparameter tuning and the reliability of early stopping. Please specify the dev split, how it was chosen, and whether cross-validation was considered.

- Limited analysis of overfitting and training dynamics. There is no analysis of training vs. dev loss/metrics across epochs or parameter updates. Given the data scarcity, plots or summaries of training and dev curves would help assess overfitting, early stopping, and convergence.

- Lack of robustness reporting across random seeds. Results are mostly reported as single runs. Please report mean, standard deviation, and ranges over multiple initializations for key settings, and apply statistical significance tests consistently. Model stability is a central concern; even ranges such as F1 ∈ [0.56, 0.61] are non-trivial and should be documented and discussed (e.g., the high variance observed for Kiperwasser).

- Regularization details are underreported. Regularization is barely discussed (only briefly for LSTM-ER in the supplement). Given the dataset size, please detail dropout placements and rates, L2/weight decay, gradient clipping, early stopping criteria, learning rate schedules, and any data augmentation. An ablation on regularization choices would be valuable.

- Hyperparameter search methodology. The current search procedure (supplementary) seems limited. Consider Bayesian optimization or at least a systematic search with clear budgets and dev-based selection. Report the parameter counts for each model to contextualize capacity vs. data size.

- Reporting and presentation. Key implementation details (pre-trained embeddings, core error analyses) live in the supplement; essential elements should move into the main paper. Additionally, please report inter-annotator agreement from the dataset paper to contextualize achievable performance and remaining headroom. Figure 1 would benefit from color/line-style choices that remain clear in grayscale prints.

Questions to authors
- What is the exact development set size and selection protocol? Were any essays from the test set used during tuning by accident (e.g., via early stopping on combined sets)?
- What regularization settings were used for each model (dropout placements/rates, L2, gradient clipping), and how sensitive are results to them?
- How many parameters does each model have? Are there capacity-controlled variants (e.g., fewer layers/hidden units) and how do they perform?
- Ablations: (i) effect of the CRF layer and of character CNNs; (ii) effect of including the distance feature d in the tag set; (iii) effect of stance encoding; (iv) MTL layer selection choices.
- How frequently do the neural models violate known structural constraints (e.g., one parent per premise), and to what extent do they nevertheless learn these constraints from data?
- Can you provide F1 score means, standard deviations, and ranges across 5–10 seeds for key systems (e.g., STag-BLCC, LSTM-ER, Kiperwasser) on both paragraph and essay levels?
