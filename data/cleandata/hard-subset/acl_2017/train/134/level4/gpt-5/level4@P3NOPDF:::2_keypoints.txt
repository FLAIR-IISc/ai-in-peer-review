- Empirical study comparing five modeling paradigms for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency-parsing, sequence labeling, MTL on sequence labeling, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
- Evaluation metric: F1 over concepts and relations.  
- Key findings: dependency-based formulations perform poorly; sequence labeling is effective; LSTM-ER performs very strongly (especially at paragraph level); sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform ILP baselines.  
- MTL substantially improves sequence labeling, with claim detection providing more benefit than the relation task.  
- Supplementary material is comprehensive and supports reproducibility (training details, hyperparameter tuning).  
- Strength: comprehensive empirical comparison including strong non-neural baselines, careful engineering, and practical lessons for the community.  
- Major concern: potential topic leakage across train/test splits may inflate absolute scores; recommend topic-disjoint (leave-one-prompt-out) evaluation or reporting performance stratified by topic overlap.  
- Major concern: limited novelty because LSTM-ER is an off-the-shelf model; paper should clearly state what was adapted, what is new, and include ablations isolating task-specific adaptations.  
- Minor request: clarify the tree-to-graph post-processing procedure—constraints, which edges are added back, and when reconstruction is guaranteed (with a small example).  
- Minor request: reorder method descriptions for clarity and clarify confusing wording about “de-couples them but jointly models them” and “decouples relation info from entity info” with explicit factorization or diagram.  
- Minor request: clarify comparability of paragraph vs essay F1 (cross-paragraph links), and possibly report intra-paragraph-only evaluation in the essay setting for apples-to-apples comparison.  
- Suggested analyses: add error analysis (typical false positives/negatives, boundary vs label errors), report precision/recall breakdowns, and ablate components to see if LSTM-ER gains come from span detection, relation modeling, or both.  
- Minor editorial: fix typos and capitalization.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed empirical study with valuable findings and guidance; addressing topic-leakage and clarification issues would strengthen the paper—reviewer leans toward acceptance.
