This paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining. The authors explore several approaches, including:
- Casting the task as a dependency parsing problem (trying several different parsers)
- Casting the task as a sequence labeling problem
- Multi-task learning (built on a sequence labeling model)
- An out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER)
- ILP-based state-of-the-art models

All approaches are evaluated using F1 defined on concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions do not perform well, whereas sequence labeling approaches are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. A very comprehensive supplement is provided, covering training details, hyperparameter optimization, and other technicalities. It is also shown that sequence labeling models can be substantially improved by the multitask approach (with the claim task helping more than the relation task). Overall, the paper is a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

- Major remarks
  - My main concern is with the dataset: is it problematic that essays in the training and test sets may be on the same topics? Writers might use the same or similar arguments in both, potentially causing information leakage from train to test and yielding overly optimistic performance estimates. That said, the same issue would affect the ILP models, so your approach does not have an unfair advantage. Still, this warrants discussion.
  - A second concern is that one of your strongest models, LSTM-ER, is essentially an out-of-the-box application from related work. However, given the success of sequence-based models and the breadth of experiments and useful insights, I believe this work deserves to be published.

- Minor remarks and questions:
  - Lines 222–226: I infer that you are arguing it is possible to reconstruct the full graph once you obtain a tree as output. This is not entirely clear; please clarify.
  - Lines 443–444: The ordering in this section is sequence tagging → dependency-based → MTL using sequence tagging. It would be easier to follow if the first two were reversed; by the time I reached this point I had forgotten what STag_T stands for.
  - Line 455: What does it mean to “de-couple them but jointly model them”? Isn’t coupling required to jointly model them? I checked Miwa and Bansal and could not find this.
  - Lines 477–479: It is confusing when you say your system de-couples relation information from entity information. My best guess is that you mean it learns some tasks as “the edges of the tree” and others as “the labels on those edges,” thus decoupling them. In any case, please make this part clearer.
  - Are the F1 scores in the paragraph- and essay-level settings comparable, particularly for the relation tasks? Paragraph-based models might miss cross-paragraph relations by default, since they never consider them.
