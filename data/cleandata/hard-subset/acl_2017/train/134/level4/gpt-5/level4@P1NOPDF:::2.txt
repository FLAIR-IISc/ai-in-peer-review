This paper presents a joint neural approach to end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore several formulations:
1) Casting the task as dependency parsing (testing several parsers)
2) Casting it as sequence labeling
3) Multitask learning (built on the sequence labeling model)
4) An out-of-the-box neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER)
5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models (as baselines)

All approaches are evaluated using F1 on concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling models are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. The supplementary material is comprehensive and covers training details, hyperparameter optimization, and other technicalities. The authors also show that sequence labeling models benefit substantially from multitask learning, with the claim task providing more benefit than the relation task. Overall, this is a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major remarks
- My main concern is the dataset split. It seems possible that essays in the training and test sets cover the same topics. If so, authors may reuse similar arguments across essays, leading to potential information leakage from train to test and thus overly optimistic performance estimates. I believe the same issue would affect the ILP baselines, so your models would not have an unfair advantage, but this deserves discussion.
- Another concern is that one of your strongest models, LSTM-ER, is essentially an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. Nonetheless, given the strong results of sequence-based models and the breadth of experiments and insights, I believe the paper merits publication.

Minor remarks and questions
- Lines 222–226: I understand you argue that the full graph can be reconstructed once a tree is produced. This part is not entirely clear; please clarify how the reconstruction proceeds and under what assumptions it holds.
- Lines 443–444: The evaluation is presented in the order sequence tagging → dependency-based → MTL using sequence tagging. It would be easier to follow if the first two were reversed, as by this point I had forgotten what STag_T stood for.
- Line 455: What does it mean to “decouple” components while “jointly modeling” them? Isn’t coupling typically required for joint modeling? I checked Miwa and Bansal but could not find this claim stated as such; please clarify the intended meaning and provide a precise citation if applicable.
- Lines 477–479: The statement that your system decouples relation information from entity information is confusing. My best guess is that you mean some subtasks learn “tree edges” and others learn “labels on those edges,” thereby decoupling them. In any case, please make this part clearer.
- Are F1 scores in the paragraph-level and essay-level settings comparable, especially for relation tasks? Paragraph-based models may systematically miss cross-paragraph relations because they never consider them. Please discuss how this affects comparability and interpretation of results.
