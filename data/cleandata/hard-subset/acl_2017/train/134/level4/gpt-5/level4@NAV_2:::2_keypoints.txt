- Paper studies end-to-end neural argumentation mining across several formulations: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, off-the-shelf LSTM-ER, and ILP-based pipelines.  
- Evaluation uses F1 on components and relations; dependency parsing formulations underperform while sequence tagging is effective.  
- Off-the-shelf LSTM-ER performs strongly, especially at paragraph level, and both sequence tagging and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baseline.  
- Supplementary material is extensive and clearly documents training details and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Multi-task learning substantially improves sequence tagging, with the “claim” component task contributing more than the relation task.  
- Overall assessment: careful, thorough empirical investigation that warrants publication despite some concerns.  
- Major concern: potential topic overlap between training and test essays could cause leakage and inflate test performance; recommend discussing or quantifying overlap.  
- Note: ILP baselines would be similarly affected by topic overlap, so leakage does not uniquely advantage the new models.  
- Concern that LSTM-ER is essentially a direct application of prior work, reducing novelty despite strong empirical results.  
- Minor clarification needed: explicitly spell out how the full argument graph is reconstructed from a tree output (lines 222–226).  
- Suggest reordering sections so dependency parsing precedes sequence tagging for clearer flow (lines 443–444).  
- Clarify confusing phrasing “De-couples them but jointly models them” and the intended distinction between coupling and joint modeling (line 455).  
- Clarify the statement that the system “de-couples relation information from entity information,” e.g., whether tasks are separated as tree edges versus edge labels (lines 477–479).  
- Clarify whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores are directly comparable, and how cross-paragraph links are handled or controlled for.
