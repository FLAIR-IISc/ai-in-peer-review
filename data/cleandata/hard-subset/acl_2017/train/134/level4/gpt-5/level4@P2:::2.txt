Summary and contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore four framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) token-level sequence tagging with labels that encode component BIO, type, stance, and inter-component distance, (3) multi-task learning (MTL) using natural subtasks as auxiliaries, and (4) an off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER). They compare these with a feature-rich ILP-based joint baseline and evaluate on the PE dataset at paragraph and essay levels using component- and relation-level F1.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A comprehensive empirical study showing that neural end-to-end models—especially BiLSTM sequence tagging and LSTM-ER—match or outperform a strong ILP-based joint baseline across settings. This establishes neural models as state of the art for end-to-end AM on PE.
2) Clear evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, while simpler BiLSTM sequence taggers with distance-coded labels are robust and handle long-range dependencies better.
3) Demonstration that MTL with natural subtasks (especially component detection) improves sequence tagging performance, providing actionable guidance on model design.

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence and coverage: The paper evaluates multiple families of neural models (parsers, taggers, MTL, LSTM-ER) against a competitive ILP baseline, at both paragraph and essay levels. Results consistently show that sequence tagging (BLCC) and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP system on paragraph-level component and relation F1. The analysis of performance degradation from paragraphs to essays is careful and informative.

- Practical modeling insights: The work identifies when more general relation modeling helps (shorter texts, fewer entities) and when a more constrained approach is preferable (longer texts, many entities). Encoding component distance in the labels biases learning toward realistic local dependencies and narrows the search space, which seems crucial on essays.

- Impact of task decoupling and multi-tasking: Decoupling entity and relation prediction (as in LSTM-ER) greatly improves component identification. Similarly, MTL with components and relations as auxiliary tasks improves the sequence tagger by several points, with component detection consistently more helpful than relation prediction.

- Thorough experimental methodology and transparency: The paper includes extensive supplementary material (training details, hyperparameter tuning, software, error analysis) and significance testing, aiding reproducibility and making the comparisons credible.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage via topic overlap: It is unclear whether train/test essays share topics, which could lead to similar arguments appearing in both splits and inflate performance. While this would affect all systems, it raises questions about generalization and should be discussed and, if possible, controlled or analyzed.

- Limited methodological novelty in best-performing model: A key strong model (LSTM-ER) is an off-the-shelf adaptation from prior work. The main contribution is empirical rather than algorithmic. This is acceptable given the breadth and depth of the study, but the paper could better emphasize original modeling beyond the tagging label design and MTL configuration.

- Clarity issues in exposition:
  • Lines 222–226: It is not sufficiently clear how the full argument graph is reconstructed from a token-level tree representation (e.g., handling multiple links from claims to major claims). A precise description or example would help.
  • Lines 443–444: The presentation order (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → MTL over tagging) makes it hard to follow references back to STag_T. Reversing the first two would improve readability.
  • Line 455: “De-couples them but jointly models them” is contradictory as written. Please clarify the training/inference coupling (e.g., separate predictors with shared parameters and joint training vs. pipelined inference).
  • Lines 477–479: Saying the system de-couples relation from entity information is confusing. If the intent is that some modules predict component spans/types and others predict edge existence/labels, please make the interface and information flow explicit.

- Comparability across paragraph vs. essay settings: It is not fully addressed whether F1 scores are directly comparable between paragraph- and essay-level evaluation, especially for relations. Paragraph-level models may inherently miss cross-paragraph links (except via major-claim conventions), potentially biasing results. A quantitative breakdown of cross-paragraph relations and how each model handles them would clarify.

Questions to authors
- Data splits and topic leakage: Do training and test sets share essay topics? If so, can you report performance by seen vs. unseen topics or provide a robustness analysis (e.g., topic-held-out evaluation) to assess generalization?

- Tree-to-graph reconstruction (Lines 222–226): Please detail the procedure to reconstruct the full argument graph from token-level dependency outputs, including how multiple links from claims to major claims are handled and how the special root is used.

- “De-couples but jointly models” (Line 455) and “de-coupling relation from entity information” (Lines 477–479): Could you clarify the architecture and training/inference schedule? For LSTM-ER, what exactly is shared, what is pipelined, and what losses are optimized jointly?

- Metrics comparability: Are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores directly comparable? How many relations cross sentence and paragraph boundaries in the PE data, and how do models that operate at paragraph level account for relations spanning paragraphs (e.g., claims to major claims)?

- Ablations: How much of the sequence tagger’s gain comes from distance encoding vs. BiLSTM-CRF-CNN capacity? Could you report ablations removing distance or stance from the tagset and quantify the impact? Similarly, can you quantify MTL gains per auxiliary task and layer choice?

Overall evaluation
This is a thorough and impactful empirical study that advances the state of the art in end-to-end AM with clear, practically useful findings. Despite limited novelty in one strong baseline (LSTM-ER), the breadth of comparison, methodological insights, and performance improvements warrant publication. Addressing the clarity issues and the topic-overlap concern would further strengthen the paper.
