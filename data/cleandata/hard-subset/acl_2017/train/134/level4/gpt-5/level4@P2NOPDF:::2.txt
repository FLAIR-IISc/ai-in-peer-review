This paper presents a joint neural approach to end-to-end argumentation mining and compares several formulations:

1) Casting the task as dependency parsing (with multiple parsers)
2) Casting it as sequence labeling
3) Multitask learning (MTL) built on the sequence labeling model
4) An off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity–relation extraction (LSTM-ER)
5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models

Evaluation is via F1 on components (“concepts”) and relations. Dependency-based solutions underperform, while sequence labeling is effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs particularly well, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP baseline. The supplementary material is comprehensive and transparent about training details and hyperparameter tuning. The sequence labeling models benefit notably from MTL (with the claim task helping more than the relation task). Overall, this is a thorough and informative investigation of neural approaches to argumentation mining.

Major remarks
- Dataset split/topic overlap: I am concerned that essays in train and test sets may cover the same topics. Writers might reuse similar arguments, creating potential leakage and inflating performance estimates. This likely affects the ILP baselines as well, so there is no unfair advantage, but the paper should discuss this risk and, if possible, quantify it (e.g., via topic-disjoint splits or robustness checks).
- Use of off-the-shelf LSTM-ER: One of the strongest models is an existing architecture applied out-of-the-box. While this raises questions about novelty in modeling, the study’s breadth, the clear success of sequence-based formulations, and the practical insights derived from the experiments make the work valuable and, in my view, publishable.

Minor remarks and questions
- Lines 222–226: You suggest the full argument graph can be reconstructed from a predicted tree. Please clarify the reconstruction procedure and constraints (e.g., how non-tree edges or cycles are handled).
- Lines 443–444: The section currently orders models as sequence tagging → dependency-based → MTL (built on sequence tagging). It would be easier to follow if the first two were reversed (dependency-based → sequence tagging → MTL), so the sequence model details are fresh when introducing MTL, and acronyms like STag_T are easier to recall.
- Line 455: “Decouples them but jointly models them” is contradictory as written. What exactly is decoupled (parameterization? prediction stages?) and what is modeled jointly (loss? decoding?)? I checked Miwa and Bansal and could not find this phrasing; please clarify or rephrase with a precise mechanism.
- Lines 477–479: The statement that your system decouples relation information from entity information is unclear. If the intent is that one component predicts “edges” (structure) and another predicts “labels on edges,” please state this explicitly and explain how the signals interact (shared representations? pipelining? joint loss?).
- Comparability of F1 across paragraph vs. essay settings: Are scores directly comparable, especially for relation extraction? Paragraph-level models may inherently miss cross-paragraph links. If so, please state this limitation and indicate whether evaluation excludes cross-paragraph relations or how they are treated.

Strengths
- Clear, systematic comparison across multiple neural formulations and a strong ILP baseline.
- Thorough experimental details and careful hyperparameter tuning.
- Convincing evidence for the effectiveness of sequence labeling and MTL, and strong paragraph-level results with LSTM-ER.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and comprehensive empirical study. Addressing the potential train/test topic overlap, clarifying the decoupling/joint modeling claims, and tightening the exposition in the noted sections would further strengthen the paper.
