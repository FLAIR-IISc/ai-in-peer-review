- Paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end argumentation mining on the persuasive essays dataset, comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER, and an ILP baseline.  
- Main contribution: neural sequence taggers outperform prior ILP-based joint approaches for end-to-end AM.  
- Finding: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective in this end-to-end setting, while BiLSTM taggers are robust and capture long-range relations via distance-encoded labels.  
- Finding: multi-task learning with natural auxiliary tasks improves tagging, with component detection helping more than relation prediction.  
- Strength: broad, in-depth evaluation across multiple framings, paragraph/essay levels, and exact/approximate matching, with detailed supplementary material.  
- Strength: clear empirical insights explaining why dependency parsing fails (global search complexity, large tree space) and why taggers work.  
- Strength: strong, practical results—BiLSTM-CRF-CNN and LSTM-ER outperform ILP; LSTM-ER excels at component detection and paragraph-level evaluation.  
- Strength: explicit distance encoding biases models toward realistic short-range relations, improving relation F1 on long documents.  
- Strength: MTL consistently benefits tagging, especially when auxiliary tasks focus on component identification.  
- Strength: reproducible reporting with ablations and analysis of performance drop from paragraph to essay level.  
- Weakness: potential data leakage if train/test essays share topics, possibly inflating performance—topic overlap should be quantified or topic-held-out splits considered.  
- Weakness: novelty concern—the best-performing LSTM-ER is largely an off-the-shelf prior model; the paper’s contribution is comparative analysis rather than a novel model.  
- Weakness: exposition issues—unclear how full graphs are reconstructed from tree outputs (Lines 222–226) and confusing phrasing about de-coupling vs joint modeling (Lines 455, 477–479).  
- Weakness: confusing section order—suggests presenting dependency parsing before sequence tagging or reintroducing STag_T for clarity (Lines 443–444).  
- Weakness: unclear whether relation F1 scores are comparable across paragraph and essay evaluations and whether cross-paragraph relations are excluded.  
- Question: Do train and test sets share topics, and can you estimate overlap or run topic-held-out evaluation?  
- Question: How exactly is the full argument graph reconstructed from token-level dependency parse outputs, especially for nodes with multiple parents?  
- Question: Can you clarify presentation order or reintroduce STag_T where needed to reduce cognitive load?  
- Question: Please explain precisely how components and relations are “de-coupled but jointly modeled” in LSTM-ER (separate modules trained together with shared parameters?).  
- Question: If you mean separating edge prediction from edge labeling, state that explicitly and describe how architectures implement this distinction.  
- Question: Are paragraph- and essay-level relation F1 scores directly comparable, and do paragraph models inherently ignore cross-paragraph relations?  
- Overall recommendation: paper is thorough, well-executed, provides practical guidance and empirical advances for end-to-end AM, and is recommended for acceptance despite limited novelty of the top model.
