- Paper presents a thorough empirical study of neural end-to-end argumentation mining (PE dataset), evaluating four neural framings (parsing, composite token-level tagging, multi-task tagging, LSTM-ER) against a strong ILP baseline with paragraph/essay C-/R-F1 metrics.
- Main contribution: systematic neural end-to-end evaluation that establishes strong baselines outperforming a state-of-the-art ILP pipeline in most settings without manual features.
- Empirical finding: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, while BiLSTM sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels is robust and outperforms parsers.
- Multi-task learning improves performance, with component tagging as an auxiliary task giving larger gains than relation tagging.
- Analysis: LSTM-ER (general joint model) excels on short paragraphs but degrades on essays due to combinatorial growth in candidate relations; constrained sequence taggers scale better to long documents.
- Modeling insight: encoding inter-component distance in tag space biases models toward realistic short-range relations and helps scale to essays.
- Strength: comprehensive, well-controlled comparison across modeling paradigms under a common evaluation protocol, lending credibility to conclusions.
- Strength: strong empirical gains with clear explanations for why parsing underperforms (global tree constraints, mismatch to span detection).
- Strength: actionable modeling choices and analyses clarifying when to prefer general joint models vs. constrained taggers.
- Strength: multi-task results provide practical guidance for building complex label-space taggers even with weaker base taggers.
- Strength: excellent reproducibility with detailed supplemental documentation of embeddings, hyperparameters, and training.
- Weakness: possible data leakage via topic/prompt overlap between train and test, which could inflate performance and requires cross-topic evaluation or split control.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty for the top model (LSTM-ER is largely off-the-shelf); contributions are mainly empirical/analytical.
- Weakness: clarity issues—procedure for reconstructing component-level graphs from token trees is not fully clear; presentation alternates framings and confuses LSTM-ER’s “de-coupled but jointly modeled” description.
- Weakness: paragraph-level evaluation is easier than essay-level, raising questions about cross-setting comparability and handling of cross-paragraph relations.
- Weakness: external validity limited to one dataset with near-tree paragraph structure; missing ablations (distance feature, CRF/char-CNN in MTL) to isolate drivers of gains.
- Question: were data splits controlled by prompt/topic, and can the authors quantify topic overlap or report cross-topic performance?
- Question: please detail the conversion from token-level trees to component-level graphs, losslessness of this transformation, and preservation of stance labels.
- Question: clarify LSTM-ER training/inference: how are predicted entities used for relation classification (scheduled sampling vs. gold) and what parameters are shared?
- Question: are paragraph- and essay-level F1s intended to be comparable, do paragraph models miss cross-paragraph links, and how is this handled in evaluation?
- Question: what is the practical size of the composite label space Y, did you cap/bucket distances, and how sensitive are results to the distance encoding?
- Question: did you try adding CRF/char-CNN to the MTL tagger or sharing only lower layers, and why does component tagging help more than relation tagging?
- Overall assessment: careful, valuable empirical study with strong baselines and useful insights; reviewer leans toward acceptance pending clarifications on splits, graph reconstruction, and cross-setting comparability.
