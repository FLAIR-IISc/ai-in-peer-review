Summary and contributions
This paper proposes neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame AM as (1) token-level sequence labeling, (2) token-level dependency parsing, and also explore (3) a multi-task learning (MTL) variant of the sequence tagger. They compare against an ILP-based pipeline relying on hand-engineered features and constraints. Key findings are that token-level dependency parsing underperforms, whereas simpler BiLSTM-based tagging models are robust across settings and capture long-range dependencies; further, adding “natural” auxiliary tasks in MTL improves performance.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) An empirical study demonstrating that a token-based dependency parsing framing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, whereas BiLSTM sequence tagging is strong and often superior to an ILP baseline.
2) A practical neural end-to-end alternative that avoids manual feature engineering and handcrafted ILP constraints, with evidence that MTL (component and relation subtasks) further improves results.
3) A thorough comparison across paragraph- vs. essay-level settings, shedding light on how sequence length and search space affect different framings.

Strengths
- Clarity and motivation: The paper is well written and easy to follow. The motivation for neural end-to-end AM—eliminating handcrafted features/constraints and avoiding pipeline error propagation—is clearly articulated. The modeling choices (sequence tagging, dependency parsing, MTL) and their intended advantages are explained clearly, with only a few missing details noted below.
- Substantive experiments and fair comparisons: The experimental setup is substantial and the comparisons appear appropriate. Evaluations on both paragraph and essay levels are particularly informative, and the analyses (e.g., distance distributions, constraints, and model search space) help explain the observed behavior of models.
- Interesting and useful findings: The negative result for token-based dependency parsing is valuable. The robust performance of BiLSTM tagging and the gains from MTL (jointly learning “natural” subtasks) are practical takeaways for the community.

Weaknesses
- Data size vs. model capacity and overfitting concerns: The dataset is quite small for training high-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (∼320 training essays, 80 test essays; development size is not reported). The number of training tags is likely only a few thousand, far below standard sequence tagging corpora with hundreds of thousands or millions of labels. This raises concerns about whether parameters are reliably learned. The paper does not analyze overfitting; training and development loss curves (per epoch, or more frequently) would be helpful. The authors themselves note that simpler local taggers are “less prone to overfitting,” which supports this concern.
- Stability and statistical reliability: The paper should report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs (different random seeds/initializations) and perform statistical significance tests to assess stability and reliability. Without this, it is difficult to tell whether improvements are due to the method or randomness. Please also report F1-score ranges; for instance, the large variance observed for Kiperwasser and even a range such as [0.56, 0.61] is nontrivial and deserves analysis.
- Regularization details: Given the small data regime, stronger emphasis on regularization is needed. The main paper does not describe regularization, and the supplement only briefly mentions it for LSTM-ER. Please detail the regularization used (e.g., dropout rates/locations, weight decay, early stopping criteria, gradient clipping), and any data augmentation or pretraining strategies beyond word embeddings.
- Hyperparameter optimization: Consider more sample-efficient methods such as Bayesian optimization rather than the current approach (described in the supplement), which may be inadequate given the small dataset and the number of hyperparameters.
- Missing or deferred details: Important information such as pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis currently appears only in the supplement. Please move these to the main paper (the extra page should suffice), as they are central to interpreting the results.
- Inter-annotator agreement (IAA): Please include IAA figures from the dataset paper. These would contextualize achievable performance and indicate headroom for improvement.
- Figure quality: Figure 1 would benefit from a color scheme that remains clear in black-and-white print.

Questions to the authors (to be addressed in the response)
- What is the size and selection protocol for the development set? How are early stopping and model selection performed?
- Please report training and development loss curves and, if available, performance curves over epochs to assess potential overfitting.
- What regularization techniques are used for each model (dropout placements and rates, L2, gradient clipping, label smoothing, etc.)? Were any tried and discarded?
- Please provide mean, standard deviation, and F1-score ranges across multiple seeds for all key settings, and add statistical significance tests comparing systems. How sensitive are the main conclusions to random initialization?
- Could you comment on the observed variance for the Kiperwasser setting and analyze why it fluctuates substantially?
- How many training instances (components, relations, and tokens with each label type) are available per class? Are class imbalances addressed?

Additional comments
- Consider moving key supplementary details (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis) into the main paper to improve reproducibility and interpretation.
