This paper presents a thorough empirical study of joint neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors investigate five families of models: (1) dependency-parsing formulations (with several parsers), (2) sequence labeling formulations, (3) multi-task learning (MTL) built on top of sequence labeling, (4) an off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER), and (5) ILP-based state-of-the-art systems. Evaluation is conducted using F1 over concepts and relations.

Overall findings are clear and well supported: dependency-based formulations perform poorly; sequence labeling approaches are effective; the off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model performs very strongly, especially at the paragraph level; and both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based baselines. The MTL setup substantially improves sequence labeling, with the claim task providing more benefit than the relation task. The supplementary material is comprehensive and facilitates reproducibility (training details, hyperparameter optimization, etc.). The paper is a careful, well-executed investigation of neural methods for argumentation mining.

Strengths
- Comprehensive empirical comparison across diverse modeling paradigms, including strong non-neural baselines (ILP).
- Clear demonstration that sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform prior ILP-based approaches.
- Insightful MTL results showing auxiliary tasks—especially claim detection—improve sequence labeling.
- Careful engineering and extensive supplementary details that aid reproducibility.
- Strong paragraph-level results and a useful set of practical lessons for the community.

Major concerns
- Potential topic leakage across train/test splits: If essays in training and test sets cover the same or closely related topics, similar arguments and lexical patterns may appear in both, inflating performance. While ILP baselines share the same splits (so relative comparisons are fair), the absolute scores may be optimistic. Please discuss this explicitly and, if feasible, add a topic-disjoint or prompt-disjoint evaluation (e.g., leave-one-prompt-out) or at least report performance stratified by topic overlap. Some analysis of lexical overlap or duplicate/similar argument spans across splits would be helpful.
- Novelty vs. off-the-shelf modeling: One of the strongest models (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of prior work. The paper’s contributions then hinge on problem formulation, empirical findings, and the MTL design rather than architectural novelty. This is acceptable for an empirical study, but the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is adapted, what is new (e.g., task-specific label sets, decoding constraints, input representations), and any task-specific tuning needed to make LSTM-ER work well here. An ablation isolating such adaptations would strengthen the contribution.

Minor comments and questions
- Lines 222–226: You suggest that it is possible to reconstruct the full graph from a tree output. Please clarify the exact post-processing procedure: what constraints define the tree; which edges are added back to form the graph; and under what conditions is reconstruction guaranteed? A small illustrative example would help.
- Lines 443–444: The presentation order would be clearer if dependency-based methods preceded sequence tagging in the description, to match the flow of the experimental sections. By this point it was easy to lose track of STag_T.
- Line 455: The phrasing “de-couples them but jointly models them” is confusing. If the intent is that entity and relation components are parameterized separately but trained/inferred jointly, please say so. I checked Miwa and Bansal but could not find a statement that matches this wording.
- Lines 477–479: Similarly, “de-couples relation info from entity info” needs clarification. If you mean a pipeline-like factorization where the model predicts (a) edge existence and (b) edge labels with partially shared representations, please make that explicit. If, instead, “decoupling” refers to separating features for nodes vs. edges while still optimizing a joint objective, spell out the factorization or provide a brief diagram.
- Comparability of F1 across paragraph vs. essay settings: Are these scores directly comparable, especially for relations? Paragraph-based models will not capture cross-paragraph links by construction, potentially depressing recall relative to essay-level evaluation. Please clarify this and, if possible, report a variant that evaluates only intra-paragraph relations in the essay setting to provide an apples-to-apples comparison.

Suggestions for clarity and analysis
- Add brief error analyses for the main neural systems: typical false positives/negatives for entity spans, common relation confusions, and whether errors are dominated by boundary detection or label assignment.
- Report precision/recall breakdowns (not just F1) for entities and relations; this will make the strengths of LSTM-ER and MTL clearer (e.g., whether gains come from precision or recall).
- Given the strong performance of LSTM-ER at the paragraph level, discuss whether improvements come from better span detection, relation modeling, or both. If available, show per-component ablations (e.g., replacing the entity module with gold spans).
- Minor editorial: fix typos and capitalization (e.g., “The aper” → “The paper”; “i” → “I”).

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed empirical study that advances understanding of neural approaches for argumentation mining. While the strongest model is adapted from prior work, the breadth of comparisons, the clear empirical takeaways, and the practical guidance (including the value of MTL with claim detection) are valuable to the community. Addressing the topic-leakage concern and clarifying the “decoupling” explanations would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
