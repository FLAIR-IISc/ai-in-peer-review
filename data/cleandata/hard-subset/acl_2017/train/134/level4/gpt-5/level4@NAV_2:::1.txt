- Strengths:
The manuscript is clearly written and accessible. The proposed approaches and the empirical findings are engaging.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation setup and the reported gains raise concerns (see details below).

- General Discussion:
This work introduces an end-to-end neural approach to argumentation mining. The authors formulate the task in two ways: (1) sequence tagging and (2) dependency parsing, and additionally explore a multitask extension of the tagging formulation. The motivation is well articulated: prior systems rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and hand-designed ILP constraints, whereas the proposed neural models avoid such manual effort. Further, the approach seeks to jointly learn AM subtasks, mitigating the error propagation typical of pipelines. Aside from a few missing details (listed below), the technical presentation is clear.

The experimental study is substantial, comparisons are appropriate, and the outcomes are interesting. My primary reservation concerns the small size of the dataset relative to the capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based architectures (BLC and BLCC). The corpus contains only about 320 training essays and 80 test essays; the size of the development set is not specified in the paper or the supplementary material. This implies very few training essays, which is a major issue. The total number of token-level tags available for learning is likely only in the low thousands, in contrast with standard sequence labeling tasks that often provide hundreds of thousands or even millions of tags. Consequently, it is unclear whether the model parameters are being reliably estimated. The paper also does not discuss overfitting. It would be helpful to present training and development loss curves (per update or per epoch). Notably, the paper itself hints at overfitting concerns: “taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting,” which suggests capacity may be an issue for other variants.

Relatedly, model stability is uncertain. Please report means and standard deviations over multiple runs with different random initializations, and include statistical significance tests to assess reliability. Without such analyses, it is difficult to determine whether observed improvements stem from methodological advances or randomness.

While the models likely use standard regularization, the dataset is sufficiently small that regularization strategy warrants special attention. The main paper does not discuss regularization, and the supplement only briefly mentions it for LSTM-ER. This should be addressed comprehensively.

For hyperparameter selection, consider replacing the current search strategy (described in the supplement) with Bayesian optimization. Also, move key details about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplement into the main text; an extra page should suffice.

Please include inter-annotator agreement statistics from the dataset paper. These figures would contextualize system performance and indicate potential headroom.

Lastly, adjust Figure 1’s styling to remain clear in grayscale (e.g., patterns or distinct line styles), improving legibility in black-and-white prints.
