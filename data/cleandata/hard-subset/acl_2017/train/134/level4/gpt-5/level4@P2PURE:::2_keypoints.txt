Paper evaluates multiple neural approaches: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
Evaluation metric: F1 on concepts and relations.  
Dependency-based methods perform poorly; sequence labeling approaches are effective.  
LSTM-ER (out-of-the-box) performs very well, especially at paragraph level.  
Sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art models.  
Multitask learning substantially improves sequence labeling, with the claim task helping more than the relation task.  
A comprehensive supplement provides training details, hyperparameter optimization, and technicalities.  
Major concern: possible information leakage from topic overlap between training and test essays, risking overly optimistic performance estimates.  
Counterpoint: the topic-overlap issue would also affect ILP baselines, so no clear unfair advantage, but it requires discussion.  
Major concern: one of the strongest models (LSTM-ER) is essentially an out-of-the-box application from prior work.  
Minor point: unclear claim about reconstructing the full graph from a tree output (Lines 222–226) — needs clarification.  
Minor point: ordering in one section (Lines 443–444) is confusing and obscures what STag_T stands for.  
Minor point: unclear phrasing “de-couple them but jointly model them” (Line 455) and lack of support from cited work (Miwa and Bansal).  
Minor point: confusing explanation about decoupling relation information from entity information (Lines 477–479) — needs clearer wording.  
Minor question: are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores for relations comparable given paragraph models cannot capture cross-paragraph relations?
