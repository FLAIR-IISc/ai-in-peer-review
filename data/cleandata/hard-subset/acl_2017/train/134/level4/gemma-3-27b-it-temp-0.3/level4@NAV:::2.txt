This work presents a comprehensive exploration of neural network approaches to joint argumentation mining, investigating several strategies: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning built upon sequence labeling, a pre-existing neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER), and comparison against established ILP-based methods. Evaluation is consistently performed using F1-score for both concepts and relations. The results demonstrate that dependency-based methods are less effective, while sequence labeling approaches yield strong performance. Notably, the LSTM-ER model achieves particularly good results, especially at the paragraph level, and both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models surpass the performance of the ILP baselines. The paper is supported by a detailed supplementary material outlining model training, hyperparameter optimization, and other technical specifics. Furthermore, the study reveals that multi-task learning significantly enhances sequence labeling models, with the claim task providing a greater benefit than the relation task. Overall, this is a thorough investigation of neural methods for end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Major Remarks:**

My primary concern lies with the dataset. The potential overlap in topics between the training and test essay sets raises the possibility of information leakage, where arguments present in the training data may reappear in the test data, leading to inflated performance estimates. While this issue also affects the ILP models, mitigating any unfair advantage, it warrants discussion. 

Secondly, the strong performance of the LSTM-ER model relies on its direct application of an existing model from related work. However, considering the overall success of sequence-based models and the extensive experimentation and valuable insights presented, I believe this work is suitable for publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions:**

*   Lines 222-226: The claim regarding reconstructing the full graph from a tree output is unclear and could benefit from further explanation.
*   Lines 443-444: The order of presentation – sequence tagging, dependency-based methods, then multi-task learning using sequence tagging – is somewhat disruptive. Reversing the order of the first two would improve readability, as the meaning of “STag_T” may be forgotten by this point.
*   Line 455: The statement that the model “de-couples” information while “jointly modeling” it appears contradictory. Clarification is needed. I was unable to find support for this claim in the cited works by Miwa and Bansal.
*   Lines 477-479: The description of decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing. My interpretation is that the model learns certain tasks as tree edges and others as edge labels, effectively decoupling them in this manner. I recommend clarifying this section.
*   Finally, are the F1 scores reported for the paragraph and essay settings directly comparable, particularly for the relation tasks? It’s possible that paragraph-based models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations due to their limited scope.
