## Review

**Strengths:**
The paper is clearly written and readily understandable. The methods employed and the reported results are of interest to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and resulting conclusions require careful consideration due to potential issues, as detailed below.

**General Discussion:**
This paper introduces a neural network-based system for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore two modeling approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing, with the sequence labeling approach further investigated within a multitask learning framework. The motivation for the proposed model is clearly articulated. Current approaches often rely on inductive logic programming (ILP), manual feature engineering, and the manual definition of ILP constraints. In contrast, this work avoids such manual effort and benefits from the joint learning of argumentation mining subtasks, mitigating error propagation inherent in pipeline methods. The methodology is generally well-explained, with only minor details requiring clarification.

The experiments are comprehensive, comparisons are appropriately conducted, and the results are intriguing. However, a primary concern is the limited size of the dataset – approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not reported in the paper or supplementary materials, which is problematic given the already small training set. This limited data size, resulting in a likely small number of total tags (potentially only a few thousand), raises questions about proper model parameter training, especially considering the capacity of the employed (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The paper lacks an analysis of potential overfitting; including training and development loss values throughout training (e.g., per parameter update or epoch) would be beneficial. The authors’ own statement (Line 622) – “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting” – can be interpreted as evidence *of* overfitting.

Consequently, the stability of the models is also uncertain. Reporting the mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different parameter initializations, along with statistical significance tests, would strengthen the reliability and interpretability of the results. Without such analysis, it is difficult to determine whether observed performance gains stem from the superiority of the proposed method or simply from chance. 

While the employed neural networks utilize regularization techniques, the small dataset size necessitates a more focused attention to regularization strategies. The paper currently lacks a discussion of regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions it in the context of LSTM-ER. This aspect requires more thorough treatment.

Consider employing Bayesian optimization methods for hyper-parameter tuning, rather than the current approach described in the supplementary materials. Furthermore, move the information regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main body of the paper; an additional page should suffice.

Finally, including inter-annotator agreement scores, as reported in the original dataset description, would provide valuable context regarding system performance and potential areas for improvement.  Please also consider revising Figure 1 with distinct colors to ensure clarity in black and white prints.
