The work proposes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several methods including casting the problem as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence labeling problem, multi-task learning based on sequence labeling, an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores defined on concepts and relations.

The experimental results show that dependency-based solutions do not perform well, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model achieves excellent results, particularly at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. The authors provide a comprehensive supplement detailing the technical aspects of training models and optimizing hyperparameters.

The paper demonstrates that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved through multi-task learning, with the claim task providing more assistance than the relation task. Overall, the paper presents a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major Remarks:

1. One concern is the potential issue with the dataset, as essays in the training and test sets may cover the same topics, leading to information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates. Although this issue may also affect ILP models, it is essential to discuss this potential problem.
2. Another concern is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. However, considering the relative success of sequence-based models and the valuable lessons learned from the experiments, this work deserves publication.

Minor Remarks and Questions:

- The explanation of reconstructing the full graph from a tree output (lines 222-226) is unclear and requires further clarification.
- Reversing the order of sequence tagging and dependency-based methods in the section (lines 443-444) would improve readability.
- The statement "it de-couples them but jointly models them" (line 455) is confusing and seems contradictory. Further clarification is needed, and a reference to Miwa and Bansal would be helpful.
- The explanation of de-coupling relation information from entity information (lines 477-479) is unclear. It would be beneficial to rephrase this section for better understanding.
- It is unclear whether the F1 scores in paragraph and essay settings are comparable, particularly for relation tasks. The authors should discuss whether paragraph-based models might miss cross-paragraph relations by default.
