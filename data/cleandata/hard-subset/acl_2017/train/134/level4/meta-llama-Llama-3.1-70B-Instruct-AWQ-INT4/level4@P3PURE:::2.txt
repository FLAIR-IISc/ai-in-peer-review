This paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several methods to tackle the problem. These include: (1) formulating the task as a dependency parsing problem, with experiments involving various parsers; (2) treating it as a sequence labeling problem; (3) employing multi-task learning, building upon a sequence labeling model; (4) utilizing an off-the-shelf neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER); and (5) comparing the performance to state-of-the-art ILP-based models. The evaluation of these approaches is based on F1 scores defined over concepts and relations.

The experimental results show that dependency-based solutions do not perform well, while sequence labeling approaches yield effective results. Notably, the out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model achieves excellent performance, particularly at the paragraph level. Furthermore, both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach.

The authors provide a comprehensive supplement, detailing the technical aspects of training the models and optimizing hyperparameters. The results also demonstrate that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved through the multi-task approach, with the claim task offering more benefits than the relation task.

This paper presents a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major Remarks:

1. One concern is the potential issue with the dataset, where essays in the training and test sets might cover the same topics. Consequently, writers may employ similar arguments, leading to information leakage from the training to the test set and potentially resulting in overly optimistic performance estimates. Although this issue may also affect the ILP models, it is essential to discuss this point.

2. Another concern is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is essentially an off-the-shelf application of a model from related work. Nevertheless, considering the relative success of sequence-based models and the valuable lessons learned from the experiments, this work merits publication.

Minor Remarks and Questions:

- The explanation in lines 222-226 regarding the possibility of reconstructing the full graph from a tree output is unclear.

- The ordering of sections 443-444, presenting sequence tagging, dependency-based, and multi-task learning using sequence tagging, would be easier to follow if the first two were reversed.

- The statement in line 455, "it de-couples them but jointly models them," is unclear. The reviewer could not find this concept in Miwa and Bansal's work.

- The explanation in lines 477-479 regarding the system's ability to de-couple relation information from entity information is confusing. The reviewer's interpretation is that the system learns some tasks as "the edges of the tree" and others as "the labels on those edges," thus decoupling them. Clarification is recommended.

- It is unclear whether the F1 scores in paragraph and essay settings are comparable, particularly for relation tasks. The reviewer wonders if paragraph-based models might miss some cross-paragraph relations by default, as they do not consider them.
