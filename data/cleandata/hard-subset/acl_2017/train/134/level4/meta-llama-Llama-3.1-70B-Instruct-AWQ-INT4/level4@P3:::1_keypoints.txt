- Paper studies neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) and compares sequence labeling, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning on the persuasive essays dataset, outperforming a feature-based ILP baseline.

- Contribution: First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.

- Contribution: Several neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.

- Contribution: Framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is shown to be ineffective.

- Contribution: Presents a neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance between components and is robust across settings.

- Contribution: Multi-task learning setup improves AM performance.

- Strength: Well-written and easy to understand.

- Strength: Methods and results are interesting and advance AM research.

- Strength: Clear motivation for the model and avoidance of manual feature engineering.

- Strength: Substantial experiments with proper comparisons.

- Weakness: Small dataset vs. large neural models raises concerns about evaluation validity.

- Weakness: No analysis or reporting of training/development loss to assess overfitting.

- Weakness: Missing mean and standard deviation over multiple runs, so model stability is unclear.

- Weakness: No discussion of regularization methods despite small dataset.

- Weakness: Hyper-parameter optimization approach may be inappropriate for small data; Bayesian methods suggested.

- Question: Provide details about the development set (size and usage).

- Question: Supply training and development loss curves to assess overfitting.

- Question: Explain how regularization will be addressed.

- Question: Report mean and standard deviation across multiple runs for stability assessment.

- Question: Consider using Bayesian optimization for hyper-parameter tuning.

- Question: Provide inter-annotator agreement scores to evaluate annotation quality and headroom.
