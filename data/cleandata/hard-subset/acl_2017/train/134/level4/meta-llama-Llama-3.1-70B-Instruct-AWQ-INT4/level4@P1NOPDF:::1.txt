**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The proposed methods and results are interesting and demonstrate a clear contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and results may be problematic, as discussed in the following comments.

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks. The authors model the problem using two approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. Additionally, they experiment with a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed model, which avoids manual effort required by existing methods based on ILP, manual feature engineering, and manual design of ILP constraints. The model jointly learns the subtasks in argumentation mining, thereby avoiding the error back propagation problem in pipeline methods. Except for a few missing details mentioned below, the methods are explained clearly.

The experiments are substantial, the comparisons are performed properly, and the results are interesting. However, my main concern is the small size of the dataset and the large capacity of the used (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset includes only around 320 essays for training and 80 essays for testing, with the size of the development set not mentioned in the paper or supplementary materials. This is concerning, as very few essays are left for training, which is a crucial problem. The total number of tags in the training data is probably only a few thousand, compared to standard sequence labeling tasks, where hundreds of thousands (sometimes millions) of tags are available. I am not sure if the model parameters are trained properly due to this limitation. Furthermore, the paper does not analyze the overfitting problem, which would be interesting to investigate by examining the training and development "loss" values during training (after each parameter update or after each epoch). The authors provide some information that can be seen as evidence for overfitting: "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" (Line 622).

For the same reason, I am not sure if the models are stable enough. To address this, the authors should include the mean and standard deviation of multiple runs (different initializations of parameters). Statistical significance tests would also provide more information about the stability of the models and the reliability of results. Without these tests, it is hard to determine whether the better results are due to the superiority of the proposed method or chance.

Although the neural networks used for modeling the tasks employ regularization techniques, the authors need to pay more attention to regularization methods due to the small dataset size. The paper does not mention regularization at all, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER. This problem needs to be addressed properly in the paper.

Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method (described in supplementary materials), consider using Bayesian optimization methods. Additionally, move the information about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material to the paper, as the extra page would be sufficient for this.

Please include some inter-annotator agreement scores, as the paper describing the dataset has relevant information. This would provide insight into the performance of the systems and the available room for improvement.

Consider illustrating Figure 1 with different colors to improve the quality for black and white prints.
