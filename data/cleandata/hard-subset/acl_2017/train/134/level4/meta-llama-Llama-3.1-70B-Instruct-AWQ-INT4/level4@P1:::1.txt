**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a neural end-to-end approach for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem as a token-based dependency parsing task, a sequence tagging task, and a multi-task learning setup. They investigate the performance of these approaches using a dataset of persuasive essays and compare the results with a feature-based ILP model. The main contributions of the paper are:

1. The first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2. A comparison of different neural approaches to AM, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.
3. An evaluation of the performance of these approaches on a dataset of persuasive essays.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The methods and results are interesting, and the authors provide a clear motivation for their approach. The experiments are substantial, and the comparisons with existing methods are properly performed. The results are also interesting and provide insights into the performance of different neural approaches to AM.

**Weaknesses**

The evaluation and the obtained results might be problematic due to the small size of the dataset and the large capacity of the used (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks. The dataset includes only around 320 essays for training and 80 essays for testing, which is a crucial problem. The total number of tags in the training data is probably only a few thousand, which is not sufficient for training the model parameters properly. The paper also does not analyze the overfitting problem, and the authors do not provide information about the training and development "loss" values during training. Additionally, the paper does not mention regularization methods, which is a problem given the small size of the dataset.

**General Discussion**

The paper proposes a system for end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks. The authors model the problem using two approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. The paper also includes the results of experimenting with a multi-task learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed model and avoids the manual effort required by existing methods based on ILP and manual feature engineering. The model jointly learns the subtasks in argumentation mining and avoids the error back-propagation problem in pipeline methods.

However, there are some concerns about the evaluation and the obtained results. The small size of the dataset and the large capacity of the used (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks may lead to overfitting and poor generalization. The paper does not provide information about the training and development "loss" values during training, which makes it difficult to assess the performance of the model. Additionally, the paper does not mention regularization methods, which is a problem given the small size of the dataset.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How did you address the overfitting problem in your experiments?
2. Can you provide information about the training and development "loss" values during training?
3. How did you regularize your models, and what methods did you use?
4. Can you provide inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset?
5. How did you select the hyperparameters for your models, and did you consider using Bayesian optimization methods?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the methods and results are interesting. However, there are some concerns about the evaluation and the obtained results. The authors should address the overfitting problem, provide information about the training and development "loss" values during training, and mention regularization methods. Additionally, the authors should consider using Bayesian optimization methods for hyperparameter tuning and provide inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset.
