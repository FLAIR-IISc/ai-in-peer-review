This paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several methods including:

1) casting the problem as a dependency parsing problem (using various parsers)
2) casting the problem as a sequence labeling problem
3) multi-task learning (based on a sequence labeling model)
4) an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER)
5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models

All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores defined on concepts and relations. The dependency-based solutions do not perform well, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs exceptionally well, particularly on paragraph-level tasks. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach.

The authors provide a comprehensive supplement, detailing the technical aspects of training the models and optimizing hyperparameters. They also demonstrate that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved through a multi-task approach (with the claim task providing more benefits than the relation task). This paper offers a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major remarks:
- One concern is the potential issue with the dataset, where essays in the training and test sets might be on the same topics, leading to writers using similar arguments and resulting in information leakage from the training to the test set. This could give overly optimistic performance estimates. Although the same issue may be present for ILP models, it is essential to discuss this potential problem.
- Another concern is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. However, considering the relative success of sequence-based models and the useful lessons learned from the experiments, this work deserves publication.

Minor remarks and questions:
- Lines 222-226: It appears that the authors argue that it is possible to reconstruct the full graph once a tree is obtained as output. However, this part is not entirely clear.
- Lines 443-444: The ordering in this section is sequence tagging -> dependency-based -> MTL using sequence tagging. Reversing the order of the first two would make it easier to follow (by the time I reached this point, I had forgotten what STag_T stood for).
- Line 455: The statement that the system de-couples relation information from entity information but jointly models them is unclear. Isn't coupling them required to jointly model them? I checked Miwa and Bansal's work but couldn't find a relevant explanation.
- Lines 477-479: It is confusing when the authors say their system de-couples relation information from entity information. My interpretation is that it learns some tasks as "the edges of the tree" and some other tasks as "the labels on those edges," thus decoupling them. I recommend clarifying this part.
- Are the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings comparable, particularly for relation tasks? I wonder if paragraph-based models might miss some cross-paragraph relations by default, as they will never consider them.
