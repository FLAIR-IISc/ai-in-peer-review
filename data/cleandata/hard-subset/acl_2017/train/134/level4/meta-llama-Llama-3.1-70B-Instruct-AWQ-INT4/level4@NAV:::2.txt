This paper proposes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring various methods including:

1) dependency parsing problem formulations using multiple parsers
2) sequence labeling problem formulations
3) multi-task learning based on sequence labeling models
4) an out-of-the-box neural model, LSTM-ER, for entity and relation labeling
5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models

These approaches are evaluated using F1 scores defined on concepts and relations. The results indicate that dependency-based solutions underperform, while sequence labeling solutions achieve better results. The LSTM-ER model demonstrates exceptional performance, particularly at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach.

A comprehensive supplement is provided, detailing the technical aspects of training models and optimizing hyperparameters. The results also show that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved through multi-task learning, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task.

This paper presents a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major concerns:

1) The dataset may be problematic, as essays in the training and test sets may cover the same topics, potentially leading to information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates. Although this issue may also affect ILP models, it is worth discussing.
2) One of the top-performing models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. Nevertheless, considering the relative success of sequence-based models and the experiments conducted, this work merits publication.

Minor remarks and questions:

- The explanation of reconstructing the full graph from a tree output (lines 222-226) is unclear.
- The ordering of sections (lines 443-444) would be easier to follow if the sequence tagging and dependency-based sections were reversed.
- The statement that the system "de-couples" relation and entity information while jointly modeling them (line 455) is unclear, as it seems to imply that coupling is required for joint modeling. This concept is not found in Miwa and Bansal's work.
- The explanation of de-coupling relation and entity information (lines 477-479) is confusing; it is recommended to clarify this point. The system appears to learn some tasks as "tree edges" and others as "labels on those edges," thus de-coupling them.
- It is unclear whether the F1 scores in paragraph and essay settings are comparable, particularly for relation tasks. Paragraph-based models may miss cross-paragraph relations by default, as they do not consider them.
