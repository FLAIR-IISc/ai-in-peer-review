**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a study on neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors propose several neural network framings for the AM problem, including sequence labeling, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning. They evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset and compare their performance to a feature-based ILP model. The results show that the neural approaches outperform the ILP model and achieve state-of-the-art results on the dataset.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2. The authors show that several neural approaches perform better than the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3. They demonstrate that a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective.
4. The paper presents a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components and performs robustly in different environments.
5. The authors show that a multi-task learning setup improves performance on the AM problem.

**Strengths**

* The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
* The methods and results are interesting and contribute to the field of AM.
* The authors provide a clear motivation for their proposed model and explain how it avoids manual feature engineering and error back propagation.
* The experiments are substantial, and the comparisons are performed properly.

**Weaknesses**

* The evaluation and obtained results might be problematic due to the small size of the dataset and the large capacity of the used neural networks.
* The paper does not analyze the overfitting problem and does not provide training and development loss values during training.
* The authors do not report mean and standard deviation of multiple runs, which makes it difficult to assess the stability of the models.
* The paper does not mention regularization methods, which is a crucial aspect of training neural networks, especially with small datasets.
* The hyper-parameter optimization method is not well-suited for the small dataset, and Bayesian optimization methods might be more appropriate.

**Questions to Authors**

* Please provide more information about the development set, including its size and how it was used in the experiments.
* Can you provide training and development loss values during training to assess the overfitting problem?
* How do you plan to address the regularization problem in the paper?
* Can you include mean and standard deviation of multiple runs to assess the stability of the models?
* Please consider using Bayesian optimization methods for hyper-parameter optimization.
* Can you provide inter-annotator agreement scores to assess the quality of the annotations and the available room for improvement?
* Please illustrate Figure 1 with different colors to make it more readable in black and white prints.
