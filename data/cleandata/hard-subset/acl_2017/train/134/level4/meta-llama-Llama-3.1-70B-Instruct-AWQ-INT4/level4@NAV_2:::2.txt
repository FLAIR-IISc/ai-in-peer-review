The work describes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining. There are several approaches explored, including casting the problem as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence labeling problem, multi-task learning based on a sequence labeling model, an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All the approaches are evaluated using F1 defined on concepts and relations.

The contributions of this paper are: 
1. The authors present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining.
2. They show that several of their approaches perform better than the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3. They demonstrate that a framing of argumentation mining as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, whereas a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly in different environments.
4. They show that a multi-task learning setup where natural subtasks of the full argumentation mining problem are added as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

The strengths of this submission are: 
1. The work provides a comprehensive investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, exploring multiple approaches and evaluating their performance.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approaches, showing that several of their models outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model.
3. The work provides useful insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches, including the benefits of multi-task learning and the limitations of dependency parsing.

The weaknesses of this submission are: 
1. The authors' reliance on a single dataset may be a limitation, as the performance of their models may not generalize to other datasets.
2. The use of an out-of-the-box model (LSTM-ER) as one of the best-performing models may raise concerns about the novelty of the approach.
3. The authors could provide more discussion on the implications of their findings, including the potential applications and limitations of their approaches.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Can you discuss the potential limitations of using a single dataset and how you plan to address this in future work?
2. How do you respond to concerns that the use of an out-of-the-box model (LSTM-ER) may not be novel enough?
3. Can you provide more details on how you plan to apply your approaches to real-world argumentation mining tasks and what potential challenges you may face?
