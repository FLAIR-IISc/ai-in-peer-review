- Proposes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining and evaluates multiple formulations: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task sequence labeling, LSTM-ER entity/relation model, and ILP baselines.  
- Uses F1 scores on concepts and relations as evaluation metrics.  
- Finds dependency-based solutions underperform compared to sequence labeling approaches.  
- Shows LSTM-ER achieves exceptional performance, especially at paragraph level.  
- Reports that sequence labeling and LSTM-ER both outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art.  
- Provides a comprehensive supplement detailing training procedures and hyperparameter optimization.  
- Demonstrates that multi-task learning substantially improves sequence labeling models, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task.  
- Concludes the paper is a thorough investigation of neural end-to-end argumentation mining.  

Major concerns:
- Dataset may leak topic information between training and test essays, risking overly optimistic performance estimates and requiring discussion.  
- LSTM-ER is an out-of-the-box model from prior work (limited novelty), though results and experiments still justify publication.

Minor remarks/questions:
- Explanation of reconstructing the full graph from a tree output (lines 222–226) is unclear and needs clarification.  
- Section ordering (lines 443–444) should swap sequence tagging and dependency-based sections for better flow.  
- The claim that the system "de-couples" relation and entity information (line 455) is unclear and possibly misleading.  
- The further explanation of de-coupling (lines 477–479) is confusing; clarify whether tasks are learned as tree edges versus labels on edges.  
- Unclear if paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores are comparable, since paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations by design.
