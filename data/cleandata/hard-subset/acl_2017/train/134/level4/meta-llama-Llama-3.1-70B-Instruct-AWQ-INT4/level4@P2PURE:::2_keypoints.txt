- Paper proposes and compares multiple neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
- Evaluation uses F1 scores on concept (entity) and relation prediction.  
- Dependency-parsing formulations perform poorly.  
- Sequence-labeling approaches are effective and improve further with multi-task learning.  
- Out-of-the-box LSTM-ER yields strong performance, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Both sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based state of the art.  
- Multi-task learning benefits sequence-labeling models substantially, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task.  
- Authors provide a comprehensive supplement with training and hyperparameter details.  
- Major concern: potential dataset leakage (train/test essays covering same topics) may inflate performance estimates and should be discussed.  
- Major concern: one top model (LSTM-ER) is an off-the-shelf application from prior work, raising novelty questions despite useful experimental insights.  
- Minor clarity issue: unclear claim (lines 222–226) that a full graph can be reconstructed from a tree.  
- Minor readability suggestion: reorder presentation (lines 443–444) for easier flow.  
- Minor wording confusion: statement that the model "de-couples" entities and relations while jointly modeling them is contradictory (line 455).  
- Minor clarification needed: the claim that the system "de-couples" relation from entity information is confusing—authors should clarify whether tasks are learned as edges vs labels (lines 477–479).  
- Minor evaluation concern: paragraph- and essay-level F1s may not be comparable because paragraph models can miss cross-paragraph relations.
