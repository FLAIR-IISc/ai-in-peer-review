**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem as a token-based dependency parsing problem, a token-based sequence tagging model, and a multi-task learning setup. They evaluate several approaches, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and compare them to a feature-based ILP model.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. The authors present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2. They show that several of their approaches perform better than the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3. They demonstrate that a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly in different environments.
4. They show that a multi-task learning setup where natural subtasks of the full AM problem are added as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper presents a comprehensive investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.
2. The authors explore several approaches and evaluate them using a robust evaluation metric.
3. The paper provides useful insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches to AM.
4. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of multi-task learning in improving performance.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. One concern is that the data set used may be problematic, as essays in the train set and test set may be on the same topics, leading to information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates.
2. Another concern is that one of the best models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work, which may not be a significant contribution.

**Minor Remarks and Questions**

1. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of how the full graph can be reconstructed from a tree output.
2. The ordering of the sections could be improved for easier readability.
3. The paper could clarify what it means for a model to de-couple entity and relation information while jointly modeling them.
4. The F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings may not be directly comparable, particularly for relation tasks, as paragraph-based models may miss cross-paragraph relations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you discuss the potential issues with the data set and how they may impact the performance estimates?
2. Can you provide more details on how the LSTM-ER model was applied and what modifications were made to adapt it to the AM task?
3. Can you clarify the differences between the various models and how they de-couple entity and relation information?
4. Can you provide more insights into the performance of the models on cross-paragraph relations?
