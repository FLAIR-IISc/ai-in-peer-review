This paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several methods including: (1) formulating the problem as a dependency parsing task (utilizing multiple parsers), (2) casting the problem as a sequence labeling task, (3) multi-task learning (based on a sequence labeling model), (4) a neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and (5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores defined on concepts and relations.

The results show that dependency-based solutions do not perform well, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs exceptionally well, particularly at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. The authors provide a comprehensive supplement detailing the technicalities of training models and optimizing hyper-parameters.

The paper demonstrates that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved through multi-task learning, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task. This work presents a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major remarks:
- One concern is the potential issue with the dataset, where essays in the training and test sets may cover the same topics, leading to information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates. Although this issue may also affect ILP models, it is worth discussing.
- Another concern is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. However, considering the relative success of sequence-based models and the useful lessons learned from the experiments, this work deserves publication.

Minor remarks and questions:
- In lines 222-226, it seems that the authors argue that it is possible to reconstruct the full graph once a tree is obtained as output. However, this part is not entirely clear.
- The ordering in lines 443-444 (sequence tagging -> dependency-based -> MTL using sequence tagging) would be easier to follow if the order of the first two were reversed.
- In line 455, the statement that the model "de-couples" entities and relations but jointly models them is unclear. It seems contradictory, as coupling them is required for joint modeling.
- In lines 477-479, it is confusing when the authors state that their system "de-couples" relation information from entity information. It is recommended to clarify this part, as it seems to imply that the model learns some tasks as "edges of the tree" and others as "labels on those edges," thus decoupling them.
- It is unclear whether the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings are comparable, particularly for relation tasks. Paragraph-based models may miss cross-paragraph relations by default, as they do not consider them.
