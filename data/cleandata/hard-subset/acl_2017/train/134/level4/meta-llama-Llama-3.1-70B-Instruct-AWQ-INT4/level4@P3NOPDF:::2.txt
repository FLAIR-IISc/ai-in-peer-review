This paper proposes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several methods to tackle this problem. The approaches include casting argumentation mining as a dependency parsing problem (utilizing various parsers), a sequence labeling problem, multi-task learning (based on a sequence labeling model), an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All methods are evaluated using F1 scores defined on concepts and relations.

The results indicate that dependency-based solutions do not perform well, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model achieves high performance, particularly at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. A comprehensive supplement is provided, detailing the technicalities of training the models and optimizing hyper-parameters.

The paper also demonstrates that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved through multi-task learning, with the claim task contributing more to the improvement than the relation task. Overall, this paper presents a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

Major Remarks:

1. One concern is the potential issue with the dataset, where essays in the training set and test set may be on the same topics, leading to information leakage from the training to the test set. This could result in overly optimistic performance estimates. Although this issue may also be present in ILP models, it is worth discussing to ensure that the results are reliable.
2. Another concern is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. However, considering the relative success of sequence-based models and the useful lessons learned from the experiments, this work still deserves to be published.

Minor Remarks and Questions:

* In Section 3.1 (lines 222-226), it is unclear how the full graph can be reconstructed from a tree output. Clarification on this point would be helpful.
* The ordering in Section 4.2 (lines 443-444) is confusing, with sequence tagging followed by dependency-based methods and then multi-task learning using sequence tagging. Reversing the order of the first two would improve the flow of the section.
* The statement that the system "de-couples" relation information from entity information while jointly modeling them is unclear (line 455). It would be helpful to provide more context or clarify this point, as it seems contradictory.
* The description of the system's ability to "de-couple" relation information from entity information is confusing (lines 477-479). It appears to mean that the system learns some tasks as "the edges of the tree" and others as "the labels on those edges," thus decoupling them. Clarification on this point would be beneficial.
* It is unclear whether the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings are comparable, particularly for relation tasks. Paragraph-based models may miss some cross-paragraph relations by default, as they do not consider them.
