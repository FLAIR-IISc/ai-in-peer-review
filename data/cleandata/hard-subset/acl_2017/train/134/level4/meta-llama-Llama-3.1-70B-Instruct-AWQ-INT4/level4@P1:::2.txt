The work describes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several approaches, including casting the problem as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence labeling problem, multi-task learning based on a sequence labeling model, an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All the approaches are evaluated using F1 scores defined on concepts and relations.

The authors find that dependency-based solutions do not work well, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially on paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. A comprehensive supplement provides technical details on training models, optimizing hyperparameters, etc.

The authors also show that sequence labeling models can be greatly improved by the multi-task approach, with the claim task helping more than the relation task. The paper is a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining.
2. They show that several of these solutions perform better than the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3. They demonstrate that a framing of argumentation mining as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective.
4. They find that a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly in different environments.
5. They show that a multi-task learning setup where natural subtasks of the full argumentation mining problem are added as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of different neural approaches to argumentation mining, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, and multi-task learning.
2. The authors evaluate their approaches using a robust evaluation metric and provide a detailed analysis of the results.
3. The paper includes a thorough supplement with technical details on training models, optimizing hyperparameters, etc.
4. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approaches on a challenging dataset and provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The authors' concern about the dataset is valid, as essays in the train set and test set might be on the same topics, leading to information leakage from the train to the test set. However, this issue is not unique to their approach and is also present in the ILP models.
2. One of the authors' best models, LSTM-ER, is an out-of-the-box application of a model from related work. While this does not detract from the overall contribution of the paper, it might be seen as a limitation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clarify how you reconstruct the full graph once you get a tree as output from the dependency parsing approach?
2. Can you explain why you chose to present the sequence tagging and dependency-based approaches in a specific order, and whether reversing this order would improve the clarity of the paper?
3. Can you provide more details on what you mean by "decoupling" entity and relation information in the LSTM-ER model, and how this differs from jointly modeling them?
4. Are the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings comparable, particularly for the relation tasks? Do paragraph-based models miss some cross-paragraph relations by default?
