**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and easy to understand, presenting interesting methods and results.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and results may be problematic due to concerns regarding the dataset size, model capacity, and potential overfitting issues.

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the problem through sequence labeling and dependency parsing approaches. The authors also explore a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed model, which avoids manual feature engineering and ILP constraints used in existing methods. Instead, the model jointly learns subtasks in argumentation mining, mitigating the error back-propagation problem in pipeline methods.

The experiments are substantial, with proper comparisons and interesting results. However, the small dataset size is a concern, with approximately 320 essays for training and 80 essays for testing. The development set size is not mentioned in the paper or supplementary materials, leaving few essays for training, which is a crucial issue. Compared to standard sequence labeling tasks with hundreds of thousands or millions of tags, the total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand. This raises doubts about whether the model parameters are properly trained.

The paper does not analyze the overfitting problem, and it would be beneficial to include training and development "loss" values during training (after each parameter update or epoch). The authors provide some evidence that can be seen as indicative of overfitting, such as the statement that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" (Line 622).

Furthermore, the models' stability is uncertain, and including mean and standard deviation of multiple runs (different initializations of parameters) would be necessary. Statistical significance tests would provide more information about the stability of the models and the reliability of the results. Without these tests, it is challenging to determine whether the better results are due to the proposed method's superiority or chance.

Although the neural networks used for modeling tasks employ regularization techniques, the authors should pay closer attention to regularization methods due to the small dataset size. The paper does not mention regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER. This issue needs to be properly addressed in the paper.

Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method, consider using Bayesian optimization methods. Additionally, move the information about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary material to the paper, as the extra page would be sufficient for this.

Including inter-annotator agreement scores would provide insight into the performance of the systems and the available room for improvement. The paper describing the dataset contains relevant information that would be beneficial to include.

Consider illustrating Figure 1 with different colors to improve the quality for black and white prints.

**Additional Comments:**
After the authors' response, I increased the recommendation score to 4. However, I still have concerns about model stability. For example, the large variance of the Kiperwasser setting needs to be properly analyzed. Even the F1 changes in the range [0.56, 0.61] are relatively large. Including these score ranges in the paper would facilitate replicating the work. Please include F1-score ranges and report mean and variance of different settings.
