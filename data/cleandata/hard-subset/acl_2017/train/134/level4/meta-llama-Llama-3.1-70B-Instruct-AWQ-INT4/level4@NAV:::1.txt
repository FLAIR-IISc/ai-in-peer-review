**Strengths:**
The paper is well-structured and presents its ideas in a clear and concise manner. The methodology and results are thought-provoking and demonstrate a good understanding of the research problem.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation methodology and results may have some limitations (see comments below).

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, tackling the problem through two approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. The authors also explore multitask learning for the sequence labeling approach. The paper effectively motivates the proposed model and clearly explains the methodology. Unlike existing methods based on ILP, manual feature engineering, and manual ILP constraint design, the proposed model avoids manual effort and jointly learns subtasks, thus mitigating the error backpropagation problem in pipeline methods. However, a few details are missing (mentioned below).

The experiments are substantial, comparisons are performed properly, and the results are interesting. However, the small dataset size and the large capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC) raise concerns. The training dataset consists of approximately 320 essays, and the test dataset has 80 essays. The development set size is not mentioned in the paper or supplementary materials, leaving only a few essays for training, which is a crucial issue. In comparison to standard sequence labeling tasks with hundreds of thousands (or millions) of tags, the total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand, casting doubt on the proper training of model parameters. The paper does not analyze overfitting, and it would be interesting to see training and development "loss" values during training. The authors provide some evidence for overfitting: "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" (Line 622).

The stability of the models is also uncertain. To address this, the authors should include mean and standard deviation of multiple runs (different initializations of parameters) and perform statistical significance tests to provide more information about model stability and result reliability. Without these tests, it is challenging to determine whether the better results are due to the proposed method's superiority or chance.

Although the neural networks use regularization techniques, the authors should pay more attention to regularization methods due to the small dataset size. The paper does not mention regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER. This issue needs to be properly addressed in the paper.

Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method, consider using Bayesian optimization methods. Additionally, move the information about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary material to the paper, which should fit within an extra page.

Including inter-annotator agreement scores would provide insight into the performance of the systems and available room for improvement. The paper describing the dataset contains relevant information.

Consider illustrating Figure 1 with different colors to improve its quality for black and white prints.
