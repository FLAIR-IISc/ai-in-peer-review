**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and easy to understand, presenting interesting methods and results.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and obtained results may be problematic, as discussed below.

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the problem through two approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. The authors also explore multitask learning for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed model, which avoids manual effort in existing methods based on ILP, manual feature engineering, and manual design of ILP constraints. By jointly learning subtasks, the model also avoids error back-propagation issues in pipeline methods.

The methods are clearly explained, except for a few missing details mentioned below. The experiments are substantial, comparisons are performed properly, and the results are interesting. However, the small dataset size and large capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC) raise concerns. The dataset consists of only around 320 training and 80 testing essays, with an unspecified development set size. This limited data may not be sufficient for training, particularly when compared to standard sequence labeling tasks with hundreds of thousands or millions of tags.

As a result, it is unclear whether the model parameters are properly trained, and the paper does not analyze the overfitting problem. Providing training and development "loss" values during training (after each parameter update or epoch) would be informative. The authors mention that taggers are simpler local models, requiring less training data and being less prone to overfitting (Line 622). However, this statement can also be seen as evidence for overfitting.

The stability of the models is also a concern, and including mean and standard deviation of multiple runs (different initializations of parameters) would be beneficial. Statistical significance tests would provide more information about the stability of the models and the reliability of the results. Without these tests, it is difficult to determine whether the better results are due to the proposed method's superiority or chance.

Although the neural networks use regularization techniques, the small dataset size necessitates more attention to regularization methods. The paper does not mention regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER. This issue needs to be addressed properly in the paper.

Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method (described in supplementary materials), consider using Bayesian optimization methods. Additionally, move the information about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary material to the paper, which should be feasible within an extra page.

Including inter-annotator agreement scores would provide insight into the performance of the systems and the available room for improvement. The paper describing the dataset has relevant information on this topic.

Consider illustrating Figure 1 with different colors to improve its quality for black and white prints.

**Additional Comments:**
After receiving answers to my questions, I increased the recommendation score to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in the paper and report mean and variance of different settings. I remain concerned about model stability, particularly the large variance of the Kiperwasser setting, which needs to be analyzed properly. Including score ranges in the paper would facilitate replication of the work.
