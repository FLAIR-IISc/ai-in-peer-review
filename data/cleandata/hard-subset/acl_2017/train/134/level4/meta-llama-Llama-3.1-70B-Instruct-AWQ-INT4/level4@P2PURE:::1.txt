- Strengths:
The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with interesting methods and results.

- Weaknesses:
The evaluation and results obtained may be problematic, as discussed below.

- General Discussion:
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the problem through two approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. The authors also experiment with a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed model, which avoids manual feature engineering and ILP constraints used in existing methods. Instead, the model jointly learns subtasks in argumentation mining, mitigating the error back-propagation problem in pipeline methods. Except for a few missing details, the methods are explained clearly.

The experiments are substantial, comparisons are performed properly, and the results are interesting. However, my main concern is the small size of the dataset and the large capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset comprises only around 320 essays for training and 80 essays for testing, with the size of the development set not mentioned in the paper or supplementary materials. This is concerning, as very few essays are left for training, which is a crucial problem. The total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand, compared to standard sequence labeling tasks with hundreds of thousands (or millions) of tags. Therefore, I question whether the model parameters are properly trained. The paper does not analyze the overfitting problem, and it would be interesting to see the training and development "loss" values during training (after each parameter update or epoch). The authors provide some information that could be seen as evidence of overfitting: Line 622 "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting".

For the same reason, I am uncertain about the stability of the models. Mean and standard deviation of multiple runs (different initializations of parameters) should be included. Statistical significance tests would provide more information about the stability of the models and the reliability of results. Without these tests, it is difficult to determine whether the better results are due to the superiority of the proposed method or chance.

Although the neural networks used for modeling tasks employ regularization techniques, the authors should pay more attention to regularization methods due to the small dataset size. The paper does not mention regularization at all, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER. This issue needs to be properly addressed in the paper.

Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method (described in supplementary materials), consider using Bayesian optimization methods. Additionally, move the information about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary material to the paper, as the extra space would be sufficient.

Please include some inter-annotator agreement scores, as the paper describing the dataset provides relevant information. This would offer insight into the performance of the systems and the available room for improvement.

Consider illustrating Figure 1 with different colors to improve its quality for black and white prints.
