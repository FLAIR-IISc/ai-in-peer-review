**Strengths:** 
The paper is well-written and easy to understand, presenting interesting methods and results.

**Weaknesses:** 
The evaluation and obtained results may be problematic, as discussed below.

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the problem through two approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. The authors also experiment with a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed model, which avoids manual feature engineering and the manual design of ILP constraints found in existing methods. By jointly learning subtasks in argumentation mining, the model also sidesteps the error backpropagation problem inherent in pipeline methods. Except for a few missing details (mentioned below), the methods are clearly explained.

The experiments are substantial, the comparisons are performed properly, and the results are interesting. However, my main concern about this paper is the small size of the dataset and the large capacity of the used (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset consists of only around 320 essays for training and 80 essays for testing, with the size of the development set not mentioned in the paper or the supplementary materials. This is concerning, as very few essays are left for training, which is a crucial problem. The total number of tags in the training data is probably only a few thousand, which is significantly less than the hundreds of thousands (sometimes millions) of tags available in standard sequence labeling tasks. For this reason, I am not sure if the model parameters are trained properly. The paper also does not analyze the overfitting problem. It would be interesting to see the training and development "loss" values during training (after each parameter update or after each epoch). The authors provide some information that can be seen as evidence for overfitting: Line 622 "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting".

For the same reason, I am not sure if the models are stable enough. The mean and standard deviation of multiple runs (different initializations of parameters) need to be included. Statistical significance tests would also provide more information about the stability of the models and the reliability of results. Without these tests, it is hard to say if the better results are due to the superiority of the proposed method or chance.

I understand that the neural networks used for modeling the tasks employ their regularization techniques. However, since the size of the dataset is too small, the authors need to pay more attention to the regularization methods. The paper does not mention regularization at all, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER. This problem needs to be addressed properly in the paper.

Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method (described in supplementary materials), consider using Bayesian optimization methods. Also, move the information about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material to the paper. The extra one page should be enough for this.

Please include some inter-annotator agreement scores. The paper describing the dataset has some relevant information. This information would provide some insight into the performance of the systems and the available room for improvement.

Please consider illustrating Figure 1 with different colors to make the quality better for black and white prints.
