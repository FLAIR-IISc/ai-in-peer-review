### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the application of various neural network architectures to the task of end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors frame the problem as both a token-based dependency parsing task and a sequence tagging task. They systematically compare the performance of these framings, explore a multi-task learning setup for the sequence tagging model, and evaluate against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive empirical study of neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining.** The paper presents a valuable comparison of four distinct neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a joint entity-relation model), establishing new state-of-the-art results on the persuasive essays dataset.
2.  **A clear demonstration that simpler, local sequence tagging models outperform more complex, global dependency parsing models for this task.** The results show that BiLSTM-based taggers are more robust, particularly on longer documents, offering an important insight for future model design in this domain.
3.  **An effective application of multi-task learning for argumentation mining.** The study shows that augmenting the main sequence tagging task with auxiliary tasks that model natural subproblems (e.g., component identification) successfully improves overall performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Novelty:** The paper is well-motivated, presenting a clear departure from previous approaches that rely on brittle pipeline architectures, extensive manual feature engineering, and complex, hand-crafted ILP constraints. By adopting an end-to-end neural approach, the work avoids these manual efforts and mitigates the error propagation issues inherent in pipeline systems.
2.  **Thorough and Systematic Experiments:** The authors conduct a substantial set of experiments comparing multiple neural framings against a competitive baseline. The analysis is methodical, particularly the distinction between paragraph-level and essay-level performance, which provides insightful evidence on how different models handle varying sequence lengths and task complexities.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The paper provides a thoughtful discussion that goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics. The analysis of why certain models excel or falter in specific conditions (e.g., why local taggers outperform global parsers, or why the LSTM-ER model's relation extraction performance degrades on longer texts) is particularly insightful and contributes to a deeper understanding of the modeling challenges in argumentation mining.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential for Overfitting Given the Small Dataset:** My primary concern is the use of high-capacity models like BiLSTM-CRF-CNN on a dataset of only ~320 training essays. This raises significant questions about whether the models are overfitting. The paper lacks a sufficient analysis to address this concern, such as learning curves showing training and development set performance over time. A more rigorous investigation of overfitting is crucial for validating the results.
2.  **Lack of Model Stability and Robustness Analysis:** The results appear to be based on single training runs. Given the stochasticity of neural network initialization and training, especially on small datasets, model stability is a major concern. The paper would be much stronger if it reported the mean and standard deviation of key metrics over multiple runs with different random seeds. Furthermore, statistical significance tests are necessary to confirm that the reported performance differences between models are reliable and not just artifacts of chance.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on Regularization:** Regularization is a critical component for training deep learning models on limited data. The paper does not describe the regularization techniques (e.g., dropout, weight decay) used for its primary models (STagBLCC and the MTL setup), mentioning it only briefly for a baseline model in the supplement. For reproducibility and to properly assess the work's approach to mitigating overfitting, a detailed account of the regularization strategies and associated hyperparameters is essential.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please specify the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning? The paper details the training and test set sizes but omits this information.
2.  Have you evaluated the stability of your models? Could you report the mean and variance of the F1 scores over multiple runs for your main models to help assess the reliability of the reported gains?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the specific regularization methods and their hyperparameters that were applied to the STagBLCC and multi-task learning models?
4.  To better contextualize the system's performance and understand the remaining challenges, could you include the inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset and discuss how your best model's performance compares to this human-level upper bound?
