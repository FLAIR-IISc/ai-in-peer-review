- Paper compares neural end-to-end AM framed as token-level dependency parsing, complex sequence tagging, and MTL, against a strong ILP baseline and Miwa & Bansal (2016).
- Key finding: simpler local sequence tagging models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) are more effective and robust than complex global models as document length increases.
- Neural sequence tagging and joint models achieve new state-of-the-art results, outperforming a feature-rich ILP system without manual feature engineering.
- Paper provides insightful analysis of model constraints, explaining failure modes (e.g., LSTM-ER degradation on long documents due to unconstrained entity-pair search).
- Novelty: first systematic comparison of diverse neural end-to-end frameworks for full AM, evaluating multiple conceptual approaches.
- Experiments are thorough and well-designed, including multiple parsers, tagging models, and paragraph vs. essay-level evaluation to show scaling effects.
- Presentation is clear and well-motivated, with helpful examples explaining the different AM framings.
- Concern about overfitting: high-capacity models trained on a very small dataset (402 essays) with unclear train/dev sizes and limited regularization details.
- Reliability/stability issue: results reported as single runs without means, standard deviations, or statistical significance tests to assess variance from random initialization or small test size.
- Missing experimental details in the main paper: unspecified train/dev/test split, incomplete regularization and pre-trained embeddings information, and absent IAA scores to contextualize performance.
- Request for learning curves (train/dev loss or F1 vs. epoch) to demonstrate generalization and overfitting behavior.
- Request for explicit regularization details (dropout rates, L2, etc.) for dependency parsers and sequence tagging models for reproducibility.
- Request to report results over multiple runs (mean ± std) and perform statistical significance tests to support claims of superiority.
