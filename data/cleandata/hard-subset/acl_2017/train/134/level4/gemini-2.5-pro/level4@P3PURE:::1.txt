### Summary

This paper proposes a neural network-based system for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore two modeling approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing, including a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling model. The paper is well-motivated, presenting its model as an alternative to traditional methods that rely on extensive manual feature engineering, ILP formulations, and hand-designed constraints. By jointly learning the subtasks of argumentation mining, the proposed model also avoids the error propagation issues common in pipeline-based systems. The methods are clearly explained, with the exception of a few missing details noted below.

### Strengths

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The proposed methods for end-to-end argumentation mining are interesting, and the experimental results are presented clearly.

### Weaknesses

My primary concern with this work relates to the evaluation and the potential instability of the results, stemming from the combination of a small dataset and high-capacity neural models.

1.  **Dataset Size and Overfitting:** The experiments are conducted on a dataset containing only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not specified. This small training set is a significant concern for the large BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC) employed, which typically require substantial amounts of data to train effectively. For comparison, standard sequence labeling tasks often utilize datasets with hundreds of thousands or even millions of labeled instances. With a training set of this size, there is a high risk that the models are overfitting. The paper does not include an analysis of this potential problem, such as plots of the training and development loss over epochs, which would be highly informative. The authors themselves seem to acknowledge this issue (Line 622: "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting"), which further underscores the need for a more thorough investigation.

2.  **Model Stability and Reliability of Results:** Given the small dataset, the stability of the trained models is questionable. The paper reports results from a single run, but performance may vary significantly across different random initializations. To demonstrate the stability of the models and the reliability of the reported gains, the authors should report the mean and standard deviation of F1-scores over multiple runs. Furthermore, statistical significance tests are necessary to confirm whether the observed improvements of the proposed model over baselines are statistically meaningful or merely due to chance.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Regularization:** Regularization is critical when training high-capacity models on small datasets. However, the paper does not mention regularization at all, and the supplementary material only makes a brief reference to it in the context of LSTM-ER. A detailed description of the regularization techniques used and their impact is needed to address the overfitting concerns.

### Actionable Suggestions

*   **Reporting:** Please specify the size of the development set. Move the details about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main paper, as this information is central to the experiments. The additional page available for the camera-ready version should provide sufficient space.
*   **Context:** To help readers better interpret the system's performance, please include the inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, which are available in the paper that originally introduced the corpus. This provides a soft upper bound and insight into the task's difficulty.
*   **Hyper-parameter Optimization:** For future work, consider using more advanced techniques like Bayesian optimization for hyper-parameter tuning instead of the current method.
*   **Presentation:** Please revise Figure 1 using distinct patterns or shades to ensure it is legible when printed in black and white.
