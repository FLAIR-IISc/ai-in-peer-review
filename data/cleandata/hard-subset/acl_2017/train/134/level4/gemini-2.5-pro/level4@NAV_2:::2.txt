This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining. The authors frame the problem in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing task, a sequence tagging task, a multi-task learning problem based on sequence tagging, and by applying an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). These neural approaches are compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The experiments demonstrate that while dependency parsing is not effective for this task, sequence tagging models perform robustly and, along with the LSTM-ER model, outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A thorough and systematic empirical evaluation of various neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining on a standard dataset. This provides a valuable benchmark and set of insights for future research in the area.
2.  The key finding that simpler, local models like BiLSTM-based sequence taggers are more effective and robust for this task than more complex, global models like token-level dependency parsing, especially when dealing with long documents.
3.  A demonstration that multi-task learning, which incorporates natural sub-problems like component identification as auxiliary tasks, can further improve the performance of the main argumentation mining task.

### Strengths

1.  **Comprehensive Investigation:** The paper conducts a very thorough investigation of multiple, distinct neural approaches to the problem. By comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and an existing joint model, the authors provide a wide-ranging analysis that highlights the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling philosophies for this task. This breadth is a significant service to the community.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models are shown to outperform a strong, feature-based state-of-the-art system. This clearly demonstrates the viability and advantages of end-to-end neural methods, which eliminate the need for laborious feature engineering and constraint design.
3.  **Valuable Analysis and Insights:** The paper provides several useful lessons. The failure of dependency parsing is an important negative result. The analysis comparing performance on paragraph-level versus essay-level inputs effectively illustrates the challenge of capturing long-range dependencies in argumentation. The finding that multi-task learning helps by adding "natural subtasks" is also a valuable insight.
4.  **High Potential for Reproducibility:** The authors have included a detailed supplementary document that covers technical details of model training and hyperparameter optimization. This commitment to transparency significantly aids in the reproducibility of the reported results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential for Data Leakage in Evaluation:** A primary concern is the nature of the dataset. If essays on the same topic appear in both the training and test sets, writers may use similar or identical arguments. This overlap could lead to an information leak from the training to the test set, resulting in overly optimistic performance estimates. While this limitation would also affect the ILP baseline, meaning the comparison is likely fair, the paper would be stronger if it acknowledged and discussed this potential issue.
2.  **Novelty of the Best-Performing Model:** One of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of an existing model from prior work (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). While the analysis of its performance in the argumentation mining context is new and insightful, the reliance on an off-the-shelf model for some of the strongest results slightly reduces the novelty of the paper's technical contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the transformation to a tree structure (Section 3, lines 222-226): You mention that claims linking to multiple major claims can be "loss-lessly" re-linked to form a tree. Could you please clarify how the full, original graph structure is recovered from the model's tree-based output at inference time?
2.  In your description of the LSTM-ER model (line 455), you state that it involves "decoupling entity and relation detection but jointly modeling them." This phrasing is somewhat contradictory. Could you please elaborate on what "decoupling" means in the context of this joint model?
3.  Are the relation F1 scores directly comparable between the paragraph-level and essay-level evaluations? By design, paragraph-level models cannot identify relations that span across paragraphs. This seems like a significant constraint that might not be reflected if the evaluation simply ignores such relations for the paragraph-level models. Could you clarify how cross-paragraph relations are handled in the paragraph-level evaluation?
