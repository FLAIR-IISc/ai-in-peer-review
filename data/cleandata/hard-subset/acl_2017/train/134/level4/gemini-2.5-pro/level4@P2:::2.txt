### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural, end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to move beyond traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and complex constraints, such as Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors explore four distinct neural frameworks for identifying argumentative components (claims, premises) and the relations between them directly from raw text. The investigated approaches include: (1) framing the AM task as a token-level dependency parsing problem, evaluated with several different parsers; (2) modeling the task as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, complex tagging scheme that encodes component boundaries, types, relation distances, and relation types into a single label; (3) extending the sequence tagging model into a multi-task learning (MTL) setup to leverage natural subtasks; and (4) applying a state-of-the-art model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER by Miwa and Bansal, 2016).

The experiments, conducted on a standard dataset of persuasive essays, show that sequence-based models significantly outperform dependency parsing approaches. The authors' proposed sequence tagger (STagBLCC) and the LSTM-ER model both achieve new state-of-the-art results, surpassing the feature-based ILP baseline. A key finding is that the optimal model depends on the context length: the more general LSTM-ER model excels on shorter paragraph-level inputs, while the more constrained sequence tagging model is more robust on longer, essay-level inputs.

### Contributions

1.  **A Thorough Comparative Analysis of Neural Architectures for End-to-End Argumentation Mining:** The primary contribution is a rigorous and wide-ranging empirical investigation into what neural architectures are suitable for this complex structured prediction task. By systematically comparing token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and a joint entity-relation model, the paper provides invaluable insights for the community. The finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective is an important negative result, while the success of sequence-based models provides a clear direction for future research.

2.  **A Novel and Effective Sequence Tagging Formulation for Argumentation Mining:** The authors propose a creative method to encode the entire argumentation structure (component boundaries, types, and relations) into a single, unified sequence tag. This formulation, which includes relative distance between components, allows a standard BiLSTM-CRF architecture to effectively tackle the full end-to-end task, demonstrating strong performance, especially on long documents.

3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Analysis:** The work establishes new state-of-the-art results on the persuasive essays dataset, demonstrating that end-to-end neural models can outperform complex, feature-engineered pipeline systems. Furthermore, the paper provides a compelling analysis of the trade-offs between different models, insightfully explaining why the performance of certain models (like LSTM-ER) degrades on longer texts due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations, thereby highlighting the importance of structural biases in model design.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Rigor and Comprehensiveness:** The paper is exceptionally thorough. Rather than proposing a single model, it evaluates a whole family of approaches, including four distinct neural frameworks and a strong non-neural baseline. The comparison is fair and detailed, supported by an extensive supplementary document covering hyperparameters and implementation details. This rigorous approach makes the findings highly credible and useful.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The presented models convincingly outperform the prior state-of-the-art ILP-based system. This is a significant result, as it validates the move towards end-to-end neural methods in this domain, obviating the need for costly feature engineering and manual constraint design that characterized previous approaches.

3.  **Insightful Analysis and Clear Takeaways:** The paper does an excellent job of not just presenting results, but also analyzing *why* certain models perform better than others. The discussion on the trade-off between model flexibility (LSTM-ER) and structural constraints (STagT) in the context of short vs. long documents is particularly insightful. The conclusion that component and relation detection should be modeled jointly but treated as distinct tasks (either via MTL or separate modules) is a valuable practical recommendation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential for Data Leakage in the Evaluation:** My main concern is that the experimental setup may produce overly optimistic performance estimates. The dataset contains essays on a set of controversial topics. If essays on the same topic appear in both the training and test sets, the models may learn topic-specific lexical cues or argument structures, rather than general argumentation patterns. While this affects the baseline ILP model as well, making the relative comparison fair, the absolute F1 scores might be inflated. A discussion of this limitation, or an analysis of performance in a cross-topic setting, would strengthen the claims.

2.  **Novelty of the Best-Performing Model:** One of the paper's best-performing models (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of a previously published method. While applying existing models to new tasks and analyzing their fit is a valid and important contribution, it slightly reduces the methodological novelty of the work. This is largely mitigated, however, by the breadth of the overall investigation and the value of the comparative analysis itself.

3.  **Clarity in Explaining Model Architectures:** Certain key concepts could be explained more clearly. For example, the description of LSTM-ER as a model that "decouples entity and relation detection but jointly models them" is confusing without further elaboration. A clearer definition of what "de-coupling" means in this context (e.g., separate modules within a jointly trained system) would improve readability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Are the F1 scores directly comparable between the paragraph-level and essay-level experiments, particularly for relations? Paragraph-level models are by definition unable to capture inter-paragraph relations. If such relations exist in the gold data, these models are solving a simplified version of the task. Please clarify how this potential discrepancy is handled or how it impacts the interpretation of the results.

2.  In Section 3, you describe modifying the argument graph into a tree by re-linking all claims to a single major claim. Could you elaborate on how the original graph structure, with claims linking to multiple major claims, is reconstructed from the model's tree-structured output? You state this transformation is "loss-less," and a clearer explanation of the reverse mapping would be helpful.

3.  Your best-performing models on the paragraph-level (LSTM-ER) and essay-level (STagBLCC) appear to have different inductive biases regarding relations. LSTM-ER considers all pairs of entities, while your STagT formulation constrains each component to have at most one outgoing relation. Do you believe this difference in structural constraints is the primary reason for their differing performance on short vs. long documents?
