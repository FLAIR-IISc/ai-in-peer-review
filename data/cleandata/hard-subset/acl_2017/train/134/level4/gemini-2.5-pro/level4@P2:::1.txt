### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive exploration of neural, end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the AM task in two primary ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem and as a sequence tagging problem. The sequence tagging approach is further extended to a multi-task learning (MTL) setting. These neural approaches are evaluated against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, with the goal of eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and complex constraint design.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Thorough Investigation of Neural End-to-End Frameworks for AM:** The paper is the first to systematically apply and compare modern neural architectures (dependency parsers, sequence taggers) to the full, end-to-end AM task. This represents a significant shift from previous pipeline systems that rely heavily on hand-crafted features.
2.  **Valuable Empirical Insights on Model Suitability:** The authors provide a clear empirical demonstration that framing AM as a token-level sequence tagging problem is more effective than framing it as dependency parsing. The results show that simpler, local models like BiLSTM-CRFs are more robust for this task, particularly given the data constraints.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed sequence tagging models, particularly in combination with multi-task learning or in the LSTM-ER configuration, achieve new state-of-the-art results on the persuasive essays dataset, outperforming a competitive ILP-based system.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Presented:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and does an excellent job of motivating the need for end-to-end neural models in AM. The authors clearly articulate the drawbacks of existing methods—namely, the reliance on manual feature engineering, complex ILP constraint design, and the issue of error propagation in pipeline architectures—and present a compelling case for their proposed joint learning approach.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The study is commendably thorough. The authors evaluate a wide range of models, including four different dependency parsers (both feature-based and neural) and several advanced sequence tagging architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, MTL). The comparison to the established LSTM-ER model and the ILP baseline provides a solid grounding for their claims and offers the community valuable insights into the relative merits of these different frameworks.
3.  **Strong and Interesting Results:** The empirical results are convincing and highlight several important findings. The superior performance of the sequence tagging models over the token-level dependency parsers is a key insight. Furthermore, demonstrating that an MTL setup can improve performance by leveraging "natural" subtasks provides a promising direction for future work on complex structured prediction tasks. The fact that these neural models outperform the feature-rich ILP system underscores the power of representation learning for this domain.

### Weaknesses

My main concerns with this paper relate to the reliability and generalizability of the results, given the combination of a small dataset and high-capacity models.

1.  **Model Complexity vs. Data Scarcity:** My primary concern is the potential mismatch between the high capacity of the employed neural networks (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) and the limited size of the training corpus (approximately 320 essays). Standard sequence labeling tasks often rely on training sets that are orders of magnitude larger. This scarcity raises a significant risk of the models overfitting the training data, which could limit the generalizability of the findings. The paper does not analyze or explicitly address the risk of overfitting (e.g., by showing training vs. development loss curves). The lack of information on the development set size further complicates an assessment of this issue.
2.  **Lack of Stability Analysis and Significance Testing:** The results appear to be based on single experimental runs. Given the small dataset and the known sensitivity of neural networks to factors like weight initialization, model performance can exhibit high variance across runs. Without reporting the mean and standard deviation over multiple runs, it is difficult to assess the stability of the models. Consequently, it is unclear whether the reported performance differences between various models (e.g., STagBLCC vs. LSTM-Parser) are statistically significant or potentially due to chance.
3.  **Insufficient Discussion of Regularization:** For a study using complex models on a small dataset, a detailed discussion of regularization is critical. The paper does not mention which techniques (e.g., dropout, L2 regularization) were used for the proposed models, nor how their corresponding hyperparameters were tuned. This information is crucial for reproducibility and for building confidence that overfitting was adequately controlled. The supplementary material only briefly mentions this in the context of the LSTM-ER model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning? To address concerns about overfitting, could you provide the learning curves (e.g., F1-score or loss on training and development sets per epoch) for your best-performing models?
2.  To demonstrate the stability of your results, could you report the mean and variance of the F1 scores over several runs (e.g., 5-10) with different random seeds for your key models (e.g., STagBLCC and LSTM-Parser)?
3.  Are the key performance differences reported in Table 1 (e.g., between STagBLCC and the LSTM-Parser on the paragraph level, or between STagBLCC and the ILP model) statistically significant?
4.  What specific regularization techniques were employed for the sequence tagging and dependency parsing models, and what were their settings (e.g., dropout rates)?
