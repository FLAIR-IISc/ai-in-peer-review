- Paper conducts a thorough empirical comparison of joint neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining.  
- Explores multiple modeling paradigms: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, sequence-labeling-based MTL, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
- Evaluates models using F1 for component identification and relation classification.  
- Main finding: dependency-parsing approaches perform poorly while sequence-labeling formulations are significantly more effective.  
- Off-the-shelf LSTM-ER achieves strong performance, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Both sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based baseline.  
- Multi-task learning substantially improves the sequence-labeling model; claim identification provides a stronger signal than relation prediction.  
- Paper includes a comprehensive supplement detailing training and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Major dataset concern: overlapping essay topics in train/test may cause topic-specific leakage and overly optimistic results.  
- LSTM-ER result is strong but offers limited novelty since it is a direct application of prior work.  
- Request clarification on whether and how a dependency-tree output can reconstruct the full argumentation graph (Lines 222–226).  
- Suggest reordering presentation (dependency → sequence tagging → MTL) to improve readability and referential clarity (Lines 443–444).  
- Point out apparent contradiction in claiming the model both “de-couples” and “jointly models” entities and relations; needs clarification (Lines 455, 477–479).  
- Question whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores, particularly for relations, are directly comparable given inability to capture cross-paragraph relations.
