This paper presents a thorough investigation into joint neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors systematically explore several modeling paradigms, including:
1)  Casting the problem as dependency parsing, using several different parsers.
2)  Casting the problem as sequence labeling.
3)  A multi-task learning (MTL) framework built upon the sequence labeling model.
4)  An off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER).
5)  Comparisons against state-of-the-art ILP-based models.

All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores for both component identification and relation classification. The key findings are that dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling formulations are significantly more effective. The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model performs very well, particularly at the paragraph level. Both the proposed sequence labeling and the LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP-based baseline. A key result is that the multi-task learning setup substantially improves the sequence labeling model, with the claim identification task providing a stronger learning signal than the relation task.

The paper is accompanied by a very comprehensive supplement detailing the technical specifics of model training and hyper-parameter optimization. Overall, this is a very solid and thorough empirical study of neural approaches for this task.

**Major Remarks**

*   A primary concern relates to the dataset construction. The presence of essays on the same topics in both the training and test sets raises the possibility of information leakage. Writers may use similar arguments or phrasing for the same topic, which could lead to the models learning topic-specific cues rather than general argumentation structures. This, in turn, might result in overly optimistic performance estimates. The reviewer acknowledges that this issue likely affects the baseline ILP models as well, ensuring a fair comparison between the proposed models and the baselines. Nevertheless, this potential limitation warrants discussion in the paper.
*   A second point concerns the LSTM-ER model, which is a direct application of a model from prior work. While this model achieves strong results, its novelty within the context of this paper is limited. However, this concern is outweighed by the paper's overall contribution. The comprehensive experimental comparison, the valuable insights into the effectiveness of sequence-based models, and the useful lessons learned from the various approaches collectively represent a significant contribution that merits publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions**

*   **Lines 222-226:** This section is unclear. Are the authors arguing that the full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from the tree output of the dependency parser? Please clarify this process.
*   **Lines 443-444:** The presentation order in this section (sequence tagging -> dependency-based -> MTL) is somewhat confusing. A more logical flow might be to present the dependency-based models first, followed by the sequence tagging models upon which the MTL approach is built. This would improve readability; for instance, by the time the MTL results are discussed, the meaning of acronyms like `STag_T` may be forgotten.
*   **Line 455:** The statement that the model "de-couples" entity and relation information while also "jointly modeling" them seems contradictory. To jointly model them, they must be coupled in some way. The reviewer was unable to find clarification on this point in the cited work by Miwa and Bansal (2016).
*   **Lines 477-479:** This is related to the point above. The paper states that the system de-couples relation and entity information. Is the intended meaning that the model learns separate representations or tasks for "the edges of the tree" (structure) versus "the labels on those edges" (relation type)? This section requires significant clarification.
*   Regarding the evaluation, are the F1 scores for the paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, particularly for relations? It seems that paragraph-level models are inherently unable to capture cross-paragraph relations, which could artificially constrain the task and affect the interpretation of the results. A brief discussion on this point would be helpful.
