### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates several neural network architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the AM task in multiple ways: as a token-level sequence tagging problem, a dependency parsing problem, and a multi-task learning (MTL) problem. They compare these approaches to each other and to a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, evaluating them on the task of identifying argument components and the relations between them.

### Contributions

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A comprehensive, first-of-its-kind exploration of neural end-to-end models for argumentation mining. The work successfully demonstrates that these models can outperform a state-of-the-art ILP-based system, eliminating the need for extensive feature engineering and manual constraint design.
2.  An insightful comparative analysis of different neural framings for the task. The paper provides valuable findings, notably that simpler sequence tagging models are more robust and effective than more complex dependency parsing models on this task, and that multi-task learning can further improve performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Timely Research:** The paper addresses the important goal of creating end-to-end systems for argumentation mining. The motivation to move beyond pipeline systems that suffer from error propagation and feature-heavy models that require significant manual effort is very clear and compelling. The proposed neural approaches represent a significant and modern direction for the field.

2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experiments:** The authors conduct a substantial set of experiments, systematically comparing four distinct neural framings of the problem. The inclusion of a strong ILP baseline makes the comparisons meaningful. The analysis is nuanced, for instance, by evaluating on both paragraph and essay levels, which reveals important differences in how models handle short and long contexts.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, and the different approaches to modeling the AM task are explained clearly. The motivation for each framing (e.g., dependency parsing for tree structures, sequence tagging for component identification) is well-articulated, making the paper easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Risk of Overfitting and Model Instability due to Small Dataset:** My main concern is the potential disconnect between the large capacity of the proposed (Bi)LSTM-based models (e.g., BLCC) and the very small size of the training dataset. The corpus contains only 402 essays in total, with 80 reserved for testing. The paper does not specify the size of the development set, but this leaves a maximum of 322 essays for training and validation. For complex sequence modeling tasks, this is an exceptionally small dataset, especially when compared to standard benchmarks that often contain hundreds of thousands of tagged tokens. This raises several critical issues:
    *   **Overfitting:** With so little training data, it is highly likely that these powerful models are overfitting. The paper does not include any analysis to mitigate this concern, such as plots of training and development loss over time. The authors themselves hint at this issue when they state, "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" (Lines 622-624), which implicitly acknowledges the risk for more complex models or settings.
    *   **Model Stability:** The results may not be stable across different runs. With a small dataset, performance can be highly sensitive to factors like random parameter initialization. The paper reports results from a single run. To ensure the reliability of the findings, results should be averaged over multiple runs with different random seeds, and standard deviations should be reported. Without this, it is difficult to conclude whether the observed performance differences between models are statistically meaningful or a result of chance.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Regularization:** Given the high risk of overfitting, the paper's lack of discussion on regularization is a significant omission. While the supplementary material briefly mentions regularization for the LSTM-ER model, the main paper does not describe what techniques (e.g., dropout, weight decay) were used for the primary proposed models (STagT, parsers) or how their hyperparameters were tuned. This is a crucial detail for assessing the validity of the results and for reproducibility.

3.  **Missing Experimental Details and Context:**
    *   The paper relegates important information, such as details on pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis, to the supplementary material. Given the one-page allowance for references, it seems these crucial details could be integrated into the main paper.
    *   The paper lacks context for interpreting the performance scores. Including the inter-annotator agreement (IAA) scores from the original dataset paper (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) would provide a useful indicator of task difficulty and a soft upper bound for system performance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please specify the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning?
2.  To address the concerns about overfitting, could you provide plots of the training and development loss/F1 scores during training?
3.  To demonstrate model stability, could you report the mean and standard deviation of F1 scores over multiple runs with different random initializations?
4.  Could you provide more details on the specific regularization techniques and their corresponding hyperparameters used for each of the neural models presented in the paper?

### Post-Rebuttal Comments

The authors' response has clarified several points. I encourage the authors to incorporate the reported F1-score ranges and the mean/variance from different runs into the final version of the paper, as this information is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding model stability. My concern about stability remains, particularly given the large variance reported for some models (e.g., the Kiperwasser parser). An analysis or discussion of this variance would strengthen the paper. For example, even a reported F1 score range of [0.56, 0.61] for a model represents a relatively large performance swing that warrants discussion.
