- Paper presents a thorough comparison of joint neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL-enhanced sequence labeling, and LSTM-ER) evaluated against an ILP baseline.

- Dependency parsing formulations perform poorly; sequence labeling approaches are highly effective.

- Off-the-shelf LSTM-ER achieves very strong performance, especially at the paragraph level.

- Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baseline on component and relation F1.

- Multi-task learning significantly improves sequence labeling, with auxiliary claim identification more beneficial than relation identification.

- Experimental setup and hyper-parameter tuning are documented comprehensively in the supplement.

- Major concern: possible information leakage from topic overlap between training and test essays, which could inflate reported performance.

- Note that dataset leakage would likely affect the ILP baseline too, so comparative claims remain reasonably fair.

- One strong model (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of prior work, but the paper’s comparative study and findings still provide substantial contribution.

- Clarify the process of reconstructing a full graph from a tree-based output (Lines 222–226).

- Reorder results presentation for readability (present dependency models first, then sequence tagging and MTL) to avoid long-distance references to abbreviations.

- Clarify confusing wording around LSTM-ER “de-coupling” vs “jointly” modeling components and relations (Lines 455, 477–479).

- Clarify whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores (especially for relations) are directly comparable given paragraph models cannot capture cross-paragraph relations.
