- Paper compares multiple neural frameworks for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning (MTL), LSTM-ER, and ILP-based baselines.  
- Evaluation uses F1 for component identification and relation extraction, with a comprehensive supplement on training and hyper-parameter tuning.  
- Main empirical findings: dependency-based approaches perform poorly; sequence labeling is effective; LSTM-ER performs particularly well (especially at paragraph level).  
- Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art baseline.  
- The proposed MTL setup significantly improves sequence-labeling performance; the auxiliary claim-identification task yields more benefit than the auxiliary relation task.  
- Major concern: possible topic overlap between training and test essays could cause information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates—this needs discussion.  
- Major concern: one top-performing model (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of prior work, raising questions about novelty despite the paper’s broad experimental value.  
- Minor: unclear claim that a full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from a dependency-tree output—needs elaboration.  
- Minor: presentation order is confusing; recommend presenting dependency-based models before sequence-tagging since sequence models underpin the MTL approach.  
- Minor: contradictory phrasing about “de-coupling” component and relation identification while “jointly modeling” them—requires clarification (Miwa & Bansal 2016 citation does not clarify).  
- Minor: repeated confusing claim about de-coupling relation and entity information—authors may mean separating link detection from link labeling but must clarify.  
- Minor: clarify whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores (especially for relation extraction) are directly comparable given paragraph models cannot capture cross-paragraph relations.
