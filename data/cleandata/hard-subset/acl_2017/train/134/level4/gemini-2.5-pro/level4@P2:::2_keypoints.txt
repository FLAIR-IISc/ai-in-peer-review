- Empirical study comparing four end-to-end neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL sequence tagging, LSTM-ER) for argumentation mining from raw text.  
- Sequence-based models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) substantially outperform token-level dependency parsing.  
- STagBLCC and LSTM-ER set new state-of-the-art results on the persuasive essays dataset.  
- Model performance depends on input length: LSTM-ER excels on short paragraphs, STagBLCC is more robust on long essays.  
- Contribution: a thorough, systematic comparison of neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining, including a negative result for dependency parsing.  
- Contribution: a novel sequence tagging formulation that encodes component boundaries, types, relation distances, and relation types into a single tag.  
- Contribution: analysis demonstrating SOTA performance and explaining trade-offs between model flexibility and structural bias.  
- Strength: strong methodological rigor and comprehensive evaluation with detailed supplementary material.  
- Strength: empirical results convincingly outperform prior ILP-based feature-engineered systems.  
- Strength: insightful analysis and practical recommendation to model component and relation detection jointly but distinctly (e.g., via MTL).  
- Weakness: potential data leakage — topic overlap between train/test may inflate absolute F1 scores and affect generalization.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty for the best-performing LSTM-ER model since it applies a prior method to a new task.  
- Weakness: some architectural descriptions (e.g., “decouples entity and relation detection”) lack clear explanation.  
- Question: Are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores directly comparable given paragraph models cannot capture inter-paragraph relations?  
- Question: How is the original, potentially non-tree argument graph reconstructed losslessly after converting to a tree by re-linking claims to a single major claim?  
- Question: Do the differing structural constraints (LSTM-ER’s all-pairs vs. STagT’s single outgoing relation) explain their performance differences on short vs. long documents?
