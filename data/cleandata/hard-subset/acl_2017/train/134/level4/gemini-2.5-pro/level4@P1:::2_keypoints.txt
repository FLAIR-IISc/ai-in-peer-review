Paper compares four end-to-end neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task sequence labeling, and LSTM-ER.  
All approaches are evaluated against a feature-based ILP baseline using F1 for components and relations.  
Framing as dependency parsing yields poor performance.  
Sequence labeling models are much more effective.  
LSTM-ER performs exceptionally well, particularly at paragraph level, but degrades on longer texts.  
Multi-task learning significantly improves the sequence labeling model.  
Neural models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) outperform the ILP baseline, producing new state-of-the-art results.  
Comprehensive supplement details experimental setup, training, and hyperparameter optimization.  
Potential dataset limitation: possible information leakage if essays on the same topic appear in both train and test sets.  
Methodological novelty concern: LSTM-ER is a direct application of a prior model (Miwa & Bansal 2016).  
Question: How would the original multi-parent claim links be recovered after forcing a tree structure for dependency parsing?  
Question: What exactly is meant by LSTM-ER “de-coupl[ing]” entity and relation detection—separate parameters/objectives or something else?  
Question: Are relation F1 scores comparable between paragraph- and essay-level experiments, and how are cross-paragraph relations handled in evaluation?
