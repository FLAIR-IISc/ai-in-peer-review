### Strengths

The paper is clearly written and accessible. The proposed methods are novel and the reported results are compelling.

### Weaknesses

The evaluation methodology and the reliability of the results raise some concerns, as detailed below.

### General Discussion

This paper presents a neural network-based system for end-to-end argumentation mining. To this end, the authors frame the problem through two distinct approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. The authors also explore a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling model.

The motivation for the proposed model is clearly articulated. A key advantage is that it avoids the extensive manual effort required by previous methods, which often rely on Integer Linear Programming (ILP), handcrafted features, and manually designed constraints. Furthermore, by jointly learning the subtasks of argumentation mining, the model avoids the problem of error propagation inherent in pipeline-based systems. With the exception of a few minor details noted below, the methods are explained clearly.

While the experiments are substantial and the comparisons are conducted appropriately, my primary concerns revolve around the following issues:

**1. Dataset Size and Overfitting:** My main concern pertains to the small dataset size relative to the high capacity of the BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The dataset consists of approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing, but the size of the development set is not specified. This limited amount of training data is a crucial concern. The total number of annotated tags in the training set is likely only a few thousand, which is orders of magnitude smaller than datasets for standard sequence labeling tasks.

Given this data scarcity, it is unclear whether the model parameters can be trained robustly without overfitting. The paper does not include an analysis of this potential problem. An analysis of training and development loss curves over epochs would be highly valuable to assess this. The authors themselves allude to this possibility on line 622, stating that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting," which could be interpreted as evidence that the more complex models are indeed overfitting.

**2. Model Stability and Statistical Significance:** A related concern is model stability. Given the small dataset and the non-convex nature of neural network training, results can be sensitive to random initialization. The paper should report the mean and standard deviation of performance over multiple runs with different random seeds. Furthermore, statistical significance tests are needed to validate the reported improvements. Without such analyses, it is difficult to determine whether the observed performance gains are due to the model's superiority or simply due to chance.

**3. Regularization:** While neural networks inherently possess regularization capabilities, the small size of the dataset warrants a more thorough discussion of the regularization strategies employed. Regularization is not mentioned in the main paper and only briefly noted for LSTM-ER in the supplement. This critical aspect needs to be addressed more comprehensively.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Consider using a more systematic hyper-parameter optimization method, such as Bayesian optimization, instead of the one described in the supplementary materials.
*   Information on pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis, currently in the supplement, is crucial for understanding the main results and should be moved into the body of the paper.
*   Including inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset (available in the original corpus paper) would provide valuable context, helping to establish a performance ceiling and frame the system's achievements.
*   Figure 1 could be improved for black-and-white printing by using patterns or distinct shades of gray in addition to colors.
