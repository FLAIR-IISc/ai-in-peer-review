- Strengths:
The paper is commendable for its clarity of presentation. The proposed methods and the corresponding results are compelling.

- Weaknesses:
There are potential concerns regarding the evaluation methodology and the robustness of the reported results, as detailed in the comments below.

- General Discussion:
This paper presents a neural network-based framework for end-to-end argumentation mining, formulating the problem through two distinct approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. The authors also investigate a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling model. The manuscript clearly articulates the model's motivation, highlighting its advantages over existing methods that rely on ILP, extensive manual feature engineering, and the manual design of constraints. The proposed end-to-end model circumvents such manual effort and, by jointly learning subtasks, mitigates the error propagation issues common in pipeline-based systems. The methodology is, with a few exceptions noted below, explained lucidly.

The experiments are comprehensive, the comparisons are conducted appropriately, and the results are noteworthy. My primary reservation stems from the small dataset size relative to the high capacity of the employed (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset consists of only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not specified in either the paper or the supplementary materials. This is a critical issue, as the number of available training instances is very low. The total number of annotated tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand, which contrasts sharply with standard sequence labeling tasks where training sets often contain hundreds of thousands, or even millions, of tags. Consequently, it is unclear whether the model parameters were trained sufficiently. The paper also omits an analysis of overfitting. It would be highly beneficial to see plots of the training and development loss curves. The authors themselves provide a statement that could be seen as evidence of overfitting: (Line 622) "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting".

For the same reason, the stability of the models is questionable. The authors should report the mean and standard deviation of results over multiple runs with different parameter initializations. Furthermore, statistical significance tests are needed to provide more insight into the models' stability and the reliability of the results. Without these tests, it is difficult to determine if the reported performance gains are due to the superiority of the proposed method or are merely a result of chance.

I acknowledge that the neural networks likely incorporate regularization techniques. However, given the dataset's limited size, the authors must pay closer attention to this aspect. Regularization is not mentioned in the main paper and is only briefly touched upon in the supplementary material concerning LSTM-ER. This point requires proper treatment in the manuscript.

I suggest considering Bayesian optimization methods for hyper-parameter tuning instead of the current approach.

Furthermore, I recommend moving the information about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main paper; the additional page should suffice.

Please include inter-annotator agreement scores. The paper that introduced the dataset contains this information, which would offer valuable insight into the task's difficulty and the remaining room for improvement.

Please consider using distinct patterns or colors in Figure 1 to improve its readability in black-and-white prints.
