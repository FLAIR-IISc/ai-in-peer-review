**Strengths:**
The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and easy to follow. The proposed methods and the corresponding results are interesting and represent a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The small dataset size raises significant concerns about the potential for overfitting and the stability of the reported results.

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes a neural, end-to-end system for argumentation mining, framing the task using two distinct approaches: sequence labeling and dependency parsing. The authors also explore a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling model. The paper's motivation is clearly articulated: in contrast to prior work that relies on ILP, extensive feature engineering, and manually designed constraints, the proposed model avoids this manual effort. Furthermore, by jointly learning the subtasks of argumentation mining, the model avoids the error propagation common in pipeline-based systems. The methods are explained clearly, with the exception of a few minor details noted below.

While the experiments are substantial and the comparisons are appropriate, my main concern relates to the small dataset size relative to the high capacity of the BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The dataset consists of only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not reported in either the paper or the supplementary material. This is a significant concern, as it leaves a very small number of essays for training, which could compromise the model's ability to learn generalizable parameters. The total number of annotated tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand, a stark contrast to standard sequence labeling tasks where hundreds of thousands or even millions of tags are available.

This small data regime raises questions about whether the model parameters can be robustly trained and makes overfitting a major concern. The paper does not analyze the potential for overfitting. It would be highly informative to see plots of the training and development loss curves (per-epoch or per-update) to inspect for divergence. The authors themselves provide what could be interpreted as evidence of this issue, stating (Line 622): "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting."

Given the small dataset, the stability of the models is also a concern. The paper would be significantly strengthened by reporting the mean and standard deviation over multiple runs with different random initializations. Without this, and without statistical significance tests, it is difficult to determine whether the reported performance improvements are due to the superiority of the proposed method or are simply the result of random variation.

I understand that neural networks can benefit from implicit regularization. However, given the dataset size, the authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the explicit regularization methods used. The main paper does not mention regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly touches on it in the context of LSTM-ER. This issue should be addressed more directly in the paper.

Further suggestions:
*   Instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization method, consider using more advanced techniques like Bayesian optimization.
*   I recommend moving the details on pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main paper. The additional page allowance for camera-ready versions should be sufficient for this.
*   Including the inter-annotator agreement scores from the original dataset paper would be beneficial. This would provide valuable context for the system's performance and help gauge the remaining room for improvement.
*   For Figure 1, please consider using patterns or different line styles in addition to color to ensure it is legible when printed in black and white.
