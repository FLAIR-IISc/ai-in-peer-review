This paper presents a thorough investigation of joint neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore and compare several distinct modeling frameworks: 1) casting the problem as dependency parsing, for which several different parsers are tested; 2) formulating it as a sequence labeling task; 3) enhancing the sequence labeling model with multi-task learning (MTL); and 4) applying an existing neural model for entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER). These approaches are evaluated against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, using F1 scores for both argument components and relations.

The experiments yield several valuable insights. Notably, the dependency parsing formulations perform poorly, whereas the sequence labeling approaches prove to be highly effective. The LSTM-ER model, though an off-the-shelf implementation, achieves very strong performance, particularly at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP-based baseline. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that the sequence labeling model's performance can be significantly improved via multi-task learning, with an auxiliary claim identification task being more beneficial than a relation identification task. The work is supported by a very comprehensive supplement detailing the model training and hyper-parameter optimization processes. Overall, this is a very thorough and well-executed study of neural approaches to this important task.

**Major Remarks**

*   A potential concern relates to the dataset split. If essays in the training and test sets cover the same topics, writers may use similar or identical arguments across both sets. This overlap could lead to information leakage from the training set to the test set, potentially resulting in overly optimistic performance estimates. While the authors should acknowledge and discuss this possibility, it is worth noting that this issue would likely affect the ILP baseline as well, meaning the comparison between the proposed models and the baseline remains fair.

*   A second point of consideration is that one of the paper's best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of an existing model from prior work. Despite this, the paper's contribution remains significant. The comprehensive experimental comparison, the clear success of sequence-based models, and the valuable lessons learned from the various approaches make this a solid and informative study that merits publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions**

*   **Lines 222-226:** The argument regarding the reconstruction of a full graph from a tree-based output is not entirely clear. Further clarification on this process would be helpful.

*   **Lines 443-444:** The presentation order in the results section (sequence tagging -> dependency parsing -> MTL) is somewhat difficult to follow. A more logical flow might be to present the dependency-based models first, followed by the sequence tagging models and their MTL extension. This would improve readability, as the reader would not need to recall abbreviations like 'STag_T' from much earlier in the text.

*   **Line 455 & 477-479:** The description of the LSTM-ER model contains some confusing phrasing. On line 455, it is stated that the model "de-couples" components while "jointly" modeling them. These concepts appear contradictory, and a check of the cited Miwa and Bansal (2016) paper did not immediately clarify this point. Similarly, the claim on lines 477-479 that the system de-couples relation and entity information is unclear. A possible interpretation is that the model learns the tree structure (edges) and the labels on that structure as separate but related tasks. Clarifying the precise meaning of 'de-coupling' in this context would strengthen the paper.

*   Are the F1 scores reported for the paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, particularly for the relation extraction task? It seems that paragraph-based models are structurally incapable of identifying cross-paragraph relations, which could lead to a skewed comparison if not properly contextualized. A brief discussion of the implications of this would be welcome.
