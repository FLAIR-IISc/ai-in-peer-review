- Paper presents a thorough empirical comparison of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining.  
- Explored frameworks: dependency parsing (multiple parsers), sequence labeling, sequence-labeling-based MTL, off-the-shelf LSTM-ER (joint entity/relation extraction), and an ILP baseline.  
- Evaluation uses F1 scores for argument components (concepts) and argumentative relations.  
- Key finding: dependency-parsing formulations perform poorly.  
- Key finding: sequence labeling approaches are highly effective and outperform ILP baseline.  
- Key finding: LSTM-ER achieves excellent performance, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Key finding: multi-task learning significantly boosts sequence labeling performance.  
- Paper includes a comprehensive supplement detailing training and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Overall assessment: thorough, valuable exploration with results that merit publication.  
- Major concern 1: possible topical overlap between training and test essays could cause lexical/structural leakage and inflate performance; authors should discuss impact.  
- Major concern 2: LSTM-ER is reused from prior work; the main contribution is empirical demonstration and comparative analysis rather than a novel architecture.  
- Minor point: clarify how a full argumentative graph can be reconstructed from the predicted tree structure (Lines 222–226).  
- Minor point: suggest reordering results to present weaker dependency-based models before sequence-tagging and MTL for better readability (Lines 443–444).  
- Minor point: clarify terminology around LSTM-ER “de-couples” vs “jointly modeling” entity and relation information (Lines 455 & 477–479).  
- Minor point: clarify whether paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores, especially for relations, are directly comparable given paragraph models can avoid cross-paragraph relations.
