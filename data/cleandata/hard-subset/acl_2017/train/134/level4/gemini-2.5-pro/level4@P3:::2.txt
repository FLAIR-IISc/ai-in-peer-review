### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a thorough empirical investigation into neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the task in several ways: (1) as a token-based dependency parsing problem, evaluating several classic and neural parsers; (2) as a sequence tagging problem using state-of-the-art BiLSTM-CRF architectures; (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem built on the sequence tagging framework; and (4) by applying an existing end-to-end model for joint entity and relation extraction (Miwa and Bansal, 2016's LSTM-ER). These neural approaches are benchmarked against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) model. The experiments, conducted on the persuasive essays dataset, reveal that sequence tagging models are highly effective, outperforming both the dependency parsing approaches and the ILP baseline. The results also highlight an interesting trade-off: the more general LSTM-ER model excels on shorter, paragraph-level texts, while the more constrained sequence tagging model is more robust on longer, full-essay texts.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Comprehensive Empirical Study of Neural Models for End-to-End AM:** The primary contribution of this work is its systematic and wide-ranging comparison of different neural framings for a complex structured prediction task. By evaluating dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and joint entity-relation extraction models, the paper provides valuable insights into which architectural assumptions are best suited for argumentation mining, moving the field beyond feature-engineered pipeline systems.

2.  **Demonstration that Simpler, Local Models Outperform Global Parsing Models:** The paper provides strong evidence that framing end-to-end AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem is ineffective. In contrast, it shows that comparatively simpler sequence tagging models, which make local decisions but can capture long-range dependencies via BiLSTMs, are significantly more robust and effective. This is an important finding that can guide future research in this area.

3.  **New State-of-the-Art Results on a Challenging AM Benchmark:** The proposed sequence tagging models, as well as the application of the LSTM-ER model, achieve new state-of-the-art results on the persuasive essays dataset. They significantly outperform a strong, feature-based ILP approach, demonstrating that neural end-to-end systems can effectively learn the complex constraints of argumentation structure without manual feature engineering or explicit constraint definition.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Systematic and Thorough Investigation:** The paper's greatest strength is its comprehensiveness. The authors do not simply propose a single new model but instead thoughtfully explore multiple, conceptually distinct ways to frame the end-to-end AM problem using neural networks. The inclusion of four different dependency parsers, a state-of-the-art sequence tagger, an MTL setup, and a pre-existing joint model (LSTM-ER) provides a rich comparative analysis. The evaluation on both paragraph and full-essay levels further strengthens the findings.

2.  **Clear and Actionable Findings:** The study produces clear takeaways that are valuable to the community. The definitive negative result for the dependency parsing approach is just as informative as the success of the sequence tagging models. The detailed analysis explaining why LSTM-ER performs well on short texts (paragraph-level) but degrades on longer ones (essay-level) provides a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs between model constraints and task complexity.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates that neural methods can substantially outperform a sophisticated, feature-based ILP system. This is a significant result, as it shows that the need for laborious feature engineering and the design of complex ILP constraints can be eliminated. The fact that the models achieve this in a challenging end-to-end setting is particularly impressive.

4.  **Excellent Analysis and Discussion:** The authors provide insightful analysis for their results. For example, the discussion about why the LSTM-Parser is the best among the parsers (due to its encoding of the full stack history) and the hypothesis for why sequence taggers are more robust on long documents (they are simpler local models less prone to overfitting on a vast search space) are well-reasoned and convincing. The analysis of how MTL improves performance is also a valuable contribution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential for Data Leakage in the Experimental Setup:** My primary concern relates to the dataset. Since the training and test sets consist of essays on a limited set of controversial topics, there is a risk that writers use similar arguments and phrasing across both sets. This could lead to topic-specific or argument-specific lexical cues being learned, potentially inflating performance and limiting the generalizability of the results to new topics. While the comparison against the ILP baseline is fair (as it operates under the same conditions), the absolute F1 scores might be overly optimistic. A discussion of this potential limitation would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Novelty of the Best-Performing Model:** One of the strongest performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of an existing model from prior work. While its application to AM and the subsequent analysis of its behavior are novel and insightful, this does diminish the methodological novelty of the paper. The work's core strength lies more in its comprehensive comparative analysis and the lessons learned, rather than in the proposal of a fundamentally new architecture.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  **Comparability of Paragraph vs. Essay Evaluation:** In Section 3, you note that argumentation structure is typically contained within a paragraph, "except, possibly, for the relation from claims to major claims." Does this mean that the paragraph-level models are structurally incapable of identifying these cross-paragraph relations? If so, the paragraph-level task seems to be an easier and potentially incomplete version of the full AM task. Could you clarify how this affects the direct comparability of the Relation F1 (R-F1) scores between the paragraph and essay settings?

2.  **Clarification on "De-coupling" vs. "Joint Modeling":** In Section 4 (e.g., lines 455, 477), you use the phrase "de-coupling ... but jointly modeling them" to describe the LSTM-ER model. This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. Could you please clarify this distinction? My interpretation is that "de-coupling" refers to using distinct architectural components for entity and relation prediction, which are then optimized together within a single "joint" end-to-end model. Is this correct? A clearer explanation would be helpful.

3.  **Recovery of Argumentation Graph from Tree Structure:** When framing AM as dependency parsing (Section 3), you mention that multi-parent relations (e.g., claims linking to multiple major claims) are handled by creating a tree structure. How is the original, non-tree argumentation graph recovered from the parser's tree output during evaluation? This detail seems crucial for understanding whether the dependency framing is truly "loss-less" as stated.
