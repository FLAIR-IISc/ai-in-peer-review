This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into joint neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore and compare several distinct methodologies:
1)  Framing the task as a dependency parsing problem, testing several different parsers.
2)  Formulating the task as a sequence labeling problem.
3)  A multi-task learning (MTL) framework built upon the sequence labeling model.
4)  An off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER).
5)  State-of-the-art models based on Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for comparison.

All methods are evaluated using F1-score computed on both concepts and relations. The empirical results show that dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling approaches are considerably more effective. The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model, in particular, achieves very strong performance, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP-based baseline.

The authors provide a thorough supplementary material section, detailing model training procedures, hyper-parameter optimization, and other technical specifics. A key finding is that the performance of sequence labeling models can be significantly enhanced through the multi-task learning approach, with the claim identification task contributing more to the improvement than the relation task. Overall, this work represents a very thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Major Remarks**

*   A primary concern relates to the dataset. If essays in the training and test sets cover the same topics, writers may use similar or identical arguments across both sets. This could lead to information leakage from the training data to the test data, resulting in overly optimistic performance estimates. Although this issue likely affects the ILP models as well—meaning the proposed models do not have an unfair advantage—it is a potential limitation that warrants discussion in the paper.
*   Another point of consideration is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of a model from prior work. However, given the demonstrated success of the sequence-based models, the extensive set of experiments, and the valuable lessons learned from the comparative analysis, this work makes a sufficient contribution to merit publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions**

*   Lines 222-226: The argument that it is possible to reconstruct the full argumentation graph from the tree-based output of the parser is not sufficiently clear. Please elaborate on this reconstruction process.
*   Lines 443-444: The order of presentation in this section is sequence tagging -> dependency parsing -> MTL. The section would be much easier to follow if the dependency-based models were discussed before the sequence tagging models, as the reader has to recall details about `STag_T` later when the MTL model is introduced.
*   Line 455: The phrase stating that the model "de-couples" components but "jointly models them" seems contradictory, as joint modeling typically implies coupling. An examination of the cited Miwa and Bansal paper did not clarify this point. Please revise this description.
*   Lines 477-479: The explanation of how the system de-couples relation information from entity information is confusing. A possible interpretation is that the model learns the structure (edges of a tree) and the labels (types of relations) as separate sub-tasks. In any case, this part should be rephrased for clarity.
*   Are the F1 scores reported for the paragraph and essay settings directly comparable, especially for the relation tasks? I am concerned that paragraph-based models may inherently miss cross-paragraph relations, as they are never presented with the opportunity to identify them, which could skew the comparison.
