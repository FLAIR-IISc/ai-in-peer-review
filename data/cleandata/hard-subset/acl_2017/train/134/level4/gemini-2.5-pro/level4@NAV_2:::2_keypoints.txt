- Paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task tagging, and LSTM-ER) and shows sequence tagging and LSTM-ER outperform a strong ILP baseline while dependency parsing fails.

Contributions:
- Thorough, systematic empirical evaluation providing a benchmark and insights for end-to-end argumentation mining.
- Key finding that simpler local models (BiLSTM sequence taggers) are more effective and robust than complex global models for long documents.
- Demonstration that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (e.g., component ID) improves main task performance.

Strengths:
- Comprehensive investigation comparing multiple distinct neural modeling approaches.
- Strong empirical results: sequence tagging and LSTM-ER beat a feature-based state-of-the-art ILP system.
- Valuable analysis and insights, including negative result for dependency parsing and paragraph vs essay performance comparison.
- High potential for reproducibility due to detailed supplementary training and hyperparameter documentation.

Weaknesses:
- Potential data leakage if essays on the same topic appear in both train and test splits, possibly inflating performance estimates.
- Reduced novelty because one of the best models (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of a prior off-the-shelf model.

Questions to authors:
- How is the original full graph recovered from the model’s tree-based output after the loss-less re-linking transformation?
- What precisely is meant by “decoupling entity and relation detection but jointly modeling them” in the LSTM-ER description?
- Are relation F1 scores directly comparable between paragraph- and essay-level evaluations, and how are cross-paragraph relations treated in paragraph-level evaluation?
