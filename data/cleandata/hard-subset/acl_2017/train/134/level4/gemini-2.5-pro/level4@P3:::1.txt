### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the task in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a complex sequence tagging problem, and a multi-task learning (MTL) problem. These approaches are compared against each other, a strong feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, and the joint entity-relation extraction model of Miwa and Bansal (2016). The primary goal is to move beyond systems that rely on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures, instead leveraging the ability of neural networks to learn features automatically and model subtasks jointly.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A thorough empirical comparison of different neural architectures for end-to-end AM.** The paper's most significant finding is that simpler, local sequence tagging models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) are more effective and robust for this task than more complex, global models like token-based dependency parsers, particularly as document length increases. This provides valuable insight into how to best model this complex structured prediction problem.
2.  **Demonstration that neural models can achieve new state-of-the-art results without manual feature engineering.** The proposed sequence tagging and joint models outperform a strong, feature-rich ILP system. This confirms that end-to-end neural methods are a highly promising direction for AM, eliminating the need for costly feature design and domain-specific constraint engineering.
3.  **An insightful analysis of model constraints and their impact on performance.** The paper effectively diagnoses why certain models fail in specific settings (e.g., the performance degradation of the LSTM-ER model on relation extraction for full essays), linking it to the unconstrained search space over entity pairs in longer documents. This analysis provides a clear takeaway for future work in this area.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Scope of Investigation.** To my knowledge, this is the first work to systematically explore and compare a diverse set of neural, end-to-end frameworks for the full AM task. The authors do not merely propose a single model but thoughtfully evaluate conceptually different approaches (global parsing vs. local tagging, single-task vs. multi-task, decoupled vs. monolithic labeling). This breadth makes the paper a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis.** The experiments are substantial and well-designed. The comparison across multiple parsers, tagging models, and a competitive ILP baseline is comprehensive. The analysis goes beyond simply reporting numbers; for instance, the discussion on why token-level dependency parsing struggles (Section 5a) and the diagnosis of LSTM-ER's performance drop on the essay-level task (Section 5d, Figure 4) are particularly insightful and convincing. The division of evaluation into paragraph-level and essay-level provides a clear view of how model performance scales with input length and complexity.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation.** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors effectively articulate the shortcomings of previous pipeline and feature-based methods (e.g., error propagation, effort in feature/constraint design) and build a strong case for exploring joint neural models. The different framings of the AM task are explained clearly with helpful examples (e.g., the sequence tagging label structure and the dependency representation in Figure 3).

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential for Overfitting and Lack of Regularization Details.** My primary concern is the combination of high-capacity models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) with a very small dataset. The corpus contains only 402 essays in total, which means the training set is likely around 320 essays. The paper does not specify the size of the development set, making it unclear how many essays were available for actual training. With such limited data, deep neural models are highly susceptible to overfitting. The authors allude to this possibility ("taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting"), but the paper lacks a rigorous analysis of this issue. A detailed discussion of the regularization techniques employed for all neural models is necessary but is absent from the main paper and only briefly mentioned for one model in the supplement.
2.  **Reliability of Results and Model Stability.** The paper reports single performance scores for each model. Given the stochastic nature of neural network training (e.g., random weight initialization) and the small size of the test set (80 essays), a single run may not be representative of the model's true performance. To ensure the reliability and reproducibility of the results, it is crucial to report the mean and standard deviation of F1 scores over multiple runs with different random seeds. Without this, it is difficult to assess the stability of the models or to be certain that the observed performance differences are not due to chance. Furthermore, claims of superior performance should be supported by statistical significance tests.
3.  **Missing Experimental Details in the Main Paper.** While the paper is generally clear, some critical experimental details are either missing or relegated to the supplementary material. The train/development/test split is not specified. Information regarding the pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis is important for a full understanding of the results and should ideally be included in the main paper, perhaps by condensing other sections. The lack of Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA) scores from the original dataset paper makes it hard to contextualize the model performance and estimate the upper bound for the task.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please explicitly state the train/development/test split of the dataset in terms of the number of essays? Given the small dataset size, a more detailed discussion of overfitting is crucial. Could you provide learning curves (loss or F1 score vs. epoch) for both the training and development sets to show how the models generalized?
2.  What specific regularization techniques (e.g., dropout rates, L2 penalty, etc.) were used for the dependency parsers and the sequence tagging models (STagBLCC/STagBL)? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for assessing the approach to mitigating overfitting.
3.  Have you performed multiple runs for your main experiments? If so, please report the mean and standard deviation of the F1 scores to demonstrate model stability. Could you also add statistical significance tests to substantiate the claims that your best models outperform the ILP baseline and the other neural models?
