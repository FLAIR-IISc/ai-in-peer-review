- Paper proposes end-to-end neural approaches for argumentation mining, framed as token-level dependency parsing and sequence tagging (with an MTL extension).  
- Main contribution: first systematic application and comparison of modern neural architectures to end-to-end AM, moving away from hand-crafted pipelines.  
- Empirical insight: sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF variants) outperforms token-level dependency parsing for AM under the data constraints.  
- Result: sequence tagging models (with MTL or LSTM-ER) achieve state-of-the-art on the persuasive essays dataset, beating a competitive ILP baseline.  
- Strength: well-motivated and clearly presented argument for joint end-to-end neural models over feature-engineered pipelines.  
- Strength: comprehensive experimental evaluation covering multiple dependency parsers and sequence tagging architectures, compared to ILP and LSTM-ER baselines.  
- Strength: strong and interesting empirical findings, including the benefits of MTL and the superiority of representation learning over feature-rich ILP.  
- Weakness: potential overfitting risk due to high-capacity neural models trained on a small corpus (~320 essays) and no presented learning curves.  
- Weakness: lack of stability analysis and significance testing — results appear to be from single runs without mean/variance across seeds.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of regularization techniques and hyperparameter tuning, hindering reproducibility and confidence in results.  
- Question: what is the development set size, and can the authors provide training/dev learning curves for top models?  
- Question: can the authors report mean and variance of F1 over multiple runs (e.g., 5–10 seeds) for key models?  
- Question: are reported performance differences statistically significant, and what specific regularization methods/settings (e.g., dropout rates, L2) were used?
