- Paper investigates several neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining framed as sequence tagging, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning, compared to an ILP baseline.
- Evaluates models on identifying argument components and relations at paragraph and essay levels.
- Contribution: first comprehensive exploration of neural end-to-end models for argumentation mining that can outperform a state-of-the-art ILP system.
- Contribution: comparative analysis finds simpler sequence tagging models more robust/effective than dependency parsing models for this task.
- Contribution: multi-task learning can further improve performance.
- Strength: well-motivated shift away from pipeline, feature-heavy systems toward end-to-end neural approaches.
- Strength: thorough, systematic experiments comparing four neural framings with a strong ILP baseline and analyses across short and long contexts.
- Strength: clear writing and good presentation of modeling choices and motivations.
- Weakness: very small dataset (402 essays, 80 test) risks overfitting given high-capacity (Bi)LSTM models.
- Weakness: model stability concerns—results reported from a single run without averages or standard deviations across random seeds.
- Weakness: lack of training/dev learning curves or analysis to assess/mitigate overfitting.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of regularization techniques and hyperparameter tuning for the main models.
- Weakness: important experimental details (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis) are relegated to supplementary material rather than the main paper.
- Weakness: missing context for scores—no inter-annotator agreement figures provided to contextualize performance.
- Questions for authors: specify development set size used for hyperparameter tuning.
- Questions for authors: provide training and development loss/F1 plots and mean/std F1 over multiple runs plus details on regularization per model.
- Post-rebuttal: authors clarified some points, but reviewers request incorporation of reported F1 ranges and mean/variance into the final paper and discussion of high variance (e.g., Kiperwasser parser).
