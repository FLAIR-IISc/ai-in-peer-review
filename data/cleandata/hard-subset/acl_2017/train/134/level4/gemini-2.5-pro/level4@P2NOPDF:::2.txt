This paper presents a thorough investigation into neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors systematically explore and compare several distinct modeling frameworks:
1)  Framing the problem as dependency parsing, tested with several different parsers.
2)  Framing the problem as a sequence labeling task.
3)  A multi-task learning (MTL) setup built upon the sequence labeling model.
4)  An off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER).
5)  Comparisons against a state-of-the-art model based on Integer Linear Programming (ILP).

All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores for both argumentation components (concepts) and argumentative relations. The experiments demonstrate that sequence labeling approaches are highly effective and outperform both the dependency parsing models and the state-of-the-art ILP-based system. Notably, the existing LSTM-ER model achieves very strong performance, especially at the paragraph level. The results also show that the sequence labeling model can be significantly improved via a multi-task learning approach. The paper is accompanied by a very comprehensive supplement that details the technical aspects of model training and hyperparameter optimization, which greatly adds to the work's value.

Overall, this is a very thorough and valuable study of neural methods for this complex task.

**Major Remarks**

My primary concerns relate to the dataset and the novelty of the best-performing model.

1.  A potential concern lies with the experimental dataset. It is not clear whether the training and test sets are partitioned by essay topic. If essays on the same topic appear in both splits, writers may use similar or identical arguments, which could lead to information leakage from the training set to the test set. This, in turn, might result in an overestimation of the models' generalization performance. While I believe this issue would likely affect the ILP baselines as well—meaning the proposed models do not have an unfair advantage—a discussion of this potential limitation and its impact on the results would strengthen the paper.

2.  One of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of a pre-existing model from related work. While this might limit the paper's novelty in one respect, this concern is largely mitigated by the paper's extensive experimental comparison and the valuable insights derived from the relative performance of the different approaches. The success of the sequence labeling paradigm, thoroughly explored here, is a significant finding in its own right. Given the comprehensive nature of the experiments and the useful lessons learned, I believe this work is a valuable contribution to the field and deserves to be published.

**Minor Remarks and Questions**

-   **Lines 222-226:** The argument that a full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from the tree-structured output of the dependency parser is not entirely clear. Further explanation of this reconstruction process would be beneficial.

-   **Lines 443-444:** The presentation order in the results section (Sequence Tagging -> Dependency Parsing -> MTL using Sequence Tagging) could be improved for clarity. Since the MTL model builds upon the sequence tagging approach, a more intuitive flow would be to present the dependency-based models first, followed by the sequence tagging models and then the MTL extension. This would make the section easier for the reader to follow.

-   **Line 455 & 477-479:** The paper states that the LSTM-ER model "de-couples" entity and relation information while modeling them "jointly" (line 455), and later that it "de-couples relation information from the entity information" (lines 477-479). This phrasing is confusing, as joint modeling typically implies some form of coupling. The authors should clarify what is meant by "de-coupling" in this context. For instance, does the model learn separate representations for entities and relations before combining them? A more precise description would be helpful.

-   Are the F1 scores reported in the paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, especially for relation identification? Paragraph-level models are inherently unable to capture cross-paragraph relations. It would be helpful to discuss how this limitation affects the evaluation and the interpretation of the paragraph-level results in comparison to the essay-level ones.
