- Paper investigates five joint neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL built on sequence labeling, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.  
- Evaluation uses F1 scores on both concept (claim/premise) and relation predictions.  
- Empirical finding: dependency-parsing solutions perform poorly while sequence-labeling approaches are considerably more effective.  
- The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves very strong performance, especially at paragraph level.  
- Both sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art baseline.  
- Multi-task learning significantly boosts sequence-labeling performance, with the claim-identification auxiliary task contributing more than the relation task.  
- Supplementary material is thorough, detailing training procedures, hyper-parameter search, and technical specifics.  
- Major concern: potential topic overlap between training and test essays could cause information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates; this limitation should be discussed.  
- Major note: LSTM-ER is a direct application of prior work, but the extensive experiments and comparative insights justify the paper’s contribution.  
- Minor: clarify how the full argumentation graph is reconstructed from the parser’s tree-based output (lines 222–226).  
- Minor: recommend reordering the methods section to present dependency-based models before sequence-tagging models for clarity (lines 443–444).  
- Minor: revise the contradictory phrase that the model “de-couples” components yet “jointly models” them (line 455).  
- Minor: rephrase the explanation of how relation information is de-coupled from entity information—e.g., whether structure (edges) and labels are learned as separate sub-tasks (lines 477–479).  
- Minor/question: are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores for relations directly comparable, given paragraph models cannot capture cross-paragraph relations and may thus be disadvantaged or skew comparisons?
