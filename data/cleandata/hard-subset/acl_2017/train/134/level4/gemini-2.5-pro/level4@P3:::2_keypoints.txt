- Thorough empirical comparison of neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, and LSTM-ER, benchmarked against an ILP baseline.  
- Sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF) outperforms dependency parsing and the ILP baseline overall.  
- LSTM-ER performs best on shorter paragraph-level texts, while sequence tagging is more robust on longer essay-level texts.  
- Main contribution: systematic evaluation across multiple neural framings providing actionable guidance for AM modeling.  
- Shows simpler, local sequence tagging models can beat more global parsing formulations for end-to-end AM.  
- Achieves new state-of-the-art results on the persuasive essays dataset with neural models.  
- Strong empirical evidence that neural models can replace feature-engineered ILP systems in complex structured AM tasks.  
- Comprehensive experimental setup (multiple parsers, tagger, MTL, LSTM-ER) and evaluation at paragraph and essay levels.  
- Clear analysis explaining parser vs tagger performance differences and why LSTM-ER degrades on longer texts.  
- Primary weakness: possible data leakage because essays cover a limited set of topics, risking over-optimistic F1s and limited generalization.  
- Methodological novelty limited because the top-performing LSTM-ER is an existing model applied to AM rather than a new architecture.  
- Questions for authors: clarify comparability of paragraph vs essay R-F1 (cross-paragraph relations), explain "de-coupling" vs "joint modeling" phrasing for LSTM-ER, and detail how multi-parent argumentation graphs are recovered from parser tree outputs.
