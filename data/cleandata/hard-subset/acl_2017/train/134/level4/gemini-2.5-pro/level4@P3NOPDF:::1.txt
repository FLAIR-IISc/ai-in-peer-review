This paper proposes an end-to-end neural network system for argumentation mining, framing the problem as either (1) a sequence labeling task or (2) a dependency parsing task. The authors also explore a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper's primary contribution is moving away from traditional methods that rely on intensive manual feature engineering and complex Integer Linear Programming (ILP) constraints. By jointly learning the subtasks of argumentation mining, the proposed end-to-end model also avoids the error propagation common in pipeline-based systems.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The motivation for adopting an end-to-end neural approach is well-articulated, effectively highlighting the limitations of prior work. The proposed models are interesting and represent a valuable step forward for the field. The experiments are substantial, the comparisons to existing methods are performed properly, and the results are promising.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

My primary concern revolves around the small dataset size in relation to the high capacity of the proposed neural models (specifically the BiLSTM-based BLC and BLCC architectures). The dataset consists of only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not specified in either the main paper or the supplementary material. This leaves a very small number of essays for training, which is a critical issue for data-hungry models like BiLSTMs. The total number of tagged instances is likely only a few thousand, a stark contrast to typical sequence labeling tasks where training data often comprises hundreds of thousands of examples.

This data scarcity raises two significant issues:

1.  **Overfitting and Regularization:** The paper does not include an analysis of overfitting. Given the model complexity and limited data, it is crucial to demonstrate that the models are not simply memorizing the training set. Plotting the training and development loss curves over epochs would be highly informative. The authors themselves hint at this issue (Line 622), noting that simpler tagger models "need less training data and are less prone to overfitting," which underscores the need for a more thorough investigation for their more complex models. Consequently, a detailed discussion of the regularization techniques used is essential but is currently absent from the main paper and only briefly mentioned in the supplementary material. This topic warrants a more comprehensive treatment.

2.  **Model Stability and Reliability of Results:** The small dataset also raises concerns about the stability of the models and the reliability of the reported results. A single training run may not be representative of the model's true performance. To address this, the paper must report the mean and standard deviation of F1-scores over multiple runs with different random initializations. Furthermore, statistical significance tests are necessary to confirm whether the observed improvements over baselines are statistically meaningful or potentially due to chance. Without these, it is difficult to ascertain if the better results stem from the superiority of the proposed method or from a fortunate random seed.

**Post-Rebuttal Update and Concluding Remarks**

Following the author's response, which helped clarify some of these issues, I have raised my recommendation. However, I strongly urge the authors to incorporate the following points into the final version of the paper to strengthen its contribution and ensure reproducibility:

*   **Report Model Stability:** Please include the F1-score ranges (or mean and variance) from multiple runs for all experimental settings. This is crucial for transparency and allows the community to properly interpret the results. For example, the large variance mentioned for the Kiperwasser et al. setting needs to be reported and analyzed. An F1-score that changes within a range like [0.56, 0.61] is a significant fluctuation, and reporting this is essential for replicability.

**Additional Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA):** Including the IAA scores from the original dataset paper would provide valuable context, helping to frame the system's performance and understand the inherent difficulty of the task and the available room for improvement.

*   **Move Key Details to Main Paper:** Important information currently in the supplementary material, such as details on the pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis, should be moved into the main paper. The additional page allowed for camera-ready versions should provide sufficient space for this.

*   **Hyper-parameter Optimization:** The authors might consider using more advanced techniques like Bayesian optimization for hyper-parameter tuning, which could lead to better-performing models.

*   **Figure 1:** Please revise Figure 1 to ensure it is clearly interpretable when printed in black and white (e.g., by using different patterns or line styles in addition to colors).
