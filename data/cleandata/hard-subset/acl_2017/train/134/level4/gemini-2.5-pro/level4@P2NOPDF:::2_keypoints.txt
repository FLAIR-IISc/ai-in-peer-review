- Paper presents a thorough empirical study of neural methods for end-to-end argumentation mining, comparing five frameworks: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, MTL on sequence labeling, LSTM-ER, and an ILP baseline.
- Experiments use F1 scores for both argument components (concepts) and argumentative relations at paragraph and essay levels.
- Key finding: sequence labeling methods are highly effective and outperform dependency parsing models and the ILP-based state-of-the-art.
- The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves very strong performance, particularly at the paragraph level.
- Multi-task learning significantly improves the sequence labeling model’s performance.
- A comprehensive supplemental section details model training and hyperparameter optimization, adding substantial value.
- Major concern: unclear whether train/test splits are partitioned by essay topic—topic overlap could cause information leakage and overestimate generalization.
- Major concern: one top-performing model (LSTM-ER) is a direct application of prior work, raising novelty questions, though mitigated by the paper’s comparative insights.
- Minor remark: unclear how a full argumentation graph is reconstructed from tree-structured dependency parser output—needs more explanation.
- Minor remark: suggested reordering of results presentation for clarity (dependency models first, then sequence tagging, then MTL extension).
- Minor remark: confusing phrasing about LSTM-ER “de-coupling” vs “joint” modeling of entities and relations—authors should clarify the representation and combination mechanism.
- Minor remark/question: paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores (especially for relations) may not be directly comparable due to paragraph models’ inability to capture cross-paragraph relations; discuss evaluation implications.
