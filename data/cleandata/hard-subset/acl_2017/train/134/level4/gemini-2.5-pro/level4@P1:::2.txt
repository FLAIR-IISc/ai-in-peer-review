### Summary

This paper presents a thorough investigation into several neural, end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining. The authors frame the problem in multiple ways: (1) as a dependency parsing task, for which they evaluate several different parsers; (2) as a sequence labeling problem; (3) as a multi-task learning problem built upon the sequence labeling model; and (4) by applying an existing model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER). All approaches are evaluated against a state-of-the-art, feature-based ILP model using F1 scores for both argument components and relations.

The key findings are that framing the problem as dependency parsing yields poor performance, while sequence labeling models are much more effective. Notably, the off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model performs exceptionally well, particularly at the paragraph level. Both the proposed sequence labeling approaches and the LSTM-ER model outperform the ILP baseline. The authors also demonstrate that a multi-task learning setup significantly improves the performance of the sequence labeling model. The paper is accompanied by a comprehensive supplement that details the experimental setup, including model training and hyperparameter optimization.

### Strengths

1.  **Comprehensive and Systematic Investigation:** This paper presents a very thorough comparative study of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore four distinct framings of the problem, providing a valuable analysis of their relative merits on the same dataset. This breadth of experimentation is a significant contribution and provides a strong empirical foundation for future work in this area.

2.  **Clear and Actionable Insights:** The experimental results yield clear and valuable lessons. The authors convincingly demonstrate that simpler, local sequence tagging models outperform more complex, global dependency parsing models for this task. Furthermore, the analysis of the LSTM-ER model's performance degradation on longer texts (essay-level vs. paragraph-level) highlights the importance of imposing structural constraints when applying such models to long documents with many potential relations.

3.  **New State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed neural models, particularly the sequence tagging (STagBLCC) and LSTM-ER approaches, successfully outperform the existing state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model. This is a strong result, as it shows that end-to-end neural methods can achieve superior performance without the need for manual feature engineering or the complex design of ILP constraints, representing a significant step forward for the field.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Dataset Limitation:** A potential concern lies with the experimental dataset. If essays on the same topic appear in both the training and test sets, there is a risk of information leakage. Writers may use similar phrasing or arguments for the same topic, which could lead the models to learn topic-specific patterns rather than general argumentation structures. This could, in turn, result in overly optimistic performance estimates. While the authors correctly compare against a baseline (ILP) that faces the same issue (ensuring a fair comparison), it would be beneficial for the authors to discuss this potential limitation and, if possible, clarify the train/test split with respect to essay topics.

2.  **Methodological Novelty:** One of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of a previously published method (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). While its application to a new domain and the corresponding analysis are valuable, this could be seen as limiting the methodological novelty of the work. This is a minor weakness, however, as the paper's primary contribution lies in its thorough comparative analysis and the valuable insights derived from it, rather than the invention of a single new architecture.

### Questions to Authors

1.  **Lines 222-226 (Tree Structure Assumption):** The paper states that since claims relate to all major claims, the argument structure is not a strict tree. You mention that claims can be "loss-lessly" re-linked to a single major claim to create a tree structure for the dependency parser. Could you clarify how the original multi-parent structure for claims would be recovered after parsing? Is the assumption that this post-processing step is straightforward?

2.  **Lines 455 & 477-479 (Model Description):** The text states that LSTM-ER "decoupl[es] entity and relation detection but jointly model[s] them." This phrasing is slightly confusing. Could you elaborate on what is meant by "de-coupling" in this context? Does it mean that the components have separate parameters or objectives within the joint model, as opposed to the STag approach where entity and relation information are encoded in a single, complex label?

3.  **Comparability of F1 Scores:** Are the relation F1 scores directly comparable between the paragraph-level and essay-level experiments? A paragraph-level model is inherently unable to predict inter-paragraph relations. Does the evaluation metric account for this, or are these cross-paragraph relations simply counted as false negatives for the paragraph-level models by default? Please clarify this point.
