This paper presents a thorough investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore and compare several frameworks:
1) Framing the task as a dependency parsing problem, for which they evaluate several parsers.
2) Framing it as a sequence labeling task.
3) A multi-task learning setup built upon the sequence labeling model.
4) An existing neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER).
5) State-of-the-art ILP-based models, which serve as a baseline.

The authors evaluate all approaches using F1 scores for both component identification and relation extraction. The results indicate that dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling approaches are effective. The existing LSTM-ER model performs particularly well, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP-based baseline. The paper is accompanied by a comprehensive supplement that details the technical aspects of model training and hyper-parameter optimization. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that the multi-task learning setup significantly improves the performance of the sequence labeling model, noting that the auxiliary claim identification task is more beneficial than the relation task.

Overall, this paper provides a valuable and in-depth comparison of different neural strategies for this complex task.

**Major Remarks**

*   A primary concern relates to the dataset. It is unclear if essays in the training and test sets cover the same topics. If so, it is possible that writers use similar arguments across both sets, which could lead to information leakage from the training to the test data. This potential overlap could result in overly optimistic performance estimates. While this issue would likely affect the ILP-based baselines as well, meaning the proposed models do not have an unfair advantage, this potential limitation warrants discussion in the paper.
*   A second concern is that one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of an existing model from prior work. However, given the breadth of the experiments, the strong performance of the sequence-based models, and the valuable insights gained from the comparative analysis, I believe the paper makes a solid contribution and merits publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions**

*   Lines 222-226: The paper seems to argue that the full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from the tree-based output of the dependency parser. This point is not entirely clear and could be elaborated upon.
*   Lines 443-444: The presentation order in this section (sequence tagging -> dependency parsing -> multi-task learning) is somewhat confusing. Readability would be improved if the dependency-based models were presented before the sequence tagging models, as the latter serve as the foundation for the MTL approach.
*   Line 455: The paper states that the model de-couples component and relation identification while jointly modeling them. This phrasing is contradictory. How can the two be de-coupled yet modeled jointly? I was unable to find clarification on this point in the cited work by Miwa and Bansal (2016).
*   Lines 477-479: This section reiterates the confusing claim about de-coupling relation and entity information. Perhaps the authors mean that the model separates the task of identifying relational links (the edges of a structure) from the task of classifying the type of those links (the labels on the edges)? In any case, this description requires significant clarification.
*   Are the F1 scores reported for the paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, especially for relation extraction? Paragraph-based models are inherently unable to capture cross-paragraph relations, which could affect the interpretation of the results. Please clarify.
