This paper presents a thorough investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors systematically explore and compare several distinct frameworks:
1.  Formulating the task as a dependency parsing problem, tested with multiple parsers.
2.  Formulating the task as a sequence labeling problem.
3.  A multi-task learning (MTL) approach built upon the sequence labeling model.
4.  An off-the-shelf neural model for joint entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER).
5.  A comparison against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based model.

All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores for both argument components (concepts) and argumentative relations. The key findings are that dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling approaches are highly effective. The pre-existing LSTM-ER model, in particular, achieves excellent performance, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models significantly outperform the ILP baseline. The paper is accompanied by a very comprehensive supplement that details the technical aspects of model training and hyperparameter optimization, which is a commendable addition. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that the multi-task learning setup provides a significant boost to the sequence labeling model's performance.

Overall, this paper is a very thorough and valuable exploration of neural models for this important task.

### Major Remarks

I have two primary concerns that the authors may wish to address.

First, my main concern is related to the dataset and the experimental setup. I am wondering if essays in the training and test sets cover the same topics. If so, it is plausible that writers make similar arguments on the same topic, leading to a lexical or structural overlap between the training and test data. This could result in information leakage and, consequently, overly optimistic performance estimates for the models. While I recognize that this issue would likely affect the baseline ILP models as well, ensuring a fair comparison, a discussion of this potential limitation and its impact on the reported scores would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

Second, one of the best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is a direct application of a model from prior work. However, the paper's contribution is not solely in proposing a novel architecture. The true value lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of this class of models for argumentation mining and, more importantly, in the extensive comparative analysis provided. The wealth of experiments, the useful insights gained (e.g., the failure of dependency parsing models), and the clear performance benefits over strong baselines make this work a significant contribution. Given the thoroughness of the investigation and the valuable lessons learned, I believe this work deserves to be published.

### Minor Remarks and Questions

*   **Lines 222-226:** The argument that a full argumentative graph can be reconstructed from the predicted tree structure is not entirely clear. Could the authors elaborate on this process or provide a more detailed explanation?

*   **Lines 443-444:** The presentation order in the results section is: sequence tagging -> dependency-based -> MTL using sequence tagging. For readability, it would be much easier for the reader to follow if the order of the first two sections were reversed. Presenting the less successful dependency-based models first, followed by the sequence tagging models upon which the MTL approach is built, would create a more logical and intuitive flow.

*   **Line 455 & 477-479:** The paper states that the LSTM-ER model "de-couples" entity and relation information while still "jointly modeling" them. This terminology is somewhat confusing, as joint modeling typically implies coupling. My interpretation, after reading these lines, is that the model learns separate representations or performs separate sub-tasks for identifying entities (nodes) and their relations (labeled edges), but does so within a single, end-to-end architecture. Clarifying this distinction would be very helpful for the reader.

*   **F1 Score Comparability:** Are the F1 scores reported in the paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, particularly for the relation extraction task? I am concerned that paragraph-based models may achieve higher precision by default simply because they never consider or attempt to classify more distant (and potentially more difficult) cross-paragraph relations. This could artificially inflate their performance relative to essay-level models that must contend with the full scope of possible relations. Could the authors comment on this?
