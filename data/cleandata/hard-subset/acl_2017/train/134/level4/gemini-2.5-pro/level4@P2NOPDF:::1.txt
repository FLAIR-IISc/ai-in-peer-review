This paper presents a neural network approach for end-to-end argumentation mining, framing the task as either sequence labeling or dependency parsing. The authors explore both single-task and multitask learning settings for the sequence labeling model. The primary motivation is to create a unified system that avoids the manual feature engineering and complex constraint design inherent in previous ILP-based methods, while also mitigating the error propagation common in pipeline approaches.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The proposed models are interesting, and the authors effectively articulate the advantages of their end-to-end, joint learning framework over prior work. The experiments are substantial, and the comparisons to existing methods appear to be conducted properly.

Despite these strengths, I have several major concerns regarding the experimental methodology and the reliability of the results.

My primary concern is the significant mismatch between the small size of the training dataset and the high capacity of the BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The dataset consists of only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing; the size of the development set is not specified, which is a crucial missing detail. This amount of training data, likely comprising only a few thousand argument-tagged tokens, is orders of magnitude smaller than that used in standard sequence labeling tasks. Consequently, it is unclear whether the models' numerous parameters can be trained effectively without significant overfitting. The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of this issue, for instance, by plotting the training and development loss curves over epochs. The authors' own observation (Line 622) that simpler taggers "need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" seems to corroborate this concern.

A related issue is the stability of the reported results. Given the small dataset, a single run's performance may not be representative. To ensure the reliability of the findings, the authors should report the mean and standard deviation of F1-scores over multiple runs with different random initializations. Furthermore, statistical significance tests are necessary to validate that the observed improvements over baselines are a result of the proposed models' superiority and not due to chance. Without these, it is difficult to draw firm conclusions from the results.

Considering the high risk of overfitting, the paper's lack of discussion on regularization is a notable omission. Effective regularization is critical when training high-capacity models on small datasets. While the supplementary material briefly mentions regularization for one model (LSTM-ER), this topic warrants a thorough treatment within the main paper for all proposed models.

I would also like to offer the following suggestions to further improve the paper:
*   **Hyper-parameter Optimization:** The authors may consider using more advanced techniques like Bayesian optimization for hyper-parameter tuning, which can be more efficient than the method described.
*   **Paper Organization:** Key details currently in the supplementary material, such as the pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis, are central to the paper's contribution and should be moved into the main body. The additional page allowed for camera-ready versions should provide sufficient space.
*   **Performance Context:** Including inter-annotator agreement (IAA) scores from the original dataset paper would provide valuable context for the results, offering a soft upper bound on task performance and better framing the room for improvement.
*   **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1 should be revised to ensure it is legible in black and white prints, for example, by using different patterns or markers in addition to colors.

**Post-rebuttal Comments:**
Following the author response, I have raised my recommendation score. However, my concerns regarding model stability and reproducibility remain. It is essential that the final version of the paper incorporates the results from multiple runs by reporting the mean and variance (or F1-score ranges) for all experimental settings. This transparency is crucial for the community to build upon and replicate this work. The authors should also include a brief analysis of the observed variance—particularly for settings that show high instability, such as the large variance for the Kiperwasser et al. baseline and the non-trivial F1 range of [0.56, 0.61] for the proposed model—as this would add significant credibility to the empirical claims.
