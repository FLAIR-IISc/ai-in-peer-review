The work presents a comprehensive neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several avenues including: 1) formulating the task as a dependency parsing problem (with evaluation of multiple parsers), 2) employing a sequence labeling approach, 3) utilizing multi-task learning built upon a sequence labeling model, 4) applying an existing neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER), and 5) comparing against state-of-the-art ILP-based models. All approaches are evaluated using F1-score for both concept and relation identification. The dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling approaches prove effective. Notably, the LSTM-ER model achieves strong performance, particularly at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP baselines. A detailed supplementary material is provided, covering model training, hyperparameter optimization, and other technical aspects. The authors also demonstrate that sequence labeling models benefit significantly from a multi-task learning framework, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task. Overall, this paper represents a thorough investigation of neural methods for end-to-end argumentation mining.

- Major Remarks
  - My primary concern lies with the dataset. Given that essays in both the training and test sets may address the same topics, it is possible that writers reuse similar arguments, potentially leading to information leakage from the training data and overly optimistic performance estimates. While this issue appears to affect the ILP models as well, meaning your model doesn't necessarily have an unfair advantage, it's a point worth discussing.
  - A secondary concern is that your best-performing model, LSTM-ER, is essentially an off-the-shelf application of an existing model. However, considering the relative success of sequence-based models, alongside the extensive experimentation and valuable insights gained, I believe this work is suitable for publication.

- Minor Remarks and Questions:
  - 222 - 226: I presume the argument here is that a full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from the tree output. This section could benefit from increased clarity.
  - 443-444: The section’s ordering – sequence tagging, dependency-based methods, then multi-task learning using sequence tagging – is somewhat counterintuitive. Reversing the order of the first two would improve readability (by this point, the abbreviation “STag_T” had become difficult to recall).
  - 455: The statement that the model "de-couples" information while "jointly modeling" it is unclear. Jointly modeling inherently requires some level of coupling. I was unable to locate the cited claim in Miwa and Bansal.
  - 477 - 479: The description of the system decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing. My interpretation is that the system learns certain tasks as representing the “edges of the tree” and others as representing the “labels on those edges,” effectively decoupling them in this manner. Regardless, I recommend clarifying this section.
  - Are the F1 scores reported for the paragraph and essay settings comparable, particularly for the relation tasks? I wonder whether paragraph-based models might inherently miss cross-paragraph relations due to the limited scope of their analysis.
