This work presents a comprehensive investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore several framings of the problem: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning (built upon sequence labeling), application of an existing neural model for relation and entity extraction (LSTM-ER), and comparison against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. All approaches are evaluated using standard F1 scores for argument components and relations. The key findings are that dependency-based solutions perform poorly, sequence labeling approaches are effective, and the LSTM-ER model achieves strong results, particularly at the paragraph level. Sequence labeling benefits from a multi-task learning setup, with the claim detection task proving more helpful than relation detection. The paper is accompanied by a detailed supplement covering implementation details and hyperparameter optimization.

**Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Neural Argumentation Mining:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that neural models can achieve competitive or superior performance to traditional, feature-engineered ILP approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. This is a significant step towards simplifying the pipeline and eliminating the need for manual feature design.
2. **Analysis of Framing Choices:** The systematic exploration of different neural framings – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning – provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach for this task. The finding that dependency parsing is ineffective, contrary to some prior expectations, is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Strong Empirical Results & Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough experimental evaluation, including a detailed comparison with a strong baseline, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The inclusion of paragraph-level and essay-level results, and a detailed analysis of multi-task learning configurations, adds depth to the study.

**Strengths:**

* **Thoroughness:** The paper presents a very thorough investigation, exploring a wide range of modeling choices and experimental configurations.
* **Strong Baselines:**  The comparison against a state-of-the-art ILP baseline provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the proposed neural models.
* **Detailed Supplemental Material:** The comprehensive supplemental material enhances the reproducibility and understanding of the work.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the different approaches and experimental results.
* **Practical Insights:** The findings regarding the effectiveness of sequence labeling and the benefits of multi-task learning offer practical guidance for future research in argumentation mining.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Potential Data Leakage:** A potential concern is the possibility of data leakage due to essays on the same topics appearing in both the training and test sets. This could lead to overly optimistic performance estimates. While this issue likely affects the ILP baseline as well, mitigating it or acknowledging its potential impact would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Incremental Novelty of LSTM-ER:** While the LSTM-ER model achieves strong performance, its core contribution lies in applying an existing model from related work to the argumentation mining task. While this application is valuable and well-executed, it is less novel than the development of new architectures or learning algorithms.
* **Clarity of Terminology (Decoupling/Joint Modeling):** The explanation of how LSTM-ER "decouples" relation information from entity information while "jointly modeling" them could be clearer. The phrasing is somewhat contradictory and requires more precise definition.
* **Paragraph vs. Essay Level Results:** The comparability of F1 scores between paragraph-level and essay-level models is unclear, particularly for relation tasks. It's possible that paragraph-based models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations. Addressing this point would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

* Could you discuss the potential impact of topic overlap between training and test essays on the reported performance?
* Could you clarify how LSTM-ER "decouples" relation information from entity information while still "jointly modeling" them? Specifically, what aspects of the model contribute to this decoupling?
* Are the F1 scores at the paragraph and essay levels directly comparable, especially for relation tasks? If not, can you provide insights into the types of errors that are more prevalent at the essay level?
* In section 222-226, you mention reconstructing a full graph from a tree output. Could you elaborate on this process and how it works?



**Additional Comments:**

* Line 443-444: Reversing the order of discussion (sequence tagging before dependency parsing) would improve the flow and readability.
* Line 455: Clarify the meaning of “decoupling” and "jointly modeling."
* Line 477-479: Expand on the meaning of decoupling relation information; perhaps rephrase for clarity.
