This work presents a comprehensive investigation of neural network approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore several methods, including framing the problem as dependency parsing (with multiple parsers), sequence labeling, multi-task learning built upon a sequence labeling model, and applying an existing neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER). They also compare these results to a strong Information Logic Programming (ILP) baseline. Evaluation is consistently performed using F1-score on both concept and relation identification. The results demonstrate that sequence labeling approaches are significantly more effective than dependency-based methods, and notably, the LSTM-ER model achieves strong performance, particularly at the paragraph level. Furthermore, the authors show that multi-task learning substantially improves sequence labeling performance, with the claim identification task proving more beneficial than the relation identification task. A detailed supplementary material provides thorough information regarding model training and hyperparameter optimization.

**Major Concerns:**

My primary concern relates to the dataset. The potential overlap in topics between the training and test sets raises the possibility of information leakage. Writers may reuse similar arguments across essays, leading to artificially inflated performance estimates. While this issue affects the ILP baselines as well, and thus doesn’t necessarily provide your models with an unfair advantage, it’s a critical point for discussion.

Secondly, the top-performing model, LSTM-ER, is essentially an application of an existing model from prior work. However, considering the overall success of sequence-based models, the breadth of experiments conducted, and the valuable insights gained, I believe this work is worthy of publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions:**

*   **Lines 222-226:** The claim that a full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from a tree structure is unclear. Clarification would be helpful.
*   **Lines 443-444:** The presentation order in this section – sequence tagging, dependency parsing, then multi-task learning *using* sequence tagging – is somewhat counterintuitive. Reversing the order of the first two would improve readability, as the acronym “STag\_T” may be forgotten by this point.
*   **Line 455:** The statement that the model “de-couples” information while “jointly modeling” it appears contradictory. Jointly modeling inherently requires coupling. The referenced work (Miwa and Bansal) does not readily clarify this point.
*   **Lines 477-479:** The description of decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing. My interpretation is that the system learns certain tasks representing graph edges and others representing edge labels, effectively decoupling them in this manner. However, this section needs to be rewritten for clarity.
*   **Paragraph vs. Essay F1 Scores:** Are the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings directly comparable, specifically for relation tasks? It’s possible that paragraph-based models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations simply by design, as they don’t consider broader context.
