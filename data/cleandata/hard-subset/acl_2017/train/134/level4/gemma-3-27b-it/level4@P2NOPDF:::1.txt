This paper presents a novel end-to-end argumentation mining system leveraging neural networks, specifically exploring sequence labeling and dependency parsing approaches. The motivation is clearly articulated: to move beyond traditional methods reliant on intensive manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design. A key strength lies in the model's ability to jointly learn argumentation subtasks, potentially mitigating error propagation issues inherent in pipeline approaches. The paper is generally well-written and the methods are explained clearly, with substantial experimental comparisons performed.

However, significant concerns remain regarding the evaluation and the reliability of the reported results. The primary issue is the limited size of the dataset – approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing – coupled with the high capacity of the BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The paper conspicuously omits the size of the development set, exacerbating concerns about insufficient training data. Given the likely small number of total tags in the training set, it is questionable whether the model parameters are effectively trained and whether overfitting has been adequately addressed. While the authors acknowledge the reduced data requirements of taggers (line 622), this does not alleviate the need for a thorough investigation of overfitting. 

Specifically, the inclusion of training and development loss curves would provide valuable insight into the training process. Furthermore, reporting the mean and standard deviation of results across multiple runs with different parameter initializations is crucial to assess model stability. Statistical significance testing is also necessary to determine whether observed performance differences are attributable to the proposed method or simply to chance. 

The paper currently lacks a detailed discussion of regularization techniques. While neural networks inherently employ regularization, the small dataset size necessitates a more focused and explicit consideration of these methods. The supplementary material briefly mentions regularization in LSTM-ER, but this should be expanded upon within the main paper.  I recommend exploring Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning as an alternative to the current approach.

Several pieces of information currently relegated to the supplementary material should be incorporated into the main paper. This includes details regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis. An additional page allowance would readily accommodate this information. Inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, readily available in the dataset’s original publication, should also be included to contextualize system performance and highlight potential areas for improvement. Finally, consider revising Figure 1 with distinct colors to improve its clarity for black and white printing.

Following the authors’ response to my initial questions, I have increased my recommendation score to 4. However, I maintain concerns about model stability. The inclusion of F1-score ranges, as requested, is a positive step towards reproducibility. Nevertheless, the observed variance, particularly in the Kiperwasser setting (e.g., F1 scores ranging from 0.56 to 0.61), requires further investigation and discussion. Reporting mean and variance across different settings is essential for understanding the reliability and generalizability of the results.
