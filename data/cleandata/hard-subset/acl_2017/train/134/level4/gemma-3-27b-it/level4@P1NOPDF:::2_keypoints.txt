- Paper investigates joint neural approaches to argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, compared to ILP baselines.
- Evaluation uses F1-score for both concepts and relations.
- Dependency-based methods perform poorly; sequence labeling approaches are effective.
- LSTM-ER yields the strongest performance, particularly at the paragraph level.
- Sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based approach.
- Extensive supplementary material details model training and hyperparameter optimization.
- Multi-task learning substantially benefits sequence labeling; claim classification contributes more than relation classification.
- Major concern: potential topic overlap between training and test sets could cause information leakage and optimistic performance estimates.
- Major concern: best model (LSTM-ER) is primarily an application of prior work, raising novelty questions despite valuable experimental insights.
- Minor issue: explanation of reconstructing the full graph from tree output (lines 222–226) is unclear.
- Minor issue: section ordering (lines 443–444) is confusing; suggest swapping sequence tagging and dependency-based descriptions for clarity.
- Minor question: phrase “decouple components while simultaneously jointly modeling them” (line 455) is contradictory and needs clarification; cited works don’t clarify.
- Minor issue: statement that the system “decouples relation information from entity information” (lines 477–479) is confusing; suggest clearer framing (e.g., tree edges vs. edge labels).
- Question: are F1 scores comparable between paragraph and essay settings, and do paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations due to limited context?
