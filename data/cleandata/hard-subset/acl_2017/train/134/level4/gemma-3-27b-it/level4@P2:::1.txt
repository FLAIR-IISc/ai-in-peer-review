This paper investigates neural network approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as both token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging, with explorations into multi-task learning. The motivation is strong: existing AM systems rely on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP), making them brittle and data-dependent. The authors aim to overcome these limitations with a jointly learned, feature-agnostic neural approach.

The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. Specifically, the paper demonstrates that while framing AM as a dependency parsing problem is ineffective, a sequence tagging approach—particularly using BiLSTMs—yields robust performance. Furthermore, the authors show that incorporating auxiliary tasks through multi-task learning improves performance. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the demonstration that neural methods can achieve competitive performance compared to a state-of-the-art ILP baseline, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough investigation of several neural network architectures for AM. Comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the sequence tagging approach.
2. **Elimination of Feature Engineering:** A key advantage of the proposed method is its ability to perform AM without relying on hand-crafted features. This simplifies the development process and makes the system more adaptable to new datasets. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of this approach, given the inherent subjectivity and domain-dependence of argumentation.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The models achieve competitive performance compared to a strong, feature-based ILP baseline. This result demonstrates the viability of neural networks for end-to-end AM, and establishes a strong foundation for future research in this area.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The authors provide thoughtful analysis of the results, particularly regarding the limitations of the dependency parsing approach and the benefits of multi-task learning. The discussion of distance encoding in sequence tagging is also insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size & Potential Overfitting:** The limited size of the dataset (approximately 320 essays for training) raises concerns about overfitting, especially given the high capacity of the BiLSTM networks. While the authors acknowledge this concern, the paper lacks sufficient analysis to definitively address it. Reporting training and development loss curves would be highly beneficial.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The results are presented without statistical significance testing. This makes it difficult to determine whether the observed performance differences are statistically meaningful or due to chance. Reporting confidence intervals or p-values would significantly strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Regularization:** The paper provides limited details regarding regularization techniques employed to mitigate overfitting. Expanding on this aspect, particularly in the main paper (rather than solely in the supplementary material), would enhance the reproducibility and robustness of the findings.
4. **Model Stability Concerns:** The observed variance in performance across different runs, particularly for the dependency parser (e.g., a range of [0.56, 0.61] for F1 scores), suggests potential instability in the models. Further investigation into this variance and reporting mean and variance values would be crucial.
5. **Missing Inter-Annotator Agreement:** The paper does not report inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, which would provide valuable context for interpreting the results and understanding the inherent difficulty of the task.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide training and development loss curves for the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models to demonstrate the learning process and assess potential overfitting?
2.  Would you be able to perform statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) to confirm the statistical significance of the observed performance differences?
3.  Could you elaborate on the regularization techniques used in each model and provide a justification for the choices made?
4.  Please report the mean and variance of the performance metrics across multiple runs with different random initializations, particularly for models exhibiting high variance like the dependency parser.
5.  What is the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The suggestion to include inter-annotator agreement scores is crucial for understanding the dataset quality.
*   Moving the error analysis and details on pre-trained word embeddings from the supplementary material to the main paper would enhance the readability and completeness of the work.
*   Consider using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning.
*   Clarifying figure 1 with more distinct colors would improve its accessibility.
