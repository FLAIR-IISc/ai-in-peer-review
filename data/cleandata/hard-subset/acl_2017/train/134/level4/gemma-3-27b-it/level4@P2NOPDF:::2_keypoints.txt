- Paper presents a comprehensive comparison of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing (multiple parsers), sequence labeling, sequence-labeling-based multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER, evaluated against a strong ILP baseline using F1 for concepts and relations.  
- Key results: sequence labeling outperforms dependency-based methods; LSTM-ER achieves strong performance (especially at paragraph level); multi-task learning significantly boosts sequence labeling, with claim identification more helpful than relation identification.  
- Supplementary material provides detailed model training and hyperparameter optimization information.  
- Major concern: possible topic overlap between training and test sets risks information leakage (writers may reuse arguments), which could inflate performance estimates and requires discussion.  
- Major concern: the top-performing LSTM-ER is essentially an application of an existing model, limiting novelty, though the breadth of experiments and insights still warrant publication.  
- Minor: Lines 222–226 — unclear claim that a full argumentation graph can be reconstructed from a tree; needs clarification.  
- Minor: Lines 443–444 — current presentation order (sequence tagging, dependency parsing, then multi-task using sequence tagging) is counterintuitive; consider reversing the first two for readability.  
- Minor: Line 455 — wording is contradictory: saying the model “de-couples” information while “jointly modeling” it needs clarification; cited work (Miwa and Bansal) doesn’t resolve this.  
- Minor: Lines 477–479 — description of decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing; likely intended to mean separate tasks for edges vs. edge labels, and should be rewritten.  
- Minor/question: Are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores directly comparable for relation tasks, given paragraph models may inherently miss cross-paragraph relations by design?
