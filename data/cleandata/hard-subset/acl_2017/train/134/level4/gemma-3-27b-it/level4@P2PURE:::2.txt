The work presents a comprehensive neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several methods: 1) formulating the task as a dependency parsing problem using various parsers, 2) employing sequence labeling, 3) utilizing multi-task learning based on a sequence labeling model, 4) applying an existing neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER), and 5) comparing against state-of-the-art ILP-based models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores for both concepts and relations. Dependency parsing-based methods proved less effective, while sequence labeling approaches demonstrated strong performance. The LSTM-ER model performed particularly well, especially at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperformed the ILP baselines. A detailed supplementary material is provided, covering model training and hyperparameter optimization. The authors also demonstrated that sequence labeling models benefit substantially from the multi-task learning approach, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task. Overall, this is a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

- Major remarks
  - A potential concern is the dataset; the overlap in essay topics between the training and test sets could lead to information leakage, where writers employ similar arguments in both sets, potentially inflating performance estimates. While this issue also affects the ILP models, avoiding an unfair advantage, it warrants discussion.
  - Another consideration is that the best-performing model, LSTM-ER, is largely an application of an existing model from related work. However, given the success of sequence-based models and the extensive experiments and insights presented, I believe this work is suitable for publication.

- Minor remarks and questions:
  - Lines 222-226: The claim that the full graph can be reconstructed from a tree output requires further clarification.
  - Lines 443-444: The section order (sequence tagging, dependency-based, multi-task learning using sequence tagging) is somewhat disruptive. Reversing the order of the first two would improve readability, as the meaning of “STag_T” may be forgotten by this point.
  - Line 455: The statement that the model “de-couples” information while “jointly modeling” it is paradoxical. Joint modeling inherently requires coupling; clarification is needed. (I checked Miwa and Bansal and could not locate the source of this claim.)
  - Lines 477-479: The description of decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing. It appears the authors mean that the model learns some tasks as “tree edges” and others as “edge labels,” thereby decoupling them. This section would benefit from clearer phrasing.
  - Are the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings directly comparable, particularly for relation tasks? Paragraph-based models may inherently miss cross-paragraph relations due to their limited scope.
