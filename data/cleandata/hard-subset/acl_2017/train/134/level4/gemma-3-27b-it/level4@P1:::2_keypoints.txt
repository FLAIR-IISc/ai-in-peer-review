- Paper investigates multiple neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, ILP) and evaluates them with F1 for concepts and relations.  
- Dependency-parsing based solutions perform poorly compared to other approaches.  
- Sequence-labeling models are effective and, when combined with multi-task learning, yield significant improvements.  
- Off-the-shelf LSTM-ER performs very well, especially at the paragraph level, and rivals or outperforms other models.  
- Both sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art approach.  
- Supplementary material is comprehensive, detailing training procedures and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Multi-task learning helps sequence-labeling models, with claim detection benefiting more than relation detection.  
- Major concern: possible topic overlap between training and test essays may cause information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates.  
- Concern that LSTM-ER is largely an off-the-shelf application rather than a novel model contribution.  
- Clarification needed on the claim that the full graph can be reconstructed from a tree output (lines 222–226).  
- Presentation order is confusing (sequence tagging, dependency parsing, then multi-task); recommend reordering for clarity (lines 443–444).  
- Request clarification on what “decouple relation information from entity information while still jointly modeling them” means—appears contradictory (line 455 and 477–479).  
- Reviewer could not find the referenced model description in Miwa and Bansal (2016); citation/description may be missing or unclear.  
- Question about comparability of F1 scores between paragraph and essay settings and whether paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations.
