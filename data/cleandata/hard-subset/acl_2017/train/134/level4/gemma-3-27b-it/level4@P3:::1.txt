This paper proposes a system for end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks. The authors explore modeling the problem using sequence labeling and dependency parsing approaches, and investigate a multi-task learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The motivation, stemming from the limitations of prior work relying on ILP, manual feature engineering, and constraint design, is clearly articulated. A key strength is the potential to jointly learn subtasks, mitigating error propagation inherent in pipeline methods. The methods are generally well-explained, though some details were initially missing (addressed in author response).

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Neural Networks to End-to-End Argumentation Mining:** The paper demonstrates the feasibility of applying neural network architectures – specifically sequence tagging and dependency parsing – to the complex task of end-to-end argumentation mining, offering a departure from traditional feature-engineered ILP-based approaches.
2. **Comparative Analysis of Modeling Approaches:**  A valuable contribution lies in the thorough comparison of different neural network framings (sequence tagging, dependency parsing, multi-task learning) for this task, revealing that a simple sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) performs surprisingly well. The finding that dependency parsing is ineffective in this setting is also noteworthy.
3. **Demonstration of Multi-Task Learning Benefits:** The paper shows that incorporating related subtasks (component detection, relation identification) as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup improves performance, highlighting the benefit of leveraging task relationships.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the concepts accessible.
2. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup is substantial, with appropriate comparisons to a strong baseline (ILP model).  The authors clearly report results for different levels of matching (exact vs. approximate).
3. **Interesting Findings:** The observation that a simple tagging model outperforms dependency parsing for this end-to-end task is a significant and counterintuitive result.
4. **Thorough Error Analysis (Partially Addressed):** The initial supplementary material contained useful error analysis, and the authors have rightly moved key insights into the main paper, enhancing its completeness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Size and Potential Overfitting:** The most significant concern is the limited size of the dataset (approximately 320 essays for training). This raises concerns about overfitting, particularly given the capacity of the BiLSTM-based models. While the authors acknowledge this, a more rigorous analysis of overfitting is needed. The inclusion of training/development loss curves throughout training would be helpful.
2. **Lack of Stability Analysis:** The results lack a robust analysis of model stability. Reporting mean and standard deviation of performance across multiple runs with different random initializations is crucial to demonstrate the reliability of the findings. The large variance observed in some settings (e.g., Kiperwasser parser) requires further investigation and explanation.
3. **Insufficient Regularization Details:** The paper initially lacked sufficient detail regarding regularization techniques used to mitigate overfitting. While the authors addressed this partially in response to questions, a more detailed description of the regularization strategies employed is necessary in the main paper. Bayesian optimization could be considered as a more efficient hyperparameter tuning method.
4. **Missing Inter-Annotator Agreement:** The inclusion of inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset would provide valuable context regarding the inherent difficulty of the task and the potential for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Overfitting Analysis:** Please provide training and development loss curves for your best-performing models, demonstrating the learning process and identifying potential overfitting.
2. **Stability Analysis:**  Please report the mean and standard deviation of performance metrics (C-F1, R-F1, F1) across multiple runs with different random initializations.
3. **Regularization Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the regularization techniques used, including specific hyperparameters (e.g., dropout rate, L2 regularization strength).
4. **Error Case Analysis:** Can you elaborate on specific error cases made by the best-performing models? Are there systematic patterns in the errors that suggest areas for future improvement?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The suggestion to improve Figure 1 for black and white printing is noted.
*   The inclusion of F1-score ranges, as suggested, is helpful for reproducibility.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of evaluating on a single dataset and the potential for domain adaptation challenges.
