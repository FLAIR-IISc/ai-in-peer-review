This paper proposes a system for end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks. The authors model the problem using two primary approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. The paper also includes results from experimenting with a multi-task learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The motivation behind the proposed model is clearly explained. Existing methods rely on integer linear programming (ILP), manual feature engineering, and manual design of ILP constraints; the proposed model avoids such manual effort. Moreover, the model jointly learns the subtasks in argumentation mining, avoiding the error propagation inherent in pipeline methods. The methods are explained clearly, with only a few minor details missing (mentioned below).

The experiments are substantial, the comparisons are performed appropriately, and the results are interesting. My main concern with this paper revolves around the relatively small dataset size and the high capacity of the employed (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset includes only approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not mentioned in the paper or supplementary materials, which is concerning as it leaves a limited number of essays for training—a potentially crucial issue. The total number of tags in the training data is likely only a few thousand. This contrasts with standard sequence labeling tasks, which often utilize hundreds of thousands, or even millions, of tags. Consequently, I question whether the model parameters are being adequately trained. The paper does not analyze potential overfitting. It would be beneficial to see the training and development loss values during training (after each parameter update or epoch). The authors acknowledge a potential issue with overfitting (Line 622: “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting”), but this needs further investigation.

For the same reason, I am unsure if the models are stable. Reporting the mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (with different parameter initializations) would be valuable. Statistical significance tests would also provide more information regarding model stability and the reliability of the results. Without these tests, it is difficult to determine if the observed performance improvements stem from the superiority of the proposed method or from chance.

While the neural networks likely employ regularization techniques, the small dataset size necessitates particular attention to regularization. The paper does not mention regularization at all, and the supplementary material only briefly discusses it for LSTM-ER. This needs to be addressed more comprehensively.

Consider employing Bayesian optimization methods instead of the current hyperparameter optimization approach (described in the supplementary materials).

Furthermore, move the information regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main paper. An additional page should suffice.

Finally, please include inter-annotator agreement scores. The paper describing the dataset provides relevant information that would provide insight into the system performance and the potential for improvement. Also, consider enhancing Figure 1 with different colors to improve clarity for black and white prints.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating the feasibility of neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining, achieving competitive results with a feature-engineered ILP baseline while avoiding manual feature design and constraint engineering. A secondary contribution is the finding that a dependency parsing framing of the problem performs poorly, while a sequence tagging approach, particularly when enhanced with multi-task learning, is more effective. A third notable contribution lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation across different modeling choices (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) and configurations, providing valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of Manual Feature Engineering:** The work successfully demonstrates that neural networks can achieve competitive performance in argumentation mining without relying on hand-crafted features, a significant advantage over traditional approaches.
2. **Joint Learning:** The investigation of multi-task learning highlights the benefits of jointly modeling argumentation subtasks, improving performance by leveraging relationships between them.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing multiple neural network architectures and configurations against a strong baseline, providing a comprehensive analysis of different modeling choices.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Size:** The limited size of the dataset raises concerns about overfitting and the generalizability of the results.
2. **Lack of Stability Analysis:** The absence of reporting mean and standard deviation across multiple runs, along with statistical significance tests, hinders a clear assessment of the model’s stability and reliability.
3. **Insufficient Discussion of Regularization:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of regularization techniques used to mitigate overfitting, given the small dataset size.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning?
2.  Can you report the training and development loss curves during training to provide evidence regarding potential overfitting?
3.  Would you be able to report the mean and standard deviation of the F1 scores across multiple runs with different random initializations?
4.  What specific regularization techniques were employed, and could you provide more details regarding their implementation and parameter settings?

**Additional Comments:**

Please clarify the range of F1-scores observed across different runs, as the variance, particularly for Kiperwasser, appears relatively large. Reporting this range in the paper will aid in reproducibility.
