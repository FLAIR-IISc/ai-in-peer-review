This work presents a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore several framings, including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, and adaptation of a pre-existing neural model (LSTM-ER). All approaches are rigorously evaluated using standard F1 metrics for components and relations, with a comprehensive supplementary material detailing experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning. The key finding is that while dependency-based solutions struggle, sequence labeling and, particularly, LSTM-ER achieve strong performance, outperforming a feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The authors also demonstrate improvements through multi-task learning, with component detection proving a more effective auxiliary task than relation detection.

**Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Neural Approaches to End-to-End AM:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that neural models, specifically sequence labeling and LSTM-ER, can effectively address the complex task of end-to-end argumentation mining, achieving state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset. This is a significant step forward, reducing reliance on hand-crafted features and complex ILP constraints.
2. **Detailed Analysis of Framing Choices:** The authors provide a valuable comparative analysis of different neural framings for AM (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning), highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each. The finding that dependency parsing is ineffective, contrasting with its success in related tasks operating at the component level, is particularly insightful.
3. **Empirical Investigation of Multi-Task Learning:** The paper offers a concrete investigation into the benefits of multi-task learning for AM, identifying component detection as a helpful auxiliary task. This provides practical guidance for future research and model development.

**Strengths:**

* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough and well-documented experimental evaluation, including detailed results for multiple models and configurations. The inclusion of a comprehensive supplementary material is commendable.
* **Clear and Detailed Analysis:** The authors provide a clear and insightful analysis of their results, offering explanations for observed trends and potential limitations.
* **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The comparison against a strong ILP baseline demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed neural approaches.
* **Practical Insights:** The findings regarding multi-task learning and the relative performance of different framings provide practical insights for researchers in the field.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Potential Data Contamination:** A legitimate concern is raised regarding potential topic overlap between the training and test sets, which could lead to overly optimistic performance estimates due to information leakage. While this issue may also affect the ILP baseline, it warrants further discussion and ideally, investigation.
* **Limited Novelty of LSTM-ER:** The reliance on LSTM-ER, an existing model from related work, as one of the top-performing approaches could be viewed as a limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the substantial experimental work and insights gained justify publication.
* **Clarity of Terminology:** Some passages could benefit from increased clarity. The description of “decoupling” relation and entity information and the explanation of reconstructing the graph from a tree are somewhat unclear.
* **Paragraph vs. Essay Level Comparison:** The paper doesn't fully explore the implications of the performance difference between paragraph and essay level evaluations, particularly regarding the potential for paragraph-level models to miss cross-paragraph relations.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Data Contamination:** Could you elaborate on the extent of topic overlap between the training and test sets? Have you considered any methods to assess the impact of potential data contamination on the reported results?
2. **LSTM-ER Interpretation:** Could you clarify why LSTM-ER performs so well, particularly given that it is an application of an existing model? What specific aspects of the model architecture or training procedure contribute to its success in the context of AM?
3. **Decoupling Explanation:** Please clarify what is meant by “decoupling” relation information from entity information. Can you provide a more concrete example to illustrate this concept?
4. **Cross-Paragraph Relations:** Could you discuss the potential limitations of paragraph-level models regarding their ability to capture cross-paragraph relations? Are there any plans to address this issue in future work?
