## Review

**Strengths:**
The paper is clearly written and readily accessible. The methods employed and the reported results are of interest to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and resulting conclusions warrant careful consideration (see detailed comments below).

**General Discussion:**
This paper introduces a neural network-based system for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors investigate two distinct approaches – sequence labeling and dependency parsing – and further explore multitask learning within the sequence labeling framework. The motivation for the proposed model is well-articulated. Current state-of-the-art methods rely heavily on inductive logic programming (ILP), manual feature engineering, and the complex design of ILP constraints. This work effectively bypasses such manual effort. Moreover, the joint learning of argumentation mining subtasks avoids the error propagation issues inherent in pipeline approaches. The methodology is generally explained clearly, with only minor details requiring further elaboration (as noted below).

The experiments are comprehensive, the comparative analysis is well-executed, and the findings are intriguing. However, a key concern is the limited size of the dataset, coupled with the high capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks utilized (BLC and BLCC). The training set consists of approximately 320 essays, with a test set of only 80. The size of the development set is not reported in the paper or supplementary materials, which is problematic given the already limited data available for training – a critical issue. The total number of tags within the training data is likely to be in the thousands, a scale considerably smaller than typical sequence labeling tasks that benefit from hundreds of thousands, or even millions, of tags. Consequently, the proper training of model parameters is questionable. The paper lacks an analysis of potential overfitting, and inclusion of training and development loss values over the training process (per parameter update or epoch) would be beneficial. The authors' own statement (Line 622: “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting”) could be interpreted as indirect evidence of overfitting.

For this same reason, the robustness of the models is uncertain. Reporting the mean and standard deviation of results across multiple runs with different parameter initializations is crucial. Statistical significance testing would further enhance confidence in the model’s stability and the reliability of the results. Without such tests, it is difficult to determine whether observed performance improvements stem from the superiority of the proposed method or simply from chance. 

While the employed neural networks incorporate regularization techniques, the small dataset size necessitates a more focused attention to regularization strategies. The paper currently lacks any discussion of regularization, with only a brief mention in the supplementary material regarding LSTM-ER. This aspect requires more thorough treatment.

Consider employing Bayesian optimization methods instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization approach (described in the supplementary materials). Additionally, integrate the information regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis currently found in the supplementary material into the main paper; an additional page should suffice. 

Including inter-annotator agreement scores, as reported in the original dataset publication, would offer valuable insight into system performance and potential areas for improvement. Finally, please revise Figure 1 with different colors to improve its quality for grayscale printing.
