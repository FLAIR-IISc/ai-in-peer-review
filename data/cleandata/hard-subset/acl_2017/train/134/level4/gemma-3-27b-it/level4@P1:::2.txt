The work describes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining. Several approaches are explored, including: 1) casting the problem as a dependency parsing problem (trying several different parsers); 2) casting the problem as a sequence labeling problem; 3) multi-task learning (based on a sequence labeling model); 4) an off-the-shelf neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER); and 5) Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based state-of-the-art models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores for concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling solutions are effective.

The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. A comprehensive supplementary material is provided, detailing the technical aspects of model training and hyperparameter optimization. It was also shown that sequence labeling models can be significantly improved by the multi-task approach, with the claim detection task being more helpful than the relation detection task. 

This paper presents a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Major Remarks**

My primary concern is the dataset. I wonder if the fact that essays in the training and test sets might cover the same topics is a problem. Writers might employ the same or similar arguments in both, leading to potential information leakage from the training to the test set and potentially overly optimistic performance estimates. However, this issue likely affects the ILP models as well, so your model doesn’t necessarily have an unfair advantage. Still, this is a point worth discussing.

My other concern is that one of your best-performing models, LSTM-ER, is largely an off-the-shelf application of a model from related work. However, given the relative success of sequence-based models and the extensive experiments and useful lessons learned, I believe this work merits publication.
