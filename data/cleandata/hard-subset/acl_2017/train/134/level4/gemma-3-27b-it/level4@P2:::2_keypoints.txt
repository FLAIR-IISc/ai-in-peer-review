- Investigates neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, and ILP baseline comparison.  
- Key finding: dependency parsing performs poorly while sequence labeling is effective; LSTM-ER achieves strong results, especially at paragraph level.  
- Multi-task learning improves sequence labeling, with claim detection helping more than relation detection.  
- Shows neural models can match or outperform feature-engineered ILP baselines, reducing need for manual feature design.  
- Systematic comparison of framings offers useful insights into strengths and weaknesses of each approach.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation with paragraph- and essay-level results and detailed hyperparameter/supplemental material.  
- Strong baselines and comprehensive supplemental material aid reproducibility.  
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly presents approaches and results.  
- Potential data leakage concern: topic overlap between training and test essays may inflate performance estimates.  
- LSTM-ER's contribution is mainly an application of an existing model, so novelty is incremental.  
- Terminology around "decoupling" vs. "joint modeling" (LSTM-ER) is unclear and needs clarification.  
- Unclear whether paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores are directly comparable, especially for relation detection (cross-paragraph relations may be missed).  
- Requests for authors: discuss topic-overlap impact, clarify LSTM-ER decoupling mechanism, compare paragraph/essay error types, and elaborate on reconstructing full graph from a tree output.  
- Minor editorial suggestions: reorder discussion (sequence tagging before dependency parsing) and rephrase ambiguous "decoupling/jointly modeling" language.
