## Review

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methods and results are interesting and promising.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and reported results raise some concerns (detailed below).

**General Discussion:**
This paper presents a system for end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks. The authors explore two approaches – sequence labeling and dependency parsing – and also investigate a multitask learning setting within the sequence labeling framework. The motivation behind the proposed model is clearly articulated. Existing approaches often rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and carefully designed ILP constraints. In contrast, this model avoids such manual effort and jointly learns the subtasks involved in argumentation mining, thereby circumventing the error propagation issues common in pipeline methods. The methods are explained clearly, with only a few minor details missing (outlined below).

The experiments are comprehensive, the comparisons are well-conducted, and the results are intriguing. My primary concern centers on the relatively small size of the dataset and the high capacity of the employed (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The dataset consists of only approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The size of the development set is not specified in the paper or supplementary materials, which is problematic given the limited training data. This small dataset size – likely containing only a few thousand tags – is a critical issue, particularly when compared to standard sequence labeling tasks which typically utilize datasets with hundreds of thousands, or even millions, of tags. Consequently, I question whether the model parameters are being adequately trained. The paper also lacks an analysis of potential overfitting. Including training and development loss curves throughout training (e.g., after each parameter update or epoch) would be informative. The authors themselves offer evidence suggestive of overfitting: in line 622, they state, “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting.”

For similar reasons, I am uncertain whether the models are sufficiently stable. Reporting the mean and standard deviation of results across multiple runs (with different parameter initializations) is essential. Furthermore, statistical significance tests would provide more confidence in the stability of the models and the reliability of the results. Without these tests, it is difficult to determine whether observed performance improvements stem from the inherent superiority of the proposed method or simply from chance.

While the neural networks undoubtedly employ regularization techniques, the small dataset size necessitates a particularly careful consideration of regularization methods. The paper currently does not mention regularization at all, and the supplementary material only briefly addresses it in the context of LSTM-ER. This aspect requires more thorough discussion in the paper.

I suggest considering Bayesian optimization methods instead of the current hyperparameter optimization approach (described in the supplementary materials). 

Additionally, please move the information regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main body of the paper. An additional page should be sufficient to accommodate this information. 

Finally, including inter-annotator agreement scores would be beneficial. Relevant information is available in the paper describing the dataset and would provide valuable insight into the systems’ performance and potential areas for improvement. Please also consider using different colors in Figure 1 to enhance its clarity for black and white printing.

**Edit:**
Thank you for addressing my questions. I have increased my recommendation score to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in your paper and report the mean and variance for different settings. I remain concerned about model stability; for example, the large variance observed in the Kiperwasser setting requires further analysis. Even an F1-score range of [0.56, 0.61] represents a substantial variation. Including these score ranges in your paper will facilitate replication of your work.
