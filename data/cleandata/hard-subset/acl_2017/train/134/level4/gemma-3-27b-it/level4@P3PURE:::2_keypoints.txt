- Paper presents a comprehensive neural investigation of end-to-end argumentation mining across five approaches (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task sequence labeling, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines).  
- All methods evaluated using F1 for concept and relation identification; dependency-based solutions perform poorly.  
- Sequence labeling approaches are effective; LSTM-ER achieves strong performance, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform prior ILP-based baselines.  
- Multi-task learning built on sequence labeling substantially improves performance, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task.  
- Detailed supplementary material is provided covering training, hyperparameters, and technical details.  
- Major concern: dataset may have topic overlap between train and test essays, risking information leakage and optimistic performance estimates.  
- The potential leakage affects ILP baselines too, but the dataset issue should be discussed.  
- Major concern: best model (LSTM-ER) is largely an off-the-shelf application of an existing model, limiting novelty.  
- Reviewer still finds value in the extensive experiments and insights, deeming the work publishable.  
- Minor: clarify lines 222–226 on reconstructing a full argumentation graph from tree output.  
- Minor: recommend reordering sections (put sequence tagging before dependency-based methods) to improve readability.  
- Minor: clarify the apparent contradiction of the model “de-coupling” information while “jointly modeling” it and provide the cited source.  
- Minor: clarify description (477–479) about decoupling relation information from entity information—explain edge vs. label interpretation.  
- Minor/question: clarify whether paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores (especially for relations) are comparable and whether paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations.
