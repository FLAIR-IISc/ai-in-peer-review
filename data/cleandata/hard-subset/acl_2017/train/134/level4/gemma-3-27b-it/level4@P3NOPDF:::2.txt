This paper presents a comprehensive investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore several methods, including framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning (built upon a sequence labeling foundation), and applying an existing neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER). They also compare these approaches to strong Information Logic Programming (ILP) baselines, evaluating all on F1-score for both concept and relation identification. The results demonstrate that sequence labeling approaches, particularly the LSTM-ER model, perform effectively, especially at the paragraph level, and outperform the ILP baselines. The paper is accompanied by a detailed supplementary material outlining model training, hyperparameter optimization, and further experimental details.

**Major Remarks:**

My primary concern lies with the dataset. The potential overlap of essay topics between the training and test sets raises the possibility of information leakage. Writers may reuse similar arguments across essays, potentially leading to overly optimistic performance estimates. While this issue likely affects the ILP baselines as well – meaning the proposed models don't necessarily have an unfair advantage – it's a critical point for discussion. Acknowledging and analyzing the extent of topic overlap would strengthen the validity of the reported results.

A secondary consideration is that the highest performing model, LSTM-ER, represents a largely out-of-the-box application of existing work. However, given the strong performance of sequence-based models in general, and the extensive experimentation and valuable insights provided by this work, I believe publication is warranted. The comprehensive comparison and analysis significantly contribute to understanding the landscape of neural argumentation mining techniques.

**Minor Remarks and Questions:**

*   **Lines 222-226:** The claim regarding reconstructing the full argument graph from a tree structure is unclear. Clarification and a more explicit explanation of this process would be beneficial.

*   **Lines 443-444:** The presentation order in this section – sequence tagging, dependency-based methods, then multi-task learning based on sequence tagging – is somewhat disjointed. Reversing the order to dependency-based methods followed by sequence tagging would improve readability, particularly as the notation "STag_T" is introduced later.

*   **Line 455:** The phrasing “de-couples them but jointly models them” is contradictory. Jointly modeling inherently requires some form of coupling. Please clarify this statement. The cited references (Miwa and Bansal) were not found to support this claim upon review.

*   **Lines 477-479:** The description of decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing. I interpret this as learning tasks corresponding to "edges of a tree" and others corresponding to "labels on those edges," effectively decoupling the *representation* of the information, but not the learning process itself. I recommend rephrasing this section for greater clarity.

*   **Paragraph vs. Essay F1 Scores:** Are the F1 scores in the paragraph and essay settings directly comparable, specifically for the relation tasks? It's possible that paragraph-based models systematically miss cross-paragraph relations, as they operate within a limited scope. Investigating this potential difference and discussing its implications would enhance the analysis.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable and thorough investigation of neural approaches to argumentation mining. While the dataset concerns require attention, the comprehensive experimentation, insightful analysis, and strong performance of the proposed models justify publication. Addressing the minor points raised above will further improve the clarity and impact of the work.
