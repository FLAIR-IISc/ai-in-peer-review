This work presents a comprehensive investigation of neural network approaches to joint argumentation mining, exploring several methodologies: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning built upon sequence labeling, a pre-existing neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER), and comparison against established ILP-based models. Evaluation is consistently performed using F1-score on both concept and relation identification. The results demonstrate that dependency parsing-based methods are less effective, while sequence labeling approaches yield strong performance. Notably, the LSTM-ER model performs particularly well, especially at the paragraph level, and both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baselines. The supplementary material is exceptionally detailed, thoroughly documenting model training, hyperparameter optimization, and experimental setup. Furthermore, the study reveals significant improvements to sequence labeling models through a multi-task learning framework, with the claim identification task proving more beneficial than the relation identification task. Overall, this is a thorough exploration of neural methods for end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Major Remarks:**

My primary concern lies with the dataset. The potential overlap in topics between the training and test essay sets raises the possibility of information leakage, where arguments used in the training data are replicated in the test data, potentially leading to inflated performance estimates. While this issue affects the ILP models as well, negating an unfair advantage, it warrants discussion.  

A second point is that the strong performance of the LSTM-ER model relies on applying an existing model to the task. However, considering the overall success of sequence-based models, the extensive experimentation, and the valuable insights gained, I believe this work is suitable for publication.

**Minor Remarks and Questions:**

Lines 222-226: The claim regarding reconstructing a full graph from a tree-structured output is unclear and could benefit from further explanation.

Lines 443-444: Reordering the presentation of results—sequence tagging first, then dependency parsing, before the multi-task learning approach built on sequence tagging—would improve readability. I had difficulty recalling the meaning of “STag_T” by this point in the paper.

Line 455: The statement that the model “de-couples” information while “jointly modeling” it is paradoxical. Clarification is needed, as joint modeling inherently implies coupling. I was unable to find support for this claim in the cited works by Miwa and Bansal.

Lines 477-479: The description of decoupling relation information from entity information is confusing. I interpret it as the model learning tasks related to graph structure (“edges”) separately from tasks defining properties of that structure (“edge labels”). Regardless, this section requires clarification.

Finally, are the F1 scores reported for paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, particularly for relation tasks? I wonder whether paragraph-based models inherently struggle to identify cross-paragraph relations, as they lack the scope to consider them.
