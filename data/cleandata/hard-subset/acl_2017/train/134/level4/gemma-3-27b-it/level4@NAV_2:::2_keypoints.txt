- Paper evaluates five neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines.
- Evaluation uses F1 scores for concepts and relations; dependency parsers perform poorly while sequence labeling and LSTM-ER perform best.
- LSTM-ER achieves especially strong results at the paragraph level and, along with sequence labeling, outperforms the ILP approach.
- Sequence labeling models benefit significantly from multi-task learning, with the claim auxiliary task providing more gain than the relation task.
- The paper includes a comprehensive supplement covering model training and hyperparameter optimization.
- Strength: systematic, comprehensive comparison of multiple neural architectures for argumentation mining.
- Strength: strong empirical results that surpass prior ILP-based state of the art.
- Strength: detailed analyses and ablation studies clarifying the impact of modeling choices.
- Strength: thorough experimental reporting and supplementary material that aid reproducibility.
- Weakness: potential data contamination from essays on similar topics appearing in both training and test sets, risking optimistic estimates.
- Weakness: limited novelty in applying the existing LSTM-ER model to this task, reducing that component’s originality.
- Weakness: some explanations and design rationales in the paper are unclear and could be clarified (specific line references given).
- Question: How might using essays on similar topics across train/test sets have biased results, and what mitigation strategies were considered?
- Question: Please clarify what is meant by LSTM-ER “decoupling” relation and entity information and how this differs from joint modeling.
- Question: Are paragraph- and essay-level F1 scores directly comparable for relations, and do paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations?
