This paper proposes a system for end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks. The authors model the problem using two approaches: (1) sequence labeling (2) dependency parsing, and also explore a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The paper clearly articulates the motivation for this work, contrasting it with existing methods reliant on ILP, manual feature engineering, and constraint design. A key benefit of the proposed model is its ability to jointly learn argumentation subtasks, avoiding error propagation inherent in pipeline methods. The methods are largely well-explained, with only minor details missing.

The experiments are substantial, comparisons are performed appropriately, and the results are interesting. However, a primary concern is the relatively small dataset size (around 320 essays for training and 80 for testing) coupled with the high capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC) employed. The size of the development set is not explicitly stated, which is concerning given the limited training data. The total number of tags is likely only a few thousand, significantly less than typical sequence labeling tasks with hundreds of thousands or millions of tags. This raises questions about proper parameter training and potential overfitting, which the paper doesn't fully address. Including training and development loss curves would provide valuable insight. The statement on line 622 – “taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting” – suggests awareness of this issue, but further investigation is needed.

Furthermore, the stability of the models requires more scrutiny. Reporting mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different parameter initializations is essential. Statistical significance tests are also necessary to assess the reliability of the results and determine if observed improvements are due to the method's superiority or chance. While regularization techniques are likely used within the neural networks, the small dataset size necessitates greater attention to regularization strategies, which are not adequately discussed in the paper or supplementary material (with the limited exception of LSTM-ER). Considering Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning is recommended.

The paper would be strengthened by including information currently in the supplementary material – specifically, details on pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis – within the main text. An additional page should suffice. Inter-annotator agreement scores, readily available from the dataset’s original publication, would provide context for the system’s performance and potential for improvement. Finally, consider enhancing Figure 1 with distinct colors for improved clarity in black and white printing.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) a thorough empirical investigation of various neural network architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining, demonstrating the viability of neural approaches compared to traditional ILP-based methods; (2) the finding that sequence tagging, particularly with BiLSTM-CRF models, provides a strong baseline for this task; and (3) an insightful analysis of the challenges associated with applying dependency parsing to argumentation mining, revealing its limitations in this context.
