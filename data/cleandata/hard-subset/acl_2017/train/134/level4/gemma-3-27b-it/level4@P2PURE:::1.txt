This paper presents a system for end-to-end argumentation mining employing neural networks, modeling the problem with both sequence labeling and dependency parsing approaches. Multitask learning is explored within the sequence labeling framework. The motivation for the proposed model is clearly articulated, contrasting it favorably with existing methods reliant on ILP, manual feature engineering, and hand-designed constraints. By jointly learning subtasks, the model avoids error propagation inherent in pipeline approaches. The methods are generally well-explained, with only minor details lacking.

The experiments are comprehensive, comparisons are appropriately conducted, and the results are initially promising. However, a primary concern is the limited dataset size – approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing – with the development set size not explicitly stated in the paper or supplementary materials. This scarcity of training data is particularly problematic given the high capacity of the BiLSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC) used. The limited number of training tags (likely only a few thousand) is significantly smaller than those available in standard sequence labeling tasks, raising questions about proper parameter training and potential overfitting. The paper lacks an analysis of overfitting, and the inclusion of training and development loss curves would be beneficial. The authors' own statement (“taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting”) could be interpreted as evidence of overfitting.

Furthermore, the model’s stability is uncertain. Reporting the mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different parameter initializations, alongside statistical significance tests, would enhance confidence in the results and differentiate genuine improvement from chance. While regularization techniques are likely employed within the neural network architectures, the small dataset size necessitates a more explicit and detailed discussion of regularization strategies, beyond the brief mention of LSTM-ER in the supplementary material.

Consider employing Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning. Move the details regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary materials into the main paper; an additional page should suffice. Inclusion of inter-annotator agreement scores, referencing the dataset's original publication, would provide valuable context for evaluating system performance and identifying areas for improvement. Finally, improve the visibility of Figure 1 for black and white printing by utilizing distinct colors.

Following clarification from the authors, the recommendation score has been increased to 4. Please include F1-score ranges and report the mean and variance for different settings. Concerns regarding model stability remain, particularly given the large variance observed in the Kiperwasser setting (F1 range of [0.56, 0.61]). Providing these score ranges will significantly improve the reproducibility of the work.
