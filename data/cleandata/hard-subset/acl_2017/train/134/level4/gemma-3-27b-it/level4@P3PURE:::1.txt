## Review for ACL 2017

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and easy to understand, presenting interesting methods and results.

**Weaknesses:**
The evaluation and obtained results raise concerns (see comments below).

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system utilizing neural networks. The authors explore two modeling approaches: (1) sequence labeling and (2) dependency parsing. They also investigate a multitask learning setting for the sequence labeling approach. The motivation behind the proposed model is clearly articulated. Existing methods rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and the manual design of ILP constraints. In contrast, this work avoids such manual effort and jointly learns the subtasks involved in argumentation mining, thereby mitigating error propagation issues common in pipeline methods. The methods are explained clearly, with only minor details missing (outlined below). 

The experiments are substantial, comparisons are performed rigorously, and the results are intriguing. However, my primary concern lies with the small size of the dataset—approximately 320 essays for training and 80 for testing—and the high capacity of the employed (Bi)LSTM-based recurrent neural networks (BLC and BLCC). The size of the development set is not specified in the paper or supplementary materials, which is problematic given the limited training data. The small number of essays available for training is a crucial issue, likely resulting in only a few thousand total tags. This is significantly smaller than the tag counts (hundreds of thousands, sometimes millions) typical of standard sequence labeling tasks. Consequently, I question whether the model parameters are being adequately trained. The paper does not address potential overfitting; including training and development loss values during training (e.g., after each parameter update or epoch) would be informative. The authors themselves provide some evidence suggestive of overfitting, stating (Line 622) that “taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting.”

For the same reason – limited data – I am unsure if the models are stable. Reporting the mean and standard deviation of results across multiple runs (with different parameter initializations) is necessary. Statistical significance tests would further clarify the stability of the models and the reliability of the results. Without such analysis, it’s difficult to determine whether observed performance gains are due to the superiority of the proposed method or simply chance.

While the neural networks employed utilize regularization techniques, the small dataset size necessitates a more focused attention to regularization methods. The paper lacks a discussion of regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions it in the context of LSTM-ER. This aspect requires more thorough treatment.

I suggest considering Bayesian optimization methods instead of the current hyper-parameter optimization approach (described in the supplementary materials). Furthermore, please move the information regarding pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis from the supplementary material into the main paper; an additional page should suffice.

Including inter-annotator agreement scores would also be beneficial. This information is available in the paper describing the dataset and would provide valuable insight into system performance and potential areas for improvement.

Finally, please consider revising Figure 1 with different colors to improve its quality for black and white printing.

**Edit:**
Thank you for addressing my questions. I have increased the recommendation score to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in your paper and report the mean and variance of different settings. I remain concerned about model stability. For example, the large variance observed in the Kiperwasser setting requires further analysis. Even an F1 range of [0.56, 0.61] is relatively large. Including these score ranges would facilitate replication of your work.
