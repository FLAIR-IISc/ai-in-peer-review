## Review for ACL 2017

This paper proposes a novel end-to-end system for argumentation mining using neural networks, offering a significant departure from traditional methods reliant on ILP, manual feature engineering, and hand-crafted constraints. The authors explore two approaches – sequence labeling and dependency parsing – and further investigate multitask learning within the sequence labeling framework. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed model's ability to jointly learn subtasks and avoid error propagation inherent in pipeline approaches is a compelling advantage. The methods are generally well-explained, with only minor details missing (addressed below). Experiments are substantial, comparisons are thorough, and the reported results are initially promising.

However, my primary concern lies with the limited size of the dataset and the substantial capacity of the employed recurrent neural networks (BiLSTMs – BLC and BLCC). The training set comprises only approximately 320 essays, with a test set of 80. Critically, the paper and supplementary materials fail to specify the size of the development set. This small dataset size raises serious questions about the robustness and generalizability of the models. The total number of tags within the training data is likely only in the low thousands, a stark contrast to standard sequence labeling tasks benefiting from datasets with hundreds of thousands, or even millions, of tagged instances. This limited training data casts doubt on whether model parameters are being effectively and reliably trained.

The paper lacks an analysis of potential overfitting. Inclusion of training and development loss curves (e.g., after each epoch or parameter update) would provide valuable insight into the learning process and potential overfitting issues. The authors’ own statement (line 622: “Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting”) can be interpreted as acknowledging the risk of overfitting given the data scarcity. 

Relatedly, the stability of the models is questionable. To address this, the paper *must* include the mean and standard deviation of results across multiple runs with different parameter initializations. Statistical significance tests are also necessary to determine whether observed performance differences are attributable to genuine method superiority or simply chance. Without these, the reported improvements remain unconvincing.

While the paper mentions the use of regularization techniques inherent in the neural network architectures, the dataset's small size necessitates a more focused discussion of regularization strategies. The current paper lacks any explicit mention of regularization, and the supplementary material only briefly addresses it in the context of LSTM-ER. This aspect requires substantial improvement.

I recommend exploring more sophisticated hyperparameter optimization techniques, such as Bayesian optimization, instead of the current approach described in the supplementary materials. Furthermore, important information currently relegated to the supplementary materials – specifically, details about pre-trained word embeddings and the error analysis – should be incorporated directly into the main paper; an additional page should be sufficient.

Finally, please include inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset. This information is available in the paper describing the dataset and will provide essential context regarding the inherent difficulty of the task and potential for further performance gains. A suggestion for visual clarity: consider using different colors in Figure 1 to enhance its quality for black and white printing.

**Post-Rebuttal Update:**

I appreciate the authors' responses to my initial questions. I have adjusted my recommendation score to 4, reflecting the responsiveness and clarification provided. However, I reiterate the importance of including F1-score ranges in the paper to aid reproducibility. Reporting the mean and variance across different settings is also crucial. Despite the clarification, my concern regarding model stability remains. The substantial variance observed in the Kiperwasser setting, specifically the F1-score range of [0.56, 0.61], requires further investigation and discussion. Including these score ranges will significantly improve the transparency and replicability of the reported results.
