- Paper investigates neural joint argumentation mining using dependency parsing, sequence labeling, sequence-labeling-based multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, and ILP baselines, evaluated with F1 on concepts and relations.  
- Dependency parsing approaches perform poorly compared to sequence labeling methods.  
- Sequence labeling yields strong performance and is further improved by multi-task learning.  
- LSTM-ER achieves particularly strong results, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform established ILP baselines.  
- Multi-task learning benefits sequence labeling models, with claim identification contributing more than relation identification.  
- Supplementary material is exceptionally detailed on training, hyperparameters, and experimental setup.  
- Overall study is thorough and provides valuable insights; deemed suitable for publication despite relying on an existing model for LSTM-ER.  
- Major concern: potential topic overlap between training and test essays may cause information leakage and inflate performance estimates; this needs discussion.  
- Clarify lines 222–226: how a full graph can be reconstructed from a tree-structured output.  
- Suggest reordering results (sequence tagging first, then dependency parsing, then multi-task) because terms like “STag_T” are hard to recall (lines 443–444).  
- Clarify line 455: resolve the paradox of the model “de-coupling” information while “jointly modeling” it and provide supporting citations.  
- Clarify lines 477–479: what is meant by decoupling relation information from entity information (edge structure vs. edge labels).  
- Question whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores are directly comparable for relation tasks, since paragraph models may miss cross-paragraph relations.
