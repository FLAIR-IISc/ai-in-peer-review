The work describes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining. There are several approaches explored including: 1) casting the problem as a dependency parsing problem (trying several different parsers), 2) casting the problem as a sequence labeling problem, 3) multi-task learning (based on a sequence labeling model), 4) an out-of-the-box neural model for labeling entities and relations (LSTM-ER), and 5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores for concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The out-of-the-box LSTM-ER model performs very well, especially on the paragraph level. The sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models both outperform the ILP approach. A comprehensive supplement details the technical aspects of model training and hyperparameter optimization. It was also shown that sequence labeling models benefit significantly from the multi-task approach, with the claim task being more helpful than the relation task. The paper is a thorough investigation of neural-based approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Strengths:**
1. **Comprehensive Exploration of Neural Approaches:** The paper systematically investigates multiple neural network architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining, offering a broad and insightful comparison. This includes exploring dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adapting an existing relation extraction model (LSTM-ER).
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The presented results demonstrate that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance on this task, surpassing the previously established ILP-based approach. The performance gains, particularly with the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models, are significant.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Ablation Studies:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of the different modeling choices, including the impact of multi-task learning and the relative contribution of different auxiliary tasks. This level of analysis facilitates a deeper understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup and Reporting:** The extensive supplementary material detailing the experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and implementation details enhances the reproducibility and credibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Potential Data Contamination:** A potential concern is the possibility of data contamination due to the use of essays on the same topics in both the training and test sets. This could lead to overly optimistic performance estimates due to information leakage. While this issue likely affects the comparison ILP models as well, mitigating this concern through discussion would strengthen the work.
2. **Limited Novelty of LSTM-ER:** While LSTM-ER achieves strong performance, its primary contribution lies in the application of an existing model to a new task. This reduces the novelty of this specific component of the research. However, the comprehensive evaluation, insightful analysis, and lessons learned from the other experiments justify publication.
3. **Clarity of Explanations:** Certain sections of the paper (e.g., lines 222-226, 443-444, 455-479) could benefit from more precise and clear explanations. The reasoning behind certain design choices and the relationships between different concepts are not always immediately apparent.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. Could you discuss the potential impact of using essays on similar topics in the training and test sets on your reported performance? Have you considered any strategies to mitigate this potential bias?
2. Regarding the statement that LSTM-ER "decouples" relation information from entity information, could you clarify this point? Specifically, how does this decoupling differ from simply jointly modeling these tasks?
3. Are the F1 scores obtained on the paragraph and essay levels directly comparable, particularly for the relation tasks? Do paragraph-based models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations?
