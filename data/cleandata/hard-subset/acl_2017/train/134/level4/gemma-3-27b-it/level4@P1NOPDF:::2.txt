The work describes a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, exploring several avenues: casting the problem as a dependency parsing task (using various parsers), as a sequence labeling task, through multi-task learning (built upon a sequence labeling model), with an off-the-shelf neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER), and by comparison to state-of-the-art ILP-based models. All approaches are evaluated using F1-score on both concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling approaches prove effective.

The LSTM-ER model demonstrates particularly strong performance, especially at the paragraph level. Both the sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models outperform the ILP approach. A very comprehensive supplementary material is provided, detailing the technical aspects of model training and hyperparameter optimization. The authors also show that sequence labeling models benefit significantly from the multi-task approach, with the claim classification task contributing more than the relation classification task. Overall, this paper presents a thorough investigation of neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining.

**Major Remarks**

My primary concern relates to the dataset. I wonder if the overlap in topics between the training and test sets could lead to information leakage – writers potentially reusing similar arguments in both sets. This might result in overly optimistic performance estimates. While this issue also affects the ILP models, meaning your model doesn't have an unfair advantage, it's a point worth discussing.

Another concern is that your best-performing model, LSTM-ER, is essentially an application of an existing model from related work. However, given the success of sequence-based models, and the extensive experiments and valuable lessons learned, I believe this work warrants publication.
