

Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the limitations of previous pipeline methods that require hand-crafted features and suffer from error propagation. The authors present a thorough empirical comparison of four neural frameworks: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and an off-the-shelf entity-relation model (LSTM-ER). The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) demonstrating that sequence tagging models (particularly STagBLCC) significantly outperform existing state-of-the-art ILP-based approaches, providing the first neural end-to-end AM solution; (2) showing that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task substantially improves performance; and (3) establishing that the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is robust across different environments, while dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM.

Strengths
1. The paper provides the first comprehensive empirical comparison of neural approaches for end-to-end AM, filling an important gap in the literature. The authors thoroughly evaluate multiple neural frameworks (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER) on the same dataset, demonstrating that sequence tagging models significantly outperform the state-of-the-art ILP approach (STagBLCC achieves 55.22% F1 vs. ILP's 55.23% on paragraph level, but with substantially less engineering effort).

2. The multi-task learning approach represents a significant contribution. The authors demonstrate that adding component detection as an auxiliary task improves performance by several percentage points (e.g., Y-3:YC-3:YR-3 achieves 37.26% vs. Y-3's 34.35% F1), with component detection proving more beneficial than relation detection. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks beyond AM.

3. The paper provides valuable insights about model choice based on document length. The authors establish that sequence tagging models perform better on longer documents (essays), while LSTM-ER excels on shorter documents (paragraphs), with the latter achieving 73.02% C-F1 (50%) on paragraph level versus 66.21% on essay level. This practical guidance will help future researchers choose appropriate architectures.

4. The thorough experimental design and comprehensive evaluation across multiple metrics (C-F1, R-F1, both exact and approximate matching) provide strong evidence for the claims. The authors also include extensive ablation studies and detailed error analysis in the supplementary material.

5. The paper effectively demonstrates that neural approaches eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and complex ILP constraint design required by previous methods, while achieving comparable or better performance.

Weaknesses
1. The data leakage concern is significant: the training and test sets may contain essays on the same topics, potentially leading to information leakage. The authors acknowledge this issue but do not provide evidence that their model's performance is not inflated by this leakage. Given that the ILP baseline would face the same issue, this is not an unfair advantage, but it warrants discussion as it impacts the reliability of the results.

2. The LSTM-ER model, while effective, is an off-the-shelf application rather than a novel contribution. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently differentiate their contribution from the original LSTM-ER work. While the adaptation to AM is valuable, the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how the AM task constraints (e.g., specific entity types and relation types) influenced their implementation.

3. The explanation of how STagT decouples component and relation information is confusing. The paper states that "STagT also does not need to produce trees, but it more severely restricts search space than does LSTMER" but fails to clearly explain how this decoupling works. This is particularly important since the authors claim this is a key advantage.

4. The paragraph-level versus essay-level comparison is problematic for relation identification. Paragraph-level models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations, which are common in the dataset (92% of relations span multiple sentences). The paper does not adequately address whether the performance gap between paragraph and essay levels is due to model limitations or inherent data structure differences.

5. The dependency parsing approach is presented as a negative finding rather than a positive contribution. While the authors correctly identify that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (unlike component-level approaches), this finding could be framed as a valuable insight about the limitations of certain neural architectures for this specific task.

Questions to Authors
1. The authors note that 92% of relations span multiple sentences. How do they ensure their paragraph-level models do not miss these cross-sentence relations, and how does this affect the validity of the paragraph-level results?

2. The paper states that "the LSTM-ER model... is not guaranteed to produce trees," yet AM data has a tree-like structure. How does the authors' implementation of LSTM-ER handle this structural constraint, and why does it still perform well despite this mismatch?

3. The authors report that the multi-task learning framework performs best when component detection feeds from the same layer as the main task. This contradicts the findings of Søgaard and Goldberg (2016). Could the authors elaborate on why this architectural choice works better for AM than for other tasks?

4. The paper claims that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective—in contrast to what has been proposed for systems that operate on the coarser component level." Could the authors clarify why the component-level dependency parsing (as in Peldszus and Stede, 2015) would work better than token-level parsing for AM?
