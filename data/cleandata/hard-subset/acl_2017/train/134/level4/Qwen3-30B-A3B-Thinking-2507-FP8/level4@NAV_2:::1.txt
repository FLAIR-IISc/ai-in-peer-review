

Strengths:
The paper presents a clear and well-structured investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining. The authors effectively contrast their methods with existing pipeline approaches and demonstrate compelling results through thorough experimental evaluation.

Strengths:
The paper makes a significant contribution by presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining, showing that neural approaches outperform state-of-the-art feature-based ILP models. The authors demonstrate that simple sequence tagging models with BiLSTMs outperform more complex dependency parsing approaches for this specific task, which is an important empirical finding. The multi-task learning setup they propose provides a meaningful improvement over single-task learning, demonstrating that auxiliary tasks can enhance performance in argumentation mining.

Weaknesses:
The primary concern relates to the limited dataset size, which may compromise the reliability of the results. The training set consists of only approximately 320 essays, with 80 reserved for testing, and the development set size is not reported. This small scale (resulting in potentially only a few thousand training tags) raises serious concerns about model overfitting and stability, particularly given the large capacity of the BiLSTM-based models used. The paper lacks an analysis of training and development loss curves, which would provide crucial evidence of overfitting. Additionally, the authors do not report the mean and standard deviation across multiple model runs with different initializations, nor do they provide statistical significance tests to confirm that performance differences are meaningful rather than due to chance. The insufficient discussion of regularization techniques, despite the small dataset size, is another critical concern that needs to be addressed.

Weaknesses:
The paper does not adequately address the model stability concerns raised by the small dataset size. The large variance in results for some models (e.g., Kiperwasser setting with F1 range [0.56, 0.61]) requires proper analysis. The authors should report F1-score ranges across multiple runs and include statistical significance tests to validate their findings. The current hyperparameter optimization method (described only in supplementary materials) should be reconsidered using more robust techniques like Bayesian optimization. The paper should also move key information about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from supplementary materials to the main text to improve accessibility.

Weaknesses:
The manuscript would benefit from including inter-annotator agreement scores to provide context for the achievable performance levels. The figure illustrating argument structures should be improved for black-and-white printing by using different colors or patterns. The authors should clarify their regularization approach and discuss how it addresses the small dataset challenges.

Questions to Authors:
Please provide the development set size and report the mean and standard deviation of F1 scores across multiple model runs. Include statistical significance tests to support your performance claims. Report training and development loss curves to demonstrate model convergence and address overfitting concerns. Clarify your regularization approach and explain how it mitigates overfitting with the limited dataset. Provide inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset.
