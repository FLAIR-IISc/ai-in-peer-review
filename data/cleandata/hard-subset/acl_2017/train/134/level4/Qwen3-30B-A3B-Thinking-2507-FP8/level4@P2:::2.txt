

Summary: This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task through four distinct neural architectures: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and an adaptation of the LSTM-ER model. The authors demonstrate that sequence tagging models (particularly with multi-task learning) significantly outperform dependency parsing and the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach. The main contributions of this work are: (1) the first neural end-to-end framework for argumentation mining, (2) the demonstration that sequence tagging models effectively capture long-range dependencies in AM, and (3) the finding that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task substantially improves performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a thorough and systematic comparison of multiple neural approaches to AM, providing valuable insights about which modeling choices work best for this specific task. The authors' finding that dependency parsing is ineffective for AM at the token level (contrary to expectations) is particularly significant and well-documented.

2. The paper demonstrates that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP-based method, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and complex constraint design. This is an important contribution to the field, as it shows neural methods can handle the complexity of AM without the engineering burden of previous approaches.

3. The multi-task learning experiments reveal valuable insights about model design. The authors show that including component detection as an auxiliary task substantially improves performance, while relation detection as an auxiliary task provides less benefit. This finding has implications for other sequence labeling tasks with similar hierarchical structures.

4. The supplementary materials are exceptionally thorough, detailing training procedures, hyperparameters, and implementation details. This level of transparency greatly enhances the reproducibility of the work.

5. The paper provides clear evidence that sequence tagging models can effectively handle long-range dependencies in AM (as demonstrated by the high performance of STagBLCC and STagBL), which is particularly valuable given the often long distances between related argument components.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not adequately address the potential for topic overlap between train and test sets, which could lead to information leakage. Since the dataset consists of student essays on specific controversial topics, it's possible that similar topics appear in both train and test sets, allowing models to exploit topic-based patterns rather than learning genuine argumentation structures. This issue affects all models, but the paper should discuss its potential impact on the reported results.

2. The LSTM-ER model (which achieves the best results) is largely an out-of-the-box application of an existing approach to the AM task, rather than a novel contribution. While the adaptation is valuable, the paper could better articulate what specific insights were gained from applying this model to AM rather than simply reporting its performance.

3. Several explanations of the models' design choices are unclear, particularly regarding how the sequence tagging model "decouples" entity and relation information. The paper states that "the LSTM-ER model is more modular than STagT because it decouples relation information from entity information," but does not sufficiently clarify what this means or how it differs from the sequence tagging approach.

4. The comparison between paragraph-level and essay-level results is confusing. The paper shows that all models perform better on paragraph-level predictions, but it's unclear whether this is a fair comparison given that paragraph-level predictions cannot capture cross-paragraph relations. The authors should clarify whether the paragraph-level evaluation is an intermediate step or whether it represents a different task formulation.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify how paragraph-level evaluation relates to the end-to-end task? Specifically, does the paragraph-level evaluation represent a different task formulation (where the model is expected to predict within paragraphs rather than across the entire essay), or is it simply an implementation detail for computational efficiency?

2. The paper states that "LSTM-ER is more modular than STagT because it decouples relation information from entity information." Could you provide a clearer explanation of what this decoupling entails, and how it differs from the multi-task learning approach used with STagT?

3. The authors note that the LSTM-ER model outperforms STagBLCC on paragraph-level data but performs worse on essay-level data. Could you elaborate on why the model's performance degrades more significantly on essay-level data than the sequence tagging models? Is this related to the model's handling of long-range dependencies or the increased number of entities in longer texts?
