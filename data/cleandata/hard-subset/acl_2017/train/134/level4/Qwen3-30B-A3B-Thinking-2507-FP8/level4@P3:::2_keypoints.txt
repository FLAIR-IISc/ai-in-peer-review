- Sequence tagging (STagBLCC) provides the first neural end-to-end AM solution and matches/exceeds ILP-based state-of-the-art with less engineering effort.
- Multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task yields substantial performance gains over single-task models.
- Sequence tagging with distance encoding is robust across settings, while token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM.

- First comprehensive empirical comparison of multiple neural frameworks (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) on the same AM dataset.
- Demonstrates MTL benefit quantitatively (component detection more helpful than relation detection) with broader implications for sequence labeling.
- Provides practical guidance on model choice by document length: sequence tagging better for essays, LSTM-ER better for paragraphs.
- Strong experimental design with multiple metrics (C-F1, R-F1, exact/approx matching), ablations, and detailed error analysis.
- Shows neural approaches eliminate heavy manual feature/ILP engineering while achieving comparable or better results.

- Significant data leakage concern: training/test may share essay topics, possibly inflating performance and reducing result reliability.
- LSTM-ER is an off-the-shelf model adapted to AM rather than a novel contribution, and the paper under-explains task-specific implementation choices.
- Explanation of how STagT decouples component and relation information is confusing and under-specified.
- Paragraph vs. essay level comparison is problematic for relation ID because many relations span multiple sentences; paper doesn't disentangle model limits from data structure.
- Dependency parsing is presented mainly as a negative result rather than framed as an informative insight into architecture limitations.

- How do paragraph-level models handle the fact that 92% of relations span multiple sentences, and does this invalidate paragraph-level results?
- Given AM’s tree-like structure, how is LSTM-ER implemented to respect or reconcile that structure despite not guaranteeing trees?
- Why does MTL perform best when component detection shares the same layer as the main task, contrary to Søgaard & Goldberg (2016)?
- Why would component-level dependency parsing work better than token-level dependency parsing for AM, and can the authors clarify the underlying reasons?
