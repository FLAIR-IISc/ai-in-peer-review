

This paper presents a thorough empirical investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining, systematically comparing five distinct methodologies: (1) dependency parsing formulations (tested with multiple parsers), (2) sequence labeling, (3) multitask learning (built atop sequence labeling), (4) an off-the-shelf LSTM-based entity-relation model (LSTM-ER), and (5) ILP-based state-of-the-art approaches. All models are evaluated using F1 scores measured at both concept and relation levels. The results indicate that dependency parsing approaches yield suboptimal performance, while sequence labeling models demonstrate strong effectiveness. Notably, the LSTM-ER model achieves particularly strong results, especially at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER significantly outperform the ILP-based methods. The authors provide an exceptionally comprehensive supplement detailing model training, hyperparameter optimization, and implementation specifics, enhancing the reproducibility of their work. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that sequence labeling models benefit substantially from multitask learning, with the claim task proving more impactful than the relation task for performance gains.

**Major Concerns**  
The primary concern pertains to dataset construction. The training and test sets appear to contain essays on identical or overlapping topics, potentially allowing writers to reuse similar arguments across splits. This could introduce information leakage, inflating performance estimates. While this issue likely affects all evaluated models—including the ILP baselines—the authors should explicitly discuss this limitation and its implications for reported results.  

The second major point addresses the LSTM-ER model, which repurposes an existing off-the-shelf architecture from prior work. While this is a valid application, the paper should more clearly articulate how the model’s success informs the broader contribution beyond simply adopting a pre-existing solution. Despite this, the paper’s extensive experimental scope, rigorous comparison of neural paradigms, and valuable insights into model design (e.g., the efficacy of multitask learning) collectively warrant publication.  

**Minor Comments**  
- Lines 222–226: The claim that a full argument graph can be reconstructed from a tree output requires clarification. The authors should explicitly detail this reconstruction process.  
- Lines 443–444: The section ordering (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → multitask) is confusing. Reversing the first two subsections would improve flow, as readers may forget the meaning of "STag_T" by the time multitask learning is introduced.  
- Line 455: The phrasing "de-couples them but jointly models them" is contradictory. Clarify whether the model treats entities and relations as separate tasks (de-coupled) while still optimizing them together (jointly). The cited reference (Miwa & Bansal) does not appear to support this specific claim.  
- Lines 477–479: The assertion that the system "de-couples relation info from entity info" is unclear. The intended meaning appears to be that the model learns edge labels (relations) separately from node labels (entities), rather than jointly modeling them. This distinction should be explicitly stated.  
- Relation-level F1 scores: The paper compares paragraph-level and essay-level results, but it is unclear whether these scores are directly comparable for relation extraction. Paragraph-level models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations, which may artificially lower relation F1 in that setting. The authors should address this potential discrepancy.  

Overall, this is a well-executed and comprehensive study that advances the state of neural argumentation mining. The methodological rigor, extensive experimentation, and practical insights into model design significantly contribute to the field. With minor revisions to address the concerns above, the paper is suitable for publication at ACL 2017.
