

The paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, evaluating multiple strategies:
1) Formulating the task as dependency parsing (testing various parsers)
2) Framing the task as sequence labeling
3) Multi-task learning (built upon a sequence labeling backbone)
4) An off-the-shelf neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER)
5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models
All methods are assessed using F1 scores computed over concepts and relations. Dependency-based approaches perform poorly, while sequence labeling solutions are effective. The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves strong results, particularly at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER models surpass the ILP approach. The comprehensive supplement details all training procedures, hyperparameter optimization, and technical implementation specifics. Furthermore, sequence labeling models demonstrate significant improvement through multi-task learning, with the claim task providing greater benefit than the relation task. This constitutes a thorough investigation of neural methods for end-to-end argumentation mining.

- Major remarks  
  - A primary concern is the dataset construction: essays in the training and test sets may share topics, potentially causing writers to employ identical or similar arguments across both sets. This could lead to information leakage from train to test, yielding overly optimistic performance estimates. Although the ILP models face the same issue, thus the neural models do not gain an unfair advantage, this limitation should be addressed in the discussion.
  - A secondary concern is that the top-performing model (LSTM-ER) is an off-the-shelf application from prior work. Nevertheless, given the strong empirical results for sequence-based models, the extensive experimentation, and the valuable insights gained, the work merits publication.

- Minor remarks and questions:  
222–226: The claim that a full graph can be reconstructed from a tree output requires clarification.  
443–444: The section ordering (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → multi-task learning using sequence tagging) is confusing; reversing the first two methods would improve flow, as the reader may have forgotten the meaning of STag_T by this point.  
455: The phrase "de-couples them but jointly models them" is contradictory; joint modeling inherently requires coupling, so this requires clarification.  
477–479: The statement that the system "de-couples relation info from entity info" is unclear. It likely means the model treats some tasks as "edges of the tree" and others as "edge labels," thereby decoupling them. This point should be explicitly clarified.  
Are the F1 scores for relation tasks in paragraph and essay settings comparable? Paragraph-based models might inherently miss cross-paragraph relations, potentially biasing the comparison.
