

Strengths:  
The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The proposed methods and results are interesting and well-motivated.  

Weaknesses:  
My primary concern relates to the evaluation methodology and results, which raise questions about the robustness of the findings.  

General Discussion:  
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the task through sequence labeling and dependency parsing. It also explores multitask learning for sequence labeling. The authors clearly articulate the motivation: existing approaches rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and handcrafted ILP constraints, whereas their model eliminates these labor-intensive steps. Crucially, the model jointly learns subtasks, avoiding error propagation inherent in pipeline methods. Beyond minor missing details (addressed below), the methodology is well-explained.  

The experimental design is substantial, comparisons are appropriately conducted, and results are intriguing. However, the dataset size is critically small: only ~320 essays for training and 80 for testing. The development set size is unreported in the paper or supplementary materials, which is concerning given the limited training data. The total number of tags in training is likely only a few thousand—far below standard sequence labeling tasks (which often use hundreds of thousands or millions of tags). This raises serious doubts about whether the model parameters were adequately trained.  

The paper lacks analysis of overfitting. It would be valuable to include training and development loss curves (e.g., per epoch) to assess this. The authors note in Line 622 that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting," but this claim requires empirical support. Without evidence, this statement weakens the argument for model reliability.  

Furthermore, the model stability is questionable. The paper reports large performance variations (e.g., F1 scores ranging from 0.56 to 0.61 for the Kiperwasser setting), yet it fails to provide mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different random initializations. Statistical significance tests are also absent, making it impossible to determine whether observed improvements stem from methodological superiority or chance.  

The authors rely on inherent regularization in BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC), but given the tiny dataset, this is insufficient. The paper does not discuss regularization techniques, and the supplementary material only briefly mentions it for LSTM-ER. This gap must be addressed. I recommend:  
1. Explicitly detailing regularization methods (e.g., dropout rates, weight decay) in the main text.  
2. Using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning instead of the current ad-hoc approach.  
3. Moving critical details from the supplementary material—such as pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis—into the main paper (one additional page is sufficient).  

Additional requests:  
- Include inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset (the dataset paper provides relevant data; these would contextualize system performance and potential for improvement).  
- Enhance Figure 1 with color differentiation for better readability in black-and-white prints.  

Edit:  
After addressing the above points, I have revised my recommendation to 4. Please explicitly report F1-score ranges (not just means) and include mean/variance across experimental settings. While the revised manuscript addresses several concerns, the large variance in Kiperwasser results (F1: 0.56–0.61) still requires deeper analysis to confirm statistical significance and methodological robustness. Including these ranges will greatly aid reproducibility.
