

Strengths:  
The paper is well-written and accessible. The methods and results are compelling.  

Weaknesses:  
The evaluation and results require further scrutiny (detailed below).  

General Discussion:  
This paper introduces an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the task via sequence labeling and dependency parsing approaches. It also evaluates multitask learning for sequence labeling. The motivation is clearly articulated: existing methods rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and constraint design, whereas the proposed model eliminates such manual effort and jointly learns subtasks to avoid error propagation in pipeline methods. Except for minor missing details (noted below), the methodology is well-explained.  

Experiments are comprehensive, comparisons are appropriately conducted, and results are noteworthy. However, the primary concern is the dataset size (only ~320 training essays and 80 test essays) coupled with the high capacity of the BiLSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The development set size is omitted in both the paper and supplementary materials, which is concerning given the limited training data. The total training tags likely number only in the thousands—vastly below standard sequence labeling tasks (hundreds of thousands to millions of tags). This raises doubts about proper model training. The paper also lacks overfitting analysis (e.g., training/development loss curves per epoch), despite evidence in Line 622 suggesting taggers are simpler and less prone to overfitting.  

Model stability is questionable without mean/std. deviation across multiple runs (different parameter initializations) or statistical significance tests. Without these, it is unclear whether superior results reflect methodological superiority or chance. While regularization techniques are used in the neural networks, the paper omits explicit regularization details (supplementary materials only briefly mention LSTM-ER regularization). Given the small dataset, this must be addressed thoroughly.  

Recommendations:  
1. Replace the current hyper-parameter optimization method (supplementary) with Bayesian optimization.  
2. Move pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from supplementary to main paper (one additional page suffices).  
3. Include inter-annotator agreement scores to contextualize system performance and improvement potential.  
4. Enhance Figure 1 with color differentiation for black-and-white print clarity.
