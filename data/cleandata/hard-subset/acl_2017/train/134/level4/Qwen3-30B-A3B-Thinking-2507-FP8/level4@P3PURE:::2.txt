

The paper presents a comprehensive investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors explore five distinct methodologies: (1) dependency parsing (evaluating multiple parsers), (2) sequence labeling, (3) multi-task learning (built upon sequence labeling), (4) an off-the-shelf neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER), and (5) ILP-based state-of-the-art models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores computed over concepts and relations. Dependency-based solutions perform poorly, while sequence labeling achieves strong results. The LSTM-ER model demonstrates particularly strong performance, especially at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER significantly outperform the ILP baseline. The paper includes an exceptionally thorough supplement detailing model training, hyperparameter optimization, and implementation specifics. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that sequence labeling models benefit substantially from multi-task learning, with the claim task providing greater improvement than the relation task. Overall, this constitutes a rigorous and well-executed study of neural methods for argumentation mining.

**Major Concerns**  
1.  The dataset construction warrants discussion: essays in the training and test sets may cover identical topics, potentially allowing writers to reuse arguments across splits. This could cause information leakage and inflate performance estimates. While this issue likely affects the ILP baselines equally (mitigating any unfair advantage for the neural models), the authors should explicitly address this limitation in the paper.  
2.  The LSTM-ER model is an off-the-shelf application of a prior model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). However, given the thorough experimental analysis, the clear demonstration of sequence-based model superiority, and the valuable insights gained, this work remains highly valuable and merits publication.  

**Minor Concerns and Clarifications**  
*   Lines 222–226: The claim that the full argument graph can be reconstructed from a tree output requires clarification. The justification for this reconstruction is currently unclear.  
*   Lines 443–444: The section order (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → MTL using sequence tagging) is confusing. Reversing the first two methods (dependency parsing before sequence tagging) would improve readability, as the reader would then better understand the acronyms (e.g., STag_T) when they first appear.  
*   Line 455: The phrase "de-couples them but jointly models them" is contradictory. Joint modeling inherently requires coupling; please clarify this terminology.  
*   Lines 477–479: The statement that the system "decouples relation info from entity info" is ambiguous. The intended meaning appears to be that the model treats relation information as edge labels and entity information as node labels (thus decoupling the tasks), but this should be explicitly stated.  
*   Are the F1 scores for relation tasks in paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable? Paragraph-level models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations, which could lead to systematically lower relation F1 scores compared to essay-level models. This potential discrepancy should be addressed.
