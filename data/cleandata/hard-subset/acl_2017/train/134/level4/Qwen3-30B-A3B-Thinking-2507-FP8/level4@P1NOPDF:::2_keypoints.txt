- Paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of five neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER, ILP-based).  
- All methods are compared using F1 scores over concepts and relations.  
- Dependency-parsing formulations perform poorly compared to other approaches.  
- Sequence-labeling models perform significantly better than dependency-based methods.  
- LSTM-ER (off-the-shelf entity/relation model) achieves especially strong results, notably at paragraph level.  
- Sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER both outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art.  
- Sequence-labeling benefits substantially from multi-task learning, with claim identification yielding larger gains than relation identification.  
- The paper includes an exceptionally thorough supplement detailing training, hyperparameters, and experimental setup.  
- Major concern: potential dataset leakage from overlapping essay topics between train and test sets could inflate performance and should be explicitly acknowledged.  
- Major concern: LSTM-ER’s use as an off-the-shelf model raises a minor novelty question, though the experimental validation and insights justify publication.  
- Minor: Clarify how full argument graphs are reconstructed from tree outputs (Lines 222–226).  
- Minor: Reorder sections (sequence tagging and dependency parsing) to improve flow and clarity (Lines 443–444).  
- Minor: Resolve contradictory wording about “de-couples them but jointly models them” and explain how tasks are jointly modeled while decoupled (Line 455).  
- Minor: Clarify the claim that the system “de-couples relation info from entity info,” explicitly stating if some tasks are learned as edges and others as edge labels (Lines 477–479).  
- Minor: Clarify whether paragraph-level and essay-level F1 scores (especially for relations) are directly comparable given paragraph models ignore cross-paragraph relations.
