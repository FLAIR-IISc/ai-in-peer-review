

Strengths:  
The paper presents a well-structured, clearly written proposal for end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks. The motivation is compelling, effectively highlighting how the approach avoids manual feature engineering and ILP constraints prevalent in prior work. The joint learning framework mitigates error propagation in pipeline methods, and the multitask learning experiments add meaningful depth. The methods are explained logically, with substantial experimental validation and appropriate comparisons.

Weaknesses:  
The primary concern centers on dataset limitations and insufficient model validation. The training set comprises only ~320 essays, with a testing set of ~80 essays. Crucially, the development set size is omitted from both the paper and supplementary materials, raising concerns about the adequacy of data for training. With total training tags likely numbering in the thousands (vs. hundreds of thousands/millions in standard sequence labeling tasks), the risk of underfitting or poor parameter learning is significant. The authors’ claim that "taggers are simpler local models" (Line 622) appears to conflate model complexity with data requirements but lacks empirical support.  

The paper also neglects critical overfitting analysis: no training/development loss curves are provided during optimization, and regularization techniques (beyond a brief mention for LSTM-ER in supplementary materials) are not discussed. Given the small dataset, this omission is problematic. Furthermore, model stability is unverified: results lack mean/std deviation across multiple runs (with different parameter initializations), and statistical significance tests are absent. Without these, it is unclear whether performance gains reflect methodological superiority or random variation—e.g., the Kiperwasser setting shows F1 scores ranging from 0.56 to 0.61, a substantial variance that requires explanation.  

Additional concerns include:  
1. **Hyperparameter optimization**: The supplementary method (grid search) is suboptimal; Bayesian optimization would better handle the small data regime.  
2. **Missing dataset details**: Inter-annotator agreement scores (referenced in the dataset paper) should be included to contextualize task difficulty and potential improvement margins.  
3. **Figure clarity**: Figure 1 should use distinct colors for better readability in black-and-white printing.  
4. **Information placement**: Details on pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis should move from supplementary materials to the main text (an extra page is sufficient).  

General Discussion:  
While the paper’s core contribution—avoiding manual design via neural joint learning—is valuable, the evaluation framework undermines confidence in the results. The dataset’s limited size (320 training essays) is incompatible with the capacity of the BiLSTM models used, especially without rigorous regularization or stability analysis. The authors’ claim that "taggers need less data" (Line 622) is not substantiated by the data, as their own results show high variance. Including F1-score ranges (as requested in the authors’ revision) and mean/variance across runs would significantly strengthen the claims. Without these, the paper’s conclusions about model superiority remain tentative.  

Recommendation:  
The paper addresses a relevant problem with a novel framework, but the experimental weaknesses are severe enough to warrant a marginal rejection. The authors must address the dataset size concerns, provide stability metrics (mean/std across runs), report loss curves, clarify regularization, and include inter-annotator agreement scores. The current results cannot reliably support the claimed improvements.  

*Note: The authors have partially addressed the F1-score range request in their revision. However, the stability concerns (e.g., large Kiperwasser variance) remain unexplained, and the development set size is still omitted. These must be resolved before acceptance.*
