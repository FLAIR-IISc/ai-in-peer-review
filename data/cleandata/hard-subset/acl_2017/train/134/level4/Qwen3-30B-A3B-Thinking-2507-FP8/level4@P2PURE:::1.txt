

- Strengths:  
The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The methods and results are interesting and well-motivated.

- Weaknesses:  
The evaluation and results require careful scrutiny (see detailed comments below).

- General Discussion:  
This paper proposes an end-to-end neural network system for argumentation mining, modeling the task through sequence labeling and dependency parsing. It further explores multitask learning for sequence labeling. The motivation is clearly articulated: existing methods rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and handcrafted ILP constraints, whereas the proposed approach eliminates such manual effort. Additionally, the model jointly learns subtasks in argumentation mining, avoiding error propagation inherent in pipeline methods. Except for minor missing details (noted below), the methodology is explained clearly.

The experiments are substantial, comparisons are appropriately conducted, and results are compelling. However, my primary concern is the dataset size (320 training essays, 80 testing essays) combined with the high capacity of the (Bi)LSTM-based models (BLC and BLCC). The development set size is not reported in the paper or supplementary materials, which is concerning given the limited training data. The total number of tags in training is likely only a few thousand—significantly smaller than standard sequence labeling tasks (which often use hundreds of thousands or millions of tags). This raises doubts about whether model parameters are adequately trained. The paper lacks overfitting analysis; reporting training and development loss curves (per epoch or update) would be essential. The authors’ own comment (Line 622: "taggers are simpler local models... less prone to overfitting") further suggests overfitting risks.

I also question model stability. The paper should report mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different parameter initializations, alongside statistical significance tests. Without these, it is unclear whether observed performance gains stem from methodological superiority or random chance. While the paper mentions regularization techniques for neural networks, it omits specific regularization methods (beyond a brief note on LSTM-ER in supplementary materials). Given the small dataset, this omission is critical and must be addressed. I recommend:  
1. Replacing the current hyper-parameter optimization method (supplementary materials) with Bayesian optimization.  
2. Moving details on pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from supplementary materials to the main paper (an additional page suffices).  
3. Including inter-annotator agreement scores (available in the dataset paper) to contextualize system performance and potential improvement margins.  
4. Enhancing Figure 1 with distinct line styles/markers for black-and-white reproduction.

Edit:  
Thank you for addressing my queries. I have increased my recommendation to 4. Please include F1-score ranges in the paper and report mean/variance across settings.
