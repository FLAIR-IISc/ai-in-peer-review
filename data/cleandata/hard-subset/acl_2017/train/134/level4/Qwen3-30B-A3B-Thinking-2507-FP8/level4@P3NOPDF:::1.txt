

**Reviewer Summary**  
This paper proposes an end-to-end argumentation mining system using neural networks, modeling the task via sequence labeling and dependency parsing, with additional exploration of multitask learning. The writing is clear, the motivation is well-justified (avoiding ILP-based manual feature engineering and pipeline error propagation), and the experimental setup is generally sound. However, critical concerns regarding dataset size, model stability, and methodological rigor require resolution before acceptance.  

**Strengths**  
The paper presents a compelling shift from manual feature engineering to neural end-to-end learning, clearly articulating the advantages over prior work. The methodological explanations are thorough, and the experiments demonstrate meaningful improvements over baselines. The multitask learning component adds significant value to the analysis.  

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Insufficient Dataset Size and Overfitting Risk**:  
   The training set comprises only 320 essays (with an undisclosed development set size), resulting in a total of approximately 3,000 annotated tokens. This is drastically smaller than standard sequence labeling datasets (e.g., CoNLL-2003 with ~150k tokens). The authors’ claim that “taggers are simpler local models” (Line 622) fails to address the fundamental risk of overfitting given the model’s capacity (BiLSTMs with 300+ hidden units). Crucially, the paper omits:  
   - Training/validation loss curves to diagnose overfitting.  
   - Statistical validation (mean ± SD across multiple runs with different random seeds) to assess model stability.  
   - Statistical significance tests for performance differences.  
   Without these, the reported F1 scores (e.g., Kiperwasser’s range [0.56, 0.61]) cannot be confidently attributed to model superiority rather than random variation. The lack of regularization discussion (beyond a brief mention in supplementary materials) compounds this risk.  

2. **Lack of Reproducibility and Analysis Rigor**:  
   The small dataset size necessitates explicit discussion of regularization strategies (e.g., dropout, weight decay, early stopping) and hyperparameter optimization methodology. The current approach (described in supplementary materials) lacks transparency; Bayesian optimization should replace grid search for robust hyperparameter tuning. Additionally, the paper omits inter-annotator agreement (IAA) scores for the dataset, which are critical for contextualizing performance ceilings and potential room for improvement (the dataset paper likely contains this information).  

3. **Missing Technical Details**:  
   - The development set size is unspecified (both in the paper and supplementary materials), undermining evaluation validity.  
   - Pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis (currently in supplementary materials) should be integrated into the main text to improve accessibility.  
   - Figure 1 should use distinct colors for better readability in black-and-white print.  

**Recommendations for Revision**  
To address these concerns, the authors must:  
- Report mean and standard deviation of F1 scores across multiple random initializations (at least 5 runs) and include statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests).  
- Provide training/validation loss curves to demonstrate overfitting mitigation.  
- Explicitly discuss regularization techniques (e.g., dropout rates, L2 penalties) and justify hyperparameter choices via Bayesian optimization.  
- Add the development set size, IAA scores, and key supplementary details (embeddings, error analysis) to the main paper.  
- Clarify the F1-score ranges (e.g., "Kiperwasser: 0.56–0.61") to enable replication.  

**Minor Issues**  
- Line 622’s justification for tagger simplicity is insufficient without evidence of overfitting analysis.  
- The supplementary material’s brief regularization mention must be expanded in the main text.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper’s core contribution is promising, but its claims are undermined by inadequate validation of model robustness and insufficient dataset context. The dataset size is a critical limitation that the authors have not adequately addressed. Addressing these points—particularly model stability, overfitting analysis, and IAA scores—would significantly strengthen the work.  

**Recommendation**  
**Major Revision (Score: 4)**  
The paper has strong potential but requires substantial revisions to resolve the methodological concerns outlined above. Once these are addressed, the paper will make a valuable contribution to argumentation mining. The authors should prioritize adding statistical validation, dataset context, and regularization details to justify their results.
