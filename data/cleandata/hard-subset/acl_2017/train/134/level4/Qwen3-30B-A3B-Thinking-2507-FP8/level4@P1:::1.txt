

Summary: This paper introduces the first neural end-to-end approach to computational argumentation mining, framing the problem as token-based sequence tagging, dependency parsing, and multi-task learning. The authors' primary contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions for argumentation mining; (2) demonstrating that sequence tagging with BiLSTMs outperforms dependency parsing for this task; and (3) showing that multi-task learning improves performance by jointly modeling natural subtasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to propose neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and constraint design required by previous pipeline methods. This addresses a key limitation in the field where prior work relies heavily on hand-crafted features that must be adapted for each new dataset.
2. The empirical results are compelling, with the authors' best model (STagBLCC) substantially outperforming the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach by 5-10% on most metrics. This demonstrates the practical viability of neural approaches for this challenging task, particularly given the complexity of argument structures.
3. The authors provide a valuable theoretical insight by showing that dependency parsing, while intuitive for argumentation structures, is ineffective when applied at the token level. This corrects a potentially misleading assumption in the literature and explains why previous approaches that operated at the component level had success while this token-level approach does not.

Weaknesses:
1. The dataset size is extremely limited (only 320 training essays), raising serious concerns about model overfitting and statistical reliability. The paper does not report development set size or provide evidence of model stability through multiple runs with different initializations. Without this, it's difficult to determine whether the performance gains are due to the proposed approach or random variation.
2. The paper lacks a proper analysis of overfitting, despite the small dataset size. The authors claim that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" (line 622), but provide no evidence for this claim. Crucially, they don't report training and validation loss curves, which would be essential for assessing model fit in such a constrained dataset scenario.
3. The paper omits discussion of regularization techniques used in the neural models, despite the dataset's small size. The supplementary materials briefly mention regularization for LSTM-ER, but the main paper provides no details. Given the risk of overfitting with such limited data, this omission significantly undermines confidence in the results.
4. The authors do not provide inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, which would help contextualize the performance of their models and indicate the maximum achievable performance. This information is available in the dataset description paper and would provide valuable context for interpreting the results.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the size of the development set and explain why it's not reported in the main paper or supplementary materials?
2. Would you be able to include training and validation loss curves for the main models to better assess overfitting given the limited dataset size?
3. Could you provide mean and standard deviation across multiple runs for the main results to demonstrate model stability, particularly for the Kiperwasser model where performance varies significantly?
4. Could you elaborate on the regularization techniques used for the neural models, especially given the small dataset size?
