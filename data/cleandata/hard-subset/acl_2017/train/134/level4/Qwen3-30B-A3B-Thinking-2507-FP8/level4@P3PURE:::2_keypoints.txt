- Presents a comprehensive comparison of five neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning (on sequence labeling), LSTM-ER, and ILP-based models.  
- Evaluates all approaches using F1 scores for concepts and relations with detailed experimental analysis.  
- Dependency-parsing approaches perform poorly.  
- Sequence-labeling approaches achieve strong results.  
- The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER yields particularly strong performance, especially at paragraph level.  
- Sequence-labeling and LSTM-ER significantly outperform the ILP baseline.  
- The paper includes an exceptionally thorough supplement covering training, hyperparameter tuning, and implementation details.  
- Multi-task learning substantially benefits sequence-labeling models, with the claim task contributing more improvement than the relation task.  
- Major concern: potential data leakage — training and test essays may share topics, allowing reused arguments across splits and inflating performance; authors should discuss this limitation.  
- Major concern: LSTM-ER is an off-the-shelf application of prior work (Miwa & Bansal 2016), though the overall experimental contribution still merits publication.  
- Minor concern: clarify the claim that a full argument graph can be reconstructed from a tree output (Lines 222–226).  
- Minor concern: reorder sections (put dependency parsing before sequence tagging) for readability and clearer introduction of acronyms (Lines 443–444).  
- Minor concern: resolve contradictory phrasing "de-couples them but jointly models them" and clarify intended meaning (Line 455).  
- Minor concern: explicitly state that "decouples relation info from entity info" means treating relations as edge labels and entities as node labels (Lines 477–479).  
- Minor concern: address whether relation F1 scores are directly comparable between paragraph- and essay-level settings, given paragraph models cannot capture cross-paragraph relations.
