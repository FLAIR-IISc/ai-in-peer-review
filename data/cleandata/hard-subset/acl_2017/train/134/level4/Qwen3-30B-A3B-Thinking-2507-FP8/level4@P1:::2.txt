

Summary: This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining, comparing several neural architectures. The main contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions for argumentation mining, (2) demonstrating that sequence tagging models with distance encoding outperform dependency parsing approaches, (3) showing that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task significantly improves performance, and (4) establishing that neural sequence tagging models provide robust performance across different argumentation environments.

Strengths: 
1. The paper provides a comprehensive and well-structured comparison of multiple neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of an existing neural model (LSTM-ER). The thorough evaluation across different architectures and settings offers valuable insights for the field.

2. The sequence tagging approach (STagT) with distance encoding consistently outperforms the dependency parsing methods and the state-of-the-art ILP-based system. The paper demonstrates that token-level sequence tagging models, particularly those incorporating distance information, effectively capture long-range dependencies inherent in argumentation structures, making them particularly suitable for this task.

3. The multi-task learning experiments provide significant practical insights, showing that adding component detection as an auxiliary task substantially improves performance (typically by a few percentage points), while relation detection as an auxiliary task provides less benefit. This finding is valuable for future work in argumentation mining and sequence labeling more broadly.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not adequately address potential data leakage due to topic overlap between train and test sets. Since the dataset consists of student essays on controversial topics, it's possible that train and test essays share similar topics, potentially leading to overly optimistic performance estimates. While this issue also affects the ILP baseline, the authors should discuss this limitation and provide evidence that the topic distribution between train and test sets is sufficiently diverse.

2. The LSTM-ER model, which achieves the best results on paragraph-level evaluation, is largely an out-of-the-box application of an existing model without significant adaptation. While the authors' adaptation is valuable, they should more clearly articulate how their implementation differs from the original LSTM-ER and why it works well for argumentation mining.

3. The paper's explanation of how LSTM-ER "de-couples" entity and relation information while "jointly modeling" them is confusing and contradictory. The description should be clarified to explain precisely how the model handles the relationship between entity detection and relation identification.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper does not provide details about the topic distribution across train and test sets. Could you clarify whether the topics covered in the training and testing essays are sufficiently diverse to prevent data leakage, and provide statistics on topic overlap between the two sets?

2. For the paragraph-level evaluation, how are relations that cross paragraph boundaries handled? Given that paragraph boundaries may split argumentative structures, does this affect the evaluation of relation extraction performance?

3. The paper states that LSTM-ER "de-couples" entity and relation information while "jointly modeling" them. Could you clarify this apparent contradiction and explain precisely how the model handles the relationship between entity detection and relation identification?

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by demonstrating the effectiveness of neural sequence tagging approaches for argumentation mining. The comprehensive evaluation and clear empirical results provide valuable insights for future work. The minor issues noted above could be addressed in a revision to further strengthen the paper. The supplementary materials provide excellent technical detail on model implementation and hyperparameter optimization, which is highly appreciated.
