

This paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining. The authors systematically explore five distinct methods: (1) dependency parsing formulations (tested with multiple parsers), (2) sequence labeling, (3) multi-task learning (built atop sequence labeling), (4) an off-the-shelf neural model for entity and relation extraction (LSTM-ER), and (5) state-of-the-art ILP-based models. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores computed over concepts and relations.

Dependency-based methods show limited effectiveness, while sequence labeling models prove significantly more successful. Notably, the LSTM-ER model achieves particularly strong results, especially at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP-based approach. The paper includes an exceptionally thorough supplement detailing model training, hyperparameter optimization, and experimental configurations.

The authors demonstrate that sequence labeling models benefit substantially from multi-task learning, with the claim identification task providing greater improvement than the relation identification task. Overall, this represents a rigorous and well-executed investigation of neural methods for argumentation mining.

**Major Concerns**  
1.  **Dataset Leakage Risk:** I am concerned that the train and test sets may contain essays on overlapping topics, potentially allowing writers to reuse similar arguments across both sets. This could lead to information leakage and overly optimistic performance estimates. While this limitation likely affects the ILP models equally, it should be explicitly acknowledged as a potential caveat in the discussion.

2.  **LSTM-ER as Off-the-Shelf Model:** A key strength of the LSTM-ER model is its off-the-shelf application from prior work. While this raises a minor question about novelty, the thorough experimental validation, clear comparative analysis, and valuable insights gained from the multi-task learning framework strongly justify publication.

**Minor Comments and Clarifications**  
*   Lines 222–226: The explanation for reconstructing the full argument graph from tree outputs remains unclear. Please clarify this step.
*   Lines 443–444: The current ordering (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → multi-task learning) is confusing. Reversing the first two sections would improve flow and prevent readers from forgetting what "STag_T" represents.
*   Line 455: The phrase "de-couples them but jointly models them" is contradictory. Please clarify how the model jointly models the tasks while decoupling them (as coupling is typically required for joint modeling).
*   Line 477–479: The claim that the system "de-couples relation info from entity info" is ambiguous. If the model learns some tasks as "edges" and others as "edge labels," please explicitly state this to avoid confusion.
*   **F1 Comparison:** Are the F1 scores reported for paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, especially for relation extraction? Paragraph-level models inherently disregard cross-paragraph relations, which could systematically affect relation F1 scores relative to essay-level models. This point merits clarification.
