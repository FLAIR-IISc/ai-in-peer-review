

The paper presents a joint neural approach to argumentation mining, systematically evaluating five distinct methodologies:  
1) Framing the task as dependency parsing (tested with multiple parsers),  
2) Modeling it as sequence labeling,  
3) Employing multi-task learning (built atop the sequence labeling framework),  
4) Utilizing an out-of-the-box neural model for entity and relation labeling (LSTM-ER),  
5) Implementing state-of-the-art ILP-based models.  

All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores computed over concepts and relations. Dependency-based methods underperform, while sequence labeling solutions prove effective. The LSTM-ER model achieves exceptional results, particularly at the paragraph level. Both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER significantly outperform the ILP approach. The thorough supplementary material details all training procedures, hyperparameter optimization, and technical implementation specifics. The paper further demonstrates that sequence labeling models benefit substantially from multi-task learning, with the claim task providing greater improvement than the relation task. Overall, this represents a rigorous and comprehensive investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining.  

- **Major remarks**  
  - A primary concern involves dataset construction: essays in the training and test sets may share topics, potentially enabling writers to reuse identical or similar arguments across both sets. This could cause information leakage from training to testing, inflating performance estimates. While the same issue likely affects the ILP baselines (so the neural model does not gain an unfair advantage), the authors should explicitly address this limitation in the discussion.  
  - A secondary concern is that the top-performing LSTM-ER model is an off-the-shelf application of a prior model. However, given the strong empirical results with sequence-based approaches, the extensive experimentation, and the valuable insights gained, this work merits publication.  

- **Minor remarks and questions**  
  - Lines 222–226: The argument that a full graph can be reconstructed from a tree output requires clarification.  
  - Lines 443–444: The section ordering (sequence tagging → dependency parsing → multi-task learning using sequence tagging) is confusing. Reversing the first two topics would improve flow, as the reader may forget the meaning of "STag_T" by this point.  
  - Line 455: The claim that the model "de-couples them but jointly models them" is contradictory. Joint modeling inherently requires coupling; please clarify the intended meaning. (Note: The reference to Miwa and Bansal could not be located.)  
  - Lines 477–479: The assertion that the system "de-couples relation information from entity information" is ambiguous. The intended meaning appears to be that the model learns certain tasks as "edges of the tree" and others as "labels on those edges," thereby decoupling them. This section requires clearer exposition.  
  - Are F1 scores in paragraph-level and essay-level settings directly comparable, especially for relation tasks? Paragraph-level models inherently exclude cross-paragraph relations, which may bias relation task performance.
