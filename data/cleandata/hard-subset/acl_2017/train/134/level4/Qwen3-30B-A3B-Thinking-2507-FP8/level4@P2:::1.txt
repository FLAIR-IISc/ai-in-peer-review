

Summary: The paper introduces neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining, addressing the limitations of previous pipeline methods that rely on manual feature engineering and ILP constraints. The main contributions are: (1) the first neural end-to-end solutions for argumentation mining, (2) demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to component-level approaches), (4) robust performance of neural sequence tagging with distance encoding across different scenarios, and (5) improved performance through multi-task learning with natural subtasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a compelling motivation for neural end-to-end approaches, clearly explaining how they overcome key limitations of existing methods that require manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design. The authors effectively argue that neural methods avoid error propagation in pipeline architectures by jointly learning subtasks.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured, comparing multiple neural approaches against the state-of-the-art ILP model. The results consistently show that neural methods outperform the ILP baseline, with the sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) achieving the best performance.

3. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for argumentation mining, contrary to what has been proposed for component-level approaches. This insight is crucial for guiding future research in this area.

4. The authors provide clear explanations of their model architectures, particularly for the sequence tagging approach that encodes component distances in the label set. The technical details are well-presented and accessible.

Weaknesses:
1. The dataset size is extremely small (320 training essays, 80 testing essays), raising serious concerns about overfitting and model reliability. Standard sequence labeling tasks typically use hundreds of thousands or millions of examples, while this paper has only a few thousand tags in training. The paper fails to report the development set size, which is critical for proper model evaluation.

2. The paper lacks sufficient analysis of model stability and generalizability. There's no discussion of mean and variance across multiple runs with different parameter initializations, statistical significance tests, or training/development loss curves. The reported F1 scores vary significantly (e.g., Kiperwasser's results range from 0.56 to 0.61), yet the authors don't analyze this variance or discuss its implications.

3. The paper inadequately addresses regularization techniques despite the small dataset size. While the authors mention that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting," they provide no empirical evidence for this claim. The supplementary materials briefly mention regularization for LSTM-ER but don't discuss it in the main paper.

4. The paper omits inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, which would provide valuable context for interpreting the results and understanding the potential room for improvement.

5. The hyperparameter optimization method (described only in supplementary materials) is insufficiently justified. Given the small dataset size, the authors should consider more sophisticated optimization methods like Bayesian optimization.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide the size of the development set used for hyperparameter tuning? This is crucial for understanding the model evaluation process.

2. Please report the mean and standard deviation of F1 scores across multiple runs with different random seeds. This would help assess model stability.

3. Could you provide training and development loss curves to demonstrate whether the models are overfitting given the small dataset size?

4. Please include inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset to help contextualize the performance metrics.

5. The paper mentions that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" - could you provide empirical evidence supporting this claim?

Additional Comments:
The paper would benefit from moving key details about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary materials to the main text. A single additional page would suffice for these additions. Additionally, please improve the visual quality of Figure 1 by using color differentiation for better readability in black-and-white prints.
