

Summary: This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as token-based dependency parsing and sequence tagging with multi-task learning. The authors claim five contributions: (1) first neural end-to-end AM solutions; (2) better performance than state-of-the-art ILP model; (3) dependency parsing ineffective for token-level AM; (4) sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly; (5) multi-task learning with natural subtasks improves performance. The actual contributions I see are: (1) demonstrating that sequence tagging with distance encoding is a robust approach for AM; (2) showing that dependency parsing at token level is ineffective for AM; (3) providing evidence that multi-task learning with natural subtasks improves performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a meaningful contribution by introducing neural end-to-end approaches to AM, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design that previous approaches relied on. The authors provide a thorough comparison of different neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning), which is valuable for the community.

2. The empirical evaluation is substantial, with careful comparison across multiple neural modeling approaches. The authors demonstrate that sequence tagging models (particularly STagBLCC) consistently outperform dependency parsing approaches and the state-of-the-art ILP model, with improvements of up to 13% for component F1 and 11% for relation F1 on the paragraph level.

3. The paper provides important insights about the limitations of dependency parsing for token-level AM, which is contrary to what might be expected given that argument structures often form trees. The authors correctly identify that dependency parsing at the token level (rather than component level) is ineffective, which is a valuable finding for future work.

Weaknesses:
1. The dataset size is critically small (only 320 training essays and 80 test essays), which raises serious concerns about overfitting and model reliability. The paper fails to provide evidence that the models aren't overfitting to this tiny dataset. Standard sequence labeling tasks typically use hundreds of thousands or millions of training examples, while this paper has only a few thousand tags. The authors' claim that "taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting" is unsubstantiated without showing training and validation loss curves or model stability metrics.

2. The paper lacks crucial model stability information. The authors don't report mean and standard deviation across multiple runs with different parameter initializations, nor do they provide statistical significance tests. This is particularly problematic given the large variance in some results (e.g., Kiperwasser's results range from 0.56 to 0.61). Without these metrics, it's impossible to determine whether the performance differences between models are statistically significant or simply due to random initialization.

3. The paper inadequately addresses regularization for the small dataset. While the authors mention regularization briefly in supplementary materials, they don't discuss which regularization techniques were used or why they were chosen. Given the tiny dataset size, the authors should have provided a detailed discussion of regularization approaches to prevent overfitting, including dropout rates, weight decay, early stopping, etc.

4. The paper omits inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset, which would help contextualize the performance of the models. Without knowing the human baseline (e.g., inter-annotator agreement), it's difficult to assess how much room for improvement exists or whether the reported performance is actually good.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide training and validation loss curves for the main models to demonstrate that overfitting isn't occurring?
2. Please report mean and standard deviation of performance across multiple runs with different random seeds, along with statistical significance tests.
3. What specific regularization techniques were used, and why were they chosen for this small dataset?
4. Can you provide inter-annotator agreement scores for the dataset to help contextualize the model performance?

Additional Comments:
- The paper should move information about pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis from the supplementary material to the main paper, as it's crucial for understanding the methodology.
- The authors should include F1-score ranges in the paper to help with reproducibility.
- The figure 1 should be improved with better color contrast for black-and-white printing.
- The paper should include a more detailed discussion of the model capacity versus dataset size, explaining why the authors believe their models can generalize despite the small dataset.
