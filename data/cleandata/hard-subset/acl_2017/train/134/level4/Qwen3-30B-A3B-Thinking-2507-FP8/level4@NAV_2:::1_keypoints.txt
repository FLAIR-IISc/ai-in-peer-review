- Clear, well-structured investigation of neural end-to-end computational argumentation mining with thorough experimental evaluation.  
- First neural end-to-end solutions showing neural models outperform state-of-the-art feature-based ILP models.  
- Empirical finding that simple BiLSTM sequence tagging outperforms more complex dependency parsing approaches.  
- Multi-task learning setup yields meaningful improvements over single-task learning via auxiliary tasks.  
- Primary concern: very limited dataset size (~320 essays training, 80 test; dev size not reported) risking overfitting and unreliable results.  
- Paper lacks training and development loss curves to demonstrate convergence and detect overfitting.  
- Authors do not report mean and standard deviation across multiple runs nor perform statistical significance testing.  
- Insufficient discussion of regularization techniques given small dataset and large-capacity BiLSTM models.  
- Model stability issues: high variance in results for some configurations (e.g., F1 range [0.56, 0.61]) need analysis and reporting of ranges across runs.  
- Hyperparameter optimization method is only in the supplement and may be weak; recommend more robust methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization).  
- Key information (pre-trained embeddings, error analysis) should be moved from supplementary material to the main text for accessibility.  
- Manuscript should report inter-annotator agreement scores to contextualize achievable performance.  
- Figure illustrating argument structures needs improvement for black-and-white printing (use different colors/patterns).  
- Requests to authors: provide development set size, mean±std of F1 over multiple runs, statistical tests, training/dev loss curves, clarify regularization, and supply inter-annotator agreement.
