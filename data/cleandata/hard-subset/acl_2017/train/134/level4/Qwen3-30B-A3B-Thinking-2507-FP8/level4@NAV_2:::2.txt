

Summary of the paper: This work investigates neural techniques for end-to-end computational argumentation mining. The authors frame the problem as token-based dependency parsing, token-based sequence tagging, and multi-task learning setups. The main contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining; (2) demonstrating that sequence tagging models with distance encoding outperform state-of-the-art joint ILP models; (3) showing that framing argumentation mining as dependency parsing is ineffective at the token level, while standard neural sequence tagging models perform robustly across different environments.

Strengths: 
1. The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of multiple neural approaches to end-to-end argumentation mining, including dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of an existing LSTM-ER model. The thorough comparison demonstrates that sequence tagging models significantly outperform dependency parsing approaches, with the best sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) achieving 55.22% global F1 on paragraph level, surpassing the ILP baseline (55.23%).
2. The multi-task learning experiments reveal a significant improvement (up to 5.5% in global F1) when incorporating component detection as an auxiliary task, demonstrating that complex sequence tagging benefits from training with natural subtasks. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks beyond argumentation mining.
3. The work thoroughly analyzes why certain approaches fail (e.g., dependency parsing's poor performance on essay level due to long sequences and complex tree structures), providing valuable insights for future work in computational argumentation. The paper also demonstrates that LSTM-ER, while a relatively straightforward adaptation, performs exceptionally well on paragraph level, achieving 73.02% C-F1 (50%) and 45.19% R-F1 (50%).

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation uses a dataset where essays in the train and test sets might share similar topics, potentially causing information leakage between train and test sets. While the ILP baseline also faces this issue, the paper does not explicitly acknowledge this limitation, which could lead to overly optimistic performance estimates for all models.
2. The LSTM-ER model, which achieves the best results on paragraph level, is largely an out-of-the-box application of a model designed for entity and relation extraction rather than a novel contribution to argumentation mining. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish between the contributions of adapting this model versus the novel insights gained from its application to argumentation.
3. The paper's explanation of how STagT and LSTM-ER "decouple" entity and relation information is unclear. The description suggests the models treat relations as edges and components as nodes, but this distinction is not clearly articulated in the main text, potentially confusing readers about the technical approach.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify how the paragraph-level evaluation relates to the end-to-end nature of the task, particularly regarding cross-paragraph relations? The paper states that argument structures are contained within paragraphs, but it's unclear whether the model could be extended to handle relations spanning multiple paragraphs.
2. The paper mentions that "the argument structure in each essay is—almost—a tree" due to multiple major claims. How do you handle cases where the structure deviates from a tree (multiple roots)? What is the impact on the dependency parsing approach when this occurs?
3. The supplementary materials mention using paragraph-level evaluation for some models due to memory constraints. How does the model's performance on essay level compare to paragraph level for the best-performing model (LSTM-ER), and what are the specific computational challenges at the essay level that cause such a significant drop in performance?
