- Paper presents a comprehensive empirical comparison of five end-to-end argumentation mining approaches (dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multitask learning, LSTM-ER, ILP) using concept- and relation-level F1.  
- Dependency parsing formulations perform poorly compared to other methods.  
- Sequence labeling models are highly effective for argumentation mining.  
- The off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves particularly strong performance, especially at the paragraph level.  
- Sequence labeling and LSTM-ER substantially outperform the ILP-based state-of-the-art.  
- Multitask learning significantly improves sequence labeling, with the claim task contributing more than the relation task.  
- The authors provide an extensive supplement with training, hyperparameters, and implementation details, aiding reproducibility.  
- Major concern: dataset construction may include essays on overlapping topics across train/test splits, risking information leakage and inflated performance estimates—this must be discussed.  
- Major concern: the LSTM-ER is a reused off-the-shelf model; the paper should better explain how its success advances understanding beyond adoption.  
- Minor: clarify how a full argument graph is reconstructed from a tree output (Lines 222–226).  
- Minor: restructure sections for better flow (swap sequence tagging and dependency parsing subsections).  
- Minor: clarify contradictory wording about “de-couples them but jointly models them” and explicitly state whether entities and relations are learned separately or jointly (Lines 455, 477–479).  
- Minor: address whether paragraph-level and essay-level relation F1 are directly comparable, given paragraph models miss cross-paragraph relations.  
- Overall verdict: rigorous, well-executed study with valuable insights; suitable for publication after minor revisions addressing the concerns above.
