

This paper presents a thorough empirical investigation of neural approaches for end-to-end argumentation mining, systematically comparing multiple architectures including dependency parsing, sequence labeling, multi-task learning (MTL), an off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model, and ILP-based methods. All approaches are evaluated using F1 scores on both concepts and relations. The key findings demonstrate that dependency-based methods perform poorly, while sequence labeling models achieve strong results. Crucially, the off-the-shelf LSTM-ER model achieves state-of-the-art performance, particularly at the paragraph level, and both sequence labeling and LSTM-ER significantly outperform the ILP baseline. The comprehensive supplementary materials detail model training, hyperparameter optimization, and experimental setups, enhancing reproducibility. Notably, the MTL approach substantially improves sequence labeling, with the claim task providing greater benefit than the relation task.

**Strengths:**  
The paper’s core contribution lies in its rigorous, multi-faceted comparison of neural architectures for argumentation mining—a domain where prior work has largely relied on ILP methods. The evaluation design effectively isolates the impact of different modeling choices (e.g., dependency vs. sequence approaches, MTL integration), providing clear evidence that sequence-based methods are superior. The LSTM-ER results are particularly compelling, demonstrating that leveraging existing entity-relation frameworks can yield strong performance with minimal adaptation. The thoroughness of the supplementary materials significantly bolsters the paper’s credibility.

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Potential Data Leakage:** The train and test sets contain essays on overlapping topics (e.g., "environmental policy" in both sets). This risks argument overlap between train and test essays, potentially inflating performance estimates via information leakage. While this issue likely affects all models (including the ILP baseline), the paper should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss its implications for reported F1 scores. A brief analysis of topic overlap between train/test sets would strengthen the evaluation.  
2. **LSTM-ER as Off-the-Shelf Adaptation:** The paper’s strongest model (LSTM-ER) is an adaptation of a prior entity-relation model (Miwa & Bansal, 2016), not a novel architecture. While the experimental validation is robust, the paper should clarify how this adaptation differs from the original work (e.g., input preprocessing, task-specific adjustments) and explicitly position it as a *validation* of existing methods rather than a new contribution. Given the paper’s broader focus on comparative neural approaches, this is a minor limitation but warrants transparent discussion.

**Minor Concerns:**  
- *Lines 222–226:* The claim that a full argument graph can be reconstructed from a tree output is unclear. Please elaborate on the reconstruction method (e.g., "edges are added via heuristics to resolve missing dependencies") or clarify if this applies only to specific cases.  
- *Lines 443–444:* The model comparison order (sequence tagging → dependency → MTL) is confusing. Reversing the first two comparisons (dependency → sequence tagging) would improve flow, as the dependency approach is introduced first. The acronym "STag_T" should be defined upon first use.  
- *Line 455:* The phrasing "de-couples them but jointly models them" is contradictory. If tasks are jointly modeled, they are inherently coupled; clarify whether "decoupled" refers to *independent* task representations during training (e.g., separate output layers) while still sharing a backbone.  
- *Lines 477–479:* The claim that "the system decouples relation info from entity info" is ambiguous. Please explicitly state whether this refers to: (a) learning entity labels and relation labels via separate subnetworks, or (b) modeling relations as *attributes* of edges (e.g., entity pairs + relation type). The current phrasing suggests a miscommunication of the architecture.  
- *F1 Score Comparability:* The paper reports F1 scores for paragraph-level and essay-level settings but does not address whether these scores are directly comparable, particularly for relation extraction. Paragraph-level models inherently miss cross-paragraph relations (e.g., arguments spanning multiple paragraphs), which could artificially inflate relation F1 in the paragraph setting. A brief discussion of this discrepancy would be valuable.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong, well-executed study that provides valuable empirical insights into neural argumentation mining. The core findings—sequence labeling and LSTM-ER as effective approaches—significantly advance the field beyond ILP baselines. While the data leakage concern requires acknowledgment and the LSTM-ER adaptation warrants clearer positioning, these do not undermine the paper’s overall contribution. The thorough experimental design, comprehensive supplementary materials, and clear comparative analysis make this work suitable for publication, provided the minor concerns are addressed in revision. The paper delivers on its promise of a "very thorough investigation" and offers concrete guidance for future work.
