

Strengths:  
The paper is well-written and accessible, with clear explanations of the methods and compelling results.

Weaknesses:  
The evaluation and results require careful scrutiny (detailed below).

General Discussion:  
This paper proposes an end-to-end neural approach for argumentation mining, modeling the task via sequence labeling and dependency parsing. It further explores multitask learning for sequence labeling. The motivation is well-justified: existing methods rely on ILP, manual feature engineering, and handcrafted ILP constraints, whereas the proposed model eliminates such manual effort and jointly learns subtasks to avoid error propagation in pipeline approaches. The methods are clearly explained, except for a few missing details (addressed below).  

The experimental setup is substantial, with appropriate comparisons and interesting results. However, two critical concerns arise:  

1.  **Dataset Size and Overfitting Risk**: The training set comprises only ~320 essays (with an unreported development set size), and the test set contains 80 essays. The total number of tags in training is likely only in the thousands—far below standard sequence labeling tasks (hundreds of thousands to millions). This severely limits the model’s capacity to generalize. The paper does not analyze overfitting, despite evidence suggesting it: Line 622 states, "Our explanation is that taggers are simpler local models, and thus need less training data and are less prone to overfitting." Without training/development loss curves (per epoch or update), this claim lacks support.  

2.  **Model Stability and Statistical Rigor**: The results lack stability metrics. Mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (with different parameter initializations) are missing, as are statistical significance tests. Without these, it is unclear whether reported improvements stem from methodological superiority or random chance. For instance, the Kiperwasser setting shows significant F1 variance (range [0.56, 0.61]), which requires explicit analysis. The paper must report F1-score ranges and mean/variance for all settings to enable replication.  

Additional concerns:  
- The paper does not discuss regularization techniques, despite the small dataset. The supplementary material briefly mentions regularization for LSTM-ER but omits critical details.  
- Hyper-parameter optimization (currently described in supplementary materials) should adopt Bayesian optimization.  
- Pre-trained word embeddings and error analysis should be moved from supplementary materials to the main paper (one additional page suffices).  
- Inter-annotator agreement scores (available in the dataset description) should be included to contextualize system performance and potential improvement margins.  
- Figure 1 should use distinct colors for better readability in black-and-white prints.  

Post-Revision Note:  
After addressing the above points (particularly F1-score ranges, mean/variance reporting, and stability analysis), I have increased my recommendation to 4. The model stability concerns remain unresolved in the current version; explicit analysis of variance (e.g., the [0.56, 0.61] F1 range) is essential before final acceptance.
