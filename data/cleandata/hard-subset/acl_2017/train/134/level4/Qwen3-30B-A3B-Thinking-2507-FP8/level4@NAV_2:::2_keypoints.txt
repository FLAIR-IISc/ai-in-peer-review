- Paper investigates neural end-to-end argumentation mining framed as token-based dependency parsing, token-based sequence tagging, and multi-task learning.  
- Main contributions: first neural end-to-end solutions for argumentation mining; showing sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms ILP; dependency parsing at token level is ineffective.  
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adapted LSTM-ER.  
- Sequence tagging models significantly outperform dependency parsing; best sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) attains roughly 55% paragraph-level global F1, outperforming the ILP baseline.  
- Multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task yields up to 5.5% absolute improvement in global F1, showing benefits of subtasks for sequence labeling.  
- Analysis explains failures of approaches (e.g., dependency parsing struggles on essays due to long sequences and complex trees).  
- Adapted LSTM-ER performs very well on paragraph level (reported ~73% C-F1 and ~45% R-F1), despite being an out-of-the-box adaptation.  
- Potential data leakage: train and test essays may share topics, which the paper does not acknowledge and could inflate performance estimates.  
- LSTM-ER result is mainly an application of an existing model rather than a novel methodological contribution to argumentation mining.  
- Explanations of how STagT and LSTM-ER “decouple” entity and relation information are unclear and insufficiently articulated.  
- Question: clarify paragraph-level evaluation’s relation to end-to-end task and whether models can handle cross-paragraph relations.  
- Question: how are non-tree argument structures (multiple roots) handled and what effect do they have on dependency parsing?  
- Question: why does essay-level performance drop (computational/memory constraints) and how does LSTM-ER compare between paragraph and essay levels?
