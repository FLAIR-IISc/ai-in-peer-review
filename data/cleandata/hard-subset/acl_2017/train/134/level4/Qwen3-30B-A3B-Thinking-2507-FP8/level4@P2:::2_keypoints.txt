- Evaluates four neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) for end-to-end argumentation mining.
- Shows sequence tagging with multi-task learning substantially outperforms dependency parsing and ILP-based state-of-the-art.
- Claims contributions: first neural end-to-end AM framework, sequence tagging captures long-range dependencies, and MTL with component detection improves performance.

- Strength: Thorough, systematic comparison identifying which neural choices work best and finding dependency parsing ineffective at token level.
- Strength: Neural methods beat ILP baseline, avoiding hand-crafted features and complex constraints.
- Strength: MTL experiments reveal component detection as a valuable auxiliary task while relation detection yields less benefit.
- Strength: Supplementary materials are detailed (training, hyperparameters, implementation), aiding reproducibility.
- Strength: Sequence tagging models (STagBLCC, STagBL) effectively handle long-range dependencies between argument components.

- Weakness: Potential topic overlap between train and test sets (student essays) not addressed, risking information leakage.
- Weakness: LSTM-ER use is largely out-of-the-box and treated as empirical success without clear novel insights from its adaptation.
- Weakness: Explanations are unclear about how sequence tagging “decouples” entity and relation information and how LSTM-ER’s modularity differs.
- Weakness: Paragraph- vs. essay-level comparison is confusing—unclear if paragraph-level is a different task or an evaluation shortcut.

- Q: Does paragraph-level evaluation represent a different task formulation or merely an implementation detail for efficiency?
- Q: What exactly does “decoupling” entity and relation information in LSTM-ER entail, and how does it differ from STagT’s MTL approach?
- Q: Why does LSTM-ER degrade more on essay-level data than sequence tagging—due to long-range dependency handling or increased entity counts?
