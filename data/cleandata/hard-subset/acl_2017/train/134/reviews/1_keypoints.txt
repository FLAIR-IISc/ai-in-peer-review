- Strength: Paper is well-written and easy to understand; methods and results are interesting.
- Weakness: Evaluation and obtained results may be problematic.
- Contribution: Proposes end-to-end argumentation mining using neural networks with two approaches (sequence labeling and dependency parsing) and a multitask learning variant.
- Motivation: Clearly explains avoiding ILP/manual feature engineering and pipeline error propagation via joint learning.
- Clarity: Methods are clearly explained except for a few missing details.
- Experiments: Substantial experiments and proper comparisons; results are interesting.
- Main concern: Very small dataset (≈320 training essays, ≈80 test essays); development set size not reported.
- Model-data mismatch: Large-capacity (Bi)LSTM models (BLC/BLCC) may be too big for the limited data, risking poor training.
- Data sparsity: Training tags likely only a few thousand versus hundreds of thousands/millions in standard sequence tasks.
- Overfitting analysis lacking: Paper does not analyze overfitting or provide training/development loss curves.
- Model stability: Need mean and standard deviation across multiple runs (different initializations) and statistical significance tests.
- Regularization: Paper omits discussion of regularization methods; supplementary material only briefly mentions LSTM-ER — needs proper treatment.
- Hyperparameter tuning: Recommend using Bayesian optimization instead of current method described in supplement.
- Move to main paper: Pre-trained word embeddings details and error analysis should be moved from supplementary material to the main paper.
- Annotation quality: Include inter-annotator agreement scores (from dataset paper) to contextualize system performance.
- Figures: Improve Figure 1 color/contrast for better black-and-white printing.
- Post-response request: Include F1-score ranges and report mean and variance for different settings; address high variance (e.g., Kiperwasser setting F1 in [0.56, 0.61]) to aid replication and assess stability.
