**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents an empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: as token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task sequence tagging, and joint entity-relation extraction using the Miwa and Bansal (2016) model. These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset of Stab and Gurevych (2016) at both the paragraph and full essay level. The main findings are that neural models, particularly a sequence tagging approach that encodes relational distance into the tagset and a joint entity-relation model, can outperform a feature-based ILP baseline, and that model effectiveness is highly dependent on document length.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A comprehensive empirical comparison of several distinct neural architectures for the end-to-end AM task on a standard benchmark. This is the first work to do so and provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling assumptions (e.g., local vs. global, coupled vs. decoupled).
2.  The introduction of a novel and effective sequence tagging formulation (STagT) that cleverly encodes component type, relational distance, and relation type into a single tag per token. This serves as a strong baseline for future work.
3.  A clear demonstration of the challenges posed by long-range dependencies in AM, highlighted by the performance discrepancies between paragraph-level and essay-level evaluations across different models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Timeliness and Scope:** The paper tackles a challenging structured prediction problem with a thorough exploration of modern neural techniques. Moving the field away from hand-crafted features and complex constraint-based systems like ILP is a significant and necessary direction. The breadth of models considered is commendable and provides a strong foundation for future research in this area.
2.  **Novelty of the STagT Formulation:** The proposal to frame end-to-end AM as a sequence tagging problem by encoding relational information (distance `d` and type `s`) directly into the tag vocabulary is simple yet powerful. This allows a standard, well-understood architecture like a BiLSTM-CRF to tackle a graph-prediction problem, and the results show this method to be surprisingly robust, especially at the essay level where other models struggle.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Granularity:** The decision to evaluate models at both the paragraph and essay level is a key strength. This analysis reveals critical differences in model behavior. For example, it exposes the brittleness of the LSTM-ER model's relation component on longer documents with a larger combinatorial space of potential relations, a crucial finding that would have been missed in a paragraph-only evaluation. This provides a clear lesson for the community about the importance of stress-testing models on longer sequences.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper successfully demonstrates that neural approaches can achieve new state-of-the-art results on this dataset, outperforming the previous ILP-based system without requiring domain-specific feature engineering or constraint design.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents interesting ideas and results, it is marred by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, technical omissions, and presentational flaws that undermine its contributions.

1.  **Ambiguity in the STagT Label Set:** The central sequence tagging model (STagT) relies on a label set Y defined in Equation (1). This definition includes a distance `d` which is, in principle, unbounded, leading to a potentially infinite label set. The authors dismiss this by noting that distances are finite in the dataset and that humans have finite memory. This is not a methodologically sound justification. Critical implementation details are missing: What was the maximum value of `d` included in the label vocabulary? How were components with relation distances exceeding this maximum handled during training and testing? This omission makes the core method irreproducible and its evaluation difficult to interpret.
2.  **Unclear and Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with the ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) is confusing. The authors state the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information," which suggests an advantage over the paragraph-level neural models. However, Table 1 presents a single set of scores for the ILP model without specifying whether it's an essay-level or paragraph-level result, making a direct comparison difficult. If the ILP model is indeed stronger due to its access to global context, the claim that neural models outperform it is weakened. A more rigorous and clearly delineated comparison is necessary.
3.  **Superficial Treatment of Dependency Parsing:** The dependency parsing experiments feel incomplete. The justification for only training on the paragraph level—that the essay level was "typically too memory-exhaustive"—is insufficient. This prevents a fair comparison with other models that were evaluated at the essay level. The hypothesis for the LSTM-Parser's poor performance on essays ("very long sequences") is stated without empirical support, such as an analysis correlating sequence length with accuracy degradation. Furthermore, the decision to "loss-lessly re-link" claims to a single major claim to enforce a tree structure is a significant data modification. This alters the original annotation semantics and is not "loss-less"; it is a simplification whose impact on the results is not discussed.
4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness that is concerning.
    *   **Figures:** Figure 2 is missing a label for the y-axis. Figure 4, which is critical for comparing LSTM-ER and STagBLCC, is poorly explained; the caption is uninformative, the method for calculating "prob. correct" is not given, and the x-axis uses absolute distance `|d|` while the text discusses signed distances.
    *   **References:** The reference list is exceptionally sloppy. There are numerous formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015", "Martnez Alonso Héctor"), incorrect use of "et al.", and a mix of citation styles. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier publication and reflects poorly on the overall quality of the submission.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact procedure for handling the distance `d` in the STagT label set? What was the maximum distance encoded, and how were instances with greater distances handled during training and inference?
2.  Regarding the ILP baseline in Table 1: are the reported scores from an essay-level or paragraph-level evaluation? Please clarify what "access to both paragraph and essay level information" entails and how this affects the fairness of the comparison.
3.  The claim that re-linking claims to a single major claim is "loss-less" seems to alter the problem definition. Could you justify this claim further and comment on whether this simplification might have disproportionately affected the dependency parsing models that rely on this artificial tree structure?
