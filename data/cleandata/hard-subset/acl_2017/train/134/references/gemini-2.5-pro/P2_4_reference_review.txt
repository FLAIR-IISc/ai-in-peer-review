### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents the first comprehensive, systematic investigation into applying various neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors critique prior state-of-the-art systems that rely on pipelines of individually trained models, hand-crafted features, and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for imposing structural constraints. To overcome these limitations, the paper frames the end-to-end AM problem in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as a complex sequence tagging problem, as a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of sequence tagging, and using an established joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus, demonstrating that several neural models outperform the feature-based ILP baseline.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **A comprehensive comparative study of different neural framings for end-to-end AM.** Rather than proposing a single new architecture, the authors provide a valuable service to the community by systematically exploring how different standard neural paradigms (parsing, tagging, joint modeling) can be adapted to this complex structured prediction task. This includes both successful and unsuccessful approaches (e.g., token-level parsing), which is itself an important finding.
2.  **An insightful analysis of model suitability based on document length and task complexity.** The paper's key finding is not just that neural models work well, but *which* models work well under specific conditions. The analysis of why the general LSTM-ER model excels on shorter texts (paragraphs) but fails on longer texts (essays) for relation extraction, while the more constrained sequence tagging model remains robust, is a significant and generalizable insight for relation extraction tasks beyond AM.
3.  **The establishment of a new, feature-free state-of-the-art on a standard AM benchmark.** By outperforming the strong ILP-based system from Stab and Gurevych (2016), the authors demonstrate the viability and superiority of end-to-end neural methods for this task, eliminating the need for brittle feature engineering and manual constraint design.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Problem Framing and Motivation.** The introduction clearly and convincingly articulates the weaknesses of previous pipeline and feature-engineered approaches to AM (error propagation, brittleness across datasets/theories, effort-intensive feature/constraint design). This provides a strong motivation for exploring truly end-to-end, representation-learning-based methods.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation.** The paper’s main strength lies in its breadth of experiments. The authors compare four distinct classes of models, including multiple parsers within the dependency parsing framework. The crucial decision to evaluate on both paragraph and full essay levels provides the basis for the paper's most interesting conclusions about model scalability and constraints. The analysis is supported by clear tables and an insightful figure (Figure 4) that visualizes the performance decay of different models over long-distance relations.
3.  **Clever and Effective Task Encoding.** The proposed sequence tagging formulation (STag) is a creative and effective way to linearize the structured prediction problem of AM. Encoding component type, BIO status, relation type, and relative distance to the relation head into a single, complex tag is an elegant solution that allows a standard BiLSTM-CRF architecture to tackle the full end-to-end task. While complex, it proves to be a robust and high-performing approach.
4.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion.** The authors do not simply report scores; they delve into *why* certain models perform better than others. The discussion on the trade-off between the generality of LSTM-ER (which considers all O(n^2) entity pairs) and the implicit constraints of the STag model (which allows each component to link to only one head) is particularly strong. This analysis provides a clear takeaway: for tasks with tree-like structures and many entities, a more constrained model may be preferable to a more general one to manage the search space.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty.** The paper's primary contribution is in the application, framing, and comparative analysis, not in the development of new neural architectures. All models used (Dyer et al.'s parser, Ma and Hovy's tagger, Miwa and Bansal's ER model, etc.) are existing, off-the-shelf systems. While this does not diminish the value of the study, the work should be recognized as a strong application paper rather than one introducing a novel machine learning method.
2.  **Conceptual Ambiguity in the STag Formulation.** The sequence tagging label Y = (b, t, d, s) includes `d`, the distance to the head component *measured in number of components*. This creates a conceptual circularity: at prediction time, the model must predict the distance to a head component before all component boundaries have been identified. The paper does not explain how this is resolved during decoding. Does the model learn to jointly predict boundaries and relative component indices? This seems like an exceptionally difficult learning task and a potential weakness of the formulation that warrants more detailed explanation.
3.  **Evaluation on a Single Dataset.** All experiments are performed on the PE corpus. While this is a key benchmark for this task, the paper’s claims about the general superiority of neural approaches and the specific trade-offs between models would be significantly strengthened if validated on at least one other AM dataset, perhaps from a different domain (e.g., legal texts, scientific articles) with potentially different structural properties. As it stands, the conclusions are strongly tied to the specific characteristics of student essays.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the learning and decoding process for the STag model, specifically concerning the distance `d`? How does the model predict the distance in *components* when the components themselves are an output of the sequence tagging process? Is this learned implicitly, or is there a specific mechanism to handle this during training or inference?
2.  The ILP baseline is described as having access to "both paragraph and essay level information." Could you elaborate on what this entails? Does this give the ILP model an advantage over the neural models, which are trained specifically on either paragraph- or essay-level inputs?
3.  Given the poor performance of token-level dependency parsing and the success of your sequence tagging models, do you see a potential for a hybrid approach? For instance, one could use a neural sequence tagger for component identification and typing (the 'C' part of your MTL task), and then use a graph-based neural model (like a dependency parser) to predict relations between the identified component spans. Would this offer a better balance of local and global modeling than the approaches tested?
