### Summary of the paper and contributions

This paper addresses the task of end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), aiming to identify argumentative components (premises, claims) and their relationships from raw text. The authors argue that prior state-of-the-art methods, which use pipelines of feature-based models combined with Integer Linear Programming (ILP), are brittle, require extensive feature engineering, and suffer from error propagation. To overcome this, the paper presents the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end approaches for this task. Four distinct neural framings are explored: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) a novel sequence tagging formulation where a single complex tag encodes component type, relation distance, and relation type, (3) a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of the sequence tagging model, and (4) an adaptation of a standard joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Experiments on the PE corpus show that the neural models, particularly the sequence tagging and joint entity-relation models, outperform the feature-based ILP baseline, and that the optimal framing depends on the complexity of the input (paragraph-level vs. full essay).

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive and systematic empirical comparison of different neural framings for end-to-end AM.** This is the primary contribution. Instead of proposing a single new architecture, the paper provides a valuable investigation into how to best formulate this complex structured prediction problem for modern neural networks. The insights derived from comparing these diverse approaches (parsing, tagging, joint modeling) are highly valuable for future research in this area.
2.  **A novel and effective formulation of end-to-end AM as a sequence tagging task.** The proposed `(b, t, d, s)` tagging scheme is a clever way to linearize the structured prediction problem, collapsing component segmentation, typing, and relation identification into a single sequence labeling task that can be effectively solved by a standard BiLSTM-CRF architecture.
3.  **Insightful analysis into the trade-offs between model complexity and task constraints.** The paper provides a clear analysis of why certain models fail, particularly the degradation of the generic LSTM-ER model on the full-essay task due to the combinatorial explosion of candidate relations, and the poor performance of token-level dependency parsing. This highlights the importance of incorporating appropriate structural biases into the model, a key takeaway for the field.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Well-Motivated Experimental Design:** The paper stands out for its systematic exploration of multiple, well-justified approaches. The authors don't just present a winning model; they investigate a range of plausible framings, from the theoretically appealing (dependency parsing) to the pragmatic (sequence tagging) and the standard (Miwa and Bansal's model). The inclusion of both paragraph-level and essay-level experiments provides a crucial dimension to the analysis, revealing important differences in model robustness. This comparative approach is far more illuminating than a paper focused on incremental SOTA gains with a single architecture.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The work successfully demonstrates that neural methods can outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline without manual feature design. The analysis of the results is a major strength. For example, the explanation for why the generic LSTM-ER model excels on the constrained paragraph-level task but fails on the essay-level relation identification task is insightful and convincing. The authors correctly identify that the model's generality becomes a weakness when the search space for relations becomes too large, whereas their sequence tagging formulation implicitly constrains this search space.
3.  **Novelty in Problem Formulation:** While the underlying neural components (BiLSTMs, CRFs, etc.) are standard, the novelty lies in how the AM problem is mapped onto them. The sequence tagging scheme is particularly creative. By encoding the distance `d` between components directly into the tag, the authors provide the model with a strong, local signal for learning relational structure, which proves highly effective and more robust on longer documents than more decoupled approaches.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivating the problem and explaining each of the proposed models and their underlying assumptions. The methodology is described in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is clear. The authors provide links to the software used, which aids reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on a Single Dataset and Annotation Scheme:** The experiments are conducted on a single corpus (PE), which has a specific, constrained argumentation structure (e.g., almost a tree, claims linking to all major claims). The success of the proposed methods, especially the single-head assumption implicit in the parsing and sequence tagging framings, may not generalize to other domains (e.g., legal or scientific texts) or annotation schemes that allow for more complex, graph-like structures (e.g., a premise supporting multiple claims). A discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Limited Novelty of Core Architectures:** As acknowledged by the authors, the work primarily adapts existing neural architectures (e.g., Ma and Hovy, 2016; Miwa and Bansal, 2016; Dyer et al., 2015) to the AM task. The contribution is in the novel application and framing, not in the development of new neural network components. While this is a valid and significant contribution, it could be seen as a limitation by some.
3.  **Unclear Handling of Out-of-Distribution Distances:** The sequence tagging model relies on encoding the distance `d` (in number of components) in the label set. It's unclear how the model handles distances at test time that were not observed during training. Is there a maximum distance threshold, beyond which all relations are mapped to a special tag? This could be a critical failure point for documents that are structured differently from the training set. A brief discussion of this practical implementation detail and its implications would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The sequence tagging formulation with distance `d` is very compelling. However, how does the model handle distances `d` at test time that were not seen during training? Is there a maximum distance cutoff, with all longer distances mapped to a special symbol? How would this approach scale to corpora with a much wider distribution of component-to-component distances?
2.  Your analysis shows that the STagBLCC model is more robust than LSTM-ER on the full-essay task because it implicitly constrains each component to have only one head. This works well for the tree-like structures in the PE corpus. How do you foresee this model adapting to argumentation graphs where a single premise might support or attack multiple different components? Would this require a fundamental change to the tagging scheme?
