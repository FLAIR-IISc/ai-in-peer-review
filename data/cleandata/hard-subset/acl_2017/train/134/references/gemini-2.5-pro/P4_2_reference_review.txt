### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural network architectures for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to identify argumentative components (Claims, Premises), their types, and their relationships (Support, Attack) directly from raw text. The authors frame this complex task in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2.1) as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, composite tagging scheme that encodes component boundaries, type, relation distance, and relation type, (2.2) as a multi-task learning (MTL) version of the sequence tagging problem, and (3) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These neural approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) model, which represents the previous state-of-the-art. The experiments show that neural models, particularly the sequence tagging and joint entity-relation architectures, outperform the ILP baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art for the task without requiring hand-crafted features or constraints.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A systematic and comprehensive comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM.** The paper's primary contribution is not a single novel architecture but a rigorous investigation into how different classes of existing neural models can be adapted to solve this complex structured prediction task. It introduces novel and plausible problem formulations, such as the token-level dependency parsing and the composite sequence tagging scheme, and evaluates them thoroughly. This provides a valuable benchmark and set of insights for future research in this area.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on a standard AM benchmark and insightful analysis of model performance.** The work demonstrates that feature-free neural methods can surpass a strong, feature-engineered ILP system on the PE dataset. The analysis provides clear explanations for why certain models excel or falter under different conditions (e.g., paragraph-level vs. essay-level inputs), highlighting the trade-offs between local and global models and the challenges posed by long-range dependencies and combinatorial complexity in longer documents.

### Strengths
1.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's core strength is its extensive and well-designed experimental setup. By framing the problem in multiple distinct ways and testing several models within those framings (e.g., four different parsers), the authors provide a robust comparative analysis. The decision to evaluate on both short (paragraph) and long (essay) documents is particularly effective, as it reveals critical differences in model behavior and robustness that would otherwise be missed.
2.  **Strong Baseline and Significant Results:** The comparison is anchored by a highly relevant and strong baseline—the ILP model of Stab and Gurevych (2016), which was the state-of-the-art system for this dataset. The fact that the proposed neural models, particularly LSTM-ER (on paragraphs) and STagBLCC (on essays), achieve superior performance is a significant result. It provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of end-to-end neural learning for this task, moving the field beyond dependency on manual feature engineering.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The authors provide clear and well-reasoned explanations for their results. For example, the analysis in Section 5d explaining LSTM-ER's performance degradation on essay-level relation extraction is particularly convincing. The authors correctly identify that the model's generality becomes a weakness when faced with a quadratic explosion of potential entity pairs in long texts, whereas the more constrained sequence tagging model is more robust. This level of analysis elevates the paper from a simple leaderboard report to a valuable scientific contribution.
4.  **Novelty in Problem Formulation:** While the underlying neural components are not new, their application and framing for AM are. The sequence tagging formulation, which collapses component segmentation, typing, and relation identification into a single tag per token using a distance-based pointer, is a creative and effective method. The exploration of token-level dependency parsing, while ultimately less successful, is also a valuable contribution as it tests a natural hypothesis about capturing the tree-like structure of arguments.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Unclear Comparison in the Multi-Task Learning (MTL) Setup:** The MTL experiments in Section 5c are based on a "weaker" sequence tagger (STagBL) that lacks the CNN and CRF layers of the main tagger (STagBLCC). This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of the MTL framework. The performance gains shown in Table 2 are relative to this weaker baseline, and it remains unclear how MTL would affect the performance of the stronger STagBLCC model. To make a clearer claim about the benefits of MTL, the experiments should ideally have been performed using the same base architecture as in the single-task setting.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Error Analysis for Pipelined Components:** The LSTM-ER model performs entity detection and relation classification in a decoupled (though jointly trained) manner. This means errors from the entity stage necessarily impact the relation stage. While the paper acknowledges this, the analysis could be strengthened by reporting relation F1 scores on gold-standard components. Such an oracle experiment would disentangle the performance of the relation module from the entity module and provide a clearer picture of whether its struggles on the essay level are due to poor entity recognition or an inherent inability of the relation classifier to handle a large number of candidates.
3.  **The Conclusion on Dependency Parsing May Be Overly Broad:** The paper concludes that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is "ineffective." While the results support this conclusion for the specific token-level formulation tested, this may not hold for all parsing-based approaches. The chosen representation creates very long dependencies and a complex label set, which could be the primary cause of failure. The conclusion could be moderated to specify that the *token-level* parsing formulation was ineffective, leaving open the possibility that other parsing framings (e.g., at the clause or component level) might be more viable.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you clarify the rationale for using the simpler STagBL model as the basis for the MTL experiments instead of the stronger STagBLCC model? Would you expect the performance gains from MTL to be additive to the gains from the CNN and CRF layers, or are there reasons to believe they might not combine effectively?
2.  In your sequence tagging formulation (STagT), the label encodes a distance `d` to the target component. During decoding, how is this distance used to identify the specific component being linked to? For example, is it a simple matter of counting `d` previously-identified components backward or forward? A brief explanation of this decoding process would improve clarity.
3.  For the LSTM-ER model, would it be possible to provide relation extraction performance on gold-standard components? This would help clarify whether its poor performance on essay-level relations is primarily due to errors propagating from the entity recognition stage or a weakness in the relation classification module itself when faced with a large number of candidate pairs.
