### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to move beyond previous pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for constraint satisfaction. The authors explore four distinct neural framings of the end-to-end AM task: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem; (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, complex tag set that encodes component boundaries, types, and relation information (including the distance to the related component); (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem where subtasks are learned jointly; and (4) using a standard joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, comparing performance on both paragraph-level and full essay-level tasks. The key finding is that a relatively simple BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagger with the proposed complex tag set outperforms dependency parsing models, the more general LSTM-ER model on full essays, and the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based model, thereby establishing a new SOTA on this dataset.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of different neural framings for end-to-end Argumentation Mining.** The paper's primary contribution is its systematic investigation of multiple, conceptually different ways to model the full AM pipeline with neural networks. By comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, and a standard joint model, the authors provide a valuable "lay of the land" that highlights the strengths and weaknesses of each approach for this specific structured prediction task. This comparative analysis is more valuable than the proposal of a single model.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for joint component and relation extraction.** The proposal to encode component boundaries (B, I, O), component type (P, C, MC), relation distance (`d`), and relation type (Support, Attack) into a single tag is a simple yet powerful idea. This formulation elegantly linearizes the structured prediction problem and, as the results show, effectively captures the inductive bias of the dataset (i.e., that related components are often textually close), leading to robust performance, especially on longer documents.
3.  **Insightful analysis of model performance, particularly the trade-off between model generality and task-specific constraints.** The paper provides an excellent analysis of *why* certain models fail. The discussion on the LSTM-ER model's performance degradation on long essays due to a combinatorial explosion of candidate relations, contrasted with the more constrained search space of the sequence tagging model, is a key insight. This is well-supported by the analysis in Figure 4 and provides a clear lesson for future work on similar tasks.

### Strengths
1.  **Thorough and well-designed experiments.** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation. They compare against multiple strong baselines, including four different dependency parsers (both feature-based and neural) and a strong, non-neural SOTA model (ILP). The decision to evaluate on both paragraph-level and essay-level data is crucial, as it clearly exposes the scalability and robustness issues of different models (especially LSTM-ER and the dependency parsers).
2.  **Significant performance improvements over prior art.** The paper demonstrates that its best-performing neural models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER on the paragraph level) substantially outperform the feature-based ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016). This is a strong result, as it shows that end-to-end neural models can learn the necessary structural constraints implicitly, without the need for manual feature engineering or explicit constraint design, which is a major advantage.
3.  **Clarity of writing and presentation.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the different model framings are explained concisely and effectively (e.g., Figure 3 for the dependency parsing view), and the experimental results are presented with insightful discussion. The analysis is not superficial; it delves into the reasons behind the observed performance differences.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The novelty is primarily in the problem formulation, not the neural architectures.** The paper applies existing, well-known neural models (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, Dyer et al.'s parser, Miwa & Bansal's model) to the AM task. The main technical novelty lies in the design of the tag set for the sequence tagging approach (`STagT`). While this formulation is clever and effective, the work is more of an excellent application and analysis paper than one that introduces a new fundamental model architecture.
2.  **Findings may be highly specific to the PE dataset's structure.** The success of the distance-based encoding `d` in the `STagT` model relies heavily on the specific characteristics of the PE dataset, where argument structures are nearly trees and most relations are relatively local (as shown in Figure 2). The authors acknowledge this structure but could discuss in more detail how this approach might fail or need adaptation for datasets with more complex, graph-like structures or very long-range dependencies (e.g., in legal texts or online debates). The strong performance of this specific encoding might not generalize well to other AM corpora.
3.  **Practical details of the complex tag set are omitted.** The proposed `(b, t, d, s)` tag creates a potentially very large and sparse label space. The paper does not specify how this is handled in practice. For instance, is there a maximum absolute value for the distance `d`? What happens if a distance greater than any seen in the training data is required at test time? A large label set can make the final softmax layer computationally expensive and difficult to train. A brief discussion of these practical implementation choices would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the sequence tagging formulation (`STagT`), could you clarify the practical implementation of the label set? What is the total size of the label set `Y` used in your experiments? Did you impose a maximum distance `d_max` and group all distances `|d| > d_max` into a single label? How does the model handle cases at inference time where the predicted head component is at a distance not seen during training?
2.  Your best-performing model on full essays, STagBLCC, seems to benefit greatly from the explicit distance encoding `d`, which captures a structural bias of the PE dataset. How do you expect this approach would fare on argumentation structures that are less tree-like and have more non-local or crossing dependencies? Would a more general model like LSTM-ER (perhaps with a distance-based attention mechanism) or a graph-based neural model be more suitable in those scenarios?
