**Summary of the paper and its contributions**

This paper investigates the application of neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—which includes identifying and typing argumentative components and the relations between them—in four different ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as sequence tagging with a complex label set, as a multi-task learning problem, and by applying an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compared against the feature-based ILP model from the same work. The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  A systematic empirical comparison of several neural network architectures for end-to-end AM on a standard benchmark, providing valuable insights into the efficacy of different modeling choices.
2.  A demonstration that neural models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-based sequence tagger, can achieve state-of-the-art results, surpassing a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline without the need for manual feature design.
3.  An insightful analysis of how model performance varies with input length (paragraph vs. essay level), highlighting a key challenge for scaling AM systems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors should be commended for conducting a broad empirical study. Instead of proposing a single model, they explore multiple distinct "framings" of the problem. This comparative approach is methodologically sound and provides the community with a much richer understanding of the problem space than a paper focused on a single architecture would. The comparison against several parsers and the established ILP model provides a solid grounding for the results.
2.  **Strong Baseline and Significant Results:** The paper compares against the ILP model of Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is the state-of-the-art system on this specific dataset and represents a well-established paradigm for structured prediction. Demonstrating a clear performance improvement over this complex, feature-rich baseline is a significant achievement and provides strong evidence that end-to-end neural methods are a viable and powerful alternative for this task.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Behavior:** The analysis presented in Section 5 is clear and well-reasoned. The discussion of why dependency parsing fails at the token level, the comparison of paragraph-level versus essay-level performance, and the diagnosis of LSTM-ER's shortcomings on long documents (Figure 4) are particularly insightful. This level of analysis elevates the paper from a simple leaderboard-climbing exercise to a useful scientific contribution that can guide future work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Generalizability is Unproven and Overclaimed:** The most significant weakness of this work is that all experiments are conducted on a single dataset (the PE corpus). This dataset has a highly regular, almost-tree-like structure, which the authors acknowledge. The central motivation of the paper is to develop methods that avoid the brittleness of hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which need to be redesigned for new datasets. However, without testing on any other AM corpus (e.g., those with graph structures or different component types), the claim that these neural methods are more general or portable is entirely unsubstantiated. The success of the proposed models, especially the STag approach, may be highly contingent on the specific structural properties of the PE dataset.
2.  **Novelty of the Proposed Methods is Limited:** The paper frames its contribution as exploring novel "framings" of AM. However, the methods themselves are direct applications of well-established techniques from the NLP literature. Using dependency parsers, sequence taggers (BiLSTM-CRF), MTL, or the Miwa and Bansal (2016) model for a structured prediction task is standard practice. While the encoding scheme for the sequence tagging model is clever, it does not represent a fundamental methodological advance. The paper's strength is its empirical contribution, not its novelty in model architecture, and this should be positioned more clearly.
3.  **Implicit Hand-Crafting of Constraints:** The paper argues that it moves away from hand-crafting constraints as required by ILP models. However, the best-performing STag model achieves its success on long documents by encoding a very strong structural constraint directly into its label set: `(b, t, d, s)`. This formulation implicitly assumes that each argumentative component has exactly one outgoing relation, which happens to align well with the structure of the PE dataset where "each premise relates to exactly one claim or premise." This is not merely a "bias" but a hard constraint imposed by the problem formulation itself. This feels less like eliminating hand-crafted constraints and more like moving them from a separate ILP module into the tag design. This crucial design choice, and its potential limitations on datasets with different structures, is not adequately discussed. The comparison with the more general LSTM-ER model, which fails precisely where this constraint is absent, makes this point even more critical.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The success of the STag model appears heavily tied to its implicit "one-outgoing-link" constraint, which matches the PE dataset's structure. How do you foresee this model performing on datasets with more complex, non-tree argument graphs, where a single premise might support multiple claims? Does this not re-introduce the problem of task-specific design, simply shifting the "hand-crafting" from ILP constraints to the label encoding scheme?
2.  The primary motivation stated is to build models that are more easily adaptable to new AM datasets and theories. Given that the study is confined to a single dataset, what is the concrete evidence supporting the claim that the proposed neural approaches, particularly STag, are more portable than the ILP baseline?
3.  The multi-task learning experiments show modest improvements over a baseline BiLSTM tagger (STagBL) that is weaker than your best single-task model (STagBLCC). Have you experimented with applying the MTL setup on top of the stronger STagBLCC architecture? This would clarify whether the gains from MTL are additive to those from a more powerful base model (i.e., one with a CRF layer and character CNNs).
