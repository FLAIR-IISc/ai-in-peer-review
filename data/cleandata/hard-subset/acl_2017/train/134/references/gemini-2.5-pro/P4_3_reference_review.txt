### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative components (e.g., claims, premises), their types, and the relations between them from unstructured text. The authors propose and systematically evaluate several neural network-based approaches, framing the task in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as a complex sequence tagging problem, as a multi-task learning problem, and using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). The experiments are conducted on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. The results show that the proposed neural models, particularly a sequence tagging model with a custom tag set and a joint entity-relation model, outperform a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, thereby establishing a new state-of-the-art for this task.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A comprehensive empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM. This is presented as the first systematic study of its kind and provides valuable insights into the suitability of different neural architectures (parsing vs. tagging) for this structured prediction task.
2.  A novel sequence tagging formulation for end-to-end AM that encodes component boundaries (BIO), component type, relation distance, and relation type into a single, complex label. This method proves to be a robust and effective way to jointly model all subtasks.
3.  An insightful analysis demonstrating how model performance varies with input length (paragraph vs. essay level). The findings show that while a general joint model like LSTM-ER excels on shorter texts, a more constrained model like the proposed sequence tagger is more robust on longer documents with more complex relational structures.

### Strengths

1.  **Systematic and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its thorough investigation of multiple, conceptually different neural approaches to a single, complex problem. Instead of proposing only one model, the authors implement and compare four distinct framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a pre-existing joint model. This comparative analysis, using various parsers and taggers (Table 1), provides a clear and valuable overview of the landscape of neural solutions for end-to-end AM. This is far more instructive for the community than a paper focused on a single architecture.

2.  **Novel and Effective Problem Formulation:** The framing of AM as a sequence tagging problem (`STagT`) is a significant methodological contribution. The design of the four-tuple label `(b, t, d, s)` is a clever way to linearize a graph-structured problem, allowing a powerful-yet-standard sequence model like a BiLSTM-CRF to tackle all AM subtasks simultaneously. This approach effectively captures local component information as well as non-local relational information (via the distance `d`) in a single pass. The strong performance of `STagBLCC` demonstrates the viability of this formulation.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Insightful Analysis:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed neural models significantly outperform a strong, feature-rich ILP baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset. The analysis goes beyond simply reporting scores. For instance, the discussion on why LSTM-ER's performance on relation identification degrades sharply on the essay level (due to the quadratic explosion of potential relations) compared to the more constrained `STagBLCC` model is particularly insightful. This is well-supported by the evidence in Figure 4, which shows `STagBLCC` is more effective at modeling longer-range dependencies. This analysis provides clear takeaways about the trade-offs between model flexibility and structural constraints based on text length.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The different framings are explained with clear examples (e.g., the tag sequence for the example sentence in §4, the dependency tree in Figure 3). The distinction between the models in terms of their modularity and implicit constraints is well-articulated, making the motivation behind the experiments transparent.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Evaluation on a Single Dataset:** The experiments and conclusions are based entirely on one dataset (PE essays). The authors themselves note in the introduction that AM tasks and annotation schemes can vary significantly across datasets and domains. While the PE dataset is appropriate for this end-to-end task, the lack of evaluation on any other corpus limits the generalizability of the findings. It is unclear if the superiority of the sequence tagging approach on longer documents, for example, would hold in a different domain (e.g., legal or scientific texts) where argumentation structures might differ.

2.  **Sub-optimal Performance of the Dependency Parsing Framing:** A substantial portion of the paper is dedicated to the dependency parsing framing, but all tested parsers perform significantly worse than the tagging-based models. The token-level tree representation proposed (Figure 3) seems somewhat arbitrary (e.g., all tokens in a component pointing to the *first* token of the head component), and it is unclear if alternative, potentially more effective, tree representations were explored. As presented, this framing serves more as a negative result, and its prominence in the paper may overstate its contribution as a viable alternative.

3.  **Use of Off-the-Shelf Architectures:** The novelty of the paper lies in the application and framing of AM, not in the neural architectures themselves. The models used (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF-CNN from Ma and Hovy (2016), LSTM-ER from Miwa and Bansal (2016), LSTM-Parser from Dyer et al. (2015)) are existing architectures applied to a new task. While this is a perfectly valid and useful form of contribution, the work does not introduce new model components or fundamental deep learning techniques.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In the sequence tagging formulation (`STagT`), the label for a token includes the distance `d` (in number of components) to its head component. During inference, the model must predict this label for each token sequentially without knowing the gold segmentation of components. How is this distance `d` handled at test time? Is it predicted relative to previously predicted components in the same sequence? If so, does this not risk significant error propagation if the initial component predictions are incorrect?

2.  Regarding the dependency parsing framing, could you clarify the motivation for designing the tree structure such that all tokens in a component point to the first token of the head component? Did you experiment with other representations, for instance, a two-stage approach where components are first identified and then parsed, or a different token-level linking scheme? Given the poor performance, it would be useful to understand if this specific representation is a key limiting factor.
