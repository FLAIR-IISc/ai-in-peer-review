### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical investigation into neural end-to-end models for argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and joint entity-relation extraction. These neural approaches are systematically evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The key finding is that neural models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN with a novel, structure-encoding tagging scheme and an existing joint entity-relation model, outperform the prior state-of-the-art without requiring manual feature engineering.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A comprehensive and systematic benchmark of neural models for end-to-end AM.** This is the first work to explore and rigorously compare multiple neural paradigms for this complex structured prediction task. The analysis across different framings and against a strong non-neural baseline provides the community with a valuable understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
2.  **An insightful analysis of how model constraints interact with text length.** A key contribution is the finding that more general models (like LSTM-ER) excel on short texts (paragraphs) but degrade significantly on longer texts (essays) for relation extraction, while a more constrained sequence tagging model proves more robust. This highlights a critical trade-off between model expressiveness and inductive bias that is relevant beyond argumentation mining.
3.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging scheme for structured prediction.** The proposed `(b, t, d, s)` tag, which encodes component boundaries, type, relation distance, and relation type into a single label, is a creative and effective method for mapping a graph prediction problem onto a sequence labeling task. The strong performance of this relatively simple framing is a significant result.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The paper stands out for its methodical and comprehensive evaluation. Rather than proposing a single model, it compares four distinct neural paradigms. The inclusion of multiple parsers, different tagging architectures, and a strong ILP baseline makes the empirical results highly convincing. Furthermore, the decision to evaluate on both paragraph and essay levels is crucial, as it uncovers important differences in model performance and leads to one of the paper's key insights.
2.  **Clear and Insightful Analysis:** The authors provide excellent analysis and compelling explanations for their results. For instance, the discussion of why the LSTM-Parser outperforms other parsers (due to its encoding of the full stack history) is plausible. The analysis of the performance gap between LSTM-ER and STagBLCC on essay-length texts is particularly strong, correctly identifying the combinatorial explosion of potential relations as the likely cause of LSTM-ER's failure. This claim is well-supported by the evidence presented in Figure 4, which clearly shows STagBLCC's superior ability to model long-range dependencies.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Results and Practical Significance:** The paper demonstrates that neural end-to-end models can significantly outperform a complex, feature-engineered ILP system. This is a strong argument for shifting to neural approaches in this domain, as they eliminate the costly process of designing features and constraints. The fact that a well-established architecture (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) paired with a clever tagging scheme achieves SOTA performance offers a valuable and practical path forward for researchers in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The primary contribution of the paper lies in its novel framings of the AM problem and its thorough empirical analysis, rather than in the development of new neural architectures. The models used are largely off-the-shelf implementations of prior work (Ma and Hovy, 2016; Miwa and Bansal, 2016; Dyer et al., 2015). While the proposed `(b, t, d, s)` tagging scheme is creative, the paper would have been stronger if it had proposed a model architecture more specifically tailored to the unique challenges of argumentation structure.
2.  **Generalizability of the Sequence Tagging Scheme:** The proposed sequence tagging formulation, while effective here, has potential limitations.
    *   **Scalability:** The size of the label set is dependent on the maximum component-to-component distance `d`. This works for the PE dataset where dependencies are relatively local, but it may not scale to other domains where relations span much larger distances, potentially leading to a very large and sparse label space.
    *   **Expressiveness:** The scheme inherently constrains each argumentative component to link to exactly one other component. This matches the (mostly) tree-like structure of the PE dataset but cannot model non-tree argumentation graphs (e.g., a premise supporting two different claims), which are common in other AM datasets and theories. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge that this strong inductive bias limits the model's applicability to datasets with different structural properties.
3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison in the MTL Experiments:** The multi-task learning (MTL) experiments are conducted using a `STagBL` model, which is weaker than the main `STagBLCC` sequence tagger (it lacks the CRF and character-level CNN components). While the results demonstrate that MTL improves the performance of `STagBL`, it is unclear if these gains would transfer to the already stronger `STagBLCC` model. A more compelling experiment would have been to apply the same MTL setup to the `STagBLCC` architecture to see if auxiliary tasks can provide further improvements over the best single-task model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The results suggest a trade-off: LSTM-ER has superior component detection, while your STagBLCC model has more robust relation detection on long texts due to its structural constraints. Have you considered a hybrid approach that might get the best of both worlds? For example, one could use a two-stage model where a high-performing component detector (like that from LSTM-ER) first identifies candidate components, and then a second model predicts relations between them, perhaps using a simplified version of your distance-based tagging scheme.
2.  The sequence tag `(b, t, d, s)` has a distance component `d` measured in the number of components. How is this distance calculated during inference when the ground-truth component segmentation is not known? Does the model predict a distance to a component that it is simultaneously in the process of identifying? A little more detail on the mechanics of predicting `d` would be helpful.
3.  Your proposed `STagT` scheme constrains each premise to have exactly one outgoing link. The PE dataset largely conforms to this, but not perfectly ("each premise relates to *exactly one* claim or premise" vs. "the argument structure ... is—*almost*—a tree"). How does your model handle cases in the training data that violate this one-parent constraint, and how does this hard constraint affect its ability to model the small number of exceptions present in the data?
