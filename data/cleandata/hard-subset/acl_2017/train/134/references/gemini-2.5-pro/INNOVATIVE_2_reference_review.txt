### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive exploration of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors move beyond previous pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) by reframing the full AM task in four distinct ways suitable for neural architectures: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem where labels encode component type, boundaries, and relational information (distance and type), (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) version of the sequence tagging problem, and (4) by adapting a state-of-the-art joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). Through experiments on a standard persuasive essay corpus, the authors demonstrate that their neural approaches, particularly the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER models, significantly outperform the feature-based ILP baseline. The paper provides a valuable analysis of the trade-offs between these different framings, concluding that simpler, more constrained models are more robust for longer documents with many potential relations.

### **Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the novel and bold conceptualization of the end-to-end AM task through different structured prediction lenses. Reframing a complex graph-prediction problem as either token-level dependency parsing or a carefully designed sequence tagging scheme represents a significant and creative leap. This exploration of how to represent the problem for neural models is more valuable than a mere application of an existing architecture.
2.  A strong secondary contribution is the insightful empirical analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each framing. The paper provides a clear explanation for why the general LSTM-ER model excels on shorter texts but fails on longer ones, while the more constrained sequence tagging model remains robust. This analysis offers valuable "policy recommendations" for future work and provides genuine scientific understanding that goes beyond simply reporting a new state-of-the-art score.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Ambition:** This work is a perfect example of the kind of ambitious, exploratory research the field needs. Instead of an incremental improvement, the authors tackle the problem from several fundamentally new angles. The idea of encoding relational distance directly into a sequence tag is both elegant and effective. The token-level dependency parsing framing, even though it proved less successful, is a creative and bold hypothesis worth testing. This willingness to explore entirely new problem representations is the paper's greatest strength.
2.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Constraints:** The paper excels in its analysis of *why* different models perform the way they do. The discussion surrounding the LSTM-ER model's performance degradation on the essay level is particularly compelling. The authors correctly identify that its generality—considering all O(m^2) pairwise relations—becomes a curse on longer documents, whereas their sequence tagging approach implicitly imposes a stronger structural prior (each component has one outgoing link). This level of analysis provides lasting insights for researchers working on relation extraction in long-form text.
3.  **Value of Negative Results:** The clear demonstration that framing end-to-end AM as token-level dependency parsing is ineffective is a significant finding in its own right. By thoroughly investigating and reporting this negative result, the authors save other researchers from pursuing a potentially unfruitful direction and contribute to a more robust understanding of the problem space.
4.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper successfully moves the state of the art forward. The proposed neural models decisively outperform the previous, highly complex feature-engineered ILP system, demonstrating that end-to-end neural learning is not only conceptually simpler but also practically superior for this task.

### **Weaknesses**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where it could be improved. As my reviewer philosophy prioritizes bold ideas, I view these as minor points rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Lack of a Unified Model:** The paper presents a "menu" of options with different trade-offs ("policy recommendations") rather than a single, unified model that captures the best of all worlds. For instance, the analysis suggests that LSTM-ER is great at component identification and STag is better at constrained relation prediction. This begs the question: could a hybrid model be developed that uses a more powerful component tagger but incorporates the distance-based constraints from the STag model for relation prediction?
2.  **Superficial Multi-Task Learning Analysis:** The MTL experiments confirm that auxiliary tasks are helpful, which is a nice finding. However, the analysis feels a bit preliminary. The paper notes that its findings on layer sharing contradict previous work (Søgaard and Goldberg, 2016) but doesn't offer a deep hypothesis as to why this might be the case for the AM task specifically. A more detailed exploration would have strengthened this contribution.
3.  **Potentially Premature Dismissal of Parsing:** The authors attribute the failure of the dependency parsing approach to the huge search space in long documents. This is a reasonable conclusion. However, it's unclear if this is a fundamental flaw of the *framing* or of the specific *parsers* used, which may not be optimized for such long sequences. A brief discussion of whether alternative parsing algorithms (e.g., those using recent advances for long-range dependencies) could potentially mitigate these issues would have made the negative result even more conclusive.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The sequence tagging label `(b, t, d, s)` is a very clever encoding. However, the distance `d` is defined in terms of argumentative components, which are themselves an output of the prediction process. Could you elaborate on how this is handled at inference time? Is the model learning to simultaneously segment components and predict the relative index of the target component it should link to, even as that target component is also being identified? A little more detail on the decoding logic would be very helpful.
2.  In your analysis of the LSTM-ER model's poor performance on essay-level relation finding, you rightly point to the combinatorial explosion of candidate pairs. Do you think this could be addressed by adding a pruning step, where only pairs within a certain token or component distance are considered for relation classification? This seems like a simple heuristic that might combine the power of LSTM-ER's decoupled architecture with the locality bias that makes your STag model so effective.
