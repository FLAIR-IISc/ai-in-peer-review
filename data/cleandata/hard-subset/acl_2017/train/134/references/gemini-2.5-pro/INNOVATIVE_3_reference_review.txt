**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). Moving away from prior state-of-the-art systems that rely on multi-stage pipelines, hand-crafted features, and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for joint decoding, this work explores several ways to frame the full AM task as a single, end-to-end learning problem. The authors investigate four distinct conceptualizations: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) a novel sequence tagging scheme that encodes relational information (distance and type) directly into the tags, (3) a multi-task learning setup using the sequence tagging framework, and (4) the application of a state-of-the-art joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on a standard dataset of persuasive essays, demonstrating that their best neural models outperform the previous feature-based ILP SOTA, particularly the sequence tagging approach which proves to be robust.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the conceptual exploration of reframing argumentation mining, a complex structured prediction task, into several distinct neural end-to-end paradigms. The willingness to test multiple bold hypotheses—especially the novel sequence tagging scheme that flattens a relational structure into a sequence, and the ambitious token-level dependency parsing approach—provides significant value to the community, even where some approaches prove less effective.
2.  A secondary, but important, contribution is the strong empirical result showing that feature-free neural models can significantly outperform a complex, feature-engineered ILP-based system on a challenging end-to-end AM task. This work effectively establishes a new, more promising direction for research in this area.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Boldness:** This work is a pioneer in applying end-to-end neural methods to this specific AM task. The authors should be commended for their ambitious approach. Instead of merely adapting a single existing model, they fundamentally rethink the problem from several different angles. The exploration of dependency parsing at the token level, while ultimately not the best performing, is exactly the kind of creative, high-risk exploration that pushes the field forward. The sequence tagging formulation is particularly clever in its attempt to encode relational distance, offering a novel way to handle structured prediction in a sequential model.
2.  **Insightful and Thorough Comparative Analysis:** The paper's main strength lies in its analysis of the *trade-offs* between the different framings. The discussion of why the highly general LSTM-ER model excels on short paragraphs but degrades catastrophically on longer essays (due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations) is contrasted brilliantly with the robustness of the more constrained sequence tagging model. This provides a clear and valuable lesson about the importance of inductive bias: the STag model's explicit encoding of distance biases it towards the local dependencies common in the data, making it more resilient on longer texts. This level of analysis is far more valuable than a simple leaderboard of F1 scores.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results Establishing a New Baseline:** The paper successfully demonstrates that its best models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER on paragraphs) surpass the previous state-of-the-art. This is a crucial result, as it validates the shift away from laborious feature engineering and complex ILP constraints towards end-to-end deep learning for this task. The paper provides a solid foundation and a new set of strong baselines for all future work in this area.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Generalizability Constrained by Dataset-Specific Assumptions:** The paper's most significant weakness is its reliance on the structural properties of the PE dataset. This dataset features an almost-tree-like structure where each premise links to exactly one other component. Both the dependency parsing and the sequence tagging models bake this "single parent" assumption directly into their design. The proposed STag label `(b, t, d, s)` can, by its nature, only encode a single outgoing relation from any given component. It is highly unclear how these frameworks would perform, or even be adapted, for argumentation datasets with more general graph structures (e.g., where one premise supports two different claims). While a valid simplification for this study, it significantly limits the claimed generality of the proposed methods.
2.  **Unclear Decoding Mechanism for the Sequence Tagger:** The paper's description of the STag model is conceptually elegant but lacks crucial implementation details. The distance `d` is defined as the number of *components* between a source and a target. However, the model predicts a tag for each *token*. It is not explained how the model can predict a component-level distance at the token-level before the components themselves have been fully identified. This seems to imply a non-trivial decoding step (e.g., a two-pass process or beam search that considers component boundaries) which is not discussed. This methodological ambiguity makes the results for the STag models slightly harder to interpret and reproduce.
3.  **The Failure of the Parsing Model:** While I value the exploration, the dependency parsing approach performs very poorly, especially on the full essay level. The performance drop is so severe that it seems less like a promising direction and more like a conclusively negative result for this framing on realistic texts. The analysis correctly identifies sequence length as a likely culprit, but this weakness is so profound that it might have been better to frame this part of the investigation more explicitly as a negative result that warns against this specific type of token-level graph modeling for long-form text.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the decoding process for the STag model? How do you resolve the token-level predictions of the component-level distance `d` to form a coherent link between two multi-token spans? For instance, if different tokens within a single predicted premise have conflicting `d` values, how is a single target component chosen?
2.  Given that your best-performing models (STag and parsing) are tailored to the single-parent structure of the PE dataset, what are your thoughts on adapting these frameworks to datasets with more complex, non-tree argumentation graphs? Would the STag approach require a completely new tagset design, perhaps allowing for a list of distances, or would a different paradigm be necessary altogether?
