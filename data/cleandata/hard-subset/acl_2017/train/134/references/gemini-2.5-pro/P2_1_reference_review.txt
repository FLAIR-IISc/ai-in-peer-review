### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the end-to-end task—which includes identifying argument components (e.g., claims, premises), their types, and the relations between them—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem where relational information (distance and type) is encoded into the tag, (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of the sequence tagging approach, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are systematically evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The key findings are that neural models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger and the joint entity-relation model, outperform the ILP baseline, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. The paper provides insightful analysis, showing that the sequence tagging framing is more robust for this task than dependency parsing, and that the performance of different models depends heavily on document length and the resulting complexity of the relational search space.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Systematic and Comparative Study of Neural Framings for End-to-End AM:** The paper's primary contribution is not the proposal of a single novel architecture, but rather a thorough investigation into *how to frame* a complex, structured prediction task for modern neural networks. By comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and joint entity-relation modeling, the authors provide valuable insights into the architectural biases and trade-offs inherent in each approach. This comparative analysis is highly valuable for researchers tackling similar problems in discourse analysis and information extraction.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Analysis on a Key AM Benchmark:** The paper establishes new state-of-the-art results for end-to-end AM on the PE dataset. More importantly, it provides a strong analysis of *why* certain models perform better than others. The discussion about the failure of dependency parsing on long sequences and the degradation of the LSTM-ER model on relation extraction due to a combinatorial explosion of candidate pairs is particularly insightful and well-supported by the evidence presented (e.g., Figure 4).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Thoroughness:** The authors explore a diverse set of plausible neural approaches to the problem. The inclusion of four distinct framings, with multiple parser implementations under the dependency parsing approach, makes the experimental setup comprehensive. This allows for robust conclusions about the suitability of different modeling paradigms for this task.

2.  **Excellent Analysis of Results:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting F1 scores. The authors provide compelling explanations for the observed performance differences. For instance, the hypothesis that the LSTM-Parser's performance drop on the essay level is due to the massive search space of possible trees is well-reasoned. The contrast between the local nature of the sequence tagger and the global constraints of the parser provides a clear explanation for their relative performance. The analysis of how LSTM-ER's generality becomes a weakness on longer documents with many entities is a key insight.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The introduction provides a strong and clear motivation for moving away from feature-engineered, pipeline-based ILP models towards end-to-end neural systems. The authors clearly articulate the problems of feature engineering, manual constraint design, and error propagation, setting the stage effectively for their proposed investigation.

4.  **Significant Empirical Improvement:** The proposed neural models demonstrate substantial improvements over a strong feature-based SOTA system (Stab and Gurevych, 2016). The fact that this is achieved without task-specific feature engineering highlights the power and flexibility of the neural approaches investigated.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Generalizability is Limited by the Choice of Dataset:** The entire study is conducted on the PE dataset, which has very specific structural constraints (e.g., each premise has exactly one parent, creating a near-tree structure). The proposed framings, especially the dependency parsing and the distance-encoded sequence tagging, are heavily tailored to these properties. It is unclear how these methods would perform on other AM corpora with more complex, graph-like structures where components can have multiple parents or relations are not strictly hierarchical. This reliance on a single, structurally unique dataset is the most significant limitation of the work.

2.  **Novelty Lies in Framing, Not Models:** The paper presents its work as the "first neural end-to-end solutions," but the models themselves are off-the-shelf applications of well-established architectures (e.g., Ma and Hovy, 2016; Dyer et al., 2015; Miwa and Bansal, 2016). The novelty is entirely in the application and the comparative framing of the AM task. While this is a valuable contribution, the framing could be more modest about architectural novelty.

3.  **The "Lossless" Conversion to a Tree Structure is Questionable:** The authors state that since claims can relate to multiple major claims, they "loss-lessly" re-link them to a single major claim to enforce a tree structure for the dependency parser. This is not a lossless transformation; it discards relational information from the original annotation scheme. This simplification makes the data fit the model, but it also means the parser is solving a slightly different, easier problem than the one defined by the gold-standard annotations. The implications of this simplification on the results and their interpretation are not adequately discussed.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The success of your sequence tagging and dependency parsing framings seems highly dependent on the "one parent per premise" rule in the PE dataset. How would you adapt these framings for datasets where argument components can have multiple parents or form more general graph structures? Would the sequence tagging approach, which encodes a single distance `d` to one parent, still be viable?

2.  Could you please elaborate on the "lossless" data conversion for the dependency parsing experiments? By re-linking a claim that targets two major claims to only one, you are explicitly losing a gold-standard relation. How does this affect the comparability of the dependency parser's scores to the other models that do not require this simplification? Was the parser evaluated against the original annotations or the modified, tree-like annotations?
