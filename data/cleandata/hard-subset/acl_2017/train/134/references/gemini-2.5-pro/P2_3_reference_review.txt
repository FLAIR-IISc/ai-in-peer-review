### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study on applying neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full end-to-end task—which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four distinct ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a complex sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning (MTL) problem, and using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). These neural approaches are evaluated against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline on a standard dataset of persuasive essays. The key finding is that neural models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF sequence tagger with a novel label schema, can outperform the feature-based SOTA without requiring hand-crafted features or explicit structural constraints.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Systematic Comparative Analysis of Neural Framings for AM:** The primary contribution is not a single new model, but a thorough investigation into *how* to best frame the complex, structured task of end-to-end AM for modern neural architectures. By comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and joint entity-relation modeling, the authors provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each paradigm for this task.
2.  **A Novel and Effective Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The proposed `STagT` formulation, which encodes component boundaries (BIO), type (Premise/Claim), relation distance, and relation type into a single, complex label, is a clever and effective way to linearize a graph-prediction problem. This approach proves to be robust, especially on longer documents where more general models struggle.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Trade-offs:** The paper provides an excellent analysis of why certain models perform better in different settings (paragraph vs. essay level). The explanation for the performance drop of the LSTM-ER model on relation identification in longer essays—attributing it to the combinatorial explosion of candidate relations—is particularly insightful and provides a clear takeaway for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Well-Designed Experiments:** The paper's empirical evaluation is its strongest aspect. The authors compare a wide range of models, from classic feature-based parsers to several state-of-the-art neural architectures. The decision to evaluate on both paragraph and full-essay levels is crucial, as it clearly exposes the scalability challenges of different approaches and highlights the strengths of their proposed `STagT` model. The comparison against the strong ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) firmly grounds the results and demonstrates a clear advance for the field.
2.  **Clear Problem Motivation and Strong Results:** The paper successfully argues against the brittleness of pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and complex constraints (ILP). The proposed neural models achieve a new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset, demonstrating that it is possible to learn the entire argumentation structure end-to-end from raw text with minimal feature engineering. The performance of `STagBLCC` and `LSTM-ER` (on paragraphs) is substantially better than the ILP baseline, validating the core thesis of the paper.
3.  **Excellent Analysis and Discussion:** The discussion in Section 5 is exemplary. The authors don't just present numbers but delve into *why* the models behave the way they do. The analysis of the dependency parsing models’ failure, the trade-offs of the local sequence tagger versus the more global LSTM-ER model, and the interpretation of the MTL results are all clear and insightful. Figure 4, which visualizes the models' differing abilities to handle long-range dependencies, is a particularly effective piece of analysis that supports their conclusions about model constraints.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the *framings* of the problem are novel, the underlying neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, Stack-LSTM parser, etc.) are standard, off-the-shelf models. The main technical novelty lies in the design of the label set for the sequence tagging approach. This is not a major flaw, as the paper's contribution is clearly empirical and analytical, but it is worth noting that the work does not propose a new neural network architecture.
2.  **Generalizability Concerns due to Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are conducted on a single corpus (PE essays) which, as the authors note, has a very specific "almost-a-tree" structure where relations are mostly contained within paragraphs. The success of the proposed methods, particularly the `STagT` model's reliance on a single outgoing link per component and the dependency parsing framing, is likely tied to these structural properties. It remains unclear how well these approaches would generalize to more complex, graph-structured argumentation found in other domains like legal text or online debates, where components may have multiple parents or relations cross more freely. A discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Unclear Connection Between Main and MTL Experiments:** The multi-task learning (MTL) experiments in Table 2 are performed with a weaker baseline tagger (`STagBL`) than the main model reported in Table 1 (`STagBLCC`). This makes it difficult to assess the true impact of MTL. While the results show that MTL helps the weaker model, it is not clear if these gains would transfer to the already powerful `STagBLCC` model. The authors should justify this choice or clarify whether the same improvements can be expected for their best-performing model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `STagT` model cleverly encodes the relative distance `d` (in number of components) to the relation's head component directly into the tag. Could you please elaborate on the decoding process? Specifically, how is the final argument graph constructed from the predicted sequence of `(b, t, d, s)` tags? How do you resolve cases where a predicted distance `d` points to an invalid target (e.g., a location with no component start, or off the end of the document), or conflicts between predictions?
2.  The MTL experiments use a `STagBL` tagger, which lacks the CRF and CNN layers of your best-performing `STagBLCC` model. What was the reason for not applying MTL to the stronger `STagBLCC` model? Do you believe the gains from adding auxiliary tasks would be of a similar magnitude on the stronger architecture?
