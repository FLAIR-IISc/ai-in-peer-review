### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a comprehensive study on applying neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors position their work against previous state-of-the-art methods that rely on pipelines of independently trained models, hand-crafted features, and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for global constraints. The paper explores four distinct neural framings for the end-to-end task, which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) a novel sequence tagging approach with a rich, composite label that encodes relation distance, (3) a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of the sequence tagger, and (4) the application of a general-purpose joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Through experiments on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, the authors demonstrate that their neural approaches, particularly the sequence tagging and joint entity-relation models, outperform the feature-based ILP baseline. They provide a detailed analysis of the models' performance, highlighting that a simple sequence tagger with a carefully designed label set is surprisingly robust, especially for longer documents.

### Contributions

1.  **A Systematic and Comparative Evaluation of Neural Architectures for End-to-End AM:** The primary contribution of this work is the thorough investigation and comparison of multiple, conceptually different neural approaches to a complex, structured prediction task. Rather than proposing a single model, the authors frame the AM problem in several ways and provide a clear analysis of their respective strengths and weaknesses (e.g., dependency parsing's failure on long sequences, the trade-offs of the LSTM-ER model). This provides the community with valuable insights into which architectural choices are suitable for this task.
2.  **A Novel and Effective Formulation of AM as Sequence Tagging:** The authors propose a creative and effective method (STagT) for casting the entire end-to-end AM problem as a sequence tagging task. The design of the composite label `(b, t, d, s)`, which encodes BIO status, component type, relation distance, and relation type into a single tag for each token, is a key technical contribution. This formulation proves to be highly effective, particularly in handling the long-range dependencies common in essay-length documents.
3.  **New State-of-the-Art Results Without Feature Engineering:** The paper successfully demonstrates that end-to-end neural models can significantly outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP-based system on a standard benchmark. This is an important result for the field of AM, showing that it can benefit from the same representation learning paradigm that has advanced other areas of NLP, thereby removing the need for costly feature engineering and constraint design.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The paper's main strength lies in its excellent experimental design and analysis. The comparison across four different model families provides a holistic view of the problem. The authors astutely use two evaluation settings (paragraph-level and essay-level) to probe the models' ability to handle sequences of varying lengths and complexity. The analysis is not superficial; for instance, the discussion of why LSTM-ER excels on short paragraphs but fails on longer essays (due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations) is backed by a clear quantitative analysis in Figure 4. This level of detail is exemplary and highly valuable.
2.  **Clarity and Novelty of the Proposed Framings:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The different framings of the AM problem are explained clearly, with helpful examples and figures (e.g., Figure 3). The sequence tagging formulation (STagT) is particularly elegant. Encoding the relative distance between components (`d`) directly into the label is a simple yet powerful idea that directly addresses the challenge of linking distant components, a known difficulty in relation extraction. This approach is more constrained and targeted than a general-purpose relation extractor, which appears to be its key advantage.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper delivers a clear and significant performance improvement over the existing state-of-the-art. The fact that multiple neural models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) outperform the ILP baseline, especially on the more contained paragraph-level task, is a strong statement. The STagBLCC model's robust performance on the more challenging essay-level task further solidifies the paper's claims and provides a strong new baseline for future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Generalizability Beyond a Single, Structurally-Constrained Dataset:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on the PE dataset, which has very specific structural properties (e.g., relations are contained within paragraphs, claims link to all major claims, premises have a single parent, resulting in an "almost-tree" structure). The success of the proposed models, especially the dependency parsing and sequence tagging framings, may be heavily reliant on these properties. It is unclear how these methods would perform on AM datasets from different domains (e.g., legal texts, online discussions) where argument structures might be non-tree-like graphs. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation more explicitly.
2.  **Scalability Concerns for the Sequence Tagging Label Space:** The size of the label set for the STagT model is proportional to the number of distinct distances `d` observed in the data. While the observed range in the PE dataset is manageable ([-11, 10]), this could become a significant issue in domains with much longer documents or more complex argumentation, potentially leading to a very large and sparse label set that is difficult for a model to learn. A discussion of this potential limitation and possible mitigation strategies (e.g., binning distances) would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Confounded Comparison in the Multi-Task Learning Experiments:** The MTL experiments in Table 2 are used to show that adding auxiliary subtasks improves performance. However, these experiments are conducted with a weaker tagging model (STagBL, a BiLSTM without a CRF or CNN layer) compared to the main, best-performing tagging model (STagBLCC). This makes the comparison somewhat difficult to interpret. While it shows MTL helps a weaker model, it does not demonstrate whether MTL could provide a similar or greater boost to the already strong STagBLCC model. Applying MTL to the strongest model would have provided a clearer picture of its standalone benefit.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The MTL experiments in Section 5(c) use a simpler STagBL model. Could you clarify the reasoning behind not applying the MTL framework to your best-performing STagBLCC model? Do you hypothesize that the CRF layer in STagBLCC already captures some of the label structure that MTL is meant to encourage, potentially leading to smaller gains?
2.  Your successful STagT model explicitly encodes the relative distance between components (`d`) in the label. How do you foresee this approach adapting to datasets where argument structures are not simple trees, such as those with components supporting/attacking multiple other components (resulting in a graph)? Would this require a fundamental change to the tagging scheme?
