**Summary and Contributions:**

This paper presents an empirical study of neural network models for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the task in four different ways: as token-level dependency parsing, complex sequence tagging, multi-task sequence tagging, and joint entity-relation extraction using an established architecture. These models are evaluated on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus and compared against a strong, feature-based system that uses Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The main findings are that neural models, particularly a sequence tagger and a joint entity-relation model, outperform the ILP baseline, and that the choice of model architecture involves a critical trade-off between expressive power and the implicit constraints needed for long-range argument structures.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A comprehensive empirical comparison of several existing neural architectures for the end-to-end AM task on a standard benchmark. This provides a valuable data point for the field, demonstrating that neural approaches can surpass heavily engineered pipeline systems.
2.  An insightful analysis of the performance characteristics of different models, particularly concerning their ability to handle inputs of varying lengths (paragraph vs. essay). The paper effectively highlights the challenges that less constrained models face with the combinatorial complexity of relation extraction in longer documents.
3.  The successful application of the Miwa and Bansal (2016) model to AM, which serves as a strong and methodologically sound baseline for future neural research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Systematic Comparison against a Strong Baseline:** The paper's greatest strength is its direct and thorough comparison against the state-of-the-art ILP model of Stab and Gurevych (2016). By showing that multiple neural approaches can outperform this system, the authors make a convincing case for abandoning manual feature engineering and complex constraint design in favor of end-to-end learning for this task.
2.  **Thoughtful Analysis of Model Trade-offs:** The authors provide a nuanced discussion of why different models succeed or fail. The analysis of the LSTM-ER model's performance degradation on the essay-level task is particularly compelling. The explanation—that its generality becomes a liability when faced with a quadratic increase in potential relations—is well-reasoned and supported by evidence (Figure 4). Similarly, the diagnosis of the token-level dependency parsing approach's failure is plausible, if brief. This level of analysis is more valuable than a simple leaderboard of results.
3.  **Methodological Breadth:** The paper explores a reasonably diverse set of modeling paradigms. Comparing a global model (dependency parsing) with local models (tagging) and a hybrid model (LSTM-ER) on the same problem provides a well-rounded view of the current architectural options. This comparative aspect is a significant service to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Scope and Generalizability:** The study's conclusions are drawn from experiments on a single dataset, the PE corpus. As the authors correctly state, the definition of an "argument" is highly dependent on the underlying theory and annotation scheme. The PE corpus has a specific, near-tree-like structure that may heavily favor certain model assumptions (e.g., that a premise links to exactly one other component). The strong claims about the general superiority of the proposed sequence tagging framework are not sufficiently substantiated without validation on other AM corpora with different structural properties (e.g., the AIFdb corpora, or data from legal or scientific domains).
2.  **Novelty of the Proposed "STag" Method:** The primary methods evaluated are applications of existing, well-established neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, Stack-LSTM parsers, Miwa and Bansal's model). The most novel proposal, the "STag" sequence tagging framework, feels more like an intricate encoding scheme than a principled architectural innovation. Cramming segmentation, component type, relation distance, and relation type into a single, complex label is a clever hack, but its robustness is questionable. The reliance on a discretized, relative distance `d` seems particularly brittle and may not generalize to argumentation graphs that deviate from the simple parent-child structure in the PE corpus.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on the STag Label Space:** The paper does not provide crucial details about the implementation of the STag model. The label set Y is potentially massive due to the distance component `d`. The authors do not specify the range of `d` used in the experiments, the resulting size of the label vocabulary, or the strategy for handling out-of-range distances at test time. This omission makes it difficult to assess the scalability and practicality of the approach, which the authors ultimately recommend for longer documents. Without these details, the method is not fully reproducible.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please clarify the practical details of the STag model's label set? Specifically, what was the vocabulary size for the complex tags, how was the range of the distance `d` determined, and how does the model handle a component whose target is at a distance not seen during training?
2.  Your conclusions are based entirely on the PE corpus, where each premise has exactly one head, facilitating your `(b, t, d, s)` encoding. How would you adapt the STag approach for a dataset where argument components can have multiple parent relations (i.e., a graph structure rather than a forest of trees)? Do you believe the core approach would remain viable, or would a different framing be necessary?
3.  The MTL experiments show modest improvements using a weaker base tagger (STagBL). Have you considered applying MTL to the stronger STagBLCC model? It is unclear if the observed gains are due to the fundamental benefit of MTL for this task or if they are simply mitigating the weaknesses (e.g., the label independence assumption) of the simpler tagging model.
