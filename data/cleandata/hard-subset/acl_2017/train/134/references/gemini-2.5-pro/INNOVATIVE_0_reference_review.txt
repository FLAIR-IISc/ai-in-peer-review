**Summary of the paper and its main contributions:**
This paper presents the first comprehensive investigation into applying neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). Moving beyond prior work that relies on pipelines and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) with hand-crafted features, the authors explore several ways to frame the full AM task—from raw text to a complete argument graph—within different neural paradigms. The core of the work is a systematic comparison of four distinct approaches: (1) framing AM as token-level dependency parsing, (2) a novel and clever reframing as a sequence tagging task where relational information (distance to the head component) is encoded directly into the tagset, (3) extending the sequence tagging model with multi-task learning, and (4) adapting a standard joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Through experiments on a standard persuasive essay dataset, the paper shows that neural models, particularly the sequence tagging approach, establish a new state-of-the-art, and it provides a valuable analysis of the trade-offs inherent in each framing.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **Conceptual reframing of a structured prediction task:** The primary contribution is the bold exploration of different conceptual *framings* for AM. The proposal to linearize the argument graph structure into a sequence tagging problem by encoding relational distance into the labels is a particularly insightful and novel idea. This shifts the focus from building complex, graph-aware architectures to finding clever representations for the problem itself.
2.  **Establishing a new direction for argumentation mining:** The paper effectively demonstrates that feature-free, end-to-end neural models can significantly outperform complex, knowledge-intensive ILP systems. This work carves out a new and promising research direction for the field, providing strong baselines and a clear methodological path forward.
3.  **Insightful analysis of model constraints vs. task complexity:** A key contribution is the analysis of why different models succeed or fail. The paper compellingly shows how the unconstrained nature of a general entity-relation model (LSTM-ER) leads to poor performance on long documents, while the more constrained sequence tagging model proves more robust, offering important lessons for applying structured prediction models more broadly.

**Strengths:**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Boldness:** This paper's greatest strength is its intellectual creativity. Instead of presenting an incremental improvement on an existing architecture, it fundamentally re-evaluates how to approach a complex problem. The sequence tagging formulation (STag) is a brilliant piece of problem-solving. It elegantly encodes a tree-like structure into a flat, linear model, challenging the assumption that such problems require explicitly hierarchical or graph-based models. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward, idea-driven research that moves the field forward.
2.  **Opens a New and Important Research Avenue:** By being the first to systematically apply and compare neural end-to-end models for AM, this work essentially opens a new subfield. It liberates AM research from the bottleneck of manual feature engineering and complex constraint design inherent in previous state-of-the-art systems. The thoroughness of the comparison provides an excellent foundation and a clear set of baselines upon which future research can build.
3.  **Strong and Illuminating Empirical Analysis:** The experiments are well-designed to highlight the conceptual differences between the models. The comparison between paragraph-level and essay-level performance is particularly insightful, providing a clear stress test that reveals the weaknesses of different approaches. The conclusion that a more constrained model (STag) is preferable for longer, more complex inputs, while a more general one (LSTM-ER) excels on shorter inputs, is a valuable and non-obvious finding with implications beyond just argumentation mining. The analysis in Figure 4, showing how STag better captures the distance distribution, is a prime example of the paper’s analytical depth.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Uncertain Generalizability of the STag Formulation:** The novel sequence tagging scheme, with its distance-based encoding `d`, seems tailored to the tree-like, single-parent structure of the PE corpus used. It is not immediately clear how this approach would handle more complex, non-tree argumentation graphs (e.g., where a premise supports multiple claims) that are common in other domains like online debates or legal text. While this does not invalidate the contribution for this dataset, a discussion of the conceptual limits of this representation would strengthen the paper. I see this not as a fatal flaw, but as a clear boundary on the current work that invites future exploration.
2.  **Dependence on Corpus-Specific Properties:** The analysis relies heavily on the paragraph/essay structure of the PE corpus. The finding that paragraph-level processing is easier is interesting, but it may be an artifact of this specific dataset where arguments are neatly contained. It would be beneficial to acknowledge how these results might change on corpora without such clean structural divisions.
3.  **Limited Architectural Innovation:** The paper's novelty lies in its problem framings, not in its neural architectures, which are adaptations of existing well-known models (BiLSTM-CRF, Stack-LSTM, etc.). While this is a perfectly valid and pragmatic approach for a study of this nature, it means the paper does not contribute a new, reusable model component. However, I believe the conceptual contribution far outweighs the lack of architectural novelty.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  The STag model encodes the distance `d` in terms of the number of argumentative *components* between the source and its parent. During inference, the model has not yet identified all components when it is tagging a given token. How is `d` predicted in a real-world scenario? Does the model make a greedy assumption about previously tagged components, or is there a more complex decoding process involved?
2.  Given that token-level dependency parsing performed poorly and sequence tagging performed well for component identification, have you considered a hybrid model? For instance, one could use the superior STag approach to identify component boundaries and types, and then apply a *component-level* neural dependency parser to establish relations. This seems like a promising direction that could combine the strengths of both paradigms.
