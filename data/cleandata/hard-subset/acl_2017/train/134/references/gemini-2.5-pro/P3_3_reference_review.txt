### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—including component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, complex tagging scheme that encodes relational information, (3) as a multi-task learning problem, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline. The experiments show that neural models, particularly the sequence tagging and joint entity-relation models, can outperform the ILP approach, thereby setting a new state-of-the-art without requiring hand-crafted features or explicit constraint engineering.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for end-to-end AM.** The core novelty lies in the tag structure `(b, t, d, s)`, which cleverly linearizes the tree-like argument structure by encoding the relation target as a relative distance `d` (measured in components). This allows a standard sequence tagger to jointly identify components and their single outgoing relation in one pass.
2.  **A systematic and insightful comparison of different neural strategies for a structured prediction task.** Rather than proposing a single model, the paper provides a valuable analysis of the trade-offs between different modeling paradigms (global parsing vs. local tagging, constrained vs. unconstrained relation prediction). The analysis of why certain models fail (parsing) or struggle with longer documents (LSTM-ER) is particularly strong.
3.  **Demonstration that feature-free neural models can surpass complex, feature-engineered pipeline/ILP systems in AM.** By outperforming the Stab and Gurevych (2016) model, the paper makes a strong case for moving towards end-to-end neural approaches in this domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimentation:** The paper stands out for its comprehensive empirical evaluation. The authors don't just test one idea but systematically explore four distinct and well-motivated neural framings of the problem. The comparison against multiple dependency parsers, the evaluation at both paragraph and essay levels, and the inclusion of a strong ILP baseline make the results robust and convincing. This comparative approach provides the community with much richer insights than a paper focused on a single model would.
2.  **Elegant and Effective Methodology:** The proposed sequence tagging approach is a key strength. It is an elegant solution to a complex structured prediction problem, collapsing component and relation extraction into a single tagging task. The analysis showing that this model's implicit constraints are beneficial for longer documents (Figure 4) compared to the more general but combinatorially explosive approach of LSTM-ER is an excellent piece of analysis that highlights the importance of inductive bias.
3.  **High Clarity and Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, the models are explained well, and the analysis of the results is insightful and easy to follow. The findings have significant implications for the field of AM, effectively showing a path away from laborious feature engineering and complex constraint solvers towards more generalizable and higher-performing end-to-end neural systems. The conclusion's "policy recommendations" based on document length are practical and well-supported by the evidence.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Generalizability of the Proposed Tagging Scheme:** The primary weakness of the novel sequence tagging model (`STagT`) is its reliance on the specific structure of the PE dataset, where each premise has exactly one outgoing link, forming an "almost-a-tree" structure. The tag `(b, t, d, s)` can only represent a single outgoing edge per component. This would fail in more complex argumentation graphs where, for instance, a single premise might support two different claims. While this is a perfect fit for the dataset used, the paper should more explicitly discuss this limitation and the potential challenges in adapting the method to datasets with more general graph structures.
2.  **Unclear Fairness of the ILP Baseline Comparison:** The paper states the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information," which makes the comparison slightly ambiguous. The neural models are trained and tested on either paragraph or essay level, but not both simultaneously. If the ILP model uses features like paragraph position within the essay (e.g., knowing it's the introductory paragraph), this is information the paragraph-level neural models lack. This could give the ILP model an unfair advantage, and the fact that neural models still outperform it would be even more impressive. A clarification of the exact features used by the ILP model is needed for a fully transparent comparison.
3.  **Sub-optimal Baseline for Multi-Task Learning Experiments:** The MTL experiments are used to support the conclusion that decoupling component and relation tasks is beneficial. However, these experiments are performed using a `STagBL` model, which is acknowledged to be weaker than the best-performing `STagBLCC` model (it lacks a CRF layer and character CNNs). While the results show a relative improvement from MTL, the final performance (Table 2) remains significantly below the single-task `STagBLCC` (Table 1). To make a stronger claim about the utility of MTL, it would have been more compelling to show that it can improve the performance of the already state-of-the-art `STagBLCC` model. As presented, the contribution of MTL feels more illustrative than conclusive.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on how your `STagT` formulation could be extended to handle non-tree-like argument structures where a component might have multiple parent/head components (e.g., a premise supporting two distinct claims)? Would this require a fundamental departure from the single-tag-per-token approach?
2.  Regarding the ILP baseline: Could you clarify what "access to both paragraph and essay level information" entails? Specifically, does the ILP model use features unavailable to the neural models in their respective settings (e.g., paragraph position within the essay for the paragraph-level comparison)?
3.  The MTL experiments convincingly show that auxiliary tasks help a weaker `STagBL` tagger. Did you attempt to apply MTL to your strongest model, `STagBLCC`? Do you believe similar gains are achievable, and could this push the state-of-the-art even further?
