### **Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a systematic, empirical comparison of neural end-to-end models for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM). The goal is to move beyond previous pipeline approaches that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) by exploring methods that learn representations and model task interdependencies jointly. The authors frame the end-to-end AM task—which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem; (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem where a single tag encodes component boundary, type, relation distance, and relation type; (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of the sequence tagging approach; and (4) using an existing joint entity and relation extraction model (LSTM-ER by Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) at both the paragraph and essay level. The results demonstrate that neural models, particularly the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER approaches, outperform the strong ILP-based baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art for this task and providing valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of each formulation.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A comprehensive empirical study of neural architectures for end-to-end AM.** This is the first work to systematically explore and compare multiple distinct neural paradigms (parsing, tagging, MTL, joint entity-relation) for the full AM task. This broad investigation provides a strong benchmark and roadmap for future research, successfully demonstrating that neural methods can surpass complex, feature-engineered pipelines.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for AM.** The proposed `STag` labeling scheme, which linearizes the structured AM task by encoding component boundaries, type, relational distance, and relation type into a single token-level tag, is a significant methodological contribution. The paper shows this formulation is particularly robust for longer documents, a key challenge in AM.
3.  **Insightful analysis of model performance and failure modes.** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of why certain models excel or fail under different conditions (e.g., paragraph vs. essay level). The diagnosis of the trade-offs between the generality of the LSTM-ER model and the more constrained nature of the `STag` model offers valuable lessons about designing architectures for complex structured prediction tasks with long-range dependencies.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and Systematic Evaluation.** The primary strength of this paper is its comprehensive experimental design. Rather than advocating for a single model, the authors evaluate a diverse set of neural architectures, including several variants within the dependency parsing framework. This comparative approach provides a much richer understanding of the problem space than a single-model paper would. The comparison against a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP system is crucial for demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed neural approaches.
2.  **Novelty and Efficacy of the Sequence Tagging Formulation.** The `STag` formulation is an elegant solution to a complex problem. By encoding relational information (distance and type) directly into the tag of a component's tokens, it allows a standard BiLSTM-CRF architecture to jointly learn component and relation extraction without requiring a separate relation-extraction module. The results show this is highly effective, outperforming other methods on the more challenging essay-level task, which highlights its ability to handle long-range dependencies.
3.  **High-Quality Analysis and Insights.** The paper extends beyond simply reporting metrics. The analysis comparing paragraph-level and essay-level performance provides clear evidence of the scalability challenges inherent in AM. The explanation for why the LSTM-ER model's performance on relation extraction degrades significantly on longer essays (due to the quadratic explosion of candidate pairs) compared to the more constrained `STag` model is well-reasoned and supported by Figure 4. This level of diagnostic analysis is exemplary and provides clear guidance for future work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on the `STag` Decoding Process and Label Dependency.** The paper's description of the `STag` label `(b, t, d, s)` is clear, but the practical implementation and decoding process are not. The distance `d` is defined as the number of *components* between the source and target. This creates an apparent circularity: to determine the correct label for tokens in a component, one must know the distance `d`, but to calculate `d`, one must have already identified the preceding argument components. It is unclear how this is resolved during inference. Is a greedy left-to-right decoding used? Is there a joint decoding over the entire label set? This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully understand the model's mechanics and potential complexities.
2.  **Potential Scalability Issues of the `STag` Label Set.** The `STag` approach combines all information into a single, large, categorical label set. As noted in the paper, distances in the data range from -11 to +10, which, combined with other label components, creates a very large output vocabulary for the softmax layer. While manageable on the PE dataset, this approach may not scale to datasets with longer documents or more complex argumentation structures that exhibit even longer-range dependencies. The paper dismisses this as unlikely due to "finite memory," which is not a technically rigorous argument. A discussion of this limitation and potential alternative solutions (e.g., predicting distance as a regression value, using a pointer mechanism) would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Ambiguity in the Adaptation of the LSTM-ER Model.** The paper states it adapts the Miwa and Bansal (2016) model, but key details are omitted. A core component of the original LSTM-ER is a TreeLSTM that uses dependency parse information to classify relations. The authors correctly point out that this is problematic for the PE dataset, where 92% of relations are inter-sentential. However, it is not specified how this component was adapted. Was the TreeLSTM provided with a different structural input, or was it effectively removed, reducing the model to a sequential relation classifier? This lack of detail makes the comparison to this model difficult to interpret and compromises the reproducibility of that part of the study.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the inference/decoding procedure for the `STag` models? Specifically, how is the distance `d` (measured in components) determined for a token when the model has not yet finished segmenting all components in the sequence?
2.  Regarding the LSTM-ER model adaptation: how was the TreeLSTM component, which relies on intra-sentential dependency paths, handled for a task where most relations are inter-sentential? Was this component modified, replaced, or removed?
3.  The dependency parsing formulation performed poorly. Do you attribute this primarily to the token-level representation? Do you hypothesize that a parsing approach operating on pre-identified component spans (even if untyped) could be more competitive?
