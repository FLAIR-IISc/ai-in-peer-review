### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural network models for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to move beyond previous pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and complex constraints (e.g., ILP), towards more robust, jointly learned models. The authors explore four distinct neural framings of the end-to-end AM task: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, complex label encoding component boundaries, types, and relation information (distance and type), (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of the sequence tagger, and (4) using a state-of-the-art joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset and compared against a strong ILP-based baseline, achieving new state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A systematic and insightful comparison of different neural framings for end-to-end AM.** The paper provides the first comprehensive benchmark of modern neural architectures for this complex structured prediction task. The analysis of what works (sequence tagging) and what doesn't (token-level dependency parsing), and under which conditions (e.g., LSTM-ER on short vs. long documents), offers valuable guidance for future research in this area.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for joint component and relation extraction.** The proposed `STagT` framework, which encodes relation distance and type directly into the token-level labels, is a simple yet powerful method for capturing argument structure. The paper demonstrates its robustness, particularly on longer documents where more general models with fewer structural priors struggle.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's core strength is its comprehensive experimental design. Instead of proposing a single model, it systematically evaluates four distinct and well-motivated conceptualizations of the AM problem. The comparison is rigorous, testing on two levels of granularity (paragraph and essay) which effectively function as "easy" and "hard" settings. This approach provides a clear and well-supported picture of the current landscape of neural methods for this task.
2.  **Strong Results and Clear Takeaways:** The proposed models establish a new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset, outperforming a strong, feature-engineered ILP system. More importantly, the paper provides insightful analysis that leads to clear, actionable conclusions. The explanation for the performance trade-offs between `STagT` and `LSTM-ER` on essay-level data (i.e., the benefit of `STagT`'s structural constraints versus `LSTM-ER`'s combinatorial explosion of candidate relations) is particularly convincing and well-supported by the analysis in Figure 4.
3.  **Innovative Problem Formulation:** The `STagT` formulation is a clever contribution. By encoding relational distance into the tag set, the authors successfully integrate tree-like structural information into a fundamentally linear sequence model. This demonstrates how task-specific knowledge can be effectively injected into neural architectures through label design, providing a strong alternative to more complex graph-based models, especially when the target structure has known properties (like predominantly local dependencies).
4.  **Excellent Analysis:** The paper goes beyond just reporting scores. The authors provide thoughtful error analysis and hypotheses for the observed performance differences. For instance, the discussion of why the LSTM-Parser outperforms other parsers (access to full stack history) and the finding that MTL with sub-tasks improves performance are valuable insights for the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Generality of the `STagT` Formulation:** The success of the `STagT` model hinges on structural properties of the PE dataset, namely that each premise has exactly one outgoing link, allowing the problem to be modeled as a tree. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently discuss the limitations this imposes. In more general argumentation graphs, a component can have multiple outgoing relations (e.g., a premise supporting two different claims), which the `(b, t, d, s)` tuple cannot represent. This makes the paper's strongest model less applicable to other AM datasets and tasks, and this limitation should be discussed more explicitly.
2.  **Unclear Decoding Process for `STagT`:** The `STagT` label encodes the distance `d` in terms of the number of *argumentative components* between the current component and its head. It is unclear how this distance is used or calculated at inference time. When the model is decoding a sequence of tokens, it is simultaneously identifying the components and their relations, so the indices and count of preceding/succeeding components are not known beforehand. This is a critical implementation detail that is missing from the paper.
3.  **The Comparison with LSTM-ER May be Disadvantageous to It:** The authors correctly note that the TreeLSTM component of the Miwa and Bansal (2016) model is rendered "ineffective" because most relations in the data are cross-sentence, while the model is given single-sentence dependency trees. This seems to be a significant handicap for the model, especially on the longer essay-level texts. The performance degradation on relation extraction might not only be due to the combinatorial problem, but also because a key architectural component is systematically mismatched with the data. A fairer comparison might have involved adapting the input to the TreeLSTM (e.g., using a sequential chain over components) to better suit the nature of the task.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the decoding process for the `STagT` model? Specifically, since the distance `d` in the label is defined over components, which are being predicted simultaneously, how does the model compute or use this distance during inference on unseen text?
2.  Your analysis suggests that LSTM-ER struggles with the O(m^2) candidate relations in long essays. Did you consider simple heuristics to mitigate this, such as adding a distance feature to the relation classifier or pruning candidate pairs that are more than a certain number of components apart? Such a hybrid could potentially combine the benefits of LSTM-ER's decoupled architecture with the powerful distance bias learned by `STagT`.
3.  The MTL experiments reveal that using component identification (`YC`) as an auxiliary task is consistently more helpful than using relation identification (`YR`). This is an interesting finding. Do you have a hypothesis for why this is the case?
