**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of end-to-end argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative components (e.g., claims, premises), their types, and the relations between them from raw text. The authors argue that previous state-of-the-art methods, which rely on pipelines of individually trained models, hand-crafted features, and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for joint inference, are brittle and difficult to adapt to new domains. To overcome these limitations, the paper conducts a comprehensive empirical study of several neural, end-to-end learning frameworks for AM. The authors explore four distinct approaches: (1) framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem; (2) framing it as a sequence tagging problem with a complex tagset that encodes component boundaries, types, relation distances, and relation types; (3) extending the sequence tagging model with a multi-task learning (MTL) setup; and (4) adapting a state-of-the-art joint entity and relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, comparing their performance against the previous feature-based ILP SOTA. The key findings are that neural models outperform the ILP baseline, that token-level dependency parsing is not effective for this task, and that the sequence tagging and joint entity-relation models perform best, with their relative strengths depending on document length.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a thorough, systematic comparison of different neural framings for the end-to-end argumentation mining task. This is the first work to explore these diverse approaches (parsing, tagging, MTL, joint entity-relation) in a unified setting. The analysis of the trade-offs between these models, particularly concerning document length and modeling constraints, is highly valuable for the community.
2.  The paper proposes a novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for the full AM task. By encoding component type, relation type, and the relative distance to the head component into a single, structured tag, the authors present a surprisingly robust method that implicitly constrains the search space and handles long-range dependencies well.
3.  The work establishes new state-of-the-art results for end-to-end AM on the PE dataset, demonstrating that end-to-end neural methods can significantly outperform prior pipeline approaches that rely on extensive feature engineering.

**Strengths**
1.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's greatest strength lies in its breadth and depth of experimentation. Instead of proposing a single new architecture, it systematically investigates four conceptually different ways to frame the AM task using neural networks. The inclusion of multiple dependency parsers (both feature-based and neural), a state-of-the-art tagger, an MTL setup, and a dedicated joint entity-relation model provides a solid, comparative foundation for future work.
2.  **Strong Analysis of Model Trade-offs:** The authors do an excellent job of not just reporting results, but also analyzing *why* certain models perform better than others in different settings. The discussion on the failure of dependency parsing (hypothesized to be due to the vast search space over long sequences) is convincing. Even more insightful is the analysis comparing STagBLCC and LSTM-ER. The paper correctly identifies that LSTM-ER's generality, which allows it to consider all O(m^2) pairs of entities, becomes a weakness on long documents, whereas the more constrained nature of the STag model makes it more robust. This is well supported by the analysis in Figure 4 and provides a clear, actionable takeaway.
3.  **Clarity and Significance:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and addresses a significant challenge in NLP. Moving AM away from feature-engineered pipelines towards end-to-end learned systems is a crucial research direction. This work provides a clear roadmap and a set of strong baselines for doing so. The problem formulations for each of the four approaches are explained clearly, making the work easy to follow and reproduce.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core architectures used in this work (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, Dyer et al.'s parser, Miwa and Bansal's LSTM-ER) are existing models applied to a new task. The main novelty lies in the framing of the problem, particularly the STag label set. While this encoding is clever and effective, it can be viewed more as a task-specific engineering solution than a fundamental modeling advance. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly framed its primary contribution as a comparative analysis rather than implying the development of entirely new models.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Sequence Tagging Label Space:** The STag approach results in a very large, combinatorial label space. The paper demonstrates that this works but does not provide a deeper analysis of its properties or limitations. For example, what is the effective size of the label vocabulary seen in the training data? How sparse is it? How does the model generalize to unseen `(b, t, d, s)` tuples at test time? A discussion of these aspects would add significant depth and help readers understand the robustness and potential failure points of this formulation.
3.  **Evaluation on a Single, Structurally Constrained Dataset:** All experiments are conducted on the PE dataset, which has a specific, near-tree-like argumentation structure where premises have a single head. The paper's conclusions, especially regarding the relative merits of dependency parsing vs. other models, are heavily influenced by this structure. It is unclear how these findings would generalize to datasets with more complex, graph-like structures (e.g., where a premise can support multiple claims). While a single-dataset evaluation is common, the paper would be more impactful if it included a discussion of how the proposed framings might need to be adapted for corpora with different underlying argumentation theories or more complex annotation schemes.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the STag formulation: Could you provide statistics on the size of the unique label set `Y` encountered in the training data versus the theoretical maximum? How does the model perform on instances where it must predict a label tuple that was not seen during training? This would help clarify the model's ability to generalize within this large, structured output space.
2.  The performance degradation of LSTM-ER on the essay level is attributed to the quadratic number of candidate relations. Did you consider applying any simple heuristics to prune the candidate pairs for the relation module (e.g., a distance threshold)? This could provide a stronger test of your hypothesis that the STag model's implicitly constrained search space is its key advantage over LSTM-ER on longer documents.
3.  You mention that the TreeLSTM component of the Miwa and Bansal (2016) model is "ineffective" for this data because most relations are inter-sentential. Does this mean the shortest dependency path features were disabled, or that they were included but provided little useful signal? Clarifying this detail would help in understanding which components of the LSTM-ER model were most critical to its performance.
