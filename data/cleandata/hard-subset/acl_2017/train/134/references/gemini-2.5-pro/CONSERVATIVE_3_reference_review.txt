### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the application of neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM pipeline—comprising component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, complex tag set that encodes relational distance, (3) as a multi-task learning problem based on sequence tagging, and (4) as a joint entity-relation extraction task using the model of Miwa and Bansal (2016). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset and compared against a strong, feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, which represents the prior state of the art. The results indicate that the proposed sequence tagging model is the most robust, outperforming the ILP baseline on the full essay task, while the dependency parsing framing is ineffective and the Miwa and Bansal model excels on shorter texts (paragraphs) but struggles with longer ones (full essays).

### Contributions

1.  The primary contribution is a thorough empirical comparison of several neural architectures for end-to-end argumentation mining. The analysis across different modeling paradigms (parsing, tagging, joint extraction) and text granularities (paragraph vs. essay) provides valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of these approaches for a complex structured prediction task.
2.  The paper introduces a novel sequence tagging formulation for AM that integrates component and relation information, including the relative distance between components, into a single tag per token. This approach proves to be empirically effective, achieving state-of-the-art results on the full-essay benchmark.
3.  The work provides a useful negative result by demonstrating that framing end-to-end AM as token-level dependency parsing is not a viable strategy, despite its conceptual appeal for tree-like structures.

### Strengths

1.  **Systematic and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a commendable empirical study, moving beyond a single model proposal to a systematic exploration of multiple neural framings for the AM task. This comparative approach strengthens the paper’s conclusions. The decision to evaluate on both paragraph- and essay-level inputs is particularly insightful, as it clearly highlights how different models' performances degrade with increasing sequence length and problem complexity.
2.  **Comparison Against a Strong, Established Baseline:** The authors rightly compare their proposed models against the state-of-the-art system of Stab and Gurevych (2016), which uses a sophisticated combination of feature engineering and ILP. Surpassing such a strong and methodologically different baseline provides compelling evidence for the viability of neural end-to-end methods in this domain, demonstrating they can be competitive without extensive feature engineering or explicit constraint definition.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Failures:** The paper offers sound explanations for the observed performance differences. For instance, the analysis of why the LSTM-ER model's performance on relation identification drops sharply on the essay level (due to the quadratic explosion of candidate pairs) compared to the more constrained sequence tagging model is convincing and provides a valuable lesson for applying such models to similar tasks.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Methodological Concerns with the Proposed Sequence Tagging (STag) Formulation:** The main novel method, encoding relation distance `d` into the tag, is methodologically problematic and under-specified.
    *   **Unbounded and Sparse Tag Space:** The distance `d` is, in principle, unbounded, which implies an infinite tag vocabulary. The authors dismiss this by observing that distances are limited in their dataset, but this is an empirical artifact, not a methodological safeguard. The paper fails to explain how the model would handle a distance at test time that was unseen during training. Furthermore, this approach leads to a very large and sparse tag set, which is known to be difficult for classifiers to learn effectively. Crucial details, such as the size of the final tag set `Y` used in the experiments, are omitted.
    *   **Lack of Generalization over Distance:** By treating distance as a discrete category within the tag (e.g., `(B,P,1,Supp)`, `(B,P,2,Supp)`) the model cannot generalize across distances. It learns each distance as an entirely separate class, failing to capture the ordinal nature of distance. A more principled approach would be to predict distance separately, perhaps as a regression target or through binning, which would likely lead to better generalization.
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** Beyond the aforementioned STag formulation, the paper primarily applies existing neural architectures (e.g., Ma and Hovy, 2016; Dyer et al., 2015; Miwa and Bansal, 2016) to the AM task. While this survey is valuable, the work's contribution is more empirical than methodological. The dependency parsing exploration is presented as a contribution but is essentially a negative result, which is generally considered a weaker form of contribution.
3.  **Inconclusive Multi-Task Learning (MTL) Experiments:** The MTL experiments are conducted using a simpler sequence tagging model (STagBL) that lacks the CRF layer of the paper's best-performing model (STagBLCC). This makes the findings difficult to interpret. The modest gains observed could simply be compensating for the weaknesses of the simpler base model rather than demonstrating a fundamental benefit of MTL for this task. To be convincing, these experiments should have been run on top of the strongest single-task architecture to see if MTL provides further improvements.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the STag formulation: Could you please specify the size of the label set `Y` in your experiments? More critically, how does your model handle a component-to-component distance `d` at test time that was not observed in the training data? Did you consider alternative methods for encoding distance, such as predicting it as a separate head of the network, which might offer better generalization?
2.  The performance gains of the STagBLCC model over the ILP baseline on the essay-level task are marginal for the exact-match metrics (e.g., R-F1 of 34.82 vs. 34.74). While these results are reported as statistically significant, the practical improvement seems minor. Could you comment on the trade-off between this small gain in performance and the loss of interpretability and explicit structural guarantees that the ILP model provides?
3.  Why were the multi-task learning experiments performed with a weaker base model (STagBL) instead of your best-performing STagBLCC model? Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether MTL offers a genuine advantage for this task or simply mitigates deficiencies (like the lack of a CRF) in the simpler architecture.
