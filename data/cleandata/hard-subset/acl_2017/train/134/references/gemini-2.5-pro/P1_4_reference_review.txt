### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors critique previous state-of-the-art approaches that rely on pipelines, hand-crafted features, and integer linear programming (ILP) for constraint enforcement. Instead, they propose and evaluate four different neural framings of the end-to-end AM task, which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification. The framings investigated are: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) sequence tagging with a novel, complex label encoding relation distance, (3) multi-task learning (MTL) using subtasks as auxiliary objectives, and (4) an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Experiments are conducted on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, comparing performance on both paragraph and full-essay levels. The key findings are that a standard BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagger with the proposed labeling scheme and the Miwa and Bansal model both outperform the previous ILP-based state-of-the-art, and that the optimal modeling strategy depends on the length of the text and the number of entities involved.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A systematic and insightful comparison of different neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining.** Rather than proposing a single model, the paper's primary strength is its broad investigation into how a complex, structured NLP task can be mapped to different standard neural architectures. The inclusion of both successful (sequence tagging) and unsuccessful (dependency parsing) framings provides valuable negative results and deepens the community's understanding of what works and why.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for joint argument component and relation extraction.** The proposed `(b, t, d, s)` tag, which encodes BIO status, component type, relation distance, and relation type into a single label, is a clever and powerful method for casting this structured prediction problem into a sequence tagging framework. This allows off-the-shelf, powerful sequence tagging models to be applied directly, achieving state-of-the-art results.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis yielding new state-of-the-art results and important takeaways for the field.** The paper establishes new SOTA performance on the widely used PE dataset. Crucially, it goes beyond simply reporting numbers by providing a nuanced analysis of the trade-offs between models, particularly the finding that a more constrained model (STagBLCC) scales better to longer documents than a more general but combinatorially complex model (LSTM-ER).

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper clearly articulates the weaknesses of prior work (brittle pipelines, costly feature engineering, inflexible ILP constraints) and convincingly argues for the need for end-to-end neural approaches. The introduction sets up a clear research question and a well-designed set of experiments to answer it.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors compare their proposed framings against a wide array of strong baselines, including both feature-based and neural dependency parsers, a strong existing entity-relation extraction model, and the previous state-of-the-art ILP system. The evaluation on both paragraph and essay levels is particularly insightful, as it reveals important differences in model scalability and performance.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The discussion of results is a major strength. The authors provide compelling explanations for their findings. For instance, the analysis of why the LSTM-Parser outperforms other parsers but still fails on the full task is convincing. The head-to-head comparison of STagBLCC and LSTM-ER, explaining their performance divergence between paragraph and essay levels through the lens of combinatorial complexity vs. built-in constraints, is particularly illuminating and a key takeaway of the paper. Figure 4 provides excellent quantitative support for this argument.
4.  **Clear Contribution to the Field:** This work provides a strong foundation and a set of powerful baselines for future neural research in argumentation mining. By showing that a relatively standard sequence tagger can outperform a complex, feature-engineered ILP system, it significantly lowers the barrier to entry for achieving high performance on this task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty of Architectures vs. Framings:** While the framings are novel for AM, the underlying neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, Dyer's parser, Miwa & Bansal's model) are not new. The main technical novelty lies in the design of the sequence tagging label `(b, t, d, s)`. While the paper is framed as a comparative study, which mitigates this, it could be stated more explicitly that the contribution lies in the application and formulation rather than in novel model architecture.
2.  **Potential Scalability Issues of the Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The `STagT` formulation encodes the component-wise distance `d` directly into the label space. As shown in Figure 2, `d` can reach values of -11 and +10 in this dataset. For corpora with even longer-range dependencies, this could lead to a very large and sparse label set, potentially harming model performance and generalizability. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential solutions (e.g., binning distances) would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Simplification of Argument Structure for Parsing:** The authors state they "loss-lessly re-link the claims to one of the major claims" to create a tree structure required by dependency parsers. The term "loss-lessly" seems strong, as the original structure is a directed acyclic graph (DAG), not a tree. This simplification might disadvantage the parsing models by feeding them a slightly artificial version of the problem. While the parsers performed poorly anyway, a more nuanced discussion of this data transformation and its potential impact is warranted.
4.  **Unclear Comparison in MTL Experiments:** The multi-task learning (MTL) experiments are performed with a `STagBL` model, which is noted to be weaker than the `STagBLCC` model used for the main sequence tagging results. This makes it difficult to assess the true benefit of MTL. The observed gains might be more pronounced on a weaker base model. It is unclear if applying MTL to the stronger `STagBLCC` model would yield similar relative improvements. This conflates the effect of the learning paradigm (MTL) with the strength of the base architecture.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the sequence tagging formulation `STagT`, how do you see this approach scaling to datasets with much larger documents where the distance `d` between related components could be significantly larger (e.g., > 50)? Would the label space become too large and sparse, and have you considered alternative ways to encode this distance information?
2.  Could you elaborate on the "loss-less" transformation of the argument structure into a tree for the dependency parsing experiments? Given that claims can relate to multiple major claims, this seems to be a conversion from a DAG to a tree. What information, if any, is lost in this process, and do you believe this simplification could have contributed to the poor performance of the parsing-based models?
3.  In your MTL experiments, you demonstrate that adding auxiliary tasks improves the performance of a weaker `STagBL` tagger. Do you have intuitions or preliminary results on whether these gains would transfer to the much stronger `STagBLCC` model? In other words, is the MTL benefit an artifact of regularizing a simpler model, or is it a generally applicable technique for this task?
