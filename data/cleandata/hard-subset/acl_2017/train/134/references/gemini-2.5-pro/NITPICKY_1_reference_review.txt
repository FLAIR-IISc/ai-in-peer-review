**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—including component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a complex tagset encoding component boundaries, types, and relation distances, (3) as a multi-task learning problem, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), and their performance is compared against a strong, feature-based ILP system.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A thorough empirical comparison of several reasonable neural approaches for end-to-end AM on a standard benchmark, providing valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each framing (e.g., local tagging models vs. global parsing models).
2.  A demonstration that relatively standard neural architectures, particularly sequence taggers, can achieve state-of-the-art results, outperforming a complex feature-engineered ILP pipeline and obviating the need for manual feature and constraint design.
3.  An insightful analysis explaining the performance differences across models and settings, particularly the degradation of certain models on longer, more complex documents (essays vs. paragraphs).

**Strengths**

1.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The paper does not just propose a single model but instead evaluates and contrasts four distinct and well-motivated neural framings for the end-to-end AM task. This comparative approach is a significant strength, providing a much richer picture of the problem space than a paper focused on a single architecture. The inclusion of multiple dependency parsers, a state-of-the-art sequence tagger, an MTL setup, and a canonical joint extraction model covers the main paradigms for this type of structured prediction task.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper successfully shows that neural models can outperform the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based system. The analysis of these results is particularly strong. For example, the discussion of why the LSTM-ER model excels at component identification but fails at relation identification on the essay level (due to the combinatorial explosion of candidate relations) is insightful and well-supported by the evidence in Figure 4. Similarly, the diagnosis of the dependency parsers' failure on long sequences is convincing.
3.  **Effective Problem Formulation:** The sequence tagging formulation (STag) is a simple yet creative way to encode a complex, graph-structured problem into a linear tagging scheme. Encoding the relative distance to the head component (`d`) directly into the tag is a clever mechanism to capture relational information within a standard tagging framework, and its empirical success is a notable finding.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents interesting ideas and a solid empirical investigation, it is marred by a significant number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its methodology, clarity, and presentation. The accumulation of these issues undermines the paper's quality and suggests a rushed preparation.

1.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   The reference section is a complete mess. It appears to be a raw dump from a bibliography tool, with BibTeX-like artifacts (e.g., `Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015, Bahdanau et al\.,`), inconsistent formatting, missing venue information, and incorrect character encoding (`Héctor` is rendered incorrectly). This level of carelessness is concerning.
    *   Figures are not optimally presented. Figure 1 is useful for illustration, but Figure 3 is too small to be clearly legible. Figure 2 is a simple histogram that could be described in text.
2.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Ambiguity:** Key details of the proposed methods are either glossed over or ambiguous.
    *   The "loss-less" data transformation to force the argument graph into a tree structure is a critical pre-processing step for the dependency parsing models. However, the procedure ("re-link the claims to one of the major claims (e.g., the last major claim in a document)") is described with a casual "e.g." and its implications are not discussed. This is not "loss-less"; it alters the ground-truth structure. The impact of this arbitrary choice on the evaluation is not addressed.
    *   The decoding process for the core `STag` model is unclear. The tag encodes a distance `d` (e.g., -2), but the paper never explains how this distance is used to identify the specific head component. If there are multiple components at a distance of -2, which one is chosen? This is a fundamental detail of the model that has been omitted.
    *   There is an inconsistency in the `STag` example provided in Section 4. The `Claim` component is assigned `d=⊥`, implying it has no relation. However, the data description in Section 3 states that "claims always relate to all other major claims." This contradiction is confusing and unexplained.
3.  **Underwhelming Novelty of Models:** The paper claims to present the "first neural end-to-end solutions," but the models themselves are largely off-the-shelf applications of existing, well-known architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, Dyer et al.'s parser, Miwa and Bansal's model, Søgaard and Goldberg's MTL framework). The primary novelty lies in the *framing* of the task for these architectures, particularly the tagset design for the sequence tagger. The contributions should be framed more accurately around this, rather than implying the development of new architectures.
4.  **Insufficient Experimental Reporting:** The paper relegates crucial experimental details like hyperparameter optimization and embedding specifics to supplementary material. While space is a constraint, some details are vital for reproducibility and understanding. For instance, given the novelty of the `STag` formulation, what was the practical size of the label set Y? This has significant implications for model complexity and training efficiency, yet it is never mentioned.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a precise algorithm for the "loss-less" re-linking of claims to a single major claim? Furthermore, could you justify this choice and discuss its potential impact on the validity of the results, especially since it modifies the ground-truth annotation?
2.  Please clarify the decoding step for the `STag` model. How is the head component of a relation uniquely identified from the distance `d` encoded in the tag, particularly in cases of ambiguity (e.g., multiple components at the same relative distance)?
3.  Can you explain the discrepancy in the `STag` model's example where a Claim is tagged with `d=⊥`, seemingly contradicting the dataset's definition that claims must relate to major claims?
4.  The reference list is currently unpublishable. Can the authors confirm this will be meticulously corrected for a camera-ready version?
