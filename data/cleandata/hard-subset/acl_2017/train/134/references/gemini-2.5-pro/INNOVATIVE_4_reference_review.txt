**Summary of the paper and its main contributions:**
This paper presents the first comprehensive investigation into applying end-to-end neural models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). It moves away from prior state-of-the-art systems that rely on multi-stage pipelines, hand-crafted features, and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for imposing global constraints. The authors explore several bold reformulations of the full AM task, casting it as different structured prediction problems amenable to modern neural architectures.

As I see them, the key contributions are:
1.  **Novel conceptual framings for end-to-end AM:** The primary contribution is the creative reimagining of the AM task. The authors propose and evaluate two particularly novel framings: (a) a token-level dependency parsing approach, where argumentative structure is modeled as a dependency tree over tokens, and (b) a sequence tagging approach, where a single, highly structured label for each token encodes its boundary, type, and relation to another component (via distance and type). These are bold, non-obvious formulations that significantly simplify the problem setup.
2.  **A rigorous comparative analysis of different neural paradigms for AM:** The paper does not simply propose one model, but instead evaluates an entire space of approaches. By comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and an off-the-shelf joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER), the authors provide crucial insights into which architectural biases and constraints are best suited for this task. The analysis explaining why different models excel on short vs. long documents is particularly valuable.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Excellent Novelty and Conceptual Value:** This paper is a significant step forward for the field of argumentation mining. The central idea of replacing a complex, feature-engineered ILP pipeline with a single, end-to-end trainable neural model is precisely the kind of ambitious work the community needs. The specific proposal to encode component boundaries, types, and relations into a single, complex sequence tag is elegant, clever, and highly effective. This contribution is not just a new model, but a new way of thinking about the problem.
2.  **Insightful and Broad Empirical Investigation:** The authors' decision to frame and compare multiple neural approaches is a major strength. Many papers would have stopped after proposing and evaluating a single model. Here, the systematic comparison provides a "map" of the solution space. The finding that token-level dependency parsing struggles, while a simpler local sequence tagger is more robust, is an important negative result. Similarly, the analysis of LSTM-ER's failure on longer documents due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations is a critical insight that will inform future work. This type of analysis is far more valuable than a simple leaderboard chase.
3.  **Demonstrably Strong Performance:** Beyond the conceptual novelty, the proposed methods work very well. The sequence tagging models establish a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark, outperforming the complex feature-based ILP system. This is a convincing demonstration that the proposed end-to-end paradigm is not just theoretically appealing but also practically superior.

**Weaknesses:**
While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few points that could be strengthened. I view these as minor issues that do not detract from the core contributions.
1.  **Incomplete Evaluation of the Dependency Parsing Framework:** The parsing models were primarily evaluated on the paragraph level due to memory limitations. While the conclusion that this framing struggles with long sequences seems plausible, it is not fully substantiated without a more thorough attempt at an essay-level evaluation. This limitation weakens the otherwise strong negative result. However, given the novelty and success of the other proposed models, I see this as an acceptable trade-off.
2.  **Scalability Concerns of the Distance-Encoded Tag:** The sequence tagging approach relies on encoding the relative distance `d` between components directly into the label. This is a creative solution that works well on the PE dataset, where most relations are local. However, this approach seems brittle and may not scale to other domains or datasets where argumentative relations span much larger distances, as this would lead to a combinatorial explosion in the size of the label set. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential alternatives (e.g., pointer-based mechanisms) would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Architectural Mismatch in MTL Experiments:** The multi-task learning experiments were conducted using a simpler tagger (STagBL) than the main sequence tagging model (STagBLCC). This makes it difficult to cleanly isolate the effect of the MTL framework itself versus the underlying power of the tagging architecture. While the reported improvements are encouraging, using the stronger STagBLCC as the base model for the MTL experiments would have made for a more direct and convincing comparison.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Following up on the scalability weakness: Do you view the distance encoding in the sequence tag as a general-purpose solution for AM, or a clever hack for this specific dataset? Have you considered pointer-based architectures, which seem like a natural fit for explicitly modeling the link from a component to its head, as a more scalable alternative?
2.  Your results convincingly show that a local sequence model (STag) outperforms a global token-level parsing model. Do you believe this is a fundamental weakness of the parsing paradigm for this task, or could a hybrid approach, such as a neural dependency parser operating on pre-identified component spans (rather than tokens), be a viable direction? This would retain the global tree constraint while operating on a much smaller number of units.
