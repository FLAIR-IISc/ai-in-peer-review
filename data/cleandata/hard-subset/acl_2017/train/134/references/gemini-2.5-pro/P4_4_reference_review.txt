### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural models for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to perform component segmentation, typing, and relation identification directly from raw text, avoiding the hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures of previous work. The authors frame the end-to-end AM task in four distinct ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a complex label scheme encoding relational information, (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). These framings are systematically evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset, comparing their performance against each other and a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model on both paragraph-level and full essay-level inputs.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining.** This is the first work to systematically investigate and contrast different neural architectures (parsing, tagging, joint entity-relation) for this complex task. The study provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling assumptions, demonstrating that neural approaches can outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline.
2.  **The proposal of a novel sequence tagging scheme that encodes relational structure directly into token labels.** By incorporating the distance (in number of components) to a related component into the label, this method provides a strong inductive bias that proves robust, particularly for identifying relations in long documents where other models falter.
3.  **An insightful analysis of model limitations.** The paper provides clear negative results and well-reasoned explanations for them. It demonstrates that framing AM as token-level dependency parsing is ineffective and that a general-purpose joint entity-relation model (LSTM-ER) fails on relation extraction in longer documents due to the combinatorial explosion of candidate pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Impact for the Field:** This work is the first to conduct a systematic exploration of neural architectures for the full end-to-end AM task. By moving beyond feature-heavy pipelines and ILP-based systems, it aligns AM with modern NLP trends and provides a strong foundation for future research. The comparative nature of the study, rather than focusing on a single proposed model, is a significant strength, offering the community a clear overview of the pros and cons of different approaches.
2.  **Technically Sound and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is thorough and convincing. The authors evaluate their models against a highly relevant and strong baseline (the ILP model from the dataset's creators). The decision to evaluate on two distinct levels of granularity—paragraphs and full essays—is particularly effective, as it clearly reveals the scalability and robustness challenges faced by different models. The analysis is supported by standard metrics and significance testing, lending credibility to the conclusions.
3.  **Excellent Analysis of Results:** The paper excels in its discussion and interpretation of the empirical results. The authors provide clear, plausible explanations for why certain models succeed or fail. For example, the diagnosis of the LSTM-ER model's poor relation performance on essays (due to the quadratic complexity of relation candidates) versus the STag model's robustness (due to the explicit distance encoding) is sharp and insightful. This level of analysis elevates the paper from a simple leaderboard comparison to a genuinely useful guide for future model design in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in the Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The proposed sequence tagging (STag) model is a central contribution, but its operational details during inference are underspecified. The label `(b, t, d, s)` includes a distance `d` "measured in number of components." It is unclear how a model can determine this component-based distance for a given token when the components themselves are being predicted simultaneously by the B-I-O tags. This suggests a potential chicken-and-egg problem that needs clarification. A more detailed description of the decoding process is necessary for the work to be fully reproducible and for its limitations to be understood.
2.  **Limited Scope of Multi-Task Learning Experiments:** The MTL experiments are used to show that adding subtasks as auxiliary objectives improves performance. However, these experiments are conducted on a weaker base tagger (STagBL, a BiLSTM) than the main single-task model (STagBLCC, a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN). This makes it difficult to ascertain whether the gains are universally applicable or if they are primarily a regularization effect on a simpler model. A more compelling experiment would have been to apply the MTL framework to the stronger STagBLCC architecture to see if the improvements hold, which would provide a clearer picture of the value of MTL for this task.
3.  **Ambiguity in Dependency Parsing Implementation:** While presenting the negative results for the dependency parsing framing is valuable, some key implementation details are missing. The paper notes that the dataset violates the single-head constraint of a dependency tree (claims can link to multiple major claims) and suggests this can be handled by "loss-lessly re-link[ing]". However, it is not explicitly stated whether this preprocessing step was performed for the parsing experiments, and if so, how it might have impacted the results. Clarifying how the data was mapped to a valid tree structure is crucial for interpreting the parser's poor performance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the inference process for the sequence tagging (STag) model? Specifically, how is the component-based distance `d` in the label `(b, t, d, s)` calculated at test time, given that the component boundaries are not known in advance?
2.  The MTL experiments demonstrate a performance boost on the STagBL model. Did you attempt to apply the same MTL setup to your strongest sequence tagger, STagBLCC? It would be valuable to know if the gains from auxiliary tasks are additive with the architectural improvements of a CRF and character-level CNNs.
3.  Regarding the dependency parsing experiments, how did you handle the one-to-many relationship from claims to major claims to satisfy the single-head constraint of a dependency tree? Was the "re-linking" strategy mentioned in Section 3 applied, and if so, could this simplification have contributed to the parsers' poor performance?
