### **Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—including component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem where relational information is encoded into the tags via component distances, (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem building on the sequence tagging approach, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) at both the paragraph and full essay level. The main findings are that the sequence tagging and entity-relation models outperform a strong, feature-based ILP baseline, while the token-level dependency parsing approach is found to be ineffective. The sequence tagging approach proves to be particularly robust for longer documents (full essays), and the MTL setup provides further performance gains.

### **Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a comprehensive empirical comparison of several distinct neural architectures for the end-to-end argumentation mining task on a standard benchmark. This is, to my knowledge, the first work to do so and provides valuable insights into the suitability of different modeling paradigms (e.g., local sequence tagging vs. global parsing) for this problem.
2.  The paper introduces a novel sequence tagging formulation for end-to-end AM that cleverly encodes relational structure by embedding the relative distance to the head component directly into the tagset. This approach proves to be both effective and robust, establishing a new state-of-the-art result.
3.  The work includes a thorough analysis of model performance under different conditions (paragraph-level vs. essay-level), yielding useful insights. For instance, the authors cogently explain the performance degradation of the general LSTM-ER model on longer texts by highlighting its unconstrained search space for relations, a weakness that their more constrained sequence tagging model avoids.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Thorough Investigation:** The paper does not simply propose a single new model but instead systematically explores four different conceptual framings of the AM problem. This comparative approach is methodologically sound and provides a much richer understanding of the problem space than a single-model paper would. The inclusion of both established parsers and state-of-the-art sequence taggers is commendable.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed models, particularly STagBLCC and LSTM-ER (on the paragraph level), significantly outperform the complex, feature-engineered ILP baseline from prior work. This demonstrates the power of neural end-to-end systems to tackle this task without the need for manual feature engineering or the explicit design of ILP constraints, which is a significant step forward for the field.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The authors provide convincing explanations for the observed performance differences. The diagnosis that the TreeLSTM component of the LSTM-ER model is ineffective due to the prevalence of inter-sentence relations is astute. Similarly, the hypothesis that global dependency parsing models struggle with long sequences due to the vast search space is well-reasoned and supported by the performance drop from paragraph to essay level. The analysis in Figure 4, which contrasts how STagBLCC and LSTM-ER handle relation distance, is particularly illuminating.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Inconsistency and Lack of Clarity in the Core Sequence Tagging Model:** The description of the proposed sequence tagging scheme (STagT) contains a critical ambiguity. The Data section (§3) states that "claims always relate to all other major claims." However, the example tag sequence in §4 shows the claim tokens receiving a label of `(B/I, C, ⊥, For)`, where the distance `d` is `⊥`. This directly contradicts the model's purported ability to encode relations. If the model cannot encode the claim-to-major-claim link, this is a significant limitation that is not discussed. Furthermore, the definition of the distance set `d` in Equation (1) omits `0`, which is correct as a component cannot link to itself, but this is an implicit detail worth stating. The entire model's validity rests on this encoding, and this apparent contradiction undermines its description.
2.  **Problem Simplification via Data Manipulation:** In §3, the authors state they can "loss-lessly" re-link claims to a single major claim to create a tree structure. This claim of being "loss-less" is questionable. The original annotation captures a one-to-many relationship (one claim to multiple major claims), which is a non-tree structure. Forcing it into a tree by arbitrarily selecting one major claim as the head is a data modification that simplifies the problem to fit the models (especially the dependency parser). This should be more transparently framed as a simplifying assumption with potential information loss, rather than a "loss-less" transformation.
3.  **Lack of Attention to Detail in Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is marred by a substantial number of minor but distracting errors, which collectively suggest a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   The introductory example (`Since it killed...`) uses a bizarre and unexplained colon-based formatting (`:::::::`) that is unprofessional and confusing.
    *   The caption for Figure 1 contains a typo ("Relationsships").
    *   The x-axis label in Figure 2 is erroneously duplicated ("d d").
    *   Most egregiously, the reference section is systematically corrupted. Nearly every entry contains BibTeX formatting errors, such as `Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015, Bahdanau et al\.,`, which includes extraneous characters and duplicated author-year blocks. This level of sloppiness in the bibliography is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the sequence tagging scheme for claims? Your example shows a distance `d=⊥` for a claim component, yet the data description says claims must link to major claims. How are these specific relations handled by your STagT model? Does the model actually predict these links, and if so, how are they encoded if not via the distance `d`?
2.  Can you provide a more rigorous justification for your assertion that re-linking claims to a single major claim is "loss-less"? What information about the argumentative structure is preserved and what might be lost by this simplification, and how might this affect the evaluation and interpretation of your results, particularly for the dependency parsing models that require this structure?
