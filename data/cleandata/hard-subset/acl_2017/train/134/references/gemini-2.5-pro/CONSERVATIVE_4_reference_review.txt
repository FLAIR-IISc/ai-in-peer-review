### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the application of neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). Moving away from established pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP), the authors systematically explore four distinct neural framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) a complex sequence tagging formulation that encodes relational information into the label set, (3) a multi-task learning extension of the sequence tagger, and (4) an adaptation of an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). The experiments, conducted on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), show that the best neural models outperform the feature-based ILP baseline.

As I see them, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A broad empirical comparison of multiple neural architectures for end-to-end AM on a standard benchmark. The finding that neural models can outperform a strong, constraint-based ILP system is a significant result for the field.
2.  A valuable negative result demonstrating that a token-level dependency parsing framing is not an effective approach for this task, despite its intuitive appeal.
3.  The introduction of a novel sequence tagging scheme that directly encodes the distance and type of relations into token labels, which proves to be a surprisingly effective, if constrained, modeling choice.

### Strengths

1.  **Comprehensive Empirical Study:** The paper presents a valuable comparative study of a diverse set of neural approaches for a complex structured prediction task. By evaluating architectures based on parsing, tagging, and joint modeling, the authors provide a solid empirical foundation that can guide future research in this area. This breadth is a major strength over papers that propose only a single model.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results on a Standard Benchmark:** The authors rightly compare their proposed models against the established SOTA system on this dataset, a feature-based ILP model. Demonstrating a clear performance improvement over this strong and well-understood baseline is a convincing argument for the viability of end-to-end neural approaches in AM.
3.  **Insightful High-Level Analysis:** The paper provides a useful analysis of the trade-offs between different modeling assumptions. The discussion of why the flexible LSTM-ER model performs best on shorter, paragraph-level texts while the more constrained sequence tagging model is more robust on longer, essay-level texts is particularly insightful. This highlights a key tension between model expressivity and the implicit biases required for complex tasks with long dependencies.
4.  **Value of Negative Results:** The clear demonstration that the token-level dependency parsing approach performs poorly is an important contribution in its own right. By rigorously testing this intuitive idea and showing its limitations, the authors help the community avoid potentially fruitless research avenues.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Questionable Methodological Soundness of the Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The core of the best-performing STagT model is its complex label set, which includes a distance `d` measured in terms of intervening argument components. This novel encoding feels ad-hoc and raises serious methodological concerns that are not addressed. The space of possible distances is theoretically unbounded, and the paper does not explain how the model handles distances at test time that were not seen during training. This makes the approach seem brittle and heavily tuned to the specific statistical properties of the single dataset used (as suggested by Figure 2), rather than a generalizable method.
2.  **Lack of Clarity and Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important implementation details are either omitted or not justified, hindering reproducibility and interpretation. For the dependency parsing model, the decision to have all tokens in a component link to the *first* token of the head component seems arbitrary. For the LSTM-ER adaptation, the authors state its TreeLSTM component is "ineffective" for this data (since most relations are inter-sentential) but do not explain how this component was handled. Was it removed entirely? If so, the model is a significant departure from Miwa and Bansal (2016), and this should be made explicit. Such omissions undermine the methodological rigor of the work.
3.  **Generalizability is Unproven:** All experiments are conducted on a single corpus of persuasive essays, which the authors admit has a peculiar, almost-tree-like structure where relations are mostly local. The paper's strong conclusions about the robustness of certain models are therefore premature. Established methods like ILP offer a transparent way to encode domain-specific constraints, a clear advantage when porting a system to new domains. In contrast, the proposed neural models, especially the distance-based STagT, seem implicitly overfitted to the structural properties of the PE dataset and may not generalize to argumentation in other domains (e.g., legal or scientific texts) that feature more complex, graph-based, or long-distance dependencies.
4.  **Analysis Could Be Deeper:** While the paper compares many models, the analysis of why they succeed or fail often remains at the surface level. For example, the failure of dependency parsers is attributed to the large search space on long sequences, which is a well-known issue. A more fine-grained error analysis would have been more illuminating. For instance, comparing the types of errors (e.g., component boundary, component type, relation attachment, relation type) made by the STagBLCC, LSTM-ER, and ILP models would provide much deeper insight into their respective failure modes than the aggregate F1 scores alone.

### Questions to Authors

1.  For the STagT model, could you clarify how the component distance `d` is handled in practice? What is the finite set of `d` values used in the label set, and how is this set determined? How does the model behave when, at test time, the correct prediction would require a distance `d` that was not in this set?
2.  Regarding the LSTM-ER model, please clarify the exact architecture you used. Given that 92% of relations are inter-sentential, how was the TreeLSTM component, which operates on intra-sentential dependency trees, adapted or used in your implementation?
3.  The ILP baseline can enforce hard structural constraints (e.g., each premise must have exactly one outgoing link). Do your neural models learn these constraints implicitly? Could you provide an analysis of how often your best models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) violate such fundamental structural rules present in the training data? A comparison on constraint satisfaction would offer a different and valuable perspective on model quality.
