**Summary**

This paper presents the first comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to perform component segmentation, classification, and relation identification starting from raw text, without resorting to hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures. The authors frame the end-to-end AM task in several ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, a complex sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning problem, and a joint entity-relation extraction problem. They conduct a thorough evaluation of various models corresponding to these framings on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). The results show that several neural approaches, particularly a sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) and a joint entity-relation model (LSTM-ER), significantly outperform a strong feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, setting a new state-of-the-art for this task. The paper also provides an insightful analysis of the trade-offs between different modeling choices, especially concerning model complexity and input sequence length.

**Contributions**

1.  **A comprehensive benchmark and analysis of neural end-to-end models for Argumentation Mining:** This is the primary contribution. The paper is the first to systematically explore and compare a diverse set of neural architectures (parsing, tagging, MTL, joint entity-relation) for the full, end-to-end AM task on a standard dataset. The resulting analysis, which highlights the superiority of neural methods over feature-engineered pipelines and provides insights into which architectural choices work best under different conditions (e.g., short vs. long documents), is a significant contribution to the field.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for joint AM:** The authors propose framing the joint task of component and relation identification as a single sequence tagging problem (STagT). This is achieved by designing a complex label that encodes BIO status, component type, relation distance, and relation type for each token. This formulation proves to be robust and effective, particularly for longer documents where more general models struggle.
3.  **Demonstration that multi-task learning improves performance:** The paper shows that decomposing the complex AM task into simpler auxiliary tasks (like component identification) and training them jointly within a multi-task learning framework improves performance over a single-task baseline. This provides empirical evidence for the utility of MTL in this complex structured prediction setting.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and systematic evaluation:** The paper's main strength lies in its breadth and depth. The authors don't just propose one model; they explore four distinct conceptual framings of the problem and evaluate multiple state-of-the-art models within those framings. The comparison against a strong, feature-based ILP system from prior work robustly establishes the benefits of the end-to-end neural approach. This work provides a very strong baseline for future research in this area.
2.  **Strong empirical results and new State-of-the-Art:** The proposed neural models, especially STagBLCC and LSTM-ER, achieve substantial improvements over the prior state-of-the-art ILP model. For example, on the paragraph level, LSTM-ER improves the overall F1 score (50% match) from 55.23% to 60.72%. This demonstrates not only the viability but the superiority of learning representations directly from data, obviating the need for labor-intensive feature engineering and constraint design.
3.  **Insightful analysis and discussion:** The paper provides a thoughtful analysis of why certain models perform better than others in different settings. The discussion about the trade-off between local models (sequence taggers) and global models (dependency parsers) is particularly valuable. The authors' explanation for why LSTM-ER excels on shorter paragraph-level inputs but falters on longer essay-level inputs (due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations) is convincing and provides a clear lesson: model constraints should match the constraints of the task. The analysis visualized in Figure 4, comparing how well models handle different relation distances, is also very illuminating.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Suboptimal framing of the dependency parsing approach:** The token-level dependency parsing formulation seems somewhat ill-suited for the task and may have unfairly disadvantaged this class of models. The decision to make the head of every token in a component be the *first* token of its target component is an arbitrary choice that is not well-justified. This creates a very dense and structurally unusual dependency graph. A more standard approach from discourse parsing would be to identify component headwords and draw links between them. It is unclear if the poor performance of the parsers is due to the inherent unsuitability of parsing for this task, or simply this specific, potentially flawed, encoding.
2.  **Lack of detail on the sequence tagging label space:** The STagT formulation creates a complex label `(b, t, d, s)`. While innovative, this leads to a combinatorial explosion of the label set. The paper does not discuss the size of this set, the sparsity of labels, or how the models handle unseen label combinations at test time. This is a critical detail for understanding the model's behavior and limitations. A model with a CRF on top of such a large, sparse output space might struggle to learn meaningful transition probabilities.
3.  **The ILP baseline seems to have an unfair advantage:** The paper notes that the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information and thus has always more information than all the neural systems". This slightly undermines the otherwise clean comparison. While the neural models still outperform it, the magnitude of the improvement might be understated. For a truly direct comparison, especially for the paragraph-level experiments, it would be valuable to know how an ILP model restricted to only paragraph-level information would perform.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for your specific token-level dependency parsing encoding? Have you considered alternative, perhaps more standard, encodings from discourse parsing, such as linking component headwords, which might create a sparser and more structurally meaningful tree?
2.  Regarding the STagT framework, what was the total number of unique labels in the training set? How does the model handle `(b, t, d, s)` tuples that were not observed during training? Does this lead to specific types of errors, for instance on relations with rare distance values `d`?
3.  The analysis of LSTM-ER's performance degradation on the essay level is very insightful. You attribute this to the O(m^2) complexity of relation prediction. Did you consider constraining the LSTM-ER model, for instance by only considering pairs of components within a certain token or component distance, to see if this would improve its performance on longer texts?
