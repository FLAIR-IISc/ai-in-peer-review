### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents an empirical study of neural end-to-end models for Argumentation Mining (AM), aiming to replace traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors frame the end-to-end AM task in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as a complex sequence tagging problem, as a multi-task learning problem, and using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Experiments on the Persuasive Essays dataset show that several neural approaches, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagger and the Miwa and Bansal model, outperform the feature-based ILP baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A comprehensive empirical comparison of multiple, non-trivial neural framings for the end-to-end AM task on a standard dataset. The exploration of what does and does not work (e.g., the ineffectiveness of token-level parsing) is valuable.
2.  The demonstration that end-to-end neural models can outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP-based system, thereby reducing the need for extensive feature engineering and manual constraint design.
3.  An insightful analysis of model trade-offs, particularly the discussion of how model constraints (e.g., the constrained search space in the sequence tagging model vs. the combinatorial explosion in the LSTM-ER model) interact with text length (paragraph vs. essay level).

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Scope:** This is the first work to systematically explore and compare a diverse set of neural architectures for the full, end-to-end AM task. Moving beyond sub-task specific solutions to a truly end-to-end paradigm is a significant step for the field, and the breadth of the explored framings (parsing, tagging, MTL) provides a strong foundation for future research.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper successfully demonstrates the superiority of neural methods over a strong non-neural baseline. The fact that multiple different framings (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) achieve state-of-the-art results lends significant weight to the central claim that neural end-to-end learning is a promising direction for AM. The performance gains are substantial, particularly on the paragraph-level task.

3.  **Thorough Analysis of Model Behavior:** The authors provide a compelling analysis of why certain models perform better in different settings. The explanation for LSTM-ER’s performance degradation on relations at the essay level (due to the quadratic increase in candidate relations) versus the robustness of the more constrained sequence tagging model is particularly insightful. This level of analysis elevates the paper from a simple leaderboard-chasing exercise to a study that offers genuine understanding and guidance for future model design in this domain.

### Weaknesses

Despite its contributions, the paper suffers from several methodological ambiguities and a general lack of rigor in its presentation that undermines the confidence in its findings.

1.  **Critical Omission in Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The proposed sequence tagging framework (STagT) encodes the distance `d` to a related component (measured in number of components) directly into the token's label. This formulation appears methodologically unsound or at least critically under-explained. At prediction time, a model processing a sequence token-by-token cannot know the segmentation of future components to calculate this distance. This implies a circular dependency. The paper provides no details on how this is implemented. Does it require a multi-pass decoding strategy? Is there a beam search that considers all possible future segmentations? This is not a minor implementation detail; it is a fundamental aspect of the proposed model that is entirely missing, making the approach difficult to understand, reproduce, and trust.

2.  **Flawed Experimental Comparisons:**
    *   The Multi-Task Learning (MTL) experiments are conducted with a weaker baseline tagger (STagBL, a simple BiLSTM) than the main sequence tagging model (STagBLCC, which adds a CRF and CNNs). This makes it impossible to isolate the contribution of MTL. The observed gains might simply be an artifact of patching deficiencies in a weaker model, rather than a genuine benefit of the MTL setup. A more convincing experiment would apply MTL to the stronger STagBLCC architecture.
    *   The comparison against the ILP baseline is not entirely fair. The paper states the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information," which is an advantage denied to the neural models that are trained and tested on one level at a time. This advantage should be more clearly acknowledged and discussed in the analysis of the results.

3.  **Lack of Precision and Attention to Detail:** The paper is marred by numerous minor yet distracting inconsistencies and omissions that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted, with inconsistent punctuation, stray characters (e.g., `.,?`), and incorrect author name formatting (`Martnez Alonso Héctor`). This level of sloppiness is unbecoming of a top-tier conference paper.
    *   In Table 1, an "Acc." column is presented without any definition of what this accuracy metric measures (e.g., token-level tag accuracy?).
    *   Figure captions and axis labels are imprecise. The y-axis in Figure 2 is just "%", and in Figure 4 it is "prob. correct", both of which are ambiguous. Further, Figure 4 uses absolute distance `|d|`, while the model itself is described as using signed distance `d`, an unexplained inconsistency.
    *   The discussion of statistical significance in the Table 1 caption is ambiguous: "Best scores in each column in bold (significant at p < 0.01)". It is unclear what comparison this significance refers to (e.g., against the next best system, or against all other systems).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a detailed explanation of how the distance `d` (measured in components) is calculated and used at inference time in your STagT model? Given that component segmentation is part of the output, how do you resolve the apparent circularity of needing to know the segmentation to predict the tags that define the segmentation?
2.  Could you justify the decision to use a weaker baseline model (STagBL) for the MTL experiments? Why was MTL not applied to the best-performing STagBLCC model to provide a more direct assessment of its benefits?
3.  Please define the "Acc." metric reported in Table 1.
4.  Regarding the data pre-processing, the paper states that claims linking to multiple major claims are "loss-lessly" re-linked to a single major claim to create a tree. Could you elaborate on why this modification, which seems to alter the original annotation semantics, is considered "loss-less"?
