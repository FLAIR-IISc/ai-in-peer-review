### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task (component segmentation, typing, and relation identification) in four distinct ways: as a token-level dependency parsing problem, as a complex sequence tagging problem, as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem, and by applying an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). The key goal is to move away from traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and complex Integer Linear Programming (ILP) constraints. The experiments are conducted on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, a standard benchmark for this task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for end-to-end AM.** The authors propose encoding the entire argument structure, including component boundaries, types, relation targets (via relative distance), and relation types, into a single, complex tag for each token. This allows powerful, standard sequence tagging architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) to be applied directly to the complex, structured task, which is a significant methodological contribution.
2.  **A thorough comparative analysis of different neural framings for AM.** Instead of proposing a single model, the paper systematically evaluates the strengths and weaknesses of four conceptually different approaches. This provides valuable insights into what architectures are (and are not) suitable for this task. The finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective while sequence-based models excel is a key takeaway.
3.  **Achieving new state-of-the-art results and providing insightful analysis.** The proposed models outperform the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based system, demonstrating the viability of feature-free neural approaches. The analysis is particularly strong, for instance, in explaining the performance differences between models on paragraph-level vs. essay-level inputs and diagnosing why the generic LSTM-ER model struggles with long-range relations in this specific task (Figure 4).

### Strengths

1.  **High-Quality Empirical Study:** This paper is an excellent example of a strong empirical study. The authors are not just applying a new model to a task; they are thoughtfully investigating how a complex structured prediction problem can be best modeled within the neural paradigm. The comparison of four distinct framings is a major strength, as it provides a much broader and more useful set of conclusions than a paper focused on a single architecture.
2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the different model framings are explained concisely with helpful examples (e.g., Figure 3 and the tagging example), and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. This clarity greatly enhances the impact of the work.
3.  **Excellent Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting F1 scores. The discussion provides deep insights into the results. For example, the analysis of why the LSTM-Parser outperforms other parsers (by encoding the whole stack history) and why it ultimately fails on longer sequences is very insightful. The comparison of STagBLCC and LSTM-ER, explaining their performance differences based on how they handle search space and long-range dependencies, is a highlight of the paper and provides a clear "policy recommendation" for future work.
4.  **Significant Results:** The paper successfully demonstrates that neural end-to-end models can outperform strong, feature-engineered pipeline systems that require domain-specific knowledge to design features and ILP constraints. The performance of the LSTM-ER and STagBLCC models, particularly on the paragraph-level task, establishes a new and strong baseline for future research in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability Concerns of the Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The core novel proposal, the STagT framework, encodes the distance `d` to the related component directly into the label. While effective on this dataset where most relations are local (Figure 2), this creates a potentially massive label space. The size of the label set is `O(Types * Distances * Stances)`. In a corpus with very long-range dependencies, this could become computationally intractable and lead to severe data sparsity for rare distance values. The paper does not discuss this limitation or potential solutions (e.g., distance binning).
2.  **Tension Between "End-to-End" Claim and Paragraph-Level Results:** The paper frames itself as providing "end-to-end" solutions. However, the best results for the top-performing models (LSTM-ER and STagBLCC) are reported on the paragraph level, where the problem is significantly simplified, as the authors acknowledge. The performance drop on the full essay level is substantial (e.g., R-F1 for STagBLCC drops from ~44% to ~39% for 50% match). While essay-level results are reported, the paper's main success is on a constrained version of the end-to-end task, which slightly weakens the central claim.
3.  **Potential Over-tuning to Dataset-Specific Artifacts:** The PE dataset has strong structural regularities (e.g., argument structures are contained within paragraphs, claims link to all major claims). The proposed STagT model, by encoding a single-headed relation (`d` points to one component) and its distance, seems particularly well-suited to the tree-like structure of this data. It is less clear how this specific formulation would handle more graph-like argumentation structures (e.g., a premise supporting multiple claims) that might exist in other domains. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how the model might be adapted to less constrained argument structures.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the sequence tagging label space: How do you envision the STagT approach scaling to domains where argumentative relations can span much larger distances (e.g., across many paragraphs or sections in a legal document)? Would the label set become prohibitively large? Have you considered alternative ways to represent the relation target, such as a pointer network-style mechanism, to avoid this issue?
2.  The performance degradation on the essay-level task is a key finding. Your analysis for LSTM-ER's failure is compelling (combinatorial explosion of candidate pairs). Could you provide more intuition for why the STagBLCC model, which constrains each component to have only one head, also sees a significant drop in R-F1? Is it primarily due to longer sequence lengths challenging the LSTM, or are there other error patterns that emerge at the essay level?
3.  You note that the ILP model has access to both paragraph and essay level information. Could you please clarify what this means in practice and how it compares to your models, which are trained either at the paragraph or essay level? Does this give the ILP model an unfair advantage, for instance, by using paragraph position within the essay as a feature?
