### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). Moving beyond prior work that relies on feature-engineered pipelines and Integer Linear Programming (ILP), the authors reframe the full AM task in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as a novel sequence tagging problem, as a multi-task learning problem, and by adapting a state-of-the-art joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). Through experiments on a standard dataset of persuasive essays, the paper demonstrates that neural approaches significantly outperform the previous feature-based SOTA, and provides a valuable analysis of the trade-offs between the different framings.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A bold and foundational exploration of neural architectures for end-to-end AM.** Rather than proposing a single model, this work offers a comparative study of multiple, conceptually different approaches. This establishes a new paradigm for the task and provides a strong set of baselines that will undoubtedly guide future research in this area.
2.  **The introduction of a highly creative sequence tagging formulation for a structured prediction task.** The idea of linearizing the argument graph by encoding component boundaries, types, relation distances, and relation types into a single complex tag `(b, t, d, s)` is a novel and powerful concept. It is a bold attempt to capture relational structure within a fundamentally sequential model.
3.  **Clear and insightful analysis of modeling trade-offs.** The paper provides a compelling explanation for why different models succeed or fail under different conditions (e.g., paragraph vs. full essay). The analysis showing that unconstrained models like LSTM-ER suffer from combinatorial explosion on long documents, while the dependency parsing framing is too rigid, provides crucial insights into the architectural choices necessary for this complex task.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Ambition.** This paper represents a significant conceptual leap for argumentation mining. It moves the field away from the confines of manual feature engineering and complex constraint design towards modern end-to-end learning. The scope of the paper—evaluating four distinct neural framings—is ambitious and provides far more value than a paper focused on a single architecture. This is exactly the kind of forward-looking, exploratory work that pushes the field in new directions.
2.  **Creative and Elegant Problem Formulation.** The sequence tagging (STagT) approach is the standout technical contribution. Encoding relational links via component-level distance `d` within a token-level tag is a clever and non-obvious method for capturing non-local structure. That this relatively simple idea proves so robust, particularly on long essays where other models struggle, is a testament to its elegance. This framing has the potential for impact beyond AM, in other domains requiring the joint extraction of entities and their relations.
3.  **Thorough and Illuminating Comparative Analysis.** The authors do an excellent job of not just presenting results, but dissecting them to understand the underlying reasons for performance differences. The head-to-head comparison on both paragraph-level (short documents) and essay-level (long documents) data is particularly effective. It cleanly demonstrates a key trade-off: LSTM-ER's flexible, decoupled approach excels when the number of entities is small, but the more constrained STagT formulation is more robust when faced with the combinatorial complexity of longer texts. This analysis provides clear, actionable takeaways for practitioners.
4.  **Convincing Empirical Results.** The proposed models, especially LSTM-ER on paragraphs and STagBLCC on full essays, establish a new state of the art on this dataset, convincingly outperforming the prior ILP-based system. This provides strong evidence for the central claim that end-to-end neural models are a superior approach for this task.

### Weaknesses

While this is a strong and exciting paper, there are a few areas where the exploration could have been deeper. I see these as opportunities for future work rather than critical flaws.

1.  **The "Distance" Encoding in STagT is Conceptually Tricky.** The tag `(b, t, d, s)` uses a distance `d` measured in *argumentative components*, not tokens. This implies that to decode the relation for the current component, the model must have some awareness of the components it has already identified in the sequence. The paper does not explain the mechanics of how this is handled during decoding. While the empirical success is undeniable, the approach feels slightly brittle and its mechanism is underspecified. A more detailed explanation would strengthen the contribution.
2.  **The Negative Result on Dependency Parsing Feels Incomplete.** The conclusion that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective is a valuable finding. However, the analysis of *why* it fails is somewhat brief, attributed mainly to the large search space. It would have been more satisfying to see a brief error analysis. For example, what kinds of structures does it fail to produce? Is the issue with attachment, labeling, or both? While I appreciate the breadth of the paper, a slightly deeper dive here would have made the negative result more conclusive.
3.  **Multi-Task Learning Rationale Could Be Clarified.** The MTL experiments show positive results, confirming that adding subtasks as auxiliary objectives is beneficial. However, the discussion of which tasks should feed from which BiLSTM layers feels preliminary. The paper notes an interesting divergence from prior work (Søgaard and Goldberg, 2016) but does not offer a strong hypothesis for why this might be the case in the context of AM. A bit more discussion here would have elevated this section from a report of empirical findings to a more insightful analysis.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the decoding process for the STagT model? Specifically, how does the model interpret the component-distance `d` during a single left-to-right pass? Does it rely on a constrained decoding algorithm (e.g., beam search with constraints) that keeps track of previously identified components?
2.  The dramatic performance drop of LSTM-ER on essay-level R-F1 is a key finding. Did you experiment with simple heuristics to mitigate the combinatorial explosion of relation candidates, such as imposing a maximum sentence or token distance between entities? It would be interesting to know how much of the gap to STagT could be closed with such a modification.
