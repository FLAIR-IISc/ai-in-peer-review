### **Summary**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to perform component segmentation, classification, and relation identification starting from raw text, without relying on hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures common in previous work. The authors systematically explore and compare four different neural frameworks: (1) framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem; (2) framing it as a sequence tagging problem with a novel, complex tagging scheme that encodes component boundaries, type, relation distance, and relation type; (3) extending the sequence tagging model within a multi-task learning (MTL) setup; and (4) applying an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER by Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Experiments conducted on the persuasive essays dataset of Stab and Gurevych (2016) show that the best neural models, particularly the sequence tagging approach and the LSTM-ER model, significantly outperform the previous state-of-the-art feature-based ILP system. The paper provides a valuable analysis of the trade-offs between the different framings, concluding that sequence tagging is a robust and effective approach, while token-level dependency parsing is not.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Comprehensive Empirical Comparison of Neural Frameworks for End-to-End Argumentation Mining:** This is the first work to systematically apply, evaluate, and compare a diverse set of modern neural architectures for the full end-to-end AM task. It provides a clear and valuable benchmark, moving the task beyond feature-engineered pipelines. The analysis offers crucial insights into which framings are effective (sequence tagging, joint entity-relation extraction) and which are less suitable (token-level dependency parsing) for this problem.
2.  **An Effective Sequence Tagging Formulation for a Structured Prediction Task:** The paper introduces a novel sequence tagging scheme that cleverly encodes component boundaries (BIO), type, relation type, and the target of the relation (via relative distance) into a single, atomic label. This demonstrates that a complex structured prediction task with tree-like dependencies can be successfully modeled using a standard, powerful sequence tagger (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), achieving state-of-the-art results without explicit structural decoding.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Constraints vs. Task Complexity:** The paper provides an excellent analysis of how model design choices interact with task characteristics, such as document length. The performance comparison between the highly constrained sequence tagging model and the more general LSTM-ER model on paragraph-level versus essay-level data highlights a key trade-off: general models that consider all possible relations suffer from a combinatorial explosion on long documents, whereas models with stronger structural biases (like the single-head assumption in the tagging scheme) are more robust.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Methodological and Empirical Soundness:** The paper is exceptionally well-executed from an empirical standpoint. It evaluates multiple, well-motivated neural architectures against each other and against a strong, contemporary SOTA baseline (the ILP model). The evaluation on both paragraph-level and essay-level inputs provides a clear and important analysis of how the models scale with sequence length and task complexity. The use of a standard dataset and evaluation metrics ensures reproducibility and allows for direct comparison with prior and future work.
2.  **High Clarity and Novelty:** The paper is very clearly written, motivating the shift from feature-based to neural end-to-end models for AM. It is the first comprehensive study of its kind for this task, making a novel contribution by establishing strong neural baselines and demonstrating their superiority. The problem framings are explained with sufficient detail, and the analysis of the results is insightful, particularly the discussion explaining the performance differences between the LSTM-ER and sequence tagging models (Section 5, Figure 4).
3.  **Significant and Impactful Results:** The work successfully demonstrates that neural end-to-end models can outperform a complex, feature-rich ILP-based system without any domain-specific feature engineering or manual constraint design. This is a strong and important result for the AM community, as it simplifies the modeling pipeline and sets a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark dataset. The reported F1 scores represent a substantial improvement, especially the LSTM-ER performance on the paragraph level.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Exploration of the Dependency Parsing Framework:** The paper concludes that framing AM as token-level dependency parsing is "ineffective." While the results support this for the specific framing chosen, this conclusion may be too strong. The chosen token-level representation creates extremely long dependency arcs and a very complex label set, which may be the primary cause of failure. An alternative framing, such as parsing over pre-segmented clauses or sentences, might be more robust and scalable. The dismissal of parsing as a viable framework seems premature without exploring these more natural granularities.
2.  **Potential Scalability Issues with the Proposed Tagging Scheme:** The success of the sequence tagging model hinges on encoding the distance `d` to the related component directly into the label space. This approach is effective for the PE dataset, where most relations are local (as shown in Figure 2). However, the scalability of this method to other AM datasets with more frequent long-distance dependencies or more complex graph structures (e.g., linked support, where one premise supports multiple claims) is questionable. The label space grows with the maximum observed distance, and the scheme inherently enforces a single-parent constraint on each component. This limitation is not sufficiently discussed.
3.  **The Multi-Task Learning Contribution is not Fully Convincing:** The MTL experiments show that adding auxiliary subtasks improves performance over a baseline tagger (STagBL). However, a weaker base model (a BiLSTM without a CRF layer) was used for these experiments, making it difficult to assess the true impact of MTL. The results are weaker than the main STagBLCC model, so it is unclear if MTL provides benefits on top of a state-of-the-art tagging architecture. To make a stronger case, it would have been beneficial to show that adding these auxiliary tasks to the stronger STagBLCC model provides further gains. As presented, this component feels more like a side experiment than a core contribution to the best-performing models.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The proposed sequence tagging scheme encodes relation distance in the label set, which seems to work well for the tree-like, mostly local structures in the PE dataset. How do you envision this approach adapting to argumentation structures that are more graph-like (e.g., a premise supporting multiple claims, requiring multiple outgoing links) or datasets with a higher prevalence of very long-range dependencies?
2.  The dramatic performance drop of the LSTM-ER model on the essay-level task is attributed to the combinatorial explosion of candidate relations. Did you consider or experiment with simple heuristics to prune the candidate relation pairs, such as imposing a maximum sentence or token distance between the two entities? This seems like a practical strategy to make the model more tractable for longer documents.
3.  The MTL experiments were conducted on a weaker tagging baseline (STagBL). This makes it hard to disentangle the benefits of the MTL setup from the known benefits of a stronger architecture like BiLSTM-CRF. Do you have results or intuitions on whether applying the same MTL framework to the stronger STagBLCC model would yield further improvements over the single-task STagBLCC results?
