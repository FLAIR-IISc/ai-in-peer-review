### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for Argumentation Mining (AM). The goal is to perform component segmentation, classification, and relation identification jointly, starting from raw text, without relying on hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures common in prior work. The authors systematically investigate four different framings of the end-to-end AM task: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a complex label set encoding component and relation information, (3) as a multi-task learning problem where subtasks of AM are used as auxiliary objectives, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Experiments on the persuasive essays dataset show that the best neural models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger and the joint entity-relation model, outperform a strong feature-based ILP system. The analysis highlights a key trade-off: more flexible models excel on short texts (paragraphs), while more constrained models are more robust on longer texts (full essays).

### Main Contributions
1.  **A rigorous and insightful comparative analysis of multiple neural paradigms for end-to-end Argumentation Mining.** The paper’s primary contribution is not a single novel architecture, but its systematic evaluation of how different modeling assumptions (e.g., parsing, sequence tagging, joint entity-relation extraction) align with the challenges of AM. The analysis of why certain approaches succeed or fail (e.g., the ineffectiveness of token-level dependency parsing, the performance drop of the LSTM-ER model on long documents) provides valuable insights for the broader field of complex information extraction.
2.  **Demonstration that a carefully designed sequence tagging formulation can effectively and robustly solve the full AM task.** The proposed STagT framework, which linearizes the structured prediction problem by encoding component boundaries, types, and relation links (via distance) into a single tag per token, is shown to be a strong and robust approach, particularly on longer documents. This finding offers a powerful yet relatively simple baseline for future end-to-end systems.
3.  **New state-of-the-art results on a standard AM benchmark without task-specific feature engineering.** By surpassing a strong ILP-based system that relies on extensive feature engineering, the paper convincingly validates the promise of end-to-end neural methods for this task, paving the way for more generalizable and less labor-intensive AM systems.

### Strengths
1.  **Thorough and systematic methodology.** The paper stands out for its breadth of exploration. By framing the same problem in four distinct ways and evaluating multiple models within each framing (e.g., four different dependency parsers), the authors provide a comprehensive picture of the current landscape of neural solutions for AM. This approach yields findings that are more general and impactful than a paper proposing a single model would.
2.  **Insightful analysis of the impact of document length.** The decision to evaluate on both paragraph and full essay levels is crucial and leads to one of the most interesting conclusions of the paper: the trade-off between model flexibility and structural constraints. The analysis of why the general LSTM-ER model excels on short texts but fails on the combinatorial complexity of longer texts, while the more constrained STagBLCC model generalizes better, is a nuanced and important contribution.
3.  **Strong empirical performance and clear SOTA improvement.** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over a competitive feature-based ILP baseline. This is a crucial result, as it shows that the move away from feature engineering does not come at the cost of performance and, in fact, can exceed it. The results are well-presented and statistical significance testing adds to their credibility.
4.  **Clarity and high-quality presentation.** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The different model framings are motivated and explained clearly with helpful examples (e.g., Figures 3). The connection between the theoretical properties of the models (e.g., local vs. global constraints) and their empirical performance is consistently well-articulated, making the paper's conclusions compelling.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Scalability concerns of the sequence tagging label set.** The proposed sequence tagging approach (STagT) encodes the distance `d` to a related component directly into the label. This creates an extremely large and potentially sparse label space, with the size dependent on the maximum component distance observed in the training data. The paper notes distances from -11 to +10, creating hundreds of possible labels. This approach may not scale well to other datasets where argumentative relations span much larger distances (e.g., across paragraphs or sections). The model's performance could degrade significantly, and the paper would benefit from a discussion of this limitation.
2.  **The novelty lies in application and analysis, not in model architecture.** The architectures used (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, Dyer et al.'s parser, Miwa and Bansal's model) are all existing, well-known models. The contribution is the clever application and framing of the AM task for these models, and the subsequent analysis. While this is a very valuable scientific contribution, the framing could be more precise about this, positioning the work as a comparative study rather than implying the development of new models from the ground up.
3.  **The evaluation could be more fine-grained.** The paper relies on the standard component and relation F1 scores, where correct relation identification is contingent on perfect component identification. This makes it difficult to disentangle errors. For instance, is LSTM-ER's poor R-F1 score on essays due to a true failure in relation classification, or is it an artifact of slightly imperfect component boundary predictions that cascade into relation errors? An additional analysis, such as evaluating relation accuracy on gold-standard components, would provide a clearer picture of the models' respective strengths and weaknesses in the relation identification subtask. The brief analysis on the last page is a good start but could be expanded.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Your STagT label encoding seems sensitive to the distribution of relation distances in the training data. Did you consider alternative methods to capture relations that might be more scalable, such as a pointer-network-style mechanism that learns to point to the head component's first token? This could potentially handle arbitrary-length dependencies more gracefully.
2.  The dramatic performance drop of LSTM-ER on the essay level is attributed to the O(m^2) candidate relations. This suggests the model is too unconstrained. Have you experimented with adding soft or hard constraints to the LSTM-ER model, for example by limiting the candidate relations to pairs of components within a certain token or sentence window? Such a modification might offer a better compromise between the model's flexibility and the task's structure.
3.  In the MTL experiments, you note that adding the component task (C) as an auxiliary objective was consistently more beneficial than adding the relation task (R). What is your intuition for this? Does this imply that for the end-to-end task, accurately identifying component spans and types is the more difficult or fundamental sub-problem for the model to learn?
