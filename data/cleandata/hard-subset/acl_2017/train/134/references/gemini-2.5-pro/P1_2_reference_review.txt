### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The goal is to perform component segmentation, classification, and relation identification starting from raw text, without relying on hand-crafted features or complex constraint-based systems like Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors frame the end-to-end AM task in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem where labels encode component type and relation information, (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) problem built on sequence tagging, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER by Miwa and Bansal). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, comparing their performance against a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model. The key findings are that neural models, particularly the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER approaches, outperform the ILP baseline. The paper provides a detailed analysis of model performance in different settings (paragraph-level vs. essay-level), concluding that simpler, more constrained models like sequence tagging are more robust for long documents, while more general models like LSTM-ER excel on shorter texts with fewer entities.

### Main Contributions

1.  **First Comprehensive Exploration of Neural End-to-End Argumentation Mining:** The primary contribution is the systematic investigation and comparison of multiple, distinct neural paradigms for the full, end-to-end AM task. Rather than proposing a single model, the authors evaluate four different framings, providing the community with valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach for this complex structured prediction problem.
2.  **New State-of-the-Art on a Standard Benchmark:** The paper demonstrates that several neural approaches, particularly the sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) and the LSTM-ER model (on the paragraph level), significantly outperform the previous state-of-the-art feature-based ILP system on the PE dataset. This is a strong, quantifiable contribution that pushes the performance boundaries for this task.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Behavior on a Complex Task:** A key contribution is the detailed analysis of why certain models succeed or fail, especially in the context of document length (paragraph vs. essay). The paper clearly shows the limitations of token-level dependency parsing for long sequences and the combinatorial challenges faced by general entity-relation models like LSTM-ER, providing clear takeaways and guidance for future research.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's main strength lies in its extensive and well-designed experiments. It compares four distinct neural approaches against each other and against a strong non-neural baseline. The inclusion of multiple dependency parsers and different tagging model variants (BL, BLCC, MTL) adds to the thoroughness. The evaluation on both paragraph and full essay levels is particularly insightful and reveals crucial differences in model robustness.
2.  **Novel and Effective Problem Formulation for Sequence Taggers:** While the underlying neural architectures are not new, the framing of end-to-end AM as a sequence tagging problem is creative and highly effective. The proposed tag set, which jointly encodes BIO boundaries, component type, relation distance, and relation type, is a clever way to collapse a complex structured prediction problem into a flat tagging task that modern BiLSTM-CRF models can handle surprisingly well.
3.  **Clear and Well-Supported Analysis:** The authors provide excellent analysis to support their conclusions. For instance, the discussion of why dependency parsing fails (massive search space on the token level) and why LSTM-ER's performance degrades on full essays (combinatorial explosion of candidate relations) is convincing. This is further supported by quantitative evidence like Figure 4, which effectively illustrates how the STagBLCC model is better at learning the distribution of relation distances from the data.
4.  **Well-Written and Clearly Structured:** The paper is very well-written, with a clear introduction that motivates the work, a detailed description of the models, and a conclusion that provides clear policy recommendations. The structure makes the complex comparisons easy to follow and understand.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The paper primarily applies existing, off-the-shelf neural architectures (e.g., Ma & Hovy's tagger, Dyer et al.'s parser, Miwa & Bansal's model) to a new problem. While the problem *framings* are novel, the work does not introduce new model components or a fundamentally new learning paradigm. This positions the paper more as a strong application and empirical analysis paper rather than one introducing a novel machine learning method.
2.  **Generalizability Concerns due to Single-Dataset Evaluation:** All experiments are conducted on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). This dataset has a specific, relatively clean tree-like structure (e.g., premises link to exactly one component, relations are mostly contained within paragraphs). The conclusions drawn, especially regarding the success of tree-enforcing models or specific tagging schemes, may not generalize to other AM domains (e.g., legal texts, online discussions) where argument structures can be more graph-like, with non-projective or many-to-many relations.
3.  **Ambiguity in Data Preprocessing for Dependency Parsing:** The paper states that to create a tree structure, claims are "loss-lessly re-linked" to a single major claim. This seems to be a data-specific heuristic that simplifies the problem to fit the model's structural assumptions. It is not a general solution for argumentation structures that are inherently graphs. The paper should be more explicit about this limitation and discuss how this preprocessing might affect the validity of the comparison, as other models (like LSTM-ER) do not require this structural simplification.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The sequence tagging model (STagT) encodes relation distance `d` as the number of components between the source and target. This seems to imply that component boundaries must be known to calculate the distance. Could you clarify the inference process? Is this a single-pass decoding where the model implicitly tracks potential components, or does it require a two-pass approach or complex post-processing to resolve the component counts and assign the final labels?
2.  The paper attributes the poor performance of LSTM-ER on the essay level to the O(m^2) complexity of considering all pairs of `m` entities. Have you considered simple pruning strategies to make this model more scalable, such as imposing a maximum sentence or paragraph distance between candidate entities? This seems like a straightforward extension that could potentially merge the component-detection strength of LSTM-ER with the relational robustness of the tagging model on longer documents.
3.  Regarding the dependency parsing framing: how would this approach handle datasets where the argument structure is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) rather than a tree (e.g., a premise supporting two distinct claims)? Does the forced conversion to a tree structure for the PE dataset fundamentally limit the applicability of this framing to more complex, real-world argumentation?
