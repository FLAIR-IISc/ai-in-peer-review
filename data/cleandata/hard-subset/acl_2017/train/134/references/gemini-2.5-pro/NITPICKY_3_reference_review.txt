**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—including component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four different ways: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing problem, (2) as a complex sequence tagging problem (STagT) where relation information is encoded as a distance in the tag label, (3) as a multi-task learning (MTL) extension of the sequence tagging model, and (4) using the existing LSTM-ER model from Miwa and Bansal (2016) for joint entity and relation extraction. These models are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compared against a strong, feature-based ILP baseline. The key findings are that the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER approaches outperform the ILP baseline and the dependency parsing framing, and that there is a trade-off between model flexibility and constraint, with more constrained models performing better on longer, more complex documents.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Novel and Effective Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The primary contribution is the STagT formulation, which cleverly encodes relational structure (distance between components) directly into the label set of a sequence tagger. This proves to be a surprisingly robust and effective method for the end-to-end task, especially on long documents.
2.  **Thorough Comparative Analysis of E2E Neural AM Models:** The paper provides the first systematic comparison of different neural architectures for this task. The analysis is insightful, particularly in explaining the performance trade-offs between models (e.g., why the highly general LSTM-ER model fails on the relation extraction subtask for full essays due to combinatorial explosion). The negative results for the dependency parsing approach are also a valuable contribution.
3.  **New State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed methods, particularly STagT and LSTM-ER (on paragraph-level data), outperform the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based system without requiring hand-crafted features or complex constraint engineering.

**Strengths**

1.  **Comprehensive and Well-Motivated Comparison:** The authors do not simply propose a single new model but instead explore and contrast four distinct and plausible neural framings of the AM problem. This breadth provides a much richer understanding of the problem space and the relative merits of different architectural biases.
2.  **Insightful and Nuanced Analysis:** The discussion goes beyond simply reporting F1 scores. The analysis of why LSTM-ER degrades on essay-level relation prediction is particularly sharp and highlights a crucial weakness of highly general entity-relation models when applied to long documents with many entities. Similarly, the observation that TreeLSTMs are largely ineffective because most relations are cross-sentential demonstrates a deep understanding of both the model and the data.
3.  **Clever Problem Formulation (STagT):** The sequence tagging formulation is elegant. It transforms a structured prediction problem into a flat tagging task that can be solved with standard, powerful machinery (BiLSTM-CRF), while still capturing the most crucial relational information (the link to another component via a distance metric). This demonstrates strong problem-modeling skills.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper successfully shows that carefully designed neural end-to-end systems can surpass complex, multi-stage, feature-engineered pipelines. This is a significant result for the field of argumentation mining.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper is marred by a lack of attention to detail, which significantly undermines its professionalism and credibility.
    *   **References:** The reference section is in a state of disarray. It is riddled with formatting errors, inconsistent styles, spurious question marks (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,?"), and misplaced punctuation. This is unacceptable for a camera-ready submission and suggests a rushed preparation.
    *   **Figures:** The figures are low-resolution and poorly labeled. In Figure 2, the y-axis is simply "%". In Figure 4, the y-axis is "prob. correct," which is ambiguous. Furthermore, Figure 4 only plots points for `|d|` = 4, 6, 8, 10, without explaining why odd or smaller values are missing, or whether the data was binned.
    *   **In-text Formatting:** The example sentence in the introduction ("Since it killed many marine livesP , ::::::: tourism...") uses a bizarre and distracting colon-based formatting that is never explained.
2.  **Unclear Methodological Details:** Several key details are either omitted or not sufficiently justified.
    *   **STagT Label Space:** The paper never quantifies the size of the label set Y for the STagT model. Given that `d` can range from -11 to +10, and there are multiple component and relation types, the label space could be very large. Its practical size is a critical detail for understanding the model's complexity and potential for data sparsity issues.
    *   **Data Preprocessing:** The claim that re-linking all claims to a single major claim is a "loss-less" transformation is questionable. It fundamentally alters the argument graph from a DAG to a tree, which is a significant simplification of the problem. This choice, which conveniently makes the data fit tree-based models, requires much stronger justification and discussion of its potential impact.
    *   **Reproducibility:** While relegating hyperparameters to supplementary material is common, the complete omission of any details about the word embeddings used (e.g., GloVe, word2vec, dimensionality) in the main text is a minor but notable oversight.
3.  **Weakness in Experimental Comparison:** The comparison to the ILP baseline is presented as a head-to-head result. However, the authors note that the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information." This is a significant advantage, as it explicitly knows the paragraph structure the neural models are evaluated on separately. This nuance is understated and makes the neural models' victory even more impressive, but the current framing of the comparison is slightly unfair.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the practical size of the label set Y for the STagT model on the PE dataset? How many unique tags were present in the training data, and did you encounter issues with sparsity for rare distance values `d`?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the "loss-less" claim regarding the simplification of the argument graph? This seems to be a crucial data manipulation step that simplifies the problem for tree-based structures. How might this affect the model's ability to generalize to data with more complex, non-tree-like argument graphs?
3.  Regarding Figure 4, could you please explain how the "probability of correct relation identification" was calculated? Why are only points for even distances `|d| >= 4` shown? Is the data binned, and if so, how?
