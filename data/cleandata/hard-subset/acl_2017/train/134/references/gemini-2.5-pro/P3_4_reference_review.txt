### **Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end models for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the full AM task—which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification—in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as a complex sequence tagging problem, as a multi-task learning problem, and using an established joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing their performance against a feature-based ILP model. The key finding is that neural models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagger with a specialized tagset, outperform the prior state-of-the-art ILP approach without requiring hand-crafted features or constraints.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A systematic and insightful comparative analysis of different neural framings for end-to-end AM.** Rather than proposing a single model, the paper's strength lies in its exploration of the problem space, demonstrating the strengths and weaknesses of parsing, tagging, and joint modeling approaches. The analysis of why certain models fail on longer documents (essays) vs. shorter ones (paragraphs) is particularly valuable.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for a structured prediction task.** The proposed tagset `(b, t, d, s)` is an elegant method for linearizing the AM problem, encoding component boundaries, types, relation targets (via relative distance `d`), and relation types into a single label per token. This is a non-trivial and effective adaptation of sequence tagging to a problem with graph-like dependencies.
3.  **New state-of-the-art results on a benchmark AM dataset.** The paper demonstrates that well-designed neural models can substantially outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline, pushing forward the performance on this challenging task.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The paper doesn't just test one new model against a baseline. It compares four conceptually different neural approaches, including multiple parsers and taggers, providing a broad view of what works. The decision to evaluate on both paragraph and essay levels is crucial and reveals important insights about model scalability and the handling of long-range dependencies, which is a core challenge in AM.
2.  **Excellent Analysis and Discussion:** The authors provide compelling explanations for their results. The diagnosis of the LSTM-ER model's failure on the essay level (combinatorial explosion of candidate relations) versus the STag model's success (implicitly constrained search space) is sharp and well-supported by the data (Fig. 4). Similarly, the analysis of why the LSTM-Parser outperforms other parsers (due to its encoding of the full stack history) is plausible and demonstrates a deep understanding of the models being used.
3.  **Practical and Impactful Contribution:** The paper successfully makes the case against complex, feature-heavy pipeline/ILP systems. By showing that a more direct, end-to-end neural approach can outperform them, it provides a clear and promising direction for future work in AM and potentially other structured prediction tasks in NLP. The elimination of task-specific feature engineering and constraint design is a significant practical advantage.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the different model framings are explained intuitively with helpful examples (Fig. 3), and the results are presented and discussed in a logical manner.

### **Weaknesses**
1.  **Novelty of the Neural Architectures Themselves is Limited:** While the *framing* of AM as a sequence tagging problem is novel, the underlying neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, Dyer's parser, Miwa & Bansal's model) are all pre-existing. The paper is more of a high-quality application and comparative study than a proposal of a fundamentally new neural network architecture. While this does not detract from its value, it's a work of insightful application and engineering rather than a breakthrough in core ML/neural methods.
2.  **Generalizability of Findings May Be Dataset-Dependent:** The success of the proposed models, particularly the single-head assumption inherent in both the dependency parsing and STag framings, seems heavily tied to the specific structure of the PE dataset, which is described as "almost-a-tree." The paper acknowledges this structure but could discuss the limitations more explicitly. In domains with more complex, graph-like argumentation (e.g., where a premise supports multiple claims), the proposed STag formulation with a single distance `d` would not be applicable without modification. The conclusions might not generalize to other AM corpora with different annotation schemes.
3.  **Ambiguity in Handling Claim-to-MajorClaim Links:** The paper states that claims link to *all* major claims, violating the tree structure. It then suggests this can be "loss-lessly" re-linked to one MC. However, the sequence tagging encoding for a claim is shown as `(B,C,⊥,For)`, with the distance `d` as `⊥`. It is unclear how the model learns or predicts this crucial link without an explicit target. This seems to be a significant simplification of the problem as defined in the gold data. If these links are handled by a special post-processing rule or are ignored in the evaluation, this should be stated clearly, as it could affect the fairness of the comparison to the ILP model, which explicitly handles global constraints.

### **Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the Claim-to-MajorClaim links: The `STagT` tag for a claim component is `(b, C, ⊥, For)`. How does the system establish a link from a Claim to a Major Claim if the distance `d` is not predicted? Is a heuristic used post-prediction (e.g., link every claim to the nearest preceding major claim)? A clarification on this point is essential to fully understand the model's capabilities and how it compares to the gold standard and the ILP baseline.
2.  The dramatic performance drop of LSTM-ER on the essay-level R-F1 is attributed to the quadratic explosion of candidate pairs. This is a known issue for such models. Did you consider any simple pruning strategies as a stronger baseline, such as limiting candidate relations to components within a certain sentence or token window? This might have provided a more competitive result for the LSTM-ER model on the essay level.
3.  Given the success of decoupling component and relation tasks (as seen in LSTM-ER's C-F1 and the MTL results), have you considered a two-stage neural model? For instance, first use a strong tagger (like STagBLCC) to identify component boundaries and types, and then use a second model (e.g., a graph neural network or a relation classifier) to predict relations only between the identified components. This would retain the decoupling benefit while avoiding the O(m^2) issue of considering all token pairs.
