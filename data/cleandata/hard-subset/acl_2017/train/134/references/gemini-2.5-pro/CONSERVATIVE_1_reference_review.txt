**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents an empirical study on applying existing neural network architectures to the task of end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem in four distinct ways: as token-level dependency parsing, as sequence tagging with a complex label set encoding relational information, as a multi-task learning problem, and by adapting a pre-existing joint entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). These neural approaches are evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset and compared against the established state-of-the-art, a feature-based Integer Linear Programming (ILP) pipeline model. The main findings are that neural sequence tagging models, particularly a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN and the LSTM-ER model, outperform the ILP baseline, demonstrating the viability of end-to-end neural methods that do not require hand-crafted features or constraints.

**Contributions of this work**

1.  The primary contribution is a comprehensive empirical comparison of several off-the-shelf neural architectures for the end-to-end AM task. This provides the community with a set of strong neural baselines on a standard dataset, moving beyond prior feature-engineered pipeline approaches.
2.  A secondary contribution is the demonstration that a relatively standard sequence tagging model, augmented with a carefully designed label schema to encode relational links and distances, can serve as a robust and effective method for this complex structured prediction task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough Comparative Evaluation:** The paper's main strength lies in its breadth. Instead of proposing a single model, the authors systematically investigate four different neural frameworks. This comparative study provides valuable insights into what types of architectural assumptions are suitable for this task. The inclusion of both positive and negative results (e.g., the poor performance of dependency parsing) is commendable and informative.
2.  **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The authors rightly compare their models against the established ILP-based system of Stab and Gurevych (2016). Outperforming this strong, feature-rich baseline provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed neural approaches and their ability to learn relevant features directly from data.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper offers a thoughtful analysis of the results. For instance, the discussion on why the LSTM-ER model excels on component detection but falters on relation detection at the essay level (due to the combinatorial explosion of candidate relations in longer documents) is insightful. This highlights the importance of structural biases, showing that the more constrained search space of the sequence tagging model is beneficial for longer texts.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Novelty:** The paper's core weakness is its reliance on existing, well-established neural architectures. The work is primarily an application of models from Ma and Hovy (2016), Miwa and Bansal (2016), and others to a new domain. While the "framings" are presented as contributions, they primarily consist of designing input/output representations for these existing models. The "STagT" label encoding, while clever, is an engineering choice rather than a fundamental advance in modeling. The paper successfully establishes new state-of-the-art results through careful application, but it does not introduce new methods.
2.  **Evaluation on a Single Dataset:** All experiments are conducted on the single PE dataset. The authors themselves state in the introduction that the notion of an "argument" is theory-dependent and that datasets differ significantly. This makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of their findings. Claims about the robustness of the sequence tagging approach, for example, would be much stronger if validated on a second dataset with a different annotation scheme or domain. The current results demonstrate effectiveness on one specific type of data structure, but not necessarily for AM more broadly.
3.  **Questionable Framing of Dependency Parsing:** The token-level dependency parsing approach appears to be a flawed formulation for this problem. Argumentative relations exist between multi-word components, not individual tokens. To model this, the authors create dense, artificial dependencies where every token in a component links to the first token of its head component. It is not surprising that this performed poorly, especially given the long-distance relations common in the data. This framing feels like a strawman, and its poor performance does not yield a particularly surprising or useful conclusion. A more meaningful comparison would have been to a system that parses pre-identified component spans, as noted in prior work.
4.  **Conflation of Model and Input Granularity:** The distinction between "paragraph level" and "essay level" evaluation is a critical but potentially confounding variable. The ILP baseline is reported to have access to both levels of information, giving it a potential advantage. The neural models, however, are trained and tested on one or the other. This makes a direct, controlled comparison difficult. The performance drop from paragraph to essay level is a function of both the model's ability to handle longer sequences and the increased complexity of the task itself. The experimental design does not cleanly disentangle these factors.
