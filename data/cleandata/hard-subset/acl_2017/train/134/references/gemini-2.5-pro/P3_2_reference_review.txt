### Summary of the paper
This paper presents a comprehensive empirical study on applying neural end-to-end models to the task of computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors aim to replace traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP) for constraint satisfaction. They systematically explore four different neural framings of the end-to-end AM task, which includes component segmentation, typing, and relation identification. The proposed framings are: (1) a token-level dependency parsing approach, (2) a sequence tagging approach with a novel, complex tag set that encodes component boundaries, type, relation distance, and relation type, (3) a multi-task learning setup to augment the sequence tagger, and (4) an adaptation of a state-of-the-art joint entity and relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). Experiments on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus show that the sequence tagging and joint entity-relation models significantly outperform the dependency parsing approach and a strong ILP-based baseline, establishing a new state-of-the-art. The analysis provides valuable insights into the trade-offs of each approach, particularly concerning document length and model constraints.

### Contributions
1.  **A thorough and insightful comparative study of different neural framings for a complex structured prediction task.** The primary contribution is not a single novel architecture, but rather the systematic exploration and analysis of how to conceptualize end-to-end AM for modern neural networks. The paper provides a clear empirical answer to which approaches (local sequence tagging, global parsing, decoupled entity-relation extraction) are most effective and why, offering valuable lessons for AM and related structured prediction tasks.
2.  **A novel and effective sequence tagging formulation for joint entity and relation extraction.** The STagT framing, which encodes relational information (distance to head component) directly into the token's tag, is a creative way to solve a graph/tree prediction problem with a fundamentally linear model. This method cleverly embeds structural constraints into the tagset, proving to be robust, especially for longer documents where more general models struggle.
3.  **Demonstration that feature-free, end-to-end neural models can surpass complex, feature-engineered systems in AM.** By outperforming the strong ILP-based system from Stab and Gurevych (2016), the paper makes a compelling case for the adoption of neural methods in this domain, obviating the need for laborious feature engineering and manual constraint design.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Experimental Design and Analysis.** The paper's main strength lies in its breadth and depth. The authors don't just propose one model; they compare four distinct and well-motivated conceptual approaches. The analysis is particularly strong, for instance, in the discussion of why the LSTM-ER model excels on shorter paragraph-level texts but degrades on essay-level relation finding. The insight that its generality becomes a weakness due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations in longer texts is crucial and well-supported by the results (Figure 4).
2.  **Clarity of Exposition.** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The different framings are explained with clarity and helpful examples (e.g., the tag sequence example in Sec. 4, Fig. 3 for the dependency representation). This makes a complex set of experiments and models easy to follow and understand. The distinction between models based on their modularity and implicit/explicit constraints is insightful.
3.  **Strong and Significant Results.** The work establishes a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark dataset for end-to-end AM. The performance gains over the ILP baseline are substantial, particularly for the LSTM-ER model on the paragraph level. This provides a strong practical motivation for the presented research. The negative results regarding the dependency parsing framing are also valuable, as they caution against a seemingly intuitive but ultimately ineffective approach for this token-level task.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Crucial Missing Detail in the STagT Decoding Process.** The sequence tagging approach (STagT) relies on predicting a distance `d` (measured in components) to the related component. However, the paper never explains how this distance is used to establish a link during decoding. At prediction time, the model outputs a sequence of tags. A post-processing step must then identify component spans and use the predicted `d` values to draw relation arcs. How is the target component identified? Does the system count `d` B-tags forwards or backwards? What happens if the predicted `d` points to a non-existent component or a location outside a valid component span? This is a critical methodological gap that undermines the completeness of the description of one of the paper's core proposals.
2.  **Generalizability Concerns due to Dataset Structure.** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (PE corpus) which has a highly constrained, near-tree structure (premises have one parent, claims link to all major claims). The success of the STagT model, which implicitly assumes a single head for each component via its distance encoding `d`, and the dependency parsing model, which explicitly enforces a tree structure, may be heavily reliant on this characteristic. The authors acknowledge this structure but do not sufficiently discuss the implications for generalizability. It is questionable whether these methods would work on AM datasets with more complex, graph-like structures (e.g., from online dialogues), where components can have multiple parents or relations are less constrained.
3.  **Limited Novelty in Underlying Architectures.** While the *framings* of the problem are novel, the paper relies heavily on existing, off-the-shelf neural architectures (Ma and Hovy 2016, Miwa and Bansal 2016, Dyer et al. 2015, etc.). The main technical novelty is the design of the tag vocabulary for STagT. While this is a valid and important type of contribution, the paper could be slightly more explicit in distinguishing its conceptual contributions (the framings) from architectural ones.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please elaborate on the decoding procedure for the STagT models? Specifically, once the tagger has produced a sequence of `(b, t, d, s)` tags for all tokens in a document, what is the algorithm used to (a) identify the component spans, and (b) resolve the relative distance `d` into a concrete link between two specific component spans?
2.  The STagT model's distance encoding `d` seems to inherently enforce a single-head constraint for each component. How do you envision this approach being adapted to argumentation structures that are not trees, where a premise might, for instance, support two different claims? Would this require a fundamental change to the tagging scheme?
