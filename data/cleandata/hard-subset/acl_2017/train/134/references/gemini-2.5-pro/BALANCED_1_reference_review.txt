### Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper presents a comprehensive empirical investigation into applying neural network models to the task of end-to-end argumentation mining (AM). Moving away from traditional pipeline systems that rely on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP) for joint inference, the authors explore four distinct neural framings of the problem: (1) as a token-level dependency parsing task, (2) as a sequence tagging task with a novel, complex label that encodes relational information, (3) as a multi-task learning problem, and (4) using an existing joint entity-relation extraction model (Miwa and Bansal, 2016). These approaches are systematically evaluated on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, comparing their performance on both paragraph-level and full essay-level inputs.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance for end-to-end AM.** The paper shows that carefully framed neural models, particularly those based on sequence tagging, outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP-based system, effectively setting a new baseline for this task and obviating the need for extensive feature engineering.
2.  **An insightful analysis of the trade-offs between different modeling choices for a structured prediction task.** By comparing architectures with varying levels of built-in constraints (from the globally-constrained dependency parser to the locally-constrained sequence tagger and the less-constrained entity-relation model), the paper provides valuable lessons on how model assumptions impact performance, particularly in dealing with the long-range dependencies common in AM.
3.  **The introduction and successful application of a novel sequence tagging scheme for joint component and relation extraction.** The proposed method of encoding the type, boundary, relation type, and relative distance to a linked component into a single token-level tag proves to be a robust and effective strategy, especially for longer documents.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Thorough and Systematic Investigation:** The paper’s greatest strength lies in its comprehensive and systematic comparison of multiple, well-motivated neural framings for AM. Rather than proposing a single model, the authors conduct a rigorous comparative study that maps out the landscape of potential neural solutions. This approach provides the community with a clear understanding of what works (sequence tagging, joint entity-relation models) and what does not (token-level dependency parsing) for this task. The inclusion of a strong ILP baseline properly contextualizes the results and underscores the practical benefits of the neural methods.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Insightful Analysis:** The paper reports new state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark dataset. The analysis extends beyond simple performance tables, offering convincing explanations for the observed outcomes. For instance, the discussion of why the LSTM-ER model excels on shorter paragraph-level texts but falters on longer essays (due to the combinatorial explosion of potential relations) is particularly insightful. This hypothesis is well-supported by both the results and the qualitative analysis in Figure 4, providing a clear takeaway for future work on relation extraction in long documents.
3.  **High-Quality Presentation and Experimental Design:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, and the different model framings are explained with lucid examples and figures. The experimental design decision to evaluate separately on paragraph and essay levels is commendable, as it allows for a controlled analysis of how sequence length affects the performance of each model, isolating a key challenge in the AM task.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application and framing of the methods for AM are novel and valuable, the core neural architectures themselves (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, Dyer et al.'s parser, the Miwa and Bansal model) are adaptations of existing work. The primary technical novelty is in the design of the label schemas for the sequence tagging and parsing approaches. The paper is transparent about this, but it positions the work primarily as a strong empirical and analytical contribution rather than a methodologically groundbreaking one. This is a minor weakness, as such thorough empirical studies are crucial for advancing the field.
2.  **Ambiguity in the Decoding Process for Sequence Tagging:** The proposed sequence tagging model (STagT) relies on a complex tag that includes the relative distance `d` (in number of components) to the target component. The paper does not sufficiently detail how this relative distance is resolved into a concrete link between two predicted components during inference. For example, if a token is predicted to be part of a premise that links to the component at distance `d=+2`, this requires knowing the locations of other predicted components. This decoding step is non-trivial and its implementation details could impact performance. A clearer explanation of this process is needed.
3.  **Evaluation on a Single Dataset/Domain:** All experiments are conducted on the PE dataset, which consists of student essays. While this is an important benchmark, the claims about the general effectiveness of different framings would be significantly strengthened by evaluation on at least one other AM dataset, preferably from a different domain (e.g., legal text, online discussions). This would help to assess whether the findings—such as the ineffectiveness of token-level parsing or the robustness of the distance-based tagging scheme—are generalizable or specific to the particular structure and characteristics of the PE corpus.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please clarify the decoding process for the STagT model? Specifically, at test time, how do you translate the predicted relative distance `d` in a tag into an absolute link between two (also predicted) argumentative components?
2.  Your analysis of the performance drop for LSTM-ER on the essay level is compelling. You argue that the STagT model's implicit constraints are beneficial. As a direct test of this hypothesis, have you considered applying similar structural constraints to the LSTM-ER model post-inference (e.g., forcing each premise to link to exactly one target)? This could help isolate whether the performance gap is due to the model's architecture or the lack of these domain-specific constraints.
3.  The MTL experiments show that adding auxiliary tasks is beneficial, but they are performed with a weaker base tagger (STagBL) than your main model (STagBLCC). Have you considered integrating the MTL framework into the stronger STagBLCC model? It would be interesting to see if these auxiliary tasks could provide a further boost to the state-of-the-art STagBLCC results.
