

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative components (premises, claims, major claims) and their relations (support, attack) in text. The authors frame AM in four different ways: (1) dependency parsing, (2) sequence tagging with distance encoding, (3) multi-task learning, and (4) a model combining sequential and tree structure information (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset and compare them to a feature-based ILP model.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them (in order of strength):

1. **Demonstration that neural end-to-end approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model** for computational AM, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and costly constraint design.
2. **Insight that framing AM as token-level dependency parsing is ineffective**, contrary to previous work on component-level parsing.
3. **Robust performance of sequence tagging with distance encoding** across different environments, which effectively captures long-range dependencies between argument components.
4. **Evidence that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks** (component detection and relation identification) improves performance on the full AM task.
5. **Clear empirical analysis explaining why certain approaches work better than others**, particularly regarding document length and the nature of argument structures.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to the AM field by introducing neural end-to-end approaches**: This is the first systematic study of neural networks for solving the full end-to-end AM problem. Previous work relied on pipeline approaches with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which are problematic due to error propagation and manual feature engineering. The authors demonstrate that neural approaches achieve better results than the state-of-the-art ILP model, providing a compelling argument for moving away from traditional feature-based methods in AM.

2. **Comprehensive and insightful empirical analysis**: The paper thoroughly evaluates multiple neural framings and provides a clear explanation for their relative performance. The authors' analysis of why dependency parsing fails at the token level (due to long sequences and the nature of argument structures) and why sequence tagging with distance encoding works well is particularly strong. Their observation that LSTM-ER performs better on components but worse on relations for longer documents provides valuable insights for future work.

3. **Clear demonstration of the benefits of multi-task learning**: The paper shows that adding auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) as subtasks improves overall performance, with component detection being more beneficial than relation identification. This finding aligns with recent trends in multi-task learning but provides specific evidence for the AM domain, which is valuable for practitioners.

4. **Strong empirical results on the PE dataset**: The neural approaches consistently outperform the ILP model across multiple metrics (C-F1, R-F1, global F1), with the STagBLCC model achieving state-of-the-art results. The paper also provides a thorough comparison of performance at both paragraph and essay levels, highlighting important practical considerations for real-world applications.

5. **Thoughtful discussion of limitations and future work**: The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approaches (e.g., LSTM-ER's degradation on longer documents) and suggest policy recommendations for different document lengths. The discussion of why certain approaches work better than others (e.g., the importance of distance encoding) provides valuable theoretical insights beyond just empirical results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty in the neural architectures used**: While the application to AM is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from the broader field of neural sequence labeling. The neural approaches (BiLSTMs, CRFs, multi-task learning) are standard techniques in NLP, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate what is novel about applying them to AM. The authors claim this is the "first neural end-to-end solution," but the novelty is primarily in the application, not in the neural methods themselves, which weakens the contribution claim.

2. **Insufficient comparison to more recent neural approaches**: The paper compares to an ILP model and some standard neural baselines, but doesn't evaluate against more recent state-of-the-art neural architectures for sequence labeling (e.g., Transformer-based models, contextualized embeddings like BERT). Given that the paper was submitted around 2016-2017, it's understandable, but the comparison is limited to the state of the art at that time, which has since advanced significantly.

3. **Overstated claims about the "end-to-end" nature of the approach**: The paper claims its approach is end-to-end, but the sequence tagging approach requires careful label design that encodes relations into the tag (a form of feature engineering). The ILP approach is also end-to-end in a different sense (solving the full problem with constraints). The paper doesn't clearly articulate why their approach is more truly end-to-end than previous work, which weakens their main claim.

4. **Lack of in-depth error analysis**: While the paper provides some error analysis in the supplementary material, it lacks detailed error analysis in the main text. A deeper analysis of where the models fail (e.g., specific types of arguments that are hard to identify, common error patterns) would strengthen the paper significantly and provide more actionable insights for future work.

5. **Insufficient discussion of the dataset limitations**: The paper uses the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which has a specific structure (with argumentation contained within paragraphs). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't thoroughly discuss how generalizable their findings are to other datasets with different structural properties. For instance, how would their approaches perform on datasets where argument structures span multiple paragraphs or documents?

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is a novel framing of AM. Could you elaborate on how this differs from previous work on relation extraction or entity recognition that might have used similar techniques (e.g., encoding relations into tags)?

2. The authors state that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, while component-level parsing has been successful. Could you explain why the token-level approach fails specifically, and whether you think there might be a way to modify the dependency parsing approach to make it work better at the token level for AM?

3. The paper doesn't compare to more recent neural architectures for sequence labeling (e.g., Transformer-based models). How do you think these might perform on the AM task, and would they provide additional improvements over the current models?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining and demonstrating their superiority over the previous state-of-the-art ILP model. The empirical findings are well-supported, and the analysis of why certain approaches work better than others is particularly strong.

However, the paper's claims about novelty are overstated, and the comparison to more recent neural approaches is limited. The lack of detailed error analysis and insufficient discussion of dataset limitations also weaken the paper.

I recommend **minor revision**. The authors should:
1. Clarify the novelty of their contribution (application to AM vs. neural architectures themselves)
2. Expand the comparison to include more recent neural approaches (even if just as a discussion point)
3. Provide more detailed error analysis in the main paper
4. Thoroughly discuss the limitations of the PE dataset and how their findings might generalize to other datasets

With these revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field and would be suitable for publication at ACL.
