

# Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the full AM problem (component segmentation, component typing, relation identification, and relation typing) without relying on hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures. The authors propose four main neural frameworks: (1) AM as dependency parsing, (2) AM as sequence tagging with distance encoding, (3) AM as multi-task learning, and (4) the LSTM-ER model. Their main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. The first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining (addressing the full AM problem directly without pipeline architectures)
2. Demonstrating that neural approaches outperform the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based model across multiple metrics
3. Providing valuable insights on modeling choices, particularly showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to component-level approaches), while sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to the field**: This is the first paper to present neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining. Previous work relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures that suffer from error propagation. The authors effectively demonstrate that neural approaches eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design while achieving superior results. This represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) consistently outperforms the ILP model across all metrics. For example, on the paragraph level, STagBLCC achieves 74.08% C-F1 (50%) and 39.83% R-F1 (50%), compared to the ILP model's 73.35% and 34.74%. The paper provides thorough experiments comparing different neural architectures and evaluation levels (paragraph vs. essay), demonstrating robustness of their approach.

3. **Important insights about modeling choices**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to what has been proposed for component-level approaches). The authors provide a clear explanation for this: AM relations often span multiple sentences (92% of relations are between components in different sentences), making dependency parsing's local context insufficient. This insight is crucial for future work in the field.

4. **Effective multi-task learning approach**: The paper demonstrates that adding natural subtasks (component detection and relation detection) as auxiliary tasks improves performance, with the best multi-task configuration (Y-3:YC-3:YR-3) achieving 54.55% C-F1 (100%) and 28.30% R-F1 (100%) on essay level. This finding is valuable not just for AM but for other sequence labeling tasks with complex dependencies.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of deeper analysis of why neural approaches work better**: While the paper shows that neural approaches outperform the ILP model, it doesn't sufficiently analyze *why* they work better. The authors mention that neural models don't require hand-crafted features, but this is superficial. A deeper analysis of how specific neural components (e.g., BiLSTM's ability to capture long-range dependencies) contribute to the improved performance would strengthen the paper. For instance, Figure 4 shows that STagBLCC has higher probability of correct relation identification for short distances, but doesn't explore why this pattern exists.

2. **Limited dataset and evaluation scope**: The paper evaluates exclusively on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which has specific characteristics (e.g., paragraphs as natural units, relations often spanning multiple sentences). This limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors should discuss how their approach would perform on other AM datasets with different annotation schemes and argument structures.

3. **Insufficient error analysis**: The paper provides performance metrics but lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding where the models fail (e.g., for long-range relations, for certain types of claims, or in specific argument structures) would provide valuable insights for future work. For example, the paper mentions that LSTM-ER degrades significantly on essay level for R-F1, but doesn't analyze the specific error patterns.

4. **Overemphasis on paragraph vs. essay level**: While the paper correctly identifies that paragraph-level evaluation is easier, it spends considerable time discussing this difference without adequately addressing the fundamental limitation that the dataset is structured in paragraphs. This is a dataset-specific limitation rather than a limitation of the approach, and the paper could better contextualize this.

5. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper compares primarily with the ILP model but doesn't adequately compare with other recent neural approaches for related tasks (e.g., relation extraction, dependency parsing). For instance, the paper mentions that LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level is due to the combinatorial explosion of possible relations, but doesn't compare with other approaches that might handle this better.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you explain the significant performance drop of the LSTM-ER model on essay level for relation identification (R-F1 drops from 45.52% to 29.56% on 50% match)? Is this primarily due to the combinatorial explosion of possible relations, or are there other factors?

2. The paper demonstrates that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM. Could you elaborate on why this is the case, given that argument structures often form trees? Is it primarily due to the token-level nature of the problem, or are there other architectural reasons?

3. The authors mention that "a model enforces a constraint does not necessarily mean that it is more suitable for a respective task." How do you reconcile this with the fact that the dependency parsing approach enforces a tree structure, which is consistent with the underlying argumentation theory, yet performs worse than the sequence tagging approach?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by being the first to present neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining. The empirical results are strong, and the insights about modeling choices (particularly regarding dependency parsing vs. sequence tagging) are significant. While the paper has some limitations in its evaluation scope and analysis depth, the core contributions are novel, well-motivated, and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
