- Neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (AM) outperform the prior feature-based ILP model.
- Token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to prior assumptions.
- Framing AM as sequence tagging with distance encoding is simple and effective.
- Multi-task learning with component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks improves performance.
- First comprehensive empirical study showing neural end-to-end methods can beat traditional ILP for AM.
- Corrects the misconception that token-level dependency parsing is suitable for AM.
- Sequence tagging with distance-encoded tags avoids complex parsing while delivering strong performance.
- Careful analysis shows which neural framings succeed and why, offering guidance for future work.
- Demonstrates that auxiliary tasks in multi-task learning benefit the main AM task.
- Neural models achieve substantial advancement over state-of-the-art without hand-crafted features.
- Evaluation is limited to a single dataset (PE), reducing generalizability across annotation schemes.
- The paper lacks detailed analysis explaining why token-level dependency parsing fails.
- Comparison with the ILP model may be unfair or unclear regarding paragraph vs. essay level evaluation.
- The manuscript provides little error analysis, especially for relation performance drops at essay level.
- Claim about avoiding error propagation is questionable and needs clarification since models still follow pipeline-like predictions.
- Request for clarification on how the approach would generalize to other AM datasets with different annotations.
- Request for concrete examples illustrating dependency parser failures on the data.
- Ask to clarify how the ILP model was applied at paragraph level to match neural model evaluations.
- Request distributional details (e.g., avg. relations per document) to explain LSTM-ER’s essay-level relation degradation.
- Ask for additional evidence supporting the sequence tagging approach's robustness beyond the PE dataset.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with significant contribution; accept with minor revisions to address dataset scope and analysis gaps.
