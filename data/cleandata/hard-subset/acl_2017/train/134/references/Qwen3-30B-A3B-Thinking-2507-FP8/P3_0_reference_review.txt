

# Peer Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary and Contributions
This paper explores neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), moving beyond traditional pipeline methods that rely on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The authors propose four neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER. Their main claims are: (1) neural end-to-end AM is superior to the state-of-the-art ILP model, (2) token-based dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to component-level approaches), (3) sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly, and (4) multi-task learning with component detection as auxiliary task improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Significant performance improvement over state-of-the-art**: The authors demonstrate clear empirical superiority of their neural approaches over the ILP-based method of Stab and Gurevych (2016), with gains of 10-13% in F1 scores. This is a major contribution to the field, as it shows neural methods can effectively handle the complex, interdependent tasks of AM without manual feature engineering.

2. **Insightful analysis of why certain approaches work better**: The paper provides valuable theoretical and empirical explanations for the observed performance differences. Their analysis of how distance encoding in sequence tagging captures long-range dependencies (with 66% of relations having distance ±2 or less) and why dependency parsing fails on longer essays due to combinatorial explosion of possible trees is particularly strong.

3. **Practical guidance for future work**: The paper offers concrete recommendations for different document lengths ("short documents" benefit from general frameworks like LSTM-ER, while "long documents" require more restrained modeling), which provides actionable insights for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The authors claim to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions," but Miwa and Bansal (2016) already proposed a neural end-to-end system for entity and relation extraction that could be applied to AM. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their contributions from this prior work, particularly since they use LSTM-ER as a baseline. This weakens the novelty claim and makes the contribution seem less significant.

2. **Inadequate justification for token-level dependency parsing failure**: The paper concludes token-based dependency parsing is ineffective, but the evidence is limited to poor performance on essay-level data. They don't explore whether token-level parsing might work better on shorter documents or with architectural modifications (e.g., attention mechanisms). The claim that dependency parsing "isn't suitable" for token-level AM seems premature without exploring alternative neural parsing architectures.

3. **Distance encoding limitations**: The sequence tagging approach encodes distance in terms of "number of components" between related elements, which is problematic because component counts vary significantly across documents. A more robust approach would use token or sentence distance, which would be consistent across documents. The paper doesn't address this limitation or provide analysis of how distance encoding affects performance across documents of varying lengths.

4. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions dependency parsing becomes memory-intensive on essay level but doesn't provide comparative training or inference times for the different approaches. For practical deployment, this information is crucial but missing.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your sequence tagging approach with distance encoding in terms of component count compare to using token-based distance measures? Would this improve consistency across documents of varying lengths?

2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with Miwa and Bansal (2016), specifically explaining how your multi-task sequence tagging approach differs from and improves upon their LSTM-ER framework for AM?

3. The paper states that LSTM-ER's relation identification degrades significantly on essay level due to increased number of possible relations. Could you provide a quantitative analysis of how the number of possible relations increases with document length, and how this specifically impacts LSTM-ER's performance?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can significantly outperform traditional feature-based ILP methods for argumentation mining. The empirical results are strong and the analysis of why certain approaches work better is insightful. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations in distance encoding and providing a more nuanced comparison with prior neural approaches. With these clarifications, this paper would represent a substantial contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining.

The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and provides clear guidance for future work. With minor revisions to address the novelty claim and distance encoding limitations, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL.
