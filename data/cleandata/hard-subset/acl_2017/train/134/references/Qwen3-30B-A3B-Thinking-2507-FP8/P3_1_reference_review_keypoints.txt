Summary: Introduces the first neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining using four architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) and claims to outperform the ILP baseline while removing hand-crafted features.

Main contributions:
- First comprehensive exploration of neural end-to-end approaches for AM beyond pipeline architectures.
- Demonstrates dependency parsing is ineffective for token-level AM, contrary to prior component-level work.
- Shows sequence tagging with distance encoding achieves robust practical performance across settings.

Strengths:
- Strong motivation and problem framing, highlighting pipeline limitations and theory arbitrariness.
- Rigorous evaluation on the PE dataset with paragraph/essay-level analyses and comparisons to ILP.
- Convincing empirical evidence that dependency parsing performs poorly for token-level AM.
- Practical MTL insight: auxiliary component detection (C) helps more than relation identification (R).

Weaknesses:
- Claim of eliminating feature engineering is misleading because distance encoding is a form of hand-designed feature.
- Insufficient explanation/analysis of why sequence tagging works and how it handles long-range dependencies.
- Limited comparisons to other neural approaches (e.g., Transformer/other sequence-labeling architectures).
- Overstates elimination of ILP constraints—sequence tagging enforces implicit constraints similar to ILP.
- Lacks analysis of failure cases or error patterns (e.g., long-distance vs short-distance relation errors).

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive is the distance-encoded sequence tagging to the dataset's relation-distance distribution and would it generalize to different distributions?
- Why did the specific dependency parsing head assignment perform poorly, and were alternative token-level dependency formulations tried?
- How do you reconcile the claim of eliminating hand-crafted features with the use of distance encoding, and what alternatives would you consider?
