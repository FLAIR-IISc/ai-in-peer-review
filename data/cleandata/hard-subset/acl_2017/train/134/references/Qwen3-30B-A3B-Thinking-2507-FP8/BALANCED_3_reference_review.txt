

# Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative units (claims, premises), classifying them, and finding relations between them. The authors propose four neural network-based framings: (1) AM as dependency parsing, (2) AM as sequence tagging with a specialized label set encoding distance information, (3) AM as multi-task sequence tagging, and (4) adapting the LSTM-ER model.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):

1. **First comprehensive study of neural end-to-end approaches for AM**: The paper provides the first systematic exploration of neural approaches to solve the full AM task without relying on hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures, addressing a significant gap in the literature.

2. **Demonstration of sequence tagging with distance encoding as the most effective approach**: The authors show that a specialized sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) outperforms both the state-of-the-art ILP model and other neural approaches, with a robust performance across different environments.

3. **Insightful analysis of why dependency parsing fails for end-to-end AM**: The paper provides a clear explanation for why dependency parsing (previously used in component-level AM) is ineffective for token-level end-to-end AM, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to a neglected area**: The paper addresses an important gap in argumentation mining literature by demonstrating that neural approaches can solve the full end-to-end AM task without manual feature engineering. Previous work relied heavily on hand-crafted features and pipeline approaches, which the authors correctly identify as problematic for the "arbitrary" nature of argumentation.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) against the state-of-the-art ILP model at both paragraph and essay levels. The evaluation is well-designed, with careful attention to the nuances of the AM task (e.g., exact vs. approximate matching).

3. **Insightful analysis of model limitations**: The paper provides valuable insights into why certain approaches fail, particularly the dependency parsing approach. The authors correctly identify that dependency parsers struggle with long sequences (essay level) and that the tree structure assumption in dependency parsing is inappropriate for the token-level end-to-end AM problem. Their explanation of why the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding works well is particularly strong.

4. **Practical recommendations**: The authors provide clear, actionable recommendations for AM practitioners (e.g., using paragraph-level processing for longer texts, using multi-task learning to improve component detection), which is valuable for real-world applications.

5. **Clear problem framing**: The sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is well-motivated and clearly explained, making it easy to understand how the authors have adapted a standard NLP technique to the specific challenges of AM.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art neural approaches**: The paper compares to the ILP model and a few neural baselines but fails to compare with more recent neural architectures that have become standard in NLP (e.g., BERT-based models). This is a significant limitation given the paper's claim of achieving "state-of-the-art results."

2. **Insufficient justification for the paragraph-level processing**: While the authors note that paragraph-level processing yields better results, they don't adequately explain why this is the case or whether this approach truly represents "end-to-end" learning from unannotated plain text. The paper's claim of "end-to-end" learning is weakened by this paragraph-level restriction, especially since the authors acknowledge that the dataset has paragraph boundaries that are not present in real-world text.

3. **Limited ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to determine which design choices are truly necessary. For example, it would be valuable to know whether the distance encoding is essential or whether simpler approaches would work just as well.

4. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper lacks a detailed analysis of where the best model (STagBLCC) fails, which would help identify remaining challenges in the field. A more thorough error analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims to be "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but the authors don't cite Potash et al. (2017), which also presents a neural end-to-end approach using pointer networks. This omission weakens the novelty claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural end-to-end solution for AM, but Potash et al. (2017) presented a pointer network-based approach for argumentation mining. Why isn't this compared to in the paper?

2. The authors use paragraph-level processing for most experiments, but claim to address "end-to-end" learning from unannotated plain text. How do they justify using paragraph-level processing as a proxy for end-to-end learning, especially since the final goal is to process entire essays?

3. The paper states that "LSTM-ER's good performance on components is due to its de-coupling of component and relation tasks," but the authors also show that their multi-task approach achieves similar performance for components. Can the authors clarify why their multi-task approach can capture this advantage without explicit de-coupling?

4. The paper doesn't compare to BERT or other transformer-based models, which have become standard in NLP. How would the proposed approaches perform if augmented with contextual embeddings, and why weren't these compared?

5. The authors claim that "dependency parsing is ineffective" for end-to-end AM. What specific aspects of the dependency parsing framework (e.g., the tree structure assumption, the parsing algorithm) make it ineffective, and how could this be addressed to make dependency parsing suitable for token-level AM?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the paragraph-level processing limitation. The authors acknowledge that "the argument structure in each essay is—almost—a tree" and that "paragraphs have an average length of 66 tokens and are therefore much shorter than essays." This suggests that the paragraph-level advantage is partly due to the data structure rather than a fundamental limitation of the models. A more thorough analysis of this relationship would strengthen the paper.

The paper's conclusion that "a more restrained modeling is preferable" for long documents is valuable, but the authors could provide more specific guidance on when to use which approach (e.g., based on document length thresholds).

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution by being the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end approaches for AM, and the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is well-motivated and performs well. However, the lack of comparison to more recent neural approaches (like BERT-based models) and the insufficient justification for paragraph-level processing as an end-to-end solution are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The authors should also clarify the novelty claim by addressing Potash et al. (2017).

With revisions addressing these concerns, particularly the comparison to more recent neural approaches and a better justification for the paragraph-level processing, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
