

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes neural end-to-end approaches for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and evaluating LSTM-ER. The authors claim their neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. They evaluate on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing neural methods against the ILP baseline.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of neural approaches against the established ILP baseline on a standard AM dataset.
2. The paper demonstrates that neural approaches can eliminate the need for hand-crafted features in AM.
3. The paper provides detailed experimental analysis of different neural approaches for AM, including insights about when different approaches work better (e.g., for short vs. long documents).

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive empirical comparison with strong baseline**: The paper provides a thorough comparison between neural approaches and the established ILP baseline (Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which is a significant contribution. The results show that neural approaches (particularly STagBLCC) outperform the ILP baseline by a substantial margin on both exact and approximate matching (e.g., C-F1(100%) 59.34% vs 60.32% for ILP on paragraph level). This is valuable because it directly addresses whether neural methods can replace feature engineering in AM, a practical concern for the field.

2. **Detailed analysis of approach limitations**: The paper provides insightful analysis of why certain approaches work better than others. For example, they demonstrate that sequence tagging approaches (STagBLCC) outperform dependency parsing approaches, and they explain that LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level is due to the combinatorial explosion of possible relations in longer texts. This analysis is more valuable than simply claiming "neural approaches work better."

3. **Methodologically sound experimental design**: The paper uses the same dataset as the previous state-of-the-art work, ensuring a fair comparison. They provide detailed results for both paragraph-level and essay-level evaluation, which is important for understanding the scalability of their approaches. The evaluation metrics (C-F1 and R-F1 at 100% and 50% matching levels) are well-chosen and consistent with prior work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the neural approaches**: The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM" but fails to demonstrate that the neural approaches themselves are novel. The methods used (BiLSTMs, CRFs, multi-task learning) are standard in NLP and have been applied to other tasks before. The novelty is in applying these standard approaches to AM, not in the approaches themselves. This is a common pattern in NLP papers, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate what makes this application novel beyond the domain shift.

2. **Insufficient evidence of generalizability**: The paper only evaluates on one dataset (the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych, 2016). This is a major limitation because it's not clear whether the results generalize to other AM datasets or domains. The authors claim their approach yields "new state-of-the-art results," but without evaluation on other datasets, this claim is premature. The paper doesn't even mention other AM datasets like Peldszus and Stede's (2016) corpus, which is a significant oversight.

3. **Lack of explanation for why neural approaches work better**: While the results show neural approaches outperform the ILP baseline, the paper doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why this is the case. They mention that neural approaches can handle long-range dependencies better, but don't provide evidence of this in their results (e.g., they don't analyze error cases where long-range dependencies are present). The paper states that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective" but doesn't provide a clear analysis of why this is the case, especially since they're using a neural parser that should be better at handling long-range dependencies.

4. **Overstated claims about superiority**: The paper makes strong claims about the superiority of neural approaches ("the first neural end-to-end solutions," "perform better than the state-of-the-art") without providing sufficient evidence. For example, they claim that "neural computational AM is as good or (substantially) better than a competing feature-based ILP formulation" but don't discuss the trade-offs between the two approaches (e.g., computational resources, data requirements, interpretability). The ILP model has advantages in terms of interpretability and constraint satisfaction that the neural approaches don't address.

5. **Inadequate justification for the sequence tagging approach**: The paper claims that their sequence tagging approach with distance encoding works well, but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this is a fundamental improvement over standard sequence tagging. The distance encoding is a clever idea, but it's not clear how much of the performance gain is due to this specific encoding versus the neural architecture itself. The paper doesn't compare against standard sequence tagging approaches on this dataset.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why this is the case, especially given that they're using a neural parser that should handle long-range dependencies better than traditional parsers. Can you provide more detailed analysis of the dependency parsing results to explain why this approach fails compared to sequence tagging?

2. The paper only evaluates on one dataset (the PE dataset). Can you provide results on other AM datasets (e.g., Peldszus and Stede's 2016 corpus) to demonstrate that your approach generalizes beyond the PE dataset?

3. The paper states that "neural computational AM is as good or (substantially) better than a competing feature-based ILP formulation." Can you provide a more nuanced comparison that discusses the trade-offs between the two approaches (e.g., computational resources, data requirements, interpretability)?

4. The paper mentions that LSTM-ER performs well on paragraph level but degrades on essay level. Can you provide more detailed analysis of these degradation patterns, particularly for long-range dependencies, and explain why your sequence tagging approach handles these better?

5. The paper claims that neural approaches eliminate the need for hand-crafted features, but doesn't provide evidence that this makes the approach more robust to changes in dataset or annotation scheme. Can you provide evidence that your neural approach would work well on a different AM dataset with different annotation guidelines?

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak rejection**. While the paper provides a thorough empirical comparison and some useful insights, the lack of novelty in the neural approaches, insufficient evidence of generalizability, and overstated claims about superiority prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper would need to provide more evidence of novelty (e.g., a truly novel neural architecture or significant theoretical contribution) and demonstrate generalizability across multiple datasets to warrant acceptance. The current work is an interesting application of standard neural approaches to a new domain, but it doesn't represent a significant advancement in the field.
