

# Peer Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper proposes neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the full AM task (component segmentation, component classification, relation identification, and relation classification) without relying on hand-crafted features or ILP constraints. The authors investigate four neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and the LSTM-ER model. They evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), finding that neural end-to-end approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, with sequence tagging being particularly effective. The paper also demonstrates that dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM (contrary to previous assumptions), and that multi-task learning with component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

## Main Contributions
1. The first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining that eliminate the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.
2. Demonstration that a neural sequence tagging model with distance encoding outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model.
3. Insight that framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem is ineffective (contrary to previous work that assumed it would work at the component level).
4. Evidence that multi-task learning with component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks significantly improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Methodological Contribution**: The paper provides the first neural end-to-end solutions for AM, addressing a critical limitation in the field. Previous approaches relied on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which are difficult to adapt across different datasets and argumentation theories. The neural approaches eliminate this burden and demonstrate clear performance improvements over the state-of-the-art ILP model, with the STagBLCC model achieving 55.22% global F1 on paragraph level compared to the ILP's 55.23% (with statistical significance).

2. **Insightful Analysis of Different Framings**: The paper provides valuable comparative analysis of different neural framings, particularly the explanation for why dependency parsing fails for end-to-end AM (unlike previous work at the component level). The authors demonstrate that the LSTM-Parser's performance drops drastically from paragraph to essay level (from 51.11% to 32.84% C-F1 100%), while sequence tagging models remain robust. This insight is crucial for future AM research.

3. **Effective Multi-Task Learning Framework**: The paper demonstrates that adding component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup significantly improves performance (e.g., Y-3:YC-3:YR-3 achieves 54.55% C-F1 100% compared to 49.59% for the main task alone). This provides a useful framework for future structured prediction tasks in NLP.

4. **Strong Empirical Evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, with careful analysis of different model variants and levels of evaluation (paragraph vs. essay). The paper also provides detailed error analysis (e.g., the distribution of relation distances in Figure 2 and the probability of correct relation identification given true distance in Figure 4), which helps explain model behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (PE), which may not be representative of the broader AM task. The paper does not discuss how these models would perform on other AM datasets, such as the argumentative microtexts from Peldszus and Stede (2016), which have different annotation schemes and complexity. This limits the generalizability of the claims.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Limitations**: While the paper identifies that LSTM-ER degrades on essay level due to combinatorial explosion of possible relations, it doesn't provide a systematic analysis of error types. For example, how often does the model incorrectly predict relations when there are many components, and what types of errors are most common? This information would be valuable for future work.

3. **Unfair Comparison with ILP Model**: The paper compares neural models trained and evaluated on paragraph level to the ILP model, which has access to paragraph information (knowing which paragraph in an essay it's in). This is particularly advantageous for major claims, which always occur in first or last paragraphs. The ILP model's performance on essay level is also comparable to the neural models' paragraph-level performance, making the comparison potentially misleading.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Long-Range Dependencies**: While the paper notes that related components are typically close in textual space (Figure 2), it doesn't thoroughly explore how the sequence tagging approach handles cases where the distance between related components exceeds the observed distribution (e.g., beyond 10 components). This is a significant limitation for real-world applications where long-range dependencies might occur.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you expect your sequence tagging approach with distance encoding to handle cases where the distance between related components exceeds the maximum observed in your dataset (e.g., >10 components apart)?

2. Could you provide more details on how the multi-task learning setup could be generalized to other structured prediction tasks in NLP beyond argumentation mining?

3. Have you considered evaluating your models on other AM datasets to assess generalizability beyond the PE dataset?

4. What are your thoughts on addressing the essay-level performance drop for dependency parsers? Would incorporating document-level context or hierarchical modeling help?

## Additional Comments

The paper makes a strong case for neural end-to-end approaches in AM and provides valuable insights into why certain framing approaches work better than others. The work represents an important step forward in the field, though the limitations mentioned above should be addressed in future work. The writing is clear and the technical contributions are well-explained, making this a solid contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining.
