

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative units (premises, claims, major claims), classifying them, and determining their relationships (support/attack). The authors compare several neural approaches: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and a model combining sequential and tree structure information (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**
1. The paper provides the first comprehensive study of neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining, addressing a significant gap in the literature where previous work relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline approaches.
2. The paper demonstrates that sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagT) outperforms the previous state-of-the-art ILP-based approach, particularly when evaluated on the paragraph level, with robust performance across different environments.
3. The paper provides important insights about the limitations of different neural approaches, showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to what has been proposed for component-level approaches), while sequence tagging with distance encoding works well.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Contribution to the Field**: The paper addresses a meaningful gap in computational argumentation mining by presenting the first neural end-to-end approaches. Previous work relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures with ILP constraints, which are problematic due to error propagation and the need for manual feature engineering. The authors successfully demonstrate that neural approaches can eliminate these limitations while achieving better or comparable performance.

2. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation of multiple neural approaches against a strong ILP-based baseline. The results are well-presented with detailed analysis of different framing techniques (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER). The paper clearly shows that sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagT) outperforms the previous state-of-the-art, particularly on the paragraph level (e.g., STagBLCC achieves 66.69% C-F1 (50%) and 39.83% R-F1 (50%) on paragraph level, compared to ILP's 62.61% and 34.74%).

3. **Important Theoretical Insights**: The paper provides valuable insights about the limitations of different neural approaches, particularly the counterintuitive finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to component-level approaches). The authors explain this by noting that AM relationships often span multiple sentences (92% of relationships are between components in different sentences), making dependency parsing's local context insufficient. This is a significant theoretical contribution that guides future work in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Analysis of Essay vs. Paragraph Performance Gap**: The paper reports a dramatic performance drop when moving from paragraph to essay level (e.g., STagBLCC's C-F1 (100%) drops from 59.34% to 44.02% and R-F1 (100%) from 74.08% to 44.38%). While the paper mentions this is due to longer sequences, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *why* this happens or how to address it. The authors should investigate whether this is a fundamental limitation of their approach or if architectural modifications could mitigate this issue. Without addressing this, the practical applicability of their approach is limited.

2. **Inadequate Justification for "First" Claim**: The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM" (contribution 1), but the references include Potash et al. (2017) titled "Here's my point: Argumentation Mining with Pointer Networks," which appears to be a neural end-to-end approach. The authors should clarify why this work is not considered in their contribution statement or why it's not comparable to their approach. This undermines the novelty claim.

3. **Lack of Generalization Analysis**: The paper only evaluates on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). It doesn't discuss how well their approach generalizes to other argumentation datasets, which is crucial for establishing the broader applicability of their method. The authors should provide at least a brief analysis of how their approach might perform on other datasets or discuss the challenges of adapting it to different argumentation structures.

4. **Insufficient Discussion of Practical Implications**: While the paper demonstrates better performance on the paragraph level, it doesn't adequately address how this translates to real-world applications where essays (not paragraphs) are the unit of analysis. The authors should discuss the practical implications of their findings and potential workarounds for the essay-level performance drop.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural end-to-end solution for computational AM, but Potash et al. (2017) appears to have proposed a neural end-to-end approach using pointer networks. Can you clarify why this work isn't considered in your contributions and why it's not comparable to your approach?

2. The paper reports a dramatic performance drop when moving from paragraph to essay level. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why this happens (e.g., is it due to the model's inability to handle longer sequences, or are there other factors?) and what architectural changes might be needed to address this issue?

3. The paper mentions that the ILP model has access to paragraph-level information, which gives it an advantage. How would you address this in a fair comparison if you were to evaluate all systems on the essay level?

## Additional Comments

- The paper mentions that 92% of relationships are between components in different sentences, but it doesn't explicitly connect this to why dependency parsing fails. Could you elaborate on this connection more clearly?

- The paper discusses the benefits of multi-task learning, but the results for MTL (STagBL) are generally weaker than STagBLCC. This seems counterintuitive given the literature on multi-task learning. Could you provide more explanation for this result?

- The paper's conclusion states that "when there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable." However, the paper doesn't provide a clear definition of what constitutes "many entities" or how to determine this threshold in practice. This is an important consideration for practical applications.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by introducing neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining and demonstrating their superiority over previous methods. The empirical results are solid, and the theoretical insights about the limitations of dependency parsing are valuable. However, the paper's claims of novelty are undermined by the Potash et al. (2017) reference, and the significant performance drop on essay-level evaluation is not sufficiently addressed.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly the novelty claim and the essay-level performance gap. With appropriate revisions, this paper could make a strong contribution to the field.
