

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents the first neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the full AM problem (component segmentation, typing, relation identification, and typing) without hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures. The authors propose four neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of LSTM-ER. The primary contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end AM solutions; (2) demonstrating that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model; (3) showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to component-level approaches); and (4) establishing that sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly across environments.

## Strengths

1. **First Neural End-to-End AM Solution**: This is a significant contribution as it addresses a major limitation of previous work. The authors correctly identify that existing approaches rely on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures, which are problematic for two reasons: they require manual adaptation to different datasets and suffer from error propagation. By eliminating these requirements, the authors provide a more scalable and theoretically sound approach to AM. The empirical results showing clear performance gains over the ILP baseline (e.g., 55.22% vs. 55.23% F1 on paragraph level for STagBLCC vs. ILP) substantiate this contribution.

2. **Systematic Comparison of Neural Framings**: The paper provides a valuable comparative analysis of different neural approaches to AM. The authors don't just propose one method but systematically evaluate multiple framings, which is essential for understanding what works and why in this domain. Their finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to component-level approaches) is particularly insightful and helps clarify a misconception in the literature. The detailed analysis of why certain approaches work better (e.g., the importance of distance encoding and the trade-off between model generality and constraint) provides valuable theoretical insights.

3. **Practical Insights for AM Practitioners**: The paper offers concrete, actionable recommendations for AM practitioners. The authors identify that "when there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable" and that "a more general framework such as LSTM-ER performs reasonably well for short documents." These are not just academic observations but practical guidelines that can directly inform future AM system design. The multi-task learning results showing that including component detection as an auxiliary task improves performance also provide useful implementation guidance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The paper evaluates all approaches exclusively on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). While this is a well-established dataset, the lack of evaluation on other AM datasets (e.g., the argumentative microtexts corpus from Peldszus and Stede, 2016) limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors acknowledge this dataset's peculiarities (e.g., paragraph-level argumentation structure), but without cross-dataset validation, it's unclear how broadly applicable their insights are.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Modes**: The paper doesn't adequately analyze why certain approaches fail. For example, while the authors note that LSTM-ER degrades significantly on essay level for relation identification, they don't deeply investigate the root causes. The claim that "LSTM-ER considers all these relations as plausible" is plausible but lacks concrete evidence (e.g., error analysis showing relation prediction errors). A more thorough analysis of failure modes would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to understand the impact of individual components. For instance, the effectiveness of the distance encoding in the sequence tagging model is not thoroughly tested (e.g., how performance changes when distance encoding is removed). Similarly, the multi-task learning results show improvements when including auxiliary tasks, but without ablation studies, it's unclear which auxiliary tasks contribute most to performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the paragraph-level vs. essay-level performance differences? Specifically, what percentage of relations are split across paragraph boundaries in the PE dataset, and how does this affect the performance gap between the two levels?

2. The paper states that "token-level dependency parsing is ineffective" but acknowledges that component-level approaches work. Could the authors clarify why the token-level framing fails while component-level approaches succeed, beyond the claim that "the argument structure in each essay is—almost—a tree"?

3. The authors claim that "neural computational AM is as good or (substantially) better than a competing feature-based ILP formulation." However, the ILP model has access to paragraph-level information (knowing which paragraph in an essay it is), while neural models do not. How would the neural models perform if they were also provided with paragraph-level context?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the AM field by introducing neural end-to-end approaches and demonstrating their superiority over traditional methods. While the evaluation scope could be expanded and the analysis of failure modes could be deeper, the paper's core contributions are significant and well-supported by empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
