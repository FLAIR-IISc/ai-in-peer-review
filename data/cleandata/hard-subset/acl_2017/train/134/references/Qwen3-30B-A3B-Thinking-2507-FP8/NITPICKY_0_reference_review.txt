

# ACL Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a combination of sequential and tree structure information. The authors claim to present the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, showing that some of their approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)

1. A systematic exploration of different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
2. Demonstrating that neural sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagT) performs well on this task, with the STagBLCC variant outperforming the previous state-of-the-art ILP model.
3. Providing evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to previous approaches that operated on the component level.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of neural networks to end-to-end AM**: The paper addresses an important problem in computational linguistics with a fresh perspective, demonstrating that neural approaches can outperform traditional pipeline methods without requiring hand-crafted features.

2. **Comprehensive comparison of different neural approaches**: The paper thoroughly evaluates multiple neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER) on the same dataset, providing valuable insights into what works well for this specific task.

3. **Clear analysis of why certain approaches work better**: The paper provides a detailed explanation for why token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (due to the long-range dependencies in the data), which is a valuable theoretical contribution.

4. **State-of-the-art results on the PE dataset**: The STagBLCC model achieves the best results on the PE dataset, which is a significant contribution to the field.

5. **Practical insights for future work**: The paper provides clear recommendations about when to use different approaches (e.g., "when there are few entities in a text, a more general framework such as that provided in LSTM-ER performs reasonably well. When there are many entities, a more restrained modeling is preferable").

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent notation and terminology throughout the paper**:
   - The paper uses "⊥" inconsistently to indicate "not filled," but in the sequence tagging example, it's used in confusing ways (e.g., "(O,⊥,⊥,⊥)" where O is already indicating non-argumentative).
   - The relation type "For" is used inconsistently with "Support" (e.g., "(B,C,⊥,For)" in the example, but "Support" in the label definition).
   - The paper switches between "component" and "entity" when referring to argumentative units, which is confusing as "entity" is typically used in NER contexts.
   - In Table 2, "Y-3" refers to a specific layer in the BiLSTM, but the paper doesn't explain what "Y-3" means, making the table difficult to interpret.

2. **Technical errors and misleading statements**:
   - The paper claims "the LSTM-Parser is over 100% better than Kiperwasser on exact spans," which is a misleading way to state the results. If Kiperwasser has 32.84% and LSTM-Parser has 68.20%, then LSTM-Parser is 35.36 percentage points better, not "over 100% better."
   - The paper states "there are O(2m^2) possible relations between m discovered components," which is incorrect. The correct complexity is O(m^2), not O(2m^2).
   - In the "Further remarks" section, the paper states "92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences," but the data shows that paragraphs have an average length of 66 tokens, which typically contains 4-5 sentences. The paper doesn't clarify whether these relationships are within the same paragraph (across sentences) or between different paragraphs.

3. **Lack of clarity on experimental setup and reproducibility**:
   - The paper states "We relegate issues of pre-trained word embeddings, hyperparameter optimization and further practical issues to the supplementary material" without providing a link to the supplementary material in the main paper.
   - The paper doesn't provide details on hardware specifications, memory requirements, or training times, which would be helpful for reproducibility.
   - The claim that "training them on essay level was typically too memory-exhaustive" lacks specific details about the memory constraints.

4. **Inconsistent claims about the ILP model**:
   - The paper states "the ILP model has access to both paragraph and essay level information," but the ILP model was trained on paragraph level, so it wouldn't have access to essay-level information during prediction. This is confusing and inconsistent with the experimental setup described.

5. **Poorly explained contributions**:
   - The paper claims that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective—in contrast to what has been proposed for systems that operate on the coarser component level," but the paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison of this approach to other token-level approaches.
   - The claim that "the ILP model has access to both paragraph and essay level information" is misleading and inconsistent with the experimental setup.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "the number of possible trees to consider on n tokens may just be too huge a search space when n is large." Can you provide specific numbers or references to support this claim, especially in the context of the PE dataset?

2. The paper claims "LSTM-Parser is over 100% better than Kiperwasser on exact spans." Can you clarify what you mean by "over 100% better" and provide the exact percentage points improvement?

3. The paper states "92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences." Can you clarify whether these relationships are between different sentences within the same paragraph or between different paragraphs in an essay?

4. The paper uses "For" as a relation type in the example but uses "Support" in the label definition. Which is the correct terminology, and why is there inconsistency?

5. The paper claims "the ILP model has access to both paragraph and essay level information." How is this implemented, given that the ILP model was trained on paragraph level?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper presents a novel application of neural networks to computational argumentation mining and demonstrates that some neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, the numerous inconsistencies in notation, terminology, and technical details significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot overlook these issues, which suggest a lack of attention to detail in the writing and presentation of the work. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these inconsistencies before it can be considered for publication at ACL.

The paper's potential contribution is valuable, but the execution is flawed in ways that make it difficult to evaluate the technical merits of the work. The inconsistencies and technical errors prevent me from being confident in the results and conclusions presented.
