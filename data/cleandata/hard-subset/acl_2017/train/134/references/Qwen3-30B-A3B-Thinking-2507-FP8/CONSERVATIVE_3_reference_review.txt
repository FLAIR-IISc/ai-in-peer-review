

# Review for ACL Submission: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper proposes several neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a complex label set, multi-task learning, and adapting an existing LSTM-ER model. The authors claim their neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) on the PE dataset while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The paper demonstrates that a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding performs well, while token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper demonstrates that neural sequence tagging with distance encoding can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP approach on the PE dataset for argumentation mining.
2. The authors provide a detailed empirical comparison of several neural approaches to AM, including a critical analysis of their relative strengths and weaknesses.
3. The paper makes a reasonable argument for why neural approaches might be preferable to pipeline approaches for AM, particularly regarding error propagation and feature engineering.

## Strengths

1. **Clear demonstration of neural approaches outperforming the state-of-the-art ILP model**: The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison showing that neural approaches (particularly the sequence tagging model) significantly outperform the established ILP-based approach on the PE dataset. The results are presented clearly with statistically significant improvements (p < 0.01), which is a valuable contribution to the field.

2. **Reasonable argument for neural approaches over pipeline methods**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of pipeline approaches (error propagation, hand-crafted features) and provides a compelling rationale for why neural approaches might address these issues. The authors' analysis of the dependency parsing approach's failure (due to long-range dependencies and essay-level complexity) is particularly insightful.

3. **Practical demonstration of multi-task learning benefits**: The paper provides evidence that adding component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning framework improves performance, which is a valuable contribution to the broader NLP community. This finding aligns with established literature on multi-task learning (Søgaard and Goldberg, 2016) while providing specific application to AM.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited validation on a single dataset**: The paper evaluates all approaches on a single dataset (PE from Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which is a major limitation. The authors acknowledge the significant performance drop when moving from paragraph-level to essay-level analysis (over 10% F1 drop), yet they don't demonstrate how their approach would perform on other datasets with different argumentation structures. Without validation on multiple datasets, the claim that neural approaches are "better" is premature and lacks generalizability.

2. **Insufficient comparison with prior neural approaches**: The paper claims to be "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with existing neural approaches to related tasks. For example, the paper doesn't compare against neural approaches to discourse parsing or argument mining that have been proposed in the literature (e.g., Peldszus and Stede, 2015; Habernal and Gurevych, 2016), which would strengthen the claim of novelty and demonstrate why their specific framing is superior.

3. **Lack of analysis of why dependency parsing fails**: The paper states that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective" but doesn't provide a sufficiently detailed analysis of *why* it fails. The authors note that the LSTM-Parser performs poorly on essay-level data due to "very long sequences" and "huge search space," but this explanation is vague. A more rigorous analysis of the specific architectural limitations (beyond just "long sequences") would be necessary to justify the rejection of this approach.

4. **No discussion of practical implementation challenges**: The paper doesn't address the practical challenges of implementing these neural approaches at scale. For instance, the significant performance drop from paragraph-level to essay-level suggests that these approaches would be difficult to apply to full documents in real-world scenarios, yet the paper doesn't discuss potential solutions or limitations of their approach in this context.

5. **Overstated claims about "first" neural end-to-end solutions**: The paper's claim that it presents "the first neural end-to-end solutions" is questionable. As noted in the related work section, previous work (e.g., Peldszus and Stede, 2015) has explored discourse parsing for argumentation mining, and there are neural approaches to related tasks that could be seen as precursors. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from these prior works.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates a significant performance drop when moving from paragraph-level to essay-level analysis. How would the authors address this limitation for real-world applications where arguments may span entire documents rather than single paragraphs?

2. The paper claims to be the "first neural end-to-end solutions" to computational AM. What specific aspects of this work distinguish it from previous neural approaches to related tasks (e.g., discourse parsing, argument extraction) that have been proposed in the literature?

3. The authors state that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective," yet they don't provide a detailed analysis of why this specific framing fails. Could you elaborate on the specific architectural limitations of dependency parsing that make it unsuitable for AM, beyond the general "long sequences" issue?

## Overall Recommendation

I am skeptical of this paper's claims due to the lack of validation on multiple datasets and the limited discussion of why the dependency parsing approach fails. While the paper demonstrates a useful empirical comparison of neural approaches on a single dataset, the generalizability of these results is questionable. The paper would benefit from additional experiments on other datasets, a more thorough comparison with existing neural approaches to related tasks, and a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed approaches. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and rigorous validation, I find the evidence presented insufficient to support the authors' strong claims about the superiority of their approach. I recommend rejection, but would consider a major revision if the authors can address the weaknesses outlined above.

**Rating: Reject**
