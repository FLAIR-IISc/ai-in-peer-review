

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative units (premises, claims, major claims), their types, and the relations between them (support, attack). The authors propose multiple neural framing approaches: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and a combined sequential/tree structure approach (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compare them against a feature-based ILP model.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions for computational AM, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraint design.
2. The authors demonstrate that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model on the PE dataset, with the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding showing particularly strong results.
3. The authors identify that dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM at the token level (contrary to previous work that operated at the component level), and that a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding provides robust performance across different environments.

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution**: The paper addresses a clear gap in the field by proposing neural end-to-end approaches for AM, which eliminates the need for hand-crafted features and costly ILP constraint design that have been problematic due to the "arbitrary" nature of argumentation theory. This is a substantial methodological contribution that could guide future work in the field.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough comparison of multiple neural framing approaches across both paragraph and essay levels. Their analysis of why certain approaches work better than others (e.g., the dependency parsing approach fails on essay level due to long sequences, while sequence tagging handles longer sequences better) is particularly valuable for the community.

3. **Insightful analysis of component-relation relationship**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the relationship between component identification and relation identification, demonstrating that while LSTM-ER performs better at component identification, it performs worse at relation identification due to its more general approach. This insight is crucial for designing effective AM systems.

4. **Clear demonstration of performance gains**: The paper shows consistent and statistically significant improvements over the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple metrics (C-F1, R-F1, global F1) on both paragraph and essay levels, with the STagBLCC model achieving the best results.

5. **Practical insights for future work**: The paper provides clear policy recommendations (e.g., "When there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable") based on their empirical findings, which will be valuable for researchers and practitioners working on AM.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable claim of novelty**: The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions" for computational AM, but the authors cite Miwa and Bansal (2016) as an applicable approach for AM and mention Potash et al. (2017) as work submitted during preparation. This weakens the novelty claim, as there appears to be prior work on neural end-to-end approaches for AM that the authors do not properly acknowledge.

2. **Limited dataset scope**: The paper evaluates only on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which has a specific structure (with major claims, claims, and premises). The paper does not address how the proposed approaches would generalize to other datasets with different annotation schemes or argumentation structures, which is a significant limitation given the "arbitrary" nature of argumentation theory mentioned in the paper.

3. **Evaluation metric concerns**: The paper uses a somewhat ad-hoc evaluation metric (C-F1 and R-F1 with 100% and 50% matching) that is not standard in the NLP community. While the authors reference Persing and Ng (2016), they don't provide sufficient justification for why this metric is superior to more standard approaches.

4. **Lack of computational analysis**: The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or training time of the proposed models, which is important for practical applications of AM systems. This omission makes it difficult to assess the real-world feasibility of the proposed approaches.

5. **Insufficient discussion of failure modes**: The paper presents strong results but doesn't thoroughly analyze the cases where the models fail. Understanding the failure modes would be valuable for improving the models and for the community to understand the limitations of the proposed approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions" for AM, but cites Miwa and Bansal (2016) as applicable to AM and mentions Potash et al. (2017) as work submitted during preparation. Could the authors clarify why they consider their work to be the first neural end-to-end solution for AM?

2. The paper shows that the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding works well, but Figure 2 indicates that there are long-range dependencies (distances up to ±10). How would the model handle cases where the distance is larger than what was observed in the training data?

3. The paper uses only the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016). How would the proposed approaches generalize to other datasets with different argumentation structures or annotation schemes, given the "arbitrary" nature of argumentation theory mentioned in the paper?

4. The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or training time of the proposed models. Could the authors provide this information to help assess the practical feasibility of their approach?
