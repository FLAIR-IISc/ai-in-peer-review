

# ACL Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper proposes neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a combination of sequential and tree structure information. The authors claim to present the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, showing that their approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The first systematic comparison of neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (though I question the "first" claim)
2. Demonstrating that sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing for end-to-end AM
3. Showing that multi-task learning with component and relation tasks as auxiliary tasks improves performance

## Strengths

1. **Novel framing of AM as sequence tagging with distance encoding**: The paper's core contribution of encoding distance information directly into the tag label (rather than using a pipeline approach) is well-motivated and effectively demonstrates that neural sequence models can capture long-range dependencies in argumentation structures. The empirical results showing STagBLCC outperforming dependency parsers on the paragraph level is convincing and addresses a key limitation of previous work.

2. **Comprehensive experimental comparison**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) against the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple evaluation metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at 100% and 50% matching levels). The analysis of why certain approaches work better than others (e.g., why LSTM-ER performs poorly on essay-level but well on paragraph-level) is insightful and well-supported by the data.

3. **Clear analysis of data structure**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the PE dataset structure, particularly noting the paragraph-level nature of argumentation (92% of relations span multiple sentences) and how this impacts model design. This is crucial for understanding why certain approaches work better than others.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical missing figures**: The paper references "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" multiple times but neither figure is present in the provided text. Figure 1 is supposed to illustrate the argumentation structure in student essays, and Figure 2 is supposed to show the distribution of distances between components. Without these figures, the paper's core claims about data structure and label distribution cannot be verified. This is a serious formatting error that undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Inconsistent and confusing table formatting**: Table 1 has a messy header structure with "Acc. C-F1 R-F1 F1 Acc. C-F1 R-F1 F1" followed by "100% 50% 100% 50% 100% 50% 100% 50% 100% 50% 100% 50%". It's unclear what "Acc." refers to (accuracy?), and the multiple percentages make the table difficult to interpret. Similarly, Table 2's header "C-F1 R-F1 F1 100% 50% 100% 50% 100% 50%" is confusing. The authors should have provided clearer, more standard table formatting.

3. **Lack of statistical significance details**: The paper claims results are significant at p < 0.01 (in Table 1 caption) but doesn't provide the actual statistical tests or p-values. The authors should have included the specific tests used (Wilcoxon signed rank test) and the exact p-values for key comparisons to support their claims about statistical significance.

4. **Inconsistent reference formatting**: The references section contains numerous formatting errors, including inconsistent use of "et al\.,? " (with backslashes and question marks), missing conference names, and inconsistent formatting of arXiv preprints vs. conference papers. For example, "Neural machine translation by jointly learning to align and translate, Dzmitry Bahdanau, Kyunghyun Cho, Yoshua Bengio., Proc. ICLR., Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015, Bahdanau et al\., " should follow standard citation format.

5. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper claims "the LSTM-ER model is not suitable for our data because this modeling exploits dependency tree structures on the sentence level, while relationships between components are almost never on the sentence level." But they don't provide evidence for this claim beyond stating "roughly 92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences." This is a key claim that needs more detailed evidence or analysis.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide the missing Figures 1 and 2, as they are essential for understanding the data structure and label distribution that underpins your approach?

2. The paper claims to present the "first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM." Can you clarify whether this is accurate, and if so, provide a clear justification for why previous neural approaches to AM were not end-to-end?

3. The paper states that "the LSTM-ER model is not suitable for our data" but then shows it outperforms other models on paragraph-level. Can you clarify why it's unsuitable for essay-level but suitable for paragraph-level? What specific aspects of the data structure make it unsuitable for essay-level but not paragraph-level?

4. The paper mentions "we relegate issues of pre-trained word embeddings, hyperparameter optimization and further practical issues to the supplementary material" but no supplementary material is provided. Can you confirm whether supplementary material is available and if so, provide a link?

5. The paper claims "the major benefit is again seen for relations (about 5% points increase from essay to paragraph level)" but doesn't specify for which models this is true. Is this true for all models or just specific ones? Can you clarify this?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a well-motivated approach with some promising results, the missing figures, inconsistent table formatting, lack of statistical significance details, and reference formatting errors are serious issues that undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance when the paper's core evidence (the figures) is missing, and the presentation is so inconsistent that key claims cannot be properly evaluated. The paper's technical content is strong, but the presentation flaws are too severe to overlook.
