

# ACL Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative structures in text, including component segmentation, component typing, and relation identification. The authors propose four neural framings for AM: (1) dependency parsing at the token level, (2) sequence tagging with distance encoding, (3) multi-task learning with auxiliary subtasks, and (4) adaptation of LSTM-ER for AM.

As a reviewer, I see the most significant contributions as:
1. **Demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP-based pipeline approach** without requiring hand-crafted features or constraints
2. **Providing a thorough empirical comparison of different neural framings for AM**, with the sequence tagging approach showing particularly strong performance
3. **Offering valuable insights about the limitations of dependency parsing for AM** (contrary to previous assumptions in the literature) and the importance of modeling component distances

The claim of being the "first neural end-to-end solutions" is partially supported, though the paper could more clearly distinguish its contribution from prior neural work on related tasks.

## Strengths

1. **First comprehensive neural end-to-end AM study**: This paper fills a significant gap in the literature by being the first to systematically investigate neural approaches for end-to-end AM. Previous work relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline approaches with ILP constraints, which the authors properly identify as problematic. The paper's thorough evaluation of multiple neural framings provides valuable empirical evidence that neural methods can be effective for this task without manual feature engineering.

2. **Strong empirical results demonstrating superiority over ILP baseline**: The paper provides convincing evidence that their sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model on the PE dataset, particularly on relation identification (R-F1). The results are statistically significant (p < 0.01), and the authors provide detailed comparisons across different evaluation metrics (100% and 50% match levels) and data levels (paragraph vs. essay). This is a substantial contribution to the field.

3. **Insightful analysis of model limitations and why certain approaches work better**: The paper provides valuable insights into why token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to previous assumptions), and why LSTM-ER performs better on paragraph-level but worse on essay-level. The authors correctly identify that the neural tagging approach's explicit encoding of component distances aligns better with the data distribution (as shown in Figure 2) than the more general LSTM-ER approach. This analysis helps clarify what makes different neural approaches suitable for this particular task.

4. **Demonstration of multi-task learning benefits**: The paper shows that including natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks improves performance, providing evidence that this approach is beneficial for complex sequence tagging problems in AM. This finding could have broader implications for other sequence labeling tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty in the proposed methods**: While the paper claims to present the "first neural end-to-end solutions" for AM, the core neural architectures used (BiLSTM-CRF, multi-task learning) are not novel. The main contribution appears to be applying existing neural techniques to AM, rather than developing new techniques specifically for AM. For a top-tier conference like ACL, this level of novelty is insufficient. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from recent neural work on related tasks (e.g., Potash et al., 2017 on pointer networks for AM).

2. **Insufficient comparison to recent neural approaches**: The paper does not compare to several relevant recent neural approaches. For example, it doesn't discuss how their sequence tagging approach compares to the pointer network method mentioned in the references (Potash et al., 2017), which is explicitly designed for argumentation mining. Additionally, the paper doesn't address how their approach would compare to more recent transformer-based models that have become dominant in NLP. This omission weakens the claim of being the current state-of-the-art.

3. **Limited analysis of why neural approaches outperform the ILP model**: While the paper reports that neural approaches outperform the ILP model, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *why* this is the case. The authors note that "models tend to produce output consistent with constraints in their training data," suggesting that neural models might be implicitly learning the same constraints as the ILP model. However, they don't provide evidence to support this hypothesis or explore whether the neural models are truly learning better representations or simply mimicking the constraints. A deeper analysis of model behavior would strengthen the paper significantly.

4. **Over-reliance on a single dataset without sufficient generalizability evidence**: The paper evaluates all approaches on a single dataset (the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which has specific characteristics (e.g., paragraph-level argumentation structures). The paper doesn't demonstrate that their approach generalizes to other datasets with different argumentation structures or to real-world texts outside the controlled PE corpus. This limits the practical significance of the findings and makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to present the "first neural end-to-end solutions" for AM, yet the references mention Potash et al. (2017) which proposes "argumentation mining with pointer networks." Could you clarify how your approach differs from Potash et al.'s work, and why it represents a distinct contribution rather than a continuation of their research?

2. You state that "token-based dependency parsing is ineffective—in contrast to what has been proposed for systems that operate on the coarser component level." Could you provide more detailed analysis of *why* this is the case, especially since dependency parsing has been successfully used for related tasks like discourse parsing? What specific characteristics of AM data make token-level dependency parsing unsuitable?

3. The paper demonstrates that the STagBLCC model outperforms the ILP model, but doesn't provide detailed error analysis. Could you provide examples of cases where the neural model succeeds where the ILP model fails, and vice versa? This would help understand the specific strengths of the neural approach.

4. The authors note that LSTM-ER performs better on paragraph-level but worse on essay-level. Could you elaborate on the practical implications of this finding? In real-world applications, we would need to process entire essays, not just paragraphs. What would be the recommended approach for processing full essays, and how would you address the performance drop?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can effectively address computational argumentation mining without the need for manual feature engineering. The empirical results are strong, and the insights about why certain neural approaches work better than others are particularly useful.

However, the paper's novelty is limited, as it primarily applies existing neural techniques to AM rather than developing new techniques specifically for the task. The comparison to recent neural approaches is insufficient, and the evaluation is limited to a single dataset without sufficient evidence of generalizability.

I recommend **weak rejection** with the understanding that the paper could be substantially improved with additional novelty, more comprehensive comparisons, and a stronger analysis of why neural approaches outperform the ILP baseline.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify novelty**: Explicitly distinguish this work from Potash et al. (2017) and other recent neural approaches to AM. If your approach is indeed a different framing of the problem, make this clear and explain why your framing is more appropriate for the specific characteristics of AM.

2. **Expand evaluation**: Include results on additional datasets to demonstrate generalizability. The PE dataset has specific characteristics (e.g., paragraph-level argumentation) that might not generalize to other types of argumentation. Testing on other datasets would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Deepen analysis**: Provide more detailed error analysis to understand *why* neural approaches outperform the ILP model. This could include examining specific failure cases and analyzing what representations the neural models are learning that the ILP model misses.

4. **Address limitations**: Be more explicit about the limitations of your approach, particularly regarding the essay-level performance drop. Discuss how these limitations could be addressed in future work, and whether there are specific use cases where your approach would be particularly valuable.

5. **Update literature comparison**: Compare your approach to more recent neural methods (e.g., transformer-based models) that have become dominant in NLP, as this would provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art.
