

# Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the challenge of identifying argument components (premises, claims, major claims) and their relations (support, attack) in text. The authors propose four novel neural framings of the AM problem: (1) dependency parsing at the token level, (2) sequence tagging with distance encoding, (3) multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks, and (4) adaptation of the LSTM-ER model.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions over methodological perfection, I see the most significant contribution as the **novel sequence tagging approach with distance encoding** (STagT), which directly encodes relation information into the tag labels. This is a bold conceptual leap that fundamentally changes how we might approach end-to-end AM, moving beyond traditional pipeline architectures.

The second most significant contribution is the **empirical demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model** on the PE dataset, showing that neural methods can effectively address this complex structured prediction task without hand-crafted features.

The third important contribution is the **methodological insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM**, contrary to what might be expected from previous work operating at the component level.

## Strengths

1. **Groundbreaking conceptual framing**: The core contribution of encoding relation information directly into the sequence tagging labels via distance encoding is genuinely novel and bold. This is not just a minor modification to existing approaches but represents a fundamentally new way to conceptualize the end-to-end AM problem. The authors' insight that related components are typically close in textual space and that this can be encoded in the label space is brilliant and could inspire similar approaches in other structured prediction tasks.

2. **Clear empirical validation of the novel approach**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that their sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at both exact and approximate matching levels). The results are consistent and robust, with improvements of 13-15% for component identification and 11-13% for relation identification on the paragraph level compared to the best parser.

3. **Important methodological insight**: The paper provides a valuable counterintuitive finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to what might be expected from previous work. This is an important contribution to the field that helps researchers avoid pursuing ineffective approaches and guides them toward more promising directions.

4. **Elimination of manual feature engineering**: The authors successfully eliminate the need for hand-crafted features that characterized previous approaches, which was a major limitation of the field. This is a significant practical contribution that makes the approach more scalable and adaptable to new datasets.

5. **Demonstration of multi-task learning benefits**: The paper shows that adding natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks improves performance, which is an interesting finding that could be applicable to other sequence labeling tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited generalizability demonstration**: The paper focuses exclusively on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which has a specific structure where argument structures are almost trees. The paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach would perform on other datasets with different argumentation theories or more complex argument structures. This limits the perceived impact of their contribution.

2. **Lack of detailed analysis of why the approach works**: While the paper shows that the distance encoding approach works well, it doesn't deeply analyze why this particular framing is effective. The paper mentions the distribution of distances in the data but doesn't thoroughly explore how this interacts with the neural model's ability to handle long-range dependencies.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of their proposed approach. For example, it's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the distance encoding versus the neural architecture itself.

4. **Limited comparison with other neural approaches**: The paper primarily compares against the ILP model but doesn't thoroughly compare against other recent neural approaches that might have been proposed for similar tasks, such as more advanced relation extraction models.

5. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding how it would handle texts with more complex argument structures than those in the PE dataset.

## Questions to Authors

1. The PE dataset has a specific tree-like structure. How would your approach handle texts with more complex argument structures that don't conform to a tree (e.g., circular arguments or multiple parents for a single component)?

2. Your distance encoding approach works well because related components are typically close in textual space. How would your approach perform on datasets where related components are more likely to be far apart?

3. Could you provide more insight into why the LSTM-ER model performs better on paragraph level but worse on essay level compared to your sequence tagging approach, particularly regarding the handling of long-range dependencies?

4. Have you considered applying your distance-encoding approach to other sequence labeling tasks with relational information, such as coreference resolution or event extraction?

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to computational argumentation mining by introducing a novel way to frame end-to-end AM as a sequence tagging problem with distance encoding. The core idea is bold, creative, and fundamentally changes how we might approach the problem, moving beyond traditional pipeline architectures.

While the paper has some limitations in terms of dataset scope and depth of analysis, the novelty and conceptual significance of the core contribution outweigh these shortcomings. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be an important step forward for the field.

The empirical results are strong and validate the core conceptual contribution. The methodological insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM is also valuable and important for the field.

This paper introduces a significant new direction for computational argumentation mining that could inspire future work across the field, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL.
