

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper proposes neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and a combination of sequential and tree structure information. The authors claim their neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016), while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and constraints. The evaluation is conducted on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper provides a thorough comparison between neural approaches and the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model on a single dataset
2. The authors identify that sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing approaches for end-to-end AM
3. The paper demonstrates that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task provides modest performance improvements

## Strengths

1. **Thorough comparison against the established ILP baseline:** The paper provides a comprehensive comparison against the feature-based ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is the current state-of-the-art for end-to-end AM. The authors carefully document the performance differences between their neural approaches and this established baseline, which is valuable for the field.

2. **Insightful analysis of data characteristics:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of the PE dataset's peculiarities, including the distribution of component distances (Figure 2), the paragraph-level structure, and the implications for different modeling approaches. This analysis helps contextualize the results and explains why certain approaches work better than others.

3. **Clear demonstration of the sequence tagging approach's effectiveness:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that the sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) outperforms all other neural approaches (including dependency parsing) on the paragraph level, with a clear explanation of why this is the case (simplicity of local models, better handling of long sequences).

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient evidence for generalizability claims:** The paper claims neural approaches are "substantially better" than the ILP model, but provides evidence only on a single dataset (PE). There is no validation on other AM datasets or across different argumentation structures. For a claim of "state-of-the-art" performance, validation on multiple datasets is essential, especially given the "arbitrary" nature of argumentation that the authors themselves acknowledge in the introduction.

2. **Overstated novelty and lack of comprehensive related work:** The authors claim this is "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but the related work section does not adequately address other neural approaches to AM that may have been published before. The paper also fails to compare with other established neural approaches (e.g., other sequence labeling methods or transformer-based models) that could be relevant to the AM task, making the novelty claim questionable.

3. **Methodological shortcomings in the dependency parsing claim:** The paper claims that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective—in contrast to what has been proposed for systems that operate on the coarser component level." However, Table 1 shows that the LSTM-Parser (a neural dependency parser) is actually the best-performing dependency parser on the paragraph level (55.68% C-F1 100%, 68.20% C-F1 50%). The authors don't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why dependency parsing would be inherently unsuitable for AM, especially when the argument structure is tree-like. This appears to be an overstatement that weakens their main contribution.

4. **Marginal improvements from multi-task learning:** The paper claims multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task improves performance, but the improvements are marginal (typically a few percentage points at most, as shown in Table 2). This is not a substantial contribution given the added complexity of the multi-task framework. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that these marginal gains justify the increased complexity.

5. **Lack of ablation studies for key design choices:** The paper doesn't provide thorough ablation studies to determine which aspects of their sequence tagging approach are actually driving the performance improvements. For example, it's unclear whether the distance encoding is truly necessary, or whether the improvements would be similar with a simpler tagging scheme. Without these ablation studies, it's difficult to assess the true value of their specific innovations.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) outperforms dependency parsing approaches, but the dependency parsing approach is framed as token-level rather than component-level. Given that the authors acknowledge that AM structures "often form trees, or closely resemble them," why would a token-level dependency parsing approach be fundamentally unsuitable for AM, especially when the authors themselves note that "the argument structure in each essay is—almost—a tree"?

2. The paper states that "neural computational AM is as good or (substantially) better than a competing feature-based ILP formulation." Given the field's acknowledged "arbitrary" nature of argumentation (where annotation varies significantly across datasets), how do the authors plan to validate that their approach generalizes beyond the single PE dataset they used?

3. The paper claims "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM." Could the authors please clarify which prior neural approaches to AM they are referring to, and why these prior approaches are not considered "end-to-end" solutions?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents some interesting observations about neural approaches for argumentation mining, particularly regarding the effectiveness of sequence tagging with distance encoding. However, the claims of novelty and superiority are overstated given the limited evidence provided. The paper fails to adequately validate its claims across multiple datasets, provides insufficient comparison with other relevant neural approaches, and makes an unsubstantiated claim about dependency parsing being ineffective for AM.

While the work has some merit in demonstrating that certain neural approaches can be effective for AM, the evidence provided is insufficient to support the strong claims made in the paper. The marginal improvements from multi-task learning and the lack of generalizability validation significantly weaken the contribution. The paper would require substantial additional experiments on multiple datasets and more comprehensive comparisons with relevant neural approaches to be considered a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend rejection for this submission. The paper presents an interesting application of neural methods to AM, but the claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their validation by testing on multiple datasets, providing more comprehensive comparisons with relevant approaches, and addressing the methodological concerns noted above.
