

# ACL Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the first neural end-to-end approach for computational argumentation mining (AM), which is a significant departure from the current state-of-the-art that relies on pipeline architectures with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The authors propose several novel framings of the AM problem: (1) as a dependency parsing problem at the token level, (2) as a sequence tagging problem with a complex label set encoding component distances, (3) as a multi-task learning problem, and (4) as an adaptation of the LSTM-ER model.

The primary contributions I see are:
1. **First neural end-to-end framework for computational argumentation mining**: This is a bold, novel direction that fundamentally changes how AM is approached, moving away from pipeline architectures and manual feature engineering.
2. **Insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM**: The authors provide a valuable correction to a potential misconception in the field (that AM could be treated as dependency parsing at the token level).
3. **Effective sequence tagging approach with distance encoding**: The proposed sequence tagging framework with distance encoding proves to be both novel and effective, outperforming the state-of-the-art ILP model.

## Strengths

1. **Groundbreaking approach to a longstanding problem**: The paper introduces the first neural end-to-end framework for argumentation mining, which is a significant conceptual shift from the current pipeline-based approaches. This is the most important contribution of the paper - it opens up a completely new direction for research in the field. The authors successfully demonstrate that neural approaches can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, which is a compelling result that validates their approach.

2. **Valuable empirical insights about different modeling choices**: The paper provides crucial insights about which approaches work well for AM and why. The finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to previous component-level approaches) is particularly insightful and helps guide future research in the field. The authors' explanation about why sequence tagging with distance encoding works well (because it naturally handles long-range dependencies) is a significant theoretical contribution.

3. **Elimination of manual feature engineering**: The neural approaches eliminate the need for hand-crafted features and complex ILP constraints, which is a major practical advantage over previous work. This makes the approach more scalable and less dependent on domain-specific knowledge, addressing a key limitation of current AM systems.

4. **Practical policy recommendations**: The paper offers clear, actionable insights for practitioners: for short documents (with few entities), a more general framework like LSTM-ER works well, while for long documents (with many entities), a more restrained modeling approach is preferable. This is a valuable contribution to the field.

5. **Strong empirical results**: The sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) significantly outperforms the ILP baseline on both component and relation identification, with gains of approximately 13% for C-F1 and 11% for R-F1 on the paragraph level. The results are statistically significant, as confirmed by the Wilcoxon signed rank test.

## Weaknesses

1. **Single-dataset evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is a significant limitation. Without evaluation on multiple datasets or a more diverse set of texts, it's difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach. The field of argumentation mining would benefit from evaluation on multiple datasets to demonstrate robustness.

2. **Limited failure case analysis**: The paper doesn't provide detailed analysis of where the models fail or why they fail. Understanding the limitations of the approach is crucial for future work, but this is missing from the paper. For example, it would be valuable to know what types of argument structures are particularly challenging for the sequence tagging approach.

3. **Comparison to more recent work**: The paper doesn't compare to Potash et al. (2017), which is mentioned in the references and presents "argumentation mining with pointer networks." This is a significant oversight, as it's a more recent approach that could provide a stronger baseline for comparison.

4. **Lack of computational efficiency discussion**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proposed models compared to the ILP baseline. This is an important consideration for practical applications, especially given that the authors note the LSTM-Parser's performance drops significantly when moving from paragraph to essay level.

5. **Theoretical justification for token-level dependency parsing**: While the authors show that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, they don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why it should have worked better in the first place. The analysis is mostly empirical, which is fine, but a more principled explanation would strengthen their findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. Have you considered evaluating your approach on other argumentation mining datasets (e.g., the one from Peldszus and Stede, 2016 or the one from Habernal and Gurevych, 2016)? If not, what are the reasons for limiting your evaluation to just the PE dataset, and how do you think your approach would generalize to other datasets?

2. The paper mentions Potash et al. (2017) in the references but doesn't compare to it. How does your approach compare to theirs, and why did you choose not to include this comparison? Would their pointer network approach be compatible with your sequence tagging framework?

3. Could you provide more details about the failure cases of your models? Specifically, what types of argument structures are most challenging for the sequence tagging approach, and why?

4. The paper claims that the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding works well because it handles long-range dependencies. Could you elaborate on why this approach is better at handling long-range dependencies than other neural approaches (e.g., the LSTM-ER model), given that the LSTM-ER model also uses neural networks?

5. How does the computational cost of your neural approaches compare to the ILP baseline? Is the improved performance worth the additional computational resources required, especially for large-scale applications?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. The primary contribution - the first neural end-to-end approach for computational argumentation mining - is highly novel and represents a bold new direction for the field. The empirical results are strong, and the insights about modeling choices are valuable. While the paper has some limitations (particularly the single-dataset evaluation), these are outweighed by the significance of the novel approach. The authors have successfully demonstrated that neural approaches can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, which is a major achievement. This paper has the potential to significantly influence future research in argumentation mining.
