

# Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative units (claims, premises), their types, and relations between them. The authors argue that previous approaches to AM rely on pipeline architectures with hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP) constraints, which are error-prone and require significant manual effort to adapt to different datasets. They propose four neural approaches: (1) framing AM as dependency parsing, (2) framing AM as sequence tagging with a novel tag set encoding component distances, (3) framing AM as multi-task sequence tagging, and (4) adapting the LSTM-ER model for AM. The authors evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) and compare them to the state-of-the-art ILP model.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **First neural end-to-end framework for computational argumentation mining**: This is the most significant contribution as it introduces a new paradigm for AM that avoids the need for hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures, addressing a meaningful gap in the literature.

2. **Empirical demonstration that neural methods outperform the state-of-the-art ILP approach**: The authors provide strong empirical evidence that their neural approaches (particularly the sequence tagging approach) achieve better performance than the previous state-of-the-art, with robust results across different evaluation metrics.

3. **Insightful analysis of effective neural approaches for AM**: The paper provides valuable explanations for why certain framing approaches (like dependency parsing) are ineffective for AM, along with clear analysis of the relationship between document length and model performance.

## Strengths
1. **Novel approach to a well-defined problem**: The paper effectively identifies a meaningful gap in the AM literature and justifies why neural methods could be more effective than previous approaches. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of pipeline-based approaches (error propagation, hand-crafted features) and provide a compelling rationale for their neural approach.

2. **Strong empirical results with thorough analysis**: The experiments are well-designed, with comprehensive results reported at both paragraph and essay levels. The authors provide valuable error analysis (e.g., Figure 4 showing probability of correct relation identification given true distance) that goes beyond simple performance metrics to explain *why* certain approaches work better than others.

3. **Insightful comparative analysis**: The paper's analysis of why token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (despite being used for component-level parsing in prior work) is particularly valuable. The authors provide a clear explanation based on the nature of argumentation structures and the search space complexity for dependency parsing on longer sequences.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited dataset scope and generalizability concerns**: The paper only evaluates on a single dataset (the PE dataset), which has specific structural properties (argument structures are "almost trees," paragraphs contain entire argument structures). The authors do not discuss whether their approach would generalize to datasets with different argumentation structures, which is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions."

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: While the paper includes some analysis of the multi-task learning approach, it lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component in their proposed models. For example, it's unclear how much of the improvement in the sequence tagging approach comes from the distance encoding versus the neural architecture itself.

3. **Lack of discussion on failure modes**: The paper provides good analysis of why certain approaches work better than others, but doesn't thoroughly discuss cases where their best approach (STagBLCC) fails. This is important for understanding the limitations of the approach and for guiding future work.

4. **Comparison to more recent work**: The paper compares to the ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) but doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches to AM that might have been published after that. The paper mentions "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" (Potash et al., 2017), but doesn't discuss how their approach compares to that work, which is a missed opportunity to position their contribution more precisely.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that the PE dataset has a special structure where argument structures are "almost trees." How would your approach generalize to datasets with more complex argumentation structures (e.g., where argument structures are not trees at all)? Could you provide examples of such datasets and discuss potential modifications needed for your approach?

2. The authors mention that "the major benefit is again seen for relations (about 5% points increase from essay to paragraph level)" for the sequence tagging approach. How does this compare to the benefit seen for the dependency parsing approach? If the benefit is similar, why does the sequence tagging approach outperform the parsing approach despite both being affected by document length?

3. The paper states that LSTM-ER performs poorly on essay-level relation identification due to the large number of possible relations (O(m²) for m discovered components). Could you elaborate on how you implemented the relation detection in LSTM-ER, and whether there are ways to constrain the relation space to make it more efficient for longer documents?

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions. The comparison to the ILP baseline is appropriate, and the empirical results are convincing. However, the limited dataset scope is a notable weakness that should be addressed in any revision. The authors might also consider discussing how their approach would scale to datasets with millions of documents, as AM is often applied in large-scale text analysis applications. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the current approach and how future work could address these limitations.

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by introducing neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining, with strong empirical results demonstrating their superiority over previous approaches. The insights provided about why certain neural architectures work better than others are particularly valuable. However, the limited dataset scope and lack of thorough ablation studies prevent me from recommending strong acceptance. If the authors can address the weaknesses noted above (particularly by expanding the evaluation to multiple datasets and providing more detailed ablation studies), this would be an excellent paper for ACL.
