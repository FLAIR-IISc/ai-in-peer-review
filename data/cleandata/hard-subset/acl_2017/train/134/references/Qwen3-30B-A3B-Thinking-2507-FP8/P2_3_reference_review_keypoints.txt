- Paper proposes four neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining and evaluates them against an ILP-based baseline on the PE dataset.  
- Main contributions: first neural end-to-end solutions for full AM, comprehensive empirical comparison showing neural superiority, and analysis showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective.  
- Strength: Introduces the first neural end-to-end frameworks for full AM, removing hand-crafted features and pipeline error propagation.  
- Strength: Systematic comparison of four neural frameworks yields practical guidance on which methods work best for AM tasks.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results—neural models outperform ILP baseline with statistically significant gains.  
- Strength: Provides theoretical and empirical analysis explaining why certain architectures succeed or fail (e.g., relation search complexity vs. constrained tagging).  
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to a single dataset (PE), reducing claims of generalizability to other corpora.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of the large performance drop from paragraph to essay level and lack of proposed mitigations.  
- Weakness: Limited comparison to other recent neural methods (e.g., BERT-based relation extraction, discourse parsing).  
- Question: Request for deeper theoretical explanation why token-level dependency parsing fails compared to component-level approaches.  
- Question: Ask for concrete examples and analysis of factors causing the essay-level performance drop.  
- Question: Ask how LSTM-ER’s de-coupling of component and relation tasks specifically aids component detection and whether other architectures can replicate this.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper with significant methodological and empirical contributions; accept for publication despite dataset and analysis limitations.
