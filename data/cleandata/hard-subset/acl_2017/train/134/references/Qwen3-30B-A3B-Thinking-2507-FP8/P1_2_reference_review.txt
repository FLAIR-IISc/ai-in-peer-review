

# Peer Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the full AM pipeline without relying on hand-crafted features or ILP constraints. The authors frame AM as four different neural problems: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and using the LSTM-ER model. They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

As I see them, the paper's main contributions are:
1. The first neural end-to-end solution for computational AM (novel framework)
2. Demonstrating neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model (empirical significance)
3. Showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to prior assumptions, providing key insights)

## Strengths
1. **Novel and important problem addressing**: Computational argumentation mining is a significant NLP task with applications in legal reasoning, education, and debate analysis. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous pipeline approaches with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, making their end-to-end neural approach highly relevant and timely for the field.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of multiple neural approaches against the state-of-the-art ILP model. The authors carefully analyze performance differences at both paragraph and essay levels, with detailed error analysis. Their finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to previous work operating on component level) is particularly valuable and well-supported by empirical evidence.

3. **Insightful multi-task learning results**: The authors demonstrate that adding natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks significantly improves performance, with the component task being more beneficial than the relation task. This finding provides important guidance for future work on AM and sequence labeling tasks more generally.

4. **Clear demonstration of neural advantages**: The paper effectively shows how neural approaches eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and costly constraint design that characterize previous work. The STagBLCC model (a neural sequence tagger with distance encoding) achieves state-of-the-art results while being simpler and more robust than previous approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with recent neural approaches**: The authors mention Potash et al. (2017) as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" but don't compare their approach to this more recent work. Given that Potash et al. also proposed neural approaches for AM, this omission weakens the claim of being the "first" neural end-to-end solution and makes it difficult to assess how their methods compare to the latest developments.

2. **Insufficient explanation of evaluation metrics**: The paper uses C-F1 (100% and 50%) and R-F1 (100% and 50%) metrics, but fails to justify why these specific matching thresholds are appropriate for AM. The authors should explain how these metrics relate to the task's requirements and why they chose these particular thresholds rather than others.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: While the paper provides some error analysis (e.g., why LSTM-ER degrades on essay level), it doesn't systematically analyze why the neural models fail in certain cases. Understanding these failure modes would be valuable for future work and would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Overstated claims about dependency parsing**: The authors claim token-level dependency parsing is "ineffective" based on their experiments, but they don't explore why this is the case beyond noting that the search space is too large. A more thorough analysis of the architectural limitations or data characteristics that make dependency parsing unsuitable would be valuable.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to be the "first neural end-to-end solution," but Potash et al. (2017) also proposed neural approaches for AM. How does your work differ from and improve upon theirs, and why isn't it cited as a relevant prior work?

2. The authors note that 92% of relationships are between components in different sentences. How does this affect the choice of modeling approach, and why might this make dependency parsing (which typically operates on sentence-level structures) particularly unsuitable for AM?

3. The paper suggests that "a more restrained modeling is preferable" for long documents. Could you elaborate on what specific architectural changes or constraints would be most effective for handling longer documents while maintaining the benefits of neural approaches?

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to computational argumentation mining by demonstrating the effectiveness of neural end-to-end approaches. The empirical results are convincing, and the insights about token-level dependency parsing being ineffective are particularly important. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison with Potash et al. (2017) and a more thorough justification of the evaluation metrics. With these improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ACL.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
