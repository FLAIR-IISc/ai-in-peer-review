

# ACL Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper explores neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem in four different ways: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a novel label encoding (incorporating distance information), multi-task learning, and adaptation of the LSTM-ER model. The authors claim their neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP-based method (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) on the PE dataset, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. The paper demonstrates a sequence tagging approach with a novel label encoding (incorporating distance information between components) that outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model on the PE dataset.
2. The paper provides valuable insights into the limitations of token-level dependency parsing for AM, correcting a misconception in the field.
3. The paper offers useful guidance on effective multi-task learning for AM, particularly by including component detection as an auxiliary task.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough empirical evaluation on a standard dataset**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of multiple neural approaches against the state-of-the-art ILP model on the PE dataset, with detailed metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at both 100% and 50% matching levels). This is a strong empirical contribution to the field, with careful attention to the evaluation metrics used in previous AM work.

2. **Novel label encoding for sequence tagging**: The authors' approach to encoding relation distances into tag labels is clever and directly addresses a key challenge in end-to-end AM. The observation that most relations have small distance values (d ∈ {-2,-1,1}) is well-supported by their data analysis, and the encoding scheme is a practical solution that enables sequence tagging to capture long-range dependencies.

3. **Important correction of a misconception**: The paper correctly identifies that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to previous assumptions in the field (e.g., Peldszus and Stede, 2015). This is a significant contribution as it prevents future researchers from pursuing this ineffective approach, saving them time and computational resources.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient evidence for general superiority**: The paper claims that neural approaches are superior to the ILP model but provides evidence only on a single dataset (PE). There is no demonstration of generalizability across multiple datasets or task settings, which is essential for establishing a new method as fundamentally better. This is particularly concerning given the authors' claim that their approach eliminates the need for manual feature engineering, which would be a significant contribution if it held across different datasets.

2. **Overstated novelty claims**: The paper positions itself as "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but this is an overstatement. Previous work (e.g., Habernal and Gurevych, 2016) has already explored neural approaches to AM, and the paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from prior work. The authors do not sufficiently acknowledge or build upon this prior work, making their novelty claim questionable.

3. **Limited comparison with appropriate neural baselines**: The paper compares against the ILP model but does not compare against other neural baselines that would be more appropriate for AM. For example, they don't compare against the BiLSTM-CRF model (Huang et al., 2015) or other standard neural sequence tagging approaches. This omission weakens their claim that neural approaches are superior, as they haven't demonstrated that their specific neural approach is better than other neural methods.

4. **Insufficient analysis of why neural approaches work better**: The paper demonstrates that neural approaches outperform the ILP model but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case. They don't explore whether the neural approaches are simply better at capturing patterns in the specific PE dataset or if they have a more general advantage. The paper states that "hand-crafted features pose a problem" but doesn't provide evidence that neural approaches overcome this problem in a generalizable way.

5. **Questionable claims about dependency parsing**: The paper claims that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, but the evaluation is limited to a few parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser, LSTM-Parser). They don't provide a thorough analysis of why dependency parsing doesn't work for AM, or whether alternative dependency parsing approaches might be more suitable. The paper states that "dependency parsing would be too complex a modeling" for long sequences, but this is not adequately supported by their results.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims neural approaches are superior to the ILP model, but this is based on a single dataset. Could you provide results on additional datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of your approach?

2. The paper positions itself as the "first" neural end-to-end solution to AM, but previous work (Habernal and Gurevych, 2016) has already explored neural approaches. How does your approach differ from theirs, and why is your work novel?

3. The paper doesn't compare against other neural sequence labeling approaches. Why didn't you compare against the BiLSTM-CRF model (Huang et al., 2015) or other standard neural sequence tagging models?

4. The paper claims that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM. Could you provide more detailed analysis on why this is the case, and whether there might be alternative dependency parsing approaches that could work better for AM?

5. The paper doesn't explore the limitations of your approach. What are the specific scenarios or datasets where your neural approaches might fail, and how might these limitations be addressed?

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a useful contribution to the field by demonstrating that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM and by introducing a novel label encoding scheme for sequence tagging, the evidence provided is insufficient to support the authors' broad claims about neural approaches being superior for end-to-end AM. The lack of generalizability across datasets, the overstated novelty claims, and the limited comparison with appropriate baselines significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The field would benefit more from a paper that thoroughly evaluates neural approaches across multiple datasets and provides a clear analysis of when and why neural methods are appropriate for AM, rather than a single-dataset study that makes broad claims about neural superiority.
