

# Review for ACL Submission: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), where the goal is to identify argumentative units (components like claims and premises), their types, and the relations between them (e.g., support or attack). The authors propose four neural approaches: (1) framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, (2) framing AM as a sequence tagging problem with a special label set encoding component type, distance, and relation, (3) framing AM as a multi-task learning problem, and (4) adapting the LSTM-ER model. They evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016) at both paragraph and essay levels.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer)
1. The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.
2. The authors demonstrate that a neural sequence tagging model with distance encoding (STagBLCC) outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model on the PE dataset, with statistically significant improvements.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to component-level approaches), and that multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of Neural Models to AM**: The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to investigate neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining. Previous work (Persing and Ng, 2016; Stab and Gurevych, 2016) used pipeline architectures with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which require significant manual effort and lead to error propagation. The neural approaches proposed here eliminate the need for these manual components, representing a substantial methodological improvement. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of previous approaches and justifies the need for neural end-to-end learning.

2. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple neural approaches and demonstrates that their proposed STagBLCC model outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model with statistically significant improvements (p < 0.01, Wilcoxon signed rank test). The results are consistent across different evaluation metrics (C-F1 and R-F1) and document lengths (paragraph vs. essay level), with STagBLCC achieving 59.34% C-F1 (100%) and 74.08% R-F1 (100%) on paragraph level, compared to ILP's 60.32% and 73.35% respectively. The paper also provides detailed error analysis showing why certain approaches work better than others.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Model Limitations**: The authors provide a particularly valuable contribution through their detailed analysis of why certain approaches are effective or ineffective. For example, they demonstrate that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (unlike component-level approaches), explain why the sequence tagging model with distance encoding performs well (due to its ability to capture long-range dependencies), and analyze why LSTM-ER performs well for components but poorly for relations in longer documents (due to the combinatorial explosion of possible relations). This analysis provides significant insights for future work in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architecture**: While the application of neural models to AM is novel, the core architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, multi-task learning) are standard in neural NLP. The paper does not sufficiently articulate how their specific adaptations for AM differ from existing work. For instance, the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is a specific adaptation, but it's not clear how this differs from existing work on relation extraction or other NLP tasks. The claim that this is the "first neural end-to-end solution" may overstate the novelty, as the core architectures are well-established in NLP.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Models**: The paper compares to the ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) but doesn't compare to more recent neural models for related tasks. For example, there are numerous recent neural approaches to relation extraction and argument mining (e.g., from 2016-2017) that could provide better context for evaluating the contribution. The authors mention LSTM-ER (Miwa and Bansal, 2016) but don't compare to other contemporary neural models, which limits the ability to assess the true novelty and impact of their work.

3. **Evaluation Limited to a Single Dataset**: The experiments are conducted only on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is the most comprehensive AM dataset. However, the paper doesn't evaluate on other datasets to demonstrate generalizability. The claim of "robustness in different environments" is only supported by comparing paragraph vs. essay level on the same dataset, not by evaluating on different datasets. This limits the significance of the results for the broader AM community.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to present the first neural end-to-end solution to AM, but the core neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, multi-task learning) are standard in NLP. Could the authors clarify how their specific adaptations for AM differ from existing work on these architectures in other domains?

2. The paper compares to the ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) but doesn't compare to more recent neural models for relation extraction or argument mining. Could the authors provide a comparison to recent neural models (e.g., from the last 2-3 years) to better contextualize their contribution?

3. The experiments are conducted only on the PE dataset. Could the authors discuss potential reasons why their approach might not generalize to other datasets, and whether they have plans to evaluate on additional datasets in future work?
