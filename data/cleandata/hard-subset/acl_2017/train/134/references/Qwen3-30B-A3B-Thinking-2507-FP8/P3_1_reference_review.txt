

# Peer Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents the first neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem through four distinct neural architectures: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER. The authors claim to demonstrate that neural approaches outperform the previous state-of-the-art ILP model while eliminating hand-crafted features and constraints.

My assessment of the paper's main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The first comprehensive exploration of neural end-to-end approaches for AM, moving beyond pipeline architectures.
2. The critical insight that dependency parsing is ineffective for token-level AM (contrary to prior component-level work), with compelling empirical evidence.
3. The robust performance of sequence tagging with distance encoding across different environments, which provides a practical solution for AM.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and problem framing**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing pipeline approaches (error propagation, hand-crafted features, ILP constraints) and the inherent "arbitrariness" of AM due to varying argumentation theories. This establishes a solid foundation for their neural approach.

2. **Rigorous evaluation design**: The authors use the standard PE dataset with careful analysis at both paragraph and essay levels. Their comparison to the ILP model (the previous state-of-the-art) is thorough, and they provide detailed analysis of performance differences across different framing approaches.

3. **Key insight on dependency parsing**: The authors' finding that dependency parsing is ineffective for token-level AM (contrary to prior work operating at component level) is a significant contribution. The experimental evidence showing dependency parsers perform poorly compared to sequence tagging approaches is compelling and corrects a misconception in the field.

4. **Practical multi-task learning insights**: The demonstration that component detection (C task) as an auxiliary task provides more benefit than relation identification (R task) offers valuable practical guidance for future AM work, particularly for the challenging relation identification subtask.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading claim about eliminating feature engineering**: While the authors claim to eliminate hand-crafted features, their sequence tagging approach with distance encoding (Y = {(b,t,d,s)}) is itself a form of feature engineering. The distance encoding is a specific design choice that requires domain knowledge, and the paper doesn't adequately address this limitation or compare it to other potential encoding schemes.

2. **Inadequate explanation of why sequence tagging works**: The paper notes that 2/3 of relations have small distances, but doesn't sufficiently explain how the model learns to predict these distances or handles the long-range dependencies (up to d=±10). The success of their approach depends on this distance distribution, but the paper doesn't analyze how sensitive the model is to this distribution or how it would perform on datasets with different distance patterns.

3. **Limited comparison to relevant neural approaches**: The authors mention encoder-decoder models as future work but don't compare to existing neural sequence labeling approaches (e.g., Transformer-based models) that might be more suitable for long-range dependencies. The paper also doesn't discuss how their approach compares to other neural sequence tagging architectures beyond BiLSTM-CRF.

4. **Overstated claim about eliminating ILP constraints**: The paper claims neural approaches eliminate the need for constraint design, but the sequence tagging framework implicitly enforces constraints (each token relates to exactly one other component) that are similar in spirit to ILP constraints. The paper doesn't acknowledge this parallel or explain why their implicit constraints are preferable to explicit ILP constraints.

5. **Lack of analysis on failure cases**: The paper provides strong overall performance metrics but doesn't analyze specific failure cases or error patterns. Understanding why certain relations are misclassified (e.g., long-distance vs. short-distance relations) would provide valuable insights for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How sensitive is the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding to the distribution of relation distances in the dataset? Would it perform well on a dataset with very different distance distributions?

2. Can you elaborate on why the dependency parsing approach (with the specific head assignment in Figure 3) performed poorly, and whether you've considered alternative dependency parsing formulations designed specifically for token-level AM?

3. The paper claims neural approaches eliminate hand-crafted features, but the distance encoding is a form of feature engineering. How do you reconcile this with your claim, and what alternatives to distance encoding would you consider?

This paper makes valuable contributions to the field of argumentation mining by introducing the first neural end-to-end approaches and providing critical insights about which neural framings are most effective. While the paper has some limitations in its claims about feature engineering and dependency parsing, the core findings are significant and well-supported by their experimental results. With some clarification on the nature of the "feature engineering" in their approach, this work would make a strong contribution to the ACL community.
