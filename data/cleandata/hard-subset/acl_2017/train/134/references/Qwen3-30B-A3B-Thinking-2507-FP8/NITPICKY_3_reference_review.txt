

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes neural end-to-end approaches for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and using the LSTM-ER model. The authors claim to present the first neural end-to-end solution for AM and demonstrate that their approaches outperform previous feature-based ILP models. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper introduces multiple neural approaches to end-to-end AM, with a focus on sequence tagging with distance encoding as the most effective method.
2. The paper provides a detailed empirical comparison showing that neural methods outperform traditional feature-based ILP approaches.
3. The paper identifies key insights about the limitations of dependency parsing approaches for AM and the importance of constraint design in relation identification.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem framing and justification**: The paper effectively establishes the problem of computational argumentation mining, the limitations of previous approaches, and the need for end-to-end neural solutions. The discussion of the challenges with hand-crafted features and pipeline approaches is well-founded.

2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The paper conducts thorough experiments across multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) with careful attention to different evaluation metrics (exact match vs. approximate match) and different levels of granularity (paragraph vs. essay).

3. **Valuable insights about modeling choices**: The paper provides important insights about the limitations of dependency parsing for token-level AM (contrary to previous work that used it at the component level) and the effectiveness of incorporating distance information into the tagging scheme.

## Weaknesses

1. **Numerous minor formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains multiple inconsistent and incorrect formatting instances that suggest a lack of careful proofreading:
   - "nonargumentative" should be "non-argumentative" (appears multiple times)
   - "loss-lessly" should be "losslessly" (in the Data section)
   - "seperately" should be "separately" (in the Conclusion)
   - "over 100% better" is technically incorrect (in the Experiments section). If Kiperwasser has 32.84% C-F1 (100%) and LSTM-Parser has 68.20% C-F1 (100%), that's a 35.36 percentage point improvement, not "over 100% better." This misleading phrasing suggests a lack of attention to detail.
   - Inconsistent use of parentheses: "C-F1 (100%)" vs. "C-F1 (100%)" (inconsistent spacing around parentheses)
   - Inconsistent relation type labeling: The paper uses "for" and "against" but earlier defines relation types as "For" and "Ag" (for "Against"), creating confusion.

2. **Inconsistency between text and tables**: The paper claims "For the LSTM-Parser, the best performance on essay level is 32.84%/47.44% CF1 (100%/50% level), and 9.11%/14.45% on RF1" but Table 1 shows C-F1 (100%) of 40.87% for LSTM-Parser on essay level. This significant inconsistency between the text and the table undermines the paper's credibility.

3. **Reference formatting errors**: The reference list contains consistent formatting errors throughout, with backslashes in author names ("et al\.,") and question marks in year fields ("? 2015"). For example, "Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015" should be "Bahdanau, Dzmitry, Cho, Kyunghyun, and Bengio, Yoshua. 2015. Neural machine translation by jointly learning to align and translate. In Proceedings of the International Conference on Learning Representations." These errors suggest a lack of attention to detail that is expected in an ACL submission.

4. **Technical inaccuracies in examples**: The introductory example has formatting artifacts (":::::") that are not part of the actual text, and the example explanation has inconsistent spacing ("(O,⊥, ⊥, ⊥)" with an extra space). These are minor but indicate a lack of careful proofreading.

5. **Inconsistent model naming**: The paper refers to "BiLSTM-CRF-CNN (BLCC)" but later uses "STagBLCC" as if it's a standard acronym. The inconsistency between the full name and the abbreviated form creates confusion.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "LSTM-Parser is over 100% better than Kiperwasser on exact spans" - could you clarify what you mean by "over 100% better" as this is technically misleading? (A 35 percentage point improvement is not "over 100% better" - it's a 107.7% relative improvement, but this is still not standard phrasing).

2. There is a significant inconsistency between the text's claim about LSTM-Parser's essay-level performance (32.84% C-F1 100%) and Table 1 (40.87% C-F1 100%). Could you clarify which number is correct?

3. The paper uses "for" and "against" as relation types but earlier defines relation types as "For" and "Ag" (for "Against"). Could you clarify which naming convention you're using consistently?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting and timely application of neural methods to computational argumentation mining. The core ideas are sound, and the empirical results are generally convincing. However, the paper contains numerous minor but persistent formatting errors, inconsistencies, and technical inaccuracies that suggest a lack of careful proofreading. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these minor flaws significant enough to recommend rejection, as they undermine the paper's credibility and professionalism. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading to fix these inconsistencies before publication. While the technical content is strong, the presentation issues are too numerous and distracting to justify acceptance at ACL.

**Recommendation: Reject**
