

# ACL Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes several neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a complex label set, multi-task learning, and adapting the LSTM-ER model. The authors evaluate these approaches on the Persuasive Essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing them against a feature-based ILP model. They claim to present the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, with several approaches outperforming the state-of-the-art ILP model.

## Main Contributions
1. First neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM
2. Demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model
3. Showing that token-based dependency parsing is ineffective for AM
4. Demonstrating robust performance of a neural sequence tagging model with distance encoding
5. Showing multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks improves performance

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies limitations of previous AM approaches (hand-crafted features, pipeline architectures) and addresses them with neural methods.
2. The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of different neural approaches to end-to-end AM.
3. The authors correctly note that LSTM-ER's TreeLSTM component is ineffective for their task due to the sentence-level dependency structure mismatch with AM relationships.

## Weaknesses (Primary to Least Important)

1. **Fundamental Flaw in Dependency Parsing Approach**: This is the most critical issue. The authors define the head of a token as "the first token of the claim or premise that it links to," which is fundamentally incompatible with dependency parsing. In standard dependency parsing, the head of a word is always a single token (e.g., "killed" in "killed many marine lives" would have "killed" as the head of "many," not "has" from a different component). The authors' definition creates a non-standard dependency structure that doesn't conform to how dependency parsing works, making their dependency parsing framing invalid. This is a severe methodological flaw that undermines one of their primary approaches.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Tests**: The paper makes numerous performance comparisons without providing statistical significance tests (e.g., "STagBLCC is ∼13% better than the best parser for C-F1"). In NLP research, this is a critical omission as it's impossible to determine if the observed differences are meaningful or due to random variation. The authors cite "significant at p < 0.01" in Table 1, but don't provide the actual statistical tests used.

3. **Poorly Formatted Tables and Figures**: The paper contains multiple formatting issues that make interpretation difficult:
   - Table 1 has misaligned columns, confusing headers ("Acc." column doesn't make sense in this context), and inconsistent formatting
   - Figure 2 has the x-axis and y-axis both labeled "d", creating confusion
   - Figure 3 is described but not included in the text
   - The paper states "the number of possible trees to consider on n tokens may just be too huge a search space when n is large" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim

4. **Unsubstantiated Claims Without Evidence**: The authors make several claims without providing supporting evidence:
   - "roughly 92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences" (no evidence provided)
   - "LSTM-ER trained and tested on paragraph level substantially outperforms all other systems" (no statistical test provided)
   - "The major benefit is again seen for relations (about 5% points increase from essay to paragraph level)" (no statistical test for this comparison)

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies and Error Analysis**: The paper lacks critical analyses to understand the model components:
   - No ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (e.g., the distance encoding in sequence tagging)
   - No detailed error analysis to understand where the models fail
   - No comparison with more recent neural models (e.g., BERT, transformers)

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide evidence for the claim that "roughly 92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences"? How did you calculate this percentage?

2. Why did you choose to encode distance d in the tag for the sequence tagging approach instead of having separate tasks for component detection and relation identification? The authors acknowledge that "naively coupling component and relation detection is not optimal" but proceed to do so in their STagT approach.

3. Can you provide statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons in the paper, particularly the key claims that your models outperform the ILP baseline?

4. Why did you not include a comparison with more recent neural models (e.g., BERT, transformer-based architectures) that have shown strong performance in other NLP tasks?

5. Can you clarify how your dependency parsing approach actually works given the fundamental flaw where the "head" is defined as the first token of a component rather than a single token?

## Recommendation

**Reject**

This paper presents a potentially interesting approach to computational argumentation mining, but the fundamental flaw in the dependency parsing approach (which is presented as one of the main contributions) is severe and undermines the validity of one of the core methods. Combined with the lack of statistical significance tests, poor formatting of key results, unsubstantiated claims, and absence of critical analyses like ablation studies and error analysis, this paper does not meet the standards expected for publication at ACL.

The methodological flaw in the dependency parsing approach is particularly concerning. The authors are attempting to use dependency parsing as a framework for AM, but they've fundamentally misunderstood how dependency parsing works by defining "head" as the first token of a component rather than a single token. This is not a minor oversight—it's a critical error that renders that entire approach invalid.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper with these substantial methodological and presentation flaws. While the paper has some good ideas, the technical errors and lack of proper statistical validation are too significant to overlook.
