

# ACL Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the limitations of previous pipeline-based methods that rely on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The authors propose four neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER (a model combining sequential and tree structure information). The main contributions I identify are:

1. **First neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining** that eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design, which are significant practical barriers in prior work.
2. **Demonstration that sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model** across multiple evaluation metrics, particularly when considering both component and relation identification.
3. **Insight that dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM** (contrary to prior work that operated on component level), while sequence tagging with distance encoding is robust across different environments.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Contribution**: The paper addresses a genuine limitation in current AM systems - the heavy reliance on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints that must be manually adapted for each new dataset. By showing that neural approaches can eliminate this barrier, the work has direct practical implications for researchers and practitioners in the field. The comparison to the feature-based ILP model (Stab and Gurevych, 2016) is particularly compelling, with the neural sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) outperforming it by 13% on component F1 (100% match) and 11% on relation F1 at the paragraph level.

2. **Thorough Evaluation and Insightful Analysis**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation across multiple neural framings and both paragraph and essay levels. The analysis of *why* certain models perform better (e.g., LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level due to the combinatorial explosion of possible relations) adds significant depth. The paper's finding that "the number of possible trees to consider on n tokens may just be too huge a search space when n is large" for dependency parsers is particularly insightful for understanding the limitations of different approaches.

3. **Clear Methodological Innovation**: The sequence tagging approach that encodes distance information between components (STagT) is a clever solution to the challenge of modeling long-range dependencies in argument structures. The paper demonstrates that this approach is not only effective but also robust, as it performs well across different text lengths and environments. This is a practical contribution that could inform future work in argumentation mining and related structured prediction tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The paper only evaluates on the persuasive essays dataset (PE) from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which has a specific structure (argumentative units mostly confined to paragraphs, almost tree-like structure). It's unclear whether the findings generalize to other datasets with different argumentation structures. The authors mention the need for more diverse datasets but don't address how their approach would scale to them, which limits the generalizability of their findings.

2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis with Other Neural Approaches**: While the paper compares to the ILP model, it doesn't compare to other recent neural approaches for AM or related tasks (e.g., neural relation extraction). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claim that their approach represents the state of the art. For instance, the paper mentions Potash et al. (2017) but doesn't incorporate their findings into the comparison.

3. **Overstatement of "First" Claim**: The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but the reference to Potash et al. (2017) at the end suggests that neural approaches for AM were being developed concurrently. The authors should clarify whether their work is truly the first or if they're referring to a specific aspect of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural end-to-end approach for AM, but Potash et al. (2017) appears to have proposed a neural approach. Could you clarify whether your work is the first, or if you're referring to a specific aspect of your approach?

2. The PE dataset has a specific structure where argumentative units are mostly confined to paragraphs. How would your sequence tagging approach with distance encoding perform on datasets where argumentative units span multiple paragraphs or entire documents?

3. The paper shows that LSTM-ER performs poorly on essay level for relation identification. Is this due to the model's inability to handle long sequences or due to the increased complexity of relations in longer texts?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in computational argumentation mining. The paper's main strength is its practical focus on eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which has been a significant barrier to applying AM systems to new domains. The experimental results are thorough and well-analyzed, and the insights about model design (particularly the effectiveness of sequence tagging with distance encoding) are valuable for the field.

The weaknesses are important but not deal-breakers. The limited dataset scope is a valid concern, but the paper's contribution remains significant for the specific dataset it evaluates. The overstatement of the "first" claim is a minor issue that can be addressed in revision.

The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining, demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can outperform previous state-of-the-art methods while eliminating the need for manual feature engineering. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
