- Contribution: First neural end-to-end solutions for full computational argumentation mining without pipeline architectures.  
- Contribution: Neural approaches outperform prior ILP-based state-of-the-art across multiple metrics.  
- Contribution: Token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, while sequence tagging with distance encoding is robust.

- Strength: Eliminates hand-crafted features and pipeline error propagation, representing a meaningful advance.  
- Strength: Sequence tagging (STagBLCC) achieves consistently superior empirical results to the ILP baseline.  
- Strength: Key insight that many relations cross sentences (92%), explaining dependency parsing failures.  
- Strength: Multi-task learning (component and relation subtasks) improves performance; best config Y-3:YC-3:YR-3 shows strong gains.

- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of why neural models outperform ILP (e.g., role of BiLSTMs, specific components).  
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to the PE dataset, limiting generalizability to other AM schemes.  
- Weakness: Lacks detailed error analysis to reveal specific failure modes (long-range relations, essay-level issues).  
- Weakness: Overemphasis on paragraph vs. essay evaluation without fully contextualizing dataset limitations.  
- Weakness: Inadequate comparison with other recent neural methods from related tasks (relation extraction, parsing).

- Question: Why does LSTM-ER suffer a large drop in essay-level R-F1 — combinatorial explosion or other factors?  
- Question: Why is token-level dependency parsing ineffective despite argument structures often forming trees?  
- Question: How do you reconcile that enforcing tree constraints aligns with theory yet performs worse than sequence tagging?

- Overall: Novel, well-executed work with strong empirical results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
