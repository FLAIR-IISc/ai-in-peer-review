

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and combining sequential and tree structure information. The authors claim to present the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, showing that neural approaches outperform traditional ILP-based methods while eliminating the need for hand-crafted features.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The authors present neural network approaches for end-to-end AM, which represents a novel contribution to the field.
2. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of different neural approaches to AM, demonstrating that sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly across different environments.
3. The authors offer practical insights about when different approaches are most effective for different document lengths (short vs. long documents).

## Strengths
1. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough comparison of multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) against a state-of-the-art ILP-based system. The evaluation is conducted on a well-known dataset (PE) with multiple metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at 100% and 50% levels), which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Clear problem framing:** The paper effectively organizes the different neural approaches by their underlying machine learning paradigms (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning), making it easier for readers to understand the relationships between the approaches.

3. **Practical insights on document length:** The paper provides valuable practical insights about the relationship between document length and model performance ("When there are few entities in a text ('short documents'), a more general framework such as that provided in LSTM-ER performs reasonably well. When there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable."). This is a useful contribution to the field that could guide future research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The paper states that "Best scores in each column in bold (significant at p < 0.01; Two-sided Wilcoxon signed rank test, pairing F1 scores for documents)" but fails to provide the actual p-values for the comparisons. This is a serious methodological flaw that undermines the main claims of the paper. Without the actual p-values, it's impossible to verify the statistical significance of the reported performance differences.

2. **Inconsistent labeling scheme and terminology:** The paper defines the label set as Y = {(b, t, d, s) | b ∈ {B, I,O}, t ∈ {P,C,MC,⊥}, d ∈ {. . . ,−2,−1, 1, 2, . . . ,⊥}, s ∈ {Supp,Att, For,Ag,⊥}}. However, in the example they provide, they use "For" for the relation type, while earlier in the paper they state "s ∈ {Supp,Att, For,Ag,⊥}". This is inconsistent with the dataset description where they say "relations and their types" include "Support" or "Attack." The use of "For" instead of "Support" is particularly confusing and undermines the clarity of the paper.

3. **Inadequate evaluation of dependency parsing:** The paper claims that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective" but only evaluates four parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser, LSTM-Parser) and doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why dependency parsing doesn't work well for AM. The evaluation is limited to paragraph-level performance, and the paper doesn't explain why they chose to evaluate only on paragraph level for dependency parsing while using essay level for other models. The claim that dependency parsing is ineffective appears to be based on insufficient evidence.

4. **Overstated novelty claim:** The paper claims to present "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM" without providing a thorough literature review to support this claim. The references include Potash et al. (2017) which is "submitted during the preparation of our current work," suggesting that other works might have explored similar approaches. The authors should clarify why they believe their work is the first.

5. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies for key design choices, such as the distance encoding in the sequence tagging approach. Without these studies, it's impossible to determine how much the distance encoding contributes to the performance. The paper states that "a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly," but doesn't provide evidence for why this specific encoding is beneficial.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the LSTM-Parser is over 100% better than Kiperwasser on exact spans." Could you clarify what "over 100% better" means in terms of actual performance metrics (e.g., absolute percentage points, relative improvement)?

2. The paper uses "For" as a relation type, but earlier states "s ∈ {Supp,Att, For,Ag,⊥}". Could you clarify the difference between "For" and "Supp" (Support)? The dataset description mentions "Support" or "Attack" as relation types, so this inconsistency is confusing.

3. The paper claims that "the C task is consistently more helpful as an auxiliary task than the R task" in the multi-task learning setup. Could you provide more details about why this might be the case? Is it related to the nature of component detection vs. relation detection in argumentation structures?

4. The paper states that "the LSTM-Parser's deterioration [from paragraph to essay level] is much more severe than the other models' surveyed below." Could you provide more detailed analysis of why this is the case, given that dependency parsers are generally designed to handle longer sequences?

5. The paper claims to be the "first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but Potash et al. (2017) is mentioned as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work." Could you clarify why you believe your work is the first, given that Potash et al. (2017) was being prepared concurrently?

## Recommendation
Reject

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation, the methodological flaws and inconsistencies are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The lack of statistical significance testing, inconsistent labeling scheme, inadequate evaluation of dependency parsing, overstated novelty claim, and lack of ablation studies are significant issues that undermine the paper's contributions. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with these fundamental methodological flaws, even though the work itself has potential value.
