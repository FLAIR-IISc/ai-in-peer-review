

# Peer Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper presents the first neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the limitations of previous pipeline methods that relied on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP) constraints. The authors propose four neural frameworks: dependency parsing (token-level), sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER (which combines sequential and tree structure information). They evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing them against the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach.

## Main Contributions
1. The first comprehensive neural end-to-end approach for computational argumentation mining, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering.
2. Empirical demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model by a significant margin (up to 13% on component F1).
3. Key insight that token-level dependency parsing (previously suggested for AM) is ineffective, contrary to component-level approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Methodological Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and important contribution by being the first to propose neural end-to-end solutions for AM. Previous work (Persing & Ng, 2016; Stab & Gurevych, 2016) relied on pipeline architectures with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which are labor-intensive and prone to error propagation. The neural approaches eliminate these limitations while achieving better performance.

2. **Rigorous Experimental Comparison**: The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of multiple neural approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER) against the ILP baseline. Their results are robust across different evaluation metrics (exact and approximate matching) and different input levels (paragraph vs. essay). The finding that sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagBLCC) outperforms dependency parsing approaches is particularly valuable and counter to previous assumptions.

3. **Important Theoretical Insight**: The paper makes a crucial contribution by demonstrating that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrary to previous approaches that operated on the component level. The authors provide a convincing explanation: dependency parsers' global view becomes too complex for long sequences, and the search space of possible trees becomes prohibitively large at the essay level.

4. **Practical Value of Multi-Task Learning**: The authors show that adding natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup improves performance by several percentage points. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks in NLP, not just AM.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The paper uses only the Stab and Gurevych (2016) persuasive essays dataset, which consists of student essays on specific topics. The authors don't sufficiently discuss the limitations of this dataset for general argumentation mining. For example, the dataset is limited to educational contexts with specific argument structures, which may not generalize to legal, political, or scientific discourse where argumentation patterns differ significantly.

2. **Inadequate Error Analysis**: While the paper provides F1 scores, it lacks a detailed analysis of error types. Understanding *why* certain approaches fail (e.g., are errors due to incorrect component segmentation, relation identification, or other factors) would provide deeper insights into the limitations of each approach and guide future work.

3. **Overstated Claim About Dependency Parsing**: The claim that "a framing of AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective" is too strong. The paper shows that the specific dependency parsing approaches they tested performed poorly, but it doesn't rule out the possibility that a dependency parser specifically designed for AM could be effective. The authors should temper this claim to reflect that token-level dependency parsing as currently implemented is ineffective, not that the approach itself is inherently unsuitable.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component in the proposed models. For example, how much of the improvement in STagBLCC comes from the BiLSTM-CRF component versus the character-level CNN? This would help readers understand what aspects of the model are most important.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the LSTM-ER model has access to both paragraph and essay level information," but the ILP model also has access to this information. Could you clarify how the ILP model uses paragraph-level information, and why this doesn't help it match the performance of LSTM-ER on paragraph level?

2. The authors claim that "the LSTM-ER model does not need to produce trees, as we observe in AM datasets." However, the paper also states that the argument structure in each essay is "almost a tree." How do you reconcile these two statements, and what specific aspects of the LSTM-ER model allow it to handle the tree-like structure without enforcing tree constraints?

3. The paper mentions that "92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences." How does this affect the design of neural approaches for AM, and why does this not make the sequence tagging approach (which relies on token-level relationships) particularly challenging?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to computational argumentation mining by introducing the first neural end-to-end approaches for the task. The empirical results are convincing, and the key insight about token-level dependency parsing being ineffective is particularly valuable for the field. The paper advances the state-of-the-art by a meaningful margin and provides a robust baseline for future work. The limitations in dataset scope and error analysis are notable but do not undermine the main contributions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above.
