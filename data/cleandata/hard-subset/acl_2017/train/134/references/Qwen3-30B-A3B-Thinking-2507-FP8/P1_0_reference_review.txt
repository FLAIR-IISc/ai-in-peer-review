

# Peer Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), a task that involves identifying argumentative structures in text (component segmentation, component classification, relation identification, and relation classification). The authors propose four neural framing approaches: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning (MTL), and adaptation of the LSTM-ER model. They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing against the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1. A comprehensive exploration of neural approaches for end-to-end AM, which is the first such study in the literature
2. The demonstration that a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods
3. Valuable insights about when different modeling approaches (constrained vs. general) are appropriate for AM

## Strengths

1. **First comprehensive neural approach for end-to-end AM**: This is a significant contribution to the field as the authors systematically explore multiple neural architectures for the end-to-end AM problem, which has previously been dominated by pipeline approaches with hand-crafted features. The paper provides the first empirical comparison of neural approaches for this task.

2. **Strong empirical results showing sequence tagging with distance encoding is effective**: The STagBLCC approach consistently outperforms the ILP baseline and other neural approaches across multiple metrics. This finding is particularly valuable because it demonstrates a practical, effective neural approach for AM that avoids the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The performance gains (e.g., 13% better C-F1 and 11% better R-F1 on paragraph level) are substantial.

3. **Important insights about modeling constraints**: The paper provides valuable theoretical and empirical insights about when different modeling approaches are appropriate for AM. The authors correctly identify that for longer texts with many components, a more constrained modeling approach (like STagT) is preferable to a general one (like LSTM-ER), as the latter suffers from combinatorial explosion of possible relations. This is a key contribution to the field's understanding of AM modeling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (PE), and the authors don't test their approaches on other AM datasets (e.g., the microtext corpus from Peldszus and Stede, 2016). This limits the generalizability of their findings. The paper claims their sequence tagging approach "performs robustly in different environments," but without testing on multiple datasets, this claim is not well-supported.

2. **Insufficient analysis of dependency parsing failure**: The authors claim that dependency parsing framing is ineffective but provide limited explanation for why this is the case. They note that performance drops drastically when moving from paragraph to essay level, but don't provide a thorough analysis of the specific failure modes or why dependency parsing is particularly unsuited to this task compared to other neural approaches. The hypothesis about "very long sequences" is plausible but not thoroughly demonstrated.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components in their proposed approaches. For example, how much does the distance encoding specifically contribute to the sequence tagging approach's success? Without these studies, it's difficult to determine which aspects of their method are most valuable.

4. **Overstated claims about "first neural end-to-end solutions"**: While the paper does present a comprehensive neural approach, the authors claim this is "the first" such solution. However, the Potash et al. (2017) work mentioned in the references (which was submitted during the preparation of this work) appears to have explored neural approaches for AM, though not necessarily end-to-end. The authors should acknowledge this work and clarify their novelty claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their STagT approach "performs robustly in different environments," but only evaluates on a single dataset. Could you provide results on other AM datasets (e.g., the microtext corpus from Peldszus and Stede, 2016) to support this claim?

2. The authors note that LSTM-ER's relation identification performance degrades significantly on essay level. Given that the paper's main contribution is to demonstrate the superiority of neural approaches, could you provide more detailed analysis of why the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding avoids this problem while LSTM-ER does not?

3. Could you elaborate on why the dependency parsing approach fails so dramatically on essay level, beyond the hypothesis about sequence length? Are there specific failure patterns that could be addressed in future work?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing the first comprehensive exploration of neural approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining. The empirical results showing the effectiveness of sequence tagging with distance encoding are strong and well-supported. However, the limited evaluation scope and lack of sufficient ablation studies weaken the paper's claims about the generalizability and component contributions of their approach. With additional evaluation on other datasets and more thorough ablation studies, this work could make an even stronger contribution to the field. The insights about appropriate modeling constraints for AM are particularly valuable for future research.
