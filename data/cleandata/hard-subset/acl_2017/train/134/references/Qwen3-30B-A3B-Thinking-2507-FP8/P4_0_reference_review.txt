

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative components (claims, premises), their types, and the relations between them. The authors argue that previous approaches relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures with error propagation, and they propose four neural end-to-end frameworks: (1) framing AM as dependency parsing, (2) framing AM as sequence tagging with distance information encoded in tags, (3) framing AM as multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task, and (4) adapting the LSTM-ER model. The authors evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing them with the state-of-the-art ILP-based approach.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer)

1. **First neural end-to-end approaches for computational AM**: The paper presents the first neural solutions for end-to-end AM, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints that characterized previous work.

2. **Demonstration of superior performance over ILP baseline**: The authors show that neural approaches consistently outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple evaluation metrics, with the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding achieving the best results.

3. **Insightful analysis of model suitability for AM task**: The paper provides a thorough analysis explaining why certain neural approaches work better than others for AM, particularly highlighting that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective while sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly.

## Strengths

1. **Significant novelty in the field**: This is the first paper to present neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining. Previous work relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures, making this a clear contribution to the literature. The authors correctly identify this as a gap in the field and fill it with multiple neural approaches that are theoretically well-motivated.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments comparing their neural approaches with the ILP baseline across multiple evaluation metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at 100% and 50% match levels) and on both paragraph and essay levels. The results consistently show neural approaches outperforming the ILP model, with the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding achieving the best results. The comparative analysis across different framing approaches is particularly strong.

3. **Insightful analysis of model behavior**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of why certain approaches work better than others, which adds significant value beyond just reporting performance numbers. For example, the authors correctly identify that dependency parsing approaches fail because the AM data doesn't form clean trees at the token level, while sequence tagging with distance encoding works well because it captures the long-range dependencies characteristic of AM. This kind of analysis is essential for understanding the problem and guiding future work.

4. **Practical implications and policy recommendations**: The paper concludes with clear, actionable recommendations for when to use which approach: "When there are few entities in a text ('short documents'), a more general framework such as that provided in LSTM-ER performs reasonably well. When there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable." This practical guidance is valuable for practitioners in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with more recent methods**: The paper compares with the ILP model from Stab and Gurevych (2016) but does not incorporate more recent neural approaches to AM that have emerged since then. The authors mention Potash et al. (2017) as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" but don't include this in their comparison. A more comprehensive comparison with the current state-of-the-art would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

2. **Lack of ablation studies for key design choices**: The authors propose encoding distance information in the tag labels for their sequence tagging approach, but they don't provide ablation studies to quantify how much this design choice contributes to performance. For example, they could have compared their approach with and without the distance information to isolate its specific contribution.

3. **Limited discussion of dataset limitations**: The PE dataset has only 402 essays (80 for testing), which is relatively small for training neural models. The paper doesn't adequately address whether the results would generalize to larger or more diverse datasets. The authors should have discussed potential limitations due to the small dataset size and how this might affect the generalizability of their findings.

4. **Insufficient error analysis**: While the authors provide some analysis of why certain models fail, they don't provide a detailed error analysis of the best-performing model. Understanding common error patterns would help identify remaining challenges and guide future work, which would strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Lack of computational efficiency discussion**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed neural approaches compared to the ILP model. Given that neural models can be computationally expensive, this is an important consideration for practical applications that the authors should have addressed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions Potash et al. (2017) as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" but doesn't incorporate their results. Could the authors provide a brief comparison of their approach with Potash et al.'s encoder-decoder approach?

2. The authors state that dependency parsing is ineffective because "the argument structure in each essay is—almost—a tree" with "several major claims" violating the tree structure. Could the authors clarify how they addressed the multi-root structure in their dependency parsing framing, and why they believe this approach is fundamentally unsuitable for AM?

3. The paper mentions that "the size of the label set Y is potentially infinite" but that "we would expect it to be finite." Could the authors provide more details on how they determined the range of distance values to include in the label space, and how they handled cases where the distance was outside this range?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a clear contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining by introducing the first neural end-to-end approaches. The experimental evaluation is thorough and the insights provided about why certain approaches work better than others are valuable. However, the paper could be strengthened by incorporating more recent methods in the comparison, conducting ablation studies for key design choices, and addressing dataset limitations more thoroughly. The weaknesses are significant but not severe enough to warrant rejection, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
