

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative structures in text including component segmentation, component typing, and relation identification. The authors compare several neural network framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER (a model combining sequential and tree structure information). They evaluate these approaches on the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016).

**Main Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength):**

1. **Demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches outperform traditional feature-based ILP models for argumentation mining**: The authors provide comprehensive empirical evidence that neural approaches achieve significantly better performance than the state-of-the-art ILP-based system (up to 13% better for component F1 and 11% for relation F1 on the paragraph level), while eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and constraint design.

2. **Proposing and evaluating a sequence tagging framework with distance encoding for AM**: The key technical contribution is their novel approach to encoding relation information directly into token-level sequence tags using component-based distance (not token-based), which effectively handles long-range dependencies in argument structures.

3. **Providing empirical evidence about the limitations of dependency parsing for AM at the token level**: The paper shows that framing AM as a dependency parsing problem at the token level is ineffective (contrary to what has been proposed for systems operating at the component level), which is an important insight for the field.

4. **Demonstrating the effectiveness of multi-task learning for AM**: The authors show that adding component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning framework improves performance on the main AM task.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for neural end-to-end approaches**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments across multiple neural network framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) and on both paragraph and essay levels. The performance gains over the ILP-based system are significant (up to 13% absolute improvement in component F1 on the paragraph level), which represents a substantial contribution to the field. The paper clearly demonstrates that neural approaches can solve the end-to-end AM problem without the need for manual feature engineering.

2. **Novel sequence tagging framework with distance encoding**: The authors' key technical contribution is well-motivated by their data analysis (Figure 2 shows that related components are typically close in terms of component distance, not token distance). This encoding approach effectively allows a sequence tagging model to handle long-range dependencies inherent in argumentation structures. The paper demonstrates that this approach is robust across different environments and outperforms other neural approaches.

3. **Important insights about dependency parsing limitations**: The paper provides valuable empirical evidence that dependency parsing approaches (which work well for syntactic parsing) are ineffective for AM at the token level, contrary to what might be expected. The authors provide a reasonable explanation for this failure (long sequences, global tree constraints not matching argumentation structure), which is important for guiding future research in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with other neural approaches**: The paper only compares its proposed approaches to the ILP-based system from Stab and Gurevych (2016). There are other recent neural approaches to related tasks (e.g., relation extraction, entity recognition) that could have been compared to provide better context for the claimed novelty. For example, the authors mention Potash et al. (2017) in the conclusion but don't compare with their encoder-decoder approach.

2. **Lack of detailed analysis of dependency parsing failures**: While the paper shows that dependency parsing performs poorly, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why it fails beyond the general statement about long sequences. A deeper analysis of specific failure modes (e.g., what types of argument structures are most problematic) would strengthen the paper's argument about the limitations of this approach.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies for key innovations**: The paper introduces a novel tagging scheme with distance encoding, but doesn't provide ablation studies to show how much of the performance gain comes from the distance encoding versus other aspects of the model (like the BiLSTM-CRF architecture). This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of their key innovation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is novel, but the idea of encoding relation information into tags is somewhat similar to approaches used in semantic role labeling (e.g., "B-ARG0" tags). How does their approach specifically differ from or improve upon these existing methods for argumentation mining, given the unique characteristics of argument structures?

2. The paper states that dependency parsing fails on essay level due to long sequences, but doesn't provide detailed analysis of how performance varies with sequence length. Could the authors provide more data on how performance changes as the sequence length increases (e.g., for different paragraph lengths)?

3. The authors mention that LSTM-ER performs better on paragraph level than on essay level. Could they provide more specific details about the challenges that arise when scaling to essay level, particularly regarding the number of components and relations (e.g., how many more possible relations exist on essay level compared to paragraph level)?
