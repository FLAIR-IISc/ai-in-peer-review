- Proposes neural end-to-end solutions for full computational argumentation mining (component segmentation, typing, relation ID) without hand-crafted features or pipelines.  
- Frames AM as four neural problems: dependency parsing, specialized sequence tagging with distance/relation encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER adaptation.  
- Evaluates on the PE dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016) and compares to the ILP feature-based baseline; reports STagBLCC achieving 55.22% global F1 at essay level.  
- Main contribution: first neural end-to-end AM solutions removing manual feature engineering and ILP constraints.  
- Shows neural approaches match/outperform the ILP baseline, with sequence tagging plus distance encoding being most effective.  
- Identifies sequence tagging with distance encoding as the best framing and explains why token-level dependency parsing is ineffective.  
- Strength: significant methodological advance demonstrating neural end-to-end methods can outperform prior pipeline approaches.  
- Strength: comprehensive experimental comparison of four framings with careful analysis of relative strengths and weaknesses.  
- Strength: practical result that a standard neural sequence tagger with distance encoding is robust and simpler to implement.  
- Strength: offers a clear theoretical explanation for token-level dependency parsing failure (prohibitively large search space for long sequences).  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to more recent neural AM models (e.g., GNNs, transformers), limiting the state-of-the-art claim.  
- Weakness: insufficient detailed error analysis, particularly regarding model failures on long-range or inter-sentence relations.  
- Weakness: inadequate ablation/justification for the complex tagging scheme encoding distance and relation info.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of why performance drops from paragraph- to essay-level and whether architectural/training fixes could mitigate this.  
- Question: How does STagBLCC compare to recent neural AM systems (GNNs/transformers) and can it be adapted to them?  
- Question: Given 92% of relations are inter-sentence, how was sentence-level information considered or incorporated?  
- Question: Can the authors provide error analysis on long-range relations (e.g., performance for distance d > 10)?  
- Question: What evidence shows the specific distance-encoding tagging scheme — rather than a standard tagger — drives the performance gains?  
- Overall: Well-executed, valuable contribution demonstrating neural end-to-end AM is viable; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing comparisons and error analysis.
