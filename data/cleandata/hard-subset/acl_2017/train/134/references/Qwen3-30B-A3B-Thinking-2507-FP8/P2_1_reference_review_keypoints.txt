- Proposes four neural end-to-end AM approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) claiming first neural end-to-end solutions.  
- Shows neural approaches outperform prior ILP state-of-the-art by up to 13% C-F1 and 11% R-F1.  
- Finds token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, contrasting with component-level parsing results.  
- Demonstrates sequence tagging with distance encoding is robust and consistently effective across settings.  
- Provides practical guidance: use LSTM-ER for short documents and more restrained models for long documents.  
- Uses strong evaluation methodology (Persing & Ng metric) with paragraph and essay-level comparisons and analysis of document length effects.  
- Novelty claim is weakened by concurrent Potash et al. (2017); needs clearer acknowledgment and differentiation.  
- Evaluation is limited to a single PE dataset, raising concerns about generalization to other AM datasets and domains.  
- Explanation for why distance encoding helps is superficial and requires deeper analysis of interactions with LSTM and long-range dependencies.  
- Reported multi-task learning gains are marginal and need clearer quantification and statistical significance testing.  
- Questions for authors: revise novelty claim given Potash et al.; provide deeper analysis of distance encoding’s contribution; explain sequence-tagging design given 92% inter-sentence relations.  
- Overall assessment: strong empirical and analytic contribution with valuable practical insights; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
