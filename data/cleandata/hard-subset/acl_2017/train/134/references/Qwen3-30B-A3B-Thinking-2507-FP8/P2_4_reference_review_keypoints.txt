- Paper introduces first neural end-to-end solutions for full argumentation mining (component segmentation, typing, relation ID and typing) without hand-crafted features or pipelines.  
- Proposes and evaluates four neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adapted LSTM-ER.  
- Neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP baseline on the PE dataset.  
- Token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, unlike component-level dependency approaches.  
- Sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly across settings.  
- First neural end-to-end AM solution addresses portability and error-propagation issues of pipeline/feature-based methods.  
- Systematic comparison of neural framings provides insights on what works and why (e.g., distance encoding importance, trade-offs between generality and constraint).  
- Practical recommendations: restrained models for long documents, LSTM-ER suitable for short documents, and component detection as an auxiliary MTL task helps.  
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset (PE), reducing generalizability; cross-dataset validation is missing.  
- Insufficient analysis of failure modes (e.g., why LSTM-ER degrades on essay-level relation ID) and lack of concrete error analysis.  
- Lack of ablation studies to quantify contributions of components like distance encoding and specific auxiliary tasks.  
- Question: need details on paragraph- vs. essay-level performance gaps and how many relations span paragraph boundaries.  
- Question: request clarification why token-level parsing fails while component-level parsing succeeds beyond tree-structure claims.  
- Question: ask how neural models would perform if given the same paragraph-level context available to the ILP model.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address dataset scope, failure-mode analysis, and ablation studies.
