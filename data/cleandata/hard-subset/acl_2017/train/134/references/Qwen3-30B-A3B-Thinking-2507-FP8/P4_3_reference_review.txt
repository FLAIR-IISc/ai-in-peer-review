

# Review for ACL Submission: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative structures in text including component segmentation, component typing, relation identification, and relation typing. The authors propose four neural network framings for the task: (1) framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, (2) framing AM as a sequence tagging problem with a specific label set encoding distance information between components, (3) framing AM as a multi-task learning problem with natural subtasks as auxiliary tasks, and (4) adapting the LSTM-ER model for AM. They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing them to a feature-based ILP model.

## Main Contributions

1. The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraint design that previous approaches relied on.

2. The authors demonstrate that a neural sequence tagging model encoding distance information between components (STagT) performs robustly across different environments and achieves state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset.

3. The paper provides empirical evidence that framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem is ineffective (contrary to previous work that operated at the component level), and that multi-task learning with natural subtasks as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Technical Approach**: The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to present neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining. Previous approaches relied on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which are difficult to adapt to different datasets. The authors demonstrate that neural approaches can outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering. This is a clear technical advancement for the field.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural network approaches across different evaluation metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at both exact and approximate match levels) and at both paragraph and essay levels. The evaluation design is appropriate for the task and reveals important insights about the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches. For example, the paper shows that sequence tagging (particularly STagBLCC) outperforms dependency parsing approaches by a significant margin (13-11% absolute improvement in C-F1 and R-F1 on the paragraph level).

3. **Insightful Analysis**: The authors provide valuable explanations for their empirical results, particularly regarding why certain approaches work better than others. They correctly identify that dependency parsing is ineffective at the token level due to the complexity of the search space for longer sequences, and that multi-task learning with natural subtasks improves performance by providing additional signals to the model. This analysis goes beyond simple results reporting and provides meaningful guidance for future work in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The paper evaluates only on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is a relatively small dataset (402 essays, 80 for testing). The authors do not discuss whether their approach would generalize to other AM datasets, which limits the generalizability of their findings. While this is the only dataset with full AM annotations, the paper would be stronger with additional evaluation on other datasets or with a discussion of potential challenges in adapting to other datasets.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: While the paper discusses the impact of multi-task learning, it lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, it's unclear how much of the improvement from multi-task learning comes from the specific auxiliary tasks versus simply having more training data. The paper mentions that including components as an auxiliary task is more helpful than including relations, but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these comparisons.

3. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper cites Potash et al. (2017) as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work," but doesn't compare their approach to this recent study. Given that Potash et al. also explores neural approaches to AM, the absence of a direct comparison limits the paper's ability to position its contribution within the current state of the art.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the PE dataset contains 402 essays with 80 reserved for testing. Could the authors provide more details about the class distribution of components (major claims, claims, premises) across the training and test sets to ensure there's no significant imbalance?

2. The authors state that dependency parsing approaches are ineffective at the token level. Could they elaborate on why they believe this is the case, especially given that previous work (Peldszus and Stede, 2015) successfully applied discourse parsing to AM at the component level? Is this limitation specific to the token-level framing, or would it apply to component-level parsing as well?

3. The paper states that LSTM-ER performs well on paragraph level but degrades significantly on essay level. Could the authors provide more details about how the essay-level evaluation was conducted (e.g., were essays split into paragraphs for the essay-level evaluation, or were they processed as single sequences?), and how the performance degradation compares to other models?
