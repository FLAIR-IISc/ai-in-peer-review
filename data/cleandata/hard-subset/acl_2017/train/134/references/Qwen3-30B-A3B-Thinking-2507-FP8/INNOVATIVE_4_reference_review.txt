

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents the first neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), challenging the existing pipeline-based methods that rely on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP) constraints. The authors propose four novel neural framings of the AM problem: (1) dependency parsing at the token level, (2) sequence tagging with a novel tag format encoding distance information between components, (3) multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks for component detection and relation identification, and (4) adaptation of LSTM-ER for AM.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. The first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraint design.
2. The demonstration that a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding outperforms the state-of-the-art ILP model and is robust across different environments.
3. The surprising finding that token-level dependency parsing (unlike component-level approaches) is ineffective for AM, which challenges previous assumptions in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Novelty**: The paper makes a strong and well-supported claim of being the first to propose neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining. This is a major contribution to the field, as previous work has relied on feature-based pipeline approaches with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The novelty of this contribution is substantial, as it opens up a new research direction for AM.

2. **Challenging Assumptions with Evidence**: The paper's finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to what had been proposed for component-level approaches) is a significant scientific contribution. The authors provide clear empirical evidence from their experiments, which corrects a potential misconception in the field and demonstrates critical thinking about the problem.

3. **Elegant and Simple Solution**: The sequence tagging approach with the novel tag format (b, t, d, s) that encodes distance information between components is both elegant and effective. This approach is simple to implement yet outperforms more complex models, showing that sometimes the simplest solution can be the most effective.

4. **Strong Baseline Comparison**: The authors provide a thorough comparison against the state-of-the-art ILP model (Stab and Gurevych, 2016), showing that their neural approaches outperform it across multiple metrics. This comparison is crucial for establishing the significance of their contribution.

5. **Practical Implications**: The paper demonstrates that neural approaches eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and costly ILP constraint design, which are significant practical barriers to adopting AM systems in real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Dataset Evaluation**: The paper evaluates all approaches on a single dataset (the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which limits the generalizability of their claims. While this is a standard dataset, it would be stronger to see results on additional AM datasets (e.g., the Peldszus and Stede corpus) to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. **Lack of Detailed Analysis**: While the paper presents strong empirical results, it lacks a deeper analysis of *why* certain approaches work better than others. For instance, it would be valuable to understand why the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding outperforms the dependency parsing approach.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their proposed approaches, particularly regarding scalability to very long documents or the impact of different text lengths on performance. This is important for understanding the boundaries of their method's applicability.

4. **Potential Overstatement of Novelty**: While the paper claims to be the first to propose neural end-to-end solutions for AM, the authors should conduct a more thorough literature review to ensure there isn't prior work they've missed. A more comprehensive comparison to existing neural approaches would strengthen their novelty claim.

5. **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or training requirements of their neural approaches, which is important for assessing their practicality and scalability for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, while component-level approaches work better. Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is this a fundamental limitation of dependency parsing for AM, or is it specific to the dataset and annotation scheme used?

2. How do your results generalize to other AM datasets beyond the PE dataset? Have you tested your approaches on the Peldszus and Stede (2016) corpus, which is another standard AM dataset?

3. The paper mentions that LSTM-ER's performance degrades on essay level due to many more entities and possible relations. Have you considered using techniques like attention mechanisms to better handle long-range dependencies in longer texts, which might mitigate this issue?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a significant and novel contribution by presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational argumentation mining. This is a bold approach that challenges the existing paradigm in the field and demonstrates strong empirical results. While there are some limitations in the evaluation scope and analysis depth, these are outweighed by the significance of the novelty and the robust performance of their proposed approaches. The paper's contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication in ACL, as it opens up a new research direction that will likely inspire future work in the field.
