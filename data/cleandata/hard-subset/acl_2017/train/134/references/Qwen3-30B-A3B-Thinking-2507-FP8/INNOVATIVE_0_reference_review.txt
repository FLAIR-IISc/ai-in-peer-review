

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Primary Contributions

This paper introduces neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which traditionally has been approached through pipeline architectures with hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP) constraints. The authors propose four neural approaches: (1) framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, (2) framing AM as a sequence tagging problem with distance encoding, (3) framing AM as a multi-task learning problem, and (4) adapting the LSTM-ER model.

The primary contributions I see (noting the authors' claims):

1. **The first neural end-to-end solution for computational argumentation mining** - This is a significant conceptual shift from the field's previous approaches that relied on hand-crafted features and pipeline architectures.
2. **Demonstration that a simple sequence tagging approach with distance encoding outperforms more complex approaches** - The authors show that their STagBLCC model (a BiLSTM-CRF with character-level CNN) outperforms both dependency parsing approaches and LSTM-ER in the end-to-end setting.
3. **Key insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM** - Contrary to what has been proposed for component-level AM systems, the authors demonstrate that token-level dependency parsing is not suitable for end-to-end AM, which is a valuable contribution that prevents future researchers from pursuing a dead-end approach.

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual framework for AM**: The paper makes a bold and significant contribution by introducing neural end-to-end learning for AM, which is a completely new direction in the field. Prior work has been limited to pipeline approaches with hand-crafted features, and the authors successfully demonstrate that neural networks can address the entire AM problem in a more unified manner. This is the most significant contribution of the paper.

2. **Strong empirical evidence for a simple solution**: The paper demonstrates that a relatively straightforward sequence tagging approach with distance encoding (STagBLCC) outperforms more complex approaches like dependency parsing and LSTM-ER. This is a valuable insight that challenges assumptions about what is needed for AM and provides a simpler, more effective solution than previously available. The authors show a clear advantage over the state-of-the-art ILP model.

3. **Important insight about token-level dependency parsing**: The paper's finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (in contrast to component-level approaches) is a significant contribution. This insight prevents future researchers from wasting time on a flawed approach and provides a clear direction for the field.

4. **Practical multi-task learning insight**: The authors demonstrate that adding component detection as an auxiliary task improves performance, which is a valuable practical contribution for the field. This finding has broader implications for sequence labeling tasks where component and relation identification are related.

5. **Clear comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper provides a thorough comparison to the ILP-based approach from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is the current state-of-the-art. The neural approaches consistently outperform this baseline, which strengthens the paper's claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited dataset scope**: The paper evaluates only on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), which is a single dataset. While this is a standard dataset in the field, the lack of evaluation on other datasets (e.g., Peldszus and Stede's microtexts) limits the generalizability of the claims. A more robust evaluation would include multiple datasets.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why simple models work better**: While the paper demonstrates that simpler models outperform more complex ones, it lacks a deep analysis of *why* this is the case. Understanding why simpler models work better could provide deeper insights for the field.

3. **Lack of discussion about limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, it doesn't address how the model would perform on datasets with different argumentation structures or on datasets with more complex argumentation trees.

4. **"First" claim potentially undermined by Potash et al. (2017)**: The paper claims to be the first neural end-to-end solution for AM, but the references mention "Argumentation Mining with Pointer Networks" (Potash et al., 2017) which was submitted during the preparation of this work. This could undermine the novelty claim.

5. **Methodological details missing**: The paper relegates important methodological details (pre-trained word embeddings, hyperparameter optimization) to supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the methodology.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural end-to-end solution for AM, but Potash et al. (2017) was submitted during the preparation of this work. How does this affect the novelty claim, and how does your approach differ from their pointer network approach?

2. The paper evaluates only on the PE dataset. How would your approaches perform on other AM datasets, such as Peldszus and Stede's microtexts corpus?

3. You demonstrate that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, while component-level parsing has been shown to work. Could you provide more insight into why this distinction exists, particularly regarding the differences in the scale of the problem (token-level vs. component-level)?

4. The paper mentions that LSTM-ER degrades significantly on essay level for relation identification. Could you provide more detail on how the number of entities affects relation identification in this context, and why this degradation is more severe than for the sequence tagging approach?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining by introducing the first neural end-to-end learning approach. The authors successfully demonstrate that a simple sequence tagging approach with distance encoding outperforms more complex neural architectures and the state-of-the-art ILP model. Their key insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM is particularly valuable and will guide future research in the field.

While the paper has some methodological limitations (single dataset, limited analysis), the novelty and boldness of the approach outweigh these concerns for me as a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection. The paper opens up a new direction for computational argumentation mining that has significant potential for future work.

The contributions are substantial, and the paper makes a clear and compelling case for why neural end-to-end learning is a promising direction for computational argumentation mining. I believe this paper would be a valuable contribution to the ACL community.
