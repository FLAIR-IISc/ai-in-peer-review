

# Peer Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary
This paper proposes neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the full AM task (component segmentation, typing, and relation identification) without relying on hand-crafted features or pipeline architectures. The authors frame AM as four different neural problems: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with specialized labels encoding distance and relations, multi-task learning, and adaptation of LSTM-ER. They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), comparing against the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **First neural end-to-end solutions for computational AM** that eliminate the need for manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.
2. **Demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model** (STagBLCC achieves 55.22% global F1 on essay level, matching the ILP model's performance while being significantly simpler to implement).
3. **Identification of the most effective framing for AM** (sequence tagging with distance encoding) and explanation of why token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for this task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution**: The paper successfully demonstrates that neural end-to-end approaches can outperform previous pipeline-based methods for AM, which is a major advancement in the field. The authors correctly identify that the main limitation of prior work is the reliance on hand-crafted features, which is particularly problematic for AM due to the "arbitrary" nature of argumentation theory.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of four distinct neural framings, with careful analysis of their relative strengths and weaknesses. The authors' insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (unlike component-level approaches) is particularly valuable and well-supported by their empirical results.

3. **Practical implications**: The results show that a standard neural sequence tagging model with distance encoding performs robustly across different environments, which is a significant practical contribution for practitioners working on argumentation mining.

4. **Clear theoretical explanation for failure modes**: The authors provide a compelling explanation for why dependency parsing fails at the token level (the search space for trees becomes prohibitively large for long sequences), which is more insightful than simply reporting poor performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent neural AM systems (e.g., graph neural networks or transformer-based architectures) that have emerged since the PE dataset was published. This makes it difficult to assess the true state-of-the-art, as the paper claims to achieve "new state-of-the-art results" but doesn't compare to work published after 2016.

2. **Lack of detailed error analysis**: While the paper provides strong quantitative results, it doesn't analyze *why* the models fail on specific examples. For instance, the authors note that 92% of relationships occur between components in different sentences, but they don't analyze whether their models struggle with these long-range dependencies or how they could be better addressed.

3. **Insufficient justification for the tagging scheme**: The authors introduce a complex tagging scheme (Y = {(b, t, d, s)}) that encodes distance and relation information in the tag. However, they don't provide ablation studies to show how much performance improvement comes from this specific encoding versus a simpler approach. The claim that "neural sequence tagging models are ideal candidates for such a framing because they can take into account long-range dependencies" needs stronger evidence.

4. **Inadequate discussion of paragraph-level vs. essay-level performance**: The paper shows a significant drop in performance when moving from paragraph-level to essay-level prediction, but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of *why* this happens. The authors attribute it to "very long sequences" but don't investigate whether this is a fundamental limitation of their approach or whether it could be mitigated with better model architecture or training techniques.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your STagBLCC approach compare to more recent neural AM systems (e.g., those using graph neural networks) that have been published since 2016? Have you considered adapting your method to these newer architectures?

2. The paper states that "92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences." How does this affect the design of your neural models, and have you considered incorporating sentence-level information to better capture these relationships?

3. Could you provide more detailed error analysis, particularly regarding the models' performance on long-range dependencies? For example, how do the models perform on relations with large distance values (d > 10)?

4. The paper claims that "a standard neural sequence tagging model that encodes distance information between components performs robustly in different environments." What evidence do you have that this specific tagging scheme (rather than just a standard sequence tagger) is responsible for the performance gains?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The paper successfully demonstrates that neural end-to-end approaches can outperform previous pipeline-based methods, which is a significant step forward. While the work has some limitations (particularly regarding comparison to more recent neural approaches and lack of error analysis), the core contributions are substantial and well-supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above. The paper provides clear evidence that neural methods are viable for end-to-end AM and identifies the most effective framing for this task, which will guide future research in the field.
