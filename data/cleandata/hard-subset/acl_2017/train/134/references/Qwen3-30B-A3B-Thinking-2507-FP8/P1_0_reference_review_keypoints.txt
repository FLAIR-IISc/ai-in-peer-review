- Paper presents the first comprehensive exploration of neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM).
- Proposes and compares four neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER adaptation.
- Key empirical claim: sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagBLCC) outperforms prior ILP-based state-of-the-art by substantial margins (e.g., +13% C-F1, +11% R-F1).
- Provides useful insights on when constrained models (e.g., sequence tagging) are preferable to general models (e.g., LSTM-ER) for longer texts with many components.
- Strength: first systematic neural empirical comparison for end-to-end AM, moving beyond pipeline and feature-based approaches.
- Strength: strong empirical results demonstrating a practical neural alternative that avoids hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.
- Strength: theoretical and empirical discussion about modeling constraints and combinatorial relation explosion in general models.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single dataset (PE), so claims about robustness and generalizability are under-supported.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of why dependency parsing framing fails, especially at essay level; sequence-length hypothesis is plausible but not demonstrated.
- Weakness: lack of ablation studies to quantify contributions of key components (e.g., the effect of distance encoding).
- Weakness: novelty claim of being the “first neural end-to-end” solution is overstated and should acknowledge related work (e.g., Potash et al., 2017).
- Questions for authors: provide results on other AM datasets (e.g., microtext) to support robustness claims.
- Questions for authors: analyze why STagT avoids LSTM-ER’s essay-level degradation and detail LSTM-ER’s failure modes.
- Questions for authors: elaborate specific failure patterns of dependency parsing on long texts and potential remedies.
- Conclusion: valuable and timely contribution with strong results and modeling insights, but needs broader evaluation and ablation studies to solidify claims.
