

# Review of "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), claiming to be the first to do so. The authors present four framing approaches: (1) dependency parsing, (2) sequence tagging with a complex label scheme encoding component type, BIO tags, distance, and relation type, (3) multi-task learning, and (4) adaptation of LSTM-ER for AM. They claim their neural approaches outperform the existing state-of-the-art ILP-based pipeline approach (Stab and Gurevych 2016) in most cases, particularly for component detection.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Empirical validation of neural approaches on the standard AM dataset, showing some neural models match or exceed the performance of the existing ILP-based pipeline approach.
2. Insight that dependency parsing is ineffective for token-level AM (contrary to prior assumptions), which corrects a potentially misleading assumption.
3. Demonstration that sequence tagging with a carefully designed label scheme can work well for AM, providing a practical alternative to pipeline approaches.

## Strengths
1. **Clear empirical comparison with the state-of-the-art baseline:** The paper provides a thorough comparison against the Stab and Gurevych (2016) ILP model, which is the current standard for end-to-end AM. The results are presented with appropriate metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at 100% and 50% levels), and the comparison is conducted on the standard PE dataset. This is valuable for the community as it establishes a concrete benchmark.

2. **Insight about dependency parsing being ineffective for token-level AM:** The authors correctly identify that dependency parsing approaches work poorly at the token level (as opposed to component level), which contradicts some prior assumptions. This is a useful contribution that corrects a potentially misleading direction in the literature.

3. **Demonstration of sequence tagging as a viable approach:** The paper shows that a standard neural sequence tagging model (STagBLCC) with a well-designed label scheme can perform robustly across different environments, which is a practical contribution for others working on AM.

## Weaknesses
1. **Questionable novelty claim:** The authors claim this is "the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM," but they adapt an existing model (Miwa and Bansal 2016) for this task. They don't clearly establish why their adaptation is novel or how it differs significantly from prior work. The related work section doesn't adequately justify this novelty claim, making it difficult to assess the paper's contribution.

2. **Flawed evaluation of dependency parsing approach:** The authors claim dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, but they only evaluate standard dependency parsers not designed for AM. They don't attempt to train a dependency parser specifically for AM, which would be necessary to properly evaluate this claim. Their evaluation is based on parsers designed for syntactic dependency parsing, not argumentation structures.

3. **Unjustified complexity of the sequence tagging label scheme:** The sequence tagging approach uses a complex four-tuple label (b, t, d, s) that encodes component type, BIO tagging, distance between components, and relation type. The paper lacks ablation studies to demonstrate why this complexity is necessary. Without showing that removing any part of this label scheme significantly degrades performance, it's unclear whether the distance encoding (d) is actually beneficial.

4. **Unfair comparison with ILP baseline:** The ILP model has access to paragraph information (since it knows which paragraph it's in), which is a significant advantage for major claims that always occur in first or last paragraphs. The neural models are evaluated only on essay level, which puts them at a disadvantage. The authors don't adequately address this in their comparison, making the "superiority" claim questionable.

5. **Modest improvements from multi-task learning:** The multi-task learning approach only provides modest improvements (a few percentage points), which don't justify the additional complexity of the multi-task framework. The paper claims this is a significant contribution, but the improvements are marginal compared to the complexity added.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you justify your claim of being the "first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM" when you adapt Miwa and Bansal (2016), which is cited in your paper as a model you're adapting? What specific contribution does your work make beyond this adaptation?

2. Have you tried training a dependency parser specifically designed for AM, rather than using standard dependency parsers not designed for AM? If not, how can you confidently claim that dependency parsing is ineffective for AM?

3. Can you provide ablation studies showing that the distance encoding (d) in your label scheme is necessary for good performance? What happens if you remove d from the label scheme?

4. The ILP model has access to paragraph information that neural models don't, which is a significant advantage. How did you account for this in your comparison, and is it fair to compare neural models trained on essay level to an ILP model that has paragraph-level information?

5. The multi-task learning approach only provides modest improvements (a few percentage points). Why is this improvement significant enough to justify the additional complexity of the multi-task framework, especially when the improvements are inconsistent across different tasks (Table 2)?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper has some useful empirical findings, particularly regarding the limitations of dependency parsing for token-level AM and the viability of sequence tagging approaches. However, the novelty claim is questionable, the evaluation of the dependency parsing approach is flawed, and the improvements from multi-task learning are modest. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous literature review, ablation studies to justify the complexity of their label scheme, and a fairer comparison with the ILP baseline. As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods and rigorous validation, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and would recommend rejection unless these issues are thoroughly addressed in a revision.
