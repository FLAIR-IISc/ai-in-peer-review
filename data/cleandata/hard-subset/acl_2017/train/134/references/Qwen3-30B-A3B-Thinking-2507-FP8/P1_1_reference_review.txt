

# Peer Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), which involves identifying argumentative structures in text (component segmentation, component typing, relation identification, and relation typing). The authors propose and evaluate four neural network framings of the AM problem: dependency parsing (token-level), sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and the LSTM-ER model. Their key findings include: (1) neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model; (2) token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to component-level approaches); (3) sequence tagging with distance encoding provides a simple yet effective solution; and (4) multi-task learning with component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks improves performance.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1. The first comprehensive empirical study demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can outperform traditional feature-based ILP models for computational argumentation mining.
2. The demonstration that token-level dependency parsing (contrary to prior assumptions) is ineffective for AM, correcting a misconception in the field.
3. The elegant and effective framing of AM as a sequence tagging problem with distance encoding, which provides a simple yet powerful solution.

## Strengths

1. **Significant advancement over state-of-the-art**: The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solution for computational argumentation mining, demonstrating that their approaches outperform the previously best-performing ILP model. This is a substantial contribution to the field, as it shows that neural methods can effectively solve the end-to-end AM problem without the need for hand-crafted features or complex ILP constraints.

2. **Clear empirical comparison and insights**: The authors provide a thorough comparison of different neural framings, with careful analysis of why certain approaches work better than others. Their finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (contrary to component-level approaches) is particularly valuable and corrects a potential misconception in the field. This insight is crucial for future work in AM.

3. **Simple yet effective solution**: The sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is elegant in its simplicity—by encoding relation distances directly in the tag labels, they avoid the need for complex dependency parsing or relation modeling. This approach demonstrates strong performance across both paragraph and essay levels, making it a practical solution for AM.

4. **Careful analysis of multi-task learning**: The paper provides valuable insights into how auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation detection) can improve performance on the main AM task. This finding has broader implications beyond AM and could inform multi-task learning approaches in other sequence labeling tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited dataset scope**: The paper only evaluates on a single dataset (the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which limits the generalizability of their findings. As the authors themselves note, "AM is to some degree an 'arbitrary' problem" where datasets differ significantly in annotation. Without evaluation on multiple datasets, it's unclear how well their approach generalizes to different argumentation theories and annotation schemes.

2. **Insufficient explanation of token-level dependency parsing failure**: The paper claims that token-level dependency parsing is "ineffective" but doesn't provide detailed analysis to support this claim. While they mention that the "global view" of dependency parsers may be too complex for long sequences, they don't provide specific examples or quantitative analysis of why this is the case. This weakens their key insight.

3. **Unfair comparison with ILP model**: The paper states that the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information," but this is misleading. The ILP model would need to be run on paragraph-level to match the neural models' evaluation, yet the paper doesn't clarify how this is implemented. The neural models are evaluated on both levels, while the ILP model is only reported at paragraph level (with essay-level performance being lower), making the comparison less meaningful.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides strong overall metrics but lacks detailed error analysis. For example, they note that LSTM-ER performs well on components but poorly on relations on essay level, but don't provide examples or analysis to explain why this happens. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen their claims about the effectiveness of different approaches.

5. **Questionable claim about error propagation**: The authors claim that neural approaches "do not require the hand-crafting of features or constraints" and avoid error propagation, but their neural models are still essentially pipeline-like in that they predict components and relations in a single pass. They should clarify how their approach avoids error propagation compared to traditional pipeline approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details on how your approach would generalize to other AM datasets, given that the authors note AM is an "arbitrary" problem with varying annotation schemes?

2. The paper claims token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM, but doesn't provide specific examples from the data to illustrate this. Could you share examples where the dependency parser fails, and explain why it fails in those cases?

3. The paper states that the ILP model "has access to both paragraph and essay level information," but it's unclear how this is implemented. Could you clarify how the ILP model is applied at paragraph level to match the neural models' evaluation?

4. The authors note that LSTM-ER's relation performance degrades on essay level due to the larger number of possible relations. Could you provide more details on the distribution of relation counts across documents (e.g., average number of relations per document at paragraph vs. essay level)?

5. The paper mentions that the sequence tagging approach is "robust in different environments," but this claim isn't supported beyond the single PE dataset. Could you provide additional examples or analysis of how the approach performs on different types of argument structures?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to computational argumentation mining by demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can outperform traditional feature-based methods. The key insight about token-level dependency parsing being ineffective is particularly valuable. However, the limited dataset scope and insufficient explanation of why token-level dependency parsing fails weaken the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these issues.
