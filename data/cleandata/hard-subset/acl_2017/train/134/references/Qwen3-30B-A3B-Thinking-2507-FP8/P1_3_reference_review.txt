

# Peer Review: "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining (AM), addressing the full AM task (component segmentation, typing, relation identification, and typing) from unannotated text. The authors propose four neural network framings: (1) dependency parsing at the token level, (2) sequence tagging with distance encoding, (3) multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks, and (4) evaluation of the LSTM-ER model. The main contributions are:

1. The paper demonstrates that neural end-to-end approaches outperform the state-of-the-art feature-based ILP model without requiring hand-crafted features or constraints.
2. The authors show that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to previous component-level approaches), while sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly across different environments.
3. They demonstrate that multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks significantly improves performance.

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution**: The paper presents the first neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining, addressing a key limitation in the field. Previous approaches relied on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, which require significant manual effort and are difficult to adapt to new datasets. The neural approaches eliminate this need for manual feature engineering, representing a substantial methodological advancement.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough comparison of multiple neural approaches against the state-of-the-art ILP model. Their careful analysis of paragraph-level versus essay-level performance is particularly valuable, as it reveals important insights about the scalability of different approaches. The results clearly demonstrate that neural approaches outperform the ILP model, with the sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) achieving the best results.

3. **Important theoretical insights**: The paper provides valuable insights about the nature of argumentation structures in text. The finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective (while component-level approaches work) challenges previous assumptions and provides a better understanding of what modeling approach is suitable for AM. This insight is likely to guide future work in the field.

4. **Practical implications**: The paper demonstrates that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) improves performance, which has broader implications for structured prediction tasks in NLP. This finding could be valuable for researchers working on other sequence labeling and relation extraction problems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient analysis of token-level dependency parsing failure**: The paper states that token-level dependency parsing performs poorly but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *why* this is the case. Given that argument structures are nearly tree-like, it's surprising that dependency parsing doesn't work well at the token level. A deeper exploration of this issue would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Omission of relevant recent work**: The authors mention Potash et al. (2017) as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" but don't include it in their evaluation. Given that this work also addresses end-to-end AM using neural methods, its omission is significant and weakens the paper's claim of being the first neural end-to-end solution. The authors should have included this comparison.

3. **Lack of discussion on label space limitations**: The sequence tagging approach uses a label space that encodes distance values between components, which is potentially infinite. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address how this would impact practical applications where components might be very far apart. A discussion of how to handle out-of-vocabulary distances would be valuable.

4. **Evaluation metric concerns**: The paper uses C-F1 and R-F1 scores with different matching levels (100% and 50%), but doesn't provide sufficient justification for why these specific matching levels are appropriate for AM. This makes it difficult to compare their results with other AM systems that might use different evaluation metrics.

5. **Insufficient detail on model parameters**: While the authors mention that key details are in supplementary material, important hyperparameters (like vocabulary size, embedding dimensions, batch size) should be included in the main paper for reproducibility and to allow for proper assessment of the model's complexity.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why does token-level dependency parsing perform worse than sequence tagging for AM? The paper states this is the case, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the underlying reasons. Could this be related to the token-level noise or the specific structure of argumentation data?

2. How do you plan to handle the potentially infinite label space in your sequence tagging approach with distance encoding? Specifically, how would your model handle cases where the distance between components exceeds the range observed in your training data?

3. The paper mentions Potash et al. (2017) as "research submitted during the preparation of our current work" but doesn't include it in the evaluation. Why wasn't this model included in the comparison? How would your results change if you had included it?

4. The paper states that LSTM-ER degrades on essay level due to the increased number of possible relations. Could you provide more details about how the number of possible relations grows with text length, and whether there are practical ways to mitigate this issue?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a significant contribution to computational argumentation mining by introducing the first neural end-to-end approaches for the task. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over the previous state-of-the-art. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by including Potash et al. (2017) in the evaluation and providing a more thorough analysis of why token-level dependency parsing fails for AM. With these improvements, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
