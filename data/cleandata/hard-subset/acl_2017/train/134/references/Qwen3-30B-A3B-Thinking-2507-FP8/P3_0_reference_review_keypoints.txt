- Paper proposes four neural end-to-end AM framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) and claims they outperform ILP, with token-level parsing failing and sequence tagging + MTL performing well.

- Strength: Demonstrates large empirical gains (10–13% F1) over prior ILP-based state-of-the-art.

- Strength: Provides insightful analysis explaining performance differences (e.g., distance encoding captures short-range relations; parsing suffers from combinatorial explosion on long essays).

- Strength: Offers practical guidance for model choice by document length (LSTM-ER for short, restrained models for long documents).

- Weakness: Novelty overstated—prior neural end-to-end work (Miwa & Bansal 2016) is not sufficiently distinguished from the paper’s contributions.

- Weakness: Insufficient exploration of why token-level dependency parsing fails—no experiments on shorter documents or alternative architectures (e.g., attention).

- Weakness: Distance encoding uses component-count distances that vary across documents; paper lacks analysis of token/sentence-based alternatives and their effects across lengths.

- Weakness: No comparative computational cost or runtime analysis across methods, despite noting memory issues for parsing.

- Question: How would component-count distance encoding compare to token-based distance measures across varied document lengths?

- Question: How exactly does this work differ from and improve upon Miwa & Bansal (2016) and the LSTM-ER framework?

- Question: Can you quantify how the number of possible relations scales with document length and how that scaling degrades LSTM-ER performance?

- Overall: Strong empirical contribution showing neural end-to-end AM outperforms ILP with useful analysis, but novelty and distance-encoding claims need clearer justification and computational-cost details before acceptance.
