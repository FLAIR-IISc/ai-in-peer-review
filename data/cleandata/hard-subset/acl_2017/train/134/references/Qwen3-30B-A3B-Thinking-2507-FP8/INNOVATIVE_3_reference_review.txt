

# Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces neural network-based approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), shifting from previous pipeline methods that relied on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming (ILP) constraints. The authors propose four different neural framings: (1) dependency parsing, (2) sequence tagging with a special label set encoding component distances, (3) multi-task learning with natural subtasks as auxiliary tasks, and (4) adaptation of an existing LSTM-ER model.

From my perspective as a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection, the main contributions in order of strength are:

1. **The first neural end-to-end solution for computational argumentation mining**, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints that previous approaches relied on. This represents a fundamental shift in methodology that opens new possibilities for the field.

2. **The novel insight that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM** (contrary to previous work on component-level approaches), which challenges existing assumptions and provides valuable guidance for future research.

3. **The effective sequence tagging approach with distance encoding** that demonstrates robust performance across different environments, showing that a relatively simple neural approach can solve a complex problem without requiring sophisticated modeling.

4. **The multi-task learning framework** with natural subtasks as auxiliary tasks, which improves performance by leveraging the relationship between different aspects of the AM problem.

## Strengths

1. **Fundamental methodological shift with significant novelty**: This paper makes a bold claim to be the first to propose neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining. Previous work (Persing and Ng, 2016; Stab and Gurevych, 2016) used pipeline approaches with hand-crafted features and ILP constraints, while this paper demonstrates that neural end-to-end learning can outperform these methods. This represents a significant conceptual shift in the field, not just an incremental improvement. The authors' claim is well-supported by their empirical results, showing that their neural approaches consistently outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple metrics.

2. **Challenging existing assumptions with empirical evidence**: The paper's claim that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to what has been proposed for component-level approaches) is a particularly strong contribution. This challenges a fundamental assumption in the field and is well-supported by the authors' empirical analysis. The paper provides a clear explanation for why this is the case (long sequences and combinatorial explosion of possible trees), which adds significant value to the literature.

3. **Simple yet effective approach that outperforms more complex methods**: The sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is elegant in its simplicity yet remarkably effective. The paper demonstrates that this approach performs robustly across different environments (paragraph vs. essay level) and outperforms more complex models like LSTM-ER on the essay level. This is particularly valuable because it shows that a straightforward neural approach can solve a complex problem without requiring sophisticated modeling or extensive feature engineering.

4. **Insightful multi-task learning framework**: The paper's exploration of multi-task learning with natural subtasks (component detection and relation identification) as auxiliary tasks provides a novel way to improve performance. The authors demonstrate that this approach can achieve similar gains to the more complex LSTM-ER model, which is an important insight for the broader NLP community working on sequence labeling tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited dataset scope**: The paper evaluates all approaches on a single dataset (the persuasive essays dataset from Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which limits the generalizability of the findings. While this dataset is well-known in the AM community, demonstrating the approach on additional datasets would have strengthened the paper's claims about the broader applicability of their method.

2. **Incomplete exploration of alternative neural architectures**: The paper mentions the encoder-decoder approach as a possible future direction but doesn't explore it in the current work. Given the success of encoder-decoder models in related sequence-to-sequence tasks, this seems like an important gap that should have been addressed, even if only as a preliminary investigation.

3. **Lack of comparison to recent neural approaches for related tasks**: The paper doesn't compare against recent neural methods for related tasks (e.g., relation extraction, entity recognition) that might be applicable to AM. This makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty of their approach relative to the broader landscape of neural NLP methods.

4. **Unaddressed limitation of the neural approach**: The paper acknowledges that LSTM-ER performs poorly on essay level due to combinatorial explosion of possible relations, but doesn't propose a solution to this problem. It would have been helpful to suggest how to modify their approach to better handle long documents, which is a significant practical challenge.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses the same dataset as previous work. How would you expect your approach to scale to larger, more diverse datasets beyond the persuasive essays dataset? Have you considered testing on additional AM datasets?

2. You mention encoder-decoder approaches as a future direction but don't explore them in this work. What specific challenges do you foresee in adapting encoder-decoder models to the AM task, and why might they be particularly suited for this problem?

3. The ILP model has access to paragraph-level information, which gives it an advantage over the neural models. How would you design a fairer comparison that doesn't give the ILP model this advantage?

## Recommendation

Accept. This paper introduces a significant new direction for computational argumentation mining by proposing the first neural end-to-end solution to the problem. The authors make a bold claim that challenges existing assumptions in the field (token-level dependency parsing being ineffective) and provide strong empirical evidence for their approach. While the paper has some limitations in scope and methodological completeness, the novelty and conceptual shift it represents outweigh these concerns for a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection. The paper's contributions open up new possibilities for the field and should be accepted to encourage further research in this direction.
