- Proposes neural end-to-end solutions for computational argumentation mining, framing AM as dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER variants.  
- Claims three main contributions: first neural end-to-end framework for AM, neural methods outperform ILP state-of-the-art, and token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM.  

- Novel and important problem focus: addresses limitations of prior pipeline/feature-based AM approaches and targets a timely, impactful task.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation: compares multiple neural approaches against ILP on paragraph and essay levels with detailed error analysis.  
- Insightful multi-task learning finding: auxiliary component detection improves performance more than relation identification, guiding future AM work.  
- Clear demonstration of neural advantages: eliminates manual feature engineering and ILP constraints; STagBLCC achieves state-of-the-art results simply and robustly.  

- Lacks comparison with recent neural work (Potash et al., 2017), weakening the novelty claim and empirical context.  
- Insufficient justification of evaluation metrics (C-F1/R-F1 at 100% and 50% thresholds); thresholds are not motivated relative to task needs.  
- Limited analysis of failure cases; more systematic error analysis of neural model failures is needed.  
- Overstates dependency parsing conclusions without deeper analysis of why token-level parsing fails beyond search-space concerns.  

- How does this work differ from or improve upon Potash et al. (2017), and why was that prior work not properly compared/cited?  
- Given 92% of relations cross sentences, how does this affect model choice and why does it make sentence-level dependency parsing unsuitable?  
- What specific architectural changes or constraints would you recommend for handling longer documents while preserving neural approach benefits?  

- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparison to Potash et al., justify evaluation metrics, and deepen analysis of failures and dependency parsing limits.
