

# Peer Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary
This paper investigates neural approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), challenging the dominant pipeline ILP-based approaches. The authors propose four neural frameworks: dependency parsing (token-level), sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and adaptation of LSTM-ER. They evaluate these on the PE dataset from Stab and Gurevych (2016), finding neural methods outperform the ILP baseline, with sequence tagging proving particularly effective.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. First comprehensive evaluation of neural end-to-end approaches for AM, demonstrating clear performance gains over state-of-the-art feature-based ILP methods.
2. Insightful empirical analysis showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to component-level approaches), while sequence tagging with distance encoding provides robust performance.
3. Demonstration that multi-task learning with component and relation tasks as auxiliary tasks significantly improves performance, with component detection proving particularly beneficial.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Performance Gains**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model across multiple metrics (C-F1 and R-F1), with STagBLCC achieving 66.69% C-F1 (50%) and 39.83% R-F1 (50%) on paragraph level - a 5-10% absolute improvement. This is a substantial contribution to the field.

2. **Critical Analysis of Modeling Choices**: The authors provide deep insights into why certain approaches work better than others. Their explanation for the failure of dependency parsing at essay level (due to "too huge a search space" when n is large) and the advantage of sequence tagging's local nature for long sequences is particularly compelling and well-supported by empirical evidence.

3. **Practical Multi-Task Insights**: The finding that including component detection as an auxiliary task significantly improves performance (e.g., Y-3:YC-3:YR-3 vs Y-3) is valuable for the field. Their observation that component detection is more helpful than relation detection as an auxiliary task challenges assumptions about task relationships and provides concrete guidance for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on one dataset (PE from Stab and Gurevych, 2016). This is a significant limitation given the well-known variability in AM annotation schemes across different corpora (e.g., Peldszus and Stede, 2016; Persing and Ng, 2016). Without cross-dataset validation, it's unclear how generalizable these findings are to other argumentation structures or annotation conventions.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: While they compare different framing approaches, the paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the impact of key design choices. For instance, it's unclear how much the distance encoding specifically contributes versus the neural architecture itself. The paper would benefit from experiments comparing STagBLCC with and without the distance encoding component.

3. **Overlooking Alternative Neural Architectures**: The authors briefly mention encoder-decoder approaches as "scope for future work" but don't evaluate them. Recent work (e.g., Potash et al., 2017) has shown promise with pointer networks for AM. Not exploring this potentially more effective architecture weakens the claim that they've presented the "first neural end-to-end solutions."

4. **Questionable Interpretation of LSTM-ER Performance**: The paper attributes LSTM-ER's poor performance on relations in long documents to its "generality" and "O(m²) possible relations," but doesn't provide statistical evidence that this is the primary cause. The authors should more rigorously analyze whether the issue is truly architectural or simply a data scarcity problem.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to address the limitation of only evaluating on one dataset? Would you consider testing on Peldszus and Stede's (2016) microtext corpus to demonstrate broader applicability?

2. Could you provide ablation results showing the specific contribution of the distance encoding component in your sequence tagging framework, rather than just comparing the full model against others?

3. Why does the paper conclude that token-level dependency parsing is "ineffective" when the LSTM-Parser achieves the best results among dependency parsers? The paper's own results show that dependency parsing *can* work well with the right neural architecture.

4. The paper claims LSTM-ER's "de-coupling of component and relation tasks" explains its strong component performance. However, LSTM-ER uses BLC for entity detection, which is similar to their STagBLCC approach. How does this de-coupling specifically manifest in the architecture to produce better results?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can significantly outperform traditional ILP-based methods for argumentation mining. The insights about sequence tagging's robustness and the benefits of multi-task learning with component detection as an auxiliary task are particularly noteworthy. However, the limited dataset scope and lack of ablation studies prevent this work from being a definitive study. With additional evaluation across multiple datasets and more rigorous analysis of key design choices, this could be a highly impactful contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a strong paper that makes a solid case for neural end-to-end AM but leaves some important questions unanswered.
