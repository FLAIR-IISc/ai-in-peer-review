

# Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), moving beyond previous pipeline methods that relied on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming. The authors propose four neural frameworks: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and adaptation of the LSTM-ER model. Their main contributions include (1) the first neural end-to-end AM solutions, (2) demonstration that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) analysis showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM (contrary to component-level approaches), and (4) evidence that sequence tagging with distance encoding robustly performs across different environments.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical contribution to AM**: The paper provides the first comprehensive neural comparison of multiple approaches to end-to-end AM, demonstrating that neural methods outperform the current state-of-the-art ILP model by significant margins (up to 13% on C-F1 and 11% on R-F1). This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as previous work has relied on feature engineering and pipeline approaches that suffer from error propagation.

2. **Insightful analysis of different modeling approaches**: The authors provide valuable insights about why certain neural approaches work better than others. Their analysis of the token-level dependency parsing failure (Section 5a) and the effectiveness of distance encoding in sequence tagging (Section 4) is particularly compelling. The finding that neural sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms dependency parsing at the token level (which the authors correctly identify as a key difference from prior component-level work) is a significant contribution to the literature.

3. **Practical implications for AM practitioners**: The paper offers clear recommendations based on document length: "When there are few entities in a text ('short documents'), a more general framework such as that provided in LSTM-ER performs reasonably well. When there are many entities ('long documents'), a more restrained modeling is preferable." These practical guidelines will be valuable for researchers and practitioners working on argumentation mining.

4. **Strong evaluation methodology**: The paper uses the standard evaluation metric from Persing and Ng (2016) and carefully compares performance at both paragraph and essay levels. The analysis of how document length affects different approaches (e.g., LSTM-ER's degradation on essay level) provides important insights about the scalability of different models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty claim**: The authors claim this is the "first neural end-to-end solutions" for AM, but they acknowledge in the conclusion that "another possible framing... was investigated in research submitted during the preparation of our current work" (Potash et al., 2017). Given that Potash et al. was submitted during the preparation of this paper, the novelty claim is significantly weakened. The paper should acknowledge this work more thoroughly and clarify how their contributions differ.

2. **Limited dataset scope**: The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (PE from Stab and Gurevych, 2016), which is a significant limitation. While the PE dataset is the largest available, the paper would be stronger with results on additional AM datasets to demonstrate broader applicability. The authors should address whether their findings generalize to other argumentation structures or domains.

3. **Superficial explanation of distance encoding effectiveness**: While the paper claims sequence tagging with distance encoding works well, the explanation provided (Section 4) is somewhat superficial. The paper notes that "related components are typically close in terms of the number of components between them" but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why this encoding outperforms other approaches. A deeper analysis of how the distance encoding interacts with the LSTM's ability to capture long-range dependencies would strengthen the paper.

4. **Overstated multi-task learning benefits**: The paper claims multi-task learning with component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks improves performance, but the improvements are marginal (typically a few percentage points). The paper should more carefully quantify the practical significance of these improvements and address whether they are statistically significant at a meaningful level.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you respond to the claim that your "first neural end-to-end" contribution is weakened by the Potash et al. (2017) work, which was submitted during your preparation? Would you consider revising your novelty claims?

2. The paper states that "the distribution of distance values found in real essays" (Figure 2) suggests related components are typically close. Could you provide more detailed analysis of how the distance encoding specifically contributes to the model's performance, beyond just noting the distribution?

3. The paper notes that "92% of all relationships are between components that appear in different sentences." How does this affect the design of your sequence tagging model, given that traditional sequence models are typically designed for intra-sentence tasks?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining by demonstrating that neural end-to-end approaches can significantly outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model. The paper provides valuable insights about different neural modeling approaches for AM and offers practical recommendations for practitioners. While the novelty claim is weakened by the Potash et al. work and the limited dataset scope are notable weaknesses, the paper's empirical results and analysis are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the novelty claim and dataset limitations.

**Recommendation: Accept**
