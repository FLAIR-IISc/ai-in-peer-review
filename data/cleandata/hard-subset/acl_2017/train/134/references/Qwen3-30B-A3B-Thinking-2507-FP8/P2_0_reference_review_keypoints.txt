- Introduces four neural end-to-end framings for argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER.  
- Claims neural end-to-end solutions remove need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.  
- Demonstrates sequence tagging with distance encoding outperforms prior ILP-based state of the art on component and relation identification.  
- Finds dependency parsing ineffective for end-to-end AM, while sequence tagging is robust across settings.  
- Practical contribution: eliminates manual feature/ILP adaptation, with STagBLCC improving component F1 by 13% and relation F1 by 11% at paragraph level.  
- Thorough evaluation across models and text lengths, with insightful analysis of model behaviors (e.g., combinatorial explosion on longer texts).  
- Methodological innovation: distance-encoding sequence tagging (STagT) effectively models long-range dependencies and is robust.  
- Limited dataset scope: evaluation only on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Lacks comparisons to other neural AM or related neural relation-extraction approaches (e.g., Potash et al. 2017).  
- Overstates “first” neural end-to-end claim given concurrent work; needs clarification.  
- Question: clarify the paper’s “first” claim relative to Potash et al. (2017).  
- Question: how would the distance-encoding sequence tagging perform when argumentative units span multiple paragraphs or whole documents?  
- Question: is LSTM-ER’s poor essay-level relation performance due to long-sequence limitations or increased relation complexity?  
- Overall: strong, practical contribution with thorough analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address dataset generalizability and claim clarity.
