Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) from raw text with neural methods, replacing feature-engineered pipelines and ILP post-hoc constraints. The authors explore four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) a novel sequence tagging formulation that jointly encodes component BIO/type, relation distance, and stance in a single label, (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with component and relation subtasks, and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) that decouples entity and relation prediction. Experiments on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus show several neural models outperform a strong ILP baseline; token-level dependency parsing underperforms; distance-augmented sequence tagging is robust; and MTL helps. They also present insightful analyses contrasting paragraph vs. essay level and the role of structural constraints.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A bold and novel end-to-end framing of AM as sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels that jointly encode components and relations, eliminating hand-crafted features and constraints and yielding SOTA performance on PE.
2) A thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER), with actionable insights about when more general vs. more constrained models win (short vs. long documents).
3) Evidence that decoupling components and relations but modeling them jointly (via MTL or LSTM-ER) improves component detection and overall F1.

Strengths
- Novel end-to-end neural formulation for AM with distance-coded labels: Encoding relation distance and stance directly in token-level tags is an original idea for AM. It is a principled way to exploit distance priors and constrain search without ILP, and it scales to long-range dependencies that feature-based CRFs struggle with. The approach is simple, broadly applicable, and removes costly feature/constraint engineering.

- Strong empirical study and state-of-the-art results: Multiple competitive neural baselines are implemented and compared (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging, MTL variants, LSTM-ER, several dependency parsers), with significance testing. On PE, the neural models outperform the feature-based ILP baseline, particularly on components, while providing a cleaner end-to-end pipeline.

- Insightful analysis of modeling choices and data regimes: The paper convincingly shows why token-level dependency parsing is ill-suited for end-to-end AM (very long inputs, global tree search explosion), while local taggers are robust to sequence length. The contrast between paragraph and essay settings, and the analysis of relation distance distributions, help explain performance differences and offer practical guidance (use more constrained models as the entity count grows).

- Positive, interpretable gains from multi-task learning: Using component and relation subtasks improves the full tagging objective. The finding that the “lower task at lower layer” heuristic is not necessarily optimal in this setting is useful for practitioners.

- Clarity and reproducibility: The problem formulations are clearly explained with concrete examples. Statistical testing and references to code/hyperparameters (supplement) increase trust in the results.

Weaknesses
- Single-domain dataset with strong structural peculiarities: All results are on the PE corpus with paragraph-bound structures and tree-like relations (e.g., premises must link; claims link to all major claims). The approach makes design choices tailored to these constraints (e.g., one outgoing link per component, distance priors). It is unclear how well the sequence tagging formulation generalizes to AM datasets with non-tree graphs, cross-paragraph links, or many-to-many relations (e.g., microtexts, web discourse).

- Label space explosion and assumptions in distance tagging: While the authors argue distance values are bounded in practice, the label set is large and data for rare distances is sparse. There is no ablation on distance capping, smoothing, or how performance degrades as |d| grows or the distribution shifts. The “exactly one relation per component” constraint is baked in and cannot represent legitimate many-to-many structures.

- Dependency parsing framing feels like a strawman at token level: Choices such as attaching all non-argument tokens to END and labeling arcs with BIO/type may deform the parsing problem. A component-level parser (with predicted spans) or a span-based structured model might be a fairer test of the “AM as parsing” hypothesis, given prior discourse/AM parsers operate over components.

- Comparability issues and limited ablations: The ILP baseline has access to both essay and paragraph context; neural models are sometimes limited by memory to paragraph-level training/testing or show unstable essay-level results. It’s not fully clear if inputs are matched across systems. For LSTM-ER, the TreeLSTM uses sentence-level syntax, which the authors note is largely irrelevant; a variant without syntactic trees or with cross-sentence modeling would be a stronger comparison. More ablations (e.g., removing stance, removing distance, varying constraints) would help isolate what drives gains.

- Limited qualitative error analysis: Beyond distance effects, there is little deep error analysis (e.g., boundary vs. type errors, stance confusions, long-distance cases), which would aid understanding and future extensions.

Questions to Authors
- Distance tagging details: Do you cap |d| at train-time or at inference? How do you handle distance values unseen in training? What is the empirical distribution of predicted vs. gold distances (confusion by bucket)?

- Relation reconstruction and constraints: How do you reconstruct component-level relations from token tags to guarantee exactly one outgoing link per component? How do you handle claims that should link to multiple major claims in the gold (your relinking heuristic)? Does this affect measured R-F1?

- Generalization beyond tree-like PE: Can your tagging scheme represent multiple outgoing relations per component or relations that cross paragraphs? If not, how would you extend it? Have you tried your models on microtexts or web argumentation datasets?

- Fairness of baselines: Did you provide the neural models with paragraph/essay positional features analogous to what the ILP uses implicitly? For LSTM-ER, did you try pruning candidate pairs by distance or simple heuristics to mitigate the O(m^2) pairing at essay level?

- Ablations: What is the performance impact of removing the distance dimension, stance, or using only (b,t)? For MTL, how sensitive are gains to which layer each task reads from, and how did you select layer assignments?

Overall, I find the work novel, bold, and impactful for AM: it proposes a clean, end-to-end neural direction with a clever label design, demonstrates strong results, and offers actionable insights about modeling choices. Despite dataset limitations and some comparisons that could be tighter, I lean toward acceptance.
