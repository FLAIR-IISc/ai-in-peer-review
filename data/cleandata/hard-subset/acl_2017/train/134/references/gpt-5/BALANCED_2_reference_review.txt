Summary
The paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) and compares several modeling framings on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset: (a) token-level dependency parsing, (b) sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO component boundaries, component type, distance to the linked component, and stance, (c) multi-task learning (MTL) variants that treat component and relation subproblems as auxiliary tasks, and (d) adapting LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) to AM. The study evaluates both paragraph-level and essay-level settings and compares against a feature-rich pipeline + ILP system (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). Results show neural models outperform the ILP baseline on paragraph-level, sequence tagging is robust across settings, MTL helps, LSTM-ER is strong for component detection but degrades for relation extraction on longer texts, and token-level dependency parsing underperforms.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence and analysis that sequence tagging with a distance-aware label space yields strong end-to-end AM performance, outperforming a strong ILP baseline and other neural framings, and is robust to document length relative to parsing and LSTM-ER.  
2) Systematic comparison of multiple neural framings (token-level parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) with insights on when general vs constrained formulations help (e.g., LSTM-ER excels with few entities; constrained tagging benefits long documents).  
3) Negative result for token-level dependency parsing as an end-to-end AM solution, with diagnosis (global tree search over long sequences, span errors), guiding future work away from this design.

Strengths
- Clear empirical takeaways with broad relevance: The comparative study across four paradigms is thorough and reveals actionable design guidance (e.g., de-couple components and relations but model jointly; constrain search space for long documents; use distance cues). The paper ties observations to data properties (component distances, number of entities, paragraph vs essay length), increasing generality of the conclusions.

- Strong experimental baselines and careful evaluation: The ILP system is a strong, task-specific prior SOTA. Statistical significance testing is provided. The evaluation protocol (exact/approx span and relation F1) follows accepted practice; the dependency parsing comparison spans classical feature-based and strong neural parsers.

- Practical modeling innovations: The distance- and stance-augmented tagging scheme is a simple, effective way to encode relation information locally without hand-crafted constraints. The MTL setup with component/relationship auxiliary tasks yields consistent gains and sheds light on task hierarchy.

- Detailed analysis and honest reporting: The authors note where models struggle (e.g., LSTM-ER on essay-level relation F1; parsers’ span inflation; effect of long-range dependencies), provide plausible explanations (O(m^2) relation pairs vs single-head constraint; length sensitivity), and discuss constraints vs learned behavior.

- Relevance to end-to-end AM and feature engineering reduction: Demonstrates competitive or better performance than ILP without hand-crafted features or constraint design, advancing the field toward more transferable, data-driven approaches.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in core architectures: Most models are adaptations of existing architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, LSTM-ER). The main novelty lies in the composite label design and the comparative study. This is valuable but incremental on the modeling side.

- Label space explosion and generalization: Encoding distances in labels yields a large, data-dependent label set and truncates to observed distances. The paper acknowledges long-range links exist; however, there is no analysis of out-of-range behavior, robustness to unseen distances, or strategies (e.g., bucketing) to control label sparsity.

- MTL only evaluated with a weaker base tagger: The beneficial MTL results are shown with a BL tagger without CRF or char-CNN, not with the stronger BLCC. It remains unclear whether MTL would yield additional gains on top of the best tagging architecture, or if CRF dependencies are complementary/redundant with MTL.

- Dataset and structural assumptions: The study uses a single dataset with specific structural regularities (e.g., claims relate to all major claims, premises have single heads, paragraphs self-contained). “Loss-less” re-linking of claims to a single MC and forcing single-head relations may bias comparisons in favor of constrained models and may not generalize to corpora with non-tree or cross-paragraph structures.

- Scope and fairness of comparisons: Some systems are only trained/evaluated at paragraph-level due to memory constraints, while others (ILP) exploit both levels. Conversely, LSTM-ER is penalized at essay-level by O(m^2) pair explosion. It would be useful to include simple pruning strategies (distance/window constraints, learned mention-pair filtering) to give LSTM-ER a fairer chance in long documents.

Questions to Authors
1) Distance label design: How sensitive is STagT to the maximum allowed distance in the label set? Did you try distance bucketing (e.g., |d|≤k exact, else >k bucket), or predicting distance as a separate scalar/classifier? Any performance vs label cardinality trade-off?

2) MTL with stronger tagger: Did you experiment with MTL using BLCC (CRF + char-CNN)? If not, do you foresee technical hurdles? Would you expect additive gains from CRF dependencies and auxiliary tasks?

3) Relation sparsity controls for LSTM-ER: Did you try pair pruning (e.g., sentence/paragraph windows, top-K candidate heads per source, distance thresholds) or curriculum strategies to mitigate O(m^2) at essay-level?

4) Generalization beyond PE: Have you tested on other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts) or domains with non-tree structures? How would your tagging constraint (single head, single relation) adapt if many-to-many relations are allowed?

5) Claim-to-major-claim handling: Can you quantify the impact of the re-linking heuristic on downstream metrics? Does it artificially favor models with single-head assumptions?

6) Error typology: For relations, what are the dominant errors (wrong endpoints vs wrong stance vs boundary-induced)? Does stance prediction benefit from component-type supervision in MTL?

7) Training details: Key hyperparameters, pre-trained embeddings, and optimization were deferred to the supplement. Could you summarize the most critical choices (e.g., embedding sources, dropout, learning rates) and their sensitivity?

Overall recommendation: I lean toward acceptance. The paper offers a careful, insightful comparison across neural framings, introduces a practical tagging formulation that outperforms a strong ILP baseline, and provides useful guidance for the community. Addressing the MTL-with-BLCC and distance-label generalization issues would further strengthen the work.
