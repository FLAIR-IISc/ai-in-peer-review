Summary and main contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) from raw text using neural models, replacing hand-crafted features and pipeline ILP post-processing. It explores four framings: (a) token-level dependency parsing; (b) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance; (c) multi-task tagging (MTL) with component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks; and (d) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER entity-relation model. Experiments on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus evaluate at paragraph- and essay-level with standard AM F1 metrics.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) Empirical finding that neural sequence tagging with distance-coded labels and BiLSTM-CRF-CNN (STagBLCC) sets strong end-to-end results on PE, surpassing a feature-based joint ILP on paragraph-level, and maintaining robust performance on essay-level; and that MTL with natural subtasks further improves tagging.
2) Systematic, negative result that token-level dependency parsing (both feature-based and neural) is ineffective for end-to-end AM, especially on long sequences, despite AM’s tree-like structures at the component level.
3) Comparative analysis showing when modular ER-style models (LSTM-ER) excel (components, short docs) vs. when constrained tagging encodings help (relations, long docs), grounded in distance distribution and search-space arguments.

Strengths
- Clear, multifaceted empirical study with strong baselines and ablations:
  - Evaluates four distinct neural framings plus a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP (Stab & Gurevych 2016), with paragraph vs. essay setups. The breadth of comparisons, including negative results, provides actionable guidance for the community.
  - Key results: on paragraph level, STagBLCC outperforms parsers and ILP (e.g., C-F1 66.69/74.08, R-F1 39.83/44.02), while LSTM-ER is strongest overall there (C-F1 70.83/77.19, R-F1 45.52/50.05). On essay level, STagBLCC is more robust on relations than LSTM-ER (R-F1 34.82/39.68 vs. 29.56/32.72), despite LSTM-ER’s advantage in C-F1.
- Novel and practical tagging formulation encoding relation distance in labels:
  - The label design captures long-range relations while imposing useful constraints (one outgoing link per component), reducing combinatorial explosion inherent in ER models on long texts. The distance distribution analysis (2/3 within {-2,-1,1}) is convincing and aligns with performance trends.
- Insightful analysis of length and structural effects:
  - The paper explains the sharp degradation of dependency parsing and LSTM-ER at essay-level via search space growth and length sensitivity, and provides evidence (relation accuracy vs. |d|). These insights generalize beyond AM to other document-level IE tasks.
- MTL finding is useful and credible:
  - Adding auxiliary component-only (YC) and relation-only (YR) tasks helps the full task, even with a weaker BL tagger; the paper reports configurations and gains (e.g., Y-3:YC-3 improves exact C-F1 by ~5 points over Y-3), suggesting a practical recipe for complex label spaces.
- Relevance and significance:
  - AM is an active area with downstream applications; demonstrating that neural end-to-end models can replace feature-based pipelines and ILP constraints is an impactful step.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete fairness/transparency in comparisons with ILP:
  - Table 1 reports ILP only on paragraph level, while several neural models are reported on both levels. Given that the ILP “has access to both paragraph and essay level information,” omitting essay-level ILP results leaves the strength of neural models on full essays less conclusive. Please add essay-level ILP numbers for C-F1, R-F1, and global F1.
- Limited dataset scope and generalization concerns:
  - All results are on a single dataset (PE), with specific structural regularities (e.g., premises have exactly one parent; claims connect to all major claims). The tagging approach enforces at-most-one outgoing link, and the parsing conversion re-links claims to a single MC. It is unclear how well the approaches (esp. STagT) generalize to corpora with many-to-many links or cross-paragraph relations. An additional dataset (e.g., microtexts, legal) or a discussion with preliminary evidence would strengthen claims.
- Distance-augmented label space and handling of unseen distances:
  - The label set grows with the range of d. While the distribution is skewed to short distances, the paper does not specify how unseen or large distances at test time are handled (binning, capping, open-class labels), nor the exact range used in training. This impacts scalability and robustness on longer documents or other domains.
- Reproducibility and missing implementation details:
  - Important choices (pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, distance range, decoding constraints for ensuring one link per component, handling of claims-to-MC special cases, paragraph segmentation at test time) are deferred to supplementary material not included here. For ACL standards, more detail in the main paper or ensured availability of code/supplement is desirable.
- Dependency parsing framing may be somewhat strawman at token level:
  - Since most AM work treats components as spans, evaluating only token-level parsing (which must recover spans and links jointly) is a severe challenge. A component-level parser (predict components first, then parse) could be a stronger parsing baseline. The paper’s negative result is still valuable, but the conclusion that “AM as token-level parsing is ineffective” should be tempered.

Questions to authors
- Please report essay-level ILP results to complete the comparison in Table 1. If unavailable, explain why, and discuss expected performance differences.
- How do you handle distances not seen during training at test time? Do you cap d to a fixed window, or use a special “far” bin? What is the exact set/range of distance labels used?
- For STagT, how do you enforce that each component links to exactly one other component at decoding time when tagging is token-level? Is there a post-processing step to reconcile token-level predictions into component-level structures?
- For LSTM-ER, given that ~92% of relations are cross-sentence, how are TreeLSTM paths constructed for cross-sentence pairs? Do you build document-level forests or connect sentence roots? Could this choice explain the drop on essay-level relations?
- Can you provide runtime/memory comparisons across models on essay-level, and any signs of overfitting given the small corpus (402 essays)?
