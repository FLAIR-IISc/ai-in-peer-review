Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods. The authors compare several framings: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes component boundaries/types plus relation distance and stance, multi-task learning (MTL) variants, and an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER (entity+relation extraction). They evaluate on the Stab & Gurevych (2016) persuasive essays dataset at paragraph and essay levels using the Persing & Ng F1 metrics, and compare primarily against the ILP-based joint pipeline.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM on a standard dataset, showing that sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) and LSTM-ER can outperform the established ILP pipeline at paragraph level, and that the proposed tagging approach is relatively robust at essay level.
2) A concrete tagging formulation that encodes relation distance (in number of components) and stance into token-level labels, with analysis of how document length and relation distance affect performance.
3) A (negative) result that naïve token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, coupled with evidence that mild modularization (decoupling components vs. relations) and MTL can help.

Strengths
- Careful empirical comparison across several plausible neural framings. The paper does not present a single model, but systematically evaluates four variants (parsing, tagging, MTL-tagging, LSTM-ER) against a strong established baseline (ILP). This breadth is useful for the community.
- Clear performance gains over the ILP baseline at paragraph level. Both STagBLCC and LSTM-ER show consistent improvements on C-F1 and R-F1 (with significance testing), and the analysis connects these gains to model bias (distance encoding, constrained search space).
- Granularity-aware evaluation (paragraph vs. essay) and insightful analysis of length effects. The degradation of LSTM-ER on essay-level relations is convincingly tied to the O(m^2) relation hypothesis space and long-range dependencies. The distance-conditioned success analysis (Figure 4) is informative.
- MTL ablations provide evidence that decoupling component and relation subtasks helps. Even with a weaker base tagger, adding auxiliary component detection improves overall F1, aligning with the paper’s thesis about joint modeling vs. hard coupling.
- Reproducibility signals and significance testing. The paper references code/software and uses Wilcoxon tests (p<0.01) for main comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: largely applications of established models to a known dataset. BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN), LSTM-ER, and MTL with shared BiLSTMs are well-known; the main novelty lies in the specific label design (distance/stance encoding) and the empirical study. The negative token-level parsing result, given the contrived token-to-head mapping, is not surprising and offers limited methodological insight.
- Single-dataset evaluation with dataset-specific assumptions. Results are entirely on Stab & Gurevych (2016), with PE-specific structural quirks (e.g., claims to all major claims; re-linking claims; paragraph-contained structures). Claims about generality (e.g., “neural end-to-end AM is as good or better”) are not validated on other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts). The re-linking heuristic and paragraph constraints may not transfer.
- Comparison fairness and scope mismatches. Neural dependency parsers are trained/tested only at paragraph level (essay-level training deemed too memory heavy), while other models are evaluated at both levels. The essay-level LSTM-ER deficit on relations seems partly self-imposed: no candidate-pair pruning or paragraph-aware constraints are applied, making the search space much larger than in the tagging setup, which bakes in a “one relation per component” constraint via labels. A stronger LSTM-ER baseline with reasonable candidate pruning would make the comparison more convincing.
- Methodological clarity gaps in the tagging setup. The composite tag set Y includes potentially unbounded distance values; the paper does not state the actual label cardinality used, whether distances are clipped, or the frequency of rare labels. It is also unclear how token-level predictions are aggregated to component-level spans and relations when inconsistencies arise (e.g., different tokens of the same component predicting different distances/stances). Any post-processing, tie-breaking, or constraint enforcement is not described, yet these choices can materially affect R-F1.
- Dependency parsing framing is arguably unnatural for this task. The token-level head assignment (all non-argumentative tokens attach to END; premises attach to the first token of the target component) creates long, non-syntactic arcs over paragraph-length spans. The poor performance is therefore not surprising; the result mainly shows a mismatch between the representation and the task rather than an inherent limitation of parsing-based approaches to AM (e.g., component-level parsing might be more appropriate).

Questions to authors
- Composite label space: What is the total number of distinct labels actually seen in training for STagBLCC? Are distances clipped (e.g., to a maximum |d|)? What fraction of test instances use unseen labels, and how is this handled?
- Mapping from token labels to components/relations: How are component spans formed from BIO predictions when inconsistencies occur? How is a single relation per component enforced if different tokens in the same span predict different (d,s) values? Is there any post-processing to ensure validity (e.g., majority vote, CRF-level constraints beyond BIO, global constraints)?
- LSTM-ER candidate pruning: Did you try simple pruning strategies (e.g., only consider pairs within the same paragraph or within a distance window in components) at essay level? If not, can you quantify the number of candidate pairs at essay level and the empirical class imbalance? This would help contextualize the drop in R-F1.
- Dependency parsers: Could you train component-level parsers (predict components first, then parse a graph/tree over components) to separate the effects of token-level modeling from the parsing paradigm? Also, for essay-level parsing, are there memory-efficient settings (e.g., projective-only, batch truncation) you tried?
- Statistical testing beyond Table 1: Are the MTL improvements in Table 2 statistically significant vs. the single-task BL baseline? Please report significance and variance across runs.

Additional comments (minor)
- Please clarify the treatment of relation types: s includes For/Against and Support/Attack; are they mutually exclusive per dataset conventions, and how is this encoded?
- Report dev/test splits and hyperparameter tuning procedures to rule out overfitting to test.
- Consider adding a strong non-neural CRF baseline with handcrafted features for component tagging to contextualize neural gains.
