Summary
This paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods that avoid hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The authors compare four framings on the Persuasive Essays dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016): (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance (STagT, with BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), (iii) multi-task tagging with auxiliary component-only and relation-only tasks, and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) that decouples entity and relation extraction. They evaluate on paragraph and essay levels using component/relationship F1 at exact and approximate matching. Main findings: token-level dependency parsing is weak; STagT is strong and robust; LSTM-ER is best on paragraphs but degrades on essays due to combinatorial relation search; multi-task auxiliary tasks improve STagT; several neural setups outperform the prior feature-based ILP joint model.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A thorough empirical comparison of plausible neural end-to-end framings for AM, with clear analyses of document length effects and task coupling. This includes a simple but effective tagging scheme that encodes relation distance and stance.
2) Evidence that neural models match or exceed the feature-based ILP state-of-the-art on the PE dataset without hand-crafted features or hard structural constraints, plus concrete guidance on when general vs constrained framings work best (paragraph vs essay).
3) Negative result on token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM, with a plausible diagnosis (tree globality + token-head encoding + long sequences) and practical alternatives.

Strengths
- Strong empirical study with multiple baselines and perspectives. The paper evaluates four distinct neural framings and two levels of granularity, using standard metrics and significance tests. Key results are compelling: on paragraphs, LSTM-ER achieves the best C-F1/R-F1 (e.g., 70.83/45.52 exact; 77.19/50.05 approx), while the proposed STagBLCC is close (66.69/39.83 exact) and consistently surpasses the best neural parser (58.86/35.63 exact). On essays, STagBLCC is most robust (C-F1 63.23/69.49; R-F1 34.82/39.68), beating LSTM-ER on relations (29.56/32.72).
- Clear insight into modeling trade-offs. The study convincingly shows that a more general relation module (LSTM-ER) excels when entity sets are small (paragraphs), but suffers on essays due to O(m^2) relation candidates; conversely, the tagger’s constrained, distance-aware coding biases towards short-range links and scales better. Figure 4 further supports this with accuracy vs |d|.
- Simple, practical sequence tagging formulation. Encoding component/stance/distance in tags is straightforward to implement and effective. STagBLCC outperforms the ILP model on paragraph-level relations (44.02 vs 44.29 approx R-F1 is comparable; overall F1 55.22 vs 55.23) and is competitive on essays without manual features or constraints.
- Useful negative result on token-level parsing. The paper shows that even strong neural parsers struggle with end-to-end AM at token level, especially on essays (e.g., LSTM-Parser exact C-F1 drops to 32.84, R-F1 9.11), and provides a plausible explanation (global tree search on very long sequences, unnatural token-head encoding for components).
- Evidence that multi-tasking helps. Auxiliary component and relation subtasks improve the weaker BiLSTM tagger (e.g., Y-3:YC-3 boosts exact C-F1 to 54.58 and R-F1 to 30.22, improving overall F1 to 38.90/50.51), reinforcing the value of decoupling while sharing representations.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. Most architectures are adaptations of existing models; the main new element is the label design encoding distances and stance. While impactful empirically, methodological contribution is incremental.
- Potentially unfair disadvantage to dependency parsing. The token-head design (every token in a component attaches to the first token of the target component; all non-argumentative tokens to END) is an unnatural parsing target and likely inflates span errors. A fairer alternative would parse at the component level (e.g., pipeline segmentation + parsing, or constituent-to-component conversion) and/or use document-level discourse parsing variants.
- Incomplete ablations on the tagging design. The paper asserts that distance encoding helps, but does not ablate distance bucketing/granularity, stance encoding, or CRF vs non-CRF effects on relations. The size of the tag space, distribution of unseen labels at test time, and strategies for resolving inconsistent token-level tags within a component (e.g., conflicting d or stance) are not detailed.
- Structural validity is not reported. Since sequence taggers and LSTM-ER do not enforce tree constraints, it would be important to report how often predicted structures violate known AM constraints (e.g., multiple outgoing links per premise, cycles) and whether post-processing was applied. This undermines the claim that constraints are unnecessary.
- Narrow dataset scope. All results are on a single domain (PE). Given the argument theory variability highlighted by the authors, testing on at least one additional corpus (e.g., microtexts) would strengthen claims of generality.

Questions to Authors
- How do you decode from token-level tags to component-level structures when tokens within a component disagree on (d, s)? What consistency heuristics or aggregation are used? How sensitive are results to this?
- What is the effective size of the tag set Y in train/test? How often do you encounter unseen (b, t, d, s) combinations at test time, and how does the model handle them?
- Did you try distance bucketing (e.g., {-1, +1, short, medium, long})? If so, how did it affect R-F1 and robustness on essays?
- For LSTM-ER, did you try candidate pruning (e.g., distance-based or paragraph boundary constraints) to reduce O(m^2) pairs on essays? Would that close the essay-level gap?
- Can you report the fraction of predictions violating AM tree-like constraints for each model, and whether simple post-hoc pruning improves R-F1?

Overall, this paper provides a valuable and timely empirical contribution with takeaways that practitioners can act on. While the novelty is moderate and some design choices and comparisons could be strengthened, the empirical insights and performance gains over a strong ILP baseline justify acceptance.
