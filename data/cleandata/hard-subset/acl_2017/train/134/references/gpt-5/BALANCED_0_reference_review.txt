Summary
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) that jointly identify argumentative components (major claims, claims, premises) and relations (support/attack/stance), starting from raw tokens. The authors evaluate four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with an enriched label space encoding BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance (STagT); (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary component/relation subtasks; and (iv) LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016), which decouples entity and relation extraction but trains them jointly. Experiments on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset (paragraph vs essay level) show that sequence tagging and LSTM-ER outperform a strong feature-based ILP pipeline, token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, and MTL brings further gains. The paper includes analyses on distance distributions and document length effects.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A comprehensive empirical comparison of multiple neural end-to-end framings for AM on a common benchmark, including negative results (token-level dependency parsing) and analyses of when different framings work best (paragraph vs essay length).
2) A simple sequence tagging formulation with distance-aware labels that delivers strong, robust performance and surpasses a prior joint ILP approach, without hand-crafted features or constraints.
3) Evidence that decoupling component and relation prediction but modeling them jointly (via MTL or LSTM-ER) improves component detection, and that constrained relational search (via tag-based distance and one-link assumptions) mitigates the combinatorial explosion in long documents.

Strengths
- Solid empirical advancement over feature-based ILP: The sequence tagging model (BLCC) and LSTM-ER outperform the ILP baseline on paragraph-level component and relation F1, despite lacking hand-crafted features and manually designed constraints. This is a meaningful step for practical AM systems that seek portability across datasets and domains.

- Clear, informative modeling comparison with insights: The study contrasts local (tagging) vs global (parsing) vs semi-decoupled (LSTM-ER) formalisms and links performance to document length and search space size. The analysis of distance distributions and the conditional accuracy curves over |d| help explain why constrained, distance-aware tagging works better in long texts and why relation prediction degrades in unconstrained models.

- Useful negative result and careful diagnostics: Demonstrating that token-level dependency parsing underperforms (especially at essay level) is valuable for the community. The authors diagnose likely causes (span boundary errors, long sequences, massive tree search space), showing that not all “tree-like” framings are appropriate for end-to-end AM.

- Sensible multi-task design and gains: Adding component and relation subtasks as auxiliary objectives yields consistent improvements, with component detection as the most beneficial auxiliary. The layer-probing study (which layers feed which tasks) adds nuance beyond a single MTL setting.

- Attention to evaluation design: Reporting both exact (100%) and approximate (50%) span/relational F1, and comparing paragraph vs essay levels, reveals where models succeed/fail. Significance testing is included for key comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset and structural idiosyncrasies: All results are on a single dataset (PE) with strong structural regularities (e.g., paragraph-local argument structures; claims link to all major claims; premises have single heads), which the models partly encode as constraints (single outgoing link; distance bias). It is unclear how well the proposed tagset and constraints generalize to datasets with cross-paragraph links, many-to-many relations, non-tree graphs, or different stance schemes.

- Tagset scalability and sparsity: The sequence tagger’s label space combines BIO × type × distance × stance, leading to a potentially large, sparse tag vocabulary and long-tail distance values. The paper shows observed distances up to ±10, but does not discuss how unseen or larger distances are handled, whether distance bucketing is used, or the effect of truncation/bucketing on performance and generalization.

- Fairness and adaptation of LSTM-ER: The evaluation notes that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, limiting TreeLSTM usefulness. However, LSTM-ER could be adapted to discourse/dependency structures at paragraph level or augmented with constraints to reduce the O(m^2) relation space; it is unclear how extensively such adaptations or constraints were explored. This leaves open whether the gap in essay-level R-F1 is inherent to the approach or to the chosen instantiation.

- Missing ablations and implementation clarity: Several crucial details are relegated to supplementary material (embeddings, hyperparameters, early stopping, optimization). Important ablations are absent: e.g., removing the distance component from tags; replacing distance with bucketing; adding lightweight global constraints (e.g., constrained decoding or CRF with structured factors); span-level prediction before linking. MTL results lack significance testing.

- Dependency parsing framing at token level only: The negative result may reflect the choice to parse at token granularity. A span-based component detector followed by component-level parsing (e.g., biaffine or pointer-based linking on spans) could be a stronger “parsing” baseline and more faithful to AM structure than token-level head selection.

Questions to Authors
- Tagset design and scalability: How many distinct labels in Y are actually observed during training? Do you bucket distances, cap them, or back off unseen distances at test time? How sensitive is performance to the maximum distance (±k)? Have you tried distance bucketing (e.g., {-1, +1, [-2,-5], [2,5], >5})?

- Generalization beyond PE: Have you tested on corpora with cross-paragraph links or non-tree graphs (e.g., microtexts, legal corpora)? How would you adapt STagT to many-to-many relations or cyclic structures?

- Constraints and search reduction for LSTM-ER: Did you try adding soft or hard constraints (e.g., one outgoing link for premises; distance-based priors) during relation decoding? Or a candidate pruning strategy using distance windows predicted by the tagger?

- Alternative span-level pipelines: Did you evaluate a two-stage neural pipeline—(1) span-level component detection via BiLSTM-CRF; (2) span-level relation prediction with biaffine scoring and simple constraints? This could fairly test “component-level parsing” vs your token-level parsing negative result.

- Impact of distance and stance components: What is the performance drop if the tagger omits d or s (or uses a separate predictor for them)? Is most of the gain coming from distance supervision vs. a generic tagger plus a learned linker?

- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models, and exact data preprocessing scripts (especially for paragraph vs essay setups and the re-linking of claims to major claims/root)?
