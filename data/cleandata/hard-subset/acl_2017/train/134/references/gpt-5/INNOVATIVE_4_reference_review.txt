Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) without hand-crafted features or ILP constraints by exploring several neural formulations and offering practical guidance. Using the persuasive essays dataset, the authors (i) cast AM as token-level dependency parsing, (ii) as sequence tagging with a novel composite label encoding components, types, link distances, and stance, (iii) as multi-task learning with auxiliary sub-tasks, and (iv) adapt LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) to AM. They compare paragraph- vs. essay-level performance and against a strong feature-based ILP baseline.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, novel sequence tagging formulation that encodes component-to-component distance and relation stance in token labels, yielding robust SOTA results versus ILP and neural parsers, and generalizing well across paragraph/essay settings.
2) A systematic empirical study contrasting multiple end-to-end neural framings for AM, including a useful negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM in realistic sequences.
3) Multi-task instantiations showing that decoupling components and relations but training jointly improves performance and offering actionable guidance on when more general relation modeling (LSTM-ER) vs. restrained tagging is preferable (short vs. long documents).

Strengths
- Novel, bold formulation with practical impact: Encoding long-range relation information (distance, stance) directly into the tag space is a clever, minimalistic idea that removes the need for global constraints yet captures key AM structure. It consistently outperforms the feature-engineered ILP and neural parsers and remains competitive against LSTM-ER depending on sequence length. This is a clean conceptual advance with low engineering overhead.

- Comprehensive comparative study and clear takeaways: Few AM papers evaluate multiple end-to-end framings side-by-side. The paper’s cross-paragraph/essay analyses and distance sensitivity (e.g., Fig. 2, Fig. 4) produce actionable guidance: use restrained tagging when there are many entities (long texts), and consider more general entity-relation models for shorter texts.

- Informative negative result on token-level dependency parsing: Showing that state-of-the-art neural/feature-based parsers struggle badly on essay-level AM (huge drop in F1, overly long spans) is valuable for the community and prevents wasted effort. The analysis (global search complexity, long sequences) is plausible and grounded.

- Multi-task benefits and design insight: The MTL experiments demonstrate consistent gains from auxiliary sub-tasks, especially component detection, and they challenge simplistic “lower tasks at lower layers” heuristics. This is useful for practitioners designing neural AM systems or other complex sequence taggers.

- Strong baselines and significance testing: Including the well-known ILP system and LSTM-ER, and reporting statistical significance (Wilcoxon) for key comparisons, strengthens claims.

Weaknesses
- Generalization beyond one dataset and annotation scheme: All results are on a single corpus with relatively tree-like, paragraph-contained structures and specific conventions (e.g., claims link to all major claims). The proposed tagging scheme, especially the one-link-per-component and finite distance assumptions, may not transfer to corpora with many-to-many graphs, cross-paragraph links, or different discourse/AM theories. Some evidence or discussion on portability (e.g., microtexts, legal corpora) would improve the contribution.

- Label space growth and distance truncation concerns: Encoding distance into labels can lead to a large tag set as distances expand, risking data sparsity and calibration issues. The paper notes long-range links exist (up to ±10+), but does not detail how distances are capped, smoothed, or backed-off during training/inference, nor ablate the contribution of distance vs. stance vs. BIO/+type. This threatens scalability to longer documents and different domains.

- Fairness and clarity of evaluation settings: Models are sometimes trained/evaluated at different granularities (paragraph vs. essay), while the ILP can leverage both. Although the paper is transparent about degradation on essays, the presentation occasionally mixes settings. A unified comparison table per setting (with identical training/evaluation scopes and resources) would help. Additionally, dependency parsers were only trained on paragraphs due to memory; this weakens the negative result somewhat.

- Limited structural validity checks: The tagging approaches do not enforce global well-formedness (e.g., trees/forests, single parent per premise), which could degrade consistency, especially on essays. While the authors argue models learn constraints implicitly, reporting rates of structural violations (e.g., dangling links, multiple heads) would strengthen claims and help downstream adoption.

- LSTM-ER adaptation and ablations: Given 92% of relations are cross-sentence, the TreeLSTM’s sentence-level features are known to be mismatched. It would be useful to include ablations that remove tree components, emphasize sequential/context features, or incorporate paragraph/document-level structure in LSTM-ER to ensure a balanced comparison.

Questions to authors
- How is the distance label d handled in practice: is there a maximum capped distance, bucketing, or smoothing for rare distances? What is the size of the final label set Y and the distribution of rare tags during training/inference?

- Can you report structural validity metrics for the tagging outputs (e.g., percent of components with 0/2+ outgoing links, cycles), and their impact on R-F1?

- How sensitive are the tagging results to the choice of stance encoding (Supp/Att/For/Ag) vs. a collapsed stance? Any gains from predicting stance only after components/links are decided?

- Could you provide an ablation for STagT without distance (only link existence), and another with distance but no stance, to quantify the contribution of each component?

- What is the runtime/memory profile for essay-level decoding across systems? The dependency parsers struggled with memory; how do the tagging models and LSTM-ER scale?

- Any preliminary results on other AM corpora (microtexts, legal, scientific) or on cross-paragraph linking, to test portability of the tagging approach?

Overall, this paper proposes a simple yet powerful neural formulation for end-to-end AM, offers valuable comparative insights (including a negative result), and provides practical guidance. Despite limitations in generality and evaluation scope, the conceptual novelty and empirical gains merit strong consideration.
