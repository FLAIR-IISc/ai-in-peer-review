Summary and main contributions
- The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) from raw text and evaluates several neural formulations that avoid hand-crafted features and pipeline ILP constraints: (a) token-level dependency parsing, (b) sequence tagging that jointly encodes component boundaries/types and inter-component relations via BIO tags augmented with distance and stance, (c) multi-task learning (MTL) where component and relation subtasks act as auxiliaries, and (d) an entity–relation neural model (LSTM-ER) combining BiLSTM-CRF entity tagging with TreeLSTM relation classification.
- On the Stab and Gurevych (2016) persuasive essays corpus, several neural models outperform a strong feature-based joint ILP system in end-to-end metrics, especially at paragraph level. The sequence tagger with BiLSTM-CRF-CNN (STagBLCC) yields robust performance; LSTM-ER gives the best paragraph-level results; dependency parsing at token level underperforms.
- The paper provides an empirical analysis of how document length affects relation extraction (paragraph vs. essay), and shows that adding natural subtasks in MTL improves sequence tagging, and that explicit distance encoding biases tags towards empirically observed short-range relations.

Strengths
- Clear framing and comprehensive empirical comparison: The paper systematically compares four distinct neural formulations for end-to-end AM, plus a feature-based ILP baseline, across paragraph and essay levels, using standard F1 metrics with exact/approximate matching. Statistical testing (Wilcoxon, p<0.01) is reported for the main table.
- Strong practical contribution: Neural models (especially STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) surpass or match the ILP baseline while removing the need for manual features and global constraint engineering. This is significant given the variability of AM annotation schemes across datasets.
- Insight on modeling choices: Results demonstrate that token-level dependency parsing is not effective for end-to-end AM, while local sequence tagging—especially with distance-aware labels—and de-coupled entity/relation modeling (LSTM-ER) are effective, particularly on shorter texts.
- Analysis of sequence length effects: The paper diagnoses why relation F1 drops on essay level for LSTM-ER (quadratic candidate explosion) and why distance-aware tagging retains robustness by constraining outputs and biasing towards short-range links; this offers guidance for model selection by document length.
- Multi-task learning benefit: Adding component and relation auxiliary tasks improves the main tagging task, with component detection as a consistently useful auxiliary. This is a concrete, reproducible architectural insight.

Weaknesses
- Structural mismatch and potential information loss for non-tree relations: The dataset allows claims to link to all major claims. Several proposed framings (token-level dependency parsing and the distance-augmented sequence tagger) enforce at most one outgoing relation per component. The paper states a “loss-less” re-linking to create a tree for parsing; however, this alters the original multi-head structure. It is unclear how multi-links are handled in the tagging setup, and whether evaluation compares against the original multi-relations. This could bias results or limit applicability to datasets with many-to-many structures.
- Label space sparsity and distance encoding details: Encoding relation distance d in the tag inflates the label space (b, t, d, s). The paper shows distances up to ±11 but does not specify whether d is capped or how unseen distances at test time are handled. The combination of sparse d×s×t labels may exacerbate data sparsity, especially at essay level. More detail on smoothing or bucketing would clarify technical soundness.
- Limited external validation: All experiments are on a single corpus (persuasive essays). While the paper argues neural models avoid dataset-specific features, no cross-domain or cross-dataset evaluation is provided. Given AM’s known domain sensitivity, this limits claims of generality.
- Dependency parsing framing at token level: Although the paper concludes it is ineffective, the chosen token-level encoding (e.g., setting all O-token heads to END; linking each premise token to the first token of its target) may confound the outcome by mixing component segmentation and relation prediction at a granularity not aligned with dependency parsing strengths. A component-level parser with learned component boundaries (e.g., via span proposals) might be a fairer test of the parsing paradigm.
- Fairness and detail of baseline comparisons: The ILP baseline uses both paragraph and essay information; some neural models are trained/evaluated only at paragraph level. Conversely, LSTM-ER leverages scheduled sampling and pretraining, but comparable techniques (e.g., CRF decoding constraints, span-level reranking) are not explored for STagBLCC. More uniform training/decode settings and ablations would strengthen comparative claims.

Questions to authors
- How are claim-to-major-claim multi-relations represented in each framing, especially for STagT? If only one outgoing relation per component is permitted, how is evaluation computed against gold structures with multiple links?
- What is the exact support of the distance label d? Is it capped or bucketed (e.g., beyond ±K), and how are unseen distances handled at test time? Did you observe sparsity issues for rare (d, s, t) combinations?
- For STagT decoding, how are component spans and relations recovered from token labels when BIO violations occur? Is there postprocessing (e.g., Viterbi with constraints, span correction)?
- For LSTM-ER on essay level, did you prune candidate pairs (e.g., by maximum distance or type constraints) to mitigate O(m^2) explosion? If not, could constrained candidate generation close the gap to STagBLCC on relations?
- The “loss-less” re-linking to create trees: can you quantify its impact on training/evaluation (e.g., how often multiple claim-to-MC links occur; whether they affect R-F1 under the Persing & Ng metric)?
- Could you report variance and significance for the MTL experiments (Table 2) and specify the auxiliary-loss weighting and layer selection protocol?

Additional comments
- Providing code and hyperparameters (embeddings, dropout, tag set cardinality, distance handling) in the main text or appendix would improve reproducibility.
- A component-level formulation (span tagging/proposal + component-level parser) may offer a stronger parsing baseline without forcing token heads.
