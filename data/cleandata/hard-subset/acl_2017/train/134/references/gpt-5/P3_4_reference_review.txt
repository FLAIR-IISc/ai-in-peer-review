Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural models. The authors compare four framings on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO, component type, link distance, and stance (STagT); (iii) multi-task tagging with auxiliary tasks for components and relations; and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER for joint entity–relation extraction. They evaluate at both paragraph and essay levels using Persing & Ng’s exact/approximate F1 metrics and compare against Stab & Gurevych’s feature-based pipeline + ILP.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A thorough empirical comparison of several neural end-to-end formulations of AM, with careful analysis of document length effects and constraints, yielding new SOTA on the PE dataset in multiple settings.
2) A simple yet effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes component-to-component distance and stance in the tag, plus evidence that auxiliary-task MTL improves performance.
3) A negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, clarifying the limits of tree-structured global modeling in long-document AM.

Strengths
- Comprehensive and well-motivated empirical study: The paper evaluates four substantially different neural framings, at both paragraph and essay granularity, and compares to a strong ILP pipeline. The analysis (e.g., Figure 2 on distance distributions; Figure 4 on length sensitivity; paragraph vs essay trends) provides actionable insights for model choice in AM.
- Simple tagging with distance encoding works: The STagT design is intuitive, requires no handcrafted features or constraints, and produces competitive or superior results to ILP and neural baselines. The explicit distance encoding plausibly biases the model towards realistic short-range links while still permitting long-range ones.
- Multi-task learning helps: Adding auxiliary component and relation taggers consistently improves the full end-to-end tagger, even with a weaker base BiLSTM (without CRF/CNN). This is a clean, replicable result of methodological interest beyond AM.
- Strong baselines and statistical testing: The comparison includes both classic and neural parsers, a state-of-the-art neural ER model, and the prior ILP pipeline. The use of Wilcoxon tests aids credibility.
- Useful practical guidance: The paper convincingly argues that more general, pairwise relation scorers (LSTM-ER) are advantageous when there are few entities (paragraphs) but become disadvantaged on long documents because of O(m^2) pairs and weak distance bias, whereas constrained taggers fare better.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is primarily empirical; methods are incremental: The core techniques (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging, MTL tagging, LSTM-ER) are established; the main novelty lies in the particular tag design (distance/stance in labels) and the breadth of the comparison. The dependency parsing formulation is interesting but unsurprisingly underperforms, and the paper offers limited architectural innovation.
- “Loss-less” re-linking of claim–major-claim structure is questionable: The PE data has claims linking to all major claims; the paper “re-links the claims to one of the major claims” and introduces a root for major claims. It is unclear how this preserves the original multi-headed structure without information loss. This assumption affects both the tagging label design and the parsing target trees and may bias evaluation.
- Tag space sparsity and decoding details are under-specified: Encoding (b, t, d, s) yields a large, sparse label space, especially as d ranges over many values. The paper does not describe whether d is clipped, how unseen distances at test time are handled, or how labels are post-processed to ensure coherent components and valid single outgoing links per component. Similarly, for the parsing formulation, the decoding from token heads/labels to component spans and relations is not fully specified. These details matter for reproducibility and may affect performance.
- Evaluation and metrics need clarification: The “global F1” is described as “the F1-score of C-F1 and R-F1,” which is nonstandard. Please specify exactly how it is computed (harmonic mean? macro over instances?). Also, several practical details (pretrained embeddings, dev split, early stopping, hyperparameters) are deferred to supplementary material and are critical to interpret the results.
- Dependency parsing framing may be unfairly handicapped: Training parsers only at paragraph level due to memory limits and assigning non-argumentative tokens to END as heads create an unusual parsing target. It is not demonstrated that stronger span-based or biaffine neural parsers (or a component-level parsing pipeline) would not close the gap. The negative result is valuable but may partly reflect the specific token-level design.

Questions to authors
1) “Loss-less” re-linking: How exactly do you transform the claim–major-claim all-to-all links into a tree without losing multiple attachments? How are stance labels preserved in this transformation?
2) Tag space and decoding: What is the maximum |d| used in training? Do you clip distances or backoff unseen distances at test time? How do you reconstruct component spans and ensure only one outgoing relation per component from token-level tags? Is any post-processing/constraint enforcement applied?
3) Metrics: How is “global F1” computed precisely? Do you average across documents or compute corpus-level micro F1? Are significance tests based on document-level paired scores?
4) Data splits and tuning: What development split is used? Which pretrained embeddings and fine-tuning scheme? Could you provide ablations for the CRF layer and the char-CNN in STagBLCC?
5) LSTM-ER configuration: Given that 92% of links are cross-sentence, did you try disabling the TreeLSTM or replacing it with a document-level encoder and a distance-aware biaffine scorer? Did you prune candidate pairs by sentence/paragraph distance?

Additional comments
- The dependency parsing negative result is still useful to the community; consider reframing it as a component-level parsing baseline to decouple segmentation.
- A span-based joint model (predict spans first, then relations with distance-aware scoring) would be a natural comparator to STagT and may mitigate label sparsity.
- References contain multiple formatting placeholders; please fix. The paper has scattered typos (“seperately”).
