- Comprehensive empirical study comparing four end-to-end neural framings for argumentation mining on the Persuasive Essays corpus, with paragraph- vs essay-level comparisons using the Persing & Ng protocol.
- Proposed a sequence-tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance in labels (distance-coded tagging), yielding strong, robust performance especially on longer documents and outperforming a feature-based ILP baseline.
- Negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; LSTM-ER helps on short documents while distance-coded tagging helps on long documents; decoupling component vs relation supervision via MTL improves performance.
- Thorough empirical comparison with strong baselines, careful analyses (distance distribution, sequence length, coupling) and statistical testing.
- Practical, generalizable insight that distance-coded tagging scales better to long documents while more general relation models work when entity sets are small; negative parsing result redirects community effort.
- Simple modeling achieves strong performance without dataset-specific features/constraints; MTL evidence that component detection as an auxiliary task is beneficial and transferable.
- Clear discussion of trade-offs between constraints and generality, supported by empirical probes and reasoning about search space.
- Relevant and timely contribution that provides neural end-to-end baselines to lower the barrier for future AM work.
- Limited architectural novelty: main contributions are task framing and comparative study rather than new architectures.
- Single-dataset scope (PE corpus) raises concerns about transferability to other AM domains with different structures and noise levels.
- Comparability/fairness issues: differing paragraph vs essay training for systems and ILP's access to paragraph position may bias comparisons.
- Unclear handling of label-space sparsity: maximum distance, clipping/bucketing, and handling of unseen distances are not specified.
- MTL setup confounded with model capacity: MTL backbone is weaker than BLCC, so gains may mix with capacity differences; layer/task design underexplored.
- Reproducibility gaps: key training and pruning details are deferred to the supplement; code or more in-paper details would help.
- Question: What maximum |d| was used for distance labels, and how are unseen/large distances handled or bucketed?
- Question: How sensitive are results to the chosen relinking strategy for multi-link claims, and were relations to major claims evaluated separately?
- Question: Does LSTM-ER prune candidate pairs at essay level, and if so what pruning rules and computational costs are used?
- Question: Was MTL tried with CRF and char-CNN added to the backbone, and would auxiliary-task gains hold with a stronger backbone?
- Question: Can ILP results be reported restricted to paragraph-internal inference, or can neural models be augmented with paragraph-position features to equalize information?
- Question: Have best-performing setups been evaluated on other AM datasets, and how would distance-coded tagging handle non-tree structures or noisier distances?
- Overall assessment — Originality: moderate (novel framing and negative result within AM; standard architectures).
- Overall assessment — Quality: high (solid experiments and analyses, but some comparability/reproducibility gaps).
- Overall assessment — Clarity: good, though important implementation details are in the supplement.
- Overall assessment — Significance: good for the AM community; provides strong neural baselines and actionable insights.
