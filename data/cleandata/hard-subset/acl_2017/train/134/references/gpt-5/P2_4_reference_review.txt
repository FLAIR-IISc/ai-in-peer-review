Summary and contributions
This paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods. It evaluates several framings on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary tasks for components and relations; and (iv) an adaptation of the Miwa and Bansal LSTM-ER entity-relation model. It compares against the feature-engineered pipeline + ILP approach of Stab and Gurevych (2016), reporting component and relation F1 at exact and 50% span match, on paragraph and essay levels, with significance testing.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A thorough, well-justified empirical comparison of neural framings for end-to-end AM, yielding clear modeling insights: sequence tagging with distance-encoding is robust; token-level dependency parsing is ineffective; entity-relation decoupling helps components but struggles with relations on long documents. Several neural framings achieve or surpass the ILP baseline.
2) A simple yet impactful tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance and stance into token labels, enabling local models to capture long-range links without hand-designed constraints; augmented with a principled MTL setup that provides measurable gains.
3) A careful analysis of paragraph vs. essay granularity and the effect of document length on relation search space, with concrete guidance on when restricted vs. general relation modeling is advantageous.

Strengths
- Strong and relevant empirical findings with explicit reasoning: The paper systematically evaluates four neural framings and links outcomes to modeling properties (local vs. global, restricted vs. general relation space, decoupled vs. joint). The explanation that distance-coded tagging reduces relation search space while still modeling long-range links is convincing and supported by distance distributions and performance gaps between paragraph and essay settings.

- Competitive or superior performance to a strong baseline: Against the Stab & Gurevych ILP system, the neural models (especially the BLCC tagger and LSTM-ER at paragraph level) match or outperform on component and relation F1, with significance testing. This demonstrates practical gains while removing hand-crafted features and manual ILP constraints.

- Valuable negative result and ablations: Showing that token-level dependency parsing underperforms for end-to-end AM is informative; the analysis (long sequences, global tree search complexity, stack-history benefits of LSTM-Parser) is insightful. The MTL ablations (Y vs. Y with YC/YR) provide evidence that auxiliary supervision on natural subtasks yields consistent improvements, and that components help more than relations.

- Clarity and transparency: The task framing, label space, dataset peculiarities (paragraph containment, claim–major-claim links), evaluation protocol (100% vs. 50% match), and statistical testing are clearly described. The discussion of limitations (e.g., TreeLSTM use in LSTM-ER mismatched to cross-sentence links) is candid.

- Practical guidance for the field: The conclusions translate into actionable recommendations (restrictive tagging model on long documents; more general entity-relation modeling on short documents), which is useful for practitioners beyond this dataset.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset scope and potential overfitting to PE structure: All results are on a single dataset with idiosyncrasies (relations contained within paragraphs; claims linked to all major claims). The re-linking of claims to a single major claim to enforce tree structure is asserted to be lossless; however, this is a nontrivial transformation that could affect the natural relation structure and comparability with prior work. The lack of cross-corpus validation (e.g., microtexts, web discourse) limits claims of generality.

- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The dependency parsing framing is token-level and global; however, it is arguably a poor fit for end-to-end AM at this granularity. A stronger neural pipeline baseline (e.g., BLSTM-CRF component segmentation/typing followed by a simple learned relation classifier with global constraints) could better isolate the benefit of the proposed tagging and MTL formulations. Similarly, LSTM-ER is adapted but its TreeLSTM sentence-level structure is mismatched; a graph neural or cross-sentence relation encoder would be a more suitable comparator.

- Label set complexity and generalization: The composite tag space grows with the range of distances and stances. The paper argues finiteness based on human proximity biases and shows observed ranges, but it does not detail how unseen or large distances are handled at test time (bucketing, clipping) or how sensitive performance is to distance cardinality. This affects scalability to longer documents or domains with sparser argument structure.

- Essay-level relation performance and practical impact: While paragraph-level results are strong, essay-level relations remain modest across models (e.g., R-F1 significantly lower), which is arguably the more realistic application setting. The paper diagnoses search space explosion but does not explore lightweight global constraints or pruning strategies (e.g., distance priors, candidate selection) that could narrow the gap.

- Reproducibility details are deferred: Hyperparameter tuning, pretrained embeddings, optimization schedules, and code availability are said to be in the supplement. For a multiframing comparison, ensuring comparable tuning and reporting training/inference cost would strengthen reproducibility and fairness.

Questions to authors
1) How are unseen or large distance values handled at test time in the tagging models? Do you bucket distances (e.g., >10), clip to max seen, or allow an “other” category? How sensitive are results to the chosen distance range?

2) Can you quantify the impact of re-linking claims to a single major claim on evaluation? Have you compared performance with and without this transformation or measured how often it changes the relation graph?

3) How were hyperparameters tuned across models to ensure fair comparison (e.g., dev set, search budget)? Could you provide a concise summary of tuning ranges and selected values, and confirm that ILP features/constraints match those in Stab & Gurevych (2016)?

4) Did you consider a neural pipeline baseline: BLSTM-CRF for component spans/types followed by a relation classifier with simple global constraints (e.g., one outgoing edge per premise)? This would help disentangle the benefits of the proposed label encoding from the general advantage of neural components.

5) For MTL, did you try adding a CRF layer to the BL backbone (to match BLCC) and observe whether auxiliary tasks still help? If not, how much of the gap to BLCC is attributable to label dependency modeling?

6) Given that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, did you evaluate replacing the TreeLSTM in LSTM-ER with a cross-sentence encoder (e.g., BiLSTM over sentence sequence or shallow discourse cues)? If not, do you anticipate gains at essay level?

Overall assessment
Novelty is moderate but appropriate for ACL: standard neural architectures are adapted thoughtfully to AM, with a novel and practical tagging formulation and solid analytical insights. Soundness and clarity are strong; significance is good within the dataset but would benefit from broader validation. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying distance handling, re-linking effects, and fairness of baselines.
