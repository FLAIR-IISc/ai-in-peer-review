- Paper evaluates several neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset, comparing token-level dependency parsing, composite sequence tagging, multi-task tagging, and an LSTM-ER adaptation against an ILP pipeline baseline.

- Main contribution: thorough empirical comparison showing sequence tagging with distance-encoding is robust, token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, and entity-relation decoupling helps components but struggles on long documents.

- Main contribution: a composite tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance and stance into token labels, enabling local models to capture long-range links without hand-crafted constraints.

- Main contribution: analysis of paragraph vs. essay granularity and document length effects, providing guidance on when restricted vs. general relation modeling is preferable.

- Strength: strong, relevant empirical findings that link outcomes to modeling properties (local vs. global, restricted vs. general relation space, decoupled vs. joint).

- Strength: neural models (BLCC tagger and LSTM-ER at paragraph level) match or outperform the Stab & Gurevych ILP baseline, reducing need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.

- Strength: valuable negative result showing token-level dependency parsing underperforms, with insightful analysis of causes (long sequences, global tree search).

- Strength: MTL ablations demonstrate auxiliary supervision on natural subtasks yields consistent improvements, with component supervision helping more than relation supervision.

- Strength: clear task framing, label space, dataset peculiarities, evaluation protocol, and candid discussion of limitations.

- Strength: practical guidance for practitioners about choosing restrictive tagging for long documents and entity-relation modeling for short documents.

- Weakness: experiments limited to a single dataset with PE idiosyncrasies (paragraph-contained relations, claim–major-claim links), limiting generality without cross-corpus validation.

- Weakness: fairness and completeness of baselines—dependency parsing may be a poor fit and stronger neural pipeline baselines or cross-sentence relation encoders are missing.

- Weakness: composite label set complexity grows with distance/states; paper lacks details on handling unseen/large distances and sensitivity to distance cardinality.

- Weakness: essay-level relation performance remains modest and the paper does not explore lightweight global constraints or pruning strategies to mitigate search-space explosion.

- Weakness: reproducibility details (hyperparameter tuning, pretrained embeddings, optimization schedules, code) are deferred to the supplement; training/inference cost comparisons are not reported.

- Question: how are unseen or large distance values handled at test time (bucketing, clipping, other) and how sensitive are results to distance range?

- Question: what is the impact of re-linking claims to a single major claim—how often does it change the relation graph and affect evaluation?

- Question: how were hyperparameters tuned across models to ensure fair comparison, and do ILP features/constraints match Stab & Gurevych (2016)?

- Question: was a neural pipeline baseline (BLSTM-CRF for components + relation classifier with simple global constraints) considered to isolate benefits of tagging/MTL?

- Question: for MTL, was a CRF layer added to the BL backbone to test whether auxiliary tasks still help relative to label dependency modeling?

- Question: given 92% cross-sentence relations, was replacing TreeLSTM in LSTM-ER with a cross-sentence encoder (e.g., BiLSTM over sentences) evaluated or considered to improve essay-level performance?

- Overall assessment: moderate novelty but strong empirical and analytical contributions; sound and clear work that would benefit from clarifications on distance handling, re-linking effects, and baseline fairness.
