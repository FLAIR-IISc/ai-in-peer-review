Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) from raw text with neural models, avoiding hand-crafted features and global ILP constraints. It explores four framings: token-level dependency parsing; sequence tagging with a novel label scheme that jointly encodes component BIO/type, link distance, and stance; multi-task learning (MTL) with component and relation subtasks; and an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) for AM. Experiments on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essay corpus show that sequence tagging and MTL outperform a strong ILP baseline, that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, and that the best modeling choice depends on document length.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A novel sequence-tagging formulation that jointly encodes component segmentation/typing and relation/link distance/stance at the token level, with an MTL setup that improves end-to-end AM.
2) A comprehensive neural end-to-end AM study establishing new SOTA on a standard dataset, with clear empirical guidance on when constrained local models versus general relation models (LSTM-ER) are preferable.
3) An instructive negative result: token-level dependency parsing is not effective for end-to-end AM, with analysis of why.

Strengths
- Novel, bold end-to-end framing with practical impact: Encoding relation distance and stance directly in token-level tags is a fresh idea that removes feature/constraint engineering while still injecting useful structural biases (e.g., distance distribution). The MTL design leveraging natural subtasks is simple and effective.

- Strong empirical results over a credible baseline: The sequence tagging models (especially BLCC) and MTL variants outperform the prior ILP pipeline on both components and relations (significant at p<0.01), establishing new SOTA without relying on bespoke features or global constraints.

- Actionable insights about modeling choices: The paper convincingly shows that de-coupled yet jointly modeled entities and relations help; that general relation models (LSTM-ER) excel when the number of entities is small (paragraph level) but degrade on long texts; and that constrained local models with distance-aware tags are more robust on long sequences. These findings can guide future AM system design.

- Thorough comparative study across formulations: Evaluations span token-level dependency parsing, neural tagging (with and without CRF/CNN), MTL, and LSTM-ER, with analyses of paragraph vs. essay granularity, distance distributions, and conditional relation accuracy. This breadth is valuable for the community.

- Negative result well-argued: The inefficacy of token-level dependency parsing is supported with multiple parsers, reasonable encodings, and a plausible explanation (search/complexity and long sequences), which helps steer future efforts away from a tempting but unproductive path.

Weaknesses
- Generalization limited by a single, idiosyncratic dataset: All results are on the persuasive essays corpus, which has specific structural properties (e.g., tree-like intra-paragraph structures, claims linking to all major claims, relations mostly cross-sentence). It’s unclear how the distance-augmented tagging and MTL gains transfer to other AM corpora (e.g., non-tree, many-to-many graphs, dialog, scientific discourse).

- Potential label-space explosion and data sparsity: The tag set that includes distance and stance can become large, especially with long-range links. The paper argues the observed distances are bounded in PE, but does not specify whether distances are capped/bucketed, how rare distance labels are handled, or the impact on training stability and decoding.

- Dependency parsing conclusion may be tied to design choices: The head/label encoding is one plausible design, but other encodings (e.g., component-level parsing after learned segmentation, or biaffine parsers with span representations) might fare better. Training only at paragraph level due to memory constraints complicates the claim that “token-based dependency parsing is ineffective” for end-to-end AM in general.

- Evaluation comparability and scope: The ILP baseline has access to both paragraph and essay context; neural models are mostly trained/evaluated per paragraph for some settings. While the paper acknowledges this, the comparison remains uneven. Also, it would help to report more detailed ablations (e.g., CRF vs. no-CRF within MTL, character CNN ablation) and clarity on resource release.

- Limited analysis of failure modes: While the paper provides interesting distance-based analyses, more qualitative error analysis (e.g., boundary errors vs. type confusions, stance errors, cross-paragraph link failures) would strengthen the diagnostic value and help explain where each approach breaks.

Questions to authors
- How large is the effective label set Y in practice? Do you cap or bucket distance d (e.g., beyond ±3 or ±5), and how sensitive are results to this choice? What is the frequency of rare d labels and their impact on training?

- Did you try combining the BLCC tagger (CRF + char-CNN) with the MTL setup (instead of BL without CRF/CNN)? If not, is it primarily due to implementation constraints? Do you expect further gains?

- For LSTM-ER on essays, did you try restricting candidate relation pairs by max distance or paragraph co-membership, or curriculum/negative sampling strategies to bias toward short-range links?

- How are cross-paragraph claim–major claim links treated in training and evaluation for the tagging and LSTM-ER models? Does the “re-linking” to a single MC introduce artifacts?

- Any results on other AM datasets (e.g., microtexts, web discourse) or preliminary cross-domain experiments? If not, can you comment on expected portability of the distance-augmented tagging approach to non-tree, many-to-many relation settings?

Overall, despite some limitations, this paper presents a novel and impactful direction for end-to-end AM with solid empirical evidence and useful insights. I lean toward acceptance.
