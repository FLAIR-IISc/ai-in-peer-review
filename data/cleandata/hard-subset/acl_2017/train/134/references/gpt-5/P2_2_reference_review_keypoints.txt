- First systematic study of neural end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset, evaluating multiple framings (token-level parsing, sequence tagging with composite labels, MTL, LSTM-ER) against an ILP baseline.
- Primary empirical finding: BiLSTM-CRF with char-CNN and informed composite labels (distance + stance) yields strong end-to-end performance, outperforming prior ILP joint models, especially at paragraph level.
- Secondary findings: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; MTL with component/relation auxiliary tasks improves tagging; explicit distance encoding helps relation detection; LSTM-ER excels at component detection but struggles with relations on long texts.
- Strength: thorough empirical comparison across four modeling paradigms, multiple metrics, paragraph/essay levels, and significance testing.
- Strength: achieves state-of-the-art end-to-end results with reduced feature engineering, showing off-the-shelf neural models can replace hand-crafted pipelines for AM.
- Strength: simple, effective design of encoding inter-component distance in labels injects useful inductive bias for relation prediction.
- Strength: useful analysis explaining when/why approaches (parsing, LSTM-ER, MTL) succeed or fail, offering actionable insights for future AM work.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—architectures are standard and main innovation is composite label design and empirical evaluation.
- Weakness: experiments are on a single domain (PE) with structure-specific properties, raising questions about generalization to other AM corpora.
- Weakness: token-level dependency parsing baseline may be a strawman—encoding choices likely hurt parsing; a component-level parsing baseline would be more competitive.
- Weakness: reproducibility and ablation gaps—many implementation details in supplement, missing ablations for distance encoding, handling unseen distances, class imbalance, and constraint-satisfaction reporting.
- Question: How are unseen distance values handled at train/test time (cap, UNK, bucketing, regression)?
- Question: What fraction of predicted graphs satisfy AM constraints (each premise has one parent, acyclicity/tree-likeness, claim-major claim links) across models?
- Question: Do you enforce valid BIO decoding and can you quantify common span boundary errors and the impact of char-CNN/CRF?
- Question: Did you try component-level parsing or transition-based joint span+link systems to fairly test parsing approaches?
- Question: Have you evaluated the best tagging setup on other AM datasets, or can you discuss expected generalization challenges and how distance encoding would adapt?
- Overall: clear, well-motivated, comprehensive empirical study that offers strong neural baselines and insights for AM; addressing reproducibility, ablations, and parsing baselines would strengthen the paper.
