Summary and claimed contributions
This paper investigates neural, end-to-end formulations for computational argumentation mining (AM) from raw text, comparing four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes component BIO/type, inter-component distance, and relation type, (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary subtasks, and (iv) a joint entity–relation model (LSTM-ER; Miwa & Bansal, 2016). Experiments are conducted on Stab & Gurevych’s persuasive essays (402 essays; 80 test), evaluating both paragraph- and essay-level performance with F1 for components and relations (strict and approximate span matching), and comparing to a feature-based pipeline+ILP system.

As I see it, the main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1) A thorough empirical study of several established neural formulations for end-to-end AM, with analyses on document length (paragraph vs essay) and model bias toward distance, yielding new SOTA on the PE dataset in some settings.
2) A simple yet effective sequence-tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance in the label space, and evidence that adding auxiliary tasks in an MTL setup yields modest gains.
3) A negative result suggesting token-level dependency parsing is a poor fit for end-to-end AM under the proposed encoding.

Strengths
- Comprehensive empirical comparison across multiple established neural architectures and modeling choices. The paper evaluates token-level parsing, BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging, MTL tagging, and LSTM-ER, and contrasts them against a strong ILP baseline. The analyses cover both component and relation F1 with exact and approximate matching, and include significance testing (Table 1).
- Useful, practically relevant insights about task formulation vs document length. The finding that more expressive relation models (LSTM-ER) excel on shorter texts (paragraphs) but degrade on essays due to combinatorics, whereas constrained formulations (distance-coded tagging) are more robust, is valuable guidance for practitioners.
- The distance-encoding idea provides an interpretable inductive bias. Encoding inter-component distance in the tag constrains the search space, aligns predictions with empirical distance distributions (Figure 2), and appears to help relation identification without hand-crafted constraints.
- The MTL results, while modest, are consistent with established literature. Adding natural subtasks (component and relation projections) improves performance of a weaker base tagger, indicating that decoupling entities and relations while learning jointly is beneficial in this domain.
- Clear articulation of limitations and negative results. The paper does not shy away from reporting when choices do not work (token-level dependency parsing, TreeLSTM sentence-level structures being mostly irrelevant), which increases credibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation scope: single dataset with idiosyncratic structure. All results are on one domain (student essays) where most relations are intra-paragraph and the global structure is almost a tree. Claims about generality of end-to-end neural AM and advantages over feature engineering are not substantiated beyond this dataset; no tests on microtexts or other corpora.
- Inconsistent and potentially unfair comparisons across levels and models. Some models are trained/evaluated only at paragraph level (dependency parsers, due to memory), while the ILP baseline exploits both levels; LSTM-ER underperforms at essay level but no candidate-pruning or standard efficiency techniques are attempted. The weaker MTL base tagger (BiLSTM without CRF/CNN) makes it hard to separate the effect of MTL from architectural capacity.
- Questionable modeling choices for the dependency parsing framing. The token-level head encoding (non-argumentative tokens attached to END, heads at first tokens of components) is ad hoc and departs from how argument structure is normally modeled (component-level graphs). The strong negative result may reflect the chosen representation more than an inherent unsuitability of parsing for AM. A component-level parser or a head-selection formulation on predicted spans would be a more informative baseline.
- Label space design and reporting details are under-specified. Encoding distance d in labels expands the label set; the paper does not clearly state the maximum distance modeled, how unseen or out-of-range distances are handled, or the impact of capping on performance. The duplication of relation polarity (For/Against vs Support/Attack) within the same label set is also unclear and could inflate sparsity.
- Reproducibility and evaluation concerns. Important training details (embeddings, hyperparameters, data preprocessing) are deferred to the supplement; significance testing is reported only for Table 1, with limited detail on the pairing and multiple comparisons. The “global F1” definition (F1 of C-F1 and R-F1) is non-standard. “Acc.” in the tables is undefined. The claim that re-linking claims to one major claim is “loss-less” warrants empirical justification, as it could affect relation evaluation.

Questions to authors
1) Distance labeling: What is the maximum |d| included in Y? How are labels for distances beyond this range handled at train/test time? How sensitive are results to this choice?
2) BIO validity and constraints: For the BL (non-CRF) MTL tagger, how often do you produce invalid BIO sequences or impossible label combinations? Do you post-process to enforce BIO or structural constraints?
3) LSTM-ER relation enumeration: Did you attempt standard pruning (e.g., sentence/paragraph windowing, learned mention-pair pruning) or distance-aware negatives to mitigate O(m^2) pairs at essay level? If not, can you report results with such pruning?
4) Dependency trees used in LSTM-ER: Which parser/treebank did you use to obtain dependency trees, and are they predicted or gold? Given that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, did you try document-level trees (e.g., discourse parses) or removing the TreeLSTM component?
5) Re-linking claims to one major claim: Please justify “loss-less” more rigorously. How does this transformation affect evaluation of relations involving major claims?
6) Metrics and significance: Define “Acc.” in Tables 1–2 and “global F1.” For significance testing, specify the paired units, which systems are compared, and any correction for multiple comparisons.
7) Generalization: Do you have results on another AM dataset (e.g., microtexts) to support claims of generality and to test robustness of the distance-tagging approach?

Overall, this is a solid empirical study using established neural methods. The improvements on the PE dataset are promising, but broader validation, clearer and fairer comparisons, and more rigorous specification of modeling/evaluation choices are needed before strong claims can be made.
