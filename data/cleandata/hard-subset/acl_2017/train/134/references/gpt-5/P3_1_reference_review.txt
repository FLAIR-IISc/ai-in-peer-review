Summary and claimed contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) that jointly identify argument components (major claims, claims, premises) and their typed relations (support/attack, for/against) directly from raw text, without hand-crafted features or ILP constraints. The authors explore four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with an augmented label space encoding BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance, (iii) multi-task tagging with auxiliary tasks for component and relation prediction, and (iv) adapting LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) to AM. Experiments on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays corpus compare paragraph- and essay-level settings to prior ILP-based joint models.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A systematic empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end AM, showing strong results for sequence tagging and MTL, and identifying failures of token-level dependency parsing and limitations of LSTM-ER on long documents.
2) A simple yet effective joint sequence-tagging scheme that encodes relation distances and stance in labels, yielding state-of-the-art or better-than-ILP performance on the PE dataset, especially at paragraph level.
3) Insightful analysis of document length and structural constraints: neural models implicitly learn tree-like structures; more constrained decodings help when entity counts are large; MTL with natural subtasks improves performance.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated exploration of alternative neural formalisms for end-to-end AM. The paper is valuable as a comparative study: it covers local sequence tagging, token-level parsing, MTL, and a joint entity–relation model (LSTM-ER) adapted to AM. The breadth helps the community understand trade-offs across modeling choices rather than proposing a single black-box model.

- Strong empirical performance and meaningful baselines. The proposed sequence tagging approach (BLCC) and the MTL variants substantially outperform the feature-based ILP system on paragraph-level evaluation and are competitive on essay-level. The use of exact and approximate span metrics and relation F1 is appropriate, and significance testing across documents is reported.

- Thoughtful analysis of length and constraints. The paper convincingly argues and empirically shows that (a) long documents harm models that consider all pairwise relations (LSTM-ER), (b) locally constrained codings (distance and single-head biases) help in long sequences, and (c) token-level dependency parsing is brittle given long sequences and global search complexity. The distance distribution analysis and the comparison of conditional correctness by distance are informative.

- Multi-task learning improves tagging. The MTL setups using auxiliary component and relation prediction yield consistent gains, suggesting that decoupling yet jointly training subproblems is beneficial. The result aligns with broader MTL literature and provides a practical recipe for AM.

- Reproducibility and fair acknowledgment of limitations. The paper points to code/parameters (supplementary), uses standard architectures (Ma & Hovy, Miwa & Bansal), and is transparent about when paragraph-level training/testing is used due to memory constraints.

Weaknesses
- Token-level dependency parsing setup likely understates parsing’s potential. The conclusion that “AM as dependency parsing is ineffective” is drawn from a token-level encoding that forces each token in a component to attach to a head token. This design introduces noisy, repeated edges and exacerbates long-range tree search. A stronger and more natural baseline would parse over components (spans) rather than tokens, with a separate segmentation stage (or a neural joint span+tree model). Without evaluating a component-level parser, the negative result risks being a strawman.

- Label space explosion and sparsity in the tagging approach. Encoding BIO × component type × distance × stance into a single label yields a large label space and data sparsity, especially for long-range distances and rare stance combinations. The paper argues distances are mostly short, but does not report label cardinality, frequency cutoffs, bucketing/clipping strategies, or ablations (e.g., distance bucketing, predicting distance and stance with separate heads). The MTL experiments partially address this, but use a weaker backbone (BL without CRF/char CNN), making it hard to disentangle gains due to MTL vs. architecture.

- Fairness of comparisons on essay level and use of structural signals. The ILP system leverages paragraph/essay structure and hand-crafted global constraints; neural models could incorporate simple structural priors (paragraph indices, sentence boundaries, positional encodings) but apparently do not. Conversely, the LSTM-ER relation module uses TreeLSTMs over sentence dependency trees, yet most gold relations are cross-sentence, which likely hurts it. Ablations that disable the tree module or add cross-sentence structures would clarify whether LSTM-ER’s weakness is due to misaligned inductive bias rather than inherent shortcomings of entity–relation factorization.

- Gold structure adaptation and decoding details are unclear. For parsing, the authors “re-link” claims to a single major claim to satisfy tree constraints, but the original gold has claims connected to all major claims. How is this many-to-many structure restored for evaluation? For tagging, how are distance predictions converted into edges when multiple candidates share the same distance, and how are stance labels (For/Against vs Support/Attack) mapped across component types? These decoding choices could materially affect results.

- Limited generalization: single dataset and modest error analysis. The conclusions are drawn entirely from the PE corpus, which has particular structural regularities (tree-like per paragraph, limited long-range distances). It is unclear whether the distance-augmented tagging would remain robust for domains with more cross-paragraph links or non-tree structures (e.g., web debates, legal opinions). The error analysis is brief; more detailed breakdowns (span boundary errors, type confusions, stance confusions, distance mispredictions) would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors
1) Dependency parsing: Did you attempt a component-level parsing baseline (e.g., segment first with a neural tagger, then parse spans), or a joint span+tree model? How do your conclusions change if nodes are components rather than tokens?

2) Decoding from sequence tags: Please detail how you recover component boundaries, heads, and relation edges from the (b, t, d, s) labels. How do you resolve ambiguities when multiple targets match the same distance? How are For/Against vs Support/Attack assigned across component types?

3) Label space and distance: What is the total number of unique labels observed during training? Did you clip/bucket distances, and if so, what thresholds? Did you try predicting distance and stance via separate heads (multitask within a strong BLCC backbone) instead of a monolithic label?

4) LSTM-ER ablations: Given that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, did you try removing the TreeLSTM or replacing it with document-level structures (e.g., BiLSTM with attention over detected entities, or discourse/sentence graph encoders)? How much does the sentence-tree module contribute?

5) Structural signals: Did you try adding simple features like paragraph index, sentence position, or absolute token positions to neural models, especially on essay level? If not, could this close the gap with ILP on major claims?

6) Evaluation alignment: When you “re-link” claims to a single major claim for parsing, how do you map back to the original multi-headed gold for evaluation? Is there any loss or bias introduced?

7) Generalization: Have you tested your models on other AM datasets (e.g., microtexts, web debate corpora)? If not, can you comment on expected behavior where relations are more non-local or non-tree-like?
