Summary and main contributions
- The paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to argumentation mining (AM) from plain text on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays corpus, covering component segmentation/typing and relation identification/typing, and compares four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, inter-component distance, and stance, (iii) multi-task tagging with auxiliary subtasks (components, relations), and (iv) an adapted LSTM-ER model (Miwa & Bansal) that jointly extracts entities and relations. It also compares against a strong feature-based pipeline+ILP baseline.
- Primary contribution: a simple neural sequence tagging formulation (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) with distance-coded labels that achieves strong end-to-end performance and, on paragraph-level, matches or exceeds the ILP baseline; adding MTL with component/relation auxiliary tasks further improves results. This is a practical, feature-free recipe that generalizes over long ranges and exploits learned biases in distance.
- Secondary contributions: (a) a careful empirical study showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM compared to tagging (large performance drop, especially on essays), with plausible explanations about global search complexity and long sequences; (b) a nuanced analysis of document length and constraint effects: LSTM-ER shines on components and on short documents but degrades on relations at essay-level due to quadratic candidate growth; the tagging formulation’s built-in one-link constraint and distance coding biases it toward the distribution observed in real data.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advances and insightful comparisons across formalisms. The work does not just propose a model; it positions four families (global tree, local tagger, MTL tagger, joint entity–relation) and explains when/why each helps. The length-based analysis (paragraph vs essay) and distance-based analysis (Figure 4) provide concrete evidence: e.g., at paragraph level, LSTM-ER outperforms all on both components and relations, but at essay level, the tagging model has higher overall F1 due to substantially better R-F1.
- Strong, feature-free baselines and fair attention to structure. The BLCC tagger with composite labels is well-motivated for end-to-end AM, handles long-range dependencies, and eliminates hand-crafted features and ILP constraints. The MTL variants add interpretable auxiliary tasks (components and relations) and demonstrate consistent gains (e.g., Y-3 to Y-3:YC-3 increases C-F1 and R-F1), supporting the claim that decoupled supervision for substructures helps.
- Negative result with justification. The token-level dependency parsing framing is carefully attempted with multiple parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser&Goldberg, Dyer et al.). The poor performance, especially on essays, is plausibly tied to the global, combinatorial nature of tree search over long sequences and mismatch between token-level parsing and span-level argument structures. This is valuable guidance for the community.
- Statistical testing and multiple granularities. The paper reports significance (Wilcoxon on document-level scores) and evaluates on both paragraph and essay levels, highlighting how granularity affects candidate space and learning difficulty. This kind of rigorous, multi-view evaluation is a strength for an end-to-end AM study.
- Practicality and clarity of modeling choices. Encoding distances in labels is simple and empirically effective (distance distribution supports short-range bias); constraining one outgoing link per component in the tagging formulation reduces combinatorics and seems crucial on long texts. The discussion relates constraints to learned behavior, not just hard-coding.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset and potential overfitting to a specific annotation scheme/constraints. All conclusions are drawn on a single corpus with near-tree structures and special properties (e.g., each premise has exactly one parent; claims connect to all major claims but are “re-linked”). The tagging formulation bakes in a one-link assumption and distance labels tied to this scheme. It is unclear how well the approach would generalize to other AM corpora with non-tree or many-to-many graphs, different component inventories, or noisier discourse (e.g., web forums).
- Label space sparsity and scalability concerns. The composite tag space includes a distance dimension with values observed from -11 to +10. While the authors argue finiteness in practice, this still risks sparsity and data inefficiency, especially beyond this dataset. There is no ablation on clipping/bucketing distances, nor an analysis of how performance changes as the maximum |d| increases or for unseen distances.
- Dependency parsing framing may be a strawman. The token-level head assignment (e.g., non-argumentative tokens attach to END) produces highly unnatural trees with long arcs and span-level semantics encoded at token granularity. Given prior AM successes at component-level parsing, this negative result may reflect the chosen representation more than an inherent limitation of parsing for AM. Span-based parsing or constituent-style formulations might be more appropriate but are not explored.
- Some evaluation/reporting clarity issues. The ILP line in Table 1 is hard to reconcile with separate paragraph/essay settings; it is unclear whether the shown ILP numbers are paragraph-level, essay-level, or best-of-both, and how the comparison is stratified. Parsers are trained only at paragraph-level due to memory; this reduces comparability on the full end-to-end scenario at essay-level. More details in the main text (not just supplement) on hyperparameters, pretrained embeddings, and LSTM-ER adaptation would improve reproducibility.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and ablations. Beyond the informative length/distance plots and MTL layer-placement variants, the paper lacks ablations for key design choices: CRF vs no-CRF, character CNN impact, removing/altering distance coding, alternative stance coding, or restricting LSTM-ER candidate pairs. Error analysis is mentioned as supplementary but would be valuable in the main text.

Questions to authors
- How do you cap or bucket distances d in practice? What is the maximum |d| allowed during training/inference, and how sensitive are results to this choice? Have you tried distance bucketing to reduce label sparsity?
- Are paragraph boundaries assumed available at test time (they are, in this dataset)? How would the tagging and LSTM-ER approaches behave without paragraph segmentation cues (e.g., on continuous text)?
- For LSTM-ER, did you restrict candidate relations by distance or heuristic pruning at essay-level? If so, how does that affect the observed R-F1 drop? If not, could lightweight pruning close the gap?
- Could a span-based formulation (predict spans first, then relations) narrow the gap between component- and token-level parsing, and would it mitigate the token-level parsing issues you observed?
- How exactly were the ILP baselines tuned in your environment, and do the numbers in Table 1 correspond to paragraph- or essay-level evaluation (or both)?

Overall, this is a solid, timely empirical study with actionable insights. Despite limitations in generality and some design choices, the paper provides clear evidence that simple neural tagging with distance coding and MTL is a strong end-to-end AM baseline, and documents important failure modes for alternative framings.
