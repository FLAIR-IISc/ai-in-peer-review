Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) on the PE dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) with neural methods that avoid handcrafted features and ILP constraints. Four framings are compared: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging (BIO) with labels that also encode relation distance and stance; (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) using auxiliary subtasks for components/relations; and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER entity-relation model. An ILP pipeline baseline is included.
- Primary contribution: a strong neural sequence tagging baseline that encodes component types and relation distances, outperforming the ILP system on paragraph and performing competitively on essay level; plus analysis showing dependency parsing at token level underperforms.
- Secondary contribution: MTL with auxiliary component/relationship tasks provides consistent gains over a single-task tagger.
- Tertiary contribution: empirical analysis of document length effects and distance biases on relation identification; and that LSTM-ER is strong for components but brittle for relations, especially in longer texts.

Strengths
- Broad and systematic comparison across modeling paradigms. The work does not just propose a single model but explores four reasonable framings, yielding useful empirical insights (e.g., sequence taggers scale better with sequence length than token-level parsers).
- Strong empirical performance and analyses. The sequence tagging with distance coding (BLCC) and LSTM-ER both beat or match the ILP baseline in several settings; the analysis of distance distributions and length effects is informative (Figure 2, Figure 4).
- Sensible architectural insight. Decoupling components from relations helps (LSTM-ER and MTL variants), but some constraint/bias (e.g., distance) is crucial to avoid combinatorial relation explosion in longer documents.
- Task framing novelty in practice. Encoding relation distance and stance into the tag space is a simple, general trick that proves robust and may transfer to other structured IE settings.
- Useful observations on when tree/stack-LSTM parsing is ill-suited (token-level trees over very long sequences; inter-sentential relations), which can inform future AM design choices.

Weaknesses
- Label-space design and reproducibility are under-specified. The tag set includes a distance d that is “potentially infinite,” but practical training requires a finite, known tag vocabulary. It is unclear whether distances were capped/bucketed, how unseen distances at test time are handled, and whether this leads to severe sparsity. The CRF must model transitions over the full 4-tuple; the paper does not specify constraints ensuring consistency within a component (e.g., that I labels share t,d,s with the preceding B). This affects reproducibility and interpretability.
- Ambiguity about relation types and evaluation scope. The label s mixes Support/Attack with For/Against. The dataset has premise-to-(claim/premise) links (support/attack) and claim-to-major-claim stances (for/against). The paper “loss-lessly” re-links claims to a single major claim for tree construction, which changes the original graph. It is not clear whether evaluation includes claim–MC links, how re-linking affects gold, and whether ILP/neural systems are compared on identical relational targets.
- Fairness/comparability of setups. Some models are trained/evaluated on paragraphs due to memory limits, others on essays; Table 1 shows mixed paragraph/essay numbers, and the ILP row’s level is unclear, despite the claim “ILP operates on both levels.” Dependency parsers are dismissed as ineffective but were not run on essays (“too memory-exhaustive”). The LSTM-ER’s poor essay-level R-F1 is attributed to O(m^2) pairs and length, but its TreeLSTM depends on intra-sentence dependency paths (admitted to be ineffective for inter-sentence relations); this confound is not disentangled. Overall, conclusions about paradigms appear influenced by differing feasibility constraints rather than like-for-like comparisons.
- Evaluation clarity and statistics. “Global F1” is defined as “the F1-score of C-F1 and R-F1,” but it is unclear whether this is the harmonic mean; this is non-standard. The “Acc.” metric is not defined (token-level BIO accuracy?). Significance testing is reported (Wilcoxon, p < 0.01), but there is no correction for multiple comparisons across many columns, nor a clear statement whether per-document F1s are used for pairing at paragraph level. These details matter with small test sets.
- Missing ablations and alternatives. The MTL experiments use a weaker BL tagger (no CRF, no char CNN), so the gains from MTL are confounded with a weaker base. A CRF-based MTL should be tested. The sequence tagger’s distance coding could be compared against simpler bucketing strategies or pointer-style decoders. The dependency parsing framing lacks critical design details (e.g., intra-component head assignment, decoding constraints, handling of non-projective edges post re-linking).
- Presentation and formatting issues. Numerous typos and inconsistent references (e.g., “dection,” “seperately,” inconsistent citation formatting with “.,?” placeholders), and equation/label notation inconsistencies (e.g., “Supp,Att, For,Ag”). Figures are referenced but not fully described; some claims (supplementary material links) are not verifiable here. These detract from clarity and suggest insufficient polishing.

Questions to authors
- Label space: What exact set of distance values d is used at training/test time? Are distances capped or bucketed? How are unseen distances handled? Do you provide the final tag vocabulary?
- Decoding constraints: How do you enforce within-component label consistency in STagT (same t,d,s across a span)? Is any constraint decoding used with the CRF?
- Relation scope: Are both premise→(claim/premise) and claim→MC links evaluated in R-F1? How does the “loss-less re-linking” of claims to a single MC affect gold and evaluation? Please clarify for both paragraph and essay settings.
- LSTM-ER: How is inter-sentential relation modeling handled, given TreeLSTM relies on sentence-level dependency trees? Did you augment with discourse/sentence-level structures?
- Table 1 clarity: Which rows/columns correspond to paragraph vs essay level for ILP and LSTM-ER? Were all systems trained and tested on identical splits and text units?
- Global F1 and Acc: Precisely define “Global F1” and “Acc.”; describe the statistical testing protocol (unit of analysis, corrections).

Additional comments
- Consider an MTL setup with a CRF and char-CNN backbone to decouple MTL benefits from backbone weaknesses.
- Provide full hyperparameters, embeddings, and code to ensure reproducibility.
- Reassess the “first neural end-to-end AM” claim in light of contemporary pointer-network work (Potash et al., 2017).

Overall recommendation: While the empirical insights are valuable and some results are strong, the paper has substantial methodological ambiguities, comparability issues, and presentation problems. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can clarify setups, strengthen fairness in comparisons, and polish the presentation.
