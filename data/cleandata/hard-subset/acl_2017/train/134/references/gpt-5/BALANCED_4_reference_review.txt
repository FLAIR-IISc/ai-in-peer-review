Summary and contributions
The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) from raw text with neural methods. It compares four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, relation distance, and stance (STagT), (iii) multi-task sequence tagging (MTL) with component and relation subtasks, and (iv) LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal), which jointly models entities and relations. Experiments on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus evaluate both paragraph-level and essay-level settings and compare against a strong feature-based ILP baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A comprehensive empirical comparison establishing strong neural end-to-end baselines for AM, with new state-of-the-art results on PE, and careful analysis of how document length affects relation extraction.
2) A practical tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance within labels, enabling a simple, effective, and length-robust end-to-end approach; plus evidence that MTL with component/relationship subtasks improves performance.
3) A negative result showing token-level dependency parsing is ill-suited for end-to-end AM on long inputs, along with analysis explaining its failure modes.

Strengths
- Strong, well-supported empirical results vs. a carefully implemented ILP baseline:
  • On paragraph-level, STagBLCC and LSTM-ER outperform ILP on both components and relations; on essay-level, STagBLCC surpasses ILP, with significance testing (Wilcoxon, p<0.01).
  • The paper reports both exact- and approximate-span F1 for components and relations, and a “global” F1, giving a balanced picture. The advantage of STagBLCC over best dependency parser (e.g., +~13 C-F1 and +~11 R-F1 on paragraphs) is substantial.

- Breadth and clarity of experimental design and analyses:
  • Parallel evaluation at paragraph vs. essay level reveals how sequence length and number of entities drive the precision/recall trade-offs, especially for relation extraction.
  • The distance-distribution analysis (Fig. 2, Fig. 4) grounds the tagging design and explains why distance-aware tagging performs robustly and why LSTM-ER degrades on long documents.
  • Statistical testing across documents improves the reliability of claims.

- Insightful negative result for token-level dependency parsing:
  • Multiple parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser, LSTM-Parser) are examined; results and qualitative analysis (overlong spans, sensitivity to long sequences) are convincing and useful to the community.

- Multi-task learning improves end-to-end tagging:
  • Adding component and relation auxiliary tasks to the main tagging task yields consistent gains, highlighting a simple recipe for boosting sequence tagging in AM without manual features or ILP constraints.

- Reduction of feature engineering and constraint design:
  • The neural models eliminate hand-crafted feature sets and ILP constraint engineering, important given annotation scheme variability across AM corpora.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: most architectures are established (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, LSTM-ER, MTL). The main technical novelty is the tagging label design (BIO, type, distance, stance) and the comprehensive study. While valuable, the methodological advance beyond prior neural IE/tagging work is limited.

- Tag-set explosion and decoding details are under-specified:
  • Encoding distance and stance into token labels can lead to a large, sparse label space; the paper argues distances are bounded in practice but does not report the realized label cardinality, whether distances are capped, or how rare labels are handled.
  • It is unclear how token-level predictions are consolidated into component spans and single relations: what if tokens in a predicted component disagree on (d, s)? What postprocessing enforces that each premise links to exactly one component? These choices could materially affect results.

- The dependency parsing framing may be a strawman at token level:
  • Mapping AM to token-heads (e.g., linking every token in a component to the first token of its target) creates long-range, non-sentential trees and non-projective structures; this likely handicaps parsers compared to more natural component-level parsing (with span identification). A fairer test would parse over detected spans (possibly jointly with span detection).

- Evaluation limited to a single dataset and re-linking heuristic:
  • Results are only on PE; generalization to other AM schemes (e.g., microtexts, legal) is untested. Given the known variability of AM annotation, cross-corpus validation would strengthen claims.
  • To enforce a tree, the paper “re-links” each claim to one major claim, despite claims linking to all major claims in the gold. Even if “loss-less” for some downstream metrics, this changes the gold structure and may bias parsing/tagging comparably; more justification or sensitivity analysis is needed.

- MTL experiments use a weaker base tagger:
  • The MTL model (BL without CRF/char-CNN) underperforms the main STagBLCC. It is unclear whether the reported MTL gains would persist (or grow) when using the stronger BLCC architecture, which would be the more relevant practical setting.

- Missing experimental details:
  • No clear description of dev set, hyperparameter selection, variance/seed sensitivity, or decoding procedures. Some is deferred to the supplement; key elements should appear in the main paper for reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Tag set and decoding:
  • What is the size of Y realized in training (unique (b,t,d,s) labels)? Did you cap distance |d|, or backoff rare distances?
  • How do you reconstruct component-level structures from token labels? Specifically, how are conflicts resolved when tokens within a predicted span disagree on (d,s), and how do you ensure each premise has exactly one outgoing relation?
  • Do you apply any global constraints at decoding time for STagT (e.g., one-parent, acyclicity), or only local postprocessing?

- Re-linking and evaluation:
  • Please justify the claim that re-linking claims to a single major claim is “loss-less.” Which metrics are invariant to this change? Did you test sensitivity to which major claim is chosen?

- MTL and architecture choices:
  • Why not run MTL with the BLCC base (char-CNN + CRF)? Do the observed gains transfer?
  • Which auxiliary task configurations were most stable across seeds? Any analysis of when YR helps vs. hurts?

- LSTM-ER details:
  • Did you constrain LSTM-ER during decoding to enforce the one-parent property for premises or prefer short distances? If not, could light constraints or a distance-aware prior narrow the essay-level gap?
  • What dependency trees were used for TreeLSTM, and did you assess performance without TreeLSTM given that most relations are cross-sentence?

- Reproducibility and scope:
  • Please include dev split, tuning protocol, number of runs and variance. Will you release code, configs, and trained models?
  • Could you report results on another AM corpus (e.g., microtexts) or cross-domain transfer to support generality?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper that advances end-to-end AM with neural methods and provides actionable insights for practitioners. Addressing the decoding, re-linking, and MTL-architecture questions—and, if possible, adding a component-level parsing baseline—would strengthen the work further. I lean toward acceptance given the breadth and clarity of the study and its practical impact on AM.
