Summary and claimed contributions
This paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) corpus. The authors compare four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing with trees over all tokens; (ii) sequence tagging with a compound label that jointly encodes BIO span, component type, relation distance (in number of components), and stance; (iii) multi-task sequence tagging where component-only and relation-only taggers act as auxiliary tasks; and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa and Bansal’s LSTM-ER that decouples entity (component) tagging and relation classification. They evaluate on both paragraph and essay levels and compare to an ILP-based joint model with hand-crafted features.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) Empirical finding: a strong, simple neural sequence tagging baseline (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) with a distance-augmented label space robustly outperforms prior feature-based ILP pipeline on PE and beats token-level dependency parsers by large margins; LSTM-ER excels on short (paragraph) contexts but degrades for relations on long (essay) contexts.
2) Methodological insight: decoupling component and relation prediction but modeling them jointly is beneficial; multi-tasking with natural subtasks (components, relations) yields small but consistent gains.
3) Negative result (important): token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM is ineffective, with severe degradation on long sequences, suggesting global tree constraints at token level are ill-suited for this task.

Strengths
- Clear end-to-end framing and thorough comparative study. The paper moves beyond pipelines with hand-engineered features and evaluates multiple neural formulations under identical data and metrics. The split between paragraph vs. essay makes the long-context challenge explicit; the results are consistent and well-motivated.
- Strong baselines and fair comparisons on components/relations. The sequence tagging model (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) with joint labels markedly outperforms the best neural parser (e.g., ~+13pp C-F1 and ~+11pp R-F1 at paragraph level) and beats the ILP model on paragraph-level evaluation, while LSTM-ER achieves the strongest paragraph-level results overall. The analysis connecting relation F1 to component F1 (conditional accuracies) is useful.
- Insightful analysis of distance/length effects. Encoding relation distance in the tag set biases the model toward short links and reduces the O(m^2) relation search space that hinders LSTM-ER at essay level; the paper quantifies distance distributions and shows differential behavior across models (Fig. 4).
- Useful negative result on token-level dependency parsing. The paper carefully designs a token-level dependency encoding, evaluates several parsers (including strong neural parsers), and shows a dramatic drop on essays, plausibly due to the enormous search space and mismatch between token-level trees and component-level structures.
- Practical implications for AM system design. The guidance—use general relation models only when the number of entities is small; otherwise prefer restrained modeling—feels actionable for practitioners building document-level AM systems.

Weaknesses
- Generalization beyond a single, specialized dataset is unclear. The PE corpus has near-tree, paragraph-contained structures and fixed roles (MC, C, P). The sequence tagging formulation depends on (a) tree-like assumptions and (b) distance distributions; performance and even label set feasibility on datasets with cross-paragraph, many-to-many links (legal, debate forums, scientific papers) are not shown. The paper would benefit from at least one additional corpus, or stronger discussion of portability and expected failure modes.
- The token-level dependency encoding and re-linking to major claims are arguably unnatural. Collapsing component-level graphs into token-level trees forces head choices (e.g., “first token of the target”) and re-links claims to a single MC to maintain a tree. This may distort supervision and limits the fairness of concluding that “dependency parsing is ineffective” for AM writ large; span-level or component-level parsing with graph constraints could be more appropriate.
- The compound label space raises sparsity/scalability concerns. The label Y includes BIO×type×distance×stance. While distances up to ±10 are observed, the approach hinges on the empirical distribution staying compact. The paper lacks ablations on maximum distance cutoffs, label cardinality, or backing off distance (e.g., bucketing). It’s unclear how the method scales to documents with longer-range links or different discourse styles.
- Limited exploration of stronger structured/neural baselines. The MTL setup uses a simpler BiLSTM without CRF/CNN, making it hard to isolate whether gains stem from multi-tasking vs. base model strength. For LSTM-ER, simple pruning (e.g., within-paragraph candidate pairs), explicit distance features, or paragraph-aware batching might mitigate the essay-level degradation but are not tried. Likewise, span-based neural models (predicting spans then relations) or neural-ILP hybrids could test whether “global constraints help” without forcing token-level trees.
- Decoding and constraint handling are under-specified. The paper does not clearly describe how token-level distance and stance tags are converted into component-level edges at inference time, how invalid tag sequences are resolved, whether constraints (e.g., “each premise has one parent”) are enforced post hoc, or how multiple MC links are reconstructed after training with re-linking.

Questions to authors
1) How exactly do you decode relations from sequence tags? Given tags on tokens, how do you form component spans, aggregate per-token distance/stance into a single edge, and resolve conflicts (e.g., multiple distances within a span)? Are any constraints enforced post hoc (e.g., one outgoing edge per premise), and how are conflicting links broken?
2) What is the effective size of the label set Y during training on PE (number of unique labels seen)? Did you cap |d| or bucket large distances? How sensitive are results to a distance cap, and what happens if distance is removed or coarsened?
3) For LSTM-ER, did you try basic candidate pruning (e.g., only pairs within the same paragraph, or within a small component-distance window), or adding distance as an explicit feature? This seems likely to reduce the O(m^2) search at essay level and might close the relation gap.
4) How is the re-linking of claims to a single major claim handled at training and evaluation time for models that enforce trees? Does this change the supervision signal for models that otherwise could support multi-headed claims? Any measured impact?
5) Can you comment on runtime/memory and training stability across settings (essay vs. paragraph), and provide hyperparameter search ranges? This is important for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation: The paper delivers a solid empirical study with actionable insights and new state-of-the-art results on PE. Despite concerns about generality and some methodological choices, I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying decoding/constraints and discussing generalization.
