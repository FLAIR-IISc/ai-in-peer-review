Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays corpus. It compares several framings: token-level dependency parsing; sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO spans, component type, distance to linked component, and stance; multi-task learning where auxiliary tasks for components and relations are added; and an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER model (joint entity/relation extraction). The models are evaluated at paragraph and essay levels against a feature-rich ILP pipeline baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order):
1) Empirical finding that neural end-to-end AM is competitive or superior to a feature-based ILP pipeline, with especially strong results for sequence tagging and LSTM-ER (paragraph level), and insights into when restrained vs general relation modeling helps (short vs long documents).
2) A simple yet effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance and stance in the label, plus evidence that adding natural subtasks in an MTL setup improves results.
3) A negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM compared to tagging, with plausible analysis of why (global search complexity, long sequences).

Strengths
- Comprehensive, well-motivated comparison across framings: The paper does not just present one model; it systematically contrasts four families (parsing, tagging, MTL-tagging, LSTM-ER) under a shared evaluation protocol. This breadth yields actionable insights (e.g., local taggers cope better with long documents than global parsers; general relation models excel when entity counts are small).

- Solid empirical gains and fair baselines: On paragraph level, STagBLCC and LSTM-ER outperform the strong ILP pipeline on both component and relation F1. On essay level, the tagging approach retains an advantage for relations. The inclusion of both exact and approximate span matching, and significance testing for main comparisons, strengthens claims.

- Simple yet effective modeling choices: The distance- and stance-augmented label space is a clean way to inject structural biases (single parent, short-range preference) into a local tagger without hand-crafted features or global decoding. The MTL setup, using component and relation projections as auxiliary tasks, is intuitive and empirically beneficial.

- Clear analysis of failure modes: The discussion on why LSTM-ER’s relation extraction degrades on essays (quadratic relation space, mild distance preference) versus the tagging model’s constrained search is convincing. Similarly, the argument for why token-level parsing struggles (huge tree space, long sequences) is plausible and supported by results.

- Relevance and timeliness: End-to-end neural AM is of substantial interest, and the paper offers state-of-the-art results on a standard dataset, removing reliance on hand-engineered features and constraints.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset and domain generality: All experiments are on a single corpus of student essays with specific structural peculiarities (e.g., paragraph-local structures, tree-like relations, predictable positions of major claims). It is unclear how well the proposed tag schema and conclusions transfer to other AM settings (e.g., web discourse, legal, multi-party dialogs, non-tree graphs). A second dataset or cross-domain test would strengthen claims.

- Potential label space sparsity and scalability: Encoding distance in the tag creates a large, potentially sparse label space, especially at essay level where distances span up to ±10. The paper notes observed distances but does not quantify the resulting tag vocabulary, unseen distance handling at test time, or sensitivity to distance clipping; this affects robustness and reproducibility.

- Token-level dependency parsing as a strawman: Mapping AM to token-level head selection (including all O tokens attached to END) seems mismatched to typical component-level graphs; unsurprisingly, span prediction suffers. A more competitive parsing baseline would operate at component level (detected or jointly inferred) or use span-based parsers, to separate weaknesses due to the modeling level from those inherent to parsing.

- Reproducibility and reporting gaps: Many practical details (embeddings, hyperparameters, distance cutoffs, CRF decoding constraints, MTL layer selection strategy) are relegated to the supplement. The main text could report more ablations (e.g., tagger without distance; with distance only; with stance only) to isolate what drives the gains. Some results show high variance (essay-level parser), but variance estimates/confidence intervals are not reported.

- Constraint handling and fairness nuances: The ILP baseline globally enforces validity constraints; the neural taggers rely on learned biases and local decoding, yet the essay vs paragraph comparison is not entirely symmetric (e.g., LSTM-ER sees all entity pairs; tagger hard-codes single-parent). It would help to report how often neural outputs violate structural constraints and whether light post-hoc constraints could close remaining gaps.

Questions to authors
- How large is the effective tag set Y in training and test (number of unique labels)? How do you handle unseen distances at test time? Did you clip distances, and if so, at what threshold?

- What is the incremental contribution of each tag component? Please report ablations: BIO+type only; +distance; +stance; distance bucketization vs exact counts.

- For MTL, how were layer choices selected? Did you try sharing only lower layers or using task-specific CRFs? Any training instabilities when combining tasks?

- For LSTM-ER, given that 92% of relations are cross-sentence, did you try a variant without dependency-tree TreeLSTMs (sequence-only) or using document-level syntactic/semantic structures?

- Can you report constraint violations (e.g., multiple parents per premise, cycles) for each neural method? Would simple post-processing (greedy pruning) improve R-F1 at essay level?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a component-level parsing baseline (e.g., span proposal followed by tree decoding) to better assess the “parsing vs tagging” conclusion.

- Report macro-averaged scores per component/stance type and per distance bucket to further validate the distance encoding’s benefit.

- Discuss computational efficiency and training/inference time across framings, especially as document length and number of entities increase.

- A brief qualitative error analysis (e.g., typical false links, stance confusions) would make the insights more concrete.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a valuable, well-executed empirical study with practical takeaways for end-to-end AM, despite some limitations in generality and analysis depth. Addressing the questions and adding key ablations would strengthen the contribution.
