Summary and main contributions
This paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural approaches. It compares several framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a novel label scheme encoding component boundaries, types, inter-component distances, and stance, (iii) multi-task tagging with auxiliary sub-tasks for components and relations, and (iv) a neural entity-relation model (LSTM-ER). Experiments on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset evaluate both paragraph- and essay-level prediction against a strong feature-based ILP system.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, effective neural sequence tagging formulation for end-to-end AM that encodes relation distance in the tag, yielding state-of-the-art results over a strong ILP baseline and other neural framings, and offering robust behavior across paragraph/essay settings.
2) A systematic empirical comparison of alternative neural framings (local tagging, global parsing, multi-task, and entity-relation modeling) with clear methodological takeaways about when constrained vs. general relation modeling is preferable (short vs. long documents).
3) A negative/diagnostic result showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, clarifying the limits of tree-structured global modeling at token granularity in this domain.

Strengths
- Strong, reproducible empirical study with clear takeaways: The paper compares multiple well-known neural architectures adapted to AM and a strong ILP baseline, across two granularity levels (paragraph vs. essay). The findings are consistent and actionable: local constrained tagging excels on long documents; more general entity-relation modeling is competitive on short documents; MTL helps. Significance tests are provided.
- Novel yet simple tagging formulation: Encoding relation distance within a BIO-style tag is an elegant way to support end-to-end detection of components and long-range relations without heavy feature engineering or global inference. It materially improves relation prediction by biasing toward realistic distances and constraining premises to a single parent.
- Careful analysis of document length effects: The paper convincingly explains the gap between paragraph- and essay-level performance and attributes the R-F1 drop of LSTM-ER to the quadratic growth of candidate relations; the distance calibration plot further supports the role of explicit distance modeling.
- MTL insights: Demonstrates that adding component and relation sub-tasks improves the full task despite a weaker base tagger (no CRF/char-CNN), and that auxiliary “component” is more helpful than “relation.” This is practically useful for designing AM systems with limited capacity.
- Candid and informative negative result on token-level parsing: The study tries several state-of-the-art parsers; the analysis (span boundary errors, stack-history benefits, and exacerbated failures with long sequences) provides useful guidance and cautions against token-level tree constraints for AM.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset scope and reliance on PE-specific structure: All conclusions are drawn from a single corpus with strong structural regularities (tree-like, relations contained within paragraphs, claims link to all major claims). The proposed tag constraints (single parent, distance-driven relations) and the re-linking of claims to a single major claim may not generalize to other AM corpora (microtexts, debates, legal opinions) with cross-paragraph links and many-to-many relations.
- Dependency parsing framing may be a strawman: Operating at token level likely handicaps parsers in ways that are known suboptimal for discourse/AM. A stronger alternative is a two-stage neural model (segment components neurally, then parse a component graph), potentially trained jointly or with latent components. The negative result thus diagnoses “token-level parsing,” not “parsing for AM” more broadly.
- Fairness of LSTM-ER adaptation: The TreeLSTM relation module uses sentence-level syntactic trees, but 92% of relations are cross-sentence; this undercuts the model’s strengths. Document-level structural signals (e.g., paragraph-level trees, discourse graphs, or hierarchical encoders) would be a more faithful adaptation. The reported underperformance on essay-level relations may partly reflect this mismatch.
- Missing ablations and training details in the main text: Key modeling choices are not dissected: impact of distance encoding (e.g., capping/max |d|, label set size explosion), contribution of CRF vs. char-CNN vs. BiLSTM, effects of scheduled sampling/pretraining in LSTM-ER, and robustness to OOV. Important implementation details are deferred to supplementary material.
- Metric and error analysis could be deeper: Results are primarily macro F1s. Additional breakdowns (by component type, by distance bins, by paragraph position, cross-/intra-sentence) would strengthen the analysis and help validate the claimed mechanisms (e.g., whether distance encoding improves long-distance links or mainly short ones).

Questions to authors
1) Distance-tagging details: What is the maximum absolute distance |d| encoded in labels? Do you cap distances or backoff for rare/large |d|? How large is the resulting tag set Y on PE, and how sensitive is performance to this size?
2) Re-linking claims to a single major claim: Please clarify the “loss-less” re-linking procedure and whether it affects evaluation. Does this transformation bias training/inference or evaluation of relations involving major claims?
3) MTL architecture: Which layer configurations yielded the best gains (exact layer indices for main/aux tasks), and are improvements statistically significant? Did you try adding a CRF layer in the MTL base tagger?
4) Ablations: For STagBLCC, what is the contribution of (i) CRF, and (ii) char-CNN, individually? For LSTM-ER, what is the effect of scheduled sampling and entity pretraining? Any attempts to replace TreeLSTM with document-level encoders (e.g., hierarchical BiLSTM) for cross-sentence relations?
5) Generalization: Have you tried your models on another AM dataset (e.g., microtexts, web debates) without paragraph-containment and strict tree assumptions? How would you adapt the tagging scheme when many-to-many relations are common?
6) Runtime and resource use: Can you report training/inference time and memory for essay-level runs, and compare scalability across framings?

Overall, this is a solid empirical contribution to AM with practical modeling guidance. The novelty is moderate (reusing known architectures), but the tagging design and the comparative insights are valuable. Broadening the evaluation scope and strengthening ablations would improve the paper further.
