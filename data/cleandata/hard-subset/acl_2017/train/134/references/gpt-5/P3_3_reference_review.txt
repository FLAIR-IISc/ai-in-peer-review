Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on persuasive essays. It compares four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite BIO tag that jointly encodes component type, relation distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task tagging with auxiliary subtasks (components and relations); and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) that decouples entity and relation extraction. The study contrasts these with a feature-engineered ILP pipeline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016), evaluating at both paragraph and essay level.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A thorough empirical comparison of neural end-to-end formulations for AM, with clear evidence that sequence tagging and LSTM-ER outperform a strong ILP baseline, especially at paragraph level. The negative result for token-level dependency parsing is informative.
2) A simple but effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance in the label, yielding robust performance and good handling of long-range dependencies, and an analysis of when general (LSTM-ER) versus constrained (tagging) formulations are preferable.
3) Evidence that multi-task learning with natural AM subtasks modestly improves performance, and a careful analysis of paragraph vs essay-level behavior.

Strengths
- Comprehensive, well-controlled empirical study: The paper evaluates multiple neural paradigms (parser, tagger, MTL, LSTM-ER) against a competitive joint ILP, using both exact and approximate span metrics and document-level significance tests. The essay vs paragraph analysis is illuminating and practically relevant.

- Clear methodological insight: The result that document-length sequences favor constrained local models (distance-aware tagging) while short documents can benefit from more general relation models (LSTM-ER) is a useful takeaway for AM and other document-level IE tasks.

- Practical, end-to-end focus with reduced feature engineering: The proposed neural approaches eliminate hand-crafted features and ILP constraints while matching or surpassing ILP performance, especially on paragraphs (notably LSTM-ER). The distance-augmented tag-space leverages empirical distance priors to regularize relation prediction.

- Negative result with analysis: The token-level dependency parsing framing underperforms substantially; the paper provides a plausible explanation (global tree search over long sequences, token-level tree mismatch, memory constraints) and contrasts it with local taggers’ robustness.

- Error analysis/diagnostics: The distance distribution analysis and conditional accuracy vs |d| for relations provide interpretable diagnostics and justify the modeling choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling and modest gains on essays: The framings largely adapt known architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, LSTM-ER, standard MTL) with a task-specific label design. On essays, improvements over ILP are small (e.g., R-F1 gains are marginal), suggesting the headline claim of “better than ILP” holds mainly at paragraph level and in specific setups.

- Label-space design and sparsity not fully addressed: Encoding distance d into tags inflates the label inventory and risks sparsity and poor generalization to unseen distances. The paper asserts finiteness but does not quantify the number of labels, whether distances are capped/bucketed, or how unseen d at test time is handled. An ablation on distance bucketing would strengthen the case.

- Fairness of LSTM-ER comparison and sentence-level trees: LSTM-ER exploits sentence-level dependency trees, while 92% of relations are cross-sentence; unsurprisingly, this hurts essay-level relation performance. This is as much a limitation of the chosen adaptation as of the architecture. Document-level graph encoders (e.g., GNNs over components) or sentence-agnostic TreeLSTM alternatives could have provided a fairer, stronger comparison.

- Structural assumptions and “lossless” relinking: To fit tree constraints, claims are re-linked to a single major claim and a special root is added. This changes the gold structure and may affect stance distributions. The “lossless” assertion needs justification, and the impact on evaluation (especially stance and distances) should be quantified.

- Limited analysis depth: The paper would benefit from per-class scores (MC/claim/premise), stance-wise results (support/attack vs for/against), and error breakdowns by distance. The MTL results use a weaker BL backbone; it is unclear whether the gains would hold (or be larger) with the stronger BLCC base. Finally, only one dataset is used; generality to different AM schemes remains untested.

Questions to authors
1) Distance-tagging details: How many distinct labels did Y contain in training? Did you cap or bucket distances (e.g., for |d| > K)? How do you handle unseen distance values at test time?

2) Structural relabeling: Please justify the “lossless” re-linking of claims to a single major claim and addition of a root. How does this affect stance labeling (For/Against vs Support/Attack), the distance distribution, and reported metrics? Can you report results without relinking?

3) LSTM-ER adaptation: Since most relations are cross-sentence, did you try removing reliance on sentence-level dependency trees (e.g., a pure sequence or document-level encoder for relation classification)? If not, do you consider the essay-level R-F1 comparison to be a fair assessment of LSTM-ER’s potential?

4) MTL backbone: Why is MTL only evaluated with a BL backbone rather than BLCC? Do the observed gains persist when using CRF and character CNNs?

5) Reproducibility: Will you release code/configurations? What development split and early stopping criteria did you use? Were hyperparameters tuned on a dev set distinct from the test folds?

6) Robustness: Can you report per-class (MC/claim/premise) and per-stance results, and performance as a function of |d|, to better understand where each approach excels or fails?

Overall, this is a solid, empirically grounded paper with useful insights for end-to-end AM. While the methodological novelty is moderate and some comparisons could be strengthened, the study provides actionable conclusions and advances the state of the art, particularly at paragraph level.
