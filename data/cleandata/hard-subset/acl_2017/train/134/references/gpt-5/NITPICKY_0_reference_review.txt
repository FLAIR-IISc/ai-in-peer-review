Summary and claimed contributions
This paper explores neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus. The authors investigate four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding BIO, component type, inter-component distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary component and relation subtasks; and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER that jointly detects entities and relations. They compare to an ILP-based joint model. They report that neural models match or outperform the ILP baseline, that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective, and that sequence tagging with distance coding and MTL works well; LSTM-ER excels on paragraph-level but degrades on essay-level relation extraction.

My view of the main contributions (in order):
1) A systematic empirical comparison of several neural end-to-end framings for AM (sequence tagging with distance coding, MTL, LSTM-ER) against a strong ILP baseline, with analysis across paragraph vs. essay settings.
2) A simple, practical sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance and stance in the tag, with evidence of robust performance and modest gains from MTL auxiliary tasks.
3) A negative result showing that token-level dependency parsing is not effective for end-to-end AM in this dataset, with insights on sequence length and search-space issues.

Strengths
- Empirical thoroughness across modeling paradigms: The paper evaluates multiple neural formulations (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER, and multiple parsers) and contrasts them on both paragraph and essay levels. This breadth provides useful comparative insights rather than focusing on a single architecture.

- Strong baselines and reasonable wins: The comparison with the ILP joint model is appropriate, and the neural tagger/LSTM-ER outperform it in several settings. The observed trade-offs (e.g., LSTM-ER strong on components, weaker on relations at essay level) are informative for practitioners.

- Sensible analysis of document length and search space: The discussion about why LSTM-ER decays on essay-level relations (quadratic candidate pairs) and why parsers struggle on long sequences is compelling, and the distance-coding rationale is supported by distance distributions.

- MTL findings: Although gains are modest, the experiments suggest that including component detection as an auxiliary task helps the full AM tagging model, and that layer assignments do not necessarily follow a strict “low-level task at lower layer” heuristic. This practical insight is valuable.

- Clarity on practical implications and policy recommendations: The guidance on when to prefer constrained/local models vs. more general joint models depending on the number of entities is well articulated.

Weaknesses
- Questionable “loss-less” structural transformation: The dataset has claims linking to all major claims. The paper states that re-linking claims to one major claim and creating a root for major claims is “loss-less.” This is not accurate: many-to-many claim–major-claim links are collapsed to one link per claim, losing information about multiple alignments and their stances. This undermines the fidelity of the parsing framing and potentially biases evaluations.

- Ambiguity and incompleteness in evaluation reporting:
  • “Global F1” is defined as “the F1-score of C-F1 and R-F1,” which is not mathematically well specified. Is this macro/micro/ harmonic combination? The tables list “F1” but do not define precisely how it is computed.
  • The “Acc.” column is unexplained. For taggers it might be token accuracy; for parsers it is unclear; for ILP it is reported though ILP is not a token tagger.
  • Table 1 intermixes paragraph and essay results, but ILP appears only once without clear segmentation; the caption claims ILP operates on both levels, yet only one row is shown. This makes comparability unclear.
  • Statistical significance: multiple pairwise comparisons appear without correction for multiple testing; pairing at the “document” level is ambiguous when some systems are trained/evaluated at paragraph vs. essay level.

- Distance-in-label design raises sparsity and generalization concerns: The label set includes distance d values, potentially large in magnitude (observed -11..+10). There is no discussion of truncation or back-off for rare/unseen distances, class imbalance, or coverage at test time. This can cause severe label sparsity and brittle generalization. An ablation removing d (or bucketing d) and evaluating relation performance would strengthen the claim that explicit distance coding is crucial.

- Fairness of comparisons and missing ablations:
  • Some models (e.g., LSTM-Parser) are only trained at paragraph level due to memory, whereas taggers are trained at both levels; this complicates cross-model conclusions.
  • There is no non-neural CRF baseline for end-to-end tagging to contextualize gains due to neural architecture vs. the label encoding.
  • For the tagging model, ablations on CRF vs. no-CRF, char CNN vs. none, and stance/distance encoding would clarify contributions. For LSTM-ER, an essay-level constrained candidate pruning would be informative.

- Reproducibility and experimental detail gaps:
  • Hyperparameters, embeddings, training schedules, random seed variance, dev split, and model selection criteria are deferred to supplementary; the main paper lacks necessary details to reproduce key results. Given observed high variance (essay-level parsers), reporting mean±std over multiple runs is important.
  • The significance test scope (which columns/systems) and the definition of “documents” used for pairing are not fully specified.

- Conceptual and notational inconsistencies:
  • The stance slot s mixes Supp/Att with For/Against; the mapping between these and dataset annotation is unclear. LSTM-ER is said to model four relation types (for, against, support, attack) while the tagging formulation uses both sets; please clarify when each is used.
  • Multi-task notation in Table 2 (e.g., Y-3:YC-3:YR-3) is cryptic; the number of BiLSTM layers and exact layer indices are not specified.
  • Dependency parsing across whole essays departs from sentence-level parsing conventions; implementation details on how parsers are adapted (single-root, END token head, cross-sentence arcs) are missing.

- Numerous editorial/formatting issues: Multiple typos and encoding problems (“seperately,” “dection,” strange colon/diacritics, inconsistent citation years “?, 2015,” inconsistent capitalization, references with incomplete venues/pages). Figures and table captions lack some critical details (e.g., which level Figure 4 is computed on). These detract from readability and polish.

Questions to authors
1) How exactly is “global F1” computed from C-F1 and R-F1? Please provide the formula. Also explain “Acc.” for each model family.

2) Clarify the “loss-less” claim about re-linking claims to one major claim. Which major claim is chosen, and how are For/Against stances preserved? What percentage of claim–major-claim links are multi-headed originally?

3) Distance coding: Did you cap or bucket distance d? What fraction of gold relations at test time have distances unseen in training? Provide an ablation with (i) no d, (ii) bucketed d, and (iii) CRF/no-CRF to assess the importance of distance coding.

4) MTL configuration: How many BiLSTM layers? What do the “-1/-3” layer indices in Table 2 denote? Are the reported gains statistically significant across runs?

5) Fairness and segmentation: For Table 1, which ILP scores correspond to paragraph vs. essay? If ILP uses essay-level context (e.g., paragraph position), are tagger/LSTM-ER deprived of this information? Please ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.

6) Variance and selection: How many random seeds per system? What dev split and early stopping criteria were used? Report mean±std, especially for essay-level parsers where variability is “drastic.”

7) LSTM-ER at essay level: Did you try candidate pruning (e.g., distance thresholds, paragraph-level constraints) during relation classification to mitigate quadratic explosion? If so, how did it affect R-F1?

8) Stance type mapping: Precisely define when s∈{Supp,Att} vs. s∈{For,Ag}. Are these mutually exclusive subsets tied to premise→(claim/premise) vs. claim↔major claim? How are these encoded in the single tag space?

Overall, the empirical study is valuable, but the structural transformation, evaluation ambiguities, missing ablations, and presentation issues are significant. With clarifications and tighter experimental controls, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, I lean toward rejection.
