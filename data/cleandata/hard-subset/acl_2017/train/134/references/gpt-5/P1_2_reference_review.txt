Summary and contributions
This paper studies end-to-end neural approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) from plain text on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays corpus. It compares four framings:
- Sequence tagging (STagT) with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, distance to the linked component, and stance, instantiated with a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger.
- Token-level dependency parsing reformulation of AM.
- Multi-task sequence tagging (MTL) using component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks.
- An adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER (BiLSTM-CRF for entities + tree-structured relation classifier).

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple and effective end-to-end neural sequence tagging approach for AM that encodes relation distance in the tag, achieving strong performance and new SOTA on the PE dataset without hand-crafted features/constraints, and robust across paragraph and essay settings.
2) An informative comparative study showing (a) token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, and (b) there is a length-dependent trade-off: a general entity–relation model (LSTM-ER) excels on shorter paragraphs but degrades on long essays, whereas the constrained tagger is more robust.
3) Evidence that multi-task training with natural subtasks (component vs relation) yields consistent, modest gains over single-task tagging.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results and clear takeaways: On paragraphs, LSTM-ER attains the best C-F1 and R-F1 (e.g., ~70.8/77.2 C-F1 and ~45.5/50.1 R-F1 at 100/50%), surpassing the ILP baseline; the STagT tagger also outperforms both the ILP and all dependency parsers. On essays, STagT remains robust and outperforms LSTM-ER on relations and overall F1. This provides concrete guidance for practitioners.
- Elegant, minimally engineered end-to-end approach: The distance-aware tagging scheme is a simple idea with substantial impact—biasing the model toward realistic locality while avoiding feature and constraint engineering. It’s a nice example of encoding structural bias within standard neural tagging.
- Comprehensive and fair comparisons: The work benchmarks against a strong joint ILP system and multiple dependency parsers (feature-based and neural) plus LSTM-ER, with significance testing and both exact and approximate span matching. The paragraph vs essay analysis is particularly illuminating for understanding model behavior.
- Negative result with value: The token-level dependency parsing framing is carefully set up and shown to underperform, especially on essays. The analysis (long sequences, huge tree search space, mismatch with sentence-level dependency features) is insightful and useful for the community.
- Multi-task findings: The consistent gains when adding component and/or relation auxiliary tasks (even with a weaker BL tagger lacking CRF and char-CNN) are practically relevant and suggest a clean way to decouple while still training jointly.

Weaknesses
- Dataset specificity and limited generalization claims: All experiments are on a single corpus with idiosyncratic properties (near-tree structures, paragraph-local relations, claims linking to all major claims). The relinking of claims to a single major claim plus a special root simplifies structure. It’s unclear how the methods generalize to corpora with cross-paragraph links, denser graphs, or different argumentation schemes. No cross-domain evaluation.
- “End-to-end” but with structural priors and segmentation: A substantial portion of the gains hinge on paragraph-level processing (shorter contexts, fewer candidate links). While essay-level results are reported, the best systems are on paragraphs; at test time, paragraph boundaries are assumed known. This weakens the end-to-end claim in settings where such segmentation is unavailable or where relations cross paragraphs.
- Dependency parsing comparison may be a strawman: Reformulating AM as token-level parsing ignores component-level nodes, which is the more natural granularity used in prior discourse/AM parsing. The poor results may reflect the suboptimal granularity rather than an inherent limitation of parsing. A component-level parser baseline (predict segments first, then parse) would strengthen the conclusion.
- Label-space sparsity and scaling not fully analyzed: The distance-augmented tag set can explode as |d| grows (distances observed up to ±11). The paper argues empirical finiteness but does not detail caps, bucketing strategies, or how unseen distances are handled. This raises concerns for longer documents or datasets with longer-range links.
- Ablation clarity: Several factors are confounded across systems (e.g., CRF and char-CNN in BLCC vs no CRF/char in MTL BL; LSTM-ER with scheduled sampling and tree LSTMs). More ablations isolating the impact of distance encoding, CRF transitions, char-CNN, and auxiliary tasks would clarify where the gains come from. Similarly, the LSTM-ER adaptation is disadvantaged on essays by not enforcing tree constraints; adding simple structural constraints might shift the trade-off.

Questions to authors
- Distance encoding: What is the maximum |d| considered in tags? Are distances beyond a threshold bucketed, and how are unseen values handled at test time? Did you explore signed vs absolute distance, or bucketing schemes, and with what effect?
- Paragraph vs essay: At test time, do you always process gold paragraph boundaries? How would the models perform if paragraph boundaries were not provided (e.g., sliding windows or learned segmentation)? Do relations ever cross paragraphs in PE, and how would your models handle that?
- Dependency parsing: Did you attempt a component-level parsing variant (e.g., first segment and type, then parse components) to separate the negative result from token-level granularity? If not, can you justify why token-level parsing is the appropriate baseline?
- CRF constraints: In STagT with BLCC, do you enforce BIO-valid transitions and any consistency between tokens within a component for (t, d, s)? If not, how often do you observe within-span inconsistencies, and do they affect evaluation?
- LSTM-ER details: How many negative pairs per document are considered during training on essays? Did you try constraining to one outgoing link per premise at inference to reduce the O(m^2) pairing? How sensitive are results to scheduled sampling and pretraining?
- Reproducibility and hyperparameters: Please provide concrete hyperparameters, pretrained embeddings, and early stopping criteria for all models, and clarify how many runs and seeds were averaged for the reported scores.
