Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods, proposing several framings that avoid hand-crafted features and ILP constraints: (i) a novel sequence tagging formulation that encodes component boundaries, types, relation distance, and stance in a single tag; (ii) a token-level dependency parsing formulation; (iii) a multi-task learning (MTL) variant using component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks; and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) to AM. On the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus, the sequence tagging and LSTM-ER approaches outperform a prior pipeline+ILP SOTA, especially at paragraph level; the token-level dependency parsing performs poorly. The authors analyze why constrained tagging helps in long documents and why LSTM-ER weakens as entity count grows.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A bold, end-to-end neural reframing of AM with a distance-aware sequence tagging scheme that jointly encodes entities and relations at token level, plus a systematic empirical comparison across four neural paradigms against a strong ILP baseline.
2) Clear empirical insights into modeling bias vs. document length: constrained taggers excel on long documents, while flexible entity–relation models (LSTM-ER) shine on shorter units (paragraphs); token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM.
3) Multi-task learning with natural AM subtasks (component-only, relation-only) yields consistent gains and closes some of the gap with specialized models.

Strengths
- Novel end-to-end perspective and tagging formulation: Encoding relation distance and stance directly in sequence tags is an original, gutsy design that renders AM tractable without hand-engineered features or global ILP constraints. This is a meaningful conceptual shift for AM and likely to influence follow-up work in other structure prediction problems with long-range links.

- Comprehensive, well-motivated comparisons: The paper contrasts four distinct neural framings (tagging, MTL tagging, token-level dependency parsing, LSTM-ER) plus a strong ILP baseline. The breadth of methods and the careful thought about their inductive biases (local vs. global, constraint strength, many-to-many vs. one-to-one relations) provide valuable guidance to the community.

- Strong empirical results and analysis: Neural models match or surpass the ILP baseline without handcrafted features. The paragraph vs. essay analyses, the distance-distribution study, and the explanation of LSTM-ER’s O(m^2) pairing burden as entity counts grow are insightful. The negative result for token-level dependency parsing is also useful for scoping future efforts.

- Multi-task learning adds value: Demonstrating consistent improvements by adding component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks reinforces the utility of MTL for complex structured tagging and suggests a reusable recipe for related tasks (e.g., morphologically rich tagging).

- Practical impact: Removing feature/constraint engineering lowers entry barriers for applying AM to new domains/annotation schemes—important given AM’s theory-dependent definitions and dataset heterogeneity.

Weaknesses
- Limited corpus scope and generality: All results are on a single dataset with very particular properties (near-tree structures within paragraphs, claims linking to all major claims). It is unclear how the distance-aware tagging or the overall findings transfer to other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts, legal, web debate) with different topologies (more cross-paragraph links, non-trees, multi-headed relations).

- Tag set scalability and long-range robustness: The sequence tagging label space grows with the range of distances. While the empirical distance distribution is short-tailed in PE, in other settings distances can be larger and more variable. The paper does not report how distances are truncated/bucketed, nor sensitivity to distance range, which raises concerns about scalability and label sparsity.

- Token-level dependency parsing as a potentially unfair target: Encoding AM as token-head trees is arguably mismatched to the problem (components are the natural units, and relations are component-level). This might underplay the potential of parsing-style models if designed over components (even predicted ones) or with span representations. The result is still informative but feels like a strawman for “parsing.”

- Evaluation design inconsistencies: Some models are trained/evaluated at paragraph level, others at essay level, and the ILP model exploits essay-level cues (e.g., positions of major claims). While the paper discusses these differences, the cross-setting comparisons muddy the picture. A uniform evaluation protocol (or learning the same scope features for all models) would strengthen claims.

- Limited ablations and reproducibility details: The paper mentions supplementary material for embeddings/hyperparameters but lacks in-paper ablations isolating the contribution of CRF vs. BiLSTM vs. char-CNN, or of the distance and stance components in the tag. Robustness across seeds and details on code availability are not explicit.

Questions to authors
1) Distance encoding: What is the maximum |d| used in training/inference? Do you truncate or bucket distances, and how sensitive are results to this choice? How often do errors involve wrong distance but correct endpoints?

2) Inference consistency: STagT allows every token to predict its own (b, t, d, s). How do you enforce that each component yields a single outgoing link (and none for claims/MCs), and that all tokens in a span share the same d, s at inference time? Is there any post-processing or global decoding?

3) Generality: Have you tried your tagging scheme on another AM corpus (e.g., Peldszus & Stede microtexts) or on cross-paragraph relations? If not, what modifications would you recommend (e.g., distance bucketing, allowing multiple parents)?

4) LSTM-ER search space: Did you try adding simple structural priors to LSTM-ER at essay level (e.g., one outgoing link per premise, or distance-aware negative sampling) to mitigate the O(m^2) explosion? How much would that close the R-F1 gap?

5) Reproducibility: Will you release code and tuned hyperparameters? How many random seeds were averaged, and are improvements over ILP stable across seeds?

Overall, this paper introduces a fresh, impactful direction for end-to-end AM with neural models, proposes a genuinely novel tagging scheme, and offers thorough empirical insights. Despite dataset and evaluation limitations, the conceptual advance and strong results outweigh the shortcomings. I lean towards acceptance.
