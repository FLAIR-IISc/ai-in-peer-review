- Paper explores four neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining (token-level dependency parsing, composite sequence tagging STagBLCC, multi-task tagging, and LSTM-ER) evaluated on the PE corpus at paragraph and essay level.
- Main contribution: STagBLCC (distance-coded BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging) achieves strong end-to-end results, outperforming feature-based ILP on paragraphs and remaining robust on essays; MTL with component/relation subtasks further improves tagging.
- Negative result: token-level dependency parsing (feature-based and neural) is ineffective for end-to-end AM, especially on long sequences.
- Comparative insight: LSTM-ER excels at component detection and short documents, while constrained tagging encodings help relation extraction and long documents—grounded in distance distribution and search-space reasoning.
- Strength: broad, systematic empirical study with strong baselines, ablations, and useful negative results that guide the community.
- Strength: novel tagging formulation that encodes relation distance, imposing useful constraints (one outgoing link) and reducing combinatorial explosion for long texts.
- Strength: insightful analysis of length/structural effects explaining parser and ER-model degradation at essay level, with relation-accuracy vs. distance evidence.
- Strength: credible MTL finding—auxiliary component-only and relation-only tasks yield measurable gains and practical recipe for complex labels.
- Strength: relevance—shows neural end-to-end models can replace feature-based pipelines and ILP constraints for AM.
- Weakness: fairness/transparency—ILP results are reported only at paragraph level; essay-level ILP results are missing and needed for a complete comparison.
- Weakness: dataset scope—experiments use only the PE corpus with structural regularities, raising concerns about generalization to many-to-many links, cross-paragraph relations, or other domains.
- Weakness: distance-augmented label space grows with d; paper omits how unseen/large distances are handled and the exact distance range used in training.
- Weakness: reproducibility—important implementation details (pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, distance range, decoding constraints, claim-to-MC handling, paragraph segmentation) are deferred to unavailable supplementary material.
- Weakness: token-level parsing framing may be a strawman since most AM treats spans as components; a component-level parser could be a stronger baseline and temper the parsing conclusion.
- Question: please report essay-level ILP C-F1, R-F1, and global F1 or explain unavailability and expected differences.
- Question: how are distances not seen during training handled at test time (capping/binning/"far" bin), and what exact distance label range was used?
- Question: how does STagT enforce one outgoing link per component when tagging at token level—what post-processing reconciles token-level labels into component-level structures?
- Question: for LSTM-ER, how are TreeLSTM paths constructed for cross-sentence pairs (document-level forests, connected sentence roots), and could this explain essay-level relation drops?
- Question: provide runtime/memory comparisons across models on essays and evidence of overfitting given the small corpus (402 essays).
- Additional minor: correct complexity notation for LSTM-ER candidate relations from O(2m 2) to O(m^2); fix inconsistent reference formatting.
