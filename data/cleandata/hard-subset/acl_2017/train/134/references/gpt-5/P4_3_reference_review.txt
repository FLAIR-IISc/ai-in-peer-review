Summary
The paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM), aiming to jointly identify argumentative components (major claims, claims, premises) and their relations (support/attack/for/against) directly from raw text. It explores four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO component boundaries, component type, relation distance (in number of components), and stance, (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) where component-only and relation-only tagging serve as auxiliary tasks, and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) that decouples entity and relation extraction using BiLSTM-CRF for components and TreeLSTM for relations. Experiments on the Stab & Gurevych (2016) persuasive essays dataset compare paragraph-level and essay-level settings using F1 metrics at exact and approximate span match. Results show that neural methods match or exceed a strong feature-based ILP baseline without hand-crafted features. Sequence tagging and LSTM-ER perform best; token-level dependency parsing is substantially weaker. MTL yields gains over single-task tagging. The paper offers analysis on the impact of document length and distance distributions.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical demonstration that neural end-to-end AM can match or surpass a strong feature-based ILP pipeline on a standard dataset, removing hand-crafted feature and constraint design.
2) A simple, effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance in the tag set, yielding robust performance across paragraph and essay settings; plus evidence that auxiliary sub-tasks (component-only, relation-only) improve the full model via MTL.
3) A systematic comparison of multiple neural framings, including a negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; and an analysis explaining when a decoupled entity–relation model (LSTM-ER) helps or hurts depending on document length.

Strengths
- Clear, comprehensive comparative study across modeling paradigms. The paper evaluates token-level dependency parsers (feature-based and neural), sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF with char CNN), MTL variants, and LSTM-ER, against a strong ILP baseline, on both paragraph and essay levels with consistent metrics (C-F1, R-F1 at 100%/50%). This breadth provides credible evidence about what works for end-to-end AM.
- Strong empirical performance without hand-crafted features. Sequence tagging (BLCC) and LSTM-ER outperform or match the ILP system, particularly at paragraph level, establishing that neural architectures can handle end-to-end AM effectively while simplifying engineering effort.
- Insightful problem encoding for sequence tagging. The composite tag design that encodes relation distance addresses long-range dependencies in AM while keeping inference local. The analysis of distance distributions and the observed bias toward short-range links help explain the method’s robustness.
- Useful analysis of document length effects. The paper shows that LSTM-ER performs best at paragraph level but degrades on essay level due to O(m^2) relation candidates, whereas the tagging model’s constraints (one link per component, distance bias) help maintain performance. This yields concrete guidance on model selection by scenario.
- Multi-task learning benefits substantiated. Adding component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks to the tagging model consistently improves F1, showing a pragmatic way to decompose complex labels and transfer structure within a shared encoder.

Weaknesses
- Limited evidence of generalization beyond a single dataset/domain. All results are on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays corpus, whose structure (relations largely within paragraphs; specific major-claim behavior) is leveraged in modeling and evaluation. It remains unclear how well the proposed encodings and findings transfer to other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts, web discourse, legal).
- Potential mismatch and confounds in model comparisons. Some dependency parsers were trained only at paragraph level due to memory limits, while other models are evaluated at both levels. The MTL models use a weaker base tagger (no CRF, no char CNN) than the main tagging baseline, making it hard to isolate the effect of MTL vs architecture differences. The ILP baseline has access to essay/paragraph context; fairness of information access is not fully harmonized.
- Token-level dependency framing may be suboptimal by construction. The chosen representation attaches all tokens of a component to the head token of its target component and sends all non-argumentative tokens to END. This yields dense, possibly non-projective structures and many redundant arcs; poor results may reflect the representation rather than the limits of dependency parsing per se. Alternative component-level parsing or span-based parsing baselines are not explored.
- Label space complexity and sparsity not fully quantified. The sequence tagging label set grows with the range of distances d. Although the paper argues distance is bounded in practice, it observes long-range links (up to |d|≈11). There is limited analysis of how many labels are realized, whether distances are capped, and how performance varies with maximum d or label sparsity.
- Reproducibility and ablations deferred. Key training details (embeddings, hyperparameters, regularization, early stopping) and ablations (e.g., effect of distance encoding, stance slot, CRF vs no-CRF) are in the supplement. The main text would benefit from core ablations to attribute gains cleanly (e.g., distance encoding vs BIO-only; MTL vs architecture).

Questions to authors
- What is the effective size of the tagging label set Y in training and test? Is there a cap on |d| during training/decoding, and how sensitive are results to the maximum allowed distance?
- How are multiple outgoing links handled for claims that connect to all major claims? The paper mentions re-linking claims to one major claim to form trees; how does this affect learning and evaluation of relations/stance?
- For MTL, did you evaluate adding CRF and character CNN to match the BLCC base? If not, can you estimate how much of the gap to STagBLCC arises from architecture vs the benefits of MTL itself?
- Can you report component-level and relation-level error breakdowns by distance buckets and by within- vs cross-sentence links for each model? This would help validate the hypothesized distance bias and the LSTM-ER degradation on long documents.
- How does the sequence tagging model enforce per-component constraints (e.g., exactly one outgoing relation for premises) at decoding time? Is any post-processing applied, or are these constraints learned implicitly?
- Did you try component-level dependency parsing or span-based parsing as an alternative to token-level parsing? If so, how did they compare?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound in its experimental methodology, and provides a useful, data-driven comparison of neural end-to-end approaches for AM, with novel and practical insights (notably the distance-encoded tagging and the length-dependent behavior of LSTM-ER). The main limitations concern generalization beyond a single dataset and some comparison confounds/ablations.
