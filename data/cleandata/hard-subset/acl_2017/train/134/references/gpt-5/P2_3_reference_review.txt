Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
- A comprehensive empirical study of neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus, comparing four framings: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with a bespoke label space that encodes component type, relation distance, and stance (STagT), multi-task learning (MTL) variants of the tagger, and an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) to AM. The study includes extensive paragraph- vs essay-level comparisons and uses the Persing & Ng evaluation protocol.
- A simple yet effective sequence-tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance in the label, yielding strong, robust performance, especially on longer documents, and outperforming a strong feature-based ILP baseline without hand-engineered features/constraints.
- An informative negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; and a nuanced analysis of when more general relation models (LSTM-ER) help (short documents) vs when restrained models (distance-coded tagging) help (long documents). The MTL experiments provide evidence that decoupling component vs relation supervision improves performance.

Strengths
- Sound, thorough empirical comparison with strong baselines: The paper evaluates multiple neural paradigms alongside a state-of-the-art ILP system and several dependency parsers, on both paragraph and essay levels, and reports both exact and approximate F1 for components and relations. The analysis is careful (e.g., distance distribution, impact of sequence length, component-relation coupling) and statistically grounded (Wilcoxon tests).
- Practical, generalizable insight: The finding that distance-coded tagging scales better to long documents while LSTM-ER excels when entity sets are small is actionable for practitioners. The negative result for token-level dependency parsing is valuable for the community and redirects effort away from a tempting but ineffective path for end-to-end AM.
- Simple modeling with strong performance and reduced engineering: The sequence tagging approach with distance and stance yields state-of-the-art results relative to the ILP baseline while avoiding dataset-specific feature/constraint design. The MTL evidence that component detection as an auxiliary task boosts the full tagger is also useful and likely transferable to other structured extraction tasks.
- Clear articulation of constraints vs generality trade-offs: The paper explicitly reasons about search space, constraints (tree vs many-to-many), and how these interact with document length and relation distance, supported by empirical probes (e.g., Figure 4).
- Relevance and timeliness: AM is an active area with educational, legal, and summarization applications. A neural end-to-end baseline suite lowers the barrier for future work and benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Limited architectural novelty: Most models are standard (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, MTL BiLSTM, LSTM-ER). The main novelty lies in task framing (distance-coded labels) and the comparative study. This is acceptable if significance is emphasized, but methodological contribution per se is modest.
- Single-dataset scope and potential overfitting to PE structure: Results and conclusions are drawn entirely from the PE corpus, which has specific properties (nearly tree-like structures, paragraph-contained links except claim–major claim, writing style). It is unclear how well findings transfer to other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts, online debate, legal). The discussion acknowledges this partially but does not test it.
- Comparability and fairness concerns: Some systems are trained/evaluated at paragraph level due to memory, while others are also run at essay level. The ILP baseline “knows” paragraph positions within essays, which benefits major claim detection; neural models mostly do not. The paper notes this asymmetry but a clearer accounting and possibly controlled ablations (e.g., restrict ILP to paragraph-only info, or give neural models paragraph index features) would strengthen claims.
- Label-space sparsity and specification: The distance-augmented label set Y is, in principle, unbounded. The paper argues distances are bounded in practice (Figure 2), but does not specify how unseen/large distances are handled (clipping? “>k” bucket?), nor the maximum distance used, which affects sparsity, learning dynamics, and generalization. More detail is needed to assess robustness and reproducibility.
- MTL setup and fairness: The MTL backbone (BL) is weaker than BLCC (no CRF, no char-CNN), which conflates the effect of MTL with model capacity. It would be more convincing to show MTL gains on top of BLCC or to control for capacity (e.g., add CRF/char-CNN to MTL). Also, the choice of which layers feed which tasks seems tuned but not fully justified or explored systematically.
- Reproducibility details deferred: Key training details (embeddings, optimization, hyperparameters, early stopping, scheduled sampling schedule for LSTM-ER, negative sampling, pair pruning) are in the supplement. For an empirical study whose contribution hinges on comparisons, more in-paper detail (or explicit pointer to code) would help reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Distance label handling: What is the maximum |d| used in labels? Are distances clipped or bucketed (e.g., >10)? How are unseen distances at test time handled? Did you explore alternative bucketings?
- Multi-link claims: You “re-link” claims to one major claim to enforce a tree. How sensitive are results to the chosen relinking strategy (e.g., last major claim vs first)? Did you evaluate relations to major claims separately to quantify any distortion?
- Pair pruning in LSTM-ER: Given O(m^2) candidate pairs at essay level, do you prune candidate relations (sentence/paragraph scope, distance thresholds, type constraints)? If not, what is the computational cost? If yes, please specify pruning rules and their impact.
- MTL backbone: Did you try adding CRF and character CNNs to the MTL model? If not, do you expect the auxiliary task gains to hold with a stronger backbone?
- Fairness of ILP comparison: Can you report ILP results restricted to paragraph-internal inference (to match the paragraph-level neural settings), and/or augment neural models with simple paragraph-position features to equalize information access?
- Generalization: Have you tried your best-performing setups on another AM dataset (e.g., microtexts or web discourse)? If not, can you comment on expected challenges (e.g., non-tree structures, noisier distances) and whether distance-coded tagging remains advantageous?

Overall assessment
- Originality: moderate (framing and negative result are novel within AM; architectures standard).
- Quality: high (solid experiments, careful analyses; some comparability/reproducibility gaps).
- Clarity: good, though some critical implementation details are in the supplement.
- Significance: good for the AM community; provides strong neural baselines and actionable insights.
