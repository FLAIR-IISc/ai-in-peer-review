Summary and contributions
This paper studies neural, end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus, comparing several framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, distance, and stance (STagT with BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary component/relation subtasks, and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016). The main empirical claims are:
1) A comprehensive empirical comparison showing that neural sequence tagging (especially BLCC) and LSTM-ER outperform a feature-based ILP pipeline on PE in several settings, with strong results at paragraph level and competitive results at essay level.
2) A pragmatic finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, while distance-aware sequence tagging is robust; multi-tasking with component/relation auxiliary tasks yields modest gains.
3) Analysis of the effect of document length (paragraph vs essay) on relation extraction performance and search space, motivating constrained formulations.

Strengths
- Broad empirical study across several established neural formulations. The paper compares multiple, well-known architectures (BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN), LSTM-ER, several dependency parsers) on the same dataset with consistent metrics (C-F1 and R-F1 at 100/50%), offering a useful empirical map for AM practitioners.
- Clear, interpretable tagging formulation with distance/stance encoding. The STagT label design is simple, leverages empirical proximity of related components, and yields robust performance. The rationale that constraining each component to link to a single other component tames the relation search space is well argued and supported by the paragraph vs essay results and the |d|-accuracy plot.
- Multi-task results that are practically informative. While gains are modest, the finding that adding component and (to a lesser degree) relation auxiliary tasks helps the full tagger is useful; it supports the intuition that decoupling but jointly training can improve component detection and, indirectly, relations.
- Baseline comparisons include a strong, widely used ILP system. The feature-based ILP pipeline of Stab & Gurevych is a reasonable reference point for this task; the paper shows improvements (especially at paragraph level) without hand-crafted features or constraint design.
- Some attention to statistical testing and analysis. The use of per-document Wilcoxon tests for the main table and discussions on long-range dependencies vs model capacity are appreciated.

Weaknesses
- Limited external validation and dataset dependence. All results are on a single corpus (PE) with tree-like paragraph structures and specific peculiarities (e.g., claims linking to all major claims). The methods’ generalizability to other AM datasets (microtexts, legal, user-generated content) or to graph-structured argumentation remains untested. Given the heavy reliance on the distance prior and per-paragraph containment, this is a serious limitation.
- Uneven, sometimes unfair evaluation conditions. Several models are trained/evaluated only at paragraph level due to memory constraints (e.g., dependency parsers), while others are reported at essay level. The ILP system “has access to both paragraph and essay level information,” which complicates fairness. The main SOTA claims should be conditioned more carefully on the evaluation unit and resource constraints. Clear side-by-side results for all systems on both paragraph and essay settings are needed.
- Insufficient methodological detail for end-to-end decoding. The paper does not fully specify how token-level labels are converted to component spans and relations: conflict resolution for BIO violations, aggregation of per-token distance labels into a single edge per predicted component when spans don’t align, handling of multiple predicted links, and treatment of claims/major claims re-linking. Without this, reproducibility and the soundness of the constraints (“each component relates to exactly one other”) in practice are unclear.
- Modest novelty; largely application of existing architectures. The primary technical contributions are design choices (distance as a tag, MTL setup) and a negative result for token-level dependency parsing. While useful, these are incremental. For instance, a straightforward two-stage neural pipeline (BiLSTM-CRF for components + relation classifier over predicted spans) is a strong, established baseline that is not reported. A neural+ILP hybrid (global constraints over neural scores) is also a standard comparator that would directly test the claimed benefits over ILP.
- Missing ablations and clarity on training. Important choices are deferred to the supplement: pretraining, embeddings, hyperparameters, label-space pruning (are |d| values truncated?), class imbalance handling for rare distances/stances, and scheduled sampling details for LSTM-ER. The MTL section would benefit from clearer layer/task assignments and significance testing. The dependency parsing negative result would be more convincing with analyses controlling for span decoding errors (e.g., punctuation over-attachment) and an entity-level parser formulation (components as nodes), which is closer to prior discourse/argument parsing work.

Questions to authors
1) Decoding: How exactly do you convert token-level (b, t, d, s) to component spans and a single outgoing link per component when token-level predictions are inconsistent? Please specify tie-breaking, BIO repair, and how you resolve multiple/zero outgoing links for a predicted span.
2) Distance label space: Do you cap |d| (e.g., to a maximum) during training/inference? How many distinct distance classes are present, and how do you handle sparsity/class imbalance?
3) Fairness of comparisons: For Table 1, were all systems trained and evaluated on the same unit (paragraph vs essay)? Please provide complete essay-level numbers for ILP and paragraph-level numbers for all neural models where feasible, or clearly qualify the SOTA claims by setting.
4) Baselines: How does a simple two-stage neural pipeline (BiLSTM-CRF for components + relation classifier over candidate pairs with distance features) perform relative to STagT and LSTM-ER? This would isolate the benefit of encoding distance in the tag versus using it as a feature in a relation model.
5) Generalization: Have you tested on any other AM datasets (e.g., microtexts) or settings with non-tree graphs? If not, what changes would be needed for STagT to support many-to-many relations?
6) LSTM-ER degradation at essay level: Can you quantify how much of the drop is due to false positives from the O(m^2) pair enumeration versus component errors? Did you try standard pruning (e.g., windowed candidate pairs based on distance) to make LSTM-ER more comparable to STagT’s constraints?

Overall, this is a valuable empirical study, but the claims would be much stronger with clearer, unified evaluation, fuller decoding details, and additional baselines/ablation to establish methodological soundness beyond the PE dataset.
