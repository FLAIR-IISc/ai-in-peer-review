Summary and contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), moving beyond feature-engineered pipeline models with global ILP constraints. The authors frame AM in four ways: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding component boundaries/types and relation distance/stance, (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary tasks (component vs. relation sublabels), and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) that jointly extracts entities and relations. Using the Stab & Gurevych (2016) persuasive essays corpus, they evaluate at paragraph and essay levels under established F1 metrics (exact and approximate match), comparing against an ILP baseline.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that neural end-to-end models (especially BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagging with distance encoding and MTL variants) match or surpass a strong feature-engineered ILP baseline on end-to-end AM, with statistically significant gains on several metrics.
2) A comparative study of neural framings showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; sequence tagging is robust; and decoupled entity–relation modeling (LSTM-ER) excels on short texts (paragraphs) but degrades on long texts (essays) for relation extraction.
3) Evidence that auxiliary-task MTL (component and relation sublabels) improves performance over a single-task tagger, supporting the value of explicitly decoupling subtasks while learning jointly.

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence with multiple model families and levels of granularity. The study systematically compares parsers, taggers, MTL variants, and LSTM-ER, on both paragraph and essay levels, and uses exact/approximate F1 for components and relations. The inclusion of statistical significance testing strengthens claims.
- Clear modeling rationale and analyses. The paper motivates sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes distance and stance, aligning with observed distance distributions. The analysis of why LSTM-ER deteriorates on essays (combinatorial growth of candidate relations and limited inductive bias toward short distances) is well-argued and supported (Figure 4).
- Practical value: removes feature engineering and handcrafted constraints. The neural approaches operate without task-specific features or ILP constraints, addressing issues of dataset-specific annotation schemes and pipeline error propagation, while achieving comparable or better performance than ILP.
- Insight on task coupling. The results and discussion convincingly support the claim that component and relation predictions should be modeled jointly but not conflated. The MTL experiments and the contrast between STag and LSTM-ER provide converging evidence.
- Transparent discussion of limitations per framing. The paper acknowledges why token-level dependency parsing and sentence-level TreeLSTMs are ill-matched to cross-sentence relations in this dataset, increasing the credibility of the comparative conclusions.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset scope and generalization. All experiments are conducted on a single corpus/domain (persuasive essays) with specific structural regularities (e.g., claims connect to all major claims; relations mostly cross sentences; argument structure largely tree-like). The claim that the tagging scheme “performs robustly in different environments” is not empirically validated across datasets or domains.
- Label space and sparsity concerns insufficiently addressed. The composite tag set includes distance and stance, potentially leading to a large, sparse label inventory. The paper assumes finiteness based on observed distances, but does not clearly specify: the maximum distance encoded; treatment of unseen distances at test time; or the impact of this design on CRF transition estimation/data sparsity. An ablation (with vs. without distance encoding or with capped distances) would clarify its contribution and robustness.
- Uneven comparisons for dependency parsers and LSTM-ER. Dependency parsers are trained only at paragraph level due to memory constraints; essay-level results are reported for one parser with instability. This hinders a fair cross-level comparison. For LSTM-ER, the reliance on sentence-level dependency trees is known to be mismatched to cross-sentence relations here; a stronger variant that models cross-sentence structure or prunes candidates based on discourse/paragraph segmentation could be a fairer baseline.
- Decoding and structural validity not fully specified. For the tagging approaches, it is not clear how outputs are post-processed to ensure valid structures (e.g., exactly one outgoing link per premise, acyclicity, handling of invalid distances). The paper states STag restricts to one target, but does not discuss how violations are handled or measured in evaluation.
- Missing implementation details in the main text. Key training details (pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, dev split, early stopping, regularization, candidate pruning for LSTM-ER) are relegated to supplementary material. Given the relative closeness of some results, these details are important for assessing soundness and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- How is the distance component of the label set Y instantiated in practice? What maximum |d| is allowed, and how are unseen distance values at test time handled? Did you experiment with distance bucketing or capping, and what was the effect?
- For the tagging models, how are token-level predictions converted into component spans and relations? Do you enforce a single outgoing relation per premise and prevent cycles at decoding? If not, how frequent are structural violations and how are they scored?
- For LSTM-ER, did you modify the relation module to consider cross-sentence pairs explicitly? Were any candidate-pair pruning strategies used (e.g., paragraph-local pairing or distance thresholds)? If not, how large was the average number of candidate pairs at essay level?
- Could you provide an ablation removing distance from the tag (only BIO+type+stance) or replacing exact distances with coarse buckets, to quantify the contribution of distance encoding?
- What development set and model selection protocol were used? Were pretrained embeddings and other resources controlled consistently across models?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-executed empirical study with clear takeaways: neural end-to-end models, particularly sequence tagging with appropriate inductive biases and MTL, are competitive or superior to an ILP-based pipeline; dependency parsing at the token level is ineffective; and decoupling entities and relations helps, especially on shorter texts. While the novelty is primarily empirical and methodological (rather than introducing a single new architecture), the study advances the state of the art in end-to-end AM and provides actionable insights. Addressing the noted comparability and specification gaps would further strengthen the work. I lean toward acceptance.
