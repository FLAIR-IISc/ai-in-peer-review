Summary and contributions
- The paper explores neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays corpus. It studies four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that jointly encodes BIO, component type, inter-component distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task sequencing tagging with auxiliary tasks; and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER entity/relation model. Comparisons are made against a feature-based ILP pipeline baseline.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Strong empirical finding that sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels (and, separately, LSTM-ER) outperform a state-of-the-art feature-based ILP baseline on paragraph-level AM, and that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM on this dataset.
  2) A careful comparison of modeling choices across paragraph vs. essay levels, showing that model inductive biases (local vs. global; constrained vs. general) interact sharply with document length and entity density.
  3) Initial evidence that multi-task learning with component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks benefits joint end-to-end tagging.

Strengths
- Empirical breadth and baselines: The paper evaluates four substantially different neural framings (parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) and compares to a strong ILP baseline, reporting both component and relation F1 at exact and 50% overlap levels. The breadth provides useful guidance for the AM community.
- Clear takeaways about modeling bias vs. document length: The study convincingly shows that general relation models (LSTM-ER) excel when entity sets are small (paragraphs) but degrade at essay level due to O(m^2) pair growth; conversely, constrained tagging with distance encoding is more robust. This is a valuable, practically actionable insight.
- Novel, simple label design: Encoding relation distance directly into the sequence tag (b,t,d,s) is a neat, domain-informed way to bias models toward short-range relations without feature engineering or ILP constraints. The distance distribution analysis (Figure 2) motivates this choice.
- Statistical testing: The paper attempts to test significance (Wilcoxon over document-level F1), which is rare in AM papers.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and sometimes unclear modeling specification:
  - The sequence-tagging decoding is underspecified. How are component spans reconstructed and enforced at test time when token-level predictions disagree (e.g., BIO inconsistencies)? How is the single-link-per-component constraint enforced given token-level tags? How are d and s aggregated from token-level predictions to component-level decisions?
  - The distance label set Y is in principle unbounded; in practice, what cap on |d| is used? How are rare/large distances handled (bucketing, clipping, “>K” bin)? This directly affects label set size and data sparsity but is not specified.
  - The stance label inventory is mixed (Supp/Att vs. For/Ag) inside the same Y. How are these disjoint relation types disambiguated in training and evaluation? The example uses (B,C,⊥,For) for claims, but earlier text suggests claims are re-linked to one MC with a real distance; this is inconsistent.
- Potentially unfair or non-parallel comparisons:
  - Dependency parsers are trained only at paragraph level due to memory; other models are evaluated at both levels. The main negative result (“parsing is ineffective”) is weakened by this mismatch. The one essay-level parser result is both very low and “varied drastically”; more controlled settings or reduced-scope parsing (e.g., component-level rather than token-level trees) would make this comparison fairer.
  - LSTM-ER leverages sentence-level dependency TreeLSTMs, yet 92% of relations cross sentences; the paper claims this is “ineffective” but still reports strong paragraph-level results. An ablation without tree information (sequence-only bi-affine or MLP pair scorer) is needed to isolate the contribution and support the claim that the tree component is indeed unhelpful.
  - Different taggers use different capacities (BLCC with char-CNN+CRF vs. BL without CRF/char). Char+CRF confounds the MTL effect; a fair MTL comparison should use the same base architecture as STagBLCC.
- Evaluation clarity and rigor:
  - The “global F1” is defined as “the F1 of C-F1 and R-F1,” which is mathematically unclear (F1 of F1s?). If it’s a macro-average, say so; if an F1 of two scores, justify.
  - No variance across random seeds is reported; yet some models show high variance. Significance claims are given but without the number of runs or clarification whether per-document pairing comes from a single run or averaged runs.
  - Hyperparameters, embeddings, and training details are deferred to supplement; however, key choices (label set size, decoding constraints) are core to reproducibility and should be summarized in the main text.
- Modeling choices may bias outcomes:
  - Token-level dependency trees connect every non-argumentative token to END, which creates a heavily skewed training signal likely to bias parsers toward predicting O arcs. A more faithful parsing baseline would compress contiguous O spans or pre-segment to candidate units to avoid overwhelming non-argument structure.
  - The tagging formulation encodes a strong single-head constraint via the label; LSTM-ER does not. This design advantage is central to the essay-level performance gap and should be contextualized as an inductive-bias difference, not just a model capacity difference.
- Presentation and formatting quality:
  - Numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “dection,” “seperately”), inconsistent notation, and several unclear or dangling references. The reference list is incomplete or malformed in many entries (missing venues/years; “?, 2016”; inconsistent punctuation). Figure/table captions and cross-references are sometimes vague. For ACL standards, the writing and bibliography need substantial polishing.

Questions to authors
- Sequence tagging decoding:
  - How do you enforce valid BIO sequences and a single outgoing relation per component at test time? What exact post-processing is applied when token-level (b,t,d,s) tags disagree within a predicted component?
  - What is the maximum encoded distance |d| and how was it chosen? How many distinct labels in Y were actually instantiated in training?
- Stance and relation types:
  - Why mix Supp/Att with For/Ag in a single label space? Are claims always assigned d=⊥ with For/Ag, or do they have actual d to a selected MC? How is the re-linking to a single MC operationalized?
- Fairness and ablations:
  - Can you provide a sentence/paragraph-independent relation scorer for LSTM-ER (no TreeLSTM) and report its effect? Conversely, can you add a simple global constraint (single parent) during decoding for LSTM-ER to test whether the gap at essay level is due to constraints?
  - For MTL, can you report results with the same BLCC base (char+CRF) to isolate MTL gains from architectural differences?
- Evaluation details:
  - Define “global F1” precisely. Report mean/std over multiple seeds and clarify how significance was computed.
  - For parsing, can you provide a component-level parsing baseline (nodes=predicted components) to reduce token-level O-dominance and make the comparison more equitable?

Overall, while the empirical insights are valuable, the paper has multiple methodological ambiguities, non-parallel comparisons, and significant presentation issues. In its current form, I lean toward rejection; with clarifications, ablations, and substantial polishing, this could become a strong contribution.
