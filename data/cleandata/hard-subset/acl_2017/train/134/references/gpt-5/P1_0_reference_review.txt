Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset. The authors explore four framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing, (2) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding component boundaries, types, relation distance, and stance, (3) multi-task sequence tagging using component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks, and (4) adapting Miwa and Bansal’s LSTM-ER entity–relation model. They compare against a strong feature-based joint ILP baseline.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical study establishing neural end-to-end AM as competitive or better than the prior ILP-based joint model, with careful comparisons across document granularity (paragraph vs. essay) and across modeling paradigms.
2) A practical and effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance into labels, achieving robust gains and offering interpretable inductive bias favoring short-range links; and demonstration that auxiliary multi-task objectives (component-only, relation-only) improve performance.
3) A negative result showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM in this end-to-end setting, together with analysis explaining sensitivity to long sequences and search space.

Strengths
- Strong, comprehensive empirical evaluation with multiple neural paradigms and competitive baselines. The paper compares sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), multi-task tagging, token-level dependency parsers (feature-based and neural), and LSTM-ER, plus the ILP baseline, across paragraph and essay levels. Reporting both exact and approximate span matching and relation F1, together with significance tests (for Table 1), makes the claims credible.

- Effective, simple tagging formulation with distance-aware labels. Encoding component distance and stance directly in the tag space is a clever way to imbue the model with locality bias without hand-crafted features or global constraints. The method yields consistently strong component and relation results and is robust to long documents compared to parsers.

- Clear analysis of length effects and modeling trade-offs. The paper convincingly shows why LSTM-ER excels on paragraphs (few entities → manageable relation space) but degrades on essays (O(m^2) candidate relations), whereas the tagging scheme’s one-to-one link and distance encoding constrain search effectively. This yields actionable guidance on when to prefer constrained vs. general relation models.

- Multi-task learning improves end-to-end AM. Adding natural auxiliary tasks (component-only and relation-only) produces measurable gains over the monolithic tagger, supporting the claim that decoupling supervision while sharing representations helps, and aligning with broader NLP MTL findings.

- Useful negative result on token-level dependency parsing. The work carefully evaluates several parsers and provides plausible explanations (global tree search over long sequences, stack-history vs. minimal features) for poor performance, which will steer the community away from unpromising directions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; contributions are primarily empirical. The architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, LSTM-ER, standard MTL) are off-the-shelf; the main novelty lies in the specific AM label design (distance, stance) and the systematic comparative study. This is valuable but methodological innovation is modest.

- Single-domain dataset; uncertain generalization to other AM schemas. All results are on the PE dataset with its specific structure (e.g., premises have exactly one outgoing link; claims link to all major claims; most relations cross sentences but rarely cross paragraphs except claim↔major claim). It is unclear whether the proposed tagging scheme (particularly the one-link constraint and distance coding) and observed trade-offs generalize to other AM datasets with different graph structures (non-trees, multi-headed relations, dialog data).

- Potential label sparsity and under-specification of the distance-coded tagging scheme. The composite label space can become large as distance grows; while the authors argue the set is finite in practice, details are missing: whether distances were capped/bucketed, how unseen distances at test time are handled, and the impact on OOV labels and data sparsity. This is especially pertinent for essays where long-range links exist.

- Fairness and completeness of baselines. The paper does not include a strong CRF (or neural-CRF without CNN) sequence tagger with feature templates tailored for AM as a non-neural baseline, nor a pipeline baseline using modern neural components for segmentation and typing. It would help to disentangle the gains due to distance-aware labels vs. gains due to modern tagging architectures. For LSTM-ER, since 92% of relations are cross-sentence, relying on sentence-level dependency TreeLSTMs may handicap the model; a document-level relation encoder (e.g., graph neural network) would be a stronger instantiation for AM.

- Reproducibility and variance reporting. Many training details are deferred to supplementary material; for MTL (Table 2), no statistical significance or variance across random seeds is reported, and the paper notes high sensitivity for some parsers. Given known variance in neural AM, reporting mean±std over multiple runs for key systems would strengthen conclusions.

Questions to authors
- Distance labels: Was there a maximum |d| enforced (capping/bucketing)? How were rare/long distances handled during training and at test time? Did you try distance bucketing and how did it affect performance/label sparsity?

- Claim–major claim relations: Claims link to all major claims in the annotation. How exactly is this represented in STagT? In the example, claim tokens have d=⊥. Are claim↔major-claim links ignored in tagging, or handled separately? If ignored for tagging, how is R-F1 computed for those links?

- Paragraph vs. essay settings: Do models know paragraph boundaries during essay-level training/inference? Does injecting paragraph segmentation (e.g., resetting states) help essay-level relation F1?

- LSTM-ER configuration: Are dependency trees gold or automatic? Given most relations are cross-sentence, did you experiment with removing the TreeLSTM or replacing it with a document-level encoder (e.g., BiLSTM over component spans or a graph encoder), and if so, how did results change?

- Variance and significance: For STagBLCC, LSTM-ER, and the best MTL configurations, what is the variance over multiple random seeds? Are improvements in Table 2 significant?

Additional comments
- An ablation showing the effect of distance coding (full tags vs. tags without d) would isolate its contribution.
- Error analysis per class (Premise/Claim/MC; Support/Attack/For/Against) would be informative.
- Public release of code and preprocessing would aid reproducibility.

Overall, this is a solid empirical contribution with clear takeaways for end-to-end AM, despite limited methodological novelty and some missing details.
