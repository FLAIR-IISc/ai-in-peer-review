Summary
The paper studies end-to-end neural approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. It compares four framings: (1) token-level dependency parsing; (2) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding BIO component boundaries, component type, inter-component distance, and stance; (3) multi-task sequence tagging using component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks; and (4) adapting an entity-relation (LSTM-ER) model that jointly extracts entities and relations. Experiments report component and relation F1 at exact and approximate span matching, on paragraph and essay levels, and compare against a feature-based pipeline with ILP constraints. Key findings: token-level dependency parsing underperforms; sequence tagging is robust and outperforms the ILP baseline; MTL improves over single-task tagging; LSTM-ER excels for components and on paragraphs but degrades for relations on essays due to combinatorial relation space. The work claims new state-of-the-art end-to-end AM results on PE without hand-crafted features.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural end-to-end framings for AM, showing sequence tagging (with distance-augmented labels) and MTL outperform a strong feature-based ILP pipeline, with analyses across paragraph vs. essay granularity. This is the primary contribution given its practical impact and comprehensive evaluation.
2) Evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, contrasting with component-level parsing results in prior work; and that decoupling entity and relation modeling (LSTM-ER) helps component detection but needs constraints for long documents.
3) A simple, domain-tailored sequence-tagging label design that encodes inter-component distance and stance, plus a demonstration that auxiliary tasks (component-only and relation-only) in MTL further improve performance.

Strengths
- Comprehensive, controlled comparison across modeling paradigms: The paper evaluates four distinct, reasonable framings under the same dataset and metrics, including both paragraph and essay settings. This breadth clarifies trade-offs (local vs. global models, decoupled vs. joint modeling) and yields actionable guidance (e.g., prefer restrained relation modeling on long documents).
- Strong empirical results and analysis: The sequence tagging model (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN) and LSTM-ER achieve or surpass the ILP baseline on paragraph level, and the tagging model remains competitive on essay level. The paper supplies per-task F1 (exact/approximate), significance testing, and analyses of distance distributions and error patterns (e.g., relation accuracy vs. true distance).
- Clear demonstration of MTL benefits: Adding component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks boosts the main task by several F1 points, particularly for components, with a transparent setup (shared BiLSTM layers, varied tapping depths).
- Useful negative and positive findings: The token-level dependency parsing negative result is informative for the community, as is the observation that simple local models handle long sequences better than global parsers under data scarcity.
- Reduction in manual engineering: The neural approaches eliminate hand-crafted features and ILP constraint design while improving or matching performance, strengthening the case for portability to diverse AM annotation schemes.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; contribution is primarily empirical: The proposed sequence tagging and MTL formulations leverage standard architectures and prior ideas (distance-aware labels resemble relation offset encodings; MTL for sequence tagging is established). The novelty lies in application and evaluation for AM rather than methodological advances.
- Incomplete methodological details in the main text: Critical training choices (pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, exact distance label handling, class balancing, inference constraints) are deferred to the supplement. This hinders reproducibility and assessment of fairness across models in the main paper.
- Constraint mismatch and fairness of comparison: The tagging formulation encodes a hard constraint of a single outgoing link per component via its label, while LSTM-ER allows many-to-many relations. This difference partly explains LSTM-ER’s essay-level relation drop and makes comparisons less direct. Similarly, the ILP baseline has access to global essay/paragraph context, potentially advantaging certain components (major claims), while some neural models are trained on paragraphs only.
- Single-dataset evaluation: All results are on the PE dataset with specific structural regularities (e.g., paragraph-local relations, claims linking to all major claims). It is unclear how well the findings generalize to other AM corpora (e.g., user-generated web discourse, legal corpora) with different structures and longer-range relations.
- Token-level parsing framing may be underspecified: Parsers were trained only at paragraph level due to memory limits; essay-level results for the best parser vary widely across runs/params. It remains unclear whether stronger data regimes, different encodings (e.g., component-level nodes), or projective/non-projective settings would change the conclusion.

Questions to Authors
- Distance label design: What is the maximum absolute distance encoded in the tag set? Are distances clipped/bucketed, and how sensitive are results to this choice? What is the effective label inventory size and its class distribution?
- Postprocessing and constraints: Do the tagging models employ any decoding constraints or post-hoc repair to ensure valid structures (e.g., exactly one outgoing link per premise, stance consistency)? If not, how are invalid outputs handled in evaluation?
- LSTM-ER adaptation: Which dependency parser and settings are used to produce the trees for the TreeLSTM relation module? Given that 92% of relations cross sentences, did you ablate the tree module or try sentence-agnostic encoders for relations?
- Re-linking claims to a single major claim: How often does this structural normalization change the original graph, and could it bias models or evaluation? Have you measured sensitivity of results to the choice of surrogate root/major claim?
- Hyperparameters and resources: Please provide the key training settings in the main paper (embedding types and dimensions, BiLSTM depth/hidden sizes, dropout, batch sizes, learning rates, early stopping) and training/inference runtimes to assess practicality.
- Generalization: Did you attempt transfer or cross-domain evaluation (e.g., microtexts, web discourse), or at least cross-validation within PE to assess robustness? If not, what are the anticipated failure modes outside PE?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound in its empirical setup, and offers domain-relevant findings. The main value is a careful comparative study and strong results for end-to-end AM without manual features, rather than architectural novelty.
