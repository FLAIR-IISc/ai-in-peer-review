- Paper compares four end-to-end neural framings for argumentation mining on the PE corpus: token-level dependency parsing, compound-label sequence tagging, multi-task sequence tagging, and LSTM-ER adaptation, evaluated at paragraph and essay levels against an ILP pipeline.  
- Main empirical finding: a BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagger with distance-augmented labels outperforms prior ILP pipelines and token-level parsers; LSTM-ER is strong on paragraphs but degrades on full essays.  
- Methodological insight: decoupling component and relation prediction while modeling them jointly (multi-tasking) yields small, consistent gains.  
- Negative result: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, especially on long sequences.  
- Strength: clear end-to-end framing with thorough, fair comparisons across neural formulations and identical metrics; paragraph vs. essay split highlights long-context challenges.  
- Strength: strong baselines and fair component/relation comparisons; sequence-tagging model shows large F1 gains at paragraph level and analysis links relation F1 to component F1.  
- Strength: insightful analysis of distance/length effects showing distance-augmented labels bias toward short links and reduce relation search complexity.  
- Strength: careful negative result on token-level dependency parsing with multiple parsers evaluated and plausible explanations for performance drop on essays.  
- Strength: practical guidance for system design—use general relation models only when entity counts are small, otherwise prefer restrained modeling.  
- Weakness: unclear generalization beyond PE corpus; sequence-tagging relies on tree-like assumptions and compact distance distributions, limiting portability to other domains.  
- Weakness: token-level dependency encoding and claim re-linking are unnatural and may distort supervision, weakening the claim that dependency parsing is ineffective for AM overall.  
- Weakness: compound label space (BIO×type×distance×stance) raises sparsity/scalability concerns; no ablations on distance caps, bucketing, or label cardinality.  
- Weakness: limited exploration of stronger structured/neural baselines and of mitigations for LSTM-ER essay degradation (pruning, distance features, span-based models, neural-ILP hybrids).  
- Weakness: decoding and constraint-handling are under-specified—conversion from token tags to component edges, conflict resolution, and constraint enforcement are not clearly described.  
- Key question: how are relations decoded from sequence tags into component spans and single edges, and what post-hoc constraints or conflict resolutions are applied?  
- Key question: what is the effective size of label set Y during training, were distances capped or bucketed, and how sensitive are results to distance handling?  
- Key question: for LSTM-ER, were candidate pruning or explicit distance features tried to mitigate O(m^2) search at essay level?  
- Key question: how is re-linking of claims to a single major claim handled in training/evaluation, and does it alter supervision for multi-headed claims?  
- Key question: provide runtime/memory, training stability details, and hyperparameter search ranges for reproducibility.  
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance—solid empirical study with actionable insights and new SOTA on PE—conditional on clarifying decoding/constraints and discussing generalization.
