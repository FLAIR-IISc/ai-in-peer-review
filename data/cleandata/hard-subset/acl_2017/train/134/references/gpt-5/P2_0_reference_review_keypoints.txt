- Paper compares neural end-to-end AM framings: token-level dependency parsing, composite-label sequence tagging, multi-task tagging, and adapted LSTM-ER on the Persuasive Essays corpus.
- Main contribution: comprehensive empirical comparison establishing strong neural baselines and new SOTA on PE, with analysis of document-length effects.
- Main contribution: practical sequence-tagging formulation encoding relation distance and stance into tags, yielding robust, local-biased relation performance.
- Main contribution: evidence-based modeling insights—token-level parsing is ineffective, decoupling entities and relations helps, and MTL yields modest gains.
- Strength: broad, rigorous empirical evaluation with paragraph- and essay-level results, exact/approx F1, and significance testing.
- Strength: simple, effective tagging that reduces relation search space and biases toward plausible local relations, improving robustness on longer documents.
- Strength: clear analysis explaining performance degradation at essay level and why constrained local models degrade less.
- Strength: practical recommendations for the community (avoid token-level parsing; prefer distance-encoded tagging; decouple entities/relations; use MTL).
- Strength: reproducible setup with standard models, referenced metrics, significance tests, and supplementary implementation details/code links.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—mainly adaptation of known architectures and composite tag design.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single, idiosyncratic dataset (PE), raising generality concerns.
- Weakness: dependency-parsing baseline is an unrealistic strawman; better baselines would parse over predicted spans or use span-based parsing.
- Weakness: MTL comparison is not apples-to-apples because MTL uses a weaker base tagger (BiLSTM without CRF/char CNN), confounding gains with model capacity.
- Weakness: missing/unclear details on metrics, distance handling, tag-space size, and hyperparameter tuning fairness across models.
- Specific missing details: “Acc.” in Table 1 undefined; handling/clipping of unbounded distance d not described; tag-space sparsity not analyzed.
- Key questions: How is distance d bounded and how sensitive are results to that threshold?
- Key questions: What is the size of the composite label set after pruning, and how many labels are rare/singleton?
- Key questions: Can MTL be reported with the BLCC backbone (CRF + char CNN) or can ablations isolate MTL benefits?
- Key questions: Have you tried a two-stage pipeline (span extraction then parsing/classification) to better assess parsing vs tagging?
- Key questions: Please define “Acc.” and clarify the exact significance testing protocol used.
- Overall assessment: Valuable, thorough empirical contribution with clear takeaways and SOTA results on PE, though stronger parsing/MTL baselines, cross-corpus validation, and clarification of distance/tagging details would strengthen the paper.
