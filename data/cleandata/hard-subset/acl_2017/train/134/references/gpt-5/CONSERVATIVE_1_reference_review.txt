Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical study of multiple neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) on the PE dataset: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance/stance encoding, multi-task tagging, and an adaptation of LSTM-ER, compared against the established feature-based ILP pipeline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016).
- A practical sequence tagging formulation that encodes component boundaries/types jointly with relation distance and stance into token-level labels, avoiding hand-crafted features/ILP constraints and yielding strong results (especially at paragraph level).
- Analysis of when more general relation models (e.g., LSTM-ER) help vs. when more constrained models (sequence tagging with single-head bias) are preferable, with insights about long sequences and distance distributions.

Strengths
- Thorough comparative evaluation across several established neural paradigms, with a clear experimental split (paragraph vs. essay) and statistical testing for the main results. This helps the community calibrate expectations for end-to-end AM and disentangles modeling choices (global parsing vs local tagging; coupled vs decoupled entity–relation).
- Practical, simple tagging formulation that encodes relation distance and stance in the label set. This is a clean way to impose useful inductive bias (single-head, near-neighbor preference) without hand-designed ILP constraints, and it performs robustly across settings.
- Sensible analysis of failure modes and trade-offs: (i) token-level dependency parsing performs poorly in this end-to-end setting; (ii) LSTM-ER excels on components but degrades on relations for long sequences; (iii) distance distributions matter (Figure 4). These observations are valuable for practitioners.
- Some evidence that multi-task setups with component and relation subtasks can improve the joint end-to-end tagger, consistent with the literature on auxiliary-task benefits.
- The comparison includes the strongest available non-neural baseline (the ILP pipeline), and the reported gains at paragraph level are meaningful for both components and relations.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. Most architectures are standard (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, TreeLSTM-based LSTM-ER, multi-task BiLSTMs). The primary novelty is the label design encoding distance, which is incremental. The negative result on token-level dependency parsing is not surprising given discourse/AM typically operate on component-level units; testing token-level parsers may be a straw man.
- Evaluation scope is narrow. All results are on a single dataset (PE). Claims about eliminating hand-crafted features/constraints and about robustness of the tagging framing would be more convincing with at least one additional corpus (e.g., microtexts), or a cross-domain test. Given known annotation-theory variability in AM, the generality of the findings is uncertain.
- Fairness and comparability concerns. Many neural models are trained/evaluated at paragraph level due to memory limits, while the ILP baseline is said to have access to both levels and paragraph position information. The mixture of paragraph- and essay-level evaluations complicates direct comparisons. A unified evaluation (same units and information) is needed to isolate model effects.
- Stability and reproducibility issues. The paper notes “drastic” variance across parametrizations (e.g., essay-level LSTM-Parser) but reports no variance across random seeds, no confidence intervals, and defers key training details (embeddings, early stopping, hyperparameters) to supplementary material. For the tagger with distance labels, the cardinality of the label set and any capping/bucketing of d are not specified; sparsity and OOV label issues at test time are not analyzed.
- Structural assumptions may distort the task. The re-linking of claims to a single major claim and introduction of a special root to enforce near-tree structure is described as “loss-less,” but it changes the original graph semantics (claims originally relate to multiple major claims). It is unclear how this affects learning and evaluation. More generally, the paper argues constraints can be “learned,” yet the empirical evidence comes mainly from the inductive bias in the tagging labels rather than from learning explicit structural constraints.

Questions to authors
- Distance labeling: What is the maximum absolute distance d represented in the label set? Do you cap or bucket distances, and how many distinct d values are present in training/testing? How often do test-time distances fall outside the training range, and how are such cases handled?
- Stability: Please report mean/std over multiple random seeds for key systems (STagBLCC, LSTM-ER) on both paragraph and essay levels. How sensitive are results to embedding choice, hidden sizes, and dropout?
- Fairness of comparisons: For paragraph-level experiments, do all systems (including ILP) operate with exactly the same inputs and knowledge (e.g., paragraph boundaries, paragraph position)? If ILP leverages global essay context (e.g., paragraph indices), can you provide a version restricted to paragraph-only information?
- Re-linking to major claims: Please clarify the exact transformation, and whether gold labels/evaluation are performed on the transformed structure or the original annotations. If transformed, can you quantify the impact on relation labeling difficulty?
- Baselines: Why not include a strong CRF-based sequence tagger baseline (with hand-crafted features) for the end-to-end setting, and/or a pipeline neural baseline (components via BiLSTM-CRF, then relation classification on predicted components)? These would better isolate the benefit of your joint tagging formulation.
- Resources: Will you release code, trained models, and scripts to reproduce the reported numbers (both paragraph and essay), including the data preprocessing and the label construction?

Overall, this is a useful empirical study with clear takeaways for practitioners, but the methodological novelty is modest and the evidence is limited to a single dataset with some comparability concerns. I encourage the authors to strengthen the experimental rigor and broaden validation.
