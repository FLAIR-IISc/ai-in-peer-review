Summary and contributions
This paper studies end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays (PE) dataset. It compares four framings: token-level dependency parsing, sequence tagging with composite labels that encode component boundaries/types, relation distance, and stance (STagT), multi-task tagging (MTL) with auxiliary sub-tasks, and an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER model. It reports results at paragraph and essay levels and compares to a feature-based ILP pipeline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical comparison of multiple neural end-to-end AM framings, with the key finding that simple sequence tagging (with distance-coded labels) and LSTM-ER outperform a strong ILP pipeline on paragraph-level, while token-level dependency parsing underperforms.
2) A practical tagging formulation that encodes inter-component distance directly into the label and an MTL setup that uses component-only and relation-only sub-tasks to improve full AM tagging.
3) Experimental analysis across paragraph vs. essay levels, articulating when more constrained vs. more general models work better.

Strengths
- End-to-end neural baselines for AM with thorough coverage: The paper evaluates four substantially different neural paradigms under a unified evaluation protocol. This breadth is valuable to the community and goes beyond single-model papers.
- Clear empirical takeaways: The results consistently show that (i) token-level dependency parsing is weak for this task, (ii) sequence tagging with distance coding is robust, and (iii) decoupling entities and relations helps (LSTM-ER, and analogously via MTL). The paragraph/essay split is informative.
- Competitive performance against a strong ILP pipeline: On paragraph level, neural models meet or exceed the ILP pipeline (which also has access to essay-level structural cues per the paper), showing practical viability without handcrafted features/constraints.
- Sensible analysis of distance effects: Encoding distances in tags biases the model toward local links and appears to mitigate the combinatorial explosion of relation pairings in long documents, which is a thoughtful design and supported by the distance-wise accuracy plot.

Weaknesses
- Questionable “loss-less” tree conversion and evaluation consistency: The dataset has claims linking to all major claims. The paper states it “loss-lessly” re-links claims to one major claim and introduces a special root. This is not loss-less if multiple gold edges are collapsed/reassigned. It is unclear how evaluation handles the original multi-target relations. If gold is altered to a tree to suit some models, this changes the task; if not, tree-enforcing models are penalized. This needs precise description and, ideally, results without altering the gold structure.
- Dependency parsing framing fairness and validity: Standard parsers assume sentence-internal trees, single roots per sentence, and no cross-sentence arcs. Here, paragraphs are parsed as single sequences, with all non-argumentative tokens attached to END and many inter-sentence arcs. This violates assumptions for all four parsers compared. Without clarifying how these parsers were adapted (e.g., allowing cross-sentence, multi-root handling, label set size, projectivity), the conclusion that token-level dependency parsing is “ineffective” is not well supported; it may be an artifact of mis-specified inputs/objectives.
- Label set explosion and distance handling underspecified: Y includes (b, t, d, s) with d unbounded in principle. The paper notes observed |d| up to 11 but does not specify capping, bucketing, or handling unseen distances at test time. With CRF decoding, the number of labels can be very large, increasing data sparsity and decoding complexity. Report exact tag set sizes used, distance bucketing (if any), and OOV-distance behavior.
- Comparability and class imbalance: LSTM-ER uses entity pretraining and scheduled sampling; were analogous training aids tried for STagT to make comparisons fair? At essay level, pairwise relation classification incurs extreme negative/positive imbalance; there is no detail on negative sampling, loss weighting, or candidate pruning for LSTM-ER, which likely explains the severe R-F1 drop. STagT’s single-link constraint hard-codes a strong bias and reduces negatives; this should be discussed as a fairness issue and ablated.
- Reporting, reproducibility, and statistics: Critical details (embeddings, hyperparameters, label inventories, training regimes, early stopping, dev selection) are relegated to supplementary material and not summarized. Table 1 includes significance tests but Table 2 (MTL) does not; multiple comparisons are not addressed; pairing per document for Wilcoxon is reasonable but not discussed for dependency between C-F1 and R-F1. Lack of variance/confidence intervals further hinders assessment.

Questions to authors
1) “Loss-less” relinking: Precisely describe how you reconcile the dataset’s multi-target claim→major-claim edges with tree constraints. Is the gold modified? If so, how do you evaluate against the original multi-target structure? Please provide scores under the original graph without relinking.
2) Dependency parsing details: How were MST, Mate, Kiperwasser, and LSTM-Parser adapted to paragraph-level, cross-sentence parsing with a global END root? Were non-projective arcs allowed? What was the arc label set (did it include composite BIO/type/stance)? Did you tune for label set size effects?
3) Tag set size and distance: What is the exact size of Y for STagBLCC/BL? How are distances capped or bucketed? How do you handle test-time distances not observed in training?
4) LSTM-ER training: How did you handle the O(m^2) relation candidates at essay level (negative sampling, class weighting, pruning by distance)? Did you try candidate restriction by distance to match the STagT inductive bias?
5) MTL selection: How were the “layers from which tasks feed” chosen (dev-only)? Did you control for multiple trials when picking “one of the best parametrizations”? Please add significance testing for Table 2 deltas.

Additional comments
- Clarity/typos: Several typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “dection”, “seperately”; Support/Attack vs For/Against terminology; inconsistent use of Supp/Att/For/Ag). Please standardize stance labels and clearly map dataset relations to your s labels.
- References and formatting: Many references are incomplete or have placeholders (“?, ”), missing venues/pages. Figures are referenced but not visible here; ensure they are legible and captioned. Table labels and notation could be clearer (e.g., define “Acc.”).
- Fairness: Some models are trained only at paragraph level due to memory; consider reporting essay-level results for all (even with truncated documents) to avoid level-specific bias, or clearly separate comparisons.
- Ablations: Please provide: (i) STagT without distance, (ii) with distance bucketing, (iii) with/without CRF/CNN, and (iv) LSTM-ER with candidate pruning by distance, to isolate where gains come from.
- Reproducibility: Include essential hyperparameters, pretrained embeddings, and code links in the main text, plus runtime/memory footprints.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The study is promising and potentially impactful, but methodological and reporting issues (especially the “loss-less” relinking, parser fairness, and incomplete training/comparison details) undermine the main conclusions. Clarifications and stronger, fairer comparisons could change this assessment.
