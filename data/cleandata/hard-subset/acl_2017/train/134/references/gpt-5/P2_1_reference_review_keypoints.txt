- Paper compares four end-to-end AM framings (token-level dependency parsing, composite-label sequence tagging, multi-task tagging, adapted LSTM-ER) on the Stab & Gurevych essays corpus and against a feature-based pipeline+ILP baseline.  
- Primary contribution: BiLSTM-CRF-CNN sequence tagging with distance-coded composite labels achieves strong end-to-end performance and matches/exceeds ILP at paragraph/essay level; MTL auxiliary tasks further improve results.  
- Secondary contributions: demonstrates token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, analyzes document-length effects (paragraph vs essay), and shows tagging’s one-link constraint and distance bias help on long texts.  
- Clear empirical comparisons across model families, with explanations of when and why each formalism helps.  
- Length- and distance-based analyses provide concrete evidence (e.g., LSTM-ER best on paragraphs; tagging model better on essays due to R-F1 gains).  
- Strong, feature-free baselines: the composite-label BLCC tagger handles long-range dependencies without hand-crafted features or ILP.  
- MTL variants yield consistent gains, supporting decoupled supervision for substructures (components and relations).  
- The negative result on token-level dependency parsing is well-justified via experiments with multiple parsers and plausible explanations related to global search and token-vs-span mismatch.  
- Rigorous evaluation: statistical testing (Wilcoxon) and both paragraph- and essay-level metrics highlight granularity effects.  
- Practical modeling choices (distance encoding, one-link constraint) are simple, interpretable, and empirically effective.  
- Main weakness: evaluation on a single, near-tree corpus risks overfitting to that annotation scheme and may not generalize to non-tree or noisier AM datasets.  
- Composite tag space sparsity and scalability concerns due to distance dimension; no ablation on bucketing/clipping or sensitivity analysis for unseen distances.  
- Dependency-parsing framing may be a strawman because token-level heads produce unnatural trees; span-based parsing alternatives are not explored.  
- Reporting clarity issues: ILP table numbers and parser training granularity (paragraph-only) are unclear, and some hyperparameter/details are relegated to the supplement.  
- Limited ablations and failure-mode analysis: missing CRF/no-CRF, char-CNN, distance-coding, stance-coding, and candidate-pruning studies in main text.  
- Key questions for authors: how distances are capped/bucketed and sensitivity to that; whether paragraph boundaries are assumed at test time; whether LSTM-ER used pruning at essay-level; whether a span-first formulation was considered; and how ILP baselines were tuned and reported.
