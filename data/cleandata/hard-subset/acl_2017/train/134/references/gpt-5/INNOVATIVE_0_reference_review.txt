Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles end-to-end argumentation mining (AM) from raw text with neural models, exploring four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a novel label design that jointly encodes component boundaries/types and relations via inter-component distance and stance, (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) where component and relation subtasks are auxiliary signals, and (iv) adopting LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) to AM. It evaluates on the Stab & Gurevych persuasive essays (paragraph- and essay-level).
- Primary contribution: a simple, bold sequence-tagging formulation with distance-coded labels that enables joint end-to-end extraction without hand-crafted features/constraints, delivering strong, often SOTA, results and robust behavior across settings.
- Secondary contributions: (a) a careful, empirical comparison showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM despite discourse-parsing intuitions; (b) evidence that MTL with natural AM subtasks (component vs relation) improves over single-task tagging; (c) insights into data-regime effects—general models (LSTM-ER) excel on short documents, restrained models (distance-tagging) are preferable on long documents.

Strengths
- Novel and impactful modeling idea: encoding relations via discrete inter-component distances in a BIO tagging scheme. This is a clever way to impose useful structural bias (one link per component, strong locality prior) while retaining the simplicity and scalability of BiLSTM-CRF tagging. It is conceptually elegant and practically effective, and—importantly—removes the need for hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.
- Meaningful end-to-end advance over prior pipelines. The systems outperform or match a strong ILP-based joint model (with hand-engineered features/constraints) particularly on exact component and relation F1 at the paragraph level, while being simpler and more portable. This moves AM toward more generalizable, data-driven methods.
- Thorough exploration of modeling space, including a negative result. The paper tests multiple paradigms (parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER), not just a single favored model. The negative result for token-level dependency parsing is informative for the community and clarifies modeling choices for end-to-end AM.
- Solid empirical analysis and insights. The paper contrasts paragraph vs essay levels (highlighting long-sequence effects), analyzes distance distributions and their impact, and explains the O(m^2) relation-candidate explosion that hurts LSTM-ER on essays. The observation that auxiliary component detection yields larger gains than auxiliary relation detection is also useful.
- Practical significance. The proposed tagging approach is easy to implement with standard architectures (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), requires minimal task-specific engineering, and should be readily adoptable for other AM datasets. Reporting statistical significance and discussing hyperparameter/practicalities (in supplementary) supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Dataset-specific constraints and generality. The persuasive-essay corpus has properties that favor the proposed approach (near-tree structures; relations mostly intra-paragraph; one outgoing link per premise). The tagging scheme encodes a single relation per component and a distance prior; this may not transfer to AM settings with many-to-many relations, cross-paragraph links, or non-tree/disconnected structures. The paper acknowledges this but does not empirically test on a dataset with different structural assumptions (e.g., microtexts, web arguments).
- Ambiguities in decoding and constraint enforcement for tagging. While the label encodes (b, t, d, s), at test time BIO violations and intra-component disagreement about d/s are likely. The paper does not detail the decoding/post-processing to ensure coherent component spans and consistent link assignments (e.g., how d is made consistent across all tokens of a component; how ties/invalid distances are handled; how non-existent targets are resolved). This bears directly on usability and may affect error modes.
- Fairness/coverage of comparisons. Dependency parsers are trained/tested only at paragraph level due to memory, and performance drops drastically at essay level for the LSTM-Parser. It would be useful to include a component-level neural parser (i.e., detect components first, then parse a graph over components) to test whether the negative result is due to token-level framing rather than “parsing” per se. Similarly, the ILP has access to both levels (and global paragraph position), while neural taggers do not leverage simple global features (e.g., paragraph index) that could close residual gaps on major claims.
- Limited ablations isolating what matters. The gains of the tagging approach could stem from several factors: CRF label dependencies, character CNNs, and the distance/stance augmentation. The paper does not report a clean ablation removing distance from labels or replacing it with a bounded bucketization to quantify its specific contribution, nor does it examine the sensitivity to maximum distance cardinality and class sparsity for larger d.
- Scope of LSTM-ER adaptation. The paper argues TreeLSTM over sentence-level parse trees is ill-matched as most links are cross-sentence, which is plausible, but the evaluation stops at unmodified LSTM-ER. Testing a variant that replaces sentence-level syntactic trees with document-level discourse/paragraph structures (or no tree at all) would strengthen the conclusion and better attribute where the degradation arises.

Questions to authors
- Tagging decoding: How do you enforce consistency of d and s across tokens of the same predicted component? Do you use CRF-only constraints, post-hoc majority voting, or explicit BIO/distance repair? What happens if the predicted distance points outside the component index range?
- Label space and sparsity: Do you cap the set of distance values during training/inference (e.g., bucketize beyond ±K)? How sensitive are results to K, given the observed long tail (−11 to +10)?
- Multiple relations: Your scheme assumes one relation per component. How would you adapt it to corpora with many-to-many relations? Could a multi-label variant or pointer-network decoding be integrated while keeping the tagging simplicity?
- Cross-paragraph and cross-document links: Have you tried the models on essays where claims link across paragraphs (or domains where this is common)? If not, what modifications would you recommend (e.g., hierarchical tagging, paragraph index features)?
- Dependency parsing framing: Did you try a two-stage neural approach—component detection first (neural tagger), then dependency parsing over component nodes? This could disentangle the token-level difficulty from the parsing paradigm itself.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The distance-aware end-to-end tagging formulation is a novel, elegant idea that materially advances AM with minimal engineering. The empirical study is broad and insightful. While some design choices are dataset-tailored and a few ablations/decoding details are missing, the conceptual contribution and demonstrated gains warrant acceptance.
