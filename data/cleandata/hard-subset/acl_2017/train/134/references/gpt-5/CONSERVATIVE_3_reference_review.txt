Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates neural, end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. The authors explore four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes component BIO/type, relation distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary subtasks for components and relations; and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER for joint entity/relation extraction. They compare to the feature-based pipeline + ILP model of Stab & Gurevych (2016).

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) An empirical comparison of several established neural architectures for end-to-end AM, with a clear negative result for token-level dependency parsing and competitive results for sequence tagging and LSTM-ER.
2) A simple yet effective sequence tagging formulation that encodes relation distance in the tag, with multi-task variants that modestly improve performance.
3) Evidence that on the PE dataset, neural models surpass the ILP pipeline on paragraph-level evaluation, though not consistently on essays.

Strengths
1) Thorough empirical study across multiple neural formulations. The paper evaluates four reasonably different modeling choices, including a negative result (token-level dependency parsing), which is valuable to the community. The comparison to a known ILP pipeline baseline is helpful for positioning.

2) End-to-end evaluation on a standard dataset with both component and relation metrics. Using the PN16-style strict and approximate span matching is appropriate, and the authors report both component and relation F1, which is essential since relation F1 depends on component detection.

3) Sequence tagging with distance-augmented labels is practical and performs competitively. Despite being conceptually simple, this design exploits the empirical proximity of linked components in PE and yields robust gains, particularly on paragraph-level data.

4) Sensible analysis of document length. The split between paragraph- and essay-level evaluation, with discussion of why models degrade on longer sequences (especially relation identification), is insightful and supported by data.

5) Some evidence of statistical testing. Table 1 indicates significant differences (Wilcoxon), which is appreciated, although see weaknesses below for missing tests elsewhere.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty; heavy reliance on standard architectures. Most models are off-the-shelf (BiLSTM-CRF(-CNN), LSTM-ER), adapted to AM with minimal task-specific innovation. The main novelty—the distance-augmented tag—while useful, is not deeply explored. There are no ablations on distance bucketing, max distance, or alternative encodings (direction-only, coarse bins), and no comparison to a strong feature-based CRF tagging baseline, which is the established approach for sequence labeling in AM.

2) Claims about beating ILP are not consistently supported. The strongest improvements appear on paragraph-level; on essay-level, the ILP is still competitive or better in global F1. Since “end-to-end” AM is naturally an essay-level task (and the ILP exploits essay-level constraints), the conclusion that neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art pipeline feels overstated. The fairness of comparing ILP at paragraph level is unclear: was the ILP retrained and tuned for paragraphs, and does that setting reflect how ILP systems are typically used?

3) Strong dependence on idiosyncrasies of the PE dataset; limited generalization evidence. The modeling decisions (distance-coded labels; constraints about one link per premise; specific stance labels “For/Against” vs “Support/Attack”; the paragraph containment of structures) heavily align with properties of PE. The “loss-less” re-linking of claims to a single major claim is asserted but not demonstrated; this transformation could alter relation structure semantics. No experiments on other AM corpora (e.g., microtexts) or cross-domain settings are provided.

4) Dependency parsing framing is unnatural at token level and underpowered. Choosing token-level heads (e.g., linking to the first token of a target component) forces parsers to solve a segmentation problem they are not designed for. This negative result is therefore unsurprising and does not exclude component-level parsing formulations. Without a strong component-level parsing baseline or a neural graph parser with component nodes, the takeaway is limited.

5) Incomplete experimental rigor and reporting. Key choices are relegated to the supplement: pre-trained embeddings, hyperparameters, label inventory size for distance (what is the cap?), how unseen/beyond-range distances are handled, and dropout/regularization. The multi-task results show small improvements but lack statistical tests. Variance across random seeds is not reported. Error analysis is only mentioned as supplemental. LSTM-ER is criticized for not enforcing tree constraints, but the authors do not try simple constraints (e.g., at-most-one outgoing link per premise) that could make the comparison fairer.

Questions to authors
1) Distance encoding: What is the final size of the label set Y in STagT (max |d|, treatment of unseen/long distances, any bucketing)? Can you provide ablations comparing (i) no distance; (ii) direction only; (iii) coarse buckets; and (iv) full integer distance?

2) ILP comparison: How exactly was ILP trained and tuned for paragraph-level evaluation? Does it have access to the same paragraph-level constraints as in essays? Please report essay-level comparisons as the primary setting and clarify where neural models do (not) surpass ILP.

3) Re-linking claims to a single major claim: Please justify the “loss-less” claim formally or empirically. How sensitive are results to this design? What happens if you keep the original multi-parent structure?

4) LSTM-ER constraints: Did you try adding simple structural constraints (e.g., at-most-one parent for premises) or distance-aware priors to LSTM-ER to reduce the O(m^2) relation search space at essay-level?

5) Generalization: Can you report results on at least one additional AM dataset (e.g., microtexts) or in a cross-domain setting to assess robustness of the distance-coded tags and MTL design?

6) Statistical significance and variance: Please provide significance tests for the MTL gains and report mean±std over multiple random seeds for all main models.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The empirical study is useful and the distance-aware tagging is a practical idea, but the methodological novelty is limited, the evidence that neural models surpass a strong ILP baseline on the core essay-level end-to-end task is mixed, and important ablations and reporting are missing. Strengthening the experimental rigor, clarifying the fairness of comparisons, and demonstrating generalization beyond PE would substantially improve the paper.
