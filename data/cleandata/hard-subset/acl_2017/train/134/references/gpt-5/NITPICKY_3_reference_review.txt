Summary and contributions
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset. The authors explore: (i) a token-level dependency parsing framing; (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding BIO, component type, distance to linked component, and stance; (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary sub-tasks; and (iv) the LSTM-ER model (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) adapted to AM. They compare against a feature-based joint ILP baseline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016), and report paragraph- vs. essay-level results.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A careful empirical comparison showing that standard neural sequence taggers (with a distance-aware label design) and LSTM-ER are competitive or superior to token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM; MTL provides modest gains.
2) A domain-specific label encoding (BIO, component, distance, stance) that operationalizes long-range relations in AM within a tagging framework and provides a strong practical baseline.
3) An analysis of paragraph- vs. essay-level behavior, with insights on why more general relational models (LSTM-ER) degrade on longer texts.

Strengths
- Comprehensive modeling sweep with informative takeaways: The paper evaluates four substantially different framings, revealing clear patterns: token-level dependency parsing is weak for end-to-end AM; BiLSTM-CRF(+char) tagging is robust; the more general LSTM-ER excels when the number of entities is small (paragraphs) and degrades at essay-level due to quadratic relation candidates. This is valuable practical guidance for the community.

- Distance-aware tagging is effective: Encoding component-to-component distance directly into tags is simple yet powerful for AM, and well-motivated by the empirical distance distribution (Figure 2). It allows local models to exploit long-range information without heavy feature engineering.

- MTL yields consistent, if modest, gains: Adding component classification (YC) as an auxiliary task improves the full end-to-end tagger, a useful finding for practitioners considering task factorization.

- Honest negative result for dependency parsing: The token-level dependency framing is explicitly shown to underperform, with plausible analysis (e.g., overly long sequences, tree search space explosion). Publishing such a negative result helps direct future efforts.

- Statistical testing and per-document pairing: The use of a Wilcoxon signed rank test (p<0.01) to mark best scores is appreciated, though see concerns below.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistent and possibly unfair comparisons to ILP; claims overstate gains: Table 1 suggests the ILP baseline has higher R-F1 on essay-level (34.74/44.29) than STagBLCC (34.82/39.68 for exact/approx; approx notably lower). The global F1 for ILP (44.68/55.23) also appears higher than LSTM-ER (40.87/45.19) and STagBLCC (44.90/50.51) at essay-level. Yet the paper claims “several” neural models outperform ILP and later states ILP is “equal to or lower” than neural systems at paragraph-level, without reporting ILP at paragraph-level. This is contradictory and undermines the central claim. Please reconcile and report ILP under the same evaluation units (paragraph and essay) or temper claims accordingly.

- Critical decoding/details for the tagging approach are missing: With Y as a 4-tuple (b,t,d,s) used as a single tag in a CRF, the label space can be large and sparse. Key questions unanswered: (i) What is the actual tag set size (max |d| modeled; any bucketing/clipping)? (ii) How are BIO consistency and per-component consistency enforced, especially for d and s across tokens within the same component? (iii) How are component-level relations recovered if tokens inside a predicted component disagree on d/s? (iv) How are illegal transitions handled in the CRF with such composite tags? These choices can materially impact both accuracy and reproducibility.

- Ambiguity in metrics and aggregation: “Global F1 is the F1-score of C-F1 and R-F1” is unclear—how exactly is F1 computed from two F1s? Also, an “Acc.” column is reported but never defined. For dependency parsing, what does “Acc.” measure? Token-level O vs. argumentative labels? Labeled attachment for AM arcs? Precise definitions are needed.

- Paragraph vs. essay evaluation protocol not fully specified: The dataset has cross-paragraph links (claims to major claims). How are these handled when training/evaluating at paragraph-level? The paper mentions “re-linking claims” and a special root, but exact procedures and the effect on metrics are not detailed. Moreover, dependency parsers were “trained and tested on paragraph level” due to memory, yet essay-level LSTM-Parser results are reported; training/test parity across models is unclear.

- Syntactic inputs and LSTM-ER configuration underspecified: LSTM-ER relies on dependency trees. Which parser supplies them? Are they gold or predicted? Which hyperparameters and scheduled sampling settings were used? The paper also argues TreeLSTM is ineffective for cross-sentence relations (92% cross-sentence), yet still uses it; a variant without TreeLSTM or with cross-sentence structure should be reported to isolate effects.

- Exploiting dataset idiosyncrasies without adequate discussion: The PE corpus constraints (e.g., claims link to all major claims; premises have exactly one parent; major claims in first/last paragraphs) are heavily leveraged (distance prior, one-parent constraint, paragraph structure). It is unclear whether the methods generalize to datasets without these properties. No experiments beyond PE or ablations removing these priors are provided.

- Presentation and citation quality issues: Many references have broken formatting (”,? 2016”), inconsistent venues, and typos (“seperately”, “dection”). Figures have labeling issues (Figure 4 axis “pr ob.c or re ct”), and stance labels mix “Supp/Att” with “For/Ag” without clearly defining when each applies. These reduce clarity.

Questions to authors
1) Tagging decoding: How do you aggregate token-level (d,s) into component-level relations when tokens within a predicted component disagree? Do you enforce per-component consistency during decoding? What is the exact size of Y (report max |d|, buckets/clipping policy), and how do you handle unseen distances at test time?

2) Metrics: Precisely define “Acc.” and “Global F1”. For global F1, provide the formula. Report per-document vs. micro-averaged choices.

3) ILP comparisons: Do you have paragraph-level ILP results? If not, please refrain from claiming superiority on that level. On essay-level, reconcile claims with Table 1 where ILP seems better on R-F1 and global F1.

4) LSTM-ER setup: Which dependency parser produced trees? Any cross-sentence structure? Exact scheduled sampling and pretraining details? Did you try a variant without TreeLSTM (sequence-only relations) to match the cross-sentence nature of AM?

5) Paragraph/essay protocol: How are cross-paragraph links handled in paragraph-level training/evaluation? Are gold links modified (e.g., re-linked to a root)? Does this change the gold distribution of distances?

6) Reproducibility: Please provide hyperparameters, embeddings, random seeds, early stopping criteria, and code. Without this, results are hard to replicate.

Overall, while the empirical exploration is useful, the paper’s core claims are weakened by comparison inconsistencies, missing methodological detail, and presentation issues.
