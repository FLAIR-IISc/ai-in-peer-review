- Paper evaluates end-to-end neural methods for argumentation mining on the Persuasive Essays corpus, comparing token-level dependency parsing, composite-label sequence tagging, multi-task tagging, and LSTM-ER against an ILP baseline at paragraph and essay levels.

- Main contributions: thorough empirical baselines achieving new state-of-the-art on PE and analysis of when local vs. global models work (paragraph vs. essay).

- Main contributions: a practical sequence-tagging formulation that encodes relation distance and stance, and a multi-task setup showing gains from auxiliary component detection.

- Main contributions: a negative result showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM, with analysis attributing this to tree complexity and long sequences.

- Strength: comprehensive, careful comparison across modeling paradigms providing actionable guidance (e.g., local taggers scale better; relation models help when candidate sets are small).

- Strength: solid performance gains and analysis—BLCC outperforms baselines at paragraph level, LSTM-ER shows strong component F1, and MTL gives consistent improvements; plausible explanations for essay-level drops.

- Strength: methodological clarity with exact/approximate span matching, separated component/relation F1, non-parametric significance testing, and discussion of constraint interactions.

- Strength: useful negative result discouraging token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM and motivating span/component-level alternatives.

- Strength: practical multi-tasking insights—auxiliary component task helps more than relation task, and layer-sharing effects do not strictly follow common heuristics.

- Weakness: limited novelty—mostly adaptations of known architectures and an ad-hoc, dataset-specific distance-augmented label space without exploring more compact alternatives.

- Weakness: token-level dependency parsing conclusion may be premature because the encoding conflates span detection and head selection; span-level or biaffine span-head methods might be fairer tests.

- Weakness: unclear generalization beyond PE—approach relies on PE-specific properties and the sequence-tagging single-outgoing-relation constraint may not hold in other corpora.

- Weakness: MTL evaluation confounded by architecture differences—the MTL backbone lacks CRF/char-CNN, making it hard to isolate multi-task benefits from base-model deficits.

- Weakness: missing details on tagging formulation—handling of unbounded/unseen distance labels, truncation, contradictory predictions, and class imbalance mitigation are not specified.

- Question: What is the maximum |d| observed and used in training, and how are unseen or invalid distance predictions handled at test time?

- Question: How does decoding map per-token (d,s) labels to target components when BIO spans are imperfect, equidistant targets exist, or predicted inventories conflict with distances?

- Question: Did you try adding CRF and char-CNN into the MTL backbone to control for the weaker base model, or can you comment on expected MTL gains starting from BLCC?

- Question: Was the ILP baseline retrained for paragraph-level evaluation or always trained on essays—how were comparable inputs ensured across granularities?

- Question: Did you try a two-stage approach (predict spans with BLCC, then run a span-level parser or biaffine head selection) as a stronger parsing baseline?

- Additional comment: An error analysis by stance type and distance bins would strengthen the empirical claims.

- Additional comment: Report training compute and hyperparameters (optimizer, embeddings, epochs) in the main text to improve reproducibility.
