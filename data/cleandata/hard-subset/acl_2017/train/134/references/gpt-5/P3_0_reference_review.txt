Summary and main contributions
This paper studies end-to-end neural approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus, covering component segmentation/typing and relation identification/typing directly from tokens. The authors compare four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO, component type, distance to linked component, and stance, (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary subtasks (component-only, relation-only), and (iv) an adaptation of Miwa & Bansal’s LSTM-ER entity-relation model. They benchmark at paragraph and essay levels against a strong joint ILP baseline.

As I see them, the key contributions are:
1) A thorough empirical study establishing neural end-to-end baselines for AM, with new state-of-the-art on the PE dataset and detailed analysis of when local vs. more general/global models work (paragraph vs. essay level).
2) A practical sequence-tagging formulation that encodes relation distance and stance in the token labels, and a multi-task setup showing consistent gains from auxiliary component detection.
3) A negative result for token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM, with plausible analysis (tree complexity and long sequences) explaining its weakness vs. taggers.

Strengths
- Comprehensive, careful comparison across modeling paradigms. The paper evaluates local (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN tagger), semi-joint (MTL tagger), and relation-centric (LSTM-ER) models against a structured ILP pipeline, on both paragraph and essay levels. This breadth provides actionable guidance for practitioners (e.g., local taggers scale better with document length; more general relation models shine when the candidate set is small).
- Solid performance gains and sound analysis. The sequence tagger (BLCC) outperforms both neural and feature-based parsers and the ILP baseline on paragraph level; LSTM-ER attains strong component F1 and competitive relation F1 on paragraphs; and MTL brings consistent improvements. The explanation linking O(m^2) relation candidates at essay level to LSTM-ER’s R-F1 drop is convincing, and the distance bias of STagT is a clear advantage on longer texts.
- Methodological clarity and reasonable controls. The paper reports both exact and approximate span matching, separates component and relation F1, uses non-parametric significance testing, and discusses the impact of document granularity. The discussion of constraints (single-parent trees vs. many-to-many edges) and their interaction with learning is insightful, highlighting that models often learn constraints implicitly.
- Useful negative result. Showing that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM (especially at essay length) prevents unproductive research directions and motivates span-level or component-level alternatives.
- Practical insights on multi-tasking. The auxiliary component task helps more than the relation task, and the best layer-sharing does not strictly follow the “lower task → lower layer” heuristic. These observations are valuable for future AM architectures.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; engineering-heavy. Three of the four framings are adaptations of known architectures; the main novelty is the distance-augmented label space for sequence tagging. While empirically useful, this design is ad hoc and dataset-specific. There is no exploration of alternative formulations that keep the label space compact (e.g., decoupling distance prediction from BIO via factorized heads or pointer-style linking).
- Token-level dependency parsing conclusion may be premature. The chosen encoding (head is first token of target; labels carry both BIO and stance) conflates span detection with head selection, which likely induces span inflation errors. A span-based or component-level parsing (with predicted spans) or biaffine head-selection over spans could be a fairer test of “parsing for AM” than token-level trees.
- Generalization beyond PE is unclear. The approach and analysis lean on PE-specific properties: tree-approximate structures, intra-paragraph relations, and claims linking to all major claims (resolved via re-linking). The sequence-tagging constraint that each component has exactly one outgoing relation is not valid in corpora with many-to-many links (microtexts, online debates). LSTM-ER can model such graphs, but its essay-level performance here is weak. The paper would benefit from a second dataset or at least a discussion of how to adapt constraints and label spaces.
- MTL evaluation is confounded by architecture differences. The base MTL tagger (BiLSTM without CRF/char-CNN) is weaker than the BLCC baseline, making it hard to isolate the benefit of multi-tasking vs. the loss from removing CRF/char features. A fairer ablation would add the same MTL protocol atop BLCC, or provide MTL+CRF/char results.
- Missing details and edge cases for the tagging formulation. The paper acknowledges potentially unbounded distance labels but does not specify how unseen distances at test time are handled, whether distances are truncated, or how decoding resolves contradictory predictions (e.g., invalid distances, multiple premises pointing to the same token offset). Class imbalance induced by the expanded label set and how it is mitigated (loss weighting, label smoothing) is not discussed.

Questions to authors
- Distance labels: What is the maximum |d| observed and used during training? How do you handle unseen distances at test time, or predictions of distances that do not map to a valid target (e.g., fewer than |d| components exist in the predicted sequence)?
- Decoding for STagT: Given per-token labels, how do you deterministically map (d, s) to a specific target component (especially when BIO spans are imperfect)? What happens if multiple targets are equidistant or if the predicted component inventory conflicts with the predicted distances?
- MTL architecture: Did you try adding CRF and char-CNN into the MTL backbone to control for the weaker base model? If not, can you comment on expected gains from MTL when starting from BLCC?
- Fairness of ILP comparison: Was the ILP baseline re-trained for paragraph-level evaluation, or is it always trained on essays and evaluated per paragraph? Since ILP “has more information,” can you clarify how you ensured comparable inputs at each granularity?
- Component-level parsing: Did you attempt a two-stage approach—predict spans with BLCC, then run a span-level parser or biaffine head selection—as a stronger parsing baseline?

Additional comments
- A brief error analysis by stance type and distance bins would strengthen the claims in Figures 2 and 4.
- Reporting training compute and hyperparameters (optimizer, embeddings, epochs) in the main text would improve reproducibility.
