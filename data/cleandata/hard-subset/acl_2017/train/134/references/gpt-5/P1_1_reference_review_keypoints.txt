- Paper compares several neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining (tagging with distance-encoded labels, token-level dependency parsing, multi-task tagging, and LSTM-ER) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset.
- Main contribution: a simple sequence-tagging scheme encoding relation distance that yields state-of-the-art results and robust paragraph/essay behavior.
- Main contribution: systematic empirical comparison of neural framings, with guidance on when constrained tagging vs. general relation modeling is preferable.
- Main contribution: diagnostic negative result showing token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM.
- Strength: strong, reproducible empirical study comparing multiple architectures and a strong ILP baseline across paragraph and essay granularity with significance testing.
- Strength: novel yet simple BIO-style tag that encodes relation distance and enforces single-parent premises, improving relation prediction without heavy feature engineering.
- Strength: careful analysis of document length effects explaining LSTM-ER’s R-F1 drop via quadratic candidate growth and distance calibration evidence.
- Strength: multi-task learning (MTL) insights showing auxiliary component sub-tasks help the full task and that component auxiliary is more helpful than relation auxiliary.
- Strength: candid, informative negative analysis of token-level parsing with diagnostics on span errors, stack-history, and long-sequence failures.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single PE corpus with strong structural regularities, raising generalization concerns to other AM domains with cross-paragraph or many-to-many relations.
- Weakness: token-level dependency parsing result may be a strawman—parsing at component granularity or joint two-stage models might perform better.
- Weakness: LSTM-ER adaptation may be unfairly handicapped by using sentence-level syntactic trees while most relations are cross-sentence; document-level encoders could be more appropriate.
- Weakness: missing ablations and training details in main text (distance encoding caps/label size, CRF vs char-CNN vs BiLSTM contributions, scheduled sampling/pretraining, OOV robustness).
- Weakness: metric and error analysis could be deeper—need breakdowns by component type, distance bins, paragraph position, and cross-/intra-sentence relations.
- Question: what is the maximum absolute distance |d| encoded, are distances capped/backed off, what is the resulting tag set size on PE, and how sensitive is performance to tag set size?
- Question: clarify the “loss-less” re-linking of claims to a single major claim and whether this transformation biases training, inference, or evaluation.
- Question: provide exact MTL layer configurations that yielded gains, significance of improvements, and whether a CRF was tried in the MTL base tagger.
- Question: provide ablation results for STagBLCC (CRF vs char-CNN) and for LSTM-ER (scheduled sampling, entity pretraining), and attempts to replace TreeLSTM with document-level encoders.
- Question: have models been tested on other AM datasets without paragraph-containment/tree assumptions, and how would the tagging scheme adapt to many-to-many relations?
- Question: report runtime and memory for essay-level training/inference and compare scalability across the different framings.
