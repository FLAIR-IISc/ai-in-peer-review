Summary and main contributions
- The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) with neural methods, comparing several framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label that encodes BIO component boundaries, component type, relation distance, and stance, (iii) multi-task sequence tagging using natural subtasks (component and relation prediction), and (iv) adapting LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal) to AM. Experiments are conducted on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus at paragraph- and essay-level granularity, with comparisons to a strong feature-based ILP joint model.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) A comprehensive, careful empirical comparison establishing strong neural end-to-end baselines for AM, with new state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset and clear analysis of document-length effects.
  2) A practical sequence-tagging formulation that encodes relation distance in the tag space, yielding robust performance and an interpretable bias toward plausible local relations.
  3) Evidence-based insights on modeling choices: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; decoupling entities and relations while modeling them jointly helps; and MTL with natural subtasks yields modest gains.

Strengths
- Strong empirical study and meaningful comparisons: The paper evaluates multiple well-motivated paradigms (parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) and compares them to a published ILP pipeline. Results are reported at both paragraph and essay levels, with exact and approximate F1 measures, and statistical significance testing. This breadth and rigor substantially strengthen the paper’s claims.
- Clear and effective tagging formulation: Encoding distance and stance into tags is simple, data-driven, and demonstrably useful. It reduces the relation search space and induces the desired locality bias (Fig. 4), leading to robust relation performance, especially on longer documents where unconstrained relation modules struggle.
- Insightful analysis of length and structure: The paper convincingly explains why models degrade at essay level (more entities, combinatorial explosion of candidate pairs), and why constrained local models (sequence tagging) degrade less than global or pairwise models (LSTM-ER). The observation that 92% of relations are cross-sentence puts the TreeLSTM dependency component of LSTM-ER into context.
- Practical guidance for the community: The results suggest clear policy recommendations: avoid token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM; prefer sequence tagging with distance encoding; decouple entities and relations; and exploit MTL with natural subtasks. These are actionable and likely to influence subsequent work.
- Reproducibility and clarity of experimental setup: Models are standard, metrics are defined per prior work, and significance tests are used. The paper references supplementary material with implementation details and code links.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is primarily empirical: The work mainly adapts well-known architectures to AM; the main technical novelty is the composite tag design with distance encoding. While this is sensible and effective, the conceptual innovation is modest. The key value lies in the thorough comparative study rather than in new algorithms.
- Dataset scope and generality: All results are on a single dataset with idiosyncratic structure (near-tree, paragraph-contained links, claims linking to all major claims). Claims about generality (e.g., no handcrafted features/constraints needed) would be stronger with cross-corpus validation (microtexts, web discourse), or at least training on one corpus and testing/adapting to another.
- Dependency parsing framing feels like a strawman: Encoding a tree over tokens, attaching all non-argumentative tokens to END, and heading premise tokens to the target’s first token creates an unnatural, very large search space. Prior AM parsing often operates over component spans. A stronger parsing baseline would first predict spans and then parse the component graph (neural span/mention-based DP), or use modern span-based parsing, to better assess “parsing vs tagging.”
- MTL evaluation is not apples-to-apples: The MTL model uses a weaker base tagger (BiLSTM without CRF/char CNN), so the reported MTL gains do not isolate the benefit of multi-tasking from base capacity. An MTL variant on top of BLCC (or ablations adding CRF/char to the MTL setup) would make the case clearer. Gains are modest and sensitivity to layer wiring is not systematically explored.
- Missing/unclear details: 
  - The “Acc.” metric in Table 1 is not defined in the evaluation section.
  - Handling of the unbounded distance d is unclear. Is d clipped? What is the maximum d allowed, and how sensitive are results to that choice? The tag space size and rarity of tags are not discussed, which matters for data sparsity.
  - Fairness of hyperparameter tuning across models and training budgets is not fully documented, and dependency parsers were only trained at paragraph level due to memory, precluding a direct head-to-head at the essay level.

Questions to authors
- How is distance d bounded during training/decoding? What is the maximum |d| in the tag set, and how sensitive are results (especially R-F1) to this threshold?
- What is the size of the composite label set |Y| after pruning/clipping, and how many labels are rare or singleton? Did you consider smoothing/backoff (e.g., binning distances)?
- Can you report MTL results with a BLCC backbone (CRF and char CNN) to isolate the effect of MTL from model capacity? Alternatively, provide ablations of CRF/char in STagBLCC.
- Have you tried a two-stage neural pipeline: sequence tagging to extract spans followed by a neural parser or pairwise classifier over component candidates? This would better disentangle “token-level parsing is ineffective” from “parsing over spans.”
- Please define the “Acc.” column and clarify your significance testing protocol (per-document pairing is good; were tests applied to all metric pairs?).

Overall assessment
The paper offers a thorough and valuable empirical contribution with clear takeaways for end-to-end AM. While methodological novelty is limited, the comparative analysis is careful and practically useful, with new state-of-the-art results on a standard dataset. Addressing the questions above (especially clarifying distance handling and strengthening the MTL and parsing baselines) would further solidify the paper.
