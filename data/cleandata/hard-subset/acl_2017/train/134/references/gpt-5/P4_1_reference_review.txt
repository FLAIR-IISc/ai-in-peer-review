Summary
The paper studies end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) from raw text, covering component segmentation/typing and relation identification/typing. It investigates several neural formulations: (a) token-level dependency parsing; (b) sequence tagging with a composite BIO label that includes component type, inter-component distance, and relation type; (c) multi-task tagging where component- and relation-only subtasks serve as auxiliaries; and (d) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (BiLSTM-CRF for entities plus TreeLSTM-based relation classifier). Experiments on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus compare both paragraph- and essay-level prediction against a feature-rich pipeline+ILP baseline. Key findings: neural models outperform the ILP baseline on most settings; tagging consistently beats token-level dependency parsing; LSTM-ER is strongest on paragraph-level but degrades on essay-level relations; multi-task auxiliary supervision improves the tagging model.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, effective end-to-end sequence tagging formulation with distance-augmented labels that achieves state-of-the-art results on PE and improves over a strong ILP baseline, while removing hand-crafted features/constraints.
2) A thorough comparative study of neural framings for end-to-end AM (token-level dependency parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER), including analysis across document length (paragraph vs. essay) and distance, and reporting negative results for the token-level parsing framing.
3) Evidence that de-coupling component and relation detection (while training jointly) and adding natural auxiliary tasks improves performance (multi-task gains for components and relations).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over a strong prior baseline without feature engineering: The BLCC tagger and LSTM-ER achieve higher component and relation F1 on paragraph level than the ILP system; the tagger remains competitive on essay level. Wilcoxon tests indicate significance. This supports the claim that neural end-to-end approaches can supplant feature-rich pipelines.
- Comprehensive comparison with informative analyses: The paper contrasts multiple neural paradigms and provides detailed breakdowns (100% vs. 50% match, component vs. relation F1, paragraph vs. essay). The analysis of distance distributions and the impact of document length (combinatorial growth of candidate relations) offers a plausible explanation for model behavior.
- Negative result on token-level dependency parsing is well-supported: Multiple parsers (feature-based and neural) are evaluated. The substantial gap to tagging, the over-long predicted spans, and the severe degradation at essay level collectively suggest token-level tree enforcement is ill-suited for this end-to-end AM setting.
- Simple label engineering that captures long-range relations: Encoding inter-component distances and stance in the tag enables a local model to exploit long-range dependencies without elaborate global inference, yielding robust performance and improved relation F1 relative to other local models.
- Multi-task learning improves a weaker base tagger: Adding component-only and relation-only auxiliary tasks yields consistent F1 gains, indicating beneficial inductive bias and suggesting a practical recipe for joint learning in AM.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and alignment of comparisons: The ILP system has access to both paragraph and essay context and encodes structural constraints; the neural models are evaluated separately per level and often do not enforce global constraints. Conversely, LSTM-ER assumes sentence-level dependency trees for TreeLSTM composition, yet 92% of relations are cross-sentence, reducing the utility of its tree module. These mismatches complicate strict apples-to-apples conclusions.
- Generality of the distance-augmented tagging scheme: Encoding distances in labels ties the output space to observed component adjacency patterns. It is unclear how the approach scales when distances are larger, structures are non-tree or many-to-many, or when cross-paragraph/cross-document relations are prevalent. Handling unseen/large distances is not detailed.
- Conclusion on “dependency parsing framing is ineffective” may be overstated: Only token-level parsing is tested. A more natural alternative for end-to-end AM would segment components first (learned), then perform component-level parsing/joint inference. The negative result might primarily reflect the difficulty of token-level trees over long inputs rather than a limitation of dependency parsing per se.
- Limited dataset scope and potential PE-specific artifacts: All experiments use a single corpus with specific structural regularities (e.g., major claims in first/last paragraph, premises link to exactly one target). It is unclear whether the findings (e.g., benefits of distance labels, MTL gains, DP shortcomings) generalize to other AM datasets with different guidelines/structures.
- Some methodological details deferred to supplement: Critical training details (pre-trained embeddings, hyperparameters, early stopping), distance vocabulary handling, and inference constraints are not in the main text. The MTL experiments use a weaker base tagger (no CRF, no char-CNN), making it hard to assess the full potential of MTL relative to the strongest tagging backbone.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Distance labels: How are distances capped or handled at test time if the predicted distance exceeds the range seen in training? Is there a special “other” bucket, and how does it affect error modes?
- Constraints at decoding: Did you try simple constrained decoding for the tagger (e.g., enforce exactly one outgoing link for premises, stance-type compatibility) to narrow relation candidates at essay level?
- Re-linking claims to major claims: Please clarify the “loss-less” re-linking procedure and its impact on training targets and evaluation. Are both training and evaluation performed on the re-linked representation?
- MTL architecture: Did you evaluate MTL with the stronger BLCC backbone (with CRF and char-CNN)? If not, what prevents it, and do you expect similar gains?
- LSTM-ER adaptation: Since most relations are cross-sentence, did you try replacing the TreeLSTM with document-level encoders (e.g., inter-sentential graph or attention) to avoid dependency on sentence trees?
- Robustness and efficiency: What are training/inference times and memory footprints for essay-level models? How does performance scale with less training data?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound, and offers substantive empirical insights and improvements for end-to-end AM, with well-motivated analyses. The main limitations concern generality beyond PE, some comparison misalignments, and incomplete exploration of component-level parsing and constrained decoding.
