Summary
This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining (AM) on the Persuasive Essays (PE) corpus, aiming to jointly identify argument components (major claims, claims, premises) and their relations (support/attack, for/against) from raw text. The authors evaluate four framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing, (ii) sequence tagging with a composite label encoding span/type/distance/stance, (iii) multi-task sequence tagging with auxiliary component/relation tasks, and (iv) LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016), which jointly extracts entities and relations. They compare against a strong feature/ILP pipeline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016), report results at paragraph and essay levels, and analyze length effects and long-range dependencies.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding that neural sequence tagging with a distance-augmented label space is a strong, robust end-to-end baseline for AM, outperforming a feature/ILP system on the PE dataset, especially at paragraph level.
2) Analysis across modeling paradigms (tagging, parsing, multi-task, LSTM-ER) with clear takeaways: token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; de-coupling entity/relation modeling (as in LSTM-ER or via MTL) helps component detection; general models (LSTM-ER) excel on short texts but degrade on long texts due to combinatorial relation space.
3) Demonstration that multi-task learning with natural AM subtasks (component vs relation) yields consistent gains over a single-task tagger, offering a practical recipe for improved end-to-end AM without hand-crafted features or constraints.

Strengths
- Thorough and balanced empirical study across multiple neural formalisms. The paper does more than propose a single model; it contrasts dependency parsing, sequence tagging (BLCC), multi-task tagging (BL), and LSTM-ER, and places them against a strong ILP baseline. This breadth provides actionable guidance for the community, not just a point result.

- Strong results and clear patterns. On paragraph-level evaluation, STagBLCC and LSTM-ER substantially outperform ILP for both components and relations; on essay-level, STagBLCC remains robust while LSTM-ER’s R-F1 drops markedly, aligning with the authors’ explanation about the O(m^2) relation space. The analyses by document length and distance distribution (Figure 4) convincingly support the claims.

- Insightful problem framing and label design. Encoding component distance and stance in the tag label is a simple idea that effectively injects structural bias for long-range dependencies without hand-crafted constraints. The authors show that most relations are near (|d| ∈ {1,2}), which the label design leverages, and they quantify long-range cases.

- Practical multi-task recipe. The paper shows consistent gains by adding auxiliary tasks for components and relations (with details on which auxiliary tasks help most), providing a concrete and easily reproducible improvement path beyond the base tagger.

- Honest negative result on token-level dependency parsing. The careful evaluation of several parsers (MST, Mate, Kiperwasser & Goldberg, LSTM-Parser) at paragraph and essay levels, and analysis of why they fail (span boundary errors, long sequences, global search complexity), is valuable for steering future work away from less promising directions.

Weaknesses
- Single-dataset focus and dataset-specific assumptions. All conclusions are drawn on the PE corpus, which has particular structural constraints (tree-like structure, paragraph-local relations, major-claim conventions). The reported gains and model preferences may not generalize to other AM datasets (e.g., microtexts, web discourse, legal) with different structures. Even a small-scale test on Peldszus & Stede microtexts or a cross-domain transfer experiment would strengthen claims.

- Limited analysis of label-set scalability and sparsity. The distance-augmented tag space Y can be large and sparse. The paper notes distances up to ±10, but does not detail the effective label cardinality, any truncation/clipping strategy, or performance sensitivity to max |d|. It is unclear how the model handles unseen distance values at test time, or how much rare tags impact learning.

- Decoding and constraint handling under sequence tagging are under-specified. It is not fully explained how component spans and single outgoing relations per premise are enforced at decode time. Do you post-process BIO boundaries for consistency? How do you aggregate token-level (d, s) predictions into component-level relations when spans are predicted with errors? Are contradictions (e.g., multiple targets per premise) possible and, if so, how are they resolved?

- LSTM-ER configuration and fairness of comparison. The paper argues TreeLSTM features are mostly ineffective because 92% of relations cross sentence boundaries, but it is unclear whether the LSTM-ER implementation used sentence-only trees, disabled tree modules, or augmented with document-level structures. Given LSTM-ER’s sensitivity to entity predictions and sampling strategies, more implementation details are needed to ensure a fair, strong baseline.

- Reproducibility details are deferred. Key choices (pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, optimization schedules, label truncation, decoding heuristics) are relegated to supplementary material. For an end-to-end comparison paper, more in-text detail or guaranteed code release would materially improve reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- What is the exact size of the tag set Y used in experiments, and what max distance |d| was allowed? How are distances beyond this cap handled at training and test time?

- How do you convert token-level tags into component spans and relations? Please detail the decoding algorithm, including handling of invalid BIO sequences, multiple target predictions for a single premise, and conflicts between token-level relation predictions within a span.

- For STagT, how do you ensure premises have exactly one outgoing relation while claims/MCs behave as required by the PE schema? Is this enforced by the label space only or by post-processing constraints?

- For LSTM-ER: which dependency trees were used by the TreeLSTM (sentence-level, paragraph-level, or none)? Did you try variants without the tree component? Could you report ablations (with/without scheduled sampling, entity pretraining)?

- Did you evaluate performance as a function of distance (|d|) for STagBLCC (beyond Figure 4) to verify long-range relation handling? Any error analysis on cross-paragraph vs within-paragraph cases?

- How sensitive are your results to pre-trained embeddings and character CNNs (for BLCC)? Any comparison to a CRF-only non-neural tagger baseline?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a valuable, carefully executed empirical study with practical insights and new state-of-the-art results on PE. Addressing the decoding/constraint details, clarifying LSTM-ER comparisons, and adding at least one additional dataset or transfer experiment would strengthen the generality and reproducibility claims.
