- Systematic empirical comparison of multiple neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining, showing strong results for sequence tagging and MTL and failures of token-level parsing and LSTM-ER on long documents.

- A joint sequence-tagging scheme (BIO × type × distance × stance) that encodes relation distances and stance yields state-of-the-art or better-than-ILP performance on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset, especially at paragraph level.

- Analysis showing neural models implicitly learn tree-like structures; constrained decodings and MTL with natural subtasks improve performance, particularly for long documents.

- Clear, well-motivated comparative study covering sequence tagging, token-level parsing, MTL, and an adapted LSTM-ER, helpful for understanding modeling trade-offs.

- Strong empirical performance with meaningful baselines; BLCC and MTL variants outperform ILP on paragraph-level evaluation and are competitive on essay-level; evaluation uses span and relation F1 with significance testing.

- Thoughtful analysis of document length and constraints: long documents hurt pairwise-relation models, local coding helps, and token-level parsing is brittle due to global search complexity.

- Multi-task learning consistently improves tagging by decoupling subproblems while training jointly.

- Good reproducibility and transparency: code/parameters referenced, standard architectures used, and limitations (e.g., paragraph-level due to memory) acknowledged.

- Token-level dependency parsing setup likely understates parsing’s potential by forcing each token to attach to a head, creating noisy repeated edges and exacerbating long-range search.

- The tagging label space explodes (BIO × type × distance × stance), causing sparsity for long-range distances and rare stance combos; missing details on label cardinality, bucketing, or separate prediction heads.

- Comparisons on essay level may be unfair: ILP uses structural/global constraints while neural models omit simple structural priors; LSTM-ER’s TreeLSTM over sentences may misalign with predominantly cross-sentence relations.

- Unclear decoding and gold-structure adaptation details: re-linking claims to a single major claim for parsing and mapping tags (b,t,d,s) to edges/stances lack explicit description and may affect results.

- Limited generalization: experiments only on the PE corpus with tree-like, short-distance relations; unclear robustness to domains with non-local or non-tree structures and error analysis is brief.

- Question: Did you try component-level parsing (segment-then-parse) or joint span+tree models rather than token-level parsing, and how would conclusions change?

- Question: Please detail how component boundaries, heads, and edges are recovered from (b,t,d,s) labels, how distance ambiguities are resolved, and how stance mappings are handled.

- Question: What is the total number of unique labels observed, did you clip/bucket distances, and did you try predicting distance and stance via separate heads within a strong BLCC backbone?

- Question: For LSTM-ER, given most relations are cross-sentence, did you try removing or replacing the TreeLSTM with document-level encoders, and how much does the sentence-tree module contribute?

- Question: Did you add simple structural signals (paragraph/sentence/position features) to neural models on essay level, and would that close the gap with ILP on major claims?

- Question: When re-linking claims to a single major claim for parsing, how is the original multi-headed gold mapped back for evaluation, and is there bias/loss introduced?

- Question: Have you tested models on other AM datasets (microtexts, web debates), and can you comment on expected behavior for more non-local or non-tree-like relations?
