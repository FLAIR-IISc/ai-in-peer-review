- Compares four neural end-to-end AM framings on Persuasive Essays: token-level dependency parsing, composite-label sequence tagging (STagT), multi-task tagging, and LSTM-ER adaptation.
- Evaluates at paragraph and essay levels using component/relationship F1 with exact and approximate matching.
- Main finding: token-level dependency parsing is weak; STagT is strong and robust; LSTM-ER excels on paragraphs but degrades on essays; multi-tasking helps; several neural models beat the ILP baseline.
- Contribution: thorough empirical comparison and analysis of document length effects and task coupling, plus a tagging scheme encoding relation distance and stance.
- Contribution: shows neural models can match/exceed feature-based ILP SOTA without hand-crafted features or hard constraints; gives guidance on when general vs constrained framings work.
- Contribution: negative result on token-level dependency parsing with plausible diagnosis (global tree, token-head encoding, long sequences) and practical alternatives.
- Strength: strong empirical study with multiple baselines, metrics, and significance tests; compelling numeric results (LSTM-ER best on paragraphs; STagBLCC most robust on essays).
- Strength: clear insight into trade-offs—LSTM-ER suffers from O(m^2) relation candidates on longer texts; tagger’s distance-aware bias scales better.
- Strength: simple, practical sequence tagging that encodes component/stance/distance and performs competitively with ILP without manual features.
- Strength: useful negative result showing neural parsers struggle on long sequences and unnatural token-head targets.
- Strength: auxiliary component/relation subtasks measurably improve the BiLSTM tagger, supporting shared-representation benefits.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—mostly adaptations of existing architectures; main new element is label encoding.
- Weakness: dependency parsing may be unfairly disadvantaged by token-head design; component-level parsing alternatives not explored.
- Weakness: incomplete ablations of tagging design—no analysis of distance bucketing/granularity, stance encoding, CRF vs non-CRF, tag-space size, unseen labels, or token-to-component aggregation.
- Weakness: structural validity unreported—no statistics on violations of AM constraints (multiple outgoing links, cycles) or whether post-processing was used.
- Weakness: narrow dataset scope—experiments only on Persuasive Essays, limiting claims of generality.
- Question: how are token-level tags decoded to component-level when tokens within a component disagree on distance or stance; what heuristics and sensitivity?
- Question: what is the effective tag set size in train/test and how often do unseen (b,t,d,s) combinations occur at test time?
- Question: were distance bucketing schemes tried (e.g., short/medium/long) and how did they affect relation F1 and essay robustness?
- Question: did LSTM-ER use candidate pruning (distance-based or paragraph constraints) to reduce O(m^2) pairs on essays, and would that close the essay gap?
- Question: can authors report the fraction of predictions violating AM structural constraints for each model and whether simple post-hoc pruning improves relation F1?
