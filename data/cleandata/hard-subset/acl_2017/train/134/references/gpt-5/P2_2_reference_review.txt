Summary and main contributions
- This paper presents the first systematic study of neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM) on the persuasive essays (PE) dataset. The authors explore multiple framings: (i) token-level dependency parsing; (ii) sequence tagging with composite labels that jointly encode BIO, component type, inter-component distance, and stance; (iii) multi-task learning (MTL) with auxiliary tasks; and (iv) an adaptation of LSTM-ER (Miwa & Bansal, 2016). They compare against the feature-based ILP pipeline of Stab & Gurevych (2016).
- Primary contribution: an empirical finding that standard neural sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF with char-CNN) with an informed label design (distance and stance slots) yields strong end-to-end performance, outperforming the prior ILP joint model, especially at paragraph level, and remaining competitive at essay level.
- Secondary contributions: (a) evidence that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM; (b) MTL with natural auxiliary subtasks (component-only, relation-only) improves over single-task tagging; (c) analysis of how document length and explicit distance encoding affect relation performance; and (d) adaptation and evaluation of LSTM-ER showing a trade-off: excellent component detection but weaker relation detection on longer texts.

Strengths
- Empirical thoroughness and comparative scope: The paper evaluates four distinct modeling paradigms (global parsing, local tagging, MTL, and LSTM-ER) on both paragraph and essay levels, with appropriate metrics (exact/approximate C-F1 and R-F1) and significance testing. This breadth provides actionable insight into what works for AM under different conditions (short vs long texts).
- State-of-the-art end-to-end results with reduced feature engineering: The sequence tagging approach (BLCC) and LSTM-ER surpass the ILP baseline on paragraph-level performance, demonstrating that off-the-shelf neural architectures, with minimal task-specific engineering, can replace complex feature sets and hand-crafted constraints.
- Simple but impactful design idea: encoding inter-component distance in the tag space. This injects a task-relevant inductive bias (nearby links are more likely) into a local model without global inference. The authors provide empirical justification (distance distribution analysis, performance gaps vs LSTM-ER on essay vs paragraph level) that this bias is especially helpful when many entities are present.
- Useful analysis and takeaways: The paper explains why token-level dependency parsing underperforms (global search over long sequences; poor span boundary predictions), why LSTM-ER excels at components but struggles on relations for long documents (quadratic relation candidates), and why MTL helps by decoupling subproblems while sharing representations. These insights are valuable for future AM system design.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; contribution is largely empirical: The core architectures are standard (BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, MTL with shared BiLSTMs, LSTM-ER). The main novelty is the composite label design with distance/stance and the careful empirical study. While still valuable, methodological innovation is modest relative to recent neural IE work.
- Single-domain dataset and structure-specific assumptions: All results are on one dataset (PE), whose argument structure has helpful properties (intra-paragraph locality; near-tree structures; major claims in boundary paragraphs). The models and conclusions may not transfer to other AM corpora with different structures or noisier discourse (e.g., forum debates, legal corpora). The paper would be stronger with a second corpus or a clearer discussion of generalization limits.
- Potential strawman design for dependency parsing and limited fairness checks: The token-level dependency framing forces heads and labels that are unusual for parsing and likely hurt span accuracy (e.g., heads to END for non-argumentative tokens; linking to first token of target component). A component-level parsing baseline (detect spans first, then parse components) or a joint transition system over components would be a more competitive parsing alternative. As is, the negative result may reflect the chosen encoding more than an inherent limitation of parsing for AM.
- Reproducibility and ablation gaps: Important details are relegated to supplementary material (embeddings, hyperparameters, training decisions, handling of distance values unseen in training, class imbalance). The paper argues that distance encoding matters, but an ablation (removing d, capping |d|, or mapping rare distances to UNK) is missing. Similarly, reporting constraint satisfaction rates (e.g., % of outputs where each premise has exactly one parent; tree-likeness) would substantiate the claim that models “learn” constraints. Finally, training at paragraph level for some models due to memory, while ILP can leverage full essays, raises comparability questions; the authors partly address this by reporting both levels, but a clearer protocol would help.

Questions to Authors
- Distance label vocabulary: How is d handled at training and test time when a distance not seen in training occurs? Is there a cap or UNK bucket? Did you explore alternative parameterizations (e.g., bucketing distances, regression to a scalar d)?
- Constraint satisfaction: What fraction of predicted graphs satisfy key AM constraints (each premise links to exactly one parent; acyclicity/tree-likeness; claims linking to major claims)? How does this differ across STagT, MTL, and LSTM-ER?
- BIO decoding and span quality: Do you use constrained decoding to enforce valid BIO sequences? Can you quantify common span boundary errors (e.g., punctuation inclusion) and whether char-CNN or CRF transitions mitigate them?
- Dependency parsing alternatives: Did you attempt a component-level parsing variant (predict spans first, then parse components), or transition-based joint systems that predict spans and links jointly? This would help disentangle weaknesses of the token-level encoding from parsing per se.
- Generalization: Have you tried your best-performing tagging setup on another AM dataset (e.g., microtexts, web discourse)? If not, can you discuss expected challenges (e.g., longer cross-sentence distances, different stance labels) and how the distance-encoding approach would adapt?

Overall evaluation
- The paper is clear, well-motivated, and provides a comprehensive empirical study with meaningful insights and new state-of-the-art results on the PE dataset. While the methodological novelty is limited, the work is sound and significant for the AM community, offering strong neural baselines and guidance on modeling choices. Addressing the reproducibility details and adding constraint/ablation analyses would further strengthen the contribution.
