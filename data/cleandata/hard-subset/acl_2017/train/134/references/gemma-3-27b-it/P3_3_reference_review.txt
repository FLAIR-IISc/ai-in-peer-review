## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), moving beyond traditional pipeline methods reliant on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors explore four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of a pre-existing relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on the Persuasive Essays (PE) dataset, demonstrating competitive performance with the state-of-the-art ILP baseline. The core finding is that while dependency parsing is ineffective, sequence tagging (especially with distance encoding) is robust, and multi-task learning provides further improvements.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Feasibility:**  The paper convincingly demonstrates that neural end-to-end AM is a viable and competitive approach.  This is significant as it moves the field away from brittle, feature-engineered systems toward more adaptable learning methods.
2. **Comprehensive Comparative Study:** The thorough exploration of different neural network framings – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of LSTM-ER – provides valuable insights into which approaches are best suited for the AM task.  This is useful for guiding future research.
3. **Highlighting the Importance of Distance Encoding:** The success of the sequence tagging approach, specifically its robustness due to encoding distances between related components, is a key finding. This points towards incorporating relational information directly into the tagging scheme, rather than treating it as a separate downstream task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The neural models consistently achieve competitive, and in many cases superior, performance compared to the strong ILP baseline, demonstrating their practical value.  The inclusion of both paragraph and essay-level results provides nuanced understanding of model performance.
2. **Thorough Exploration of Framing Choices:**  The paper’s investigation into diverse neural network framings is commendable. The insightful analysis of why dependency parsing fails and why sequence tagging works well demonstrates a deep understanding of the task and the models.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Discussion:** The authors provide a thorough discussion of the results, offering plausible explanations for observed trends (e.g., the challenges of long sequences for dependency parsing, the benefits of multi-task learning).  This enhances the paper's impact beyond merely presenting numbers.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem definition, related work, and experimental setup are easy to follow.  The inclusion of the example in the introduction is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The reliance on a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016) is a limitation.  Argumentation structures can vary considerably across domains and genres.  The generalizability of the findings needs to be further investigated.  While acknowledged, this warrants more discussion.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the multi-task learning experiments are insightful, more detailed ablation studies would be beneficial. Specifically, systematically exploring the impact of different auxiliary tasks (beyond 'C' and 'R') and layer configurations would provide a deeper understanding of the benefits of MTL.
3. **LSTM-ER Implementation Details:** The description of the LSTM-ER implementation feels somewhat cursory.  More details on how the model was adapted to the argumentation mining task, and why it performs worse on long texts, would be valuable. Specifically, the authors state the de-coupling of component and relation detection as the reason for LSTM-ER performance, but that’s not explored. The benefit of de-coupling on long texts is an intuition and needs explanation.
4. **Distance Encoding Limitation:** The distance encoding within the sequence tagging approach, while effective, is a somewhat simplistic representation of relational information. The authors acknowledge the potential for more sophisticated relational modeling, but don't explore it.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Generalizability:**  Do you have plans to evaluate these models on other AM datasets? If so, can you comment on potential challenges related to adapting the models to different annotation schemes or domains?
2. **LSTM-ER Refinement:**  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when adapting LSTM-ER to the AM task, and what steps could be taken to address the performance degradation on longer texts? Specifically, what are some alternatives to tackle the large number of possible relations at essay level?
3. **Multi-Task Ablation:**  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of different auxiliary tasks in the multi-task learning setup? What other tasks did you consider, and why were they not included in the final experiments?
4. **Distance Encoding Alternatives:** Have you considered alternatives to the current distance encoding scheme? For example, could incorporating learned embeddings for distances improve performance?




**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work is comprehensive, but could benefit from mentioning recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs) for relation extraction, which might be applicable to AM.
* The paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis, showcasing specific examples of errors made by different models to provide insights into their strengths and weaknesses.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The experimental results are convincing, and the analysis is insightful. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
