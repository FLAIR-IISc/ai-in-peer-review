## Peer Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

**Summary:**

This paper investigates neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) designed for entity and relation extraction. They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset, comparing performance to a feature-based ILP baseline.  The key finding is that neural sequence tagging, particularly when encoding distance information between components, performs well. Dependency parsing proves ineffective, and multi-task learning shows some improvement.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, in order of strength):**

1.  Presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2.  Demonstrating that several neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3.  Showing that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective.
4.  Demonstrating that a sequence tagging model encoding distance information is robust.
5.  Showing that a multi-task learning setup improves performance.

**My Assessment of Contributions:**

1. **Demonstrating the viability of neural sequence tagging for end-to-end AM is the most significant contribution.** The authors successfully adapt sequence tagging techniques (specifically BiLSTM-CRF) to capture both component identification and relational information in a unified manner, achieving strong performance. The inclusion of distance encoding is a noteworthy improvement.
2. **Providing a comprehensive empirical evaluation of several neural framing options for AM is valuable.** The comparison across different neural architectures and against a strong baseline (ILP) provides insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. This is particularly useful for the community as it clarifies which avenues are most promising.
3. **The negative result regarding token-based dependency parsing is a valuable contribution.** While the authors acknowledge prior work using dependency parsing at the component level, demonstrating its failure at the token level provides a clear direction away from this approach for end-to-end systems.


**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors present convincing empirical evidence demonstrating that neural sequence tagging outperforms the existing ILP baseline on the PE dataset. The consistent performance gains across different evaluation metrics (C-F1, R-F1) strengthen this claim.
2.  **Comprehensive Exploration of Framing Options:** The paper thoroughly investigates different neural network framings, offering a broad overview of potential approaches for end-to-end AM. This allows for a nuanced understanding of the problem and its suitability for various modeling techniques.
3.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand their experimental setup.  The inclusion of the argument structure example in Figure 1 is helpful.
4.  **Insightful Error Analysis (limited, but present):** The discussion regarding the limitations of dependency parsing and the challenges with LSTM-ER on longer sequences demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying issues.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The experiments are conducted solely on the PE dataset. While this dataset is high-quality, its domain specificity (persuasive essays) raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Testing on additional datasets with different text types and argumentation structures is crucial.
2.  **Lack of Ablation Study for Distance Encoding:** While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of distance encoding in the sequence tagging model, they lack a detailed ablation study to quantify the contribution of this feature specifically.  How much does distance encoding *improve* performance compared to a standard sequence tagging model *without* it?
3. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The reference to state-of-the-art is largely confined to the ILP approach of Stab and Gurevych (2016). A more thorough comparison to recent advancements in neural relation extraction or sequence labeling could provide a more robust assessment of the proposed methods' novelty and performance.  For example, recent transformer-based models might offer a strong baseline.
4. **Insufficient discussion of LSTM-ER's parameters:** The results using LSTM-ER are somewhat confusing. The authors state it performs well on components but poorly on relations, but don’t provide enough details about parameter tuning or the specific configurations used.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:**  Have you considered evaluating your best-performing model (STagBLCC) on other AM datasets, even if with limited annotations? What are your expectations regarding the transferability of your approach to different domains?
2.  **Ablation Study:**  Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the specific contribution of the distance encoding feature to the performance of the sequence tagging model (STagBLCC)?
3.  **LSTM-ER Parameterization:** Please provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for LSTM-ER. Specifically, what choices were made regarding entity and relation representation, and why?  Was pre-training used, and if so, on what data?
4.  **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide more detailed examples of the types of errors made by the sequence tagging model?  Are there specific types of relations (e.g., long-distance dependencies) that are consistently misclassified?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work in neural relation extraction, as this field is highly relevant to the proposed approaches.
*   Consider including a visualization of the predicted argument structure for a sample essay to provide a more intuitive understanding of the model's output.
*   The discussion of multi-task learning could be strengthened by providing a more rigorous analysis of the interplay between the auxiliary tasks and the primary AM task.




Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the limitations regarding dataset diversity and the lack of a thorough ablation study should be addressed, the positive results and clear presentation make this work worthy of acceptance.
