This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore several framing approaches – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging an existing model (LSTM-ER) – and compare their performance against a strong feature-based ILP baseline.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) a thorough empirical comparison of multiple neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, establishing a strong baseline for future research in this area; (2) the demonstration that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, challenging a previously proposed approach; and (3) a detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling choices (e.g., sequence tagging with distance encoding vs. LSTM-ER) under varying data conditions (paragraph vs. essay level). The paper also provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of multi-task learning for AM, specifically highlighting the benefits of incorporating component detection as an auxiliary task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors perform a rigorous evaluation of four different neural network approaches and compare them to a strong baseline (ILP model). The experiments are well-designed and cover a significant range of configurations, providing a robust assessment of the proposed methods. The inclusion of results at both paragraph and essay levels allows for a nuanced understanding of the challenges inherent in each approach.
2. **Clear Identification of Challenges in Dependency Parsing:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the ineffectiveness of framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, particularly at the essay level. This is a valuable finding that guides future research away from a potentially unproductive direction. The authors provide plausible explanations for this result, attributing it to the complexity of global tree structures in longer texts.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Model Behavior:**  The authors go beyond simply reporting results and offer insightful analyses of why certain approaches perform better than others.  The discussion of the trade-offs between different modeling choices (e.g., sequence tagging vs. LSTM-ER) and the impact of long-range dependencies are particularly noteworthy. The analysis of LSTM-ER’s performance degradation at the essay level is well-reasoned and highlights the importance of considering task-specific constraints.
4. **Comprehensive Dataset Utilization:** The work effectively utilizes the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset, which is a valuable resource for the AM community. The authors clearly describe the dataset’s characteristics and limitations, providing context for their experimental results.
5. **Detailed Discussion of Multi-Task Learning:** The exploration of multi-task learning is thorough, and the findings regarding the benefits of incorporating component detection as an auxiliary task are insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of these techniques and their application to AM is valuable, the individual components (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, dependency parsing) are well-established in the NLP literature. The novelty primarily lies in the systematic application and comparison of these methods within the AM context.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks specific details about the search process. Providing more information about the hyperparameter settings and the optimization strategy would enhance the reproducibility of the results.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While some error analysis is alluded to, a more detailed qualitative analysis of the errors made by each model would provide further insights into their strengths and weaknesses. Identifying specific types of errors and patterns could help guide future improvements.
4. **Presentation of Results:** The tables are dense and could benefit from improved formatting or the inclusion of statistical significance testing indicators more consistently. While some significance testing is mentioned, it’s not uniformly applied across all comparisons.
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The computational cost of each approach is not discussed. Providing information about training and inference times would be useful for practitioners considering these methods.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter optimization process, including the search space and the selection criteria?
2. Could you elaborate on the types of errors that each model makes? Are there specific patterns or challenges that consistently lead to errors?
3. Could you provide some quantitative information about the computational cost (training and inference time) of each approach?
4. You mention that LSTM-ER's general framework may be problematic for longer texts. Have you considered any techniques to mitigate this issue, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical modeling?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are overly complex and could benefit from simplification.
*   The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   The code should be released to facilitate reproducibility.
