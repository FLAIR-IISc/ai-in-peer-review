## Peer Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores neural network approaches to the challenging task of computational argumentation mining (AM), moving away from traditional pipeline methods relying on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming. The authors investigate four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) combining sequential and tree structures. They demonstrate that several of these neural approaches outperform a strong baseline ILP model on a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016).  A key finding is that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, while sequence tagging with distance encoding proves robust.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, ordered):**

1.  Presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2.  Demonstrating that several neural approaches outperform the state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3.  Showing that a token-based dependency parsing framing is ineffective.
4.  Demonstrating the robustness of a sequence tagging model encoding distance information.
5.  Showing that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks improves performance.

**Contributions (as I see them, ordered):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM:** The paper provides a valuable empirical study showing the feasibility and promising performance of several neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, offering a compelling alternative to traditional methods.
2. **Negative Result on Dependency Parsing:** The clear demonstration that directly applying dependency parsing to the token level for AM is ineffective is a significant finding, guiding future research away from potentially unproductive avenues.
3. **Robustness of Distance-Encoded Tagging:** The successful application of sequence tagging with distance encoding showcases a practical and effective approach for relation identification in AM.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Exploration:** The authors explore a diverse range of neural network architectures, providing a broad overview of potential approaches to end-to-end AM.  This allows for a comparative analysis and insightful observations regarding the strengths and weaknesses of each framing.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently improved performance over a strong baseline (ILP) for multiple neural models, establishing their potential for practical applications in AM. The use of the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset, a standard in the field, adds to the significance of these results.
3. **Clear and Insightful Analysis:** The discussion of why dependency parsing fails and why sequence tagging succeeds is well-reasoned and provides valuable guidance for future research. The connection to the inherent characteristics of AM tasks (long-range dependencies, local nature of tagging) is well articulated.
4. **Practical Focus:** The focus on sequence tagging with distance encoding highlights a relatively simple and implementable approach that achieves strong performance, making it a practical contribution for researchers and practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Models:** While the *combination* of these models with the AM task is novel, the individual models themselves (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER, etc.) are well-established in the NLP literature. The paper’s novelty lies primarily in the application and comparative analysis, rather than the introduction of groundbreaking new architectures.
2. **Over-reliance on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset. While this is a standard dataset, it is crucial to assess the generalizability of the findings to other datasets with different characteristics. The performance gains observed might be specific to this particular dataset's properties.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the authors mention error analysis, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the types of errors made by each model. A more granular error analysis would provide deeper insights into the limitations of each approach and guide future improvements.  Understanding *why* the models fail in certain cases is crucial.
4. **MTL Results Somewhat Unclear:** The results from the multi-task learning experiments are not as convincing as the other findings. The gains are modest and the optimal configuration seems sensitive to hyperparameters. A more thorough investigation of the best auxiliary tasks and their interplay with the main AM task is needed.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:** Have you explored the performance of your models on other argumentation mining datasets, or datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different domains, annotation schemes)? If not, could you discuss the potential challenges and expected performance variations on different datasets?
2.  **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the best-performing models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER)? Specifically, what types of components and relations are most frequently misclassified?
3.  **MTL Configuration:** Could you elaborate on the specific hyperparameter tuning strategies used for the multi-task learning experiments? What factors influenced the choice of auxiliary tasks and their respective weighting?
4.  **Distance Encoding:** The distance encoding in the sequence tagging model seems like a crucial element.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing the specific distance encoding scheme and discuss potential alternatives?  Did you experiment with other ways of encoding relational information?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of each of the models, especially compared to the ILP baseline? This is important for practical considerations.




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections could benefit from further elaboration (e.g., the description of the LSTM-ER model).
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics (number of parameters, training time) of each model for easier comparison.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining by providing a comprehensive empirical study of neural network approaches and offering valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities of end-to-end learning. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly the lack of error analysis and limited dataset scope) would further strengthen the paper's impact.
