The paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing neural model (LSTM-ER). The authors claim (1) the presentation of the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, (2) superior performance compared to a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) the ineffectiveness of token-based dependency parsing for AM, (4) the robustness of a sequence tagging model encoding distance information, and (5) the benefits of a multi-task learning setup. 

A primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of multiple neural network architectures applied to the challenging end-to-end AM problem. The systematic evaluation, despite the varying degrees of success, provides a valuable benchmark for future work. The empirical comparison against the ILP baseline is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Exploration:** The paper’s strength lies in its breadth. It doesn't focus on a single approach but instead explores several viable neural framings for AM. This provides a useful comparative landscape.
2. **Robust Evaluation:** The authors utilize a recognized dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and adhere to established evaluation metrics, facilitating comparison with prior work. The distinction between paragraph and essay-level performance highlights important practical considerations.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis (Partial):** The discussion surrounding the limitations of dependency parsing and the impact of sequence length on LSTM-ER reveals a thoughtful understanding of the challenges and trade-offs inherent in each approach. The explanation for why LSTM-ER performs better on paragraph-level data than essay-level data is particularly insightful.
4. **Identification of Sequence Tagging as a Strong Baseline:** The results demonstrate that a relatively simple sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) achieves competitive performance, establishing a strong baseline for future endeavors.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is a contribution, many of the individual components—BiLSTMs, CRFs, dependency parsing—are well-established. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper doesn’t present significant algorithmic innovations *within* those components.
2. **Inconsistent Reporting & Overly Dense Presentation:** The tables are difficult to parse (pun intended). The use of abbreviations (e.g. Y-3:YC-3:YR-3) is excessive and hampers readability. The paper would greatly benefit from clearer labeling and more concise presentation of results.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The text mentions hyperparameter optimization, but provides almost no details. This is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the performance gains. Without knowing the specific parameter settings, it is difficult to assess the validity of the comparisons.
4. **Minor Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor errors are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent punctuation, awkward phrasing). While individually minor, their cumulative effect detracts from the paper’s professionalism. Specifically, the repeated formatting of citations (e.g., “Persing and Ng\.,? 2016”) is jarring. The inclusion of "CoRR" in the references instead of "arXiv" is also a formatting issue.
5. **Weak Motivation for MTL Task Selection:** The rationale for choosing component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks in the multi-task learning setup feels somewhat arbitrary. A more principled justification, potentially grounded in argumentation theory, would strengthen this aspect of the work. The analysis of why “C” is more helpful than “R” as an auxiliary task is weak.
6. **Missing Ablation Studies:** The paper presents numerous models, but lacks ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components within each model. For example, how much does the character-level CNN contribute to performance in STagBLCC?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process used for each model, including the search space, optimization algorithm, and validation strategy?
2. Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for selecting component detection and relation identification as auxiliary tasks in the multi-task learning framework?
3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors made by each model? Specifically, what types of relation errors are most common?
4. Why wasn't a more recent or competitive LSTM-ER implementation explored? The one utilized appears outdated.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent and needs careful review.
*   Consider expanding the related work section to include more recent advancements in neural relation extraction.
*   A clearer visual representation of the argumentation structure (Figure 1) would be beneficial.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable exploration of neural approaches to AM, the lack of significant algorithmic novelty, the dense and sometimes unclear presentation, and the numerous minor flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I would suggest a *major revision* addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
