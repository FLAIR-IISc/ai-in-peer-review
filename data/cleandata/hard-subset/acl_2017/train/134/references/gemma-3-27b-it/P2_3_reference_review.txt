## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores several neural network approaches to tackle the end-to-end Computational Argumentation Mining (AM) task, moving away from traditional pipeline and feature-engineered methods. The authors investigate framing AM as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging an existing entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER).  They demonstrate that several of their neural approaches outperform a strong, existing ILP-based baseline on a standard AM dataset.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM’s Viability:** The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation showing that neural models *can* effectively address the end-to-end AM problem, achieving competitive performance with a feature-engineered ILP baseline. This is a significant step forward, showing the potential of eliminating manual feature engineering in this domain.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Framing Choices:** The authors systematically explore a range of neural network framings for AM – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning – providing valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach in this specific context.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Dependency Parsing & Long-Range Dependencies:** The paper's negative result for token-level dependency parsing is interesting, suggesting that the global tree structure assumption isn’t well-suited to the nature of argumentative relationships. The analysis regarding the importance of capturing long-range dependencies, particularly in relation tagging, is a valuable observation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of the neural models (particularly the sequence tagging approaches) compared to the ILP baseline is a strong point. The provided F1 scores offer clear evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors demonstrate a reasonable experimental setup, including a comparison against a strong baseline, evaluation on both paragraph and essay levels, and exploration of different neural architectures.  The use of a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016) enhances reproducibility and comparability.
3. **Clear Problem Framing & Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for end-to-end learning in AM, highlighting the limitations of pipeline approaches and hand-crafted features, which are susceptible to domain adaptation issues and error propagation.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Different Approaches:** The authors don’t just present results; they provide detailed analysis of *why* certain approaches work better than others (e.g., the limitations of dependency parsing due to long sequences and the benefits of sequence tagging for capturing long-range dependencies).
5. **Effective Use of Multi-Task Learning:** The exploration of auxiliary tasks (component and relation identification) demonstrates the potential of multi-task learning to further improve performance. The finding that component classification as an auxiliary task is more helpful than relation classification is a nuanced and valuable result.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of neural networks and end-to-end AM is valuable, the individual neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are not novel contributions in themselves.  The primary novelty lies in *applying* these well-established architectures to the AM problem. The paper acknowledges this, but it’s worth explicitly stating.
2. **LSTM-ER’s Performance Discrepancy:** The significant drop in performance of LSTM-ER on essay-level data (particularly for relation identification) is concerning and requires further investigation. While the authors offer a plausible explanation (increased combinatorial complexity), a more in-depth analysis of the errors and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the paper.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  The paper would benefit from an ablation study to understand the contribution of different components within the sequence tagging models (e.g., the impact of the CNN layer, the importance of distance encoding). This would help pinpoint the key factors driving performance.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper could be improved by providing a more detailed error analysis.  What types of errors are the models making?  Are there specific types of argumentative relationships that are particularly challenging? Providing concrete examples of errors would add valuable insights.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the significant performance drop of LSTM-ER on essay-level data, could you provide a more detailed error analysis?  Are there specific types of relations (e.g., those spanning longer distances) where the model struggles most?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific hyperparameters used for each model (STagBLCC, LSTM-ER, etc.)? Providing a table of key hyperparameters would improve reproducibility.
3.  In the MTL experiments, why did feeding the C task from the same layer as the full task yield the best results?  Does this suggest a strong dependency between component detection and the overall AM task?
4. Could you expand on the practical considerations and computational cost associated with each approach, particularly the dependency parsing approach?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of Computational Argumentation Mining. While the individual components aren’t groundbreaking, the comprehensive exploration of neural network framings, the strong empirical results, and the insightful analysis make this paper worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly the LSTM-ER performance discrepancy and the lack of ablation study) would further strengthen the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
