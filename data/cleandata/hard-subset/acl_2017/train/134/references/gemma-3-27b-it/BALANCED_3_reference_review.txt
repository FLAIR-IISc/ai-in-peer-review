This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), a task traditionally tackled with pipeline approaches and hand-crafted features. The authors explore several neural framings – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of an existing entity-relation extraction model (LSTM-ER) – and compare their performance to a strong ILP baseline. The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive empirical study demonstrating the feasibility and effectiveness of neural approaches for end-to-end AM, specifically showing that several neural models outperform the state-of-the-art ILP model. A secondary contribution lies in the finding that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, while sequence tagging with distance encoding proves robust. Finally, the investigation of multi-task learning as a way to improve performance is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough and well-structured empirical evaluation, comparing multiple neural approaches and a strong baseline (ILP) on a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). This provides a strong basis for drawing conclusions about the effectiveness of different modeling choices. The inclusion of both paragraph-level and essay-level evaluations, highlighting the challenges of long-range dependencies, is particularly valuable.
2. **Novel Application of Neural Techniques to AM:** The exploration of different neural network architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, TreeLSTM, dependency parsing) for end-to-end AM is novel and pushes the field beyond traditional pipeline methods. The adaptation of LSTM-ER for this task is also a significant contribution.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Model Performance:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting results and provides insightful analysis of why certain models perform better than others. The discussion of the limitations of dependency parsing in this context, and the effectiveness of distance encoding in sequence tagging, demonstrates a deep understanding of the task and the models. The analysis of LSTM-ER performance, connecting it to the challenges of long-range dependencies, is particularly strong.
4. **Practical Recommendations:** The conclusion provides clear and actionable recommendations for future research, highlighting the trade-offs between different modeling choices and suggesting promising avenues for improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Model Architectures:** While the *application* of these architectures to AM is novel, the architectures themselves (BiLSTM-CRF, TreeLSTM) are fairly standard in the NLP literature. The paper doesn't introduce any significant architectural innovations.  This isn't a fatal flaw, but it limits the overall impact of the work.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While some discussion of error types is present, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. For example, are there specific types of argumentative relations that are consistently misclassified? Understanding the types of errors made by the models could lead to more targeted improvements. The mention of potential error analysis in the conclusion feels incomplete without further details.
3. **Hyperparameter Optimization Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but provides limited details about the process. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to different hyperparameter settings. A more thorough discussion of hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the reproducibility and reliability of the findings.
4. **MTL Results are Less Compelling:** While the investigation of multi-task learning is valuable, the reported results are not consistently better than the baseline sequence tagging model. The benefits of MTL appear to be marginal and context-dependent, making it less of a strong contribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically identifying the most common types of errors made by the different models (e.g., misclassifying relation types, incorrect span identification)?
2.  Regarding hyperparameter optimization, what strategies were employed (e.g., grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization)? Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter ranges explored and the rationale behind the chosen settings?
3.  The paper mentions a performance drop on essay level for the dependency parsing model due to long sequences. Did you explore any techniques to mitigate this issue, such as truncation or hierarchical parsing?
4. For the MTL experiments, could you analyze *which* auxiliary tasks contribute the most to performance gains, and why?  Is there a consistent pattern regarding which subtasks are most helpful?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section is adequate but could be expanded to include more recent work on neural relation extraction and argumentation mining.
*   Consider providing a table summarizing the key differences between the various models and their strengths/weaknesses.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the individual model architectures are not particularly novel, the comprehensive empirical evaluation and insightful analysis of results make it a worthwhile read. The identified weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revised version of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
