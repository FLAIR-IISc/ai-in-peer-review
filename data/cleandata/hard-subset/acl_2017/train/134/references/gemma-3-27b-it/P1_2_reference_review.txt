## Peer Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores neural network approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), tackling the problem end-to-end rather than via traditional pipelined methods. The authors investigate four framing techniques: dependency parsing, sequence tagging with distance encoding, multi-task learning, and adaptation of a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) combining sequential and tree structures. They evaluate these methods on the Stab & Gurevych (2016) persuasive essay dataset, comparing them to a strong feature-based ILP baseline.  The paper demonstrates that several neural approaches outperform the ILP baseline, and identifies sequence tagging with distance encoding as a particularly robust and effective method. They also show dependency parsing is ineffective and MTL provides some gains.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM:** The paper provides a valuable empirical evaluation of several neural architectures applied to the end-to-end AM task, establishing a strong baseline for future work. The results convincingly show that neural methods are competitive with, and often surpass, feature-engineered ILP approaches.
2. **Insightful Analysis of Framing Choices:** The systematic exploration of different framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL) provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach for the AM problem.  The finding that dependency parsing is ineffective is a particularly important negative result.
3. **Effective Sequence Tagging Formulation:** The sequence tagging formulation with distance encoding proves to be a strong performer, demonstrating the power of leveraging RNNs for capturing long-range dependencies in argumentative structures. This specific formulation represents a concrete contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently reports competitive or superior results with the neural models compared to the strong ILP baseline. The detailed tables and analysis of performance differences (e.g., paragraph vs. essay level) provide a convincing demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration:** The investigation of four distinct neural framing techniques demonstrates a thorough approach to tackling the problem.  This breadth allows for a more nuanced understanding of the challenges and opportunities in neural AM.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized.  The motivation, methodology, and results are presented in a logical and accessible manner.  The inclusion of Figure 1 to illustrate the argumentative structure is helpful.
4. **Important Negative Result:** Demonstrating the ineffectiveness of the dependency parsing approach is valuable. It prevents future researchers from pursuing this potentially unproductive avenue and guides the field toward more promising directions.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention links to software and implementation details, increasing the potential for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide performance numbers, deeper error analysis would strengthen the paper. Understanding *where* the models struggle (e.g., specific relation types, long-distance dependencies) could provide valuable insights for future improvements. The mention of some limited error analysis in the supplementary material is not sufficient.
2. **LSTM-ER Performance Discrepancy:** The significant performance drop of LSTM-ER on essay-level data for relation identification is concerning and warrants further investigation. The explanation provided (too many potential relations) feels somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of why LSTM-ER struggles with long-range dependencies in this context is needed.
3. **MTL Gains are Modest:** While the paper shows that MTL can improve performance, the gains are relatively small compared to the sequence tagging approach. The authors could explore more sophisticated MTL architectures or weighting schemes to maximize the benefits of auxiliary tasks.  The framing of auxiliary tasks also seems somewhat arbitrary.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The paper acknowledges the peculiarities of the PE dataset (e.g., paragraph structure). The generalizability of the findings to other AM datasets with different characteristics is not fully addressed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on the types of errors made by the different models? For example, are certain relation types consistently misclassified?  Are there specific structural patterns that the models struggle with?
2. **LSTM-ER Degradation:** Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the performance drop of LSTM-ER on essay-level data for relation identification? What specific aspects of the model are failing to scale to longer sequences? Are there architectural modifications that could address this issue?
3. **MTL Design:**  What considerations led to the specific choice of auxiliary tasks in the MTL experiments?  Have you explored other potential auxiliary tasks, or different weighting schemes for combining the loss functions?
4. **Generalizability:** How do you anticipate the performance of these models to change on a different AM dataset with different characteristics (e.g., a dataset with longer, more complex argumentative structures, or a dataset from a different domain)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost of the different models.
*   The referencing of related work is generally good, but some key papers in the broader field of relation extraction could be mentioned to provide further context.
* The phrase "loss-lessly" feels somewhat imprecise when describing the major claim relinking process.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The empirical results are compelling, the analysis is insightful, and the writing is clear. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance.
