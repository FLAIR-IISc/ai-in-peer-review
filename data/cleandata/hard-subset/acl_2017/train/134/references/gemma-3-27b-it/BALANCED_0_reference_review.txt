This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), a challenging task involving component segmentation, classification, and relation identification. The authors explore several framings – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a pre-existing neural model (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) – and compare their performance against a strong, feature-based ILP baseline.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical evaluation of different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. While the idea of applying neural methods isn't novel, the systematic exploration of these diverse framings and their comparison against a strong baseline provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. A secondary contribution lies in the observation that, despite theoretical expectations, framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, while a simple sequence tagging model with distance encoding performs robustly. Finally, the demonstration that multi-task learning can improve performance by leveraging auxiliary tasks is also a noteworthy contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation, testing four distinct neural framings against a robust ILP baseline on a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). This thoroughness provides a strong foundation for drawing conclusions about the suitability of different approaches.
2. **Practical Insights:** The finding that dependency parsing performs poorly for token-level AM, contrary to initial hypotheses, is a significant practical insight. This challenges previous assumptions and directs future research toward more promising avenues. The robustness of the sequence tagging approach is also a valuable observation.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the different models and experimental setup. The motivation for each framing is well-explained, and the results are presented in a digestible format. The inclusion of ablation studies (in the MTL section) further enhances clarity.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention making the software available, which would contribute significantly to the reproducibility of their results and encourage further research in this area. (though the review doesn't confirm this)

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Individual Components:** The individual neural architectures (BiLSTMs, CNNs, TreeLSTMs) are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of these architectures to the specific task of end-to-end AM and the comparative analysis. While valuable, the paper lacks a truly novel architectural contribution.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, it lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding the *types* of errors each model makes would provide valuable insights into their limitations and guide future improvements.  For example, what kinds of relations are consistently misclassified? Why does dependency parsing fail so dramatically?
3. **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are conducted solely on the PE dataset. While this dataset is a standard benchmark, it is crucial to evaluate the generalizability of the findings to other AM datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different argumentation schemes, domains, or annotation styles).
4. **MTL Parameter Search:** The description of the multi-task learning experiments feels somewhat superficial. The optimal configuration (which tasks to combine, which layers to share) appears to be determined through experimentation, but the details of the parameter search are not fully elaborated.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, illustrating specific examples of errors made by each model? This would help understand *why* certain models perform better or worse than others.
2.  How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameter settings (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension)? Were any systematic hyperparameter optimization techniques used?
3.  Have you considered evaluating the models on other AM datasets besides the PE dataset? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the challenges of applying these models to different datasets?
4.  In the MTL experiments, can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing specific auxiliary tasks and layer sharing configurations?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 2 is helpful for visualizing the distribution of relation distances.
*   The discussion of LSTM-ER's performance degradation with longer sequences is insightful and highlights a key challenge in applying this model to real-world data.
*   The inclusion of a discussion of related work is appropriate and provides context for the contributions of the paper.
*   The paper would benefit from a concluding section that explicitly outlines directions for future research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the paper does not present a groundbreaking novel architecture, the thorough empirical evaluation and practical insights are significant. The weaknesses are relatively minor and addressable. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, addressing the points raised in the questions and comments above.
