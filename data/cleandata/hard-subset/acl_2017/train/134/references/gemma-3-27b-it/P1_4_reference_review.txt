## Peer Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores neural network approaches to the challenging problem of end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adapting a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) designed for entity and relation extraction.  They evaluate these approaches on a dataset of persuasive essays, comparing performance against a strong feature-based ILP baseline. The core finding is that neural sequence tagging, particularly with distance encoding, provides robust performance, while dependency parsing proves ineffective for this task.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural Baselines for End-to-End AM:** The paper provides a valuable, comprehensive empirical investigation of several neural network architectures applied to the challenging end-to-end AM task. This establishes a baseline for future research in this area, demonstrating the viability (and limitations) of neural approaches.
2. **Insightful Comparison of Framing Choices:** The systematic comparison of different framing choices – dependency parsing vs. sequence tagging vs. multi-task learning – provides valuable insights into which approaches are best suited for this specific problem. The finding that dependency parsing struggles in this context is a particularly important negative result.
3. **Strong Performance of Distance-Encoded Sequence Tagging:** The paper highlights the effectiveness of encoding distance information into the sequence tagging framework, demonstrating its robustness and competitiveness against the ILP baseline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, experimenting with multiple models, datasets splits, and evaluation metrics. This provides a strong foundation for their claims.
2. **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of AM and the limitations of existing feature-engineered approaches, making a compelling case for exploring neural alternatives.
3. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the methodology and results.  The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful for understanding the data structure.
4. **Meaningful Negative Result:** The finding that dependency parsing struggles is important. It guides future research away from a potentially unproductive direction.
5. **Practical Insights:** The discussion of the limitations of LSTM-ER in long-document settings and the benefits of constraint modeling offer practical guidance for researchers working in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Models:** While the *application* of these models to AM is novel, the models themselves (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER, etc.) are established architectures. The core novelty lies in the framing of AM within these existing frameworks, rather than proposing new model architectures.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions some observations about error types, a more in-depth error analysis would be valuable. For instance, analyzing specific examples where the sequence tagging model fails to capture long-range dependencies could provide further insights.
3. **Dependency on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is performed solely on the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset.  While this is a high-quality dataset, generalizing the results to other types of argumentative text is difficult without evaluation on additional datasets.  The paper acknowledges this in the conclusion, but it remains a limitation.
4. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t address the computational cost of these neural models compared to the ILP baseline. This is an important practical consideration, especially for resource-constrained environments.
5. **Overly Complex Tagging Scheme:** The tag set `Y` in the sequence tagging approach appears quite complex, incorporating BIO tags, component types, distances, and relation types. This complexity may increase the difficulty of training and potentially limit generalization performance. A discussion on the impact of this complexity would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on examples where the sequence tagging model struggles with long-range dependencies? What types of errors are most frequent?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of your best-performing model (STagBLCC) compared to the ILP baseline?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of STagBLCC to the maximum distance considered in the tag set (e.g., restricting distances to {-2, -1, 0, 1, 2})?  Would this simplification significantly impact performance?
4.  You mention that the "natural subtasks" C and R are helpful in the MTL setting.  Can you elaborate on *why* these subtasks are beneficial? Is it simply providing more training signal, or is there an underlying interaction between the tasks?
5.  Given the limitations of LSTM-ER on long documents, did you experiment with any techniques to mitigate this issue, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive and well-formatted.
*   The paper would benefit from including some qualitative examples of predicted argument structures alongside the quantitative results. This would help readers understand the strengths and weaknesses of the different models more concretely.
*   The discussion of the relationship between the dependency parsing framing and the tree-like structure of the dataset could be expanded.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the individual models used are not novel, the systematic evaluation and insightful comparison of different framing choices are significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
