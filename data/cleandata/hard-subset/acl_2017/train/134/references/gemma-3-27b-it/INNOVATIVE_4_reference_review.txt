This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task in four ways: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). The core contribution is applying neural methods to this traditionally feature-engineered field. A secondary contribution is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of these different neural framings on a standard dataset. Finally, the paper provides a valuable comparative analysis, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem – end-to-end AM – with a fundamentally different approach than prior work. Shifting from hand-crafted features and ILP to neural networks represents a significant paradigm shift and opens up new avenues for research. While neural sequence models are common, their application to the *entire* AM pipeline, as presented here, is novel.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors don't settle on a single approach. Instead, they rigorously explore four distinct framings, providing a valuable comparative analysis. This breadth of experimentation is commendable and helps understand the suitability of different neural architectures for different facets of AM.
3. **Insightful Findings:** The paper yields several insightful findings. The ineffectiveness of token-level dependency parsing for end-to-end AM, despite success at the component level, is a surprising and important observation. The strong performance of sequence tagging, particularly when incorporating distance information, is another key takeaway. The observation that MTL is beneficial warrants further investigation.
4. **Strong Baseline & Dataset:** Evaluating against a strong feature-based ILP baseline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and utilizing a well-established dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) lends credibility to the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Depth of Analysis:** While the evaluation is comprehensive in *breadth*, it lacks depth. The authors mention hyperparameter optimization but provide limited details. A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different LSTM configurations, embedding dimensions, and other hyperparameters would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Performance Gap:** While the neural models perform competitively with the ILP baseline, the gains aren’t overwhelmingly significant in many cases. This raises the question of whether the complexity of the neural models is justified by the improvements achieved, particularly given the computational cost.
3. **LSTM-ER Performance Discrepancy:** The drastically different performance of LSTM-ER between paragraph and essay levels needs more investigation. The authors hypothesize that this relates to the increased number of possible relations, but a deeper dive into error analysis and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., relation pruning, attention mechanisms) is needed.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the authors touch on error types in some instances, a more systematic error analysis would provide valuable insights into the weaknesses of each model and guide future research. What types of errors are each model making? Are there specific argument structures that are particularly challenging?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search space was explored for each model, and what criteria were used to select the best hyperparameters?
2.  The LSTM-ER model shows a significant performance drop when moving from paragraph to essay level. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of this model on the essay level data? What types of relations are most frequently misclassified?
3.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, could you elaborate on the criteria used for selecting which auxiliary tasks to include? Did you experiment with different combinations of auxiliary tasks?
4.  You mention the potential for a more sophisticated relation pruning or attention mechanism to address the scalability issues of LSTM-ER. Have you explored any preliminary versions of these ideas?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise. The related work section provides a good overview of the field. The figures are helpful in illustrating the argument structures and model architectures. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the study and potential directions for future work.
