- Paper shows neural models can perform end-to-end argumentation mining (component segmentation, typing, relation ID) competitively without handcrafted features.
- Authors compare multiple neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER.
- Empirical results indicate several neural approaches match or outperform a strong feature-based ILP baseline.
- Study provides nuanced analysis by dataset characteristics (paragraph vs essay) and relation distance effects.
- Highlights the importance of modeling long-range dependencies and explicit relation encodings for AM.
- Strong experimental setup using the Stab & Gurevych dataset and varied architectures (BiLSTM, CRF, CNN).
- Detailed error analysis explains why certain models fail (e.g., dependency parsing on long sequences, LSTM-ER with many entities).
- Multi-task learning experiments show benefits of auxiliary tasks and task decoupling for AM.
- Paper is clearly written and well organized, with useful visualizations (e.g., relation distance distribution).
- Limited exploration of newer architectures like Transformers, which could address long-range dependency issues.
- Multi-task results lack thorough ablation studies and exploration of task weighting or auxiliary task selection.
- Dataset is limited to student essays, leaving generalization to other domains (news, legal) uncertain.
- Dependency parsing framings underperform and would benefit from deeper error breakdowns by relation type.
- More implementation and hyperparameter details are needed to improve reproducibility despite code link.
- Reviewer asks whether Transformer-based models (BERT/RoBERTa) were tried or considered and their expected impact.
- Questions about criteria and alternatives for selecting auxiliary tasks in multi-task learning (e.g., POS, NER).
- Requests more detailed error analysis for dependency parsing (mispredicted relation types, head errors, label errors).
- Suggests exploring data augmentation or transfer learning to improve domain generalization.
- Asks for computational cost details (training time, memory) across models, especially for LSTM-ER.
- Reference list is comprehensive and Figure 2 is a helpful inclusion.
- Recommends adding brief discussion of ethical considerations related to argumentation mining.
- Overall assessment: solid empirical contribution demonstrating neural AM viability; accept with minor revisions.
