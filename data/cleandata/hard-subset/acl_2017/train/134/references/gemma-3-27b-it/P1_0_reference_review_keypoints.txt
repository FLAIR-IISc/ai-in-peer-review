- Paper explores four neural framings for end-to-end argumentation mining: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER, evaluated on the PE dataset.
- Main finding: neural sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagBLCC) performs best and outperforms the ILP baseline.
- Token-based dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM.
- Multi-task learning yields some improvement.
- Contribution: first neural end-to-end solutions for computational AM and a comprehensive empirical comparison of framings.
- Contribution: negative result steering away from token-level dependency parsing for AM.
- Strength: strong empirical results with consistent gains across metrics (C-F1, R-F1).
- Strength: thorough exploration of modeling options and comparison to a strong ILP baseline.
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation and helpful examples/figures.
- Strength: some insightful error analysis highlighting dependency parsing and LSTM-ER limitations on long sequences.
- Weakness: experiments are limited to the PE dataset, raising concerns about generalizability to other domains.
- Weakness: missing ablation study quantifying the specific benefit of distance encoding in sequence tagging.
- Weakness: incomplete comparison to broader state-of-the-art methods (e.g., transformer-based relation extraction).
- Weakness: insufficient details on LSTM-ER hyperparameterization and tuning, making results hard to interpret.
- Question: will STagBLCC generalize to other AM datasets and domains?
- Question: please provide an ablation study isolating the distance encoding contribution.
- Question: provide detailed hyperparameter/tuning choices and any pretraining used for LSTM-ER.
- Question: provide more detailed error analysis, especially for relation types and long-distance dependencies.
- Suggestion: expand related work discussion to include neural relation extraction literature.
- Suggestion: include visualization of predicted argument structures for intuition.
- Suggestion: strengthen analysis of multi-task learning interactions and its effects on primary AM performance.
- Overall verdict: solid, well-executed work with valuable empirical contributions and worthy of acceptance, though addressing dataset diversity and ablation gaps would improve the paper.
