This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for Computational Argumentation Mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). The core contribution, as presented by the authors, is the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, surpassing a strong ILP baseline. A secondary claim is the surprising ineffectiveness of token-level dependency parsing for this task, contrasted with component-level approaches. Further contributions include demonstrating robust performance of a distance-encoding sequence tagger and showing benefits from a multi-task learning setup.

However, while the exploration of various neural framings is valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues that, cumulatively, outweigh its contributions. The presentation is often imprecise, the empirical analysis lacks rigor, and the claims are frequently overstated or unsupported by the results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Exploration of Multiple Framing Approaches:** The paper's strength lies in its breadth of investigation. Examining dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning provides a valuable, albeit preliminary, comparative analysis of different neural approaches to AM. This exploration encourages future research along these lines.
2. **Identification of Distance Encoding Benefit:** The finding that encoding distance information within the sequence tagging framework is effective is a notable observation. This suggests that relational information can be successfully incorporated into a tagging scheme, and warrants further investigation.
3. **Dataset Utilization:** Using the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset provides a consistent platform for comparison and facilitates progress within the AM community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation & Overstated Claims:** The paper frequently claims “superior performance” without sufficient justification. Improvements are often marginal and not always statistically significant (the authors only mention significance testing *after* presenting results). The claim of surpassing the ILP baseline is particularly weak, as the ILP results are presented as a single number without detailed breakdown, making a fair comparison impossible. Furthermore, several experimental setups lack clarity. For example, hyperparameter optimization details are relegated to the supplementary material, hindering reproducibility.
2. **Dependency Parsing Implementation and Interpretation:** The dependency parsing experiments are poorly motivated and executed. The authors acknowledge that modern parsers employ features, but their implementation seems to ignore this, leading to predictably poor results. More importantly, the conclusion that *token-level* dependency parsing is ineffective, in contrast to *component-level* approaches, is a weak generalization. The comparison isn't equitable, as component-level parsing operates on a coarser granularity and likely benefits from simpler structure. The paper fails to adequately address potential reasons for the poor performance—such as the inherent complexity of AM structures—beyond simply stating it’s “ineffective”.
3. **Inconsistent Reporting and Missing Details:** The paper contains numerous inconsistencies and omissions. For instance, the description of the MTL setup lacks specificity regarding which layers are used for which tasks. The explanation of the label set 'Y' is convoluted and difficult to follow. The description of LSTM-ER adaptation is also very brief. These omissions make it difficult to assess the validity of the experimental design and results.
4. **Over-reliance on Ablation without Comprehensive Analysis:** While ablation studies (e.g., in the MTL experiments) are present, they are often presented without thorough analysis. The authors simply state whether adding a subtask improves performance, without explaining *why* this might be the case. The observation that the 'C' task is more helpful than the 'R' task, for example, is not sufficiently explored.
5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting errors and stylistic inconsistencies. The figures are not fully self-explanatory, and the sentence structure is sometimes awkward. While these issues are minor individually, they collectively detract from the paper’s overall readability and professionalism.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the ILP baseline performance, including component and relation F1 scores at both 100% and 50% levels?
2. What specific hyperparameter optimization techniques were used for each model, and why were those techniques chosen?
3. In the MTL experiments, can you provide a more detailed explanation of the layer connections and the rationale behind the chosen architecture?
4. Why was LSTM-ER’s performance on relations so severely affected at the essay level? What steps were taken to mitigate this issue?
5. Could you elaborate on the limitations of token-level dependency parsing for AM beyond simply stating its "ineffectiveness"? Were any attempts made to address the inherent complexity of argumentation structures through feature engineering or architectural modifications?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive but lacks some formatting consistency. Minor grammatical errors are present throughout the paper. The paper would significantly benefit from a more focused narrative and a more rigorous empirical analysis.
