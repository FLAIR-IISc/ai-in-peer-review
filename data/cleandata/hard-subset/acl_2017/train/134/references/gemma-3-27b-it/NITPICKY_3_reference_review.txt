This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). The primary claimed contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, (2) achieving performance surpassing a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) demonstrating the ineffectiveness of a token-based dependency parsing approach, (4) showing the robustness of a sequence tagging model encoding distance information, and (5) illustrating performance gains with a multi-task learning setup.

A significant contribution, as I see it, is the comprehensive empirical exploration of different neural framing approaches for end-to-end AM. The authors systematically evaluate several options, providing valuable insights into their strengths and weaknesses within this specific task. The comparative analysis against a strong baseline (the ILP model) is also beneficial. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper rigorously evaluates multiple neural network architectures and framing strategies. The authors don't simply propose a single solution but provide a thorough comparative analysis, which is commendable. The experiments cover different levels of granularity (paragraph vs. essay level) and offer a nuanced understanding of each approach's performance.
2. **Clear Identification of Limitations:** The authors honestly acknowledge the weaknesses of the dependency parsing approach, a result that contradicts initial expectations. This demonstrates intellectual honesty and a critical self-assessment of their work.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Results:** The discussion goes beyond simply presenting numbers; the authors attempt to explain *why* certain approaches perform better or worse, providing valuable insights into the characteristics of the AM task and the suitability of different modeling choices. The observation about the performance drop with longer sequences in dependency parsing, and the discussion of distance encoding are particularly insightful.
4. **Practical Insights:** The findings regarding the benefits of multi-task learning, specifically incorporating component and relation identification as auxiliary tasks, offer practical guidance for future research in AM. The identification of component tasks as particularly helpful is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and Presentation Inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting issues. For example, inconsistent use of bolding, overly verbose sentence structures, and occasionally awkward phrasing detract from the overall readability.  Figure 1 is low resolution and difficult to interpret.  The table captions are overly brief and could benefit from more detail.  The reference formatting is also inconsistent.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing Detail:** While the authors mention statistical significance in the caption of Table 1, the details of the tests are only vaguely stated. A more thorough presentation of the statistical tests performed (e.g., specifying p-values for each comparison) would strengthen the validity of the claims. The claim of "significantly better" requires more rigorous backing.
3. **Overly Optimistic Framing of Contributions:** The claim of presenting the "first" neural end-to-end solutions feels overstated. Previous work, even if not directly comparable in framing, has explored neural approaches to components of the AM pipeline. While this paper offers a comprehensive *integration* of these components, labeling it as the absolute first is inaccurate.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While some discussion of error patterns is present, a more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying specific types of errors made by each model (e.g., misclassifying specific relation types, incorrect span identification) could provide valuable clues for future improvements. The qualitative analysis feels superficial.
5. **The MTL Results are Convoluted:** The presentation of MTL results in Table 2 is difficult to decipher. The notation (e.g., "Y-3:YC-1:YR-2") is unclear and requires careful study to understand. A more intuitive presentation of the experimental setup and results would significantly improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the statistical tests performed to determine the significance of the results presented in Table 1? Specifically, please provide p-values for each comparison.
2. Regarding the claim of presenting the "first" neural end-to-end solutions, could you clarify how this work differs from previous approaches that have explored neural networks for individual AM subtasks or partially integrated pipelines?
3. Can you elaborate on the types of errors made by each model? Specifically, what are the most common misclassifications or span identification errors? Provide specific examples.
4.  Could you clarify the notation used in Table 2 for the MTL experiments? A more intuitive presentation of the experimental setup and results would be greatly appreciated.
5. Could you provide a higher-resolution version of Figure 1, or alternatively, recreate it with clearer labels and better visual clarity?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused writing style. Several sentences are overly verbose and could be streamlined.
* The introduction could be slightly more focused on motivating the need for end-to-end neural AM, rather than providing a general overview of the AM task.
* Please carefully proofread the paper for any remaining grammatical errors or inconsistencies.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable empirical study and offers several insightful findings, the numerous minor flaws – particularly concerning formatting, statistical rigor, and clarity of presentation – significantly detract from its overall impact. I lean towards **rejection** due to the cumulative effect of these issues. Addressing these weaknesses would substantially improve the quality of the manuscript and make it more compelling for acceptance.
