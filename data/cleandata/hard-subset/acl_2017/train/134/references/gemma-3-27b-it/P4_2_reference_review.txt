This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). The primary contributions are (1) demonstrating the feasibility of neural end-to-end AM, (2) achieving state-of-the-art performance on a persuasive essay dataset compared to a feature-based ILP baseline, and (3) showing that a token-based dependency parsing framing is ineffective while sequence tagging with distance encoding is robust.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Neural Methods to End-to-End AM:** The core strength is the exploration of neural network architectures for a traditionally feature-engineered task. While individual components of AM have seen neural approaches, a comprehensive end-to-end investigation is genuinely novel and valuable. The exploration of different framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning) demonstrates a breadth of investigation.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The presented results, particularly the performance of STagBLCC and LSTM-ER on the paragraph level, demonstrate that neural methods *can* surpass the ILP baseline. The ablation studies in the multi-task learning experiments and the detailed analysis of LSTM-ER’s performance provide further insights into what works and why. The substantial performance improvement on the paragraph level, compared to the essay level, provides a concrete observation about the inherent difficulty of scaling the methods.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting numbers. The authors analyze the results, discuss the limitations of the dependency parsing approach, and provide insights into why certain methods perform better than others. The detailed analysis of distance dependencies in the sequence tagging model is particularly commendable. The inclusion of results at both 100% and 50% matching levels offers a nuanced view of performance.
4. **Clear Problem Definition and Related Work:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of AM and positions the work within the existing literature. The related work section effectively summarizes previous approaches and justifies the motivation for exploring neural methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset & Generalizability:** The evaluation is performed on a single dataset (Stab and Gurevych 2016), which is relatively small. While the authors acknowledge the dataset’s peculiarities, the generalizability of the findings to other AM datasets is unclear. The performance drop on the essay level suggests that the models struggle with longer sequences and more complex argument structures which raises concerns about scaling to real-world applications.
2. **Framing Choices & Lack of Justification:** While the authors explore several framings, the justification for *why* certain framings were chosen is sometimes weak. For example, the claim that dependency parsing is a "natural choice" because argument structures resemble trees is not fully convincing, given the results. The framing of relations as distance encoding in sequence tagging feels somewhat arbitrary; alternative relation modeling techniques might be more effective.
3. **Complexity and Readability:** The paper is dense and packed with technical details. This makes it challenging to follow the various experiments and analyses. While thoroughness is appreciated, a more streamlined presentation could improve clarity.  Specifically, the extensive discussion of different model variations (e.g., within the sequence tagging experiments) makes it difficult to isolate key findings.
4. **Insufficient Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison with the ILP baseline is valuable, exploring other strong neural baselines—beyond simply adapting existing architectures—could further strengthen the evaluation. For instance, a more recent or specialized transformer-based model might provide a more challenging comparison point.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The authors mention the potential for long-range dependencies in the data, but the performance drop on the essay level suggests the models struggle with them. Could you elaborate on techniques explored (or considered) to better handle long-range dependencies, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical models?
2. The distance encoding scheme in sequence tagging seems like a specific choice. Did you experiment with other methods of encoding relational information, and if so, what were the results?
3. How sensitive is the performance of the multi-task learning approach to the choice of auxiliary tasks? Were other auxiliary tasks considered, and if so, why were the ones chosen ultimately selected?
4.  The paper mentions LSTM-ER and STagBLCC performing similarly on components but diverging on relations. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis for relation prediction in both models to understand the specific types of errors each model makes?

**Additional Comments:**

* Figure 2 could be improved with a logarithmic scale to better visualize the tail of the distance distribution.
* The formatting of the equations and tables is slightly inconsistent.
* Consider providing code availability to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
