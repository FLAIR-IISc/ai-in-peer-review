This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing LSTM-ER model. The authors claim (1) the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, (2) outperformance over a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) demonstration of the ineffectiveness of token-level dependency parsing for AM, (4) robustness of a distance-encoding sequence tagging approach, and (5) improvement via multi-task learning.

A primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical investigation of various neural network architectures applied to a complex, structured prediction task – argumentation mining. The comprehensive experimentation, encompassing multiple framing choices and comparison against a strong baseline (the ILP model), is valuable for the community. The authors' identification of the limitations of a token-based dependency parsing approach is a useful negative result. Furthermore, the demonstration that sequence tagging with distance encoding performs well is a solid practical finding.

Strengths:

1. **Extensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper systematically explores several neural network architectures, providing a substantial amount of experimental data. The comparison against the ILP baseline is crucial, and the observed performance gains are encouraging. The ablation studies in the MTL section, while limited, provide some insights into the benefit of auxiliary tasks.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional, feature-engineered approaches to AM, laying the groundwork for the proposed neural solutions. The motivation for avoiding feature engineering and embracing end-to-end learning is well-established.
3. **Useful Negative Result:** The finding that a direct application of dependency parsing to the token level is ineffective is valuable. This steers future research away from potentially unproductive avenues and highlights the importance of considering task-specific constraints.
4. **Strong Performance of Sequence Tagging:** The robust performance of the distance-encoding sequence tagging model suggests a promising direction for future research. This provides a relatively simple and effective approach for AM.

Weaknesses:

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methods:** The neural architectures employed (BiLSTMs, CRFs, LSTMs) are standard and lack significant methodological novelty. The primary contribution lies in the *application* of these techniques to the AM problem, rather than the introduction of new modeling approaches. While applying established methods to new problems is valuable, the paper doesn’t strongly justify why these specific architectures were chosen over alternatives.
2. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the authors mention error analysis, the discussion is superficial. A more in-depth analysis of where the models struggle (e.g., specific types of arguments, long-range dependencies, ambiguous relationships) would provide valuable insights and guide future development. The limited discussion of the models' weaknesses hinders the assessment of their true potential.
3. **Overreliance on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is conducted solely on the PE dataset. While this dataset is a valuable resource, the generalizability of the findings to other domains and argumentation styles is uncertain. A broader evaluation across diverse datasets is needed to establish the robustness of the proposed approaches.
4. **Insufficient Justification for MTL Configurations:** The rationale behind the specific MTL configurations (which auxiliary tasks to include, which layers to connect) is not clearly articulated. The results appear somewhat ad-hoc, lacking a strong theoretical or empirical justification.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific types of errors made by each model and potential reasons for those errors? Specifically, what kinds of relations are most often misclassified, and what characteristics of the argument structure contribute to those errors?
2. What steps were taken to tune the hyperparameters for each model, and how sensitive are the results to different hyperparameter settings? Could you provide more details regarding the optimization process?
3. Do you have any insights into why the dependency parsing approach failed so dramatically? Was it related to the length of the sequences, the complexity of the argument structures, or limitations of the parsing algorithms themselves?
4. What are the computational costs (training time, inference time) of each approach, and how do they compare to the feature-based ILP model?



Additional Comments: The discussion of LSTM-ER feels somewhat disconnected from the main narrative. Its performance seems less competitive than other approaches, and the analysis of its shortcomings is limited. The formatting of Table 2 is also difficult to parse.
