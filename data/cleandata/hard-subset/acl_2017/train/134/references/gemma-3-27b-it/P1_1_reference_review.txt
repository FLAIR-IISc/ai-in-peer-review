## Peer Review: Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining

**Summary:**

This paper investigates neural network approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), moving beyond traditional pipeline methods reliant on hand-crafted features. The authors explore framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning problem, and leveraging a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) designed for relation extraction. They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset, comparing them to a strong ILP baseline. The core finding is that while dependency parsing is ineffective, sequence tagging (specifically BiLSTM-CRF variations) and LSTM-ER show promising results, and multi-task learning provides further improvements.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, ordered by strength):**

1.  Presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to computational AM.
2.  Demonstrating that several neural models outperform a state-of-the-art joint ILP model.
3.  Showing that a token-based dependency parsing framing is ineffective.
4. Demonstrating that a standard sequence tagging model encoding distance information performs robustly, and that multi-task learning improves performance.

**My Assessment of Contributions:**

1. **Demonstrating the viability of neural end-to-end AM (primary):** The paper convincingly shows that neural models can achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance to traditional feature-engineered ILP approaches. This is a significant step forward for the field, potentially reducing reliance on manual feature design and making AM more adaptable to new datasets.
2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation of different neural framings (secondary):**  The authors explore a good range of neural architectures applied to AM, providing valuable insights into which approaches are most promising (sequence tagging, LSTM-ER) and which are less effective (dependency parsing). This offers a useful roadmap for future research.
3. **Highlighting the importance of relation modeling and task interaction (tertiary):**  The investigation of multi-task learning and the analysis of LSTM-ER’s performance demonstrates that effectively modeling relations and leveraging auxiliary tasks are crucial for achieving strong performance in AM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset provides a strong, well-annotated baseline for comparison. The authors perform extensive experiments and compare against a relevant and strong baseline (the ILP model).
2.  **Clear Problem Framing & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional AM approaches (feature engineering, error propagation) and motivates the exploration of neural methods.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The ablation studies and analysis of results (e.g., the discussion around dependency parsing, the impact of distance encoding, and the benefits of multi-task learning) are well-reasoned and provide valuable insights for the research community.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors mention providing links to software, which significantly enhances the reproducibility of the results. This is a commendable practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the authors offer some explanation for the dependency parsing failure, a more thorough error analysis of *all* models would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the models fail (specific types of errors, types of arguments) would provide valuable direction for future improvements.  Specifically, discussing examples of errors made by the best performing models would provide insights into their limitations.
2.  **Complexity of the Tagging Scheme:** The sequence tagging scheme (STagT) is complex, incorporating BIO tags, component types, distances, and relation types. While effective, this complexity may hinder interpretability and potentially increase training difficulties. A clearer justification for each component of the tag set, and potentially an analysis of the impact of removing or simplifying certain aspects, would be helpful.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Neural Relation Extraction Models:** The LSTM-ER model is compared to the ILP baseline and the author's other approaches, but not to more recent or specifically designed neural relation extraction models. A comparison to these models could provide a more complete picture of the performance landscape.
4.  **Hyperparameter Optimization Detail:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, it lacks details on the specific strategies employed. Providing more information on this aspect would improve the reproducibility and credibility of the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of errors made by the best-performing models (STagBLCC, LSTM-ER)? What types of arguments or relationships are most frequently misidentified?
2.  What was the rationale behind including all four components (BIO, component type, distance, relation type) in the STagT tag set? Did you experiment with simpler tag sets (e.g., removing the distance encoding)? If so, what were the results?
3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter optimization process? What search strategies were used, and what were the key hyperparameters tuned for each model?
4. Regarding the long-range dependency issue observed with LSTM-ER, did you explore any techniques to mitigate this problem, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
