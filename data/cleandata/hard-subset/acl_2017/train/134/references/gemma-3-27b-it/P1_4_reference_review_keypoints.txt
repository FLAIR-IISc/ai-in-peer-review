- Paper evaluates neural end-to-end approaches for argumentation mining (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) on persuasive essays, finding sequence tagging with distance encoding effective and dependency parsing ineffective.
- Empirically establishes neural baselines for end-to-end AM and maps out viability and limitations of these approaches.
- Systematic comparison of framings yields actionable insight: sequence tagging outperforms dependency parsing for this task.
- Distance-encoded sequence tagging (STagBLCC) shows strong, robust performance competitive with an ILP baseline.
- Evaluation is comprehensive across models, splits, and metrics, supporting the claims.
- Problem motivation and rationale for neural approaches are clear and well-explained.
- Paper is well-written, organized, and includes helpful figures (e.g., Figure 1) clarifying data structure.
- The negative result that dependency parsing struggles is meaningful and guides future work.
- Practical observations about LSTM-ER limitations on long documents and benefits of constraint modeling are useful.
- Models used are established architectures; novelty lies mainly in their framing for AM rather than new architectures.
- Paper lacks a detailed error analysis, especially for sequence tagging failures on long-range dependencies.
- Evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016), limiting generalizability to other argumentation texts.
- Computational cost (training/inference time) of neural models versus ILP baseline is not reported.
- The sequence tagging label set is complex (BIO, component types, distances, relation types), which may hinder training and generalization.
- Request for detailed error analysis: what error types and examples show sequence tagging struggling with long-range dependencies?
- Request for report of computational cost of STagBLCC compared to ILP baseline (training and inference times).
- Ask how sensitive STagBLCC is to maximum distance encoding and whether restricting distances harms performance.
- Request explanation why subtasks C and R help in MTL—more signal versus deeper task interaction.
- Ask whether authors tried attention or hierarchical models to mitigate LSTM-ER issues on long documents.
- Suggest adding qualitative predicted-structure examples and expanding discussion on dependency parsing vs. dataset tree-like structure.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with valuable contributions and systematic evaluation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
