- Paper evaluates end-to-end neural approaches for argumentation mining and finds several neural models outperform a strong ILP baseline, with sequence tagging + distance encoding particularly effective.
- Contribution: provides an empirical benchmark showing neural methods are competitive or superior to feature-engineered ILP approaches.
- Contribution: systematically compares framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) and identifies strengths/weaknesses, notably that dependency parsing is ineffective.
- Contribution: introduces an effective sequence tagging formulation with distance encoding that captures long-range argumentative dependencies.
- Strength: strong empirical results with detailed comparisons (paragraph vs. essay level) demonstrating model effectiveness.
- Strength: comprehensive exploration across four neural framings yields nuanced insights for the field.
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation and helpful illustrative figures.
- Strength: important negative result showing dependency parsing is not promising for this task.
- Strength: authors provide code/implementation details to aid reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited/error analysis is shallow; more detailed breakdowns of model failures are needed.
- Weakness: LSTM-ER shows a large performance drop on essay-level relation ID, with insufficient analysis of the causes.
- Weakness: MTL yields only modest gains and auxiliary-task choices/weighting seem underexplored.
- Weakness: results rely on the PE dataset which has particular structure, raising questions about generalizability to other AM datasets.
- Question: provide detailed error analysis—are particular relation types or structural patterns consistently mispredicted?
- Question: analyze why LSTM-ER degrades on longer essays and whether architectural changes could mitigate this.
- Question: justify auxiliary-task choices in MTL and whether other tasks or loss weightings were tried.
- Question: how will these methods generalize to datasets with different/domain-specific or more complex argumentative structures?
- Additional: discuss computational costs of the models.
- Additional: cite more relation-extraction literature for broader context.
- Additional: the term "loss-lessly" is imprecise and should be revised.
- Overall: paper is strong, insightful, and recommend acceptance.
