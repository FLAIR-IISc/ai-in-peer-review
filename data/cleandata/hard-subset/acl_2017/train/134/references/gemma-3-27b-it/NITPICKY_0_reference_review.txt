This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing LSTM-ER model. The authors claim (1) the first neural end-to-end AM solutions, (2) superior performance to a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) demonstration of the ineffectiveness of token-level dependency parsing for AM, (4) robustness of a distance-encoding sequence tagging model, and (5) improvements from a multi-task learning setup.

A primary contribution of this work is the extensive empirical evaluation of diverse neural network architectures applied to the challenging end-to-end AM task. While end-to-end AM has been explored previously, this paper offers a valuable comparative analysis of different neural framing choices. A secondary contribution is the clear demonstration of the challenges of applying dependency parsing directly at the token level for AM – a finding that contradicts prior suggestions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of multiple neural network architectures – dependency parsing (with several variants), sequence tagging (BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM-CRF-CNN), multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER – offering a valuable empirical comparison. The consistent evaluation across paragraph and essay levels provides nuanced insights.
2. **Clear Demonstration of Dependency Parsing Limitations:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that framing AM as a token-level dependency parsing problem is ineffective, highlighting the difficulties of applying this approach to complex argument structures. This negative result is important for guiding future research.
3. **Robustness of Sequence Tagging with Distance Encoding:** The findings indicate that a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding is a promising direction for end-to-end AM, exhibiting robust performance across different environments.
4. **Thorough Error Analysis (Implied):** While not extensively detailed in the main text, the discussion of performance differences between paragraph and essay levels, along with observations about LSTM-ER’s behavior, suggests a careful consideration of error patterns.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance Reporting:**  The tables present performance numbers, but lack statistical significance tests to confirm whether the observed differences are genuinely meaningful. The single mention of a Wilcoxon signed-rank test in the caption of Table 1 feels inadequate—results *throughout* should be accompanied by p-values. This severely weakens the claims of superiority.
2. **Insufficient Detail in Hyperparameter Optimization:** The paper states hyperparameters were optimized but provides limited detail about the process. This makes it difficult to assess the reproducibility of the results and the fairness of the comparisons.  Were the same optimization strategies used for all models? What search space was used?
3. **Limited Discussion of Ablation Studies:** While the paper explores different modeling choices, a deeper analysis through ablation studies (e.g., removing the CNN layer from BiLSTM-CRF) would have provided more granular insights into the contribution of each component.
4. **Vague Descriptions and Formatting Issues:** The writing is occasionally imprecise. For instance, the description of the sequence tagging label set (Y) lacks clarity in its initial presentation. The numerous equations and lists break the flow of the text. Furthermore, the use of “cf.” is excessive and often unnecessary. The figure captions are also sometimes uninformative.
5. **Incomplete Comparison to ILP:** The ILP comparison is only mentioned briefly. A more detailed discussion of the ILP model’s architecture, features, and limitations would strengthen the argument for the benefits of the neural approaches.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for all reported performance differences?
2. Please detail the hyperparameter optimization process used for each model, including the search space and optimization strategy.
3.  Could you provide results from ablation studies to assess the contribution of specific components within your models (e.g., CNN layer in BiLSTM-CRF)?
4. Please clarify the precise details of the ILP model used for comparison, including feature sets and constraint definitions.
5. The phrase “complex argument structures” is used multiple times. Can you provide a concrete example of what makes a specific argument structure “complex” in the context of this dataset?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please proofread the paper carefully for grammatical errors and inconsistencies.
*   Consider using more informative figure captions.
*   Simplify the notation and presentation of equations where possible.
*   Provide links to the code and data to encourage reproducibility.



Despite the strengths of the empirical evaluation, the lack of statistical rigor, insufficient methodological detail, and minor formatting issues raise significant concerns. I recommend **rejection** at this time. Addressing the weaknesses and providing the requested information are crucial for justifying the claims made in this paper.
