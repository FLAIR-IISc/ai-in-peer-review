## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores several neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), aiming to move beyond traditional pipeline approaches reliant on hand-crafted features. The authors investigate framing AM as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilize a pre-existing LSTM-ER model. They evaluate these approaches on the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation of Neural Approaches to End-to-End AM:** The paper provides a valuable, large-scale empirical comparison of several neural architectures for this task.  This is a significant contribution as it establishes a baseline for future research and demonstrates the feasibility of neural approaches.
2. **Demonstration of Sequence Tagging Effectiveness:** The authors convincingly show that a sequence tagging approach, particularly with distance encoding, is a robust and effective method for end-to-end AM, outperforming the ILP baseline and other explored methods.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Framing Choices:** The paper offers a clear analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of different framing choices (parsing vs. tagging, modularity, constraints), providing useful guidance for researchers in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Setup & Baseline:** The use of the Stab & Gurevych dataset, a well-regarded resource in the AM community, and a robust comparison against a strong ILP baseline lends credibility to the results.  The clear separation of paragraph and essay-level performance is particularly helpful.
2. **Thorough Exploration of Different Approaches:** The authors systematically investigate a range of neural architectures, providing a comprehensive overview of their applicability to the AM task. This breadth is valuable for the community.
3. **Clear and Insightful Analysis:** The paper doesn’t just *report* results; it provides thoughtful analysis of *why* certain methods perform better than others, highlighting the importance of local vs. global modeling and the impact of long-range dependencies.  The discussion of the distance encoding in sequence tagging is particularly strong.
4. **Practical Recommendations:** The concluding remarks offer clear recommendations for future research directions, based on the empirical findings.  The suggestion that a more restrained modeling is preferable for longer documents is a particularly useful insight.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Models:** While the combination of neural architectures and the AM task is valuable, most of the individual models (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are established techniques adapted from other NLP areas. The primary novelty lies in the *application* and *comparison* of these models to AM, not in the models themselves. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but it limits the impact.
2. **LSTM-ER's Performance Discrepancy:** The substantial performance drop of LSTM-ER on essay level for relation identification is a major concern. While the authors offer a plausible explanation, it raises questions about the model's scalability and practical applicability to real-world documents, which are often longer than the paragraphs used in this study.  More investigation into mitigating this issue is needed.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the multi-task learning experiments are interesting, a more detailed ablation study – systematically removing auxiliary tasks to assess their individual contribution – would strengthen the findings and provide a clearer understanding of what auxiliary tasks are most effective.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** Although mentioned, a more in-depth error analysis, focusing on specific types of errors made by different models, could provide further insights into their weaknesses and guide future improvements.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the significant performance drop of LSTM-ER on essay level for relation identification, could you elaborate on potential strategies to address this issue?  For example, have you experimented with techniques to limit the number of potential relations considered, or with different architectures for relation classification?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter optimization process?  Were specific hyperparameters tuned for each model, and if so, what was the tuning methodology?
3.  In the MTL experiments, what criteria were used for selecting the auxiliary tasks? Was this selection based on intuition, or on preliminary experiments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   It would be helpful to include a more detailed description of the dataset's characteristics, such as the distribution of argument types and relation types.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the individual models employed are not groundbreaking, the comprehensive evaluation, insightful analysis, and practical recommendations make this work a significant advancement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.  The potential to explore solutions to LSTM-ER's scaling issues, and a more detailed ablation study would further strengthen the paper.
