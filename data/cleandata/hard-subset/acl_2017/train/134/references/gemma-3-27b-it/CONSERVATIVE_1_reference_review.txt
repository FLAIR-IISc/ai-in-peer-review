This paper explores neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing neural model (LSTM-ER). The authors claim several contributions: (1) the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM; (2) outperformance of a strong ILP baseline; (3) demonstration of the ineffectiveness of token-level dependency parsing for AM; (4) robust performance of a sequence tagging approach with distance encoding; and (5) performance gains from a multi-task learning setup.

A primary contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation of various neural network architectures applied to the AM task. While end-to-end neural AM is a natural progression, the sheer breadth of explored framings—dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adapting an existing model—provides a valuable comparative analysis, revealing the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The authors diligently compare against a well-established, feature-engineered ILP baseline, lending credibility to their findings. The insights into the limitations of dependency parsing as a direct approach to AM are particularly noteworthy. 

Another strength is the careful consideration of the nuances of the dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). The authors correctly identify the peculiarities of the data – the tree-like structure and the distance between related components – and attempt to address these in their modeling choices (e.g., distance encoding in sequence tagging).  This demonstrates a good understanding of the data's impact on model performance.

Finally, the practical experimentation and detailed results presented (particularly Table 1) allow for a clear assessment of the methods. The ablation studies within the multi-task learning experiments offer valuable insight into the benefits of incorporating auxiliary tasks.

However, several weaknesses hinder a strong endorsement of this work. Firstly, the claims of novelty regarding "the first neural end-to-end solutions" feels overstated. Neural sequence labeling approaches to component identification have existed for some time (Habernal & Gurevych, 2016).  The novelty lies in the *combination* of end-to-end learning with these techniques *and* the systematic evaluation across multiple framings, but the claim requires nuance.

More critically, the performance gains over the ILP baseline, while statistically significant, are not particularly large.  While the neural models are competitive, the improvement doesn’t represent a paradigm shift, and the authors don’t fully address *why* the neural models achieve this improvement.  Is it simply the elimination of feature engineering, or is there a more fundamental advantage to the neural approach in capturing complex relationships? 

A significant weakness is the lack of detailed error analysis. While some discussion exists, a more in-depth investigation of where the models fail (e.g., types of relations most often misclassified, common errors in component identification) would strengthen the conclusions and provide more direction for future research. The authors mention issues with long-range dependencies impacting LSTM-ER, but further investigation of these errors would be beneficial.

Finally, the motivation for some design choices, particularly the specific encoding scheme for the sequence tagging approach, feels somewhat arbitrary.  While the authors justify it based on data characteristics, a more thorough exploration of alternative encoding strategies could be valuable.



Questions to Authors:

1. The claim of being the "first" neural end-to-end solution seems broad. Can you clarify what specific aspects of your work represent a genuinely novel contribution compared to prior work on neural AM, even if it doesn't constitute a full end-to-end system?
2. The performance gains over the ILP baseline are modest. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* the neural models outperform the baseline, beyond simply eliminating feature engineering? What specific linguistic phenomena are the neural models better at capturing?
3. Please provide a more detailed error analysis. What are the most common types of errors made by each model, and what insights can be gleaned from these errors to improve performance?
4.  You mention that the long sequence lengths impact LSTM-ER. Have you explored techniques for handling long sequences, such as hierarchical attention mechanisms or sequence truncation?
5. Why were specific hyperparameters chosen for each model? Was a thorough hyperparameter search performed, and if so, can you provide details of the search space and methodology?
