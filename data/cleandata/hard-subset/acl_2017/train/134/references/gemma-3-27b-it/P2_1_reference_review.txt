## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates neural network approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), moving away from traditional pipeline architectures reliant on hand-crafted features. The authors explore four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging (with distance encoding), multi-task learning, and adaptation of a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on a persuasive essay dataset, comparing performance against a strong ILP baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation of Neural AM Approaches:** The paper provides a valuable, broad empirical comparison of various neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. This is a significant contribution as it offers practical insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling choices for this challenging task.
2. **Demonstration of Viability of Neural End-to-End AM:** The authors show that neural models can achieve competitive or superior performance to the established feature-based ILP approach, suggesting a promising direction for future research.
3. **Insights into Framing Choices:** The work provides nuanced insights into the suitability of different framings (dependency parsing vs. sequence tagging) for AM, particularly concerning the handling of long-range dependencies and the impact of architectural constraints.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, exploring several neural architectures and comparing them against a strong baseline. They also appropriately consider both paragraph-level and essay-level performance.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges of traditional AM methods and the potential benefits of neural end-to-end learning, focusing on the limitations of feature engineering and error propagation in pipeline approaches.
3. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Using the state-of-the-art ILP model provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed neural approaches.  The results clearly demonstrate that neural methods are viable alternatives.
4. **Detailed Analysis of Results:** The authors don’t simply report numbers; they provide insightful analysis of *why* certain approaches work better than others, drawing connections to the characteristics of the data and the architectural properties of the models. The discussion regarding dependency parsing and long-range dependencies is particularly well-articulated.
5. **Practical Insights:** The observation that sequence tagging models are robust and benefit from auxiliary tasks (multi-task learning) provides practical guidance for researchers working in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The evaluation is performed on a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). While this is a high-quality dataset, the generalizability of the findings to other types of argumentative text remains uncertain.  This limits the broader impact of the work.
2. **LSTM-ER Implementation Details:** The description of LSTM-ER adaptation feels somewhat brief.  More detail on how the model was specifically configured for the AM task (entity and relation types) would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding its performance.
3. **Performance Gap on Long-Range Dependencies:** While the authors identify the challenge of long-range dependencies, the LSTM-ER model doesn't fully resolve it. Further investigation into methods for explicitly modeling these dependencies within the neural architectures would strengthen the work.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the authors experiment with several models, more targeted ablation studies (e.g., removing the distance encoding in the sequence tagging model) would help isolate the contribution of specific components and provide a deeper understanding of model behavior.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding LSTM-ER, could you provide more details on how the entity and relation types were mapped to the original LSTM-ER framework? Specifically, how were the relations defined, and what changes were made to accommodate the argumentative structure?
2.  You mention that the independence assumptions of the BL tagger in the MTL setup led to more local errors. Could you elaborate on the specific types of errors observed and how these differ from those made by the BLCC model?
3.  Given the limitations of the dataset, what steps do you believe are necessary to ensure the generalizability of these findings to other argumentative domains?  Would transfer learning or domain adaptation techniques be a promising avenue for future research?
4.  What specific modifications could be made to address the challenges of modeling long-range dependencies more effectively in LSTM-ER or the sequence tagging framework?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the existing literature.
*   The references are properly formatted.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The thorough empirical evaluation, insightful analysis, and practical recommendations make it a worthwhile read for researchers in this area. While the limited dataset and some implementation details could be improved, the overall quality of the work is high, and I recommend acceptance.
