This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into neural end-to-end learning for computational argument mining (AM). The authors explore several framings – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and adaptation of an existing entity-relation extraction model – and establish strong baselines on a challenging dataset. The key contribution is demonstrating the feasibility and effectiveness of neural approaches to this complex task, surpassing a prior ILP-based state-of-the-art. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is a thorough empirical analysis revealing the strengths and weaknesses of different neural framing choices for AM.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates that several neural approaches outperform the prior state-of-the-art ILP model, particularly on the paragraph level. This is a significant result, indicating the potential of neural methods to advance the field of AM. The performance gains are statistically significant as evidenced by the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Framing Choices:** The authors systematically investigate diverse neural framings for AM. This thoroughness is commendable, allowing for a nuanced understanding of which approaches are best suited for different aspects of the task and data characteristics. The comparison of dependency parsing, sequence tagging, and multi-task learning provides valuable insights.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Insightful Discussion:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results; it offers a thoughtful analysis of *why* certain approaches succeed or fail. The discussion of the challenges of dependency parsing on long sequences and the benefits of sequence tagging for capturing long-range dependencies is particularly insightful. The observation regarding the impact of distance encoding in sequence tagging is also valuable.
4. **Well-Established Baselines:** The use of a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline provides a solid foundation for comparison. The inclusion of other neural models (LSTM-ER) allows for a robust assessment of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains on Essay Level:** While performance is strong on the paragraph level, the drop in performance when applying the models to full essays is substantial, particularly for dependency parsing and LSTM-ER. This suggests a limitation in the ability of the models to handle longer contexts and more complex argument structures. The paper acknowledges this, but a deeper investigation into potential solutions (e.g., hierarchical models, attention mechanisms) would strengthen the work.
2. **Insufficient Exploration of Hyperparameter Optimization:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, the details are relegated to the supplementary material. A more thorough discussion of the hyperparameter search process and its impact on the results would be beneficial. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to specific hyperparameter choices.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** Although some error analysis is mentioned, a more systematic and in-depth error analysis would provide further insights into the shortcomings of the models. Examining specific error cases could reveal patterns and suggest avenues for improvement. For example, what types of relations are most frequently misclassified?
4. **Framing as Dependency Parsing is Quickly Dismissed:** Although the paper rightly concludes dependency parsing is ineffective, the dismissal feels somewhat abrupt given the initial framing. A more nuanced discussion of *why* it struggles so much, beyond just the length of sequences, could be beneficial. Perhaps the inherent tree structure assumption is fundamentally mismatched to the nature of argumentative structure.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper notes LSTM-ER's advantage in component detection but struggles with relation classification on the essay level. Could you elaborate on the types of errors made by LSTM-ER in relation classification? Are these errors related to the long-range dependencies or other factors?
2.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, did you investigate different weighting schemes for the auxiliary tasks? How sensitive are the results to the relative weight given to each task?
3.  You mention a potential benefit of hierarchical models for handling longer documents. Have you considered experimenting with such architectures, even in a preliminary fashion?
4.  The discussion around the distance encoding in sequence tagging suggests a potential for optimization. Did you experiment with different ways of encoding distance (e.g., using continuous values instead of discrete bins)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of clear and concise notation throughout the paper is appreciated.
*   The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the field.
*   The writing quality is generally excellent, making the paper easy to understand.



Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of computational argument mining. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
