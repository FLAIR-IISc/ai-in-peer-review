This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors explore framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, a multi-task learning problem, and utilizing a pre-existing model combining sequential and tree structure information (Miwa & Bansal, 2016). The primary contribution is a comprehensive empirical study of these different neural approaches to end-to-end AM, demonstrating that several outperform a strong feature-based ILP baseline. A secondary contribution is the observation that framing AM as token-based dependency parsing is ineffective, while a sequence tagging approach encoding distance information proves robust. Finally, the authors show that incorporating subtasks as auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup improves performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of multiple neural network architectures for end-to-end AM.  The authors don't focus on a single approach but systematically investigate diverse framings, providing valuable insights into their relative strengths and weaknesses. This is a significant contribution as it maps out the landscape of neural approaches to this complex task. The inclusion of a strong baseline (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) allows for a meaningful comparison.
2. **Practical Insights into Framing Choices:** The paper identifies specific challenges and benefits of each framing. The finding that dependency parsing is ineffective, contrary to some prior assumptions, is particularly notable.  The success of the sequence tagging approach with distance encoding offers a concrete design guideline for future work. These insights are valuable for researchers interested in applying neural methods to AM.
3. **Demonstrated Performance Improvements:**  The authors demonstrate that their neural models achieve competitive or superior performance compared to the established ILP baseline, particularly on the paragraph level.  The performance gains, while not always dramatic, represent a meaningful advancement in the field, especially given the elimination of manual feature engineering.
4. **Analysis of Multi-Task Learning:** The investigation of multi-task learning demonstrates a clear benefit from incorporating related subtasks, offering a strategy for further performance improvement. The analysis of which subtasks are most helpful (component detection being more impactful than relation identification) is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While results are presented, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* certain models fail in specific cases would provide more actionable insights. For example, the authors mention long-range dependencies being a challenge for the dependency parser; however, further analysis illustrating the specific types of errors made in these situations would be helpful.
2. **Overreliance on Paragraph-Level Evaluation:** The significantly better performance on the paragraph level compared to the essay level raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.  The authors acknowledge this, but the focus remains heavily on paragraph-level results. A more in-depth discussion of the challenges specific to essay-level processing and potential solutions is needed.
3. **Model Complexity and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks detail. Given the numerous models and configurations explored, a more systematic discussion of the tuning process and its impact on results would strengthen the study. It’s unclear how sensitive the findings are to specific hyperparameter choices.
4. **LSTM-ER's Discrepancy:** The significant performance disparity between LSTM-ER on paragraph vs. essay level demands further investigation. While the authors offer a plausible explanation related to the number of entities, a more rigorous analysis validating this hypothesis would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis for the dependency parsing approach? Specifically, what types of errors are most common, and can you illustrate them with examples from the dataset?
2. What strategies did you employ to address the performance drop observed when scaling from paragraph-level to essay-level processing? Did you experiment with techniques specifically designed for long-sequence modeling (e.g., attention mechanisms, hierarchical models)?
3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process used for each model? What specific techniques (e.g., grid search, random search) were employed, and how did you select the final hyperparameters?
4. Regarding LSTM-ER’s performance drop on essays, can you quantify the relationship between the number of entities in a document and the model’s performance? (e.g., correlation analysis)




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from including ablation studies to better understand the contribution of each component within the various models (e.g., the impact of CNNs in the BiLSTM-CRF models).
*   The reference list is extensive and well-organized.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, though some sections could benefit from further elaboration (e.g., the explanation of the MTL framework).
