This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), a task traditionally tackled with pipeline approaches and hand-crafted features. The authors explore four different neural framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) combining sequential and tree structures. The primary contribution is demonstrating that neural approaches *can* achieve competitive results with a strong ILP baseline, and identifying that a sequence tagging approach, particularly when incorporating distance information, is robust. A secondary contribution lies in the negative result for token-level dependency parsing, challenging prior proposals. Finally, the investigation into multi-task learning provides insights into the benefit of auxiliary tasks for AM.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Neural Networks to End-to-End AM:** The paper represents an important first step in applying neural network architectures to a complex task that has historically relied on feature engineering and pipeline approaches. This is a significant contribution as it opens up new avenues for research and potentially more robust and generalizable models.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors thoroughly evaluate four different neural framings on a challenging dataset, providing a comparative analysis of their strengths and weaknesses. The inclusion of a strong ILP baseline provides a crucial point of reference. The systematic experimentation with different model configurations and auxiliary tasks adds to the rigor of the evaluation.
3. **Insightful Negative Result for Dependency Parsing:** The finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for end-to-end AM is a valuable contribution. This result directly challenges previous suggestions and provides guidance for future research, indicating that focusing on coarser-grained representations or alternative parsing strategies might be more fruitful.
4. **Demonstrated Effectiveness of Sequence Tagging with Distance Encoding:** The successful application of sequence tagging, especially with the encoding of distance information, is a key strength. This approach demonstrates a practical and effective way to capture relationships between argument components in a neural network. The performance achieved is competitive with and, in some cases, surpasses the ILP baseline.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the overall combination and application to AM is novel, the individual neural architectures used (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are well-established in the NLP community. The novelty primarily resides in the *application* to this specific task rather than the development of fundamentally new neural models.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While some observations are made regarding error types, a more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the models fail (e.g., specific relation types, complex argument structures) could provide valuable insights for future improvements. The figure showing probability of correct relation identification given distance is a good start, but more detailed analysis is needed.
3. **Hyperparameter Optimization Details are Insufficient:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks details on the specific strategies used. A more transparent description of the hyperparameter search process would increase reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of the models’ sensitivity to different parameters.
4. **The Benefit of MTL is Limited:** The gains from multi-task learning appear modest. A clearer justification for the choice of auxiliary tasks and a more thorough investigation of different MTL configurations would strengthen this part of the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter optimization process, including the search space and the evaluation metric used for selection?
2. What specific types of errors are most common in the sequence tagging model? Are there particular relation types or argument structures that the model struggles with?
3. Given the limited success of dependency parsing, do you foresee any potential modifications or extensions to this approach that could make it more effective for AM? (e.g., graph-based parsing, different dependency schemes)
4. You mention that LSTM-ER's performance degrades on longer texts. Could you investigate the effect of segmenting longer essays into smaller chunks and applying LSTM-ER to each chunk individually?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the PE dataset and the potential challenges of generalizing the findings to other datasets with different annotation schemes.
* The code and data are not provided, preventing independent verification of the results. This is a critical omission for a research paper.
* The conclusion could be more concise and focused on the key takeaways from the research.
