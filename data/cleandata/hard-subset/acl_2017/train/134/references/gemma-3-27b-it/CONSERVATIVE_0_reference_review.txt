This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing an existing LSTM-ER model. The authors claim (1) they present the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, (2) several outperform a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) token-based dependency parsing is ineffective, (4) sequence tagging with distance encoding is robust, and (5) multi-task learning improves performance. 

I find the most significant contribution of this work to be the comprehensive empirical evaluation of various neural network architectures applied to the end-to-end AM task, specifically on the Stab & Gurevych dataset. While neural approaches to AM are not entirely novel, the systematic comparison across different framings (parsing, tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) provides valuable insights into their strengths and weaknesses. The observation that a simple sequence tagging approach with distance encoding performs well is a practical finding. The authors’ diligence in implementing and comparing these methods is commendable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper’s strength lies in its extensive experimentation. Testing four different neural network architectures, alongside a strong baseline (ILP), provides a solid foundation for drawing conclusions about the efficacy of each approach. The inclusion of both paragraph and essay-level evaluations highlights the challenges of scaling these methods.
2. **Insightful Analysis of Framing Choices:** The authors correctly identify that the choice of framing (parsing vs. tagging vs. MTL) significantly impacts performance. The analysis of why dependency parsing struggles, particularly at the essay level, is insightful. Their discussion of the trade-offs between modularity and constraints within different frameworks is also well-reasoned.
3. **Practical Findings on Sequence Tagging:** The strong performance of the sequence tagging approach, especially with distance encoding, is a practical and valuable result. This suggests a relatively simple and effective approach for tackling AM. The emphasis on the importance of distance information is a key takeaway.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproduction Potential:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the different models and experimental setups. Providing details regarding implementation and referencing publicly available resources improves reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques and the comprehensive evaluation are valuable, the individual neural architectures employed (BiLSTMs, LSTMs, CRFs) are well-established and not novel in themselves. The paper doesn’t introduce new neural network building blocks or learning algorithms. The claim of presenting “the first neural end-to-end solutions” feels overstated given prior work applying neural networks to aspects of AM, even if not in a fully integrated fashion.
2. **Over-Reliance on a Single Dataset:**  The entire evaluation is performed on the Stab & Gurevych dataset. While this is a strong dataset, generalizing the findings to other datasets with different annotation schemes or text genres is risky. Demonstrating robustness across multiple datasets would significantly strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** Although some error analysis is mentioned (e.g., regarding dependency parsing), a more detailed qualitative analysis of the errors made by each model would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the models fail in certain cases could inform future research directions. The mention of LSTM-ER's long-range dependency issues, while noted, lacks a substantial exploration of failure cases.
4. **Incremental Improvements in MTL:** The gains from multi-task learning are relatively modest. The paper suggests that adding auxiliary tasks can help, but the improvements are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity. The exploration of different auxiliary tasks and weighting schemes seems limited.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the performance drop from paragraph to essay level is "immense" for the dependency parser. Can you quantify this drop in more detail, and provide a more in-depth explanation of *why* the parser struggles with longer sequences? Is it solely due to computational complexity, or are there other factors at play?
2.  Regarding the LSTM-ER model, you state that its performance on relation identification degrades significantly at the essay level. Do you have any hypotheses about the specific types of relations that are most difficult to identify in longer texts? Could techniques like attention mechanisms or graph neural networks help mitigate this issue?
3.  You mention the distribution of distances between argument components.  Have you experimented with different ways of encoding this distance information (e.g., using embeddings or relative position encoding)? Would a more sophisticated encoding scheme improve performance?
4.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? How did you select the optimal hyperparameters for each model? Were any automated hyperparameter optimization techniques used?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Potash et al. (2017) is a bit awkward – it is cited as “research submitted during the preparation of our current work.” It would be stronger to either include it if it is now published, or acknowledge it is not publicly available.
*   Consider including a table comparing the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the different models. This would provide a more complete picture of their practicality.
