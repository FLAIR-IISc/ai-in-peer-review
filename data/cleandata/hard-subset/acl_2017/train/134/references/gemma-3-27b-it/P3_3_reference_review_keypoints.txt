- Neural end-to-end argumentation mining is viable and competitive with ILP-based pipelines.  
- Four framings compared: dependency parsing (ineffective), sequence tagging (robust, best with distance encoding), multi-task learning (improves results), and adapted LSTM-ER.  
- Sequence tagging benefits from encoding distances between related components rather than treating relations as a separate task.  
- Empirical contribution: demonstrates neural approaches can match or exceed strong ILP baselines on the Persuasive Essays dataset.  
- Comparative contribution: thorough exploration of multiple neural framings provides guidance for future AM work.  
- Strength: strong empirical results with both paragraph- and essay-level evaluations.  
- Strength: careful analysis explaining why parsing fails and tagging/multi-tasking succeed.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation and helpful introductory example.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych 2016), raising generalizability concerns.  
- Weakness: insufficient ablation studies on multi-task learning choices and layer configurations.  
- Weakness: LSTM-ER adaptation lacks detail and deeper analysis of its poorer performance on long texts.  
- Weakness: distance encoding is simplistic; more sophisticated relational modeling is not explored.  
- Question: will models be evaluated on other AM datasets and how will differing annotation schemes/domains be handled?  
- Question: what specific issues arose adapting LSTM-ER and how can the relation explosion at essay level be mitigated?  
- Question: provide detailed ablation of auxiliary tasks in the MTL setup and rationale for task selection.  
- Question: considered alternatives to distance encoding, such as learned distance embeddings?  
- Additional: discuss recent graph neural network approaches for relation extraction as relevant work.  
- Additional: include qualitative error analysis to illustrate model strengths and weaknesses.  
- Overall: strong paper with convincing results and analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
