## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:** This paper investigates several neural network architectures for end-to-end computational argument mining (AM), moving away from traditional pipeline approaches reliant on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors explore framing AM as dependency parsing, sequence tagging (with distance encoding), multi-task learning, and leveraging a pre-existing entity-relation model (LSTM-ER). They evaluate these approaches on the PE dataset, demonstrating that several outperform the state-of-the-art ILP baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of viable neural end-to-end AM:**  The paper successfully demonstrates that neural models can achieve competitive performance on the challenging task of end-to-end AM without relying on hand-engineered features. This is a significant step toward more adaptable and scalable AM systems.
2. **Comprehensive comparative analysis of different neural framings:** The work provides a valuable comparative analysis of several neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) for AM, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses. This offers useful insights for future research.
3. **Insightful analysis of the limitations of dependency parsing for AM:** The paper convincingly shows that framing AM as a dependency parsing problem is ineffective, challenging prior assumptions in the field.  This negative result is as valuable as positive findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to the state-of-the-art ILP model from Stab & Gurevych (2016) provides a solid benchmark and demonstrates the potential of the proposed neural approaches.  The authors fairly acknowledge the benefits of the ILP approach (access to broader contextual information) while still showcasing the gains achieved by the neural methods.
2. **Thorough Exploration of Architectures:** The authors explore a diverse set of neural architectures, demonstrating a commendable breadth of investigation. This avoids a narrow focus on a single approach and provides a more nuanced understanding of the problem.
3. **Clear Problem Framing & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional AM approaches (feature engineering, error propagation) and motivates the need for end-to-end neural solutions.
4. **Thoughtful Error Analysis:** The discussion regarding the performance differences between paragraph-level and essay-level evaluation, and the insights regarding long-range dependencies, demonstrate a deep understanding of the data and the models. The analysis of LSTM-ER's shortcomings on longer texts due to combinatorial explosion of possible relations is particularly insightful.
5. **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of details about hyperparameter optimization and software availability adds to the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset & Generalizability:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (PE essays). While this dataset is well-annotated, it’s crucial to assess how well the proposed models generalize to other types of argumentative text (e.g., debates, legal documents, scientific articles).  The dataset’s specific structure (argumentation largely within paragraphs) may influence the results.
2. **Tagging Complexity & Distance Encoding:** The proposed sequence tagging scheme (STagT) with distance encoding is complex. The paper briefly mentions the potential for an infinite label space, and while they argue it's finite in practice, it raises concerns about data sparsity and model generalization.  A more detailed analysis of the label distribution and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies for MTL:** While the multi-task learning experiments show some improvement, the analysis lacks detailed ablation studies. It would be helpful to understand the relative contribution of each auxiliary task and the optimal layer at which to incorporate them. 
4. **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't provide a comparative analysis of the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the different approaches. This is an important consideration for practical applications.
5. **Limited Exploration of Advanced Neural Architectures:** While the explored architectures are reasonable starting points, the paper could benefit from exploring more recent advances in neural sequence modeling, such as Transformers or graph neural networks, which may be better suited for capturing complex relationships in argumentative text.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the label distribution in the STagT scheme, including the frequency of different distance values and relation types? How does this distribution vary across different sections of the essay?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size, LSTM hidden size, learning rate)?  Did you perform a comprehensive hyperparameter search, and if so, could you share more details?
3.  Could you discuss the potential benefits of using more advanced neural architectures (e.g., Transformers, graph neural networks) for this task? Are there any specific challenges that would need to be addressed when applying these architectures to AM?
4.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the different approaches compared to the ILP baseline?
5.  Beyond the PE dataset, what types of argumentative texts do you envision these models being most applicable to, and what modifications might be necessary to adapt them to different domains?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise. However, some sections (e.g., the description of dependency parsing) could benefit from more intuitive explanations for readers unfamiliar with the field.
*  Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of each approach (e.g., modularity, constraints, computational complexity).



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them, and I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance. The empirical results are compelling, the analysis is insightful, and the work opens up new avenues for research in this important area. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
