This paper presents a comprehensive exploration of neural network approaches to end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors frame the problem in four different ways – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER) – and compare their performance against a strong ILP baseline. 

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical investigation of the applicability of various neural architectures to the complex task of end-to-end AM. The paper demonstrates that neural models can achieve competitive performance with traditional, feature-engineered approaches, while simultaneously avoiding the manual effort required for feature design. A second significant contribution is the insightful analysis revealing that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, while sequence tagging with distance encoding proves more robust. Finally, the demonstration of the benefits of a multi-task learning setup adds to the practical guidance for tackling this problem.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper systematically explores and evaluates multiple neural network approaches for end-to-end AM. This breadth of experimentation provides a valuable resource for researchers in the field, offering insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling choices. The inclusion of a strong ILP baseline allows for a fair comparison.
2. **Insightful Analysis of Dependency Parsing:** The clear finding that token-level dependency parsing is ineffective for AM is a strong result. It challenges a previously proposed direction and provides justification for focusing on alternative approaches. The authors’ explanation – that the complexity of parsing long sequences outweighs its benefits – is plausible and well-articulated.
3. **Effective Sequence Tagging with Distance Encoding:** The success of the sequence tagging approach, particularly with the incorporation of distance information between related components, is a key strength. This highlights the importance of capturing relational information within the tagging framework and provides a practical solution.
4. **Practical Recommendations:** The study provides concrete guidance for future research, suggesting that multi-task learning and careful consideration of model complexity (e.g., favoring simpler taggers over complex parsers for longer sequences) are important factors for success.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). While this dataset is high-quality, the results may not generalize to other types of argumentative texts (e.g., debates, online forums). Evaluating the models on a more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions some error analysis, it is not detailed enough to fully understand the limitations of the models. A more in-depth examination of specific error cases could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, analyzing common errors in relation classification could reveal challenges in capturing nuanced argumentative relationships.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on MTL:** The multi-task learning results show some improvement, but the ablation study lacks granularity. It would be helpful to see the performance impact of adding each auxiliary task individually and in different combinations, to better understand their relative contributions and potential interactions.
4. **LSTM-ER's Performance Discrepancy:** The significant drop in LSTM-ER’s performance on relation identification when moving from paragraph to essay level is puzzling. The authors offer a plausible explanation, but a more detailed investigation into the specific types of errors made by LSTM-ER at the essay level could be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the types of errors made by the dependency parsing models? Were there specific types of relationships that were consistently misidentified?
2. For the multi-task learning experiments, could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the performance improvement achieved by adding each auxiliary task (component detection and relation identification) individually and in combination?
3.  Regarding LSTM-ER’s performance drop on the essay level, could you provide examples of misclassified relations, and analyze why they occurred? Are these errors related to the increased complexity of identifying relations across longer distances or other factors?
4.  The paper briefly mentions the limitations of the evaluation metric. Are there alternative evaluation metrics that might better capture the nuances of argumentation mining, such as considering the validity or strength of arguments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 2 is a bit difficult to parse. Consider a clearer layout to improve readability.
*   The reference list is extensive.
*   The description of the label set Y for sequence tagging is clear and helpful.



Overall, this is a well-written and informative paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The thorough empirical evaluation and insightful analysis provide a solid foundation for future research. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its shortcomings, and I recommend acceptance.
