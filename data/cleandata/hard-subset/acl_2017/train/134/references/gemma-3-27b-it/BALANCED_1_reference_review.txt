This paper investigates neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), a task traditionally tackled with pipeline approaches relying on hand-crafted features. The authors explore framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, a sequence tagging problem, a multi-task learning problem, and utilizing a pre-existing model (Miwa & Bansal, 2016) designed for entity and relation extraction. The primary contributions, as I see them, are (1) a comprehensive empirical evaluation of several neural network framings for end-to-end AM, demonstrating their viability and competitive performance against a strong ILP baseline; (2) the identification of sequence tagging, particularly when incorporating distance information, as a robust and effective approach to AM; and (3) a detailed analysis highlighting the limitations of dependency parsing for this task and the benefits of multi-task learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation of four different neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. This breadth of experimentation is a significant strength, allowing for a direct comparison of different modeling approaches and providing valuable insights into their respective strengths and weaknesses. The inclusion of a strong ILP baseline provides a solid benchmark for comparison.
2. **Practical Insights on Framing Choices:** The authors’ analysis convincingly demonstrates the ineffectiveness of framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, a finding that contradicts prior suggestions.  Their detailed explanation of why this framing fails – related to the difficulty of handling long sequences and the complexity of global tree structures – is particularly insightful. Conversely, the robustness of the sequence tagging approach, especially with distance encoding, is a valuable practical contribution.
3. **Demonstrated Competitive Performance:** The sequence tagging models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER) achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to the ILP baseline. This result is significant as it demonstrates the potential of neural networks to effectively learn AM without the need for extensive feature engineering or constraint design. The improvement observed through multi-task learning further strengthens this conclusion.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Error Analysis:**  The paper provides a nuanced discussion of the challenges in relation extraction, particularly as sequence length increases, and offers a plausible explanation for the performance degradation of LSTM-ER on longer texts. This level of detailed analysis elevates the work beyond a simple presentation of results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Optimization:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, the details are relegated to the supplementary material. A more thorough discussion of the hyperparameter search process, particularly for the different models, would increase confidence in the reported results.  It's unclear to what extent performance differences are due to architectural choices versus hyperparameter tuning.
2. **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are conducted solely on the Stab & Gurevych (2016) dataset. While this dataset is a valuable resource, the results may not generalize to other AM datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different argumentation schemes, domains, or annotation styles). Assessing the generalizability of the findings is crucial.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  While the multi-task learning experiments demonstrate performance gains, there's a lack of detailed ablation studies to dissect the contribution of each auxiliary task. A more granular analysis, for example, removing specific auxiliary tasks or varying their weighting, would provide a clearer understanding of their impact.
4. **Potential for Overlap with Concurrent Work:** The description of LSTM-ER's application to AM and the discussion of its performance compared to STagBLCC feels somewhat limited given that a separate paper (Potash et al., 2017, cited in the conclusion) appears to explore similar ground. A more detailed comparison and justification for the choices made in this work relative to the other is needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter optimization process for each model? Specifically, what search space was explored, and what optimization algorithm was used?
2. Do you have any preliminary results or plans to evaluate the proposed models on other AM datasets? If so, could you briefly describe them?
3. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study of the multi-task learning experiments, analyzing the contribution of each auxiliary task to the overall performance?
4. Could you clarify how this work differs from or builds upon the work presented in Potash et al. (2017)?  What specific aspects of AM are investigated here that are not addressed in the other work?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the field.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the different models.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The authors have conducted a thorough empirical evaluation of several neural network framings and provided valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. While some aspects could be strengthened with further analysis and experimentation, the paper's findings are significant and offer a promising direction for future research. I recommend acceptance.
