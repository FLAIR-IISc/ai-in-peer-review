This paper presents a neural end-to-end approach to computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). The authors claim (1) the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, (2) performance superior to a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) ineffectiveness of token-based dependency parsing, (4) robustness of sequence tagging with distance encoding, and (5) performance improvement via multi-task learning.

The primary contribution is the comprehensive empirical investigation of multiple neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, coupled with a robust evaluation on a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). The systematic exploration of different framing approaches (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER) provides valuable insights into the suitability of each for this complex task. The clear presentation of results and comparison with a strong baseline (ILP model) solidifies this contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the dataset usage, providing a significant benchmark for future work in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper thoroughly investigates multiple neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, offering a nuanced comparison of their strengths and weaknesses. This contrasts sharply with approaches focusing on a single architecture. The use of a consistent dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and a strong baseline (ILP model) provides a reliable basis for comparison.
2. **Clear Identification of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and clearly demonstrate the limitations of the dependency parsing approach, challenging prior assumptions. This self-critical assessment strengthens the validity of the research and prevents over-generalization of the findings.
3. **Demonstrated Effectiveness of Sequence Tagging:** The authors showcase the robustness of the sequence tagging approach, particularly when incorporating distance information, which is crucial for identifying relationships between argumentative components. This framing proves to be a strong performer across different experimental settings.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Multi-Task Learning:** The exploration of multi-task learning demonstrates the potential benefits of leveraging auxiliary tasks (component detection and relation identification) to improve overall AM performance. The results offer guidance on optimal task combinations and architecture design.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (BiLSTM-CRF, dependency parsing, multi-task learning) are not novel in themselves. The paper’s novelty resides in applying these techniques specifically to the AM problem in an end-to-end fashion, and in the thorough comparative evaluation.
2. **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art:** Although improvements over the ILP baseline are reported, the absolute F1 scores remain relatively modest. This suggests there is still significant room for improvement in end-to-end AM, and highlights the challenges inherent in the task.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors made by each model could offer valuable insights into their limitations and guide future research directions. While some discussion exists in the experiments section, a more systematic analysis would be helpful.
4. **Potential for Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks a detailed description of the search process. It’s unclear to what extent the reported results are sensitive to specific hyperparameter choices, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by each model (e.g., component misclassification, incorrect relation identification, span boundary errors)?
2.  Regarding the hyperparameter optimization process, what specific search strategies were employed, and what was the range of hyperparameters explored for each model?
3.  The LSTM-ER model shows strong performance on paragraph level but degrades significantly on essay level. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for this degradation beyond the increased sequence length, and discuss possible mitigation strategies?
4.  The authors observe that the C task is more helpful than the R task in the MTL setting. Can you hypothesize *why* this might be the case, and explore whether adjusting the weighting of the auxiliary tasks could further improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The writing style is clear and concise.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost associated with each approach.
* The conclusion could benefit from a more forward-looking discussion of future research directions, building on the insights gained from this study.
