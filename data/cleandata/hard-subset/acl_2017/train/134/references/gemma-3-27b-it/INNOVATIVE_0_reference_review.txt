This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging an existing LSTM-ER model. The authors identify limitations of prior ILP-based approaches reliant on hand-crafted features and aim to address them with neural methods.

The primary contribution is the exploration of multiple neural network architectures for end-to-end AM, demonstrating that they can achieve competitive or superior performance to a strong baseline ILP model. A secondary contribution is the surprising finding that framing AM as a token-based dependency parsing problem is ineffective, contrasting with successful component-level parsing approaches. Finally, a robust performance is demonstrated with a sequence tagging model incorporating distance information between argument components.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The exploration of end-to-end neural AM is a significant step. While individual components (sequence tagging, dependency parsing) are well-studied, applying them jointly and comprehensively to the full AM pipeline represents a genuinely novel direction. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous, feature-engineered approaches.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper evaluates *four* distinct neural framings, providing a valuable comparative analysis. This breadth of experimentation is commendable and offers insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. **Counterintuitive Finding:** The negative result regarding token-level dependency parsing is particularly interesting. Challenging existing assumptions and providing evidence against them is a hallmark of strong research. This finding warrants further investigation, and the authors correctly speculate on possible reasons.
4. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Results:**  Comparing against a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline demonstrates the viability of the proposed neural approaches. Achieving comparable or better performance, especially given the lack of manual feature engineering, is a positive outcome.
5. **Practical Insights:** The finding that sequence tagging with distance encoding performs robustly offers a practical approach for AM tasks. The suggestion that auxiliary tasks in a multi-task setting can boost performance is a valuable observation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While results are presented, there is a lack of in-depth error analysis. Understanding *where* the models fail (e.g., specific relation types, long-range dependencies) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.  The mention of Figure 2 hinting at long-range dependencies but then limited discussion is a missed opportunity.
2. **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). While this dataset is well-regarded, the results may not generalize to other domains or types of argumentative text. The authors acknowledge the data peculiarity, but the limited scope remains a concern.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks sufficient detail. Knowing the search space and specific settings used would enhance reproducibility and allow for better assessment of the results.
4. **LSTM-ER Performance Discrepancy:** The significant drop in LSTM-ER performance on essay level relation identification warrants more discussion. The authors’ explanation regarding the increased number of possible relations is plausible, but a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by LSTM-ER would be beneficial.  Why is the *component* detection still strong at the essay level?
5. **Framing Justification:** While the authors *attempt* to justify the framings, some choices remain somewhat arbitrary. For example, the specific BIO tagging scheme with distance and stance encoding feels ad-hoc and could benefit from a more principled justification.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, particularly focusing on the types of errors made by each model (e.g., misidentified relation types, failures in long-range dependency capture)?
2. What were the specific hyperparameter settings used for each model and how were these settings determined (e.g., grid search, random search)?
3. Can you elaborate on the specific architectural choices within LSTM-ER that might contribute to its performance drop on long-form text, specifically related to relation identification?
4.  What considerations led to the choice of including distance information in the sequence tagging scheme? Did you experiment with other ways to encode relational information?
5.  Given the negative result for token-level dependency parsing, do you foresee any modifications to this approach (e.g., different parsing models, different feature representations) that might yield better results?
