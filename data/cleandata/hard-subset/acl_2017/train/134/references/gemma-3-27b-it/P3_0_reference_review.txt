## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:** This paper investigates several neural network approaches to tackle the end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM) task, encompassing component segmentation, typing, and relation identification. The authors explore framing AM as a dependency parsing problem, sequence tagging problem, multi-task learning problem, and by leveraging a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). They demonstrate that these neural approaches outperform a feature-based ILP baseline on a persuasive essay dataset.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation of Neural AM Approaches:** The paper provides a valuable and extensive empirical comparison of various neural network architectures for end-to-end AM. This allows for a clearer understanding of which approaches are more promising and what their limitations are. 
2. **Demonstration of Feasibility of Neural End-to-End AM:**  The paper successfully demonstrates that neural models *can* achieve competitive performance on the complex task of end-to-end AM, reducing reliance on manual feature engineering and ILP constraint design.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Framing Choices:** The authors offer a clear discussion of the trade-offs between different framings (dependency parsing vs. sequence tagging) and highlight the importance of considering task complexity and data characteristics when selecting a modeling approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a well-established dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and rigorously compare multiple neural architectures against a strong baseline. The inclusion of both paragraph-level and essay-level evaluations is insightful.
2. **Clear Problem Formulation & Motivation:** The paper articulates the challenges of traditional AM approaches (feature engineering, error propagation) and convincingly motivates the exploration of end-to-end neural solutions.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Results:**  The authors go beyond simply reporting numbers and provide valuable analysis of *why* certain methods perform better or worse.  Their discussion of the limitations of dependency parsing and the benefits of sequence tagging, especially with distance encoding, is well-reasoned.
4. **Practical Insights & Recommendations:** The conclusion offers actionable recommendations regarding model selection based on data characteristics (short vs. long documents), making the work directly relevant to practitioners.
5. **Exploration of Multi-Task Learning:** The investigation of MTL, and the discovery that component and relation detection as auxiliary tasks improve performance, is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Models:** While the *combination* of these models for AM is a valuable contribution, most of the individual neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, TreeLSTMs) are not novel in themselves. The primary novelty lies in applying and evaluating them specifically within the AM context and demonstrating their effectiveness compared to the ILP baseline. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Dependence on Distance Encoding:** The success of the sequence tagging approach heavily relies on the effective encoding of distances between related components.  While this is a clever approach, the authors could benefit from a deeper analysis of *why* distance encoding is so crucial and how it interacts with other features. Exploring alternative methods for capturing relational information could strengthen the work.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  While the paper investigates different frameworks, there's a lack of systematic ablation studies *within* each framework. For example, for the sequence tagging models, how much does the BiLSTM contribute versus the CRF layer? Understanding the relative importance of different components within a single model would provide further insights.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While some error analysis is mentioned, a more detailed qualitative examination of errors made by different models would be beneficial.  Identifying common error patterns could suggest avenues for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the LSTM-Parser’s deterioration from paragraph to essay level is likely due to the longer sequences. Did you experiment with techniques to handle long sequences, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical models, within the dependency parsing framework? If so, what were the results?
2.  The paper highlights the importance of distance encoding in the sequence tagging approach. Did you explore different ways of encoding distance (e.g., using embeddings instead of discrete values) and did any of these alternatives perform better?
3.  Could you provide more details about the specific hyperparameter tuning process? Which hyperparameters were found to be most critical for performance, and how did you select their optimal values?
4.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, you found that including component detection as an auxiliary task was beneficial. Did you investigate the impact of weighting the loss functions for the different tasks? Could a dynamic weighting scheme improve performance further?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advancements in neural relation extraction.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of each model (e.g., local vs. global, feature engineering required, computational complexity).
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections could benefit from further elaboration and contextualization.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the individual models employed are not entirely novel, the comprehensive evaluation, insightful analysis, and practical recommendations make this work a worthwhile read. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
