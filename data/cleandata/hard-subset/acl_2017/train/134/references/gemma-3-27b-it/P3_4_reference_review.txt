## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores several neural network approaches for end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM), tackling component segmentation, typing, and relation identification simultaneously. The authors investigate framing AM as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). They demonstrate that neural methods can outperform a strong, feature-based ILP baseline and identify key strengths and weaknesses of different neural framings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM's Viability:**  The paper provides a compelling empirical demonstration that neural models *can* achieve competitive performance on the challenging task of end-to-end AM, without relying on handcrafted features. This is a crucial step toward scalable and adaptable AM systems.
2. **Comprehensive Comparative Study:** The thorough exploration of different neural framing approaches – dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER – offers valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of each for this specific task. The comparison isn't just about overall scores but also nuanced analysis regarding document length and relation types.
3. **Insights into Data Characteristics & Modeling Choices:** The authors highlight the impact of long-range dependencies and the importance of explicitly modeling relationships (via distance encoding or dedicated relation tasks) in AM, tying it back to the characteristics of the PE dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Results:** The comparison against the state-of-the-art ILP system provides a solid benchmark. The fact that several neural models match or exceed the ILP’s performance is significant, considering the feature engineering effort in the latter.
2. **Well-Justified Experimental Setup:** The choice of the Stab & Gurevych dataset is appropriate given its rich annotation scheme. The thorough experimentation with different model architectures (BiLSTM, CRF, CNN) within each framing is commendable. The division into paragraph and essay-level evaluation reveals important insights.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis & Discussion:**  The paper doesn’t simply present numbers; it dives into *why* certain approaches succeed or fail.  The discussion around dependency parsing’s struggle with long sequences and the LSTM-ER's difficulties with many entities is particularly insightful. The analysis of distance values in relations is a valuable contribution.
4. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the methodology and results.  The motivations for each framing are clearly explained.
5. **Multi-task learning insights:** The experiments with multi-task learning show the benefits of auxiliary tasks like component and relation detection, highlighting the importance of task de-coupling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Advanced Neural Architectures:** While the paper covers a range of standard neural models, it doesn't explore more recent, potentially relevant architectures like Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). Given the long-range dependency issues highlighted, a Transformer-based model could offer significant advantages.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The multi-task learning experiments are a good start, but more detailed ablation studies would strengthen the analysis. For example, systematically varying the number and type of auxiliary tasks, or investigating different weightings between tasks, would provide a deeper understanding of their impact.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The PE dataset, while valuable, is limited in domain and writing style (student essays).  The generalization ability of the models to other text types (e.g., news articles, legal documents) remains unknown. The authors acknowledge this, but it's a key limitation.
4. **Dependency Parsing Framings’ Performance:**  The underperformance of the dependency parsing approaches, even with LSTM-parsers, feels somewhat underexplored in the discussion. While the authors correctly identify the length issue, a deeper dive into *where* the parsing fails (e.g., specific relation types) would be helpful.  The rapid dismissal of feature-based parsers feels a bit strong given their established performance on other NLP tasks.
5. **Implementation Details:** While the authors mention a link to code, more details about hyperparameters and training procedures would improve reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with Transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for any of the proposed framings? If not, could you speculate on their potential benefits and challenges for this task?
2.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments: What criteria were used to select the auxiliary tasks? Did you try incorporating other potential auxiliary tasks (e.g., POS tagging, named entity recognition)?
3.  Could you provide more detailed error analysis for the dependency parsing experiments? Specifically, which relation types were most frequently mispredicted, and what types of errors were observed (e.g., incorrect head selection, incorrect relation label)?
4.  Given the dataset’s limitation to student essays, have you considered any data augmentation or transfer learning techniques to improve generalization to other domains?
5.  What was the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the different models?  This is particularly important for the LSTM-ER model, which appears to be the most computationally intensive.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-maintained.
*   The inclusion of Figure 2 illustrating the distance distribution of relations is particularly helpful.
*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of ethical considerations related to argumentation mining (e.g., potential for misuse in propaganda detection or manipulation).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper provides a solid empirical foundation for further research and demonstrates the potential of neural networks for tackling this complex task. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
