## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:**

This paper explores several neural network architectures for tackling the end-to-end Computational Argumentation Mining (AM) problem, moving away from traditional pipeline approaches reliant on hand-crafted features and Integer Linear Programming (ILP). The authors investigate framing AM as a dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). They demonstrate that several of their neural approaches outperform a strong ILP baseline on a standard dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM’s Viability:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that neural network architectures can effectively address the end-to-end AM task, achieving competitive or superior performance to a well-established feature-based ILP system. This is a significant step towards automating AM without extensive feature engineering.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Framing Choices:** The paper systematically explores several different neural framing options for AM, offering valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.  This comparative analysis is helpful for researchers entering the field.
3. **Insights into the Importance of Sequence Tagging & Multi-Task Learning:** The authors demonstrate the robustness of a sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) and show that incorporating auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setting can improve performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results consistently show neural models outperforming the ILP baseline, particularly on the paragraph level. The gains in F1 scores, while not massive, are statistically significant and demonstrate a clear advantage.
2. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore multiple neural architectures and variations (e.g., different tagging models, MTL configurations), providing a comprehensive evaluation of different approaches.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional AM methods (feature engineering, error propagation) and motivates the need for end-to-end neural solutions.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis (to a degree):**  The discussion regarding the limitations of dependency parsing and the impact of long sequences on LSTM-ER provides valuable insights into the challenges of applying these models to AM.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of neural techniques for end-to-end AM is a contribution, the individual components (BiLSTMs, CRFs, dependency parsing) are well-established. The novelty lies in the application to this specific task, rather than in architectural innovation.
2. **Performance Gap on Essay Level:** The significant performance drop on the essay level, particularly for the dependency parsing approach, raises concerns about the scalability and generalizability of these models to longer, more complex texts. The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth analysis of why this occurs is needed.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  While the paper compares different models, it lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific components within those models (e.g., the effect of the CNN in BiLSTM-CRF-CNN, the impact of different distance encoding schemes in sequence tagging). This makes it difficult to understand *why* certain models perform better than others.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Types:** While some error analysis is provided, a more detailed examination of the types of errors made by each model would be beneficial.  For example, are there specific types of relations that are consistently misclassified? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that pose challenges?
5. **Distance Encoding Concerns:** The rationale for using a fixed distance encoding seems potentially limiting, especially given the wide range of observed distances. It would be useful to explore more sophisticated distance encoding schemes.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors made by each model (e.g., misclassified relations, incorrect component spans)?
2.  Regarding the significant performance drop on the essay level, could you provide more insights into the specific challenges encountered when processing longer texts? Is it primarily related to the difficulty of capturing long-range dependencies, or are there other factors at play?
3.   You mention that the dependency parsing framing struggles with longer sequences.  Have you experimented with techniques to address this, such as hierarchical dependency parsing or models designed for long-range dependencies?
4.   What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the different models presented? This is an important consideration for practical applications.
5.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the hyperparameters related to the distance encoding scheme in the sequence tagging models?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "Potash et al., 2017" as an "arxiv preprint" is acceptable, but providing a link to the preprint would be helpful.
*   The discussion of multi-task learning could be strengthened by comparing the performance of different auxiliary tasks and exploring the optimal layer configuration for knowledge sharing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. The empirical results are encouraging, and the systematic exploration of different neural framing options provides useful insights. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the application to end-to-end AM and the demonstrated performance gains are significant.  I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding error analysis and scalability.
