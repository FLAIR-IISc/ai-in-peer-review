- Comprehensive empirical comparison of neural architectures (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, MTL, LSTM-ER) for end-to-end argumentation mining.
- Shows neural models can match or outperform a strong ILP feature-based baseline, demonstrating viability of end-to-end neural AM.
- Provides insights into framing choices, especially trade-offs between dependency parsing and sequence tagging for long-range dependencies.
- Thorough experimental setup with multiple architectures and paragraph- and essay-level evaluations.
- Clear problem motivation highlighting limitations of pipeline, feature-engineered AM and benefits of end-to-end learning.
- Strong baseline comparison using the state-of-the-art ILP model for meaningful benchmarking.
- Detailed analysis explaining why certain models perform better, linking results to data characteristics and architectural properties.
- Practical recommendation that sequence tagging models are robust and benefit from auxiliary tasks (multi-task learning).
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016), raising concerns about generalizability to other argumentative domains.
- LSTM-ER adaptation lacks sufficient implementation detail (entity/relation mapping) for reproducibility and understanding.
- LSTM-ER and other models still struggle with long-range dependency modeling; gap not fully resolved.
- Missing ablation studies (e.g., removal of distance encoding) to isolate contributions of specific components.
- Request for more details on how entity and relation types were mapped into LSTM-ER for the AM task.
- Request for clarification on error types caused by BL tagger independence assumptions versus BLCC model errors.
- Suggestion to explore transfer learning or domain adaptation to improve generalizability across argumentative domains.
- Inquiry about specific model modifications to better capture long-range dependencies in LSTM-ER and sequence tagging.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, with a comprehensive related work section and properly formatted references.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with insightful empirical evaluation and recommendations; recommend acceptance despite dataset and detail limitations.
