This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging an existing LSTM-ER model. The authors demonstrate that several of their neural approaches outperform a feature-based ILP baseline.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of neural end-to-end AM – a relatively under-explored area, particularly concerning direct application of standard neural architectures without extensive feature engineering. The authors present a comprehensive empirical study, investigating multiple neural framings for a complex NLP task. While individual results aren't groundbreaking, the breadth of experimentation and the clear demonstration that neural methods *can* compete with, and in some cases surpass, traditional ILP approaches is valuable. A secondary contribution is the insightful analysis of the challenges and limitations of each framing – notably, the ineffectiveness of dependency parsing when applied at the token level for end-to-end AM, and the impact of sequence length on LSTM-ER's performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** While individual components (LSTMs, CRFs, dependency parsing) aren’t novel, applying them in a genuinely end-to-end fashion to AM, without heavy reliance on hand-crafted features, represents a significant step. The exploration of multiple framing choices (parsing, tagging, MTL) is a strength, allowing for a nuanced understanding of what works and what doesn't.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation against a strong baseline (the ILP model). The analysis on paragraph vs. essay level performance is particularly insightful, revealing important practical considerations.
3. **Clear Identification of Limitations:** The authors are remarkably candid about the limitations of each approach. The analysis of why dependency parsing fails at the token level and the challenges LSTM-ER faces with longer sequences demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying issues. This honesty is refreshing and helps position the work within the broader research landscape.
4. **Insightful Analysis of MTL:** The exploration of multi-task learning, and the observation that component and relation detection can serve as useful auxiliary tasks, is a valuable contribution. The discussion of the impact of layer selection in the MTL framework is also insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Deep Dive into Error Analysis:** While the authors mention error analysis, it’s not presented in sufficient detail. Understanding *why* the models fail (beyond the general observations about sequence length and parsing) would be extremely valuable.  Specific examples of misclassifications and their causes are needed.
2. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison is primarily to the Stab & Gurevych (2016) ILP baseline. A comparison to more recent AM systems, even if they are still feature-based, would provide a better context for the reported results.  Are these improvements statistically significant against other published results?
3. **Suboptimal Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, but the details are relegated to the supplementary material.  A more detailed discussion of the search space and the rationale for the chosen hyperparameters would strengthen the work. Did the authors explore a diverse range of architectures beyond those presented?
4. **Overly Complex Tagging Scheme:** The proposed sequence tagging scheme (STagT) with its combined BIO, component type, distance, and stance information is incredibly complex. While the authors justify this complexity, it’s unclear whether it’s truly necessary, or if a simpler scheme would yield comparable results. The increased label space may be hindering performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more detailed examples of error cases for each model, specifically highlighting common types of misclassifications and their potential causes?
2. Have you experimented with simpler tagging schemes (e.g., separating component identification and relation classification into distinct tagging layers)? If so, how did performance compare to the current STagT scheme?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter search process and the rationale for the chosen values?
4.  How do the results of your best performing model (e.g., LSTM-ER on paragraph level) compare to any recently published state-of-the-art systems in AM (even those utilizing feature engineering)?
