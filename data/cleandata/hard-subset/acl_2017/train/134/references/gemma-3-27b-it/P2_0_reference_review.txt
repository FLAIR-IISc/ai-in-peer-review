## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable exploration of applying neural network architectures to the challenging problem of end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The work is well-motivated, investigates multiple approaches, and provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation. While the dependency parsing approach disappoints, the sequence tagging and multi-task learning framings show promise and establish strong baselines. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Summary & Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation of Neural AM Approaches:** The primary contribution is a thorough experimental comparison of several neural network framings (dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, LSTM-ER) for end-to-end AM on a standard dataset. This provides a valuable benchmark for future research.
2. **Demonstration of Effective Sequence Tagging for AM:**  The authors convincingly demonstrate that a neural sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) is a strong performer for AM, achieving competitive results and highlighting the benefits of capturing long-range dependencies.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Framing Choices:** The paper provides a thoughtful discussion of the trade-offs between different modeling approaches, particularly concerning modularity and constraints, offering valuable guidance for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Setup & Baseline:** The authors use a well-established dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016) and compare their approaches against a strong feature-based ILP baseline.  The detailed reporting of results (component and relation F1 scores, 100% and 50% matching) enables a nuanced assessment of performance.
2. **Thorough Exploration of Neural Architectures:** The investigation of four different neural framings demonstrates a commendable breadth of exploration.  The analysis of *why* certain approaches (e.g., dependency parsing) fail is particularly insightful.
3. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for the work is effectively conveyed, and the experimental setup is easy to follow. The inclusion of an example tagging scheme further enhances clarity.
4. **Valuable Insights on Sequence Tagging & MTL:** The results regarding sequence tagging and multi-task learning are particularly strong. The finding that incorporating auxiliary tasks (component detection) improves performance is a significant contribution. The analysis regarding the importance of distance encoding in sequence tagging is also noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination and evaluation of these techniques within the AM context is valuable, the individual neural architectures themselves (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are not novel. The primary contribution lies in applying and evaluating them for this specific task.
2. **Dependency Parsing Results are Disappointing, but Analysis Could Be Deeper:**  While the authors correctly identify that dependency parsing performs poorly, the explanation focuses primarily on sequence length. Exploring the mismatch between the tree structure enforced by the parser and the inherent complexity/non-tree-like nature of argumentation structures could be further elaborated.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The adopted evaluation metric (Persing & Ng, 2016) relies on matching spans of components. While reasonable, this metric might not fully capture the nuances of argument structure. Exploring alternative evaluation methods that consider the *relationships* between components could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4. **LSTM-ER Scaling Issue:** The dramatic performance drop of LSTM-ER on longer essays suggests a scaling issue that warrants further investigation. The authors briefly mention the increased number of potential relations, but a more detailed analysis of this bottleneck would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the adopted evaluation metric and whether you considered alternative evaluation approaches that focus on the structural correctness of the argument graph rather than just component matching?
2.  Regarding the dependency parsing results, could you delve deeper into the specific types of errors the parser makes (e.g., incorrect head selection, incorrect relation labeling) and how these errors relate to the inherent challenges of modeling argumentation structure as a tree?
3.  For LSTM-ER, could you provide more details on the computational resources required and the potential strategies for mitigating the scaling issue with longer documents (e.g., approximate inference, relation filtering)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The inclusion of a table summarizing the label sets (Y, YC, YR) would be helpful for quick reference.
*   It would be interesting to see a qualitative error analysis, highlighting examples of cases where the models succeed and fail.
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of the PE dataset and potential biases it may introduce.
