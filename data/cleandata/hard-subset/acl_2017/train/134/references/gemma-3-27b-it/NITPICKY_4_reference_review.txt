This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing the LSTM-ER model. The primary claimed contributions are: (1) presenting the first neural end-to-end solutions to AM, (2) demonstrating superior performance compared to a state-of-the-art ILP model, (3) showing the ineffectiveness of token-based dependency parsing for AM, (4) demonstrating robust performance of a sequence tagging model encoding distance information, and (5) showing improved performance via multi-task learning.

A key contribution, as I see it, is the comprehensive empirical evaluation of multiple neural network architectures applied to the end-to-end AM task, offering a valuable benchmark and comparative analysis. The systematic investigation of different framing approaches—dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning—provides useful insights into their strengths and weaknesses within this specific domain. The dataset used, while not novel, is a reasonable choice and allows for meaningful comparison.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Experimental Evaluation:** The breadth of the experimentation, covering diverse neural architectures and framing techniques, is commendable. The authors don’t simply propose a single model but thoroughly explore different possibilities.
2. **Clear Identification of Limitations of Dependency Parsing:** The finding that a token-based dependency parsing approach performs poorly for AM is significant and challenges previous suggestions. This negative result is as valuable as positive findings, as it directs future research away from potentially unproductive avenues.
3. **Demonstrated Effectiveness of Sequence Tagging:** The robust performance of the sequence tagging model, particularly with distance encoding, highlights a promising direction for AM research. This result provides a strong baseline for future work and suggests that relatively simpler models can achieve competitive results.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis (Implicit):** While not explicitly presented as a dedicated section, the discussion of the differences in performance between paragraph and essay-level prediction, and the insights into the LSTM-ER model's limitations, demonstrate a good understanding of the model's behavior and potential failure points. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is a contribution, many of the individual neural network architectures used (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are well-established in the NLP community and not novel in themselves. The paper’s novelty lies in *applying* these models to AM, but this application isn't substantially transformative.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks specifics. The sensitivity of model performance to hyperparameters is well-known, and a more detailed discussion of the tuning process and rationale behind choices would strengthen the results.  Was a validation set used? What search strategy was employed?
3. **Limited Discussion of Error Types:** The paper briefly touches upon error analysis but lacks a thorough qualitative examination of the types of errors made by each model. A deeper dive into specific examples of misclassifications could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, what types of relationships are consistently misidentified? Are there patterns in the errors that can be addressed through feature engineering or model architecture modifications?
4. **Overly Dense Writing & Formatting Inconsistencies:** The writing is often dense and difficult to follow, with long sentences and complex phrasing. The formatting suffers from several minor inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, spacing around equations). These details detract from the overall professionalism of the paper.
5. **Unclear Ablation Studies:** The multi-task learning experiments are presented, but there's a lack of clear ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task.  How much does each task *individually* improve performance? Is there any negative transfer from certain tasks?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the size of the validation set, the search strategy used, and the rationale behind the final hyperparameter choices?
2.  Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, showcasing specific examples of misclassifications made by each model and discussing potential reasons for these errors?
3.  Regarding the multi-task learning experiments, can you present ablation studies to quantify the individual contributions of each auxiliary task (C and R)?
4.  The paper references a supplementary material. Is this available, and does it contain a more detailed error analysis, hyperparameter details, and code for reproducibility?
5.  Why was the LSTM-ER model’s performance so significantly degraded when moving from paragraph to essay level? Is this a common phenomenon in other relation extraction tasks?



**Additional Comments:**

The formatting requires careful review and correction. Consider using a style checker to identify inconsistencies in spacing, italics, and other stylistic elements. The paper would benefit from more concise writing and clearer explanations of complex concepts. The references are presented but not formatted according to ACL guidelines.
