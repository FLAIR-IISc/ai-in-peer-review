## Review for "Neural End-to-End Learning for Computational Argumentation Mining"

**Summary:** This paper explores neural network approaches to the challenging problem of end-to-end computational argumentation mining (AM). The authors investigate four framings: dependency parsing, sequence tagging (with distance encoding), multi-task learning, and application of a pre-existing model (LSTM-ER). They demonstrate that these neural approaches can outperform a strong, feature-engineered ILP baseline, and provide insights into the effectiveness of different framings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural AM:** The paper provides a valuable empirical demonstration that neural networks *can* effectively tackle the complex, end-to-end AM task, achieving competitive performance with a strong baseline. This is a significant step toward moving away from heavily engineered feature sets.
2. **Comparative Analysis of Framing Choices:** The study offers a useful comparative analysis of different neural framing choices for AM, revealing that sequence tagging with distance encoding (STagBLCC) performs particularly well. The ablation studies in the multi-task setting are also informative.
3. **Dataset-Specific Insights:** The work highlights the challenges presented by the PE dataset and the impact of document length on the performance of different models (particularly the parsing approach).

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline and Clear Evaluation:** The comparison to the established ILP baseline provides a credible benchmark, and the use of standard evaluation metrics (F1 scores) facilitates clear comparison.
2. **Thorough Exploration of Approaches:** The authors systematically explore multiple neural network architectures and framing strategies, demonstrating a broad understanding of the relevant techniques.  The experiments are well-designed and cover a range of configurations.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion regarding the limitations of dependency parsing and the advantages of distance encoding in sequence tagging is well-reasoned and demonstrates a strong grasp of the underlying principles. The explanation for LSTM-ER’s performance drop on longer texts is particularly insightful.
4. **Practical Considerations & Dataset Analysis:** The detailed description of the PE dataset, including its unique structure and peculiarities, is valuable for future research.  The discussion about paragraph vs. essay level prediction highlights a practical challenge.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of these techniques for AM is valuable, many of the individual neural network architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are well-established and not novel in themselves. The primary contribution lies in applying and evaluating them within the specific AM context.  This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies for LSTM-ER:**  The LSTM-ER model is presented as a strong performer, but the ablation study is relatively limited. It would be beneficial to see a more systematic investigation of the impact of different components of the LSTM-ER architecture (e.g., removing the tree LSTM, altering entity representation) on performance.
3. **Distance Encoding Limitation:** While distance encoding is shown to be effective, the encoding scheme itself is relatively simple (linear distance). Exploring more sophisticated encoding methods (e.g., learned embeddings, relative position encoding) could potentially yield further improvements.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t provide much detail about the computational resources required to train and run the different models.  This information is increasingly important for reproducibility and practical adoption.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the distance encoding:** You mention that roughly 2/3 of all relation distances are within {-2, -1, 1}. Did you experiment with truncating or quantizing the distance values to further simplify the encoding scheme and potentially reduce the dimensionality of the output layer?
2. **Concerning LSTM-ER:**  What impact did pre-training the LSTM-ER model have on its performance? Were different pre-training strategies (e.g., on general text, on related AM tasks) explored?
3. **Multi-Task Learning:** You found that the 'C' task (component detection) was more helpful as an auxiliary task than the 'R' task (relation detection). Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Is it due to the inherent difficulty of relation detection, or is there something specific about the way the two tasks interact within the MTL framework?
4. **Generalizability:** Given that the PE dataset focuses on persuasive essays, how well do you anticipate these approaches will generalize to other text genres (e.g., news articles, legal documents)? Are there specific aspects of the PE dataset that might limit generalizability?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is clear and well-organized.
* The inclusion of a figure illustrating the argument structure is helpful.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.



**Overall Assessment:**  This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of computational argumentation mining. While the individual components are not necessarily groundbreaking, the systematic exploration of neural network approaches, the comparative analysis, and the insightful discussion of the results make this a worthwhile read.  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the weaknesses outlined above during the author response phase.
