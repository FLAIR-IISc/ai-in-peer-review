This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task in four different ways: dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging an existing model (LSTM-ER) designed for entity and relation extraction. The authors aim to move beyond traditional pipeline approaches relying on hand-crafted features and ILP constraints.

The primary contribution of this work is the *exploration of multiple neural architectures for tackling the complex, end-to-end AM problem*. While individual components (e.g., sequence tagging for component classification) have been explored neurally, this work presents a broad empirical evaluation of various framing choices for the *complete* task, offering valuable insights into their relative strengths and weaknesses. A secondary contribution is the identification of limitations in applying dependency parsing to this task, specifically highlighting issues with scaling to longer texts, despite its theoretical appeal. Finally, the demonstration that a simple sequence tagging approach with distance encoding can perform robustly is a noteworthy finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Exploration & Broad Empirical Evaluation:** The paper attempts a difficult and important problem—end-to-end AM—and explores several approaches rather than focusing on incremental improvements to existing methods. This breadth of experimentation is commendable and offers valuable practical guidance for future research.
2. **Clear Identification of Limitations:** The authors are forthright about the failures of the dependency parsing approach, a result that challenges a potentially intuitive framing. This honest assessment adds credibility to the work.
3. **Robust Performance of Sequence Tagging:** The demonstration that a relatively straightforward sequence tagging model, augmented with distance information, achieves competitive results is a significant finding. This suggests that sophisticated architectures aren’t always necessary, and that careful feature engineering within a simpler model can be effective.
4. **Useful Comparative Analysis:** The comparison against the existing ILP baseline is valuable, showing that neural approaches are competitive and can potentially surpass traditional methods. The analysis of performance across paragraph and essay levels also provides a nuanced understanding of the challenges involved.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Deep Architectural Innovation:** While the exploration of different *framings* is valuable, the *architectures* themselves (BiLSTM-CRF, LSTM-ER) are relatively standard. The paper doesn't introduce any fundamentally new neural building blocks or learning techniques. The contribution is more about *applying* existing techniques to a new problem, rather than *advancing* the state-of-the-art in neural network design.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper acknowledges performance variations, the error analysis is superficial. A more detailed investigation into *why* certain approaches fail in specific cases would provide more actionable insights. For example, delving into errors made by LSTM-ER concerning long-range dependencies would be useful, given the authors' observations.
3. **Dataset Specificity:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset (Stab & Gurevych, 2016). While this allows for a thorough evaluation within that context, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other argumentation datasets with different characteristics.
4. **MTL Results are Underdeveloped:** The multi-task learning experiments show promise, but the exploration of auxiliary tasks feels somewhat cursory. A more systematic investigation of different task combinations and layer-sharing strategies could yield more significant improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on cases where the dependency parsing approach fails and highlighting the types of errors that LSTM-ER makes in long-range dependency scenarios?
2. You mention the potential for an encoder-decoder approach. Can you elaborate on why you didn't pursue this framing in the current work, and what challenges you anticipate in applying it to AM?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly those related to the distance encoding in the sequence tagging model? Did you explore a wider range of distance encoding schemes?
4. Given the dataset's structure (arguments primarily within paragraphs), how much does pre-segmenting the text into paragraphs impact performance for different models, and are there benefits to processing longer spans even with the increased complexity?



**Additional Comments:** The phrasing regarding “novelty” in the introduction feels a little defensive (“first neural end-to-end solutions”). While technically true, framing it as a more fundamental shift in approach would be stronger. The supplementary material should include more details regarding the hyperparameter tuning process.
