- Paper evaluates neural end-to-end approaches for computational argumentation mining, comparing dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and LSTM-ER on Stab & Gurevych (2016).
- Empirically demonstrates neural models can match or outperform a strong ILP-based, feature-engineered baseline.
- Provides a systematic comparison of different neural framings for AM, offering practical insights for researchers.
- Shows sequence tagging (STagBLCC) is robust and that multi-task learning with auxiliary tasks can improve performance.
- Strong empirical results: neural models yield statistically significant F1 gains, especially at the paragraph level.
- Thorough experimentation across multiple architectures and configurations.
- Clear motivation and problem definition highlighting limits of feature engineering and error propagation in pipelines.
- Includes useful error analysis on limits of dependency parsing and LSTM-ER with long sequences.
- Paper is well written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.
- Limited novelty in individual components—uses established models (BiLSTM, CRF, dependency parsing) applied to AM.
- Significant performance drop on essay-level texts, raising concerns about scalability and handling longer contexts.
- Lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate contributions of specific components (e.g., CNN, distance encodings).
- Error analysis is limited; more detailed breakdown of error types and frequently misclassified relations is needed.
- Fixed distance encoding is potentially limiting given varied relation distances; more sophisticated schemes should be explored.
- Request for deeper error analysis on model-specific mistakes (misclassified relations, incorrect spans).
- Ask for insights into why essay-level performance drops—long-range dependencies or other factors?
- Inquire whether techniques for long-range dependencies (e.g., hierarchical parsing) were tried to help dependency parsing.
- Request reporting of computational costs (training/inference time) for practical considerations.
- Ask about sensitivity to hyperparameters, especially distance encoding choices in sequence tagging.
- Suggest adding a link for Potash et al., 2017 and strengthening MTL discussion by comparing auxiliary tasks and layer-sharing choices.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed empirical paper with valuable insights and recommendation for acceptance pending minor revisions on error analysis and scalability.
