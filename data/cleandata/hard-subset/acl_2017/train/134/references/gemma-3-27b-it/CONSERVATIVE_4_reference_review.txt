This paper investigates neural end-to-end approaches to computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the problem as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, a multi-task learning problem, and using the LSTM-ER model. The authors compare these approaches to a feature-based ILP baseline.

The primary contribution of this work is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of several neural network architectures for end-to-end AM on the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset. While the idea of applying neural networks to AM is not entirely novel, this work provides a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the potential – and limitations – of different neural framings. A secondary contribution is the finding that a relatively simple sequence tagging approach (STagBLCC) performs competitively with more complex models, and that incorporating auxiliary tasks in a multi-task learning setup provides further improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a well-conducted set of experiments, comparing a variety of models on a standard dataset. The authors systematically explore different neural architectures and provide detailed results, including ablation studies and analysis of the impact of multi-task learning. This level of detail is crucial for advancing the field.
2. **Clear Identification of Challenges:** The paper honestly acknowledges the limitations of the dependency parsing approach, a framing the authors initially proposed. This self-critique is commendable and demonstrates a rigorous approach to research. The observation that the performance drop from paragraph to essay level is significant is particularly insightful.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The STagBLCC model achieves competitive results, demonstrating that neural sequence tagging can be a viable approach to end-to-end AM, often surpassing the established ILP baseline. The improvements gained through multi-task learning further strengthen this finding.
4. **Practical Insights:** The analysis of distance dependencies between argument components provides valuable insights into the nature of argumentation structures. The finding that related components are often close together suggests opportunities for further optimization of tagging models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methods:** The individual neural network architectures used (BiLSTMs, CRFs, LSTMs, TreeLSTMs) are well-established and not novel contributions. The primary novelty lies in *applying* these methods to the AM task and systematically comparing them. While this is valuable, it limits the paper’s overall impact. The claim of presenting the “first neural end-to-end solutions” is somewhat overstated, given prior work in related areas.
2. **Over-Reliance on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset. While this dataset is a valuable resource, the results may not generalize to other domains or argumentation schemes. The authors should acknowledge the potential limitations of this dataset and suggest future work to evaluate their methods on more diverse data.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper provides some discussion of the models’ performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the models make mistakes could lead to further improvements. For example, the authors could investigate specific types of errors or identify cases where the models struggle to capture long-range dependencies.
4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but does not provide sufficient detail about the process. This makes it difficult to reproduce the results and assess the robustness of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search strategies were used, and how were the optimal hyperparameters selected?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding methods, and if so, how did they affect performance?
3.  The paper highlights the challenges of long-range dependencies. Have you explored any techniques specifically designed to address this issue, such as attention mechanisms or hierarchical neural networks?
4.  Given the limitations of evaluating on a single dataset, what steps do you recommend for future research to improve the generalizability of these models to other domains and argumentation schemes?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of some of the tables is slightly inconsistent.
*   The notation in equation (1) could be clarified.
*   The explanation of LSTM-ER could be expanded to provide a more intuitive understanding of its architecture and how it addresses the AM task.
