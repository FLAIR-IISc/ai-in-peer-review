This paper explores neural end-to-end approaches to Computational Argumentation Mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and leveraging an existing LSTM-ER model. The authors identify limitations of prior feature-engineered, pipeline approaches and demonstrate that neural models can achieve competitive or superior performance.

The primary contribution is the exploration of several neural architectures for end-to-end AM, demonstrating the feasibility and promise of moving beyond traditional, feature-heavy methods. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation across different framing choices and model complexities on a challenging dataset. Finally, the insightful analysis revealing the ineffectiveness of a token-level dependency parsing approach, despite its theoretical appeal, is noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The exploration of neural end-to-end models for AM is genuinely novel. While individual components like sequence tagging have been applied to AM subtasks, this work systematically investigates multiple neural framings for the *entire* pipeline, a significant step forward. This is particularly commendable given the historical reliance on hand-crafted features and ILP.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors don't focus on just one model. They thoroughly evaluate dependency parsing, sequence tagging (with various LSTM configurations), multi-task learning, and adapt an existing relation extraction model (LSTM-ER). This breadth provides valuable insights into the suitability of different neural architectures for different aspects of AM.
3. **Counterintuitive Finding:** The finding that token-level dependency parsing *fails* for end-to-end AM, despite its intuitive appeal, is a strong result. This demonstrates a willingness to challenge conventional wisdom and offers a crucial negative result that will guide future research. The authors rightly connect this to the complexity of global tree constraints at the essay level.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The best-performing models (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER on paragraph level) achieve competitive results, surpassing the feature-based ILP baseline, solidifying the potential of neural approaches.  The gains on component identification are particularly encouraging.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the different modeling choices and experimental setup. The motivation for each approach is well-articulated, and the results are presented in a digestible format.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability Analysis:** While the performance drop from paragraph to essay level is acknowledged, the authors don’t deeply investigate *why* the parsing approaches falter at the longer sequence lengths. A more thorough analysis of computational complexity and memory constraints would be valuable. It’s not enough to state the performance degrades; understanding *why* is critical.
2. **Suboptimal Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter optimization but lacks detail on the specifics. It's unclear whether a truly exhaustive search was conducted, and it's possible that more aggressive tuning could improve the performance of some models. This is especially important for LSTM-based architectures, which are sensitive to hyperparameters.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The multi-task learning experiments would be significantly strengthened by ablation studies. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand the individual contributions of each auxiliary task (component detection, relation detection) and whether certain combinations are more effective than others.
4. **Over-reliance on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is performed solely on the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset. While this is a high-quality dataset, it is crucial to assess the generalizability of the findings to other corpora with potentially different annotation schemes or argument structures.
5. **Distance Encoding Limitations:** The fixed distance encoding in the sequence tagging model, while a clever idea, might be overly simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated ways to represent relational information (e.g., learned embeddings for distance) could potentially improve performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process used for each model? Specifically, what search space was explored, and what optimization strategy was employed?
2.  What is the computational cost (memory usage and training time) associated with each model, particularly as sequence length increases?
3.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of applying LSTM-ER to longer sequences? Is it primarily a memory issue, or are there other factors at play?
4.  Would you expect the performance gap between paragraph and essay-level parsing to narrow with larger models (e.g., deeper LSTMs, more parameters)?
5.  What are your plans for exploring other datasets and argument structures to validate the generalizability of your findings?
