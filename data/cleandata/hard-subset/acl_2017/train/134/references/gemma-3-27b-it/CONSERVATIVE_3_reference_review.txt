This paper explores neural end-to-end learning for computational argumentation mining (AM), framing the task as dependency parsing, sequence tagging, multi-task learning, and utilizing a pre-existing LSTM-ER model. The authors aim to move beyond traditional pipeline approaches reliant on hand-crafted features and integer linear programming.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a thorough empirical exploration of various neural network architectures applied to the end-to-end AM task, demonstrating that neural methods can achieve competitive performance with existing feature-engineered ILP baselines. A secondary contribution is the identification of limitations in a dependency parsing framing for this task, contrary to initial expectations. Finally, the investigation into multi-task learning and its potential benefits for AM is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a substantial set of experiments, comparing multiple neural approaches against a strong baseline (ILP). The breadth of experimentation provides a valuable resource for the community and highlights the strengths and weaknesses of different modeling choices.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that neural approaches, particularly sequence tagging (STagBLCC and LSTM-ER), can achieve comparable or superior performance to the existing state-of-the-art. This is a significant step towards more robust and generalizable AM systems.
3. **Insightful Negative Result:** The finding that a token-based dependency parsing approach is ineffective is valuable. This challenges a potentially misleading direction in the field and guides future research away from less promising avenues.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the different modeling approaches and experimental setup. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful for understanding the data structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methods:** The individual neural components (BiLSTMs, CRFs, LSTMs, TreeLSTMs) are well-established. The primary novelty lies in *applying* these methods to the AM task in an end-to-end fashion, and in the exploration of different framing choices. While this is a worthwhile endeavor, the methodological breakthroughs are incremental rather than transformative.  The paper doesn't introduce genuinely *new* neural architectures.
2. **Over-Reliance on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to the Stab and Gurevych (2016) dataset. While this is a high-quality dataset, the generalizability of the findings to other argumentation corpora is unclear.  Different argumentation schemes and annotation styles could significantly impact performance. A cross-dataset evaluation, even with a smaller dataset, would have strengthened the conclusions.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the authors mention some error analysis, it feels superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by each model would provide valuable insights for future improvement.  For example, understanding *why* the dependency parser fails would be particularly useful. The comment about LSTM-ER struggling with long documents could be explored further with examples.
4. **Complexity of Sequence Tagging Labels:** The proposed sequence tagging scheme with a highly complex label set (incorporating BIO tags, component types, distances, and stance) feels overly ambitious and potentially brittle. The paper acknowledges the potentially infinite size of the label space, and its practical effectiveness is not entirely convincing.  Simpler tagging schemes might prove more robust.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more concrete examples of errors made by the dependency parser, and elaborate on why you believe it struggles with longer sequences?
2.  Given the complexity of the sequence tagging label set, did you explore any dimensionality reduction techniques or label smoothing strategies to mitigate potential overfitting or sparsity issues?
3.  What steps could be taken to improve the generalizability of your findings to other argumentation datasets with different annotation schemes?
4.  The performance gap between paragraph and essay level parsing is substantial. Is this primarily due to longer sequences, or are there other factors at play (e.g., increased ambiguity in longer texts)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The discussion of related work is adequate, but could benefit from a more critical assessment of the limitations of previous approaches.
*   The supplementary material, if available, will be crucial for assessing the implementation details and hyperparameter tuning process.



Overall, this paper presents a solid empirical investigation of neural end-to-end argumentation mining. While the methodological contributions are incremental, the thorough evaluation and insightful negative results are valuable.  However, the reliance on a single dataset and lack of detailed error analysis limit the impact of the work.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses raised above in their response.
