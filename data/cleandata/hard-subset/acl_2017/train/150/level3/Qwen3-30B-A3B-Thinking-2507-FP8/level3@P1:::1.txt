

This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) model that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a two-recurrent neural network (RNN) word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a character-level translation architecture that automatically learns morphemes without requiring subword segmentation algorithms like BPE, (2) a demonstration that this approach achieves competitive BLEU scores with just one epoch of training (surpassing BPE-based models at this point), and (3) the model's ability to handle unseen words through morphological reasoning.

Strengths:
1. The paper's most significant contribution is the demonstration that character-level translation can achieve competitive results with minimal training while learning meaningful morphological representations. The model outperforms BPE-based models after just one training epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks, which is remarkable for a character-level approach. The authors provide compelling evidence that their word encoder learns morphemes (Figure 3), enabling the model to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" that were never seen during training (Table 2). This capability directly addresses a major limitation of word-based NMT.

2. The efficiency of the training process is a strong contribution. Despite having six recurrent networks, the authors demonstrate that their model trains significantly faster than comparable character-level models (Table 1). While Luong and Manning (2016) required "3 months" to train their character model, DCNMT achieves comparable results in "7 days" (for En-Fr). The hierarchical decoder with the HGRU design (Section 3.2) is a clever solution to the inefficiency of character-level decoding, making the approach practical.

3. The authors provide valuable empirical evidence that the model learns meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 shows that their word encoder produces representations where morphologically related words cluster together, unlike the ordinary RNN encoder. This is particularly impressive for morphologically rich languages like Czech (En-Cs and Cs-En tasks), where the model shows significant gains over other approaches.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to explicitly state which WMT test and development sets were used (newstest2015/test and newstest2013/dev), which is critical for reproducibility. While the authors mention these in their README, they should be clearly stated in the main paper. This omission makes it difficult to compare results with other submissions.

2. The architecture diagram (Figure 2) is misleading and does not match the described six-layer architecture. The paper claims "six recurrent neural networks" but the figure appears to show fewer layers. A clear, complete architecture diagram would significantly improve the paper's clarity and technical soundness.

3. The comparison with state-of-the-art systems is incomplete. The authors compare with BPE-based models and other character-level approaches but omit more recent results from WMT competitions or matrix.statmt.org. For example, the authors claim their model is "comparable to state-of-the-art," but they don't acknowledge that higher BLEU scores have been reported for En-Fr translation (e.g., 31.0+ BLEU on newstest2015). This weakens their claim of being competitive with the best systems.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify which WMT test and development sets were used in the experiments (newstest2015/test and newstest2013/dev) in the main paper.
2. Please provide a clearer, more complete architecture diagram that matches the described six-layer model.
3. Please expand the comparison to include recent state-of-the-art results from WMT 2016 or matrix.statmt.org to better contextualize the performance claims.

Minor issues include several English disfluencies, LaTeX errors (reversed quotation marks), and the software README lacking clear runnable examples and a --help flag. These should be addressed in a revision.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to character-level NMT that successfully demonstrates how learning morphology can improve translation quality and handling of unseen words. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive for a character-level model. While the paper has some presentation issues that need addressing, the core technical contribution is strong and should be considered for acceptance. The model's ability to handle nonce words through morphological reasoning is particularly noteworthy and could have broader applications beyond translation.
