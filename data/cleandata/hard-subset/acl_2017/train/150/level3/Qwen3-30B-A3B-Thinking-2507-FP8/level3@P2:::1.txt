

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors introduce a six-recurrent-network architecture that translates at character level while learning meaningful morpheme representations. The model avoids the large vocabulary bottleneck of word-based models, trains more efficiently than conventional character-level models, and demonstrates the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words through morphological learning. The authors release code and models for multiple language pairs, enhancing reproducibility.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphology using two recurrent networks to represent morphemes and their combinations, enabling the model to handle unseen words.
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently translates at character level while maintaining reasonable training efficiency, with the model achieving competitive BLEU scores after just one training epoch.
3. Empirical demonstration that the model learns meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships, as evidenced by PCA visualizations and successful translation of nonce words.

## Strengths
1. The model's ability to achieve competitive translation quality (comparable to state-of-the-art systems) after only one training epoch is impressive and addresses a key practical challenge in neural machine translation. As shown in Table 1, the authors' model outperforms the bpe-based baseline after just one epoch, while conventional character-level models require significantly more training time.

2. The paper provides compelling evidence for the model's ability to learn morphology through multiple analyses. The PCA projections in Figure 3 demonstrate that the model produces meaningful word representations where morphologically related words cluster together, unlike ordinary RNN word encoders. The successful translation of nonce words like "convenienter" in Table 2 further validates the model's morphological understanding.

3. The work makes a valuable contribution to the under-explored area of character-level neural machine translation. The authors successfully address key challenges (vocabulary bottleneck, training efficiency) that have hindered previous character-level approaches, demonstrating that character-level translation can be both practical and effective with the right architecture.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper lacks clarity about which WMT test and development sets were used. The key points indicate that the README mentions newstest2015/test and newstest2013/dev, but this should be explicitly stated in the main paper to ensure reproducibility.

2. Figure 2 and its caption are confusing and do not match the described six-layer architecture. The paper claims six recurrent networks but the figure appears to show fewer layers, creating ambiguity about the model's structure that should be resolved.

3. The comparison with state-of-the-art systems is incomplete. The authors omit comparisons with recent non-character-based neural machine translation systems that might outperform their approach, and fail to acknowledge higher BLEU scores reported on WMT/matrix.statmt.org, which weakens the claim of "comparable to state-of-the-art."

4. The software release lacks proper documentation. The README does not include clear runnable examples, and the tool does not support a --help flag, making it difficult for others to reproduce the experiments.

5. The paper contains minor issues with English, LaTeX formatting (e.g., reversed quotation marks), and presentation that should be addressed before publication.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify which specific WMT test and development sets were used for the experiments (newstest2013/dev and newstest2015/test as mentioned in the README, or others?).

2. Could you provide a more detailed architecture diagram (even in an appendix) that clearly shows all six recurrent networks and their connections?

3. Why does the paper omit comparisons with recent non-character-based neural machine translation systems that might have higher BLEU scores? How does your approach compare to these state-of-the-art systems?

## Recommendation
This is a valuable contribution to the character-level neural machine translation literature that addresses key challenges in the field. The model's ability to learn morphology and handle unseen words is particularly compelling. However, the paper requires significant revisions to address the clarity issues with the architecture description, experimental setup, and comparison to state-of-the-art systems before it can be considered for publication. With these revisions, this work would make a strong contribution to ACL.
