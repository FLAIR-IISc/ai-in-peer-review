

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that aims to address the vocabulary bottleneck in word-level NMT by learning morphology through a novel architecture. The main contributions are: (1) a word encoder with two RNNs that learns morphemes and their combinations, (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining efficiency, and (3) empirical evidence suggesting the model learns meaningful morphological representations.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a well-structured approach to character-level NMT that successfully avoids the large vocabulary issue while achieving competitive performance. The model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems after just one epoch of training (28.13 vs. 29.31 BLEU for En-Fr), demonstrating significant training efficiency advantages.

2. The architecture is novel in its combination of character-level processing with morphological learning. Unlike BPE-based approaches, DCNMT learns subword units automatically rather than relying on external segmentation. The hierarchical decoder design (with HGRU) efficiently handles character-level generation without requiring multiple forward passes.

3. The morphology analysis (Section 5.2) provides some interesting qualitative evidence that the model captures meaningful morphological relationships. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 show that words with similar morphological structures cluster together in the representation space, suggesting the model learns meaningful word representations.

Weaknesses:
1. The claim that DCNMT "learns morphology" is not sufficiently supported. The evidence consists of anecdotal examples (Figure 3-5) rather than formal experiments. The paper should either provide statistical evidence for morphological learning or soften its claims about morphology. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 is particularly misleading as it shows morphologically related words clustering together without demonstrating the model's ability to generalize to unseen morphological forms.

2. The comparison with BPE-based models is incomplete and misleading. The paper reports BLEU scores for En-Fr but not for Cs-En with BPE2char, and the training time comparison for Cs-En shows BPE2char is 8 days faster (Table 1), contradicting the paper's efficiency claims. The En-Fr BPE2char model is also not evaluated, making it impossible to judge the relative efficiency claims.

3. The reported BLEU improvements over BPE-based models are marginal (0.5-1.0 BLEU points), and no statistical significance testing is provided. For example, DCNMT's final BLEU score for En-Fr is 30.56 vs. BPE2BPE's 29.70, but the paper doesn't establish whether this difference is statistically significant.

4. Several technical details are unclear or inconsistent. The term "energy" (Section 5.2) is introduced without proper definition or explanation, and the color mapping in Figure 4a appears incorrect for some characters. The paper inconsistently capitalizes BPE, and the claim that French is "morphologically poor" is inaccurate and should be rephrased.

5. The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and typos, and several references are incomplete or improperly formatted. The claim about "no monolingual corpus" in Section 4.1 is unnecessary and distracting.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the BLEU score differences between DCNMT and BPE-based models?
2. How do you define and measure "morphological learning" in a rigorous way? Can you provide quantitative evidence beyond the qualitative examples shown in Figures 3-5?
3. Why are the Cs-En results for BPE2char (model 3) not reported in the cited paper (Chung et al. 2016)? Did you compute these results yourself?
4. The paper claims French is "morphologically poor" - can you provide a more precise characterization of French morphology that avoids this misleading simplification?

The paper presents an interesting approach to character-level NMT with some promising results, but the claims about morphology learning are overstated and the evaluation is incomplete. With more rigorous analysis of morphology learning and a more comprehensive comparison with BPE-based approaches, this work could make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, the marginal BLEU improvements without statistical significance testing and the incomplete evaluation undermine the paper's main claims.
