

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that aims to address the vocabulary bottleneck in neural machine translation by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combination rules using two recurrent networks, (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining similar encoding length to word-based models, and (3) a model that achieves competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art systems while avoiding the large vocabulary issue.

However, upon careful reading, I believe the most significant contribution is the hierarchical architecture that enables character-level translation without explicit segmentation. The claim about morphology learning, while interesting, is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The most concrete contribution is the efficient training of a character-level model that avoids vocabulary bottlenecks.

## Strengths

1. The paper presents a well-structured and clearly written proposal for addressing the vocabulary bottleneck in NMT. The problem statement is well-motivated and the proposed solution is logically developed. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of why character-level models have been challenging to train, particularly regarding attention mechanisms.

2. The model architecture is novel and well-justified. The word encoder using two RNNs to learn morphemes and the hierarchical decoder for character-level translation represent a creative approach that avoids the need for explicit segmentation or vocabulary limitations. The implementation details are sufficiently clear to be reproducible.

3. The experimental results on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs and Cs-En) are promising, with the model achieving comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems. The demonstration of translating misspelled words and nonce words correctly (Table 2) is a compelling practical benefit that addresses a real-world challenge in NMT.

## Weaknesses

1. The claims about morphology learning are not adequately supported. The analysis in Section 5.2 consists of only a few examples (Figure 3) and anecdotal evidence (Figure 4), with no formal experiments or statistical validation. The paper claims the model "is able to learn morphology," but provides no metrics or systematic evaluation of morphology learning. This is a significant weakness as it's central to the paper's contribution.

2. The BLEU score improvements over BPE-based models are marginal and not statistically significant. The differences between DCNMT and the best BPE-based model (bpe2bpe) are less than 1 BLEU point on all tasks (Table 1). The paper fails to report statistical significance testing, making it impossible to determine if these differences are meaningful. The claim that "our model obtains a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch" is misleading as the difference is minimal (e.g., 28.13 vs 29.31 on En-Fr).

3. The training time comparison is incomplete and potentially misleading. The paper claims DCNMT is more efficient to train than character-level models, but the comparison with bpe2bpe (the most relevant baseline) is incomplete: En-Fr results for bpe2bpe are not provided, En-Cs training time for bpe2bpe is missing, and the Cs-En comparison shows bpe2bpe as 8 days faster but doesn't provide the actual training times. The authors should provide complete training time comparisons to support their efficiency claims.

4. The paper contains multiple presentation issues that undermine its credibility: numerous undefined acronyms (HGRU, PCA, etc.), inconsistent capitalization of BPE, grammatical errors, and a misleading claim that French is "morphologically poor" (which is debatable and potentially offensive to French linguists). The reference to Figure 5's "peaks" without explaining what they represent is particularly problematic.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can the authors provide statistical significance testing for the BLEU score differences between DCNMT and BPE-based models to determine if the observed improvements are meaningful?

2. The paper claims DCNMT "is able to learn morphology" but provides only anecdotal evidence. How would the authors formally evaluate morphology learning in NMT, and what metrics would they propose?

3. The claim that French is "morphologically poor" is questionable. Could the authors rephrase this more carefully, acknowledging that French does have morphological complexity (e.g., verb conjugations, adjective agreements)?

4. The energy values in Figure 4a appear to have inconsistent color mapping. Could the authors clarify how these values were computed and why the color mapping seems inconsistent for some characters?

5. The paper states that "no monolingual corpus is used" in Section 4.1. Is this a necessary detail to include, or is it potentially misleading since monolingual data is rarely used in NMT without explicit mention?

## Additional Comments

The paper has a promising core idea but requires significant strengthening of its claims about morphology learning, more complete and statistically sound evaluation, and careful attention to presentation details. The writing contains multiple grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology that should be addressed. The authors should provide a link to the WMT'14 training corpora they used and fix the incomplete references. The claim that the model "learns morphology" should be softened to "may learn morphology" or "demonstrates potential for learning morphology" until formal evidence is provided.
