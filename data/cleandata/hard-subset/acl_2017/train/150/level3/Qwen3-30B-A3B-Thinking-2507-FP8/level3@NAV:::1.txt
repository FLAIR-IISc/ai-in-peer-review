

This paper proposes a novel character-level neural machine translation (NMT) system that learns morphology through a two-RNN word encoder and hierarchical decoder, avoiding the large vocabulary bottleneck of traditional word-based models. The main contributions are: (1) a morphological learning mechanism that enables the model to handle unseen words by backing off to morphemes, (2) a character-level architecture that achieves competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art systems after just one training epoch, and (3) the release of code and models for three language pairs, enhancing reproducibility.

The paper's strengths include the effective handling of out-of-vocabulary words through morphological learning, as demonstrated with nonce word examples (e.g., "convenienter"), and the impressive efficiency of the model which shows reasonable translation quality after only one training epoch. The system's ability to learn plausible morphological tokenizations and its competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En tasks represent significant contributions to the character-based NMT literature.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. First, the paper fails to explicitly state which WMT test/dev sets were used (the README indicates newstest2015/test and newstest2013/dev, but this should be in the main text). Second, Figure 2's caption describes a 6-level architecture, but the figure appears to show fewer layers, creating confusion about the model's structure. Third, the comparison with state-of-the-art systems omits important non-character-based NMT approaches that have achieved higher WMT scores, weakening the claims of competitiveness. Additionally, the software README lacks runnable examples and a --help flag, hindering reproducibility despite the code release. Minor English errors and LaTeX issues (e.g., reversed quotation marks) also require correction.

While the morphological learning approach is valuable, the paper would benefit from more complete experimental comparisons, clearer architecture visualization, and explicit reporting of evaluation settings. The model's efficiency and OOV handling are promising, but these weaknesses prevent the paper from making a stronger case for its contribution.
