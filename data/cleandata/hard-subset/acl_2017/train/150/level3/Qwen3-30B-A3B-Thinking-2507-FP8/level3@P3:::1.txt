

# Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a hierarchical architecture. The model starts and ends with character-level processing but uses morpheme representations in between, consisting of six recurrent networks organized in a hierarchical structure. The authors claim their approach achieves competitive BLEU scores while avoiding the large vocabulary bottleneck of traditional word-based models.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel architecture that learns morphology through a word encoder with two RNNs and a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level, enabling handling of unseen words through morpheme-based backoff.
2. The model's ability to produce reasonable translation quality after just one training epoch, demonstrating significant training efficiency compared to other character-level models.
3. The model's capacity to learn meaningful morphological representations, as evidenced by its ability to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" that were never seen during training.

## Strengths
1. The core contribution of learning morphology through a character-based model is significant for morphologically rich languages. The model's ability to handle unseen words by leveraging morpheme-level representations is particularly valuable and well-demonstrated through compelling examples in Table 2.

2. The authors' commitment to reproducibility is commendable - they release code and final models for three language pairs (fr-en, cs-en, en-cs), which greatly enhances the paper's value to the research community.

3. The training efficiency claim is well-supported: the model achieves reasonable translation quality after a single epoch (27.02 BLEU on En-Fr) and continues to improve with more training (30.56 BLEU), outperforming the BPE-based baseline after just one epoch.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper fails to explicitly state which WMT test/dev sets are used (the README indicates newstest2015/test and newstest2013/dev, but this should be clearly stated in the paper itself). This omission makes it difficult to properly evaluate the results against other work.

2. The software README lacks clear runnable examples and the tool does not support a --help flag, which significantly hinders reproducibility and usability of the code. A minimal working example would greatly improve the paper's practical value.

3. Figure 2 and its caption are inconsistent with the paper's description - the authors claim a 6-level architecture but the figure appears to show fewer layers. A complete architecture diagram (even in the appendix) is needed to clarify the model structure.

4. The comparison section omits state-of-the-art non-character MT systems and fails to acknowledge higher WMT/matrix.statmt.org results, making it difficult to properly assess the model's relative performance. The authors should discuss these results and explain why their model's performance compares favorably.

5. The paper contains minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues (e.g., reversed quotation marks) that should be corrected before publication.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify which specific WMT test sets are used for evaluation (newstest2013/dev and newstest2015/test)? This should be explicitly stated in the paper.

2. Can you provide a minimal working example in the README that demonstrates how to run the model on a small dataset? The current README lacks clear runnable examples.

3. Could you provide a more complete architecture diagram (even in the appendix) that clearly shows all six recurrent networks and their connections as described in the paper?

4. How does your model compare to the highest-scoring systems on the WMT 2015/2016 benchmarks? The paper does not acknowledge higher BLEU scores reported on matrix.statmt.org.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the character-based neural machine translation literature by demonstrating an elegant approach to handling morphology. While the core idea is strong and well-validated through experiments, the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the unclear presentation of experimental setup and architecture. With these improvements, this work would be a strong contribution to ACL 2017.
