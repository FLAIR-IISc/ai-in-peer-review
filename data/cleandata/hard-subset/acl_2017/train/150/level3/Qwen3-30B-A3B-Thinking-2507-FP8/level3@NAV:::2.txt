

This paper proposes DCNMT, a deep character-level neural machine translation model that combines character-level benefits with subword representations through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The main contributions I identify are: (1) a morphology-aware word encoder using two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combination rules, (2) a hierarchical decoder enabling efficient character-level translation, and (3) demonstrated competitive BLEU scores on multiple language pairs.

Strengths: The paper is well-structured and clearly presents an interesting approach to character-level translation. The core idea of learning morphology through a two-RNN word encoder is novel and potentially valuable for addressing out-of-vocabulary issues. The model achieves competitive BLEU scores with a character-level approach, which is significant.

Weaknesses: The most critical weakness is the lack of formal evidence supporting the claimed morphological learning advantage. Section 5.2's morphology analysis relies solely on anecdotal examples and PCA visualizations without statistical validation or controlled experiments, making the primary claim unsubstantiated. Second, the performance comparison with bpe2char is incomplete: BLEU differences are minimal (0.5-1.0 points), no statistical significance testing is reported, and training times for En→Cs and En→Fr are missing. Third, the paper contains numerous technical issues including undefined acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, BPE), inconsistent capitalization, and grammatical errors that undermine credibility. The inappropriate characterization of French as "morphologically poor" further weakens the presentation.

Additional concerns include Figure 4a's inconsistent color mapping, Table 1's unverified results for Chung et al. (2016), and the unnecessary emphasis on "no monolingual corpus used." While the core idea has potential, the current evidence does not sufficiently demonstrate DCNMT's advantages over existing approaches. The authors should conduct formal morphology experiments and complete the comparative evaluation before claiming superiority. The paper would benefit from more rigorous methodology and careful language.
