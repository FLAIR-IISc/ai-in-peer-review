

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that combines character-level processing with subword representations through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The model aims to address vocabulary size limitations in word-level NMT while learning morphology implicitly. The authors claim their model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems with improved training efficiency and the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel architecture that learns morphology through a dual-recurrent network word encoder, avoiding explicit segmentation.
2. A hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining similar encoding length to word-based models.
3. Demonstration that character-level modeling can achieve competitive BLEU scores while solving the out-of-vocabulary problem.

## Strengths
1. **Well-structured and clear presentation**: The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The figure explanations are particularly effective in illustrating the model's architecture, making the technical content accessible to readers.

2. **Original approach to morphology learning**: The dual-recurrent network word encoder that learns morpheme representations through energy-based weighting is a novel concept. The qualitative analysis in Section 5.2 provides compelling visual evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.

3. **Competitive results with efficiency**: The model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems while training significantly faster than character-level baselines (e.g., 7 days vs. 90 days for Luong & Manning, 2016). The claim that DCNMT outperforms bpe2char after just one training epoch is notable and well-supported by the data.

## Weaknesses
1. **Unsupported claims about morphology learning**: The assertion that DCNMT "learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches" is not substantiated with formal experiments. The analysis in Section 5.2 relies solely on anecdotal examples and PCA visualizations, which don't provide statistical evidence or comparative metrics against other morphology learning methods.

2. **Inadequate comparison with bpe2char**: The BLEU differences between DCNMT and bpe2char are extremely small (e.g., 0.5 BLEU on En-Fr), yet the authors claim DCNMT "outperforms" this baseline. The paper lacks statistical significance testing, and the training-time comparison is incomplete (En→Cs training time not reported, En→Fr bpe2char not evaluated).

3. **Insufficient evaluation of core claims**: The paper claims DCNMT "is able to learn morphology" based on qualitative examples, but provides no formal evaluation of morphology learning. The morphology analysis should be moved to an appendix or the claims significantly softened, as the current evidence is insufficient to support the primary claim.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims DCNMT "learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches" but provides no formal comparison metrics. How would you formally evaluate and compare the morphology learning capability of your model with other approaches?

2. The BLEU differences between DCNMT and bpe2char are minimal (e.g., 0.5 BLEU on En-Fr). Given the small magnitude of improvement, what statistical significance testing was performed to confirm these differences are meaningful?

3. The table 1 entry for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) states Cs→En results were not reported in the cited paper. Did the authors compute these results themselves, or should this be clarified in the paper?

## Additional Comments
- The writing contains multiple undefined acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) that should be properly introduced.
- The inconsistent capitalization of "BPE" should be standardized throughout the paper.
- The claim that French is "morphologically poor" is inaccurate and should be rephrased more carefully.
- The figure 4a energy color mapping appears incorrect for certain characters.
- Several references are incomplete or missing.
- The statement about "no monolingual corpus is used" in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary and could be removed.
- The paper contains multiple grammatical/typo errors that need correction.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach to character-level NMT with a novel word encoder, the current evidence does not sufficiently support the primary claim that the model "learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches." The evaluation is incomplete, particularly regarding the comparison with bpe2char. I recommend the authors provide more rigorous evaluation of the morphology learning claim, including formal experiments, and complete the training-time comparison with bpe2char before considering this work for publication. The core idea has merit but needs stronger evidence to support the main claims.
