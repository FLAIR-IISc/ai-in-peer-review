This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) model designed to learn morphology through a novel architecture combining character-level encoding/decoding with sub-word representations. The core contribution lies in the hierarchical structure, utilizing two recurrent networks for morphology learning and a hierarchical decoder to translate at the character level, thereby avoiding large vocabulary issues. A secondary contribution is the exploration of morphological learning within an NMT framework, aiming to outperform existing sub-word models, particularly on morphologically rich languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:** The paper is logically organized and presents the method in a clear and understandable manner. The motivation for the work is well-articulated, outlining the limitations of word-level and traditional character-level NMT. Figures 1 and 2 are particularly helpful in visualizing the model architecture.
2. **Original Ideas and Interesting Approach:** The proposed DCNMT architecture, with its emphasis on learning morphology through dedicated recurrent networks, is a novel approach to address the challenges of NMT. The hierarchical decoder is a sensible design choice for character-level translation.
3. **Promising Initial Results:** The initial BLEU score improvements over the bpe-based model on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks (after only one epoch) are encouraging, suggesting the potential of the proposed approach. The claim of achieving comparable performance to state-of-the-art systems is a significant positive.
4. **Focus on Morphology:** The explicit attempt to model and learn morphology within an NMT framework is a valuable contribution, given the known benefits of morphological awareness in machine translation. The attempt to demonstrate this learning through analysis (Section 5.2) is commendable, even if needing refinement (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Doubtful Result Interpretation and Statistical Significance:** While the reported BLEU score improvements are positive, the differences with bpe2char are very small. Crucially, the paper lacks any statistical significance testing to validate these improvements. It is unclear whether the observed differences are statistically significant or due to random variation.
2. **Unsupported Claims About Learning Morphology:** The analysis presented in Section 5.2 regarding morphology learning is anecdotal and based solely on example visualizations (PCA plots and energy mappings). There are no formal experiments or quantitative metrics to support the claim that the model *actually* learns morphological features effectively. This weakens the central argument of the paper.
3. **Incomplete Training-Time Comparison:** The training time comparison is incomplete and potentially misleading. While the paper mentions DCNMT being faster for Cs→En, it doesn't report training time for En→Cs. Furthermore, the En→Fr bpe2char training time wasn't evaluated, making a fair comparison impossible. The claim of "efficiently train the deep model" lacks strong supporting evidence.
4. **Acronym Overload and Definition Issues:** The paper is riddled with undefined or late-defined acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE). This hinders readability and makes it difficult for readers to follow the technical details. Consistent use of full names initially and then acronyms is recommended.
5. **Unclear Explanation of “Energy”:** The concept of “energy” introduced in Section 5.2 lacks a clear reintroduction and explanation. The interpretation of peaks in Figure 5 is not well-defined, making it difficult to understand the analysis.
6. **Inconsistent BPE Capitalization:** The capitalization of “BPE” is inconsistent throughout the paper, causing confusion.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:** Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) to demonstrate whether the observed BLEU score differences are statistically significant.
2. **Complete Training Times:** Provide complete training time comparisons, including training time for En→Cs for both DCNMT and bpe2char. Also, report the training time for bpe2char on the En→Fr task.
3. **Morphology Evaluation:** Could you elaborate on potential formal experiments (e.g., probing tasks, morphological segmentation accuracy) that could be used to objectively assess the model's ability to learn morphology?
4. **Energy Definition:** Please clearly define what “energy” represents in Section 5.2 and explain how the peaks in Figure 5 relate to morphological features.
5. **Table 1 Clarification:** Were the Cs→En results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) obtained by you, or were they reported in the original paper? If computed by you, please state that explicitly.

**Additional Comments:**

* The statement about not using a monolingual corpus in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary and doesn’t add significant information.
* Figure 4a’s energy color mapping appears incorrect or missing for some characters. Please verify and correct the visualization.
* Please rephrase the statement about French being “morphologically poor.” This is a subjective and potentially inaccurate characterization. Consider rephrasing it as "French exhibits relatively limited morphological variation compared to Czech."
* Provide a direct link to the WMT’14 training corpora.
* Correct the incomplete references (e.g., “Bahdanau et al.,? 2015”).
* Thoroughly proofread the paper for grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "convenienter," "UNk").



Overall, this paper presents a promising and novel approach to character-level NMT with a focus on morphology. However, the lack of statistical significance testing, the weak evidence supporting the claims about morphology learning, and the incomplete experimental comparisons raise significant concerns. Addressing these weaknesses would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
