This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level and existing character-level approaches. The core innovation lies in a hierarchical architecture featuring a novel word encoder that learns morphology through two recurrent neural networks and a hierarchical decoder that translates at the character level. This approach avoids the large vocabulary problem inherent in word-based models while addressing the training difficulties and length limitations of purely character-based systems. The authors demonstrate competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, showing performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems and notable improvements even after a single training epoch. The experiments and analyses also suggest the model successfully learns morphological structure, enabling handling of unseen words and potential translation of misspelled inputs.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed architecture for deep character-level NMT, effectively integrating morphological learning into the translation process.  While character-level NMT is not entirely novel, the specific design – combining two RNNs within the word encoder to represent preceding characters and the weighting of that representation, alongside the hierarchical decoder – offers a unique and promising solution.  A second, significant contribution is the publicly released code and trained models, facilitating reproducibility and further research in this area.  This openness is particularly valuable in the rapidly evolving field of NMT. Finally, the demonstration of the model's ability to learn morphological representations and handle unseen words provides a valuable insight into the potential benefits of morphology-aware NMT.

Several aspects of the submission are strong. The motivation is well-articulated; the authors clearly identify the shortcomings of existing word-level and character-level NMT systems and present a logical rationale for their approach. The experimental setup, including the use of multiple language pairs (En-Fr and En-Cs) and comparison to baseline models, is appropriate. The reported results demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance, particularly considering the relatively short training time (one epoch for initial results). The analysis demonstrating the model’s capacity to learn morphology, through visualizations of character energy and subword segmentation, is insightful and supportive of the core claims.  The discussion of advantages in handling misspelled or nonce words is compelling.

However, there are also several weaknesses. The paper lacks clarity regarding the specific test and development sets used from the WMT campaigns. While the README mentions newstest2015/test and newstest2013/dev, this information should be explicitly stated within the main body of the paper.  Furthermore, the provided software documentation is incomplete. The absence of a `--help` flag and lack of clear, runnable examples in the README hinders reproducibility and ease of use. The architecture figure (Figure 2) is insufficient. The paper describes a six-layer architecture, but the figure doesn't clearly illustrate all components and their connections. A more comprehensive diagram, potentially included in the appendix, is necessary.  Critically, the comparison with existing work is incomplete.  The paper omits discussion of state-of-the-art, non-character-based NMT systems and doesn’t address potentially higher results reported on the WMT leaderboard (matrix.statmt.org).  Acknowledging and discussing these results is crucial for accurately positioning the contribution of this work. Finally, the paper contains minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX errors, which detract from its overall readability and professionalism.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please explicitly state which WMT test and development sets were used for evaluation in the main body of the paper.
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the differences between your model and the existing state-of-the-art NMT systems (word-based and character-based), especially regarding performance trade-offs and computational costs?
3.  Could you provide a high-resolution, detailed diagram of the complete architecture, illustrating all six recurrent network layers and their interconnections?
4.  Will you release a more comprehensive software documentation including a `--help` flag and runnable examples?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the paper to address minor English and LaTeX errors.
*   Consider exploring the effect of deeper recurrent networks or longer sequence lengths on model performance.
*   It would be beneficial to include a qualitative analysis of translation examples, highlighting the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
