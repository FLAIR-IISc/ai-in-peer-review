## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that leverages character-level processing while incorporating morphological information. The core idea is to use two recurrent networks to learn representations of preceding characters and the weights of those representations, effectively learning morphemes, within a six-layer architecture. The authors release code and models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, which is commendable for reproducibility. 

The strongest contribution of this work lies in the proposed hybrid character-level approach. By integrating morpheme-level representations *between* character-level boundaries, the model addresses the limitations of both purely word-based and purely character-based NMT systems, particularly regarding handling out-of-vocabulary words and capturing morphological relationships. The reported results, showing reasonable translation quality even after a single training epoch and improvement with further training, are encouraging. The ability to learn plausible morphological tokenizations and handle unseen words through backoff to morphemes is a significant advantage. 

Another valuable aspect of the submission is the publicly available code and models. This facilitates further research and comparison within the community. Finally, the demonstrated efficiency in training a deep model with six recurrent networks is a positive outcome.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The most critical is the lack of explicit mention of the WMT test/dev sets used, relying instead on the README file.  This should be clearly stated in the main text.  Furthermore, the software's lack of a `--help` flag and the unclear runnable examples in the README hinder usability and adoption. 

The figure illustrating the architecture (Figure 2) is insufficient. The description mentions a six-layer architecture, yet the figure appears to show fewer layers. A more detailed and comprehensive architecture diagram, even as an appendix, is crucial for understanding the model’s structure. The comparison is also lacking; omitting state-of-the-art non-character MT systems and failing to acknowledge higher WMT/matrix.statmt.org results weakens the evaluation. 

Finally, the paper contains minor English disfluencies, spelling errors, and LaTeX issues that require careful correction.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please explicitly state which WMT test/dev sets were used for evaluation in the main text.
2.  Can you provide a clearer and more detailed architectural diagram, possibly in the appendix, to accurately represent the six-layer structure?
3.  Could you discuss the results from state-of-the-art non-character-based NMT systems and compare them to your findings, addressing any discrepancies?
4.  Please provide runnable examples for the software in the README, and implement a `--help` flag for easy usage.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion on the PCA projection in Figure 3 and the example word segmentation in Figure 5 are insightful and help to illustrate the model’s learning of morphology.
*   The authors' claim of benefiting from learning morphology is supported by the experimental results.
