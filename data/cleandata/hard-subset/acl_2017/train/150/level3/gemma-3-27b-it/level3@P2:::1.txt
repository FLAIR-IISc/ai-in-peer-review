This paper presents a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, aiming to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the large vocabulary issue and out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problems. The core contribution lies in the architecture which learns morphology through two recurrent networks within a word encoder, coupled with a hierarchical decoder translating at the character level. A secondary, valuable contribution is the release of code and trained models for En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, facilitating reproducibility and further research. Finally, the demonstrated ability to translate misspelled or nonce words, leveraging the character-level representation, represents a noteworthy advantage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture for Morphology Learning:** The proposed architecture, specifically the dual-RNN word encoder, offers a compelling approach to capturing morphological information. By decoupling the representation of preceding characters and the weighting of these representations, the model appears to learn meaningful subword units automatically, mitigating the need for pre-defined segmentation techniques like byte-pair encoding. The analysis presented in Figure 3 and Section 5.2 provides initial evidence supporting this claim, demonstrating a more organized embedding space for morphologically related words compared to a standard RNN encoder.

2. **Reproducibility and Open Science:** The authors’ decision to release code and trained models is commendable. This significantly enhances the paper’s impact, allowing the research community to build upon their work, verify their results, and further explore the potential of deep character-level NMT. This openness is a strong indicator of responsible research practice.

3. **Competitive Performance & Fast Initial Training:**  The reported BLEU scores demonstrate that the model achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art character-level NMT systems.  Furthermore, the observation that competitive results are achieved even after a single epoch of training suggests efficient learning and a potential advantage over models requiring extensive training. The comparison to the bpe-based model and other character-level approaches provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Architecture Description & Visualization:** The description of the six-layer architecture is somewhat ambiguous, and Figure 2 doesn’t fully reflect the claimed complexity. The figure seems to represent fewer layers than described in the text, making it difficult to fully grasp the model’s structure. A more detailed architectural diagram, perhaps in the appendix, is crucial for understanding the flow of information and the interactions between the various components.

2. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons are made to other character-level models, the paper omits a comparison with strong, non-character-level NMT systems. Acknowledging and discussing results from systems reported on the WMT matrix (matrix.statmt.org) would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model’s performance relative to the broader NMT landscape.  The paper should address why these systems were excluded from the comparison.

3. **Software & Documentation Issues:** The reported issues with the software README – lack of runnable examples and the absence of a `--help` flag – hinder usability and accessibility. These are important practical concerns that detract from the value of releasing the code. A well-documented and easily usable codebase is essential for enabling wider adoption and continued development.  The mention of WMT test/dev sets in the README rather than explicitly in the paper also presents a reproducibility issue.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed diagram (potentially in the appendix) illustrating the complete six-layer architecture, clarifying the connections and functionalities of each component?
2. What is the rationale for not including comparisons with state-of-the-art, non-character-level NMT systems from the WMT matrix?
3. What steps will be taken to improve the usability and documentation of the released code, addressing the issues of runnable examples and the missing `--help` flag?
4. The paper briefly mentions the potential for applying the approach to other tasks. Could you elaborate on the challenges and potential benefits of adapting the model to tasks such as speech recognition or text summarization?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper contains minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues (e.g., reversed quotation marks) that should be addressed during the revision process. These issues, while not critical, detract from the overall polish and readability of the manuscript.



Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of character-based neural machine translation. The proposed architecture presents a promising approach to learning morphology and handling OOV words. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly clarifying the architecture, providing a more comprehensive comparison, and improving the software usability, will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
