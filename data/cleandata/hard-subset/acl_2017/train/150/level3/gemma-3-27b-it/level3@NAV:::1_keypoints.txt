- Proposed a novel encoder-decoder NMT that combines character-level processing with learned morpheme representations using two recurrent networks within a six-layer architecture.
- Core mechanism: two recurrent networks learn representations of preceding characters and weights to discover morpheme-like units between character boundaries.
- Hybrid character-level approach mitigates OOV issues and better captures morphological relationships than purely word- or character-based systems.
- Reported results show reasonable translation quality after one epoch and further improvement with additional training; the model can back off to morphemes for unseen words.
- Demonstrates ability to learn plausible morphological tokenizations; PCA projection (Fig.3) and segmentation examples (Fig.5) are informative.
- Authors released code and models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, supporting reproducibility.
- Training is efficient despite the depth (six recurrent networks).
- Major omission: paper does not explicitly state which WMT test/dev sets were used in the main text.
- Usability problems: software lacks a --help flag and README lacks clear runnable examples.
- Presentation flaw: Figure 2 is insufficient and appears inconsistent with the claimed six-layer architecture; a more detailed diagram is needed.
- Evaluation gap: missing comparisons to state-of-the-art non-character-based NMT systems and higher WMT/matrix.statmt.org results.
- Minor issues: English disfluencies, spelling mistakes, and LaTeX problems require correction.
- Questions for authors: specify WMT sets used; provide a clearer six-layer architectural diagram; compare to SOTA non-character NMTs and explain discrepancies; add runnable examples and a --help flag.
- Overall: experimental results support the authors’ claim that learning morphology benefits translation.
