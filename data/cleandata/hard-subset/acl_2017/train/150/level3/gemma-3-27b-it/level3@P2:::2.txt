## Review for ACL 2017

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) designed to learn morphology and overcome limitations of both word-level and traditional character-level approaches. The core contribution lies in the novel architecture featuring a word encoder based on two recurrent neural networks and a hierarchical decoder, enabling translation at the character level while avoiding large vocabulary issues. The authors demonstrate competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, suggesting the model's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its ideas. The proposed architecture is original and addresses a significant challenge in NMT – handling morphology and out-of-vocabulary words. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly superior, are promising and demonstrate that the DCNMT model is competitive with state-of-the-art systems. The attempt to analyze whether the model actually learns morphology is also a commendable effort, demonstrating a thoughtful approach to understanding the model's behavior. The inclusion of training time comparisons (although incomplete – see weaknesses) provides valuable context. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, highlighting the drawbacks of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness lies in the interpretation of results and the somewhat unsupported claims regarding morphology learning. While the authors present examples in Section 5.2 suggesting the model learns morphological information, this analysis is largely anecdotal and lacks rigorous quantitative validation. The "energy" concept introduced is not clearly explained and the interpretation of peaks in Figure 3 requires further clarification. The claim that the model learns morphology *better* than other approaches isn't sufficiently substantiated. 

Furthermore, the comparison with the bpe2char model (Luong & Manning, 2016) is incomplete. While DCNMT shows comparable or slightly better results, the reported BLEU differences are small and lack statistical significance testing. Crucially, the training time comparison isn’t comprehensive. The reported 8-day faster training time for Cs→En with bpe2char is significant and should be addressed. The En→Cs training time is missing, and the En→Fr bpe2char performance isn’t reported, hindering a fair assessment. A more complete comparison is needed to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of DCNMT.

The paper suffers from several minor issues that detract from its polish. Numerous acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are either undefined or defined *after* their first use. Inconsistent capitalization of “BPE” appears throughout the text. The statement about not using monolingual corpora in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary. Figure 4a’s color mapping appears incorrect or missing for some characters. Table 1 also contains a discrepancy: the Cs→En results for Chung et al. (2016) were not reported in the original paper, and the authors should clarify whether they computed these values themselves.  Finally, phrasing like describing French as “morphologically poor” is potentially misleading and should be rephrased more carefully.



**Contributions:**

I identify two main contributions. First, the proposed deep character-level NMT architecture, incorporating a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder, represents a significant architectural innovation. This allows for character-level translation without the computational burden of purely character-based models and avoids the vocabulary limitations of word-level approaches. Second, the exploration of morphology learning within the context of NMT is a valuable contribution. Although the current analysis is limited, it highlights an important research direction and motivates further investigation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the BLEU score improvements over the bpe2char baseline?
2. Please provide a more complete training time comparison, including the En→Cs training time for DCNMT and the En→Fr performance for bpe2char.
3. Can you elaborate on the “energy” concept introduced in Section 5.2 and clarify the interpretation of the peaks observed in Figure 3? What specifically do these peaks represent?
4. What steps were taken to verify the accuracy of the Cs→En results reported for Chung et al. (2016) in Table 1?
5. Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of using deeper recurrent networks in the model?

**Additional Comments:**

- Several grammatical and typographical errors need correction throughout the paper (e.g., "less efficient" should be "less efficiently" in Section 3.2).
- Please provide a direct link to the WMT’14 training corpora used in the experiments.
- Correct incomplete references (e.g. Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015).
- I recommend moving the anecdotal morphology analysis in Section 5.2 to the appendix or softening the associated claims. Consider expanding this analysis into a separate, more formal experimental study.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper presents a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of NMT. However, the authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning the validation of morphology learning claims and the completeness of the comparative evaluation. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
