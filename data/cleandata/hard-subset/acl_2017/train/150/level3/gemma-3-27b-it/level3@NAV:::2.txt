## Review of “Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology”

This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) architecture designed to learn morphology and overcome the limitations of both word-level and subword-level NMT models. The core contribution lies in the combination of a character-level encoder with a hierarchical decoder, allowing the model to operate directly on characters while still capturing subword information. The authors claim that DCNMT learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches, achieving competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

The strongest aspect of this submission is the clear and well-structured presentation of the proposed architecture. The paper is easy to follow, and the motivation behind the design choices is well explained. The idea of explicitly modeling morphology within a character-level framework is also intriguing and potentially valuable. Finally, the initial results, while not overwhelmingly superior, show promising performance comparable to other character-level models.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of rigorous statistical significance testing for the reported BLEU score improvements, particularly the small differences observed between DCNMT and bpe2char. The training-time comparison is incomplete; while faster training for Cs→En is noted, En→Cs training time is missing, and the En→Fr comparison with bpe2char isn't conducted. This makes it difficult to definitively assess the efficiency claims. Furthermore, the claims about improved morphology learning (Section 5.2) are largely anecdotal, based on visual examples and PCA plots, lacking formal experimentation or quantifiable metrics. The capitalization of "BPE" is also inconsistent throughout the text. Numerous acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are either undefined or defined *after* their first use, hindering readability. 

Finally, the statement about not using a monolingual corpus (Section 4.1) seems unnecessary and its purpose isn't clear. The French language is described as “morphologically poor,” a characterization that should be rephrased for sensitivity. Figure 4a’s energy color mapping appears incomplete and possibly incorrect. Table 1 references results for model (3) in Chung et al. (2016) that are not reported in the original publication; clarification is needed on whether these were recomputed.


**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical significance testing results (e.g., bootstrap resampling) to support the BLEU score improvements reported in Table 1?
2.  Please complete the training time comparison, including En→Cs training time and an evaluation of bpe2char on the En→Fr task.
3.  How were the results for model (3) in Chung et al. (2016) obtained, as they are not reported in the cited paper?
4.  Would you consider moving the morphology analysis in Section 5.2 to the appendix, or softening the claims to reflect the lack of formal experimental validation?
5.  Please provide a link to the WMT’14 training corpora for reproducibility.
