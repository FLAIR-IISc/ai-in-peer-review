This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) aimed at addressing the limitations of word-level NMT, particularly the large vocabulary problem and difficulties in handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating two recurrent networks for learning morphology and a character-level decoder, resulting in a six-layer recurrent model. The authors demonstrate competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art systems, and claim their model learns morphology more effectively than existing approaches.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the novel architecture combining character-level modeling with a learned subword representation, effectively bridging the gap between purely word-based and subword-based NMT systems. This approach circumvents the large vocabulary bottleneck without relying on pre-defined subword units like byte-pair encoding (BPE), and allows for efficient training despite the increased model depth. A secondary contribution is the proposed hierarchical decoder that leverages word-level representations to guide character-level generation, improving the feasibility of character-level NMT.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed architecture is well-motivated and offers a compelling approach to character-level NMT. The integration of morphology learning via the dual RNN word encoder is an interesting concept and a potentially significant advancement.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The reported BLEU scores are competitive with existing state-of-the-art systems, demonstrating the viability of the approach. Achieving comparable performance to stronger baselines with a relatively simpler model (in terms of parameter count, as indicated in Table 1) is encouraging.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and structured, making the core ideas and experimental setup reasonably easy to follow. The diagrams (Figures 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the model architecture.
4. **Addresses a Key Problem:** The work directly tackles the well-known challenges of vocabulary size and OOV words in NMT, offering a potential solution that avoids the limitations of existing techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the model achieves competitive results, the comparisons with baseline models, particularly the bpe-based approaches, are insufficient. The BLEU score differences are often small (e.g., En-Fr) and statistical significance testing is absent, making it difficult to definitively conclude that DCNMT is superior. The incompleteness of the training time comparison (missing En-Cs bpe2char time, only reporting Cs-En bpe2char training time advantage) weakens this aspect.
2. **Unsupported Claims About Morphology Learning:** The analysis presented in Section 5.2, purporting to demonstrate that the model learns morphology, is largely anecdotal and lacks rigorous experimental validation. The PCA visualizations (Figure 3) and character energy analysis (Figure 4) provide suggestive evidence but are insufficient to support the strong claims made by the authors. The analysis should be moved to the appendix or significantly toned down.
3. **Insufficient Details and Inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from several inconsistencies and a lack of detail. Numerous acronyms are either undefined or defined *after* their first use (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE). Capitalization of “BPE” is inconsistent. The sentence regarding the lack of monolingual data usage in Section 4.1 feels unnecessary. A crucial energy explanation and meaning of peaks in Figure 5 are missing.
4. **Potential Issues with Figure 4a:** The color mapping of the “energy” plot in Figure 4a appears either incorrect or incomplete, making it difficult to interpret the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:** Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) for the BLEU score differences between DCNMT and the baseline models?
2. **Morphology Evaluation:** What formal experiments were conducted to validate the claim that DCNMT learns morphology? If none were performed, would you consider revising the claims or moving the analysis to an appendix?
3. **Energy Explanation:** Please clarify the meaning of “energy” in Section 5.2 and explain what the peaks in Figure 5 represent.
4. **Training Time Detail:** Can you please provide the En-Cs training time for the bpe2char model to complete the comparison with Table 1?
5. **WMT Dataset Link:** Can you provide a link to the WMT’14 training corpora used in the experiments?

**Additional Comments:**

- The term “morphologically poor” to describe French is imprecise and potentially misleading. Rephrase this to something more neutral.
- Several references are incomplete. Please provide full citation details.
- Multiple grammatical and typographical errors require correction (e.g., “peak energy” in Figure 5, incorrect capitalization).
- Table 1 needs clarification regarding the Cs→En results for the model cited as (3) Chung et al. (2016); confirm these results were not computed by the authors.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of NMT. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the comparative analysis and providing rigorous validation for the morphology learning claims, is crucial for acceptance. The authors should also address the minor inconsistencies and provide greater clarity in their presentation.
