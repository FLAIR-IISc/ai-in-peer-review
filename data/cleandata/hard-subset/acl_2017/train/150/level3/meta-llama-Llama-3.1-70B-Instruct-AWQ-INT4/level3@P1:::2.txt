**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that combines the benefits of character-level and sub-word representations. The authors introduce a deep word-encoding/decoding layer that learns morphology and uses a hierarchical decoder to translate at the character level. The proposed model, called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT), is evaluated on English-to-French, English-to-Czech, and Czech-to-English translation tasks. The results show that DCNMT achieves a higher BLEU score than the byte pair encoding (BPE)-based model after training for one epoch and is comparable to the state-of-the-art systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Deep word-encoding/decoding layer**: The authors propose a novel architecture that combines the benefits of character-level and sub-word representations. The deep word-encoding/decoding layer learns morphology and uses a hierarchical decoder to translate at the character level.
2. **Hierarchical decoder**: The authors introduce a hierarchical decoder that generates a character sequence based on the output of the first-level decoder. The second-level decoder either continues the character-level states or resets using word-level states at boundaries.
3. **Learning morphology**: The authors claim that their model learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches. They provide anecdotal evidence and examples to support this claim.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-structured paper**: The paper is well-organized and easy to follow.
2. **Original ideas and interesting results**: The authors propose a novel architecture and provide interesting results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Clear presentation**: The authors present their ideas clearly and concisely.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Doubts about result interpretation**: The BLEU differences between DCNMT and bpe2char are very small, and no statistical significance testing is reported.
2. **Unsupported claims about learning morphology**: The authors provide anecdotal evidence and examples to support their claim that DCNMT learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches, but no formal experiments are conducted to support this claim.
3. **Incomplete training-time comparison**: The training time for bpe2char is not reported for En→Cs, and En→Fr bpe2char is not evaluated.
4. **Lack of formal experiments to support morphology claims**: The authors should conduct formal experiments to support their claims about learning morphology.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more complete comparisons with bpe2char to demonstrate the advantages of DCNMT?
2. Can you provide formal experiments to support your claims about learning morphology?
3. Can you clarify the concept of “energy” in Section 5.2 and describe what peaks mean in Figure 5?
4. Can you provide a link for WMT’14 training corpora and fix several incomplete references?
5. Can you correct multiple grammatical/typo errors throughout the paper?

**Additional Comments**

1. The authors should avoid calling French “morphologically poor” and rephrase more carefully.
2. The authors should provide a clear explanation of the concept of “energy” in Section 5.2.
3. The authors should fix the energy color mapping in Figure 4a.
4. The authors should state if they computed the Cs→En results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) or if they were not reported in the cited paper.
5. The authors should provide a clear explanation of the concept of “energy” in Section 5.2 and describe what peaks mean in Figure 5.
