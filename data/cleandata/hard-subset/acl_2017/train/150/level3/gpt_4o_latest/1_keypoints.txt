- Summary: Proposes a deep character-level NMT (DCNMT) with a hierarchical decoder and word encoder that learns morphological representations, evaluated on En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En with competitive BLEU and released code/models.  
- Strength: Novel architecture combining character, subword, and word-level representations that learns morphological tokenizations and backs off to morphemes for unseen words.  
- Strength: Training efficiency—achieves competitive BLEU scores after just one epoch, addressing computational challenges of character-level NMT.  
- Strength: Practical contribution through release of code and trained models, aiding reproducibility and further research.  
- Strength: Robustness to misspellings and nonce words, improving over typical word-level NMT limitations.  
- Strength: Qualitative evidence that learned morphemes capture meaningful linguistic structures.  
- Weakness: Dataset reporting is incomplete—paper omits explicit mention of WMT test/dev sets, forcing readers to consult the code.  
- Weakness: Figure 2 and architecture visualization lack clarity and sufficient explanation.  
- Weakness: Results are competitive but do not surpass state-of-the-art, and this limitation is not fully discussed.  
- Weakness: README/documentation could be improved with explicit examples and a `--help` flag for usability.  
- Weakness: Minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX formatting issues reduce readability.  
- Question: Why do results not surpass state-of-the-art and what avenues could improve performance?  
- Question: Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., compound or agglutinative languages) where the model excels or struggles?  
- Question: How does performance scale with longer training epochs or larger datasets?  
- Conclusion: Valuable and efficient contribution to character-based NMT with recommended acceptance pending minor revisions on documentation, visualization, and contextualization.
