- Paper proposes DCNMT: a hierarchical deep character-level NMT using sub-word representations to tackle large vocabularies and OOVs, claiming competitive BLEU and morphology learning.  
- Contribution 1: Novel word encoder that learns morphemes and compositional rules for efficient sub-word representations.  
- Contribution 2: Hierarchical decoder that generates at the character level to address OOVs and improve translation quality.  
- Contribution 3: Experimental results showing competitive BLEU scores versus state-of-the-art and qualitative analyses suggesting morphology learning.  
- Strength: Innovative hierarchical architecture combining character- and sub-word-level representations addressing vocabulary and OOV challenges.  
- Strength: Claimed computational efficiency—fewer parameters and faster training than other character-level models despite using six RNNs.  
- Strength: Useful qualitative analyses (e.g., PCA visualizations, morphology examples) that add interpretability.  
- Strength: Paper is well-written and logically structured.  
- Weakness: Evidence for morphology learning is qualitative (PCA, examples) and lacks formal quantitative evaluation.  
- Weakness: BLEU improvements over the bpe2char baseline are minimal and no statistical significance is reported.  
- Weakness: Incomplete comparisons and training-time reporting across datasets; advantages over bpe2char are not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Weakness: Ambiguities and missing details (undefined acronyms, poorly explained concepts like “energy”/“peaks,” missing corpus links, incomplete references).  
- Weakness: Typographical and formatting issues, inconsistent “BPE” capitalization, and unclear figures (e.g., missing energy color values).  
- Questions: Provide quantitative metrics for morphology learning and formal evaluations for Section 5.2.  
- Questions: Clarify training-time comparisons versus bpe2char across datasets and overall training efficiency.  
- Questions: Explain why BLEU gains over bpe2char are minimal and whether they are statistically significant.  
- Questions: Clarify the necessity of mentioning the absence of a monolingual corpus in Section 4.1.  
- Recommendation: Accept with major revisions contingent on stronger experimental support and addressing the listed weaknesses.
