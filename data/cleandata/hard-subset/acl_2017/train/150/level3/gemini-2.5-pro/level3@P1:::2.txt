### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and the computational bottleneck of large vocabularies. The proposed architecture, named Deep Character-Level NMT (DCNMT), is composed of six recurrent neural networks in a hierarchical structure. On the source side, a novel word encoder uses two RNNs to compute a weighted sum of character representations, aiming to learn sub-word units (morphemes) automatically. On the target side, a hierarchical decoder uses a word-level RNN to guide a character-level RNN (HGRU), generating translations character by character within word boundaries. The authors evaluate their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, reporting BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems and arguing that their model learns morphology, is efficient to train, and can handle misspelled or nonce words.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The primary contribution is a deep architecture that combines the benefits of character-level modeling (no OOV issues) with the structural advantages of word-level systems. The model introduces a novel source-side word encoder that learns a weighted composition of character states to form a word representation, and a target-side hierarchical decoder that generates words as character sequences guided by a higher-level word decoder state. This provides an end-to-end method for learning sub-word representations without requiring explicit segmentation algorithms like BPE.

2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Efficient Learning and Robustness:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed model learns quickly, outperforming a BPE-based baseline after just one epoch of training on two language pairs. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis shows a valuable practical advantage: the model can correctly translate misspelled words and unseen morphological variants (nonce words), a task where traditional word-level models with fixed vocabularies inherently fail.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The proposed DCNMT architecture is original and intelligently designed. The source word encoder, which uses one RNN to scan characters and another BiRNN to learn weights for each character's hidden state, is a plausible mechanism for learning compositional word representations that capture morphology. Similarly, the hierarchical decoder is an elegant solution for generating character sequences while maintaining a word-level structure. The overall design is well-motivated by the goal of learning morphology automatically within an end-to-end NMT framework.

2.  **Strong Qualitative and Early-Training Results:** The analysis in Section 5.3 and Table 2 provides strong qualitative support for the benefits of the character-level approach. The ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words like "convenienter" is a significant advantage over models reliant on fixed vocabularies and demonstrates the model's robustness. Furthermore, the claim that the model achieves strong performance after only one training epoch (Table 1) suggests that the architecture's inductive bias for morphology may lead to more efficient learning compared to sub-word baselines.

3.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors effectively explain the limitations of previous work and motivate their design choices. The figures, particularly Figure 1 and Figure 2, are instrumental in helping the reader understand the complex six-RNN architecture.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Weak Quantitative Comparison and Lack of Statistical Testing:** The central claim of achieving state-of-the-art performance is not strongly supported by the quantitative results. The final BLEU scores for DCNMT are very close to those of other character-level models like `bpe2char` and `char2char` (e.g., 16.96 vs 16.86 on En-Cs; 22.48 vs 22.42/22.46 on Cs-En). Given these small margins, the absence of any statistical significance testing makes it impossible to conclude that DCNMT is genuinely superior. The claim of competitive performance is tenable, but claims of outperforming others are not justified.

2.  **Incomplete and Potentially Misleading Efficiency Comparison:** The comparison of training efficiency in Table 1 is incomplete. For En-Fr, there is no comparison against other character-level models. For En-Cs, the training time for the `bpe2char` model is not reported. Most notably, for Cs-En, the `bpe2char` model is reported to train in 14 days, which is 8 days *faster* than the 22 days reported for DCNMT. This contradicts the general claim of superior training efficiency. To make a convincing case, a more thorough and consistent comparison is needed.

3.  **Unsupported Claims about Learning Morphology:** The claims that the model "learns morphology" are based on anecdotal evidence and visualizations (Section 5.2). While the PCA plots and energy diagrams are interesting, they are selective and not a substitute for formal experimentation. The analysis does not constitute rigorous proof that the model has learned generalizable morphological rules. These strong claims should be softened, or supported with more systematic quantitative evaluations (e.g., using morphology-focused test suites or probing tasks). As it stands, this analysis is more suited for an appendix than as a core result supporting the paper's main claims.

4.  **Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The paper has several minor but distracting presentation issues:
    *   Many acronyms are used before they are defined (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN). BPE is capitalized inconsistently (bpe vs BPE).
    *   The term "energy" is used in Section 5.2 without a clear definition or re-introduction from the model description. The meaning of "peak energy" in Figure 5 is also unclear.
    *   The description of French as "morphologically poor" is an oversimplification and should be phrased more carefully.
    *   Figure 4a appears to have a rendering issue, as the color mapping for energy is missing for several characters.
    *   The paper states it uses the WMT'14 dataset for En-Fr but provides no link. Several references in the bibliography are incomplete.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide statistical significance tests for your BLEU score comparisons, particularly against the `bpe2char` and `char2char` models, to substantiate the claims of comparable or superior performance?

2.  To make the efficiency comparison in Table 1 more complete, could you clarify the training time for `bpe2char` on En-Cs and ideally provide results for a character-level baseline (like `bpe2char`) on the En-Fr task? Could you also comment on why your model was significantly slower to train than the baseline on the Cs-En task?

3.  The results for the `bpe2char` model (Chung et al., 2016b) on Cs-En are not reported in the cited paper. Did you run this experiment yourselves? If so, this should be clearly stated to ensure proper attribution and reproducibility.
