### Summary of the paper and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical neural machine translation (NMT) model that operates entirely at the character level, thus eliminating the need for a fixed word vocabulary and inherently handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. The core of the model is a novel word encoder composed of two RNNs that learns morphological representations by computing a weighted sum of character-level hidden states. This is paired with a hierarchical decoder that first predicts a word-level representation and then uses a second-level RNN to generate the corresponding character sequence. The full architecture consists of six RNNs. The authors evaluate their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against other character- and subword-level models and showing strong qualitative evidence of learning morphology.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT:** The model effectively bridges pure character-level and word-level approaches. By learning intermediate representations that correspond to words and morphemes, it aims to capture the compositional structure of language while maintaining the flexibility of a character-based system.
2.  **Strong empirical results and impressive training efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with other character-level systems. A particularly notable finding is its ability to reach a high level of performance after just a single training epoch, in some cases outperforming the final scores of a BPE-based baseline.
3.  **Effective morphological learning and generalization:** The paper provides compelling qualitative analyses showing that the proposed word encoder learns plausible morphological segmentations and produces meaningful representations. This allows the model to successfully translate misspelled words and unseen nonce words, a key advantage over systems reliant on a fixed vocabulary.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The proposed architecture is a creative and well-reasoned approach to the character-level NMT problem. Instead of treating a sentence as a flat sequence of characters, the model imposes a hierarchical structure that mirrors linguistic compositionality (characters to morphemes to words). The word encoder, which learns to weigh character-level RNN states to form a word vector, is an interesting alternative to standard CNN or simple RNN encoders and appears effective at capturing morphology. This is a solid contribution to the literature on character-aware neural models.

2.  **Impressive Training Speed and Performance:** The claim of training efficiency is well-supported by the results in Table 1. Achieving a BLEU score on En-Fr and En-Cs after just one epoch that surpasses the final score of the BPE baseline is a significant practical advantage. This suggests the model's inductive bias for morphology helps it learn much faster. The final BLEU scores are strong and place the work competitively within the sub-field of character- and subword-level NMT.

3.  **Convincing Qualitative Analysis of Morphological Learning:** Sections 5.2 and 5.3 are a major strength of the paper. The PCA plots (Figure 3) and morpheme energy visualizations (Figure 4) provide intuitive and compelling evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful sub-word structure, rather than just memorizing character sequences. Furthermore, the examples in Table 2, showing the model's ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words like "convenienter," are a powerful demonstration of the practical benefits of this approach. This goes beyond simply reporting BLEU scores and gives insight into *why* the model works.

4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for planning to release their code and trained models for all three language pairs. This greatly enhances the value of the work, allowing other researchers to verify the results, build upon the model, and apply it to new tasks.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Model Architecture Description:** The paper's primary weakness is the unclear presentation of its central contribution—the model architecture. The introduction describes a six-level hierarchy ("character→ subword→ word→ source sentence → target word → target character") and the model is said to contain six RNNs, but Figure 2 does not clearly map to this description. It is very difficult to trace the complete data flow and understand how all six RNNs interact. A more detailed and comprehensive diagram of the full architecture is essential for reader comprehension and reproducibility.

2.  **Incomplete Comparison to the State-of-the-Art:** While the comparisons to other character-level and BPE models are relevant, the paper omits any comparison to the overall state-of-the-art NMT systems from the relevant WMT shared tasks. The claim of being "comparable to the state-of-the-art systems" is therefore not fully substantiated, as top-performing systems at the time (even if BPE-based) achieved significantly higher BLEU scores. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging these scores and positioning its contribution more precisely—as a highly competitive model within the character-level paradigm with unique benefits (OOV handling, morphological awareness), rather than as a contender for the absolute highest BLEU score overall.

3.  **Issues with Experimental Details and Reproducibility:** While the intent to release code is a strength, the current submission has some clarity gaps. The specific WMT development and test sets used should be stated more prominently (e.g., in the caption of Table 1) for immediate clarity. The reviewer's notes also indicate that the associated software package is difficult to use, lacking clear runnable examples or a help flag. For the code release to be truly valuable, it must be accompanied by sufficient documentation to allow others to run it.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor but distracting English disfluencies, typos, and LaTeX formatting errors (e.g., reversed quotation marks). These issues detract from the overall quality of the presentation and should be corrected with a careful proofreading pass.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed architectural diagram that clearly illustrates all six RNNs and the flow of information between them? This would greatly clarify the complex structure described in the text and shown partially in Figure 2.

2.  Your results are compared to other character-level models. Could you please contextualize your BLEU scores by discussing how they compare to the top-performing non-character-level systems from the corresponding WMT evaluation campaigns (e.g., results on matrix.statmt.org)?

3.  The source word encoder uses an unnormalized, weighted sum of hidden states passed through a `tanh` activation: `tanh(Σ w_t * r_t)`. This is an unconventional choice compared to a standard softmax-normalized attention mechanism. Could you provide some intuition or ablation results justifying this design? Did you experiment with a more standard attention formulation for the word encoder?
