- Proposes DCNMT, a fully character-based NMT with a four-layer, six-RNN architecture that avoids sub-word segmentation.  
- Core components: a novel character-level word encoder that weights character sequences and a hierarchical character-level decoder (HGRU).  
- Evaluated on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En; claims competitive BLEU, training efficiency, and robustness to OOV/misspellings.  
- Contribution: introduces a novel end-to-end character-level NMT architecture that aims to discover sub-word units automatically.  
- Contribution: provides qualitative analyses suggesting the encoder captures morphological/semantic similarities and handles nonce/misspelled words.  
- Strength: original and well-motivated model design; weighted hidden-state encoder and hierarchical decoder are intuitive innovations.  
- Strength: paper is generally clear and well-structured with effective figures illustrating the architecture and data flow.  
- Strength: qualitative examples (nonce words, misspellings) and visualizations offer intriguing insights into learned representations.  
- Weakness: empirical validation is insufficient; BLEU improvements over bpe2char are marginal and lack statistical significance.  
- Weakness: efficiency claims are undercut by incomplete and inconsistent training-time comparisons across baselines and tasks.  
- Weakness: claims of learning morphology are unsubstantiated—analyses are anecdotal, based on hand-picked examples and PCA/energy plots.  
- Weakness: needs formal morphological evaluation or probing tasks; current analysis should be reframed as qualitative or moved to an appendix.  
- Weakness: clarity and reproducibility issues—undefined acronyms, unclear definition/interpretation of “energy,” and figure/table problems.  
- Weakness: writing and formatting problems—typos, inconsistent capitalization (e.g., BPE), imprecise language about language morphology, incomplete references, and missing dataset links.
