### **Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"**

**Summary and Contributions**
This paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that operates on characters at the input and output layers but learns morpheme-like representations internally. Using a hierarchical structure with six recurrent networks, the model aims to learn morphology automatically, thereby eliminating the need for a fixed vocabulary and better handling rare or unseen words.

As I see them, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel deep NMT architecture that combines character-level processing with an intermediate, learned morphological representation, offering a new way to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this character-level model can achieve competitive translation quality, learn plausible morphological segmentations, and generalize to nonce words.
3.  The public release of source code and pre-trained models, which is a valuable contribution to reproducibility in the field.

**Strengths**
1.  The core architectural innovation is the paper's greatest strength. By learning subword units automatically within an end-to-end character-level framework, the model presents an elegant solution to the OOV problem without relying on pre-processing steps like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE).
2.  The model demonstrates strong empirical performance. It achieves reasonable translation quality after only a single training epoch and shows a clear ability to handle morphological variations and even unseen nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"), providing compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.
3.  The authors' commitment to open science through the release of their code and models is commendable. This significantly lowers the barrier for other researchers to verify, replicate, and extend this work.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The experimental comparison is insufficient. The evaluation omits comparisons to state-of-the-art non-character-based NMT systems. To properly assess the model's standing, it must be benchmarked against the strongest contemporary baselines, not just other character-level methods. The paper should also acknowledge and discuss the higher scores reported on public leaderboards for the same WMT tasks.
2.  The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in key areas. Figure 2, meant to illustrate the core architecture, is confusing and does not appear to match the textual description of a 6-level model. Additionally, the specific test and development sets are not explicitly named in the paper itself, a critical omission for rigorous experimental reporting.
3.  While the release of code is a strength, its usability is subpar. The README reportedly lacks clear, runnable examples, and the software does not support a standard --help flag, hindering practical reproducibility and ease of use. Minor but frequent English disfluencies and formatting errors also detract from the paper's polish.
