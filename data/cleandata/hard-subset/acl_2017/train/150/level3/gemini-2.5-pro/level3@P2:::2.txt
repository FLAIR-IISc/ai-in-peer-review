### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, DCNMT, that operates without a fixed vocabulary. The architecture is hierarchical, composing representations from characters to subwords, to words, and finally to the source sentence. The model consists of six recurrent neural networks in total. A key component is a novel word encoder built from two RNNs, which the authors claim learns morphological representations of words. On the decoder side, a hierarchical structure with a custom Gated Recurrent Unit (HGRU) is used to generate target sentences character by character, conditioned on word-level states. The authors evaluate their model on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, showing competitive BLEU scores and faster initial training convergence compared to a BPE-based baseline.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The paper introduces a deep, fully character-to-character architecture that integrates sub-word level representation learning within an end-to-end framework. The design, particularly the two-level decoder (word-level to character-level), is an interesting and original approach to making deep character-level models more structured and efficient.
2.  **Empirical Demonstration of a Purely Character-Based Model:** The work provides a strong empirical result showing that a purely character-level model can achieve BLEU scores competitive with state-of-the-art subword-based (BPE) and other character-aware NMT systems on both morphologically rich and less-rich language pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed DCNMT architecture is original and thoughtfully designed. The hierarchical approach (character → word → sentence → word → character) is a principled way to manage the long dependencies inherent in character-level NMT. The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and mechanics of the novel word encoder and the hierarchical decoder, aided by helpful figures. This clarity makes a complex six-RNN model understandable.

2.  **Effectively Addresses the Open-Vocabulary Problem:** By operating directly on characters, the model elegantly sidesteps the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem that plagues word-level models. The qualitative examples in Table 2, where the model successfully translates misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words ("convenienter"), provide compelling evidence for the practical benefits of this character-level approach. This is a significant advantage over models that rely on fixed vocabularies or subword tokenization schemes like BPE.

3.  **Strong and Efficient Initial Learning:** The results in Table 1 show that DCNMT achieves very competitive BLEU scores after just one epoch of training (e.g., outperforming the final BPE baseline score on En-Fr and En-Cs). This suggests that the model's architecture is highly effective at capturing relevant structure early in training, which is a strong argument for its efficiency and design.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unsupported Claims about Learning Morphology:** The central claim that the model "learns morphology" is not sufficiently supported by rigorous evidence. The analysis in Section 5.2 is entirely anecdotal and qualitative. The PCA plots (Figure 3) are illustrative but do not prove superiority over other character-aware encoders (e.g., CNNs). The "energy" plots (Figure 4) and claims of morphological segmentation (Figure 5) are based on visual inspection of an ambiguously defined "energy" metric, without any quantitative evaluation against gold-standard morphological analyses. These strong claims require more formal experiments to be substantiated.

2.  **Incomplete and Unconvincing Empirical Comparison:** The comparison to the key baseline, bpe2char (Chung et al., 2016b), is weak. The final BLEU score differences are marginal (e.g., +0.1 on En-Cs, +0.06 on Cs-En) and no statistical significance testing is reported, making it difficult to conclude that DCNMT is truly superior in translation quality. Furthermore, the claims of superior training efficiency are not fully supported by the data provided. DCNMT is significantly slower than bpe2char on Cs-En (22 days vs. 14 days), while training time for bpe2char is missing for En-Cs and the model is not evaluated at all on En-Fr. This incomplete comparison undermines the claims about the model's advantages.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from a number of presentation issues that detract from its overall quality.
    *   Many acronyms are used without being defined first (e.g., HGRU, CNN, PCA, BPE, LSTM).
    *   The term "energy" is central to the analysis in Section 5.2 but is never formally defined, making Figures 4 and 5 difficult to interpret.
    *   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the text. References are incomplete.
    *   The description of French as "morphologically poor" is imprecise and should be rephrased more carefully.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The BLEU score differences between your model and the bpe2char baseline are quite small. Have you performed statistical significance tests to validate that your model's improvements are statistically significant? To provide a complete picture of training efficiency, could you fill in the missing training time for bpe2char on En->Cs and also provide a comparison for the En->Fr task?

2.  The claims about learning morphology are a key motivation for your architecture. Could you elaborate on why the qualitative evidence provided is a stronger indicator of morphological learning than what might be observed in other character-level encoders (e.g., CNNs)? Have you considered a quantitative evaluation for the subword segmentation shown in Figure 5, for instance, by comparing against a gold-standard morphological parser?

3.  In Table 1, you report results for the bpe2char model on Cs-En translation. However, the cited paper (Chung et al., 2016b) does not appear to include results for this language direction. Could you clarify if you ran this experiment yourselves or the source of this result?

4.  Could you please provide a more formal definition for the "energy" metric used in your analysis in Section 5.2 and explain precisely how "peak energy" is identified and used to determine the subword boundaries shown in Figure 5?
