### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) model, a fully character-based system that avoids explicit sub-word segmentation methods like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE). The architecture is composed of four layers and six recurrent neural networks. Its core components are a novel word encoder that learns weighted representations of character sequences to model morphology, and a hierarchical decoder that generates target words character-by-character. The authors evaluate the model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, claiming it achieves competitive BLEU scores, trains efficiently, and successfully learns morphology, enabling it to handle out-of-vocabulary and misspelled words.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Deep Character-Level NMT Architecture:** The primary contribution is the DCNMT architecture itself. The design, which combines a character-level word encoder that learns to weight character prefixes (termed "energy") with a hierarchical character-level decoder (HGRU), is a novel approach for building an end-to-end character-based translation system. This method attempts to automatically discover sub-word units instead of relying on pre-processing steps like BPE.
2.  **Qualitative Analysis of Morphological Learning:** The paper provides an interesting qualitative analysis suggesting that the proposed word encoder learns representations that capture morphological and semantic similarities. The demonstration of the model's ability to translate misspelled words and unseen morphological variants (nonce words) is a compelling, albeit anecdotal, contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Model Design:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture. The word encoder's mechanism for learning sub-word representations via weighted hidden states is an original and intuitive idea for capturing morphology from raw characters. The hierarchical decoder is also a clever method for structuring character-level generation, aiming to retain the efficiency of word-level decoding while operating on characters.
2.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making the complex six-RNN architecture reasonably easy to follow. Figures 1 and 2 are particularly effective at illustrating the core components of the model and the overall data flow during training.
3.  **Interesting Qualitative Results:** The analyses in Sections 5.2 and 5.3, while not rigorously validated, provide compelling examples of the model's capabilities. The ability to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" and handle misspellings is a significant potential advantage of this type of model, and the visualizations offer intriguing insights into what the model might be learning internally.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation of Performance and Efficiency:** The central claims of superior performance and efficiency are not fully supported by the experiments.
    *   **Marginal BLEU Gains:** The BLEU score improvements over the `bpe2char` baseline are very small (e.g., +0.1 on En-Cs and +0.06 on Cs-En). Without any statistical significance testing, it is impossible to conclude that DCNMT is actually better. Given the added complexity of the model, these marginal gains do not make a strong case for its superiority.
    *   **Incomplete Efficiency Comparison:** The efficiency claims are undermined by an incomplete comparison in Table 1. For Cs→En, DCNMT takes 22 days while `bpe2char` takes only 14. The training time for En→Cs is not reported for the `bpe2char` baseline, and `bpe2char` is not evaluated at all on En→Fr, making a comprehensive comparison impossible. A more thorough and fair comparison is required to substantiate the claim of efficiency.

2.  **Unsubstantiated Claims about Learning Morphology:** The paper repeatedly claims that the model "is able to learn morphology," but this strong claim is supported only by anecdotal evidence and visualizations.
    *   The analysis in Section 5.2 is interesting but not a formal evaluation. The PCA plots and "energy" visualizations are based on a few hand-picked examples. This does not constitute rigorous proof of morphological learning.
    *   To validate such a claim, the authors should conduct formal experiments, for instance, by evaluating on a morphologically annotated test set or designing specific probing tasks. As presented, this section feels more like a preliminary exploration, and its strong claims should be significantly softened. I would recommend moving this analysis to an appendix or reframing it as a qualitative case study. The investigation itself is valuable and could form the basis of a separate, more focused paper.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from numerous small but collectively significant issues that hinder readability and reproducibility.
    *   **Undefined Acronyms:** Many standard acronyms (e.g., RNN, LSTM, CNN, BPE, PCA) are used before being defined, or are never defined at all.
    *   **Unclear Terminology:** The concept of "energy" is central to the word encoder but is not clearly defined upon introduction and is only explained via its computation. In Section 5.2, the text needs to better explain what this "energy" represents and how to interpret the peaks in Figure 5.
    *   **Figure and Table Issues:** The color mapping in Figure 4a appears incorrect or missing for some characters. In Table 1, the Cs→En results for Chung et al. (2016) are cited, but this result does not appear in the referenced paper; the authors should clarify if they reproduced this result themselves.
    *   **Writing and Formatting:** There are multiple grammatical errors and typos. Capitalization of "BPE" is inconsistent. Phrasing like calling French "morphologically poor" should be rephrased more carefully (e.g., "less morphologically complex than Czech"). Finally, several references in the bibliography are incomplete, and the link to the WMT'14 corpora should be provided for reproducibility.
