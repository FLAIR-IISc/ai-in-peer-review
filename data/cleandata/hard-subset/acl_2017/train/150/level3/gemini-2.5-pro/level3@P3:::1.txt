### Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model with a novel encoder-decoder architecture. The model processes input and generates output at the character level, but internally, it constructs intermediate representations corresponding to morphemes and words. The source-side encoder uses two recurrent networks to learn a weighted representation of each word from its characters, aiming to capture morphological structure. The decoder is hierarchical, consisting of a word-level RNN whose state initializes a character-level RNN, which then generates the character sequence for a target word. The full architecture is deep, comprising six RNNs across four layers. The authors evaluate their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, showing that it achieves competitive BLEU scores compared to other character- and subword-level models. The paper also includes qualitative analyses suggesting the model learns plausible morphological segmentations and can handle out-of-vocabulary phenomena like misspellings and nonce words.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The primary contribution is the specific six-RNN architecture that bridges character-level operations with word-level semantics. The source-side word encoder, which learns to compose character representations into a word vector, and the hierarchical decoder, which uses a word-level state to drive a character-level generator, are both innovative. This design elegantly tackles the OOV problem without suffering from the excessively long sequences that challenge naive character-to-character models.

2.  **Publicly Released Code and Models for Reproducibility:** A significant contribution to the community is the release of the source code and final trained models for all three language pairs. This greatly enhances the paper's value by enabling direct replication of results and providing a strong foundation for future research in character-level NMT.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration of Morphological Learning and OOV Handling:** The paper provides compelling evidence that a purely character-level model can learn meaningful subword units without explicit supervision. The analysis showing plausible morphological segmentation and the model's ability to correctly translate misspelled and nonce words effectively demonstrates a key advantage of this approach over traditional word-based systems.

### Strengths
1.  **Innovative and Well-Motivated Model Design:** The proposed architecture is a clever synthesis of character-level and word-level modeling. By starting and ending with characters but building word-like representations in between, it attempts to gain the benefits of both: open vocabulary from the former and more manageable sequence lengths from the latter. The model's ability to outperform a BPE baseline after only a single epoch suggests this structure may indeed accelerate learning.

2.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors should be commended for releasing their code and pre-trained models. This is a crucial practice for advancing the field, allowing other researchers to verify the claims, analyze the model's behavior, and build upon the work. This makes the contribution more impactful and long-lasting.

3.  **Strong Qualitative and Efficiency Analysis:** The analyses in sections 5.2 and 5.3 provide convincing qualitative evidence that the model is learning what it is intended to learn—namely, morphology. The examples of translating misspelled and nonce words are particularly effective. Furthermore, the paper makes a good case for the model's efficiency relative to other character-level approaches, both in terms of training time and parameter count.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Systems:** The paper's experimental comparisons are limited to other subword or character-level models. It omits any comparison or discussion of the top-performing systems on the WMT 2015 tasks, which achieved substantially higher BLEU scores using word or BPE-level units. While the paper's focus is on advancing character-level methods, failing to acknowledge the performance gap with the overall state-of-the-art makes it difficult to assess the practical trade-offs of this approach. A more complete picture would situate these results within the broader NMT landscape.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Presentation and Experimental Details:**
    *   **Figure 2 is unclear:** This figure is central to understanding the full architecture but is difficult to interpret. It does not clearly depict the six RNNs or the four-layer structure described in the text. A more detailed diagram, even if placed in an appendix, would be invaluable for helping the reader grasp the model's complex data flow.
    *   **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper fails to state precisely which WMT test sets are used for each language pair in the main text (e.g., `newstest2015` for En-Fr, `newstest2014` for Cs-En, etc.). This critical information is relegated to the code's README, but it is essential for reproducibility and should be stated explicitly in the Experiments section.

3.  **Software Usability Issues:** While the release of the code is a major strength, its usability could be improved. The README lacks a simple, copy-pasteable example for running inference on new data. Furthermore, the command-line tool does not support a standard `--help` flag, which forces users to read the source code to understand its functionality. These are minor but important practical details that affect the utility of the software release.

4.  **Minor Writing and Formatting Issues:** The paper contains several minor grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and LaTeX formatting mistakes (e.g., reversed quotation marks). These issues, while not affecting the core scientific contribution, detract from the paper's overall polish and should be addressed in a final version.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the architecture depicted in Figure 2? It seems to show fewer than the six RNNs described. A more detailed diagram showing the connections between the four layers (source character->word, source word->sentence, target word->word, target word->character) would be very helpful.

2.  Your BLEU scores are compared against other character/subword models. Could you comment on how these results compare to the overall state-of-the-art systems from the WMT15 shared task (e.g., from matrix.statmt.org)? A brief discussion of this context would help readers better understand the trade-offs of your approach.

3.  Regarding the software release, would you consider adding a `--help` argument to the main script and including a minimal, runnable example in the README? This would significantly improve its usability for other researchers wishing to test or build upon your work.
