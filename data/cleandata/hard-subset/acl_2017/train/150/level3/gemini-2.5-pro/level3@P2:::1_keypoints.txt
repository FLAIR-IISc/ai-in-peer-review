- Novel hierarchical character-level NMT: a source word encoder uses two RNNs to compose weighted character-prefix representations to implicitly learn morphology.  
- Efficient deep character-to-character system: achieves strong BLEU scores very quickly, notably after a single training epoch.  
- Qualitative morphological learning evidence: analyses show plausible morphological segmentations and correct translation of unseen nonce words.  
- Strength — motivated architecture: dual-RNN prefix-weighting is a principled, intuitive approach to modeling morphemes without pre-segmentation.  
- Strength — training efficiency: outperforms or matches baselines early in training, offering practical speed advantages for deep character models.  
- Strength — OOV/nonce handling: convincingly translates misspellings and novel morphological forms, demonstrating generalization.  
- Strength — reproducibility: authors released source code and trained models.  
- Weakness — limited SOTA comparison: experiments omit strong contemporary word-level/hybrid WMT systems, weakening claims of being comparable to state-of-the-art.  
- Weakness — unclear model description/figures: dense diagrams (Figure 2) and insufficient depiction of component roles (e.g., target word encoder) hinder understanding.  
- Weakness — missing experimental details: specific development and test sets used are not specified in the paper.  
- Weakness — minor presentation issues: grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and LaTeX formatting problems.  
- Question — clarify architecture: provide a simplified block diagram showing all six RNNs and their interconnections/roles.  
- Question — contextualize BLEU: compare results to top word-level systems from WMT 2014/2015 for the evaluated language pairs.  
- Question — specify datasets: explicitly state which dev and test sets were used for each language pair (e.g., newstest2013, newstest2015).
