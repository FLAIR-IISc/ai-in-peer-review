- Proposes a deep character-level NMT with a novel six-RNN, four-layer encoder–decoder that builds intermediate morpheme/word representations.  
- Source encoder composes word vectors from characters using two recurrent networks; decoder is hierarchical: word-level RNN initializes a character-level generator.  
- Evaluated on En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En and achieves competitive BLEU vs. other character/subword models.  
- Qualitative analyses show the model learns plausible morphological segmentation and handles misspellings and nonce/OOV words.  
- Main contribution: the specific hierarchical six-RNN architecture bridging character and word semantics.  
- Contribution: publicly released code and trained models for all three language pairs.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that character-level models can learn subword units and robustly handle OOV phenomena.  
- Strength: innovative, well-motivated model combining open vocabulary and manageable sequence lengths; fast early learning (beats BPE after one epoch).  
- Strength: strong commitment to reproducibility via code and model release.  
- Strength: convincing qualitative analyses and claims of efficiency (training time and parameter count) relative to other character approaches.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to overall WMT15 state-of-the-art systems, leaving practical performance trade-offs unclear.  
- Weakness: presentation issues—Figure 2 is unclear and does not clearly depict the six RNNs/four layers.  
- Weakness: missing experimental details in the paper (which WMT test sets were used), relegated to the README.  
- Weakness: software usability problems—README lacks runnable example and main tool lacks a standard --help flag.  
- Weakness: minor grammatical and LaTeX formatting errors that reduce polish.
