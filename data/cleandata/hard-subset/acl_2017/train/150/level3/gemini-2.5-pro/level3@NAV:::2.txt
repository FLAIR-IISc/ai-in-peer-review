**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) model designed to learn morphology implicitly. The primary contribution is a novel architecture featuring a deep word encoder, which uses two recurrent networks to generate weighted character-based representations, and a hierarchical character-level decoder. This design allows the model to operate entirely on characters, thus eliminating the out-of-vocabulary problem, while attempting to capture sub-word structure automatically without predefined segmentation like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE).

**Strengths**

The paper is well-structured and presents an original and interesting architecture. The core idea of using a weighted sum of character-level RNN states to form word representations is novel and provides a plausible mechanism for learning sub-word units. The qualitative results are a key strength; the examples in Table 2, which show the model successfully translating misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"), effectively showcase the practical benefits of the proposed character-level approach over traditional word- or subword-level systems.

**Weaknesses**

The submission's most significant weakness is its set of strong claims about learning morphology, which are not substantiated by rigorous evidence. The analysis in Section 5.2 is anecdotal, relying on a few selected examples and PCA visualizations. To support such a central claim, formal experiments quantitatively measuring morphological awareness are necessary. Without such evidence, the claims should be significantly softened, and this analysis is better suited for an appendix.

Secondly, the empirical results are not convincing enough to demonstrate the model's superiority over strong baselines. The reported BLEU score differences with the bpe2char model are marginal (e.g., +0.1 BLEU on En→Cs) and are presented without any statistical significance testing, making it impossible to ascertain if these minor gains are meaningful. Furthermore, the training-time comparison is incomplete, with crucial data points missing for the bpe2char baseline, which undermines the claims of DCNMT's efficiency advantages.

Finally, the paper suffers from numerous presentation issues that detract from its clarity and professionalism. Multiple acronyms (BPE, LSTM, CNN) are undefined or used before definition, capitalization of "BPE" is inconsistent, and several figures and table entries lack necessary clarification (e.g., Figure 4a's color map, the source of results in Table 1). These issues, combined with several typos and incomplete references, need to be thoroughly addressed.
