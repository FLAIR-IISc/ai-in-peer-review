### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-to-character neural machine translation (NMT) model with a novel hierarchical architecture designed to implicitly learn morphology. The core of the model is a source-side word encoder that uses two recurrent networks to compose word representations from characters, learning a weighted combination of character prefixes. This is hypothesized to capture morphological structure. On the target side, a hierarchical decoder uses a word-level RNN to guide a character-level RNN, generating the translation one character at a time. The full model comprises six RNNs. The authors evaluate their system on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation, demonstrating competitive performance against other character-level and subword-level models, notable training efficiency, and an ability to handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) and nonce words.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel hierarchical character-level NMT architecture**, especially the source word encoder that learns to represent words by weighting character prefixes. This provides a compelling method for modeling morphology without relying on explicit pre-segmentation, representing the main technical novelty of the paper.
2.  **An effective and efficient deep character-to-character NMT system.** The model achieves strong BLEU scores after just one epoch of training, highlighting a significant practical advantage in training efficiency compared to other complex character-level models.
3.  **Strong qualitative evidence of morphological learning.** The paper presents compelling analyses and examples showing that the model learns plausible morphological segmentations and can correctly translate unseen words with known morphological patterns (e.g., "convenienter"), demonstrating a robust solution to the OOV problem.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture for Morphological Learning:** The proposed source word encoder is a creative and intuitive approach to learning sub-word representations directly from character sequences. Unlike methods that rely on fixed segmentation (e.g., BPE) or standard character RNNs/CNNs, the dual-RNN design that computes a weighted sum of prefix representations (Figure 1) is a principled way to model morphemes implicitly. The qualitative analyses in Figures 3, 4, and 5 provide strong supporting evidence that the model learns meaningful and well-structured representations that correlate with morphological units.

2.  **Impressive Training Efficiency:** A standout feature of the proposed model is its training efficiency. As shown in Table 1, the model achieves highly competitive BLEU scores after just a single training epoch, even outperforming the final BPE baseline on the En-Fr and En-Cs tasks. This rapid learning is a significant practical advantage, especially for deep character-level models which are often notoriously slow and difficult to train.

3.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** The paper provides excellent qualitative examples in Table 2 that showcase the model's ability to handle previously unseen words, including misspellings and novel morphological forms (e.g., translating "convenienter" into its correct French equivalent). This demonstrates a core benefit of character-level modeling that is particularly well-realized by this architecture, moving beyond simple OOV handling to show true generalization over morphological rules.

4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors have released their source code and trained models. This is a commendable practice that significantly strengthens the paper's contribution to the community, as it allows for independent verification of the results and facilitates future research building upon this model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inadequate Comparison to the State-of-the-Art:** The experimental comparison in Table 1 is primarily restricted to other character-level or subword-level models. It conspicuously omits comparisons to strong contemporary word-level or hybrid systems, including the top-performing systems from the relevant WMT shared tasks. This makes it difficult to assess the claim that the model is "comparable to the state-of-the-art systems" in the broader NMT landscape. To properly contextualize the performance, a comparison against a wider range of top systems is necessary.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Model Description and Figures:** The overall architecture is difficult to fully grasp from the text and diagrams. The paper mentions a six-RNN, four-layer architecture, but the main diagram (Figure 2) is dense and hard to parse, making it challenging to understand the data flow and the precise role of each component. For instance, the target word encoder is not clearly depicted. A simplified, high-level block diagram illustrating the connections between the six RNNs would greatly improve the paper's clarity.

3.  **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper fails to explicitly state which specific development and test sets were used for evaluation (e.g., newstest2013, newstest2015). While this information may be available in the supplementary code, it is critical for reproducibility and fair comparison that these details be included in the main paper.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and LaTeX formatting issues (e.g., incorrect quotation marks). While not a major flaw, these issues detract from the paper's professionalism and should be addressed in a final version.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the full architecture with a simplified block diagram that clearly illustrates how all six RNNs are interconnected and what specific role each one plays?
2.  Could you provide some context on how your model's BLEU scores compare to the top-performing word-level systems from the WMT 2014 and WMT 2015 evaluations for the language pairs tested? This would help situate your work more accurately within the state-of-the-art at the time.
3.  For the sake of clarity and reproducibility, could you please explicitly state in the paper which specific dev and test sets (e.g., newstest2013, newstest2015) were used for your experiments on each language pair?
