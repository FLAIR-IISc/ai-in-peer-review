## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that leverages character-level processing with an intermediate morphological representation. The core idea is to avoid large vocabularies by operating at the character level, while still capturing sub-word information through a learned morphological structure. The authors release code and models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, which is a significant contribution to reproducibility. The system demonstrates reasonable translation quality even with limited training and improves with more epochs, and analyses suggest the model learns plausible morphological tokenizations, enabling handling of unseen words.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies in the *combination* of character-level NMT with an explicit attempt to model morphology within the encoder. While character-level NMT is not new, the introduction of the two-RNN encoder to learn morpheme representations and weights is a noteworthy architectural choice. The second key contribution is the release of code and trained models, facilitating further research and comparison. Finally, the demonstration that the model can learn meaningful morphological representations and handle OOV words is a valuable empirical finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architectural Design:** The proposed six-layer architecture, incorporating a morphological encoder, offers a unique approach to character-level NMT. It attempts to bridge the gap between purely character-based and word-based models, potentially benefiting from both.
2. **Reproducibility:** The release of code and pre-trained models is a major strength, enabling other researchers to build upon this work and conduct fair comparisons. This is particularly important in the rapidly evolving field of NMT.
3. **Early Performance & Morphological Learning:** The reported BLEU scores, particularly the improvement after just one epoch, are encouraging. The analyses demonstrating the model's ability to learn morphological representations adds credibility to the approach.
4. **Handling of OOV Words:** The inherent nature of character-level models allows for better handling of out-of-vocabulary words, a common challenge in NMT. The paper provides evidence supporting this benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Missing Dataset Details:** The paper fails to explicitly state which WMT test/dev sets were used, relying instead on the README. This is a critical omission and should be corrected for clarity and reproducibility.
2. **Poor Software Documentation:** The README lacks clear runnable examples, and the tool does not support a `--help` flag. This significantly hinders usability and adoption of the released code.
3. **Unclear Architecture Diagram:** Figure 2 is insufficient to fully understand the six-layer architecture described in the text. A more detailed diagram, potentially in the appendix, is necessary.
4. **Limited Comparison:** The comparisons omit state-of-the-art non-character-based MT systems and do not acknowledge higher results reported on WMT/matrix.statmt.org. A discussion of these results is crucial for contextualizing the findings.
5. **Minor Polish Issues:** The paper contains minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX errors that detract from its overall presentation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you please explicitly state which WMT test/dev sets were used for evaluation?
2. Can you provide a more detailed diagram of the six-layer architecture, perhaps in the appendix?
3. How do the computational costs (training time, memory usage) of your model compare to other character-level and word-level NMT systems?
4. Could you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings?
