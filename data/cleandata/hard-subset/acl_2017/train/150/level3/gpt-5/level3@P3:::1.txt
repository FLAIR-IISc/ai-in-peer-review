Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture that begins and ends at characters while inducing morpheme- and word-level representations internally. A two-RNN word encoder assigns “energy” weights to prefix states to learn plausible morpheme boundaries and composition, and a hierarchical decoder uses a word-level RNN to condition a character-level HGRU, enabling efficient character decoding. The model claims fast learning (competitive BLEU after one epoch), competitive final BLEU on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, and improved robustness to OOVs, misspellings, and nonce forms. The authors release code and final models for three directions to aid reproducibility.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An architecture that couples a morphology-aware (two-RNN) word encoder with a hierarchical decoder that conditions character generation on word-level states; this gives an efficient, fully character-level MT system with a clear backoff path to morphemes for unseen words.
2) Empirical evidence that the model learns plausible morphological tokenizations and delivers reasonable BLEU after the first training epoch, improving with more epochs, while being competitive with prior character-level baselines.
3) Reproducibility assets: public code and final trained models for fr↔en and cs↔en directions.

Strengths
- Architectural novelty and clarity of intuition: The word encoder’s separation of representation and energy (morpheme weight) is an elegant way to induce morphology without BPE, and the hierarchical decoder design reuses word-level states to make character decoding practical. This is a substantive step in character-based NMT.
- Early-epoch effectiveness and training efficiency: The paper shows strong BLEU already after one epoch (e.g., outperforming a BPE baseline on En–Fr and En–Cs), and competitive final BLEU with a relatively small model. This is practically valuable for low-compute settings and fast iteration.
- Robustness to OOV and nonce forms: The system sensibly translates misspellings and nonce morphology (e.g., “convenienter”), demonstrating the intended backoff to morphemes and generalization beyond memorized subwords—an advantage over word- and fixed-subword systems.
- Analysis suggests learned morphology: PCA visualizations, energy profiles, and qualitative segmentations (e.g., consumers → consum-er-s) indicate the encoder captures morpheme boundaries and compositionality, supporting the central claim.
- Reproducibility: Code and trained models for fr–en, cs–en, and en–cs are released, which meaningfully lowers the barrier to replication and follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete comparison to non-character SOTA: The evaluation does not include strong non-character systems (e.g., state-of-the-art BPE-based NMT available at the time) nor does it contextualize results against the best WMT/matrix.statmt.org numbers. Claims of being “comparable to the state of the art” should be tempered, and a discussion of the gap to top systems is needed.
- Dataset/test-set clarity and evaluation protocol: The paper does not explicitly and prominently state the exact WMT dev/test sets and evaluation settings in the experimental overview (README suggests newstest2013/dev and newstest2015/test). The paper should clearly specify: WMT year, tokenization, case sensitivity, detokenization, BLEU script, beam size, length penalty, and stopping criteria. Without this, BLEU comparability is harder to assess.
- Figure 2 and architectural exposition: The text describes a six-RNN architecture, but Figure 2 and its caption appear to show fewer distinct layers/modules, and the interplay between the two source-side RNNs, the sentence encoder, the word-level decoder, and the HGRU is not visually complete. A full architecture diagram (even as an appendix figure) is necessary. The HGRU definition and the unfolding matrix R would also benefit from clearer, self-contained notation.
- Usability/documentation gaps: The software README lacks clear, end-to-end runnable examples (e.g., exact command lines to reproduce each reported number) and the tool lacks a --help flag. Given the emphasis on efficiency and fast convergence, providing scripts for data prep, training, and evaluation would substantially improve replicability.
- Writing/formatting quality: There are minor English disfluencies, typos, and LaTeX issues (e.g., reversed quotation marks, spacing/punctuation), which distract from the technical content. Polishing would improve readability and impact.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Please explicitly confirm the dev/test sets and evaluation details for each language pair: WMT year, tokenization/detokenization, case sensitivity, BLEU script, beam size, length penalty, and any normalization.
- Can you add comparisons to strong contemporary non-character systems (e.g., competitive BPE-based NMT) and discuss the gap to the best results on matrix.statmt.org for the same test sets?
- Could you provide a complete architecture figure that shows all six RNNs and the data flow, including the two source-side RNNs, sentence encoder, target word encoder, word-level decoder, and character-level HGRU, along with the unfolding mechanism?
- What is the ablation impact of the energy-weighted word encoder vs. a simple char-RNN encoder on both BLEU and OOV/nonce handling? Similarly, how crucial is the word-level feedback rYt−1 in Eq. (8)?
- What is the decoding speed in chars/sec and sentences/sec compared to BPE systems under your beam settings? Any trade-offs when increasing beam width?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of delimiter handling and the HGRU reset mechanism? Have you tried LSTM variants?

Additional comments
- Please add clear, runnable examples and a --help flag to the released tool, plus exact scripts to reproduce each table entry (including random seeds).
- Consider reporting statistical significance for BLEU improvements where claims of superiority are made.
- Calibrate claims of SOTA-comparability to acknowledge stronger results from non-character systems and discuss when character-level modeling is most advantageous (e.g., morphologically rich languages, OOV-heavy domains).

Overall evaluation
This is a valuable contribution to character-based NMT: the architecture is interesting, the empirical behavior is practical (fast early gains), and the analysis convincingly shows morphological learning and robustness to OOV/nonce forms. With clearer comparisons to non-character SOTA, improved clarity of the architecture presentation, and better documentation, this work would be a strong addition to the ACL program.
