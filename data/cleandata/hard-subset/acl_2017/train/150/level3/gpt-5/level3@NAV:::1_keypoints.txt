- Proposes a char-in/char-out NMT with a morpheme-aware word encoder and hierarchical character decoder (HGRU), forming a six-RNN stack that avoids large target vocabularies and learns plausible morphological segmentations with good early-epoch performance on En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En.

Contributions:
- Morpheme-aware word encoder composes words from characters via learned per-position weights, enabling plausible morphological tokenizations and back-off to subword structure for unseen forms.
- Hierarchical character decoder (HGRU) with settable state enables efficient batched character generation conditioned on word-level states.
- Open-source release of code and trained models for fr–en, cs–en, and en–cs.

Strengths:
- Conceptual advance: decoupling character composition and morpheme weighting learns morphology without explicit segmentation or BPE, supported by qualitative analyses.
- Robustness to OOV/nonce/misspellings: morpheme back-off improves coverage and yields reasonable translations for perturbed inputs.
- Training efficiency: achieves usable BLEU after the first epoch and converges faster than prior purely character-based baselines.
- Reproducibility: public code and released models across multiple language pairs.

Weaknesses:
- Dataset clarity: paper fails to explicitly state which WMT dev/test sets were used.
- Incomplete comparisons: omits strong non-character baselines and contextual WMT/matrix.statmt.org scores, weakening competitiveness claims.
- Architecture presentation unclear: Figure 2 and caption do not clearly show the claimed six RNNs or 6-level flow; a complete diagram is needed.
- Usability/reproducibility gaps: README lacks clear runnable examples and the tool provides no --help flag.
- Presentation quality: minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues distract from the paper.

Questions to authors:
- Specify the exact WMT dev/test sets and tokenization/evaluation protocol used for all reported numbers.
- Provide a full architecture figure showing all six RNNs and clarify HGRU/unfolding-matrix training mechanics.
- Provide ablations for (a) ordinary vs proposed word encoder, (b) hierarchical vs standard character decoder, and (c) resetting vs continuing character states.
- How does the model compare to strong non-character baselines trained on the same data (e.g., BPE-based Transformer or contemporary RNN systems)?
