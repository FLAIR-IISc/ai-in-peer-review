Summary
This paper proposes DCNMT, a character-level NMT architecture that introduces a word encoder intended to capture morphology and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters conditioned on word-level states. The authors claim DCNMT combines the advantages of character-level modeling with subword-like structure, improves training efficiency, and learns morphology better than prior approaches. Results on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show competitive BLEU with small gains in some settings.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A deep word encoding/decoding layer that composes characters into word representations and decodes words into characters, integrating character-level benefits with subword-like structure.
- Secondary: An empirical comparison showing DCNMT is competitive with strong character/subword baselines in BLEU and exhibits some efficiency advantages.
- Tertiary: Qualitative analyses suggesting DCNMT induces morpheme-like segmentations.

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured presentation of an original architecture (word encoder + hierarchical decoder) with an intuitive rationale.
- Competitive translation quality across multiple language pairs with relatively compact models; ability to handle OOVs and misspellings demonstrated via examples.
- Training efficiency claims are partially supported (e.g., faster than some fully-character baselines; smaller model size).
- The attempt to explicitly connect character composition to morphology is interesting and could inspire follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Claims about learning morphology are mostly anecdotal (Section 5.2) and not supported by formal evaluations; the “energy” notion is introduced without rigorous definition or validation.
- BLEU gains over bpe2char are very small; no statistical significance testing is reported, weakening the central efficacy claim.
- Incomplete baselines/time reporting: bpe2char is 8 days faster on Cs→En; En→Cs training time missing; En→Fr bpe2char not evaluated. A more complete bpe2char comparison is needed to substantiate advantages.
- Presentation issues: many acronyms undefined at first use (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE); inconsistent capitalization of BPE; unclear description of “energy” peaks and Figure 5; Figure 4a’s color mapping appears incorrect/missing for some characters.
- Table 1: Cs→En results for model (3) were not in the cited paper—clarify if authors recomputed them. References contain incomplete entries; avoid labeling French “morphologically poor.” Minor: the note “no monolingual corpus is used” seems unnecessary.

Questions to Authors
- Please report statistical significance for BLEU differences and add En→Fr bpe2char and En→Cs training-time comparisons.
- Precisely define “energy,” how it is computed/normalized, and what peaks indicate; clarify Figure 5 and fix Figure 4a mapping.
- Did you compute the Table 1 Cs→En results for (3) yourselves? If so, provide settings and code.
- Can you provide formal morphology evaluations (e.g., morpheme boundary F1, paradigm clustering, or probing tasks)?
- Please define all acronyms at first use, provide the WMT’14/’15 corpus links, fix incomplete references, and revise the “morphologically poor” phrasing.
