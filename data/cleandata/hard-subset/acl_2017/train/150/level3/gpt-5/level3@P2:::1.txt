Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT system that combines (i) a word encoder designed to learn morphology from characters via two RNNs (one producing prefix representations, one producing “energy” weights) and (ii) a hierarchical decoder that produces target text at the character level. The decoder includes a modified GRU (HGRU) whose state is periodically reset by word-level states, together with an efficient “unfolding” mechanism to allow single-pass batched training.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An effective hierarchical character-level NMT architecture with a novel word encoder and a batched, character-level decoder (HGRU + unfolding) that together address large-vocabulary/OOV issues while remaining efficient to train.
2) Empirical evidence that the model learns plausible morphology (PCA analyses, subword boundary detection) and is robust to misspellings and nonce words; it reaches competitive BLEU and surpasses a BPE baseline after one epoch.
3) Release of code and pretrained models for multiple language pairs, which supports reproducibility.

Strengths
- Strong design addressing OOV while retaining efficiency: The model translates purely at the character level, removing the large-vocabulary softmax bottleneck, yet maintains word-like conditioning via the hierarchical decoder. Despite using six RNNs, the reported parameter counts are modest relative to comparable char models, and the method trains quickly (e.g., surpassing BPE after one epoch on En–Fr/En–Cs).
- Clear, useful decoder innovation: The HGRU with reset-by-word-state plus the unfolding matrix is a practical contribution that enables efficient batched character-level decoding without two forward passes. This idea is likely reusable in other hierarchical generation tasks.
- Compelling qualitative analysis of morphology and robustness: PCA visualizations, subword boundary detection examples, and handling of misspellings/nonce words all support the claim that the model captures morphological regularities that aid generalization. The “convenienter” example nicely illustrates extrapolation from morphemes.
- Reproducibility: Code and final models for En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En are made available, which is valuable for the community and strengthens the paper’s impact beyond the reported numbers.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and potentially unfair empirical comparisons: The paper omits comparisons to strong non-character NMT systems and does not acknowledge higher BLEU results reported on WMT leaderboards (e.g., matrix.statmt.org) for the same test sets. Some baselines are cited from prior work rather than reimplemented under common settings, making cross-paper comparisons hard to interpret. Claims of efficiency would benefit from normalized, controlled measurements (tokens/sec, wall-clock to a fixed BLEU, beam sizes, identical data/length filters).
- Dataset/split specification is unclear: The manuscript does not clearly state which WMT dev/test sets are used (README suggests newstest2013 for dev and newstest2015 for test). This must be explicit in the paper to ensure comparability and avoid confusion.
- Architecture presentation lacks clarity: Figure 2 and its caption do not clearly expose the full “six RNNs” architecture; the visual shows fewer layers than the text suggests. Some notation choices (e.g., using tanh rather than a normalized attention for word-encoder weights; variable overloading in HGRU equations) may confuse readers. A more complete schematic (even in the appendix) would greatly help.
- Reproducibility/usability gaps in software: The README lacks a clear end-to-end runnable example and the tool does not support a --help flag. Practical details (preprocessing, character sets, beam settings, training schedules, hardware/time) are scattered and make it harder to replicate the reported results.
- Writing and formatting issues: Minor English disfluencies, typos, and LaTeX problems (e.g., reversed quotes) detract from readability and perceived polish.

Questions to authors
- Please specify precisely the training/dev/test splits (WMT years) for each language pair, any sentence-length filtering, and the exact number of training updates/epochs used for reported results.
- What are the beam search settings (beam size, length normalization, early stopping) for all systems? Are they identical across models?
- Can you provide controlled efficiency metrics (e.g., wall-clock time to reach a fixed BLEU, characters/sec, GPU type) for your model and a strong BPE baseline trained by you?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of character vocabulary size (e.g., 120 characters) and to the tanh weighting in the word encoder (vs softmax/normalization)?
- Do you have ablations isolating the contribution of the word encoder (vs ordinary RNN/CNN), and the HGRU/unfolding decoder (vs simpler alternatives)?
- How do you segment into “words” on the target side during training to define delimiters for the hierarchical decoder, given you claim no explicit segmentation? Is this simply whitespace-based tokenization?

Additional comments
- Please add a full architecture diagram labeling all six RNNs and data flows (character → subword/morpheme → word → sentence → word-state → character).
- Discuss and cite stronger non-character baselines and position your final BLEU relative to contemporaneous best WMT systems. Even a brief paragraph acknowledging the gap would calibrate expectations.
- Improve the software packaging: add a --help flag, a minimal working example with commands from raw data to BLEU, default configs, and beam parameters; ensure versioned dependencies.
- Proofread for English style and LaTeX typography; fix quote marks, equation numbering, and figure references.
- Consider releasing scripts that produce the morphology visualizations and subword boundary detections to support claims about learned morphology.
