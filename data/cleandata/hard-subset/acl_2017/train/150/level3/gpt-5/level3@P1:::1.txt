Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a hierarchical, deep character-level NMT system that avoids fixed word vocabularies by (i) learning word representations from characters with a morphology-aware word encoder and (ii) decoding to characters with a hierarchical decoder that conditions character generation on word-level states via a settable-state HGRU. The approach aims to alleviate large-vocabulary bottlenecks while retaining efficiency and robustness to OOVs.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Architecture: A two-part contribution combining a morphology-sensitive word encoder (two RNNs with learned positional “energy” weights over character prefixes) and a hierarchical character decoder (HGRU with word-level state resets and a batching-friendly unfolding mechanism). This is the primary novelty.
2) Empirical findings: Competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En, with notable data efficiency (bpe baseline matched/surpassed after one epoch) and qualitative evidence that the encoder captures morphemic structure; improved handling of nonce/misspelled words.
3) Reproducibility: Release of code and final models (Fr–En, Cs–En, En–Cs), facilitating verification and reuse.

Strengths
- Addresses vocabulary and OOV issues without external segmentation: Modeling purely at the character level while keeping word-level structure in the latent state neatly sidesteps large softmaxes and rare-word hacks. The HGRU with settable state and the unfolding trick make character-level decoding batched and practical, contrasting with earlier char models that required complex training schedules.
- Strong learning efficiency and competitive accuracy: The model surpasses a bpe baseline after a single epoch on En–Fr/En–Cs and reaches BLEU comparable to recent character-based systems with fewer parameters and shorter training time. On morphologically rich Czech, the approach is especially compelling, supporting the paper’s motivation.
- Evidence of morphological generalization: PCA plots and the “energy” weights align with plausible morpheme boundaries; the system correctly translates misspellings and nonce morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”), demonstrating robustness that is difficult for word-level models and often awkward for subword methods.
- Practical model size and training profile: Despite comprising six RNNs, the overall parameter count is moderate and training appears faster than some prior char-level systems (e.g., avoiding multi-month training runs). The hierarchical decoder avoids an explicit target word softmax, simplifying deployment.
- Code and model release: Availability of implementations and trained checkpoints increases confidence in reproducibility and lowers the barrier to follow-up research.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete positioning vs. state-of-the-art non-character systems: The paper claims to be comparable to state-of-the-art but does not systematically compare against the strongest contemporary WMT systems, nor does it contextualize reported BLEU scores with matrix.statmt.org/WMT leaderboard results. This weakens the strength of the “comparable to SOTA” claim.
- Missing or ambiguous experimental details: The paper text does not clearly specify which WMT dev/test sets are used; only the README indicates newstest2013 (dev) and newstest2015 (test). Important details such as beam size, character-level length penalties, early stopping criteria, and preprocessing beyond “usual tokenization” should be explicitly stated in the paper for reproducibility.
- Architectural clarity: Figure 2 and its caption do not clearly reflect the stated six-RNN architecture; the mapping between described layers (source char→subword→word→sentence→target word→target char) and the diagram is hard to follow. A more complete, labeled architecture diagram (possibly in an appendix) is necessary to make the design reproducible and unambiguous.
- Limited ablations and quantitative morphology evaluation: The paper presents qualitative plots and examples, but no quantitative evaluation of morphology learning (e.g., segmentation F-scores against a gold standard or probing tasks). Ablations isolating the effect of the energy-weighted word encoder, the target word feedback, and the HGRU vs. a standard char decoder would significantly strengthen the claims.
- Usability gaps in released software: The README reportedly lacks clear runnable examples and the tool does not support a --help flag. These issues will hinder reproducibility despite code release and should be remedied.

Questions to authors
- Please confirm the exact dev/test sets for all language pairs and describe preprocessing choices (tokenization, truecasing, normalization). What beam sizes and length penalties were used for character-level decoding?
- Can you provide ablation studies: (a) replace the energy-weighted word encoder with a simple last-state or CNN encoder; (b) replace HGRU with a standard GRU/LSTM decoder; (c) remove target word feedback rYt−1 in Eq. (8)? How do these changes affect BLEU, training speed, and robustness to nonce/misspelled words?
- What are the average training and decoding speeds (tokens or characters per second), GPU memory usage, and epoch times compared to your bpe baseline and to Lee et al. (2016)/Luong & Manning (2016)? Please include beam-search latency and any batching specifics for the unfolding matrix.
- How exactly is the unfolding matrix implemented in the computation graph? Is it differentiable w.r.t. word boundaries during teacher forcing? How do you handle exposure bias at word boundary prediction time?
- Could you contextualize your BLEU with respect to contemporaneous WMT best systems and discuss where your model stands relative to non-character NMT and phrase-based SOTA?
- Will you provide end-to-end training/decoding scripts, example commands, pretrained checkpoints, and add a command-line --help? What is the license and dependency stack?

Additional comments
- The manuscript would benefit from proofreading (minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues such as quotation marks). Clearer figure captions and consistent notation (e.g., variable indices, delimiter notation) would also help.
- If space permits, include an appendix with a fully annotated architecture diagram and pseudocode for training/decoding (including the HGRU reset logic and batching/unfolding scheme).

Overall recommendation
A valuable contribution to character-based NMT that proposes a practical hierarchical design with convincing efficiency and robustness benefits. With stronger positioning vs. SOTA, clearer experimental details and figures, and a set of ablations/quantitative morphology evaluations, this paper would make a solid addition to ACL.
