Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a character-in/character-out NMT architecture that learns morpheme-aware intermediate representations via a novel word encoder and a hierarchical character decoder (HGRU), yielding a “deep” six-RNN stack. The system avoids large target vocabularies, learns plausible morphological segmentations, and demonstrates reasonable early-epoch performance on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En.
Contributions (as I see them):
1) A morpheme-aware word encoder that composes words from characters via learned per-position weights, enabling plausible morphological tokenizations and back-off to subword structure for unseen/nonce forms.
2) A hierarchical character decoder with settable state (HGRU) that allows efficient batched character generation conditioned on word-level states.
3) Open-source release of code and trained models for fr–en, cs–en, and en–cs, enhancing reproducibility.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance in character-level NMT: The encoder’s decoupling of character composition and morpheme weighting is a principled way to learn morphology without explicit segmentation or BPE, supported by qualitative analyses (e.g., energy peaks at morpheme boundaries, handling of “convenienter”).
- Practical robustness to OOV/nonce/misspellings: Character-level generation with morpheme back-off improves coverage and produces reasonable translations for perturbed inputs, an important real-world advantage over word-level systems.
- Training efficiency and early quality: The model achieves usable BLEU after the first epoch and improves with more epochs, indicating that learning morphology accelerates convergence relative to purely character-based baselines noted as slow in prior work.
- Reproducibility: Public code and released final models across multiple language pairs lower the barrier for verification and extension.

Weaknesses
- Dataset clarity: The paper does not explicitly state which WMT dev/test sets are used; the README suggests newstest2013 (dev) and newstest2015 (test). This must be made explicit in the paper.
- Incomplete comparisons: The empirical section omits state-of-the-art non-character systems and does not contextualize results against higher WMT/matrix.statmt.org scores; this undercuts claims of competitiveness.
- Architecture presentation: Figure 2 and its caption are unclear; the text describes six RNNs and a 6-level flow, but the figure appears to show fewer layers. A complete architecture diagram (even in appendix) is needed.
- Usability/reproducibility gaps: The README lacks clear runnable examples and the tool provides no --help flag, making replication harder despite code release.
- Presentation quality: Minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues (e.g., reversed quotes) distract and should be corrected.

Questions to authors
- Please specify the exact WMT dev/test sets and tokenization/evaluation protocol used for all reported numbers.
- Provide a full architecture figure with all six RNNs and data flow, and clarify the HGRU/unfolding-matrix training mechanics.
- Include ablations: (a) ordinary vs proposed word encoder, (b) hierarchical vs standard character decoder, and (c) effect of resetting vs continuing character states.
- How does the model perform compared to strong non-character baselines trained on the same data (e.g., BPE-based Transformer or contemporary RNN systems)?
