Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that (a) composes source and target word representations from characters with a two-RNN word encoder that assigns learned weights (“energy”) to character-prefix states, and (b) decodes with a hierarchical two-level decoder: a word-level decoder whose state initializes a character-level decoder (HGRU) that emits characters until a delimiter. The approach aims to capture morphological regularities while avoiding large softmaxes and OOV issues. Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report competitive BLEU vs. subword (BPE) and character baselines, faster learning in early epochs, and qualitative evidence of morphology learning.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical character→subword/word→sentence→word→character architecture with a “settable-state” HGRU allowing efficient batched character decoding conditioned on word-level states, including the unfolding trick to align word-level outputs to character time steps.

2) A two-RNN word encoder that assigns learned weights to character-prefix hidden states to compose word representations intended to capture morphemic structure.

3) Empirical evidence that the model is competitive with strong subword/character baselines and robust to misspellings/nonce forms.

Strengths
- Novel hierarchical decoding mechanism: The settable-state HGRU and unfolding matrix enable end-to-end character decoding without separate passes or special attention supervision, addressing a known efficiency pain point in character-level NMT.

- Compact model with competitive BLEU: Despite using six RNNs, the parameter count is smaller than some prior character models. BLEU is comparable to strong char-based systems, and the “after one epoch” results are promising on En–Fr and En–Cs.

- Open-vocabulary and robustness benefits: Demonstrations on misspellings and nonce words illustrate a practical advantage of character-level generation that word/BPE models often mishandle.

- Clear, implementable architecture: The high-level design, equations for the HGRU, and training/generation procedures are described in sufficient detail to guide reproduction.

Weaknesses
- Morphology-learning claims are anecdotal: Section 5.2 relies on PCA plots and a few hand-picked examples. There is no formal evaluation (e.g., morpheme boundary detection, probing for morphological features, or improvements on morphology-sensitive phenomena). The claim that the model “learns morphology” better than SoTA is not convincingly supported.

- Incomplete and potentially non-comparable baselines: The comparison with bpe2char is partial. For Cs→En, bpe2char trains ~8 days faster; En→Cs training time is not reported; En→Fr bpe2char is not evaluated. Without a full apples-to-apples wall-clock and throughput comparison across language pairs (same hardware, same length limits, same beam), efficiency claims remain tentative.

- Small BLEU differences without significance testing: Several gains vs. bpe/bpe2char are within typical variance. No statistical significance testing is reported, making it hard to assess whether DCNMT is truly better rather than comparable.

- Ambiguity around “energy” and figures: The notion of “energy” (weights wt) is not consistently defined across sections. Figure 4a/5 “energy” peaks are not clearly explained; the color mapping appears incorrect/missing for some characters, limiting interpretability.

- Presentation and documentation issues: Many acronyms are undefined on first use (e.g., HGRU, LSTM, CNN, PCA, BPE). Inconsistent capitalization of BPE. Some references are incomplete; a link to WMT’14 training corpora is missing. Table 1 includes Cs→En results for (3) that were not reported in the cited paper—if computed by the authors, that should be stated.

Questions to Authors
- Significance: Are the BLEU differences vs. bpe/bpe2char statistically significant (e.g., bootstrap resampling)? Please report.

- Efficiency and fairness: For each pair, report training wall-clock time, tokens/sec, max length settings, beam/search parameters, and hardware for all systems (including bpe2char and char baselines) to support efficiency claims.

- bpe2char coverage: Why is En→Fr bpe2char omitted? Can you add it, and report En→Cs training time for DCNMT? For Cs→En, bpe2char is faster—how does DCNMT compare when tuned for speed?

- Morphology: Can you provide a formal experiment—e.g., boundary detection F1 against a morphological analyzer, probing classifiers for morphological features, or targeted evaluation of agreement/inflection—rather than PCA plots?

- Word encoder ablations: How much do the learned weights (“energy”) contribute? Please compare to (i) char RNN taking last state, (ii) max/avg pooling over char states, (iii) CNN word encoder, and (iv) removing the BiRNN that computes weights.

- HGRU ablations: Compare HGRU to a standard GRU/LSTM character decoder reinitialized at boundaries without the unfolding trick. What are the speed/quality trade-offs?

- Character set coverage: You select 120 most frequent characters, mapping the rest to <unk>. What percentage of dev/test characters are OOV? Does this harm the “pure character-level” claim?

Additional Comments
- Terminology: Rather than calling French “morphologically poor,” consider “less morphologically inflectional than Czech” or similar.

- Clarify “energy”: Define it consistently where introduced and when visualized; explain how peaks correspond to hypothesized morpheme boundaries and how colors map to weight magnitudes.

- Figure issues: Figure 4a seems to have missing/incorrect color mapping for some characters; please fix and add a legend. Figure 5: explain the peak detection procedure used to derive boundaries from energies.

- Style and acronyms: Define all acronyms at first mention (RNN, BiRNN, GRU, LSTM, CNN, BPE, PCA, BLEU). Use consistent capitalization for BPE. Avoid duplicated words (e.g., “the the first-level”).

- References and data: Provide the WMT’14/’15 corpus links. Complete missing bibliographic fields and ensure that results attributed to prior work are actually reported there; otherwise label them as your re-runs.

- Typos/grammar (non-exhaustive): “coveres”→“covers”; “start-of-the-art”→“state-of-the-art”; “it will be intractable or inefficient to conditionally pick outputs from the the first-level decoder”→remove duplicate “the”; subject-verb agreement in “both consists”; several missing accents in French examples.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The architectural idea is interesting and potentially impactful, and the empirical results are competitive. However, the morphology-learning claim should be softened or supported with formal evaluation, and the baseline and efficiency comparisons need to be completed and made fully comparable. If the authors address these concerns in the rebuttal and camera-ready, the paper would be a solid contribution.
