Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) that avoids a large word softmax by encoding source words from characters and decoding target sentences at the character level with a hierarchical two-level decoder. The word encoder uses two RNNs to produce a morphology-aware word representation via a weighted sum over prefix states, and the decoder uses a word-level RNN to condition a character-level RNN (HGRU) that generates characters until a delimiter. Experiments on WMT14 En→Fr and WMT15 En↔Cs report competitive BLEU, faster early-epoch gains than a BPE baseline, and qualitative evidence that the model learns morphological structure.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A hierarchical char→subword→word encoder and word→char decoder architecture: a novel word encoder that decouples character-composition and weighting (intended to capture morphemes) and a hierarchical decoder with a settable-state HGRU enabling end-to-end character decoding without explicit segmentation.
- An empirical study showing competitive BLEU to prior char/BPE systems with a relatively small model and claims of faster training, plus demonstrations of handling misspellings/nonce forms.
- Qualitative analyses suggesting the model captures morphological regularities (e.g., PCA plots, “energy” peaks, and segment-like boundaries).

Strengths
- Architectural idea is interesting and clearly motivated. The decoupling in the word encoder (character RNN for composition plus BiRNN-derived scalar weights) is a neat way to bias toward morpheme-like units. The hierarchical decoder with a settable-state HGRU is a practical, end-to-end alternative to two-pass hybrid training.
- Competitive performance with modest capacity. Despite six RNNs, the total parameter count is smaller than some char baselines, and the model reaches strong BLEU after few epochs. The “1-epoch beats BPE” result (En–Fr, En–Cs) showcases a promising optimization profile.
- Open-vocabulary behavior demonstrated. The qualitative examples on misspellings and nonce formations (e.g., “convenienter”) show clear advantages of character-level decoding that word/BPE systems often lack.
- Paper is generally well structured and readable, with useful figures that help convey the encoder/decoder mechanisms and training-time batching trick for the hierarchical decoder.

Weaknesses
- Claims about “learning morphology” are largely anecdotal. Section 5.2 relies on PCA plots and hand-picked examples (“any*”, “every*”) without quantitative evaluation. No probing tasks (e.g., supervised/unsupervised segmentation accuracy, morphological tagging/inflection prediction, or controlled generalization tests) are reported. As written, the morphology claim overreaches the evidence.
- Empirical comparison is incomplete and sometimes uneven. BLEU differences versus bpe2char are small and no significance testing is reported. Training-time comparisons are not comprehensive: bpe2char is reported as 8 days faster on Cs→En; En→Cs training time is not reported; En→Fr does not include a bpe2char baseline. Beam settings, max sequence lengths, and hardware parity are not fully specified, making efficiency claims hard to assess.
- Result interpretation occasionally overstates conclusions. Statements such as “character-level models are better than subword-level models” are not consistently supported by the tables (differences are small and vary by direction), and some baselines are missing or drawn from different setups.
- Reproducibility/reporting issues. Table 1 includes Cs→En numbers for model (3) that were not reported in the cited paper; if reproduced by the authors, the setup needs to be clearly documented. Several references are incomplete; a link for WMT14 training data is missing; capitalization of BPE is inconsistent.
- Clarity issues. Many acronyms appear before being defined (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE). The term “energy” is introduced in Section 3.1 but not reintroduced/normalized in Section 5.2; the meaning of peaks in Figure 5 is not explained. Figure 4(a) appears to have incorrect/missing color mapping for some characters. Language such as “French is morphologically poor” should be softened/qualified. Numerous typos/grammar issues remain.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Morphology: Can you provide quantitative evidence (e.g., segmentation F1 against a gold standard such as Morfessor/MorfBank, or a probing classifier for morpheme boundaries, or inflection/derivation generalization tests)? How exactly are “energies” computed and normalized in Sec. 5.2; what does a peak mean operationally?
- Comparisons: Why is En→Fr bpe2char omitted? For Cs→En, bpe2char appears faster—can you reconcile this with your efficiency claims? Please report En→Cs training time, beam sizes, and exact hardware for all systems. Provide statistical significance tests for BLEU differences.
- Table 1 provenance: Were the Cs→En numbers for model (3) reproduced by you? If so, please detail preprocessing, vocabulary, max lengths, and training schedules. Are all models trained with the same sentence/character length limits?
- HGRU details: What is the practical gain of HGRU over a standard GRU/LSTM with resets at delimiters? Any ablations (e.g., ordinary GRU, no unfolding matrix) to isolate where gains come from?
- Vocabulary and <unk>: What fraction of training/test characters map to <unk>? Does this affect cross-lingual transfer for diacritics (notably in Czech)?

Additional comments
- Consider moving the qualitative morphology section to the appendix or softening claims in the main text unless you add quantitative evaluation.
- Define all acronyms at first use; standardize capitalization (e.g., BPE). Reintroduce and clearly define “energy” in Sec. 5.2; explain peaks in Figure 5.
- Provide the WMT14 link; fix incomplete references; ensure all cited results match the original papers or clearly label your reimplementations.
- Avoid calling a language “morphologically poor”; instead say “less morphologically rich than Czech” and cite a source.
- Clean up typos/grammar and ensure figures are legible (check Figure 4a color mapping).

Overall, the architectural idea is promising and the empirical results are competitive, but stronger, more complete comparisons and a quantitative morphology evaluation are needed to support the key claims.
