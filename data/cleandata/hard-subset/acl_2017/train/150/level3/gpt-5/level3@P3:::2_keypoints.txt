- Proposes DCNMT: a deep character-level NMT with a character-based word encoder and hierarchical (word→character) decoder using an HGRU and unfolding matrix for efficient batching.  
- Claims DCNMT learns morphology, trains efficiently, and achieves competitive BLEU vs BPE and char baselines on En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En.

Contributions:
- Introduces a hierarchical character-level decoder with an unfolding matrix for practical efficient character decoding conditioned on word states.  
- Proposes a character-based word encoder that weights prefix representations (“morpheme energies”), avoiding word embeddings/large softmax.  
- Provides empirical comparisons to BPE and char baselines plus qualitative visualizations suggesting morphological patterns.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-structured presentation and a novel architecture combining character-level benefits with word-level conditioning.  
- Competitive results with relatively small model size and promising one-epoch performance on some language pairs.  
- Demonstrates feasibility of end-to-end character decoding with encoder sequence lengths near word-level.  
- Qualitative analyses and robustness to misspellings suggest subword/morphological awareness.

Weaknesses:
- Morphology claims are anecdotal and lack formal evaluation or rigorous definition of “energy” and “peaks.”  
- Incomplete/inconclusive comparison to bpe2char: small BLEU gaps, no significance testing, missing training-time/En→Fr results, and DCNMT slower in one reported case.  
- Table 1 provenance unclear: some baseline results may have been recomputed without explicit reporting of methods/hardware.  
- Writing issues: undefined acronyms, inconsistent capitalization (BPE), grammatical errors, and imprecise phrasing (e.g., “morphologically poor”).  
- Figure/clarity problems: energy color mapping, undefined legend/scale, and imprecise definition of the plotted quantity.

Questions to authors:
- Provide statistical significance testing (e.g., paired bootstrap) for BLEU differences vs bpe2char and char baselines.  
- Report bpe2char training time for En→Cs and evaluate bpe2char on En→Fr; compare decoding speeds.  
- Precisely define “energy”: the mathematical quantity plotted and what peaks denote.  
- Clarify which Table 1 entries were recomputed and provide full experimental details (tokenization, BPE ops, batch/beam sizes, hardware).  
- Ablation: compare HGRU vs standard GRU/LSTM reset mechanisms and test necessity of the unfolding matrix.

Recommendations to strengthen the paper:
- Complete bpe2char comparisons across all directions, include training/decoding speeds, and run significance tests and matched-compute learning curves.  
- Replace or support anecdotal morphology claims with formal evaluations (segmentation F1, probing tasks, reinflection, peak-to-boundary precision/recall, nonce tests).  
- Add ablations: remove weighting RNN, try uniform weights/CNN encoder, or bypass word encoder to quantify component impacts.  
- Improve figures (legends, scales), define “energy” clearly, and fix writing/terminology errors.

Reproducibility and style suggestions:
- Define all acronyms at first use, use consistent capitalization for BPE, and correct grammatical errors.  
- Provide preprocessing scripts, links to WMT corpora, and release code if possible.  
- Clarify the “no monolingual corpus is used” statement to avoid ambiguity.

Typographical examples to fix:
- “effect the performance” → “affect the performance”; “It is much difficult” → “It is much more difficult”; several other typos and repeated words.

Overall assessment:
- Interesting, well-presented architecture with practical ideas and promising results, but morphological and bpe2char advantage claims are not convincingly demonstrated without further experiments and clearer reporting.
