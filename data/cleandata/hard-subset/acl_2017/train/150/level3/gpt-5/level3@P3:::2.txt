Summary
The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that combines a character-based word encoder with a hierarchical (word→character) decoder. The encoder uses two RNNs to weight prefixes of a word (treated as “morpheme energies”), and the decoder introduces a state-resettable HGRU to generate characters conditioned on word-level states. Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report competitive BLEU and training-time comparisons to BPE-based and character-level baselines. The paper claims DCNMT learns morphology and trains efficiently.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A hierarchical character-level decoder with a practical batching/training trick (the unfolding matrix) enabling efficient character decoding while conditioning on word-level states.
2) A character-based word encoder that weights prefix representations, intended to capture morpheme structure, and avoids word embeddings/large softmax.
3) An empirical study comparing DCNMT to BPE and char-level baselines on WMT tasks, with qualitative visualizations suggesting morphological regularities.

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured presentation of a novel architecture. The design combines character-level benefits (open vocabulary, robustness to misspellings) with word-level conditioning via a hierarchical decoder. The proposed HGRU and unfolding trick are practical ideas likely of interest beyond this paper.
- Competitive results with relatively small model size. Across En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, DCNMT is comparable to strong char baselines while using fewer parameters than some prior char2char models. One-epoch results are promising, particularly on En–Fr/En–Cs.
- End-to-end character decoding without explicit segmentation. The approach shows the feasibility of fully character-level decoding while keeping encoder sequence lengths close to word-level, addressing a known bottleneck in char-based NMT.
- Qualitative analysis illustrates behavior consistent with morphology. Visualizations (PCA plots and “energy” peaks) and robustness to misspellings/nonce forms (e.g., “convenienter”) are compelling anecdotal demonstrations of subword awareness.

Weaknesses
- Overstated morphology claim; analysis is anecdotal. Section 5.2 relies on isolated examples and PCA plots. There are no formal experiments (e.g., segmentation F1 against gold, morphological probing, reinflection accuracy, or correlations with morphological analyzers). The notion of “energy” is not clearly reintroduced in Sec. 5.2, and what “peaks” mean in Figs. 4–5 is not rigorously defined. The claim that DCNMT “learns morphology” better than SOTA is unsupported as written.
- Incomplete and inconclusive comparison to bpe2char. BLEU gaps vs. bpe2char are small; no statistical significance testing is reported. DCNMT is 8 days slower than bpe2char on Cs→En (22 vs. 14 days). En→Cs training time for bpe2char is not reported, and bpe2char is not evaluated for En→Fr. This weakens the efficiency/quality claims relative to a key baseline.
- Table 1 provenance issues. The Cs→En results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016b) were not reported in the cited paper; if computed by the authors, this must be stated with full experimental details. Clarify whether all baselines used the same hardware, training length, and maximum sequence lengths; otherwise training-time comparisons are hard to interpret.
- Writing and terminology. Many acronyms are undefined or defined after use (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE). Inconsistent capitalization of BPE. Several grammatical errors/typos and incomplete references. Referring to French as “morphologically poor” is imprecise; please rephrase (e.g., “less inflectional morphology than Czech”).
- Figures/clarity issues. Figure 4a appears to have incorrect/missing energy color mapping for some characters; captions do not explain the scale. The concept of “energy” should be precisely defined where it is analyzed, with a legend and units.

Questions to Authors
- Please provide statistical significance testing (e.g., paired bootstrap resampling) for BLEU differences vs. bpe2char and char baselines on all test sets.
- Report En→Cs training time for bpe2char and evaluate bpe2char on En→Fr under your setup. Are decoding speeds comparable?
- Clarify “energy”: is it the unnormalized exp(aff(h_t)), or the tanh-compressed weight? What exactly is plotted in Figs. 4–5? What do peaks denote quantitatively?
- Table 1: Which entries did you recompute (e.g., (3) Cs→En)? Provide details (tokenization, BPE operations, batch size, beam size, early stopping).
- HGRU: Provide an ablation comparing HGRU vs standard GRU/LSTM reset mechanisms; is the unfolding matrix essential to gains?

Recommendations to strengthen the paper
- Provide a more complete bpe2char comparison: report bpe2char on all language directions you evaluate, include training/decoding speed, and run significance tests. If DCNMT’s advantage is training speed at early epochs, quantify with learning curves and matched compute budgets.
- Turn the morphology section into formal experiments or soften/relocate claims. Options include:
  - Morphological segmentation F1 against a gold resource (e.g., MorphoChallenge or language-specific analyzers).
  - Probing tasks on word representations (predicting POS, morphological tags, and affix boundaries).
  - Morphological reinflection or nonce-word paradigm tests (systematically generated).
  - Correlation of “energy” peaks with gold morpheme boundaries; report precision/recall.
  - Cross-lingual extension to additional morphologically rich languages.
- Add ablations: remove the weighting RNN (uniform weights), swap in a CNN word encoder, or bypass the word encoder; quantify each component’s impact.

Additional comments (clarity, style, reproducibility)
- Define all acronyms at first use; ensure consistent capitalization (BPE).
- Rephrase “no monolingual corpus is used” unless it is a controlled difference with prior work; otherwise it reads as extraneous.
- Provide links to WMT’14/’15 training corpora and your pre-processing scripts; release code if possible.
- Fix figure issues: ensure correct color mapping, add legends/scales, and explain peaks in captions.
- Clean up references (years/venues/authors) and ensure all citations are complete and accurate.

Typos/examples (non-exhaustive)
- “effect the performance” → “affect the performance”
- “It is much difficult” → “It is much more difficult”
- “will result a relative large energy” → “will result in a relatively large energy”
- “start-of-the-art” → “state-of-the-art”
- “coveres” → “covers”
- “the the first-level decoder” → “the first-level decoder”
- “start of sentence” → “start-of-sentence”
- Use “BPE-based” consistently

Overall assessment
Interesting and well-presented architecture with promising results and practical training ideas. However, claims about morphological learning and advantages over bpe2char are not yet convincingly demonstrated. With a more complete baseline comparison, statistical testing, and a formal morphology study (or softened claims), this would be a strong submission.
