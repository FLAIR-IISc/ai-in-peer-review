## Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture designed to address the vocabulary bottleneck in word-level NMT and the training difficulties of traditional character-level models. The core idea is to learn morphological structure using two recurrent networks within a hierarchical decoder, resulting in a six-layer recurrent network. The authors claim to achieve competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, surpassing BPE-based models and approaching state-of-the-art performance, while also demonstrating the model's ability to learn morphology.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed hierarchical architecture that effectively integrates character-level modeling with morphological awareness. The use of two RNNs to encode character representations and weights, coupled with the hierarchical decoder, is a well-motivated approach to capture subword information without relying on pre-defined segmentation like BPE. The reported BLEU scores, particularly the improvement over the BPE baseline after only one epoch of training, are a significant finding. Furthermore, the analysis demonstrating the model's ability to learn morphological features, through PCA visualization, adds credibility to the approach.

Strengths of the paper include a clear problem statement, a well-defined architecture, and a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The comparison against both BPE-based and other character-level models provides a good benchmark. The ablation study, while not extensive, offers some insight into the effectiveness of the proposed components. The authors also acknowledge the computational demands of character-level models and present a relatively efficient training approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The description of the HGRU unit is somewhat dense and could benefit from a clearer explanation. While the PCA visualization is suggestive, a more rigorous quantitative analysis of the learned morphological representations would be beneficial. The experiments are limited to a few language pairs, and the impact of the model on morphologically *poor* languages is not explored. The paper lacks a detailed error analysis, which would help identify the types of errors the model still makes and guide future research. Finally, the comparison to state-of-the-art systems is somewhat limited; a more thorough comparison with recent advancements in NMT is warranted.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the HGRU unit, perhaps with a diagram or pseudocode?
2. What quantitative metrics were used to assess the learned morphological representations beyond the PCA visualization?
3. How does the performance of your model vary across different morphological complexities of the source and target languages?
4. Can you provide a detailed error analysis, highlighting common translation errors and potential areas for improvement?
