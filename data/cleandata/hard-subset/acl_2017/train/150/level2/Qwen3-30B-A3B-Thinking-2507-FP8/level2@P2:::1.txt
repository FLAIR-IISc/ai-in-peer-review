

Summary: This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that learns morphology through a novel word encoder with two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation. The authors claim it avoids large vocabulary issues, trains more efficiently than word-based models, and achieves competitive BLEU scores.

Main Contributions (as I see them):
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through two RNNs, potentially enabling meaningful word representations without a large vocabulary.
2. A hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining efficiency through a novel HGRU mechanism.
3. Empirical demonstration that the model can learn morphology, handle misspelled words, and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art systems.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NMT: the vocabulary bottleneck and OOV issue, which is particularly relevant for morphologically rich languages. The model's ability to handle misspelled words (Table 2) is a compelling practical demonstration of the approach's potential value.

2. The analysis in Section 5.2 showing that the model learns meaningful word representations (Figure 3) is well-executed and provides strong evidence that the model learns morphology as claimed. The PCA visualization effectively demonstrates how the model's representations cluster words with similar meanings.

3. The training efficiency claim is reasonably supported: the model achieves competitive BLEU scores after just one epoch (Table 1), outperforming BPE models that require 50 epochs. This is a significant practical advantage for training time.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is significantly overstated. The paper builds heavily on prior work without clear distinction: the hierarchical decoder (Section 3.2) is directly adapted from Ling et al. (2015b), and the character-level modeling approach is similar to Lee et al. (2016) and Luong and Manning (2016). The paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these prior works, making it difficult to assess what's truly new.

2. The claimed advantage of "avoiding the large vocabulary issue radically" is misleading. The model still uses a vocabulary of 120 characters (Section 4.1), which is not a true solution to the vocabulary problem. Character-level models still face challenges with sequence length and representational efficiency, which the paper doesn't adequately address.

3. The experimental evaluation is incomplete. The paper compares with some character-level models (e.g., C2W, CNMT) but doesn't compare with the standard BPE-based models that are now the de facto standard in NMT (Sennrich et al. 2016). The paper claims "our model is comparable to the state-of-the-art," but the state-of-the-art in 2016 was largely BPE-based, which the paper doesn't properly benchmark against.

4. The model complexity (six RNNs) isn't justified by clear performance gains. The paper claims "more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models," but the training time comparisons (Table 1) are with models that were specifically trained differently (e.g., the C2W model took "3 months to train"). The paper doesn't show how training time compares to standard BPE models, which are typically more efficient than pure character-level models.

5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of character-level modeling. For morphologically poor languages like English-French, the benefits of character-level modeling are less clear. The paper should provide more nuanced analysis of when this approach is particularly beneficial versus when word-level modeling might still be preferable.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your model compare to the current state-of-the-art BPE-based models (e.g., Sennrich et al. 2016) in terms of BLEU score and training time? The paper claims to be competitive with "state-of-the-art" but doesn't compare with the most widely used approach.

2. The paper claims the model "avoids the large vocabulary issue radically," but it still uses a character vocabulary of 120. How is this fundamentally different from conventional character-level modeling, which also uses a fixed character vocabulary?

3. The hierarchical decoder (Section 3.2) seems to require special handling of delimiters. How does this affect the model's ability to handle languages without clear word boundaries?
