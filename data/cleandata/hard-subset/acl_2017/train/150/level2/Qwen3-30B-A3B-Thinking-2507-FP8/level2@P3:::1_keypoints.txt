- Proposes a deep character-level NMT with a two-RNN word encoder and a hierarchical character-level decoder claiming to learn morphology and match state-of-the-art BLEU.

- Contribution: a word encoder that learns morphemes and combination rules via two recurrent networks.

- Contribution: a hierarchical decoder (HGRU variant) that translates efficiently at the character level.

- Contribution: empirical results claiming morphology learning and competitive BLEU scores.

- Strength: Addresses OOVs by learning morphology at character level and reports comparable BLEU with much faster training (1 epoch vs. 300 for baselines).

- Strength: Visualization (Figure 3) and PCA (Figure 4) provide evidence that the encoder captures meaningful morphological relationships.

- Strength: Demonstrates practical robustness to misspellings and nonce words (Table 2), useful for real-world noisy input.

- Strength: Training efficiency supported by smaller parameter count and faster training time compared to other character-level models (Table 1).

- Weakness: Novelty unclear—insufficient differentiation from prior work (e.g., C2W, hybrid word-character models), with performance only comparable.

- Weakness: Efficiency claim may be misleading due to unequal training schedules; no comparison of all models after equivalent training.

- Weakness: Lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate contributions of the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder.

- Weakness: Morphology evidence is limited to English examples despite claims about morphologically rich languages like Czech.

- Question: How does the model handle morphological variations not seen in training (e.g., forming "unhappiness" from seen morphemes)?

- Question: Is the HGRU modification necessary—how does performance change using a standard GRU?

- Question: Does the model require explicit morpheme segmentation for training despite claiming to learn morphology automatically, and how does it handle languages without clear morpheme boundaries?

- Question: What exact preprocessing was done for morphologically rich languages (e.g., Czech) and how were morphological features handled?
