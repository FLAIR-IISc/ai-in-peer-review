- Summary: Proposes a deep character-level NMT with a novel word encoder (two RNNs) and hierarchical decoder that learns morphology and yields meaningful word representations and competitive BLEU with efficient training.

- Strength: Tackles OOVs by learning morphology instead of relying on external segmentation; word encoder produces linguistically meaningful word embeddings (Fig. 3).

- Strength: Strong training efficiency — achieves comparable BLEU after one epoch while other character-level models require far more training.

- Strength: Morphology analysis (PCA projections and segmentation examples) shows the model captures useful linguistic structure for rare and morphologically varied words.

- Weakness: Lacks comparison with the latest SOTA systems (e.g., BPE-based models) and doesn't clearly show improvement beyond training-efficiency claims.

- Weakness: The claim of “radically” avoiding large-vocabulary issues is unclear since the model still uses a 120-character vocabulary and may not address extremely rare/test-set words fully.

- Weakness: Hierarchical decoder appears derivative (based on Serban et al. 2015) and HGRU is a minor modification; paper doesn't justify necessity versus simpler architectures or isolate why efficiency gains occur.

- Question: How does the model handle extremely rare or unseen words (e.g., proper names or domain terms) compared to BPE-based approaches?

- Question: Please clarify the actual number of distinct RNNs used and the parameter-sharing strategy among the claimed six RNNs.

- Question: How does training efficiency and BLEU gap evolve with more epochs — would the advantage over BPE-based models widen or narrow with continued training?
