1. Summary: Proposes DCNMT — a deep character-level NMT with a two-RNN word encoder and hierarchical HGRU decoder, claiming to avoid large vocabularies, train efficiently, and match BLEU scores.

2. Contribution: Novel word encoder using two RNNs to learn morphemes and compose meaningful word representations without a large word vocabulary.

3. Contribution: Hierarchical character-level decoder with an HGRU mechanism intended to keep character decoding efficient.

4. Contribution: Empirical claims that the model learns morphology, handles misspellings, and achieves competitive performance.

5. Strength: Tackles vocabulary/OOV bottleneck relevant for morphologically rich languages and demonstrates robustness to misspellings (Table 2).

6. Strength: Convincing analysis (Section 5.2, Fig.3) showing learned word representations that cluster by meaning/morphology.

7. Strength: Training efficiency claim supported by achieving competitive BLEU after one epoch versus models needing many epochs (Table 1).

8. Weakness: Novelty is overstated — approach closely follows prior work (Ling et al. 2015b; Lee et al. 2016; Luong & Manning 2016) without clear differentiation.

9. Weakness: Claim of radically avoiding large vocabularies is misleading — model still uses a 120-character vocabulary and doesn't address sequence-length/efficiency trade-offs.

10. Weakness: Experimental evaluation is incomplete — lacks comparison to standard BPE-based state-of-the-art (Sennrich et al. 2016).

11. Weakness: Model complexity (six RNNs) isn’t justified by clear performance or fair training-time comparisons, especially versus BPE models.

12. Weakness: Paper doesn't analyze limitations for morphologically poor language pairs or when word-level modeling may be preferable.

13. Question: How does the model compare to BPE-based models in BLEU and training time?

14. Question: How is using a 120-character vocabulary fundamentally different from conventional character-level modeling?

15. Question: How does the hierarchical decoder handle languages without clear word boundaries given its delimiter handling?
