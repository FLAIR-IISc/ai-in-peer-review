

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that learns morphology through a novel word encoder with two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder. The main contributions are: (1) a morphology-aware word encoder that learns subword units automatically; (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining training efficiency; (3) experimental evidence demonstrating comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems with significantly fewer training epochs.

Strengths: The paper provides compelling experimental results showing DCNMT achieves higher BLEU scores than BPE-based models after just one epoch of training. The qualitative analysis in Figure 3 demonstrates the model's ability to learn meaningful word representations that cluster morphologically related words together. The model's ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words correctly (Table 2) is particularly noteworthy as it addresses a practical limitation of word-based models. The comparison with relevant baselines is thorough, and the hierarchical decoder design effectively resolves efficiency issues in previous character-level approaches.

Weaknesses: The claim that the model "avoids the large vocabulary issue radically" is misleading—the model still uses a character vocabulary (120 characters), just smaller than word-level vocabularies. The evidence for morphology learning (Figure 3) shows more clustered representations but doesn't demonstrate how this directly improves translation quality beyond BLEU scores. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of six RNNs versus simpler alternatives. The comparison with Luong and Manning (2016) is problematic as they don't provide details about training conditions that might explain the 3-month training time difference.

The paper would benefit from a more precise definition of how their approach differs from other character-level methods. While the model shows promising results, the novelty is somewhat limited as similar ideas appear in Ling et al. (2015) and Lee et al. (2016). The authors should clarify the practical advantages of their specific architecture over these prior works.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing the vocabulary bottleneck in NMT, but the claims about novelty and efficiency require stronger justification. With minor revisions to clarify the contributions and provide more detailed comparison with related work, this could be a strong submission.
