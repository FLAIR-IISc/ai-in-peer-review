

Summary of the paper: This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The main contributions are: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two RNNs, (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining efficiency, and (3) empirical evidence that the model learns meaningful word representations and achieves competitive BLEU scores with efficient training.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant problem in neural machine translation - the out-of-vocabulary issue - through a novel approach that learns morphology rather than relying on external segmentation methods like BPE. The word encoder's ability to generate meaningful representations of words (as shown in Figure 3) is a compelling contribution that demonstrates the model's linguistic understanding.

2. The training efficiency claim is well-supported by the experimental results. The paper shows that their model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems after only one epoch of training, while other character-level models require significantly more training time (e.g., 3 months for Luong and Manning, 2016). This efficiency advantage is particularly valuable for practitioners working with limited computational resources.

3. The analysis of morphology learning provides valuable insights into the model's internal representations. The PCA projections in Figure 3 and the word segmentation examples in Figure 5 demonstrate that the model can learn meaningful linguistic structure, which is not only theoretically interesting but also practically useful for handling rare words and morphological variations.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art models. While the paper compares with several character-level models, it doesn't compare with the latest NMT systems that might incorporate similar morphological learning techniques. For example, the recent work by Sennrich et al. (2016) on BPE has been widely adopted, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed model differs from or improves upon BPE-based approaches beyond the training efficiency claim.

2. The claimed advantage of "avoiding the large vocabulary issue radically" needs further clarification. While the model uses character-level processing, it still requires a vocabulary of 120 characters (as mentioned in Section 4.1), which is a different form of vocabulary limitation. The paper doesn't adequately address how this approach handles extremely rare words that might still appear in the test set, especially in morphologically rich languages.

3. The hierarchical decoder architecture appears to be a combination of existing techniques rather than a truly novel contribution. The paper mentions using a hierarchical decoder from Serban et al. (2015), and the HGRU variant seems to be a minor modification of standard GRU. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific architecture is necessary compared to simpler alternatives, and the claimed efficiency gains could be due to other factors like model size rather than the hierarchical structure itself.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your model handle extremely rare words that might not appear in the training data at all (e.g., proper names or domain-specific terms), and how does this compare to BPE-based approaches?

2. The paper mentions that your model consists of six RNNs, but the description in Section 3.3 suggests some of these might be reused or share parameters. Can you clarify the actual number of distinct RNNs and their parameter sharing strategy?

3. In Table 1, your model achieves a higher BLEU score than BPE-based models after one epoch, but the final scores are comparable to other character-level models. How does the training efficiency of your model scale when training for more epochs? Would the performance gap between your model and BPE-based models widen or narrow with additional training?
