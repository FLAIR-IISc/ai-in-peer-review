

Summary: This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a novel architecture consisting of two recurrent networks for word encoding and a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation. The main contributions are: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combination rules through two recurrent networks, (2) a hierarchical decoder that efficiently translates at character level, and (3) empirical evidence demonstrating the model's ability to learn morphology while achieving competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art models.

Strengths: 
1. The paper effectively addresses the OOV problem in neural machine translation through a character-level approach that learns morphology, rather than relying on subword segmentation or vocabulary expansion. The results in Table 1 show that the model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art character-level models while being significantly more efficient to train (only 1 epoch vs. 300 epochs for some baselines).
2. The evidence for morphology learning is compelling, with Figure 3 demonstrating how the word encoder produces more meaningful representations of words with similar morphology compared to ordinary RNN encoders. The PCA projections in Figure 4 further validate that the model can capture morphological relationships.
3. The practical advantage of handling misspelled words and nonce words (Table 2) is well-documented and represents a valuable capability for real-world applications where input may contain errors or novel terms.
4. The training efficiency claim is supported by the model's smaller parameter count and faster training time compared to other character-level models (Table 1), which is a significant practical contribution.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the approach is not sufficiently established. The paper claims to learn morphology "automatically" but doesn't clearly differentiate from prior work such as the C2W model (Ling et al., 2015b) or the hybrid word-character model (Luong and Manning, 2016). The comparison in Table 1 shows comparable performance to existing models but doesn't demonstrate clear advantages beyond training efficiency.
2. The claim of "efficiency" is somewhat misleading without a fair comparison. The model achieves better scores after 1 epoch than other models after 300 epochs, but the paper doesn't show how the model performs after training for the same number of epochs as baselines. The comparison appears to favor the proposed model by using different training schedules.
3. The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (the word encoder vs. hierarchical decoder). Without these, it's difficult to determine which aspect of the architecture provides the most benefit.
4. The evidence for morphology learning is limited to English examples (Figures 3-5). The paper claims the model works for morphologically rich languages (En-Cs, Cs-En), but provides no analysis of how well the model handles morphology in Czech compared to English.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims the model learns morphology, but how does it handle morphological variations that are not present in the training data? For example, how would the model handle "unhappiness" if it had only seen "happy" and "un-" in training?
2. The hierarchical decoder (HGRU) is described as a variant of GRU, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this specific modification is necessary or beneficial. How does the performance change when using standard GRU instead of HGRU?
3. The model appears to require explicit segmentation of words into morphemes for training (as seen in Figure 5), but the paper claims to learn morphology "automatically." How does the model handle languages without clear morpheme boundaries or with different morphological structures?
4. The paper uses the same dataset as prior work for comparison (WMT'14/15), but the exact preprocessing details for morphologically rich languages like Czech are not sufficiently described. How were morphological features handled during preprocessing?
