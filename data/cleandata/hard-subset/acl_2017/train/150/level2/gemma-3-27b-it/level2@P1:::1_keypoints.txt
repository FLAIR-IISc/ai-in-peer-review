- Novel hierarchical character-level architecture combining two RNNs for morphology learning with a hierarchical decoder to avoid vocabulary bottlenecks.  
- Dual-RNN encoder that weights preceding character representations to automatically capture subword/morphological structure.  
- Efficient training and compact model achieving competitive BLEU on En–Fr and En–Cs, reportedly with a single epoch.  
- Addresses OOV/misspelling issues by operating at character level, a clear advantage over word-based NMT.  
- Creative and well-explained architectural design (dual RNNs and hierarchical decoder) with helpful Figure 2.  
- Reported BLEU scores are competitive given the fast training time.  
- PCA visualization offers preliminary evidence the model learns morphological information and weighs initial/final characters interestingly.  
- Demonstrated ability to handle misspellings and nonce words, showing robustness beyond word-level models.  
- Morphology analysis is superficial; lacks rigorous quantitative probing of learned morphemes or hidden-state behavior.  
- Limited comparison to strong baselines and recent subword methods (e.g., BPE/SentencePiece); trade-offs with prior work not fully explored.  
- HGRU decoder description and the unfolding step are unclear and need stronger justification.  
- No reported hyperparameter sensitivity or ablation studies to assess robustness to design choices.  
- Minimal preprocessing (only tokenization) may limit performance; more preprocessing experiments would help.  
- Request for detailed analysis demonstrating morpheme representation and its use during translation.  
- Question about computational cost and scaling of the hierarchical decoder’s unfolding step with sequence length and batch size.  
- Ask for sensitivity analysis of performance to hyperparameters (RNN size, embedding dim, learning rate).  
- Suggestion to compare against standard subword tokenization baselines (BPE/WordPiece/SentencePiece).  
- Request justification for the tanh-based weight calculation and why it outperforms normalization.  
- Minor grammatical and stylistic errors noted; paper needs proofreading.  
- References are extensive but formatting is inconsistent and should be standardized.  
- Overall: promising and novel work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
