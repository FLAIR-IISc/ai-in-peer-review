This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the vocabulary bottleneck in traditional word-level NMT and the challenges of training character-level models. The core idea is to learn morphology using two RNNs – one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation – and a hierarchical decoder that translates at the character level. The authors claim to achieve comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, and demonstrate through analysis that their model learns morphological information.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hierarchical Character-Level Architecture:** The primary contribution is the novel architecture combining two RNNs for morphology learning with a hierarchical decoder. This allows for character-level translation without the computational burden of purely character-based models and avoids the vocabulary limitations of word-level approaches.
2. **Morphology Learning:** The authors explicitly address and attempt to learn morphological structure within the encoder. The dual RNN encoder, weighting preceding character representations, is a distinct design choice aimed at capturing subword units automatically.
3. **Efficient Training:** The authors demonstrate a relatively efficient training process, even with six recurrent networks, achieving competitive results with a single epoch of training, and demonstrating a compact model size compared to alternatives.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper tackles the significant problem of handling out-of-vocabulary words and the limitations of large vocabularies in NMT. Moving to a character-level approach is a logical step in this direction.
2. **Novel Architectural Design:** The proposed hierarchical architecture, and particularly the dual RNN encoder, represent a creative approach to incorporating morphological information into an NMT system. The explanation of the architecture, with the accompanying Figure 2, is clear and well-presented.
3. **Competitive Results:** The BLEU scores reported are competitive, especially given the relatively quick training time (one epoch reported for initial results). Achieving comparable performance to state-of-the-art models is a strong positive indicator.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization in Figure 3 provides some initial evidence supporting the claim that the model learns morphological information. The observation about the weighting of initial and final characters is interesting.
5. **Handles Misspellings/Nonce Words:** The discussion and example in section 5.3 demonstrate a potential advantage of character-level models - ability to deal with OOV words or misspelled words, showcasing a benefit not readily available to word-based NMT models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Morphology:** While the PCA visualization is a start, the analysis of whether and how the model *actually* learns morphology is superficial. More rigorous quantitative analysis is needed. For example, analyzing the learned weights or probing the hidden states for morpheme boundaries or relationships would be beneficial.
2. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to existing character-level models (e.g., Luong & Manning, 2016) is somewhat limited. The paper mentions the longer training time of Luong & Manning, but doesn’t delve into the trade-offs between model complexity, training time, and performance in a comprehensive way. A more direct comparison with more recent subword methods (e.g., SentencePiece) would also be helpful.
3. **Decoder Details:** The description of the HGRU decoder is somewhat complex and lacks clarity. The explanation of how the unfolding of the first-level decoder outputs is done could be made more explicit. The necessity of this unfolding step needs further justification.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices. A brief ablation study exploring the impact of different RNN sizes, embedding dimensions, or training parameters would strengthen the results.
5. **Data Preprocessing:** The limited preprocessing applied (only tokenization) might hinder performance. Exploring the use of more sophisticated preprocessing techniques, like lowercasing or stemming, could be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned morphology? Specifically, can you demonstrate that the model learns to represent morphemes in a meaningful way, and how this representation is used during translation?
2. What is the computational cost associated with the unfolding step in the hierarchical decoder? How does this cost scale with sequence length and batch size?
3. How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., RNN size, embedding dimension, learning rate)? 
4. Have you considered using more standard subword tokenization methods like Byte Pair Encoding or WordPiece as a baseline for comparison?
5. Could you elaborate on the choice of tanh activation in the weight calculation (wt = exp(aff(ht)) and provide justification as to why it works better than using a normalization factor?

**Additional Comments:**

*   There are a few minor grammatical errors and stylistic inconsistencies throughout the paper. A careful proofread would improve readability.
*   The reference list is extensive but could benefit from consistency in formatting.



Overall, this is a promising paper that presents a novel and interesting approach to character-level NMT. The proposed architecture is well-motivated and the results are encouraging. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the analysis of morphology learning and comparison to stronger baselines, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
