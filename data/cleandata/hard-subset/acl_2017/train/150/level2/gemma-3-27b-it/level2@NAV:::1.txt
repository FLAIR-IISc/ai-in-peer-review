## Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

This paper presents a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level and existing character-level approaches. The core idea is to leverage morphological structure by employing two recurrent networks within the encoder to learn character representations and their weighting, coupled with a hierarchical decoder for character-level generation. The primary contribution is the proposed architecture, which aims to bypass the large vocabulary problem and improve training efficiency while retaining the benefits of character-level modeling. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating, through analysis, the model’s ability to learn morphological features.

The strengths of this submission are considerable. The proposed architecture is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in NMT – vocabulary size and OOV words – in a conceptually sound manner. The experimental results on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks are promising, achieving competitive BLEU scores, and notably, outperforming a BPE-based model after only one epoch of training. The analysis illustrating the model’s ability to learn morphological structures, via PCA visualization, is convincing and supports the authors’ claims. The model's efficiency, with a relatively small parameter count, is also a positive aspect.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies. For example, the specific choices for the RNN dimensions and layer numbers aren’t fully justified. A more thorough comparison with strong baselines, beyond the BPE model, would strengthen the findings. While the authors mention achieving “comparable” BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems, a direct comparison against these systems, with quantitative results, is missing. The explanation of the HGRU and its efficiency gains could be more elaborated. Finally, the impact of morphological richness of languages (beyond Czech) on the model’s performance isn't deeply explored.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the rationale behind the chosen values?
2.  How does the performance of your model compare to more recent, strong character-level NMT baselines (e.g., those utilizing subword regularization or attention modifications)?
3.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the unfolding step in the hierarchical decoder and how it affects training speed compared to alternative character-level approaches?
