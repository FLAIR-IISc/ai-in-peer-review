This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model that leverages morphology through a novel architecture consisting of two recurrent networks for encoding word structure and a hierarchical decoder for character-level generation. The core idea is to avoid large vocabularies and improve training efficiency compared to word-based and conventional character-based models. The authors claim their model achieves higher BLEU scores than a BPE-based model after one epoch and comparable results to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, and demonstrate through analysis that the model learns morphology.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hierarchical Character-Level Architecture:** The primary contribution is a novel architecture for character-level NMT incorporating a word encoder learning morphology and a hierarchical decoder. This structure aims to bridge the gap between word-level and character-level approaches, addressing the vocabulary bottleneck and training challenges.
2. **Morphology Learning via RNNs:** The use of two recurrent networks within the word encoder—one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation—is a significant design choice intended to explicitly model morphological structure.
3. **Efficient Training of Deep Character Model:** The authors demonstrate the ability to train a relatively deep model (six recurrent networks) effectively, potentially addressing the difficulty of training deep character-level models highlighted in prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture, especially the integrated word encoder learning morphology, is a novel approach. The concept of explicitly learning morpheme representations within the encoder is interesting and addresses a key limitation of existing character-level approaches.
2. **Empirical Results:** The reported BLEU scores, achieving comparable performance to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs, are encouraging. The claim of outperforming a BPE-based model even after just one epoch suggests faster convergence, a significant benefit.
3. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides a compelling visual demonstration that the model learns representations reflecting morphological similarity. The example of “convenienter” in Section 5.3 further supports this claim, demonstrating the ability to handle unseen morphological variations.
4. **Addressing OOV issue:** The model intrinsically handles OOV words by operating at the character level, eliminating the need for explicit OOV handling mechanisms. This is a major advantage over word-based models.
5. **Efficiency claim:** The authors claim reasonable training efficiency given the depth of the network, a positive sign.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the paper compares against a BPE model and references other works, the comparison lacks depth. A more detailed comparison against strong character-level baselines (e.g., subword NMT with ByteNet or Unigram Language Model) and the hybrid word-character model of Luong & Manning (2016) using identical datasets and settings is needed to establish the model's true advantages.  The claim of “comparable” to SOTA needs stronger justification beyond the presented BLEU scores.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks sufficient discussion of hyperparameter tuning. The optimal learning rate, RNN hidden size, and other parameters are crucial for performance, and the sensitivity of the model to these settings should be explored.
3. **Ablation Studies:** An important weakness is the absence of ablation studies. It’s unclear which component (morphology-aware encoder, hierarchical decoder, etc.) contributes most to the performance gains. Removing components and evaluating the resulting performance would help pinpoint the key innovations.
4. **PCA Interpretation:** While the PCA visualization is suggestive, it’s not a rigorous proof of morphological learning. The authors should consider more quantitative metrics to assess the model's understanding of morphology, such as analyzing the weights learned for different morphemes or evaluating performance on morphological segmentation tasks.
5. **Decoder Complexity:** The detailed explanation of the hierarchical decoder and the HGRU variant is somewhat convoluted. It would benefit from a clearer, more intuitive explanation with a simplified diagram.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison with strong character-level baselines, including subword NMT models and the Luong & Manning (2016) hybrid model?  Please present results with identical datasets and settings.
2.  What hyperparameters were critical for achieving the reported performance, and how sensitive is the model to their values?
3.  Have you conducted ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of your architecture (morphology-aware encoder, hierarchical decoder, etc.)?
4.  Can you provide more quantitative evidence of morphological learning beyond the PCA visualization?
5.  Could you provide more clarification and a simplified diagram of the HGRU implementation within the hierarchical decoder?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper’s organization is generally good, but some sections (e.g., Section 3.2) could benefit from clearer prose and more concise explanations.
*   The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in character-level NMT and subword modeling.
*   The provided references are complete.
