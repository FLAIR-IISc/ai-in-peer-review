This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level and existing character-level models. The core idea is to learn morphology through two recurrent networks (one for preceding characters and one for weighting their representation) and a hierarchical decoder. The primary contribution is a novel architecture integrating these components to achieve competitive translation performance while avoiding large vocabulary issues. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration that the model can learn morphological features, evidenced through analysis of character-level representations. A third, though less prominent, contribution is the efficient training of a deep model with six recurrent networks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical challenge in NMT – handling large vocabularies and out-of-vocabulary words – offering a character-level solution that avoids explicit vocabulary construction. This is a strong motivation and a relevant research direction.
2.  **Novel Architecture:** The proposed architecture, particularly the combination of the morphology-learning word encoder and the hierarchical decoder, represents a genuinely novel approach to character-level NMT. The use of two RNNs in the word encoder to capture morphemes and their weighting is an interesting design choice.
3.  **Competitive Performance:** The results presented demonstrate that the proposed model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems, even with a smaller model size, particularly on morphologically rich languages like Czech. This provides empirical validation for the approach.
4.  **Evidence of Morphology Learning:** The analysis presented in Section 5.2, including the PCA visualization and discussion of character energies, provides some evidence that the model is indeed learning morphological features. This lends credibility to the core design principle.
5.  **Efficient Training:** The paper highlights the efficient training of the deep model despite its complexity. The results in Table 1 comparing training time with other models suggest a practical advantage of this approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the paper compares against a few baselines, the comparison is not comprehensive. Specifically, a more detailed comparison against recent subword models (e.g., Byte Pair Encoding with larger vocabularies) and other character-level approaches with similar model sizes would be valuable. The claim of being "comparable to state-of-the-art" feels unsupported without a more thorough comparison.
2.  **Analysis Depth:** The analysis of morphological learning, while present, could be more rigorous. While the PCA plot is illustrative, it lacks quantitative metrics to demonstrate the separation of morphemes. A more systematic evaluation of how the model handles morphological variations (e.g., different suffixes, prefixes) would strengthen the claims.
3.  **Hierarchical Decoder Details:** The description of the hierarchical decoder, particularly the HGRU mechanism and the matrix unfolding technique, is somewhat dense and difficult to follow. Providing a more intuitive explanation or a simplified diagram would improve clarity.
4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity. It is unclear how the performance is affected by changes to key parameters such as hidden unit size, embedding dimension, or learning rate. Exploring this aspect would provide insights into the robustness of the model.
5. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** Ablation studies are missing. Examining the impact of removing either the morphology learning word encoder or the hierarchical decoder would help determine the contribution of each component to the overall performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your model with recent subword models (e.g., BPE with larger vocabularies) in terms of BLEU score, model size, and training time?
2.  Can you provide quantitative metrics (beyond the PCA plot) to demonstrate the separation of morphemes in the learned representations?
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of tanh activation for normalization in the word encoder and explain why it outperforms other normalization methods?
4.  How sensitive is the model's performance to changes in the key hyperparameters (e.g., hidden unit size, embedding dimension, learning rate)?
5.  What is the computational cost of the matrix unfolding step in the hierarchical decoder compared to other batch training approaches for character-level models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is incomplete (e.g. missing citations).
*   The paper would benefit from a clear diagram illustrating the full architecture of the proposed model, including the data flow between the different components.
*   The code would be a valuable addition to reproducibility.
