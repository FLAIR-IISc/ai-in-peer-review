- Proposes DCNMT: a character-level NMT with a word encoder (two RNNs learning morphology) and a hierarchical multi-layer character decoder.
- Claims to avoid large-vocabulary bottlenecks while being more efficient to train than prior word- and character-level models.
- Reports competitive BLEU scores on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En tasks.
- Demonstrates handling of OOV and misspelled words with qualitative examples.
- Shows the model learns meaningful morphological representations and morpheme segmentation.
- Novel hierarchical architecture that explicitly models morphology is a major contribution.
- Achieves competitive performance with significantly reduced training time versus prior character models.
- Practical utility for real-world errors (OOVs, misspellings) highlighted.
- Provides empirical validation across multiple language pairs with favorable training time and model size.
- Insightful analysis of learned linguistic structures supports the model’s claims.
- Lacks direct comparisons with recent subword/tokenization-free methods (e.g., SentencePiece).
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate contributions of the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder.
- Scalability concerns due to use of six RNNs; computational trade-offs for very large datasets/long sequences not discussed.
- Limited discussion of limitations, especially for languages with complex morphology or non-alphabetic scripts.
- Asks how the model generalizes to non-alphabetic scripts (e.g., Chinese, Japanese).
- Requests more details on hierarchical decoder efficiency compared to other character-level models for long sequences.
- Suggests exploring combination with pre-trained embeddings or transfer learning.
- Recommends acceptance contingent on addressing the noted comparisons, ablations, and scalability/limitation discussions.
