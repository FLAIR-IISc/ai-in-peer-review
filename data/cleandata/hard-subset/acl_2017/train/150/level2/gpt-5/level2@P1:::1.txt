Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that (a) replaces word embeddings with a learned character-compositional word encoder designed to capture morphology, and (b) introduces a hierarchical decoder that conditions a character-level decoder on word-level decoder states via a settable-state GRU (HGRU) and an efficient batching trick. The authors claim improved training efficiency and competitive BLEU without explicit subword segmentation. I see the primary contributions as:
1) A morphology-aware word encoder: two RNNs compute prefix representations and per-position “energies,” which are combined into a word vector. This is novel relative to common CNN/RNN char-to-word encoders and is targeted at learning morphemes and their composition.
2) A hierarchical character decoder with a settable-state GRU and an unfolding matrix to efficiently condition character generation on word-level states in batch training.
3) Empirical evidence that the approach achieves competitive BLEU on En–Fr/En–Cs/Cs–En with fewer parameters and faster convergence than some char-level baselines, plus qualitative analyses suggesting morphological awareness and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and practicality: The word encoder cleanly decouples (i) learning prefix representations from characters and (ii) learning positional weights via a BiRNN-derived energy. The decoder-side HGRU and unfolding trick are elegant, enabling end-to-end character generation while preserving word-level conditioning and batch efficiency. This architectural combination is original and addresses real pain points (large softmax, training difficulty for fully character models).
- Competitive results with good training efficiency: On WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En, DCNMT achieves BLEU comparable to prior char-level systems with fewer parameters (~54M) and shorter reported training time (e.g., one-epoch scores beating BPE baselines; final En–Fr 30.56). The explicit reporting of both “1-epoch” and final numbers supports the claim of faster convergence.
- Qualitative evidence of learning morphology and robustness: PCA plots contrasting the proposed word encoder vs a simple RNN encoder are convincing that the proposed encoder better clusters morphological variants by meaning. Energy visualizations over “any*/every*” and automatic segmentation examples (e.g., “consum-er-s”, “foot-ball”) support the morpheme-learning claim. The misspelling and nonce-word examples (e.g., “convenienter”) illustrate genuine advantages of character-level decoding.
- Reduced dependency on large vocabularies: The approach avoids target softmax over large word vocabularies and uses a small character set, which is attractive for open-vocabulary MT and morphologically rich languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations and under-justified design choices: The paper does not isolate the contribution of each component. For example, (i) how much of the BLEU gain derives from the morphology-aware encoder vs. simply using any char-to-word encoder; (ii) how much HGRU improves over a standard char decoder conditioned on word states; (iii) the effect of using unnormalized weights w_t = exp(aff(h_t)) and the final tanh on the summed vector vs. a normalized attention/softmax over positions. Stating that “tanh performs best” without reporting comparisons leaves a gap.
- Comparisons and fairness: The baselines vary in settings and training budgets (e.g., Lee et al. 2016/Luong & Manning 2016 with different depths and training times). It is hard to assess “more efficient” without normalized throughput metrics (tokens/sec), beam sizes, or matched training compute. The BPE baselines appear relatively weak here; stronger BPE configurations (e.g., larger merge operations, joint BPE, tied embeddings) are not explored. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Open-vocabulary claim tempered by character set pruning: Restricting to 120 most frequent characters and mapping others to <unk> may be problematic for languages with diacritics (Czech) and reduces the purity of open-vocabulary modeling. The impact of this pruning on BLEU and errors is not analyzed.
- Inference/search details and reproducibility: The paper states that decoding searches directly on characters, but does not specify beam sizes (word-level vs char-level beams), length/coverage controls, or how delimiter generation interacts with beam pruning. Training/inference memory footprint and speed are not quantified. Code or sufficient pseudocode for HGRU/unfolding would help reproducibility.
- Scope of linguistic analysis: The morphology-learning analysis is suggestive but qualitative. No evaluation against a morphological analyzer or subword segmentation benchmarks (e.g., Morfessor) is provided, and it focuses on English examples rather than Czech, where the gains would matter most.

Questions to authors
1) Word encoder normalization: Why not normalize the positional weights (e.g., softmax over positions) and feed a weighted sum without the outer tanh? Did you try attention-like normalization or gating, and what were the BLEU/learning speed differences?
2) Ablations: Can you report BLEU and training speed for (a) DCNMT with a conventional char-to-word CNN/RNN encoder in place of your morphology-aware encoder; (b) HGRU replaced by a standard GRU/LSTM char decoder with concatenated word state? This would clarify each component’s contribution.
3) Character set: What are the exact 120 characters for En/Cs? How often does <unk> appear at training/test time, and what is the BLEU impact if you include all Unicode letters observed in training?
4) Decoding/search: What beam size(s) did you use at the character level? Do you maintain a separate beam at the word level? How do you prevent pathological long-word generation before hitting a delimiter?
5) Efficiency: Please report tokens/sec (source and target chars) and wall-clock per epoch on your GPU, and memory usage. How does training speed compare to a matched char2char baseline on your hardware?
6) Misspellings/nonce words: Could you quantify robustness (e.g., perturbation study on dev/test) rather than anecdotal examples?

Additional comments
- Table 1 is informative but somewhat dense; clarifying formatting (e.g., separating “1-epoch” vs “final” rows per model) would aid readability.
- Please provide more details on regularization (dropout, gradient clipping, layer norm) and optimization schedules, and the beam/length penalty settings.
- A small error analysis on En–Cs (common morphology-related improvements vs errors) would strengthen the case for morphology learning.
