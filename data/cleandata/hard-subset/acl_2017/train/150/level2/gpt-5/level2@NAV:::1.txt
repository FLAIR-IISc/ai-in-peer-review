Summary: The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture that seeks to learn morphology explicitly via a two-RNN word encoder and a hierarchical decoder (word-level to character-level) with a settable-state HGRU. The approach aims to remove large target vocabularies, improve training efficiency over word-based and fully character-level models, and handle misspellings/nonce words. Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report BLEU comparable to prior character-level systems and better than a BPE baseline after one epoch; qualitative analyses suggest the encoder captures morphological structure.

Main contributions (as I see them):
- A hierarchical character decoder (word→character) with an HGRU that conditions generation on word-level states, improving trainability/efficiency of character-level decoding.
- A morphology-aware word encoder that aggregates character-level RNN states with learned position-dependent weights, enabling subword/morpheme-like composition without explicit BPE/CNNs.
- Empirical evidence that the model trains faster to competitive BLEU and handles misspellings/nonce words better than word-level/BPE systems.

Strengths:
- Addresses a practical pain point (large softmax/vocab) with a principled character-level alternative that keeps encoder lengths manageable via a hierarchical design.
- Clear architectural description with equations for the HGRU and unfolding strategy; end-to-end training without layer-wise tricks is a plus.
- Quantitative results show competitive BLEU with smaller model sizes and fewer epochs than some prior char-level systems; qualitative examples convincingly demonstrate robustness to misspellings and morphological generalization.
- The morphology analysis (PCA clusters, energy patterns, subword boundary cues) is insightful and aligns with the model’s stated design goals.

Weaknesses:
- Fairness and strength of baselines are unclear. BPE numbers are taken from different sources and may use different preprocessing/beam sizes; no strong in-house BPE baseline under identical settings. No significance testing.
- Efficiency claims rely on wall-clock days from different papers/hardware; tokens/sec and per-epoch time on the same GPU across baselines are missing.
- “Open vocabulary” is weakened by restricting to the 120 most frequent characters and mapping others to <unk>; byte-level or larger char sets are not explored.
- Limited evaluation breadth: only BLEU, no human eval, and morphology learning is shown qualitatively without quantitative segmentation metrics. Ablations (e.g., ordinary char encoder vs proposed, HGRU vs LSTM, effect of the weighting mechanism) are missing.
- Some implementation details are under-specified (beam size, dropout/regularization, delimiter handling across languages), limiting reproducibility; code is not provided.

Questions to authors:
- Can you report a strong, reimplemented BPE baseline with identical tokenization, training budget, and beam, including significance tests?
- What is the beam size at character level, and how does search depth affect quality/latency?
- How sensitive is performance to the 120-char limit? Have you tried byte-level decoding?
- Can you provide ablations isolating the contribution of the weighted word encoder and the HGRU vs a standard GRU/LSTM?
- Do you have quantitative morphology metrics (e.g., morpheme boundary F1) on a labeled corpus?
