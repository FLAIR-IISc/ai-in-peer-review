- Paper proposes DCNMT: a character-composed word encoder (prefix RNN + BiRNN energy weights) and a hierarchical decoder (word-level states + HGRU character generator with an unfolding/batching trick).
- Empirically, DCNMT matches prior character-level NMT BLEU, outperforms the paper’s BPE baseline on En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En, and shows fast early-epoch gains and qualitative robustness to misspellings.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary translation with a coherent architecture that decouples word-level planning from character-level surface realization.
- Practical training speedups via a simple unfolding/batching trick; model size remains modest (~54M) despite multiple RNNs.
- Competitive BLEU versus prior character-level work and ablation evidence that the word encoder helps.
- Qualitative analyses (PCA, energy peaks, nonce word handling) support morphology learning claims.

Weaknesses
- Encoder weighting is unnormalized and the final tanh is unexplained; magnitude grows with word length, casting doubt on morphological interpretation.
- HGRU formulation, indexing, reset semantics, decoding behavior, and batching matrix R are under-specified and unclear.
- BPE baseline and comparison fairness are weak: missing BPE settings, vocabulary details, and inconsistent training-time comparisons across papers/hardware.
- Evaluation gaps: BLEU/tokenization details unclear, inconsistent dev/test splits, no significance testing, no systematic robustness or quantitative morphology evaluation.
- Presentation and referencing issues, unexplained design choices (e.g., 120-char <unk>), and some unsupported claims about simplicity/size.

Key questions for authors
- Why not normalize prefix weights (softmax/sigmoid) and can you ablate normalization/nonlinearities and word-length sensitivity?
- Clarify HGRU equations, reset/indexing rules, delimiter prediction, beam sizes, and how looping before delimiters is prevented.
- Provide full BPE baseline details (merges, shared vocabs, tuning) and compare to stronger Sennrich-style baselines under identical preprocessing.
- Report standardized throughput (chars/sec, updates/sec), convergence vs wall-clock under identical hardware, and how unfolding scales with long words/sentences.
- Provide quantitative morphology/segmentation evaluations and robustness tests for non-segmented or templatic languages.

Suggested fixes and experiments
- Run clearer ablations (char-CNN, BiLSTM-last-state, standard char decoder without HGRU resets, remove target-side word feedback).
- Report BLEU with standard WMT tokenization/casing, include significance tests, and clarify data splits per language pair.
- Expand robustness tests with controlled input noise and OOV/nonce evaluations; fix typos, equation inconsistencies, complete citations, and consider releasing code.

Overall
- Combining a learned morphological word encoder with an efficient hierarchical character decoder is promising, but the paper needs clearer exposition, stronger/fairer baselines, and more rigorous empirical support.
