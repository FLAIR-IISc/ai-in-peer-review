Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that combines a morphology-aware character-to-word encoder with a hierarchical decoder that generates target words as character sequences. The core ideas are:
1) An explicit morphology-learning word encoder: two RNNs are used to compute (a) prefix-based character representations and (b) position-wise weights, aggregated into a word vector. This aims to learn morpheme-like units and their composition.
2) An efficient hierarchical character decoder: a first-level “word” decoder produces a state for each target word; a second-level character decoder (HGRU) generates characters conditioned on this state and resets at delimiters. A batching trick (“unfolding” matrix) enables efficient training.
3) Empirical results on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En showing competitive BLEU to state-of-the-art character-level systems and stronger early-epoch performance than a BPE baseline, with qualitative evidence of morphology learning and robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.

Strengths
1) Clear architectural motivation and design. The model keeps encoder time steps at the word level while decoding at the character level, directly addressing large-vocabulary softmax and OOV issues without sacrificing alignment/attention quality. This is a well-motivated compromise between pure-char and word-level models.
2) Competitive results with appealing training dynamics. DCNMT achieves BLEU comparable to prior character systems (e.g., Cs–En matching Lee et al., 2016; En–Cs close to Luong & Manning, 2016) and surpasses the cited BPE baseline. The observation that one-epoch performance already exceeds BPE on En–Fr and En–Cs is practically valuable for resource/time-constrained training.
3) Efficiency contributions in the decoder. The HGRU with settable state and the unfolding/batching mechanism address a known bottleneck in hierarchical char decoders (cf. Ling et al., 2015b; Luong & Manning, 2016). Even if absolute wall-clock comparisons across papers are imperfect, the combination of smaller parameter count and reduced passes is technically meaningful.
4) Evidence that the encoder learns morphology. The position-wise “energy” profiles peaking at morpheme boundaries and PCA plots separating derivational pairs suggest that the encoder captures morpheme structure better than a plain char-RNN. The qualitative robustness to misspellings and nonce derivations aligns with the intended benefits of character-level modeling.
5) End-to-end training without explicit segmentation. Unlike some earlier hierarchical approaches requiring additional supervision or layer-wise training, the paper presents a single-stage training procedure, broadening accessibility.

Weaknesses
1) Limited and unclear novelty relative to prior hierarchical decoders. Hierarchical word→character decoding and resetting per word were introduced in earlier NMT work (e.g., Ling et al., 2015b; Luong & Manning, 2016). The HGRU’s “settable state” and the unfolding trick are useful engineering choices, but their conceptual difference and empirical impact over stronger baselines (e.g., a standard GRU/LSTM with per-word reset and simple broadcasting) are not isolated. An ablation specifically comparing HGRU vs. a standard char decoder with the same training pipeline is needed.
2) Insufficient ablations for the word encoder. The weighted sum of prefix states (with exp-scores and no normalization) is plausible but ad hoc. The paper compares against an “ordinary RNN encoder” using the last state, but does not benchmark against common alternatives such as CNN encoders (Kim et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2016) or a biRNN with character-level attention over positions. Ablations on (a) normalization vs. no normalization of weights, (b) removing the weighting network, and (c) replacing the encoder with CNN/biRNN+attention would clarify what drives the gains.
3) Morphology evaluation is mostly qualitative. Energy heatmaps and PCA are suggestive but not conclusive. A quantitative segmentation evaluation (e.g., F1 vs. Morfessor or gold morph boundaries) or analogy/inflection probing would substantiate the claim of “learning morphology.” The misspelling/nonce examples are anecdotal; a controlled robustness evaluation (e.g., perturbation tests on dev/test) would strengthen the case.
4) Comparability of efficiency and BLEU numbers. Training days/epochs and model sizes are compiled from heterogeneous sources. Without controlled throughput measurements (tokens/sec, updates/sec) and identical preprocessing/beam settings, the efficiency comparisons are hard to interpret. BLEU reporting would also benefit from specifying tokenization, case-sensitivity, beam size/length penalty, and detokenization scripts to ensure apples-to-apples comparison.
5) Reproducibility details are thin. Important training choices (dropout, gradient clipping, attention variant, normalization, initialization, beam size/penalties) are not fully specified, and code availability is not indicated. The handling of characters mapped to <unk> in languages with rich diacritics, and its effect on accuracy, should be clarified.

Questions to be addressed in rebuttal (essential)
- Decoder novelty and impact: In what precise ways does HGRU differ from prior hierarchical character decoders (Ling et al., 2015b; Luong & Manning, 2016)? Can a controlled ablation (same data/hyperparameters) quantify the benefit of (a) settable state vs. simple reset, and (b) the unfolding trick vs. naive broadcasting?
- Word encoder design: Why choose unnormalized exp(aff(h_t)) weights followed by tanh over the sum? Were softmax-normalized weights or attention mechanisms tried, and with what effect on BLEU and speed?
- Evaluation settings: What beam size, length penalty, and tokenization/detokenization were used for BLEU? Are scores case-sensitive and computed with the official WMT mteval/multi-bleu?
- Efficiency fairness: Can throughput (characters/sec, updates/sec) and wall-clock training time be reported for DCNMT and a strong reimplementation of a prior hierarchical baseline on the same hardware?
- Character vocabulary: What percentage of training/test characters are mapped to <unk> for Czech and French, and how sensitive are results to this list?

Suggestions for improvement (feasible within a revision cycle)
Essential:
- Add targeted ablations for the word encoder (normalization, removal of weighting network, CNN/bidir-attention baselines) and the decoder (HGRU vs. standard GRU/LSTM), reporting BLEU and training speed.
- Provide thorough evaluation details and standardized BLEU computation; report throughput and memory usage to substantiate efficiency claims.

Nice-to-have:
- Include a small-scale quantitative morphology experiment (e.g., segmentation F1 against Morfessor on PTB or a morphologically annotated subset; or a robustness study with controlled misspellings).
- Release code or detailed pseudocode for HGRU and the unfolding matrix construction to aid reproducibility.
- Clarify and tidy Table 1 (consistent notation, ensure that cited numbers match their settings), and correct minor textual issues in references.
