Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT system (DCNMT) that aims to learn morphology explicitly and decode at the character level while keeping encoder sequence lengths close to word-level models. The two main technical components are:
1) A word encoder that composes word representations from characters using two RNNs: one RNN produces prefix representations, and a second BiRNN produces scalar “energy” weights per prefix to form a weighted sum. This is intended to discover morphemes and their composition rules.
2) A hierarchical decoder: a first-level (word-level) decoder produces per-word states; a second-level “HGRU” character decoder emits characters until a delimiter, resetting to the next word-level state at boundaries. A batching/unfolding trick repeats the word-level state across the characters of a word to train efficiently.

Empirically, DCNMT achieves BLEU comparable to recent character-level systems and outperforms a BPE baseline on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, with relatively fast early improvements (after one epoch). Qualitative analyses suggest the encoder attends to morpheme boundaries and the model handles misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary translation with a coherent architecture. The hierarchical decoder reuses word-level contextualization while generating characters, offering a clean way to decouple semantic planning (word-level) from surface realization (character-level).
- Practical training speedups. The unfolding/batching trick for conditioning the character decoder on word-level states is simple and effective. Despite six RNNs, model size remains modest (~54M), and the early-epoch BLEU gains are compelling.
- Competitive results with prior character-level work. On En–Fr and En–Cs, final BLEU is comparable to Lee et al. (2016) and Luong & Manning (2016), with better-than-BPE scores under the paper’s setup; the ablation (CNMT vs. DCNMT) suggests the proposed word encoder contributes.
- Qualitative evidence of morphology learning and robustness. PCA plots, energy peaks at morpheme boundaries, and examples of handling misspellings/nonce words align with the paper’s goal and are valuable for understanding what the model learns.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and rigor of the encoder formulation are insufficient. The weighting scheme wt = exp(aff(ht)) is unnormalized; the final tanh on the weighted sum is introduced “as it performs best,” but without justification or ablation. Without normalization, the magnitude of the sum grows with word length, which could confound the claimed morphological interpretation. More careful analysis (e.g., softmax vs. sigmoid vs. no norm, effect on performance/interpretability) is needed.
- HGRU specification and decoding details are under-specified. Eq. (2) reuses gt−1 on both sides; indexing and reset semantics are unclear. Beam-search settings, delimiter prediction behavior, and failure modes (over/under-segmentation) are not reported. The batching/unfolding matrix R is described informally; complexity and implementation details are not fully clear.
- Baselines and fairness of comparisons. The BPE baseline appears weak relative to published WMT results for the same years; details such as number of merges, vocabulary sizes, and shared vs. separate BPE are missing. Training time comparisons mix different model sizes, datasets, and hardware budgets across papers. The claim “more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models” is not convincingly substantiated beyond anecdotal wall-clock numbers. Tokens/sec, convergence curves, and controlled ablations would help.
- Evaluation gaps. BLEU computation/tokenization settings are not fully specified; dev/test sets differ across language pairs (WMT’14 data but newstest2015 test?), making it hard to compare externally. No significance testing, no systematic robustness evaluation (e.g., controlled noise rates), and no quantitative morphology evaluation (e.g., segmentation accuracy vs. Morfessor) are provided.
- Presentation and referencing issues. Several equations and figures are hard to follow; references are incomplete; some claims (e.g., “our model is the simplest and the smallest”) are not consistently supported by reported numbers. The “120 most frequent characters” choice introduces a character-level <unk>, contradicting the “avoids large vocabulary radically” claim; its impact is not analyzed.

Questions to Authors
- Word encoder: Why not normalize the weights (e.g., softmax over positions) as in attention? Please provide ablations comparing normalized vs. unnormalized weighting and different nonlinearities after summation. How sensitive is performance to word length with the current scheme?
- HGRU: Please clarify Eq. (2) and the exact reset/update equations, including indexing of si and delimiter handling at training and test time. What beam sizes (word-level/character-level) are used? How do you prevent pathological looping before delimiter emission?
- BPE baseline: How many merges? Are vocabularies shared across source/target? Did you tune BPE settings for each language pair? Can you compare to a stronger BPE+attention baseline (e.g., Sennrich et al., 2016) under the same data/preprocessing?
- Efficiency: Please report standardized throughput (characters/sec, updates/sec) and convergence vs. wall-clock plots under identical hardware. How does the unfolding trick scale with long words and long sentences?
- Morphology: Can you provide a quantitative segmentation evaluation on a morphologically annotated corpus (precision/recall), or at least intrinsic probes (e.g., prefix/suffix similarity, analogies)? How robust is the approach for non-segmented scripts or languages with rich templatic morphology?

Additional Comments and Suggestions
- Provide stronger and clearer ablations: (i) replace the proposed encoder with char-CNN and BiLSTM-last-state; (ii) use a standard char decoder without HGRU resets but with the same batching trick; (iii) remove the target-side word-encoder feedback rYt−1 to measure its impact.
- Report BLEU with standard WMT tokenization and indicate casing; include significance tests. Clarify data splits (train/dev/test) per language pair.
- Expand robustness evaluation: systematically corrupt inputs at varying noise levels; evaluate OOV handling on constructed nonce words and true OOVs.
- Fix typos/equation inconsistencies; complete citations. Consider releasing code to support reproducibility.

Overall, the idea of combining a learned morphological word encoder with an efficient hierarchical character decoder is interesting and promising, and the early-epoch gains are appealing. However, the paper would benefit from clearer technical exposition, stronger baselines, and more rigorous empirical support for both efficiency and morphology-learning claims.
