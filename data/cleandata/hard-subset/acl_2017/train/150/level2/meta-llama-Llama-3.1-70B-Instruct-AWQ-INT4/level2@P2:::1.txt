**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that learns morphology at the character level. The model, called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT), consists of six recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and uses a hierarchical decoder to generate translations at the character level. The authors claim that their model avoids the large vocabulary issue and is more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel architecture for character-level NMT**: The authors propose a new architecture that learns morphology at the character level, which is a significant contribution to the field of NMT.
2. **Hierarchical decoder for efficient training**: The authors introduce a hierarchical decoder that allows for efficient training of the model, which is a major advantage over previous character-level models.
3. **Ability to learn morphology**: The authors demonstrate that their model is able to learn morphology, which is a key aspect of language understanding.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The authors show that their model achieves a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks.
2. **Efficient training**: The authors demonstrate that their model is more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models.
3. **Ability to translate out-of-vocabulary words**: The authors show that their model is able to translate misspelled words and nonce words, which is a significant advantage over word-based models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Complexity of the model**: The authors' model consists of six RNNs, which may be too complex and difficult to train for some users.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on three language pairs, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach.
3. **Comparison to state-of-the-art models**: The authors do not compare their model to the latest state-of-the-art models in NMT, which may make it difficult to assess the significance of their contributions.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the authors' model handle languages with complex morphology, such as Arabic or Russian?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the training procedure, including the hyperparameters and the optimization algorithm used?
3. How does the authors' model perform on other language pairs, such as En-De or En-Es?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' model is a significant contribution to the field of NMT, and their evaluation results are promising. However, the authors should consider providing more details on the training procedure and evaluating their model on a wider range of language pairs to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach.
