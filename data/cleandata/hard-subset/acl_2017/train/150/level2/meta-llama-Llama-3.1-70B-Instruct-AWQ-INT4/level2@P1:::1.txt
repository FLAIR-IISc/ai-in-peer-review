**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A word encoder that learns the representation of morphemes and the rules of how they are combined, which can be viewed as learning distributed representations of words explicitly.
2. A hierarchical decoder that decodes at character level, which can efficiently generate a character sequence based on the given state until generating a delimiter.
3. A deep character-level neural machine translation model that achieves a high translation performance comparable to the state-of-the-art neural machine translation models.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. **Novel Architecture**: The proposed DCNMT architecture is novel and has the potential to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issue that word-level models suffer from. The use of two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder is an innovative approach to learning morphology and decoding at character level.
2. **Efficient Training**: The authors demonstrate that their model can be efficiently trained in an end-to-end fashion, which is a significant advantage over other character-level models that require layer-wise training and supervision for the attention component.
3. **Competitive Results**: The experimental results show that the proposed DCNMT model achieves competitive results with the state-of-the-art neural machine translation models, including word-level and character-level models.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. **Complexity of the Model**: The proposed DCNMT model consists of six recurrent neural networks, which may be computationally expensive and require significant resources to train.
2. **Limited Analysis**: The authors provide limited analysis on the effectiveness of their model in learning morphology and its impact on translation performance. More in-depth analysis and comparison with other models would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Comparison with Other Character-Level Models**: The authors only compare their model with a few other character-level models, and it would be useful to include more comparisons with other state-of-the-art character-level models.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the training process and the hyperparameters used for the DCNMT model?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of rare words and out-of-vocabulary words in the proposed DCNMT model?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the effectiveness of the word encoder in learning morphology and its impact on translation performance?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed architecture and experimental results. However, there are some minor issues with the formatting and referencing that need to be addressed.
