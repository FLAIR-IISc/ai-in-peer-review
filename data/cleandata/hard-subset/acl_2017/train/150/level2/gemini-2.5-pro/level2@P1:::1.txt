**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to tackle the out-of-vocabulary problem and model morphology explicitly. The model features two main architectural innovations. First, on the encoder side, it introduces a novel word encoder composed of two RNNs. This encoder computes a representation for each word as a weighted sum of its character-prefix representations, with the weights learned by a separate BiRNN, aiming to automatically discover and weigh morphemes. Second, on the decoder side, it employs a hierarchical, two-level architecture. A word-level decoder generates a sequence of hidden states, and a character-level decoder uses these states to initialize its own state and generate characters for each target word. The paper presents an efficient batching method for this hierarchical structure. The entire six-RNN model is trained end-to-end. Experiments on English-French and English-Czech translation show that the model is competitive with state-of-the-art character and subword-level systems, while being more parameter-efficient and converging significantly faster. Qualitative analyses are provided to demonstrate the model's ability to learn morphology.

**Main contributions:**

1.  A novel character-level word encoder architecture that learns representations by dynamically weighting character prefixes, providing a way to explicitly model and learn morphological structure within words.
2.  An efficient, hierarchical character-level decoder that combines word-level guidance with character-level generation, making a deep character-level model practical to train in batches.
3.  Strong empirical results on both morphologically poor (En-Fr) and rich (En-Cs, Cs-En) language pairs. The model demonstrates impressive training efficiency, achieving strong performance after only one epoch, and its final results are competitive with contemporary state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed architecture is a clear strength. Both the source-side word encoder and the target-side hierarchical decoder are well-motivated and thoughtfully designed. The idea of learning a weighted combination of character prefixes to represent a word is an intuitive way to capture morphology, and the qualitative analysis in Figure 4 provides compelling evidence that it works as intended. Similarly, the hierarchical decoder is an elegant solution to the challenge of generating long character sequences while maintaining some higher-level (word) structure, and the proposed unfolding matrix for efficient batching is a valuable technical contribution.

2.  **Impressive Training Efficiency and Performance:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that the model is not only effective but also highly efficient. The results in Table 1, showing that the model outperforms a strong BPE-based baseline after just one epoch of training, are particularly striking. This fast convergence is a significant practical advantage over other character-level models that are often reported to be slow and difficult to train. The final BLEU scores are competitive with other state-of-the-art systems of the time, validating the overall approach.

3.  **Strong Qualitative Analysis:** Beyond reporting BLEU scores, the authors provide insightful analyses that support their core claims about learning morphology. The PCA visualizations (Figure 3) effectively contrast the representations learned by their encoder with a standard RNN encoder. The heatmaps showing learned morpheme weights (Figure 4) and the examples of handling misspelled and nonce words (Table 2) provide strong qualitative evidence that the model is learning useful linguistic structure, which is a desirable property and helps to explain the model's success. This level of analysis significantly strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Formulations:** Some central parts of the model description could be clearer, which makes reproducibility difficult.
    *   In Section 3.1, the description of how the word representation is computed is ambiguous. The text says `wt = exp(aff(ht))` but then states that instead of normalization, a `tanh` activation is used because it "performs best". It is unclear if the final word representation is `tanh(sum(exp(aff(ht)) * rt))` or if `tanh` replaces the `exp` function in the weight calculation itself. A precise mathematical equation for the final word vector `r_word` would resolve this.
    *   In the description of the HGRU (Section 3.2), Equation (2) appears to contain a typo, with `g_{t-1}` appearing on both sides of the update rule for the gated state. This makes the crucial state-resetting mechanism unclear.

2.  **Comparison to CNN-based Encoders:** The paper dismisses CNN-based character encoders by stating that the proposed RNN-based encoder "is able to generate a meaningful representation of the word," implying that CNNs cannot. This claim is not sufficiently substantiated. CNNs are also known to learn powerful and meaningful subword features. A more direct empirical comparison (e.g., swapping the proposed word encoder with a CNN encoder within the same framework) or a more detailed justification for this claim would make the argument stronger.

3.  **Details of the Hierarchical Decoder Training:** The unfolding matrix `R` is a clever trick for efficient batching, but its description is purely textual. A small, concrete numerical example showing the word-level decoder outputs, the target character sequence, and the resulting explicit matrix `R` would significantly improve the reader's understanding of this important implementation detail.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the exact mathematical formula for the final word representation computed by the source word encoder in Section 3.1? Specifically, how are the weights `w_t` and prefix representations `r_t` combined, and where is the `tanh` function applied?

2.  Regarding the HGRU update in Section 3.2, could you please clarify or correct Equation (2)? It seems to have the same term `g_{t-1}` on both sides of the equation.

3.  The training efficiency noted in Table 1 is a key result. To help contextualize this, could you provide the approximate training speed (e.g., in source words/sec) for your model and for the bpe2bpe baseline, as measured on the same hardware? This would provide a more direct comparison of computational cost.
