- Novel source word encoder that composes word vectors from characters using two RNNs and attention-like weights to capture morphology.
- Efficient two-level hierarchical decoder: a word-level RNN produces states and an HGRU character RNN generates characters with an unfolding mechanism for batched training.
- Strong empirical results on En–Fr and En–Cs: outperforms BPE baseline, matches state-of-the-art char models, and converges much faster (notably strong after one epoch).
- Well-motivated architecture: char-level self-attention encoder and hierarchical decoder address limitations of word- and standard char-level NMT.
- Fast convergence and strong BLEU scores: demonstrates practical advantage of learning morphology through quicker generalization.
- Convincing qualitative analysis: PCA of embeddings, encoder weight visualizations, and examples show morpheme sensitivity and robustness to misspellings/nonces.
- Encoder design choices are under-justified: unnormalized exp weights and a tanh on the final sum deviate from standard attention and may cause magnitude/optimization issues.
- Insufficient ablation study: need experiments isolating the contributions of the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder and alternative encoder/decoder combos.
- Inference procedure is under-described: training uses a precomputed unfolding matrix with ground-truth boundaries, but generation steps and any discrepancy with batched training are unclear.
- Question: why not normalize encoder weights with softmax and why apply tanh to the final weighted sum—any comparisons to standard attention?
- Question: during inference (no known target boundaries), is feedback r_{Y,t-1} computed by re-encoding the generated character sequence since the last delimiter?
