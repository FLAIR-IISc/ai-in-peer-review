- Proposes DCNMT, a deep character-level NMT that learns word morphology without explicit segmentation using a hierarchical architecture.
- Novel source word encoder uses two RNNs to compute weighted character-sequence representations (capturing morphemes); hierarchical decoder generates target words character-by-character guided by a word-level RNN.
- Strong empirical results: competitive BLEU on En–Fr and En–Cs and notably fast early convergence compared to subword models.
- Architectural innovation: intuitive and effective word encoder that avoids pre-defined vocabularies or BPE-style segmentation.
- Quantitative strength: matches state-of-the-art BLEU while showing superior training efficiency in early epochs.
- Qualitative strength: visualizations and examples (e.g., misspellings, nonce words) demonstrate learned morphological relationships and robustness to unknown forms.
- Clarity issue: the hierarchical decoder’s training mechanism—particularly the unfolding matrix R—is under-specified, hindering reproducibility and assessment of batching efficiency.
- Ablation gap: lacks detailed component-wise analysis (e.g., impact of each encoder part, tanh vs softmax for character weights).
- Efficiency claims need finer-grained profiling (e.g., steps/sec or FLOPs) since the model uses six separate RNNs and current metrics (days/epochs) are coarse.
