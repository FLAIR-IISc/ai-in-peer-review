- Novel character-based word encoder: two coupled RNNs produce prefix representations and learn weights to compose word embeddings, explicitly modeling morphology.  
- Hierarchical character-level decoder: two-level decoder (word-level states + character-level HGRU) with an "unfolding" matrix enabling efficient batch training.  
- Strong empirical results: competitive BLEU on En-Fr and En-Cs, very fast training (strong performance after one epoch), and qualitative evidence of morphological awareness.  
- Well-motivated, original architecture: encoder that weights morpheme-like prefixes is intuitive and moves beyond simple char-RNN/CNN composition.  
- Impressive training efficiency and performance: outperforms BPE baseline rapidly and trains faster than cited character-level models.  
- Thorough qualitative analysis: robust handling of misspellings and nonce/morphologically complex words demonstrates generalization.  
- Decoder description unclear: construction and role of the unfolding matrix R and auxiliary sequence a in HGRU and batch training lack precise, reproducible detail.  
- Limited ablations: insufficient component-level analysis (e.g., encoder benefits on En-Cs/Cs-En, and hierarchical decoder vs. flat char decoder).  
- Model complexity and inference speed under-discussed: six RNNs and ~54M params; decoding latency (character-by-character inference) not evaluated.  
- Question — inference delimiting: how is auxiliary sequence a (word-boundary signals) determined during beam search/inference when ground-truth delimiters are unavailable?  
- Question — encoder weighting: why use tanh for prefix weights instead of a normalized function (e.g., softmax); what motivates and empirically justifies unnormalized weights?
