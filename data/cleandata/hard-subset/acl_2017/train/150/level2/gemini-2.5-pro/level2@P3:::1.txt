**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the challenges of large vocabularies and morphologically rich languages. The model employs a hierarchical architecture that learns representations at multiple granularities: from characters to subwords, words, and sentences. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel character-based word encoder:** The work proposes a new method for composing word representations from characters. It uses two coupled RNNs: one to generate representations for character prefixes and another to learn a weight for each prefix. The final word embedding is a weighted sum of these prefix representations. This architecture is designed to explicitly model and learn morphology.
2.  **An efficient hierarchical character-level decoder:** To avoid the computational cost of decoding long character sequences, a two-level decoder is proposed. A word-level decoder generates a sequence of state vectors, and a character-level decoder uses these vectors to condition the generation of character sequences for each target word. A specific mechanism involving a custom GRU variant (HGRU) and an "unfolding" matrix is introduced to enable efficient batch training.
3.  **Strong empirical results showing high training efficiency and morphological awareness:** The proposed model achieves BLEU scores competitive with state-of-the-art character-level systems on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks. Notably, the model demonstrates remarkable training efficiency, achieving strong performance after just one epoch. The paper also provides compelling qualitative analyses to support the claim that its word encoder successfully learns morphological patterns.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and novel model architecture:** The architecture is thoughtfully designed to tackle known issues in NMT. The word encoder's design, which learns to weight character prefixes corresponding to morphemes, is an original and intuitive approach to building word representations. This moves beyond simpler character-composition functions like standard RNNs or CNNs. The qualitative analyses in Section 5.2 (Figs. 3, 4, and 5) provide convincing evidence that this design is effective at learning structured and meaningful representations that capture morphological regularities.
2.  **Impressive training efficiency and performance:** The empirical results are strong. The model not only achieves competitive final BLEU scores but does so with significantly less training time compared to other character-level models cited in Table 1. The ability to outperform a BPE-based model after just a single training epoch is a significant practical advantage, suggesting that the architecture's inductive biases facilitate rapid learning.
3.  **Thorough qualitative analysis:** The paper provides excellent supporting analysis beyond aggregate performance metrics. The demonstration that the model can correctly translate misspelled words and morphologically complex nonce words (e.g., "convenienter" in Table 2) is a powerful illustration of its robustness and generalization capabilities. These examples clearly showcase the practical benefits of the proposed approach over traditional word-level or subword-level models that are brittle to OOV phenomena.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of clarity in the description of the decoder:** The mechanism for efficient batch training in the hierarchical decoder could be explained more clearly. Section 3.2 describes an "unfolding matrix" `R` and an auxiliary sequence `a` in the HGRU, but the connection between them and the overall training procedure is difficult to follow. A more detailed walkthrough of how `R` is constructed from a batch of target sentences and how it relates to the gate `a` in Equation 2 would improve the clarity and reproducibility of this core component. Given that efficiency is a major claimed contribution, a precise description is essential.
2.  **Limited ablation studies:** The model combines several novel components, and the paper would be strengthened by ablating them to isolate their individual contributions. While Table 1 includes a comparison between the proposed DCNMT and a simpler "CNMT" on the En-Fr task, this comparison is absent for the En-Cs and Cs-En tasks, where the proposed morphological encoder is expected to provide the greatest benefit. Further analysis comparing the hierarchical decoder against a standard flat character decoder, while keeping the encoder fixed, would also help quantify the benefits of the hierarchical design.
3.  **Under-discussed model complexity and inference speed:** The model is complex, involving six separate RNNs. The paper claims it is "the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size," but its parameter count (~54M) is comparable to several other models in Table 1. While training time per epoch is shown to be fast, the decoding speed at inference time is not discussed. Character-by-character generation can be significantly slower than word- or subword-level generation. A discussion of inference latency would provide a more complete picture of the model's practical trade-offs.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The HGRU update (Eq. 2) relies on an auxiliary sequence `a` to signal word boundaries. This is straightforward during training when the target sentence is known. How is this sequence `a` determined during inference with beam search, when the ground-truth delimiters are not available? Does the model learn to predict a special delimiter token, and is this prediction used to dynamically construct `a`?
2.  In the proposed word encoder (Section 3.1), the weighted sum of character prefix representations uses a `tanh` activation function rather than a more conventional normalization like softmax over the weights `wt`. Could the paper elaborate on the motivation for this choice? What is the intuitive or empirical advantage of not forcing the weights to sum to one?
