### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The architecture is composed of four main hierarchical layers incorporating a total of six RNNs. Its primary contributions are:

1.  A novel source word encoder that composes a word representation from its characters. It uses two RNNs: one to generate character-level hidden states and a second (bidirectional) RNN to compute an unnormalized attention-like weight for each state. The final word vector is a weighted sum of these character states, which the authors show is effective at capturing morphological structure.
2.  An efficient, two-level hierarchical decoder. A word-level RNN generates a sequence of states, and for each state, a character-level RNN (a modified GRU called HGRU) generates the corresponding character sequence until a word delimiter is produced. The paper introduces a clever unfolding mechanism to make this hierarchical generation process efficient for batch training.
3.  Strong empirical results on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks. The model outperforms a competitive subword (BPE) baseline and is comparable to other state-of-the-art character-level models. Crucially, it demonstrates significantly faster learning, achieving strong results after just one epoch of training, and provides compelling qualitative analysis showing its ability to handle morphological variants and misspelled words.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed architecture is thoughtfully designed to tackle the limitations of both word-level and standard character-level NMT. The source word encoder, with its character-level self-attention mechanism, is a principled approach to composing word representations that are sensitive to morphology, moving beyond simple final-state or max-pooling methods. The hierarchical decoder is an elegant engineering solution to the challenge of generating long character sequences efficiently while still being guided by higher-level (word-like) semantics.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Faster Convergence:** The paper demonstrates impressive performance. The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with or superior to strong baselines, including BPE and other character-level systems, on both a morphologically poor (French) and a morphologically rich (Czech) language. The most striking result is the model's performance after a single training epoch, where it already surpasses the fully-trained BPE baseline on En-Fr and En-Cs. This supports the claim that by learning morphology, the model can generalize more quickly, which is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Excellent Qualitative Analysis to Support Claims:** The paper goes beyond BLEU scores to provide convincing evidence for its central claim of "learning morphology." The PCA projections in Figure 3 clearly show that the proposed encoder produces a more semantically structured embedding space for morphologically related words than a standard RNN encoder. Furthermore, the analysis of the encoder's internal weights in Figure 4 provides a compelling visualization of how the model learns to identify morpheme boundaries (e.g., segmenting "any-one" and "every-thing"). Finally, the examples in Table 2, showing correct translations for misspelled and nonce words ("convenienter"), are a powerful demonstration of the practical benefits of the model's robustness.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices in the Word Encoder:** The word encoder is the paper's core contribution, but some of its design choices are unusual and not well-justified. The weights `w_t` are computed using `exp(aff(h_t))` but are not normalized with a softmax, as is standard in attention mechanisms. The final representation is then passed through a `tanh` activation. This means the weights are not a probability distribution and the final vector's magnitude could grow with word length, potentially leading to optimization instability. The authors state that `tanh` "performs best in experiments," but a more thorough explanation or ablation comparing this choice to a standard normalized attention mechanism is needed to understand why this specific formulation is superior.

2.  **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The model is complex, with six RNNs and two key novel components (the word encoder and the hierarchical decoder). While the "CNMT" baseline provides some insight, a more detailed ablation study would significantly strengthen the paper by isolating the impact of each component. For example:
    *   How does the proposed word encoder perform with a standard flat character decoder?
    *   How does the hierarchical decoder perform with a simpler word encoder (e.g., a standard char-RNN or CNN)?
    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to determine how much of the performance gain comes from the morphological encoder versus the hierarchical decoding structure.

3.  **Clarity of the Inference Procedure:** The paper explains the training of the hierarchical decoder clearly, noting the use of an unfolding matrix `R` that relies on ground-truth target word boundaries. However, the description of the generation procedure (inference) in Section 3.4 is brief. It is implied that the model generates characters one by one until a delimiter is produced, at which point the generated word is encoded to produce the feedback for the next word-level state `s_{t+1}`. This process seems straightforward but should be stated more explicitly to confirm there are no discrepancies between the efficient batched training setup and the step-by-step inference process.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the word encoder (Section 3.1): Could you please elaborate on the motivation for not normalizing the weights `w_t` and for applying a `tanh` to the final weighted sum? Have you experimented with a standard softmax-normalized attention mechanism here, and if so, how did it compare? Understanding the reasoning behind this specific formulation is crucial for appreciating its novelty.

2.  The hierarchical decoder uses a pre-computed unfolding matrix `R` for efficient batch training, which depends on the known boundaries of target words. Could you please clarify how inference is performed, where these boundaries are not known a priori? Specifically, is the feedback representation `r_{Yt-1}` computed by running the target word encoder over the sequence of characters generated since the last delimiter?
