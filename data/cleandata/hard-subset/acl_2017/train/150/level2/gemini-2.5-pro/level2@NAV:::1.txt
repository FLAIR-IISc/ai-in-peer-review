**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) designed to learn word morphology without explicit segmentation. The model addresses the out-of-vocabulary problem inherent in word-level models while aiming for greater training efficiency than previous character-level approaches. I see two main contributions in this work:

1.  A novel and elegant hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT. This includes a unique source word encoder that uses two RNNs to compute a weighted representation of character sequences, intuitively capturing morphemes, and an efficient hierarchical decoder that generates target words character-by-character, guided by a word-level RNN.
2.  Strong empirical evidence demonstrating the model's effectiveness. The paper shows that DCNMT achieves competitive BLEU scores on both morphologically simple (En-Fr) and rich (En-Cs) language pairs, while also showing remarkably fast initial convergence compared to subword-based models.

**Strengths**

1.  The core architectural innovation is a significant strength. The proposed word encoder provides an intuitive and effective method for learning subword representations directly from character sequences. This design elegantly avoids the need for pre-defined vocabularies or subword segmentation algorithms like BPE.
2.  The quantitative results are compelling. The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems while demonstrating superior training efficiency, particularly in early epochs. This is a crucial practical advantage, suggesting the model learns useful representations quickly.
3.  The qualitative analysis is a highlight of the paper. The visualizations effectively argue that the word encoder is learning meaningful morphological relationships. Furthermore, the examples of successfully translating misspelled and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") provide powerful, concrete evidence of the model's robustness and its practical advantages over systems that treat such words as unknown tokens.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The description of the hierarchical decoder's training mechanism could be clearer. The "unfolding" matrix `R`, which is critical for efficient batching, is introduced with a brief example but lacks a formal definition. This ambiguity could hinder reproducibility and makes it difficult to fully assess the implementation's efficiency.
2.  The paper would benefit from a more extensive ablation study. While comparing DCNMT to a simpler RNN encoder (CNMT) is valuable, it is unclear how much each component of the novel word encoder contributes to the final performance. For example, an analysis of the choice to use `tanh` over a standard `softmax` for the character weights would strengthen the authors' claims about their design choices.
3.  The claims of efficiency are based on total training days and epochs. While informative, a more fine-grained analysis of computational cost, such as training steps per second, would provide a clearer comparison to other deep models, especially given that this architecture comprises six separate RNNs.
