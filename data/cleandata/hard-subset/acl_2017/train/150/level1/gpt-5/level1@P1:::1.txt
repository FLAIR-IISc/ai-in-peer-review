Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that combines (a) a novel character-based word encoder designed to capture morphology via two RNNs (one building prefix representations, the other assigning position-wise “energy” weights), and (b) a hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” GRU (HGRU) that generates target characters conditioned on a word-level decoder state, with a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to train efficiently. The model aims to avoid large-vocabulary softmaxes while retaining word-level sentence encoding length, and to train faster than fully character-level systems.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A morphology-aware word encoder that composes characters into word embeddings via decoupled RNNs and unnormalized energy weights, purportedly discovering morphemes and their composition.
2) A hierarchical character decoder with an HGRU that resets at word boundaries and an efficient batching/unfolding mechanism, enabling competitive training speed for character-level decoding.
3) Empirical results on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En showing competitive BLEU vs BPE and prior char-level systems, with faster early convergence, and qualitative analyses suggesting learned morphology and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Clear, principled approach to open-vocabulary MT with morphology-aware composition. The word encoder explicitly decouples (i) character-prefix composition and (ii) position-wise weighting, aligning well with the intuition that morpheme boundaries are salient. Compared to naive char-to-word encoders, the proposed weighting may better highlight morpheme endpoints without external segmentation.
- Practical hierarchical decoder design. The HGRU reset mechanism and the unfolding matrix R to align word-level states to character spans are technically neat and address a central bottleneck of hybrid word/character models: how to train end-to-end in batches without two separate passes or complicated conditional control flow.
- Competitive results with smaller models and faster early gains. On En–Fr, the model surpasses a BPE baseline after one epoch and reaches 30.56 BLEU, close to stronger char-only systems reported to require much longer training. On En–Cs and Cs–En, final BLEU is comparable to recent character-level systems. The parameter count (~54M) is modest relative to some prior char models, supporting the efficiency claim.
- Informative qualitative analyses. The PCA plots showing better clustering of derivationally related words with the proposed encoder (vs a simple RNN word encoder), the per-character energy profiles marking plausible morpheme boundaries (e.g., any-, every-, -way), and the ability to translate misspellings and nonce morphological forms (e.g., convenienter) all support the morphology-learning and robustness claims in a way that is intuitive and easy to inspect.
- End-to-end character decoding without explicit segmentation at the target side. The approach avoids post-hoc OOV handling and demonstrates the desirable property of generating plausible forms unseen during training.

Weaknesses
- Novelty vs prior character-aware/hierarchical NMT is somewhat incremental and under-validated. Related work (Ling et al., Lee et al., Luong & Manning, Serban et al.) already explores char-to-word encoders and hierarchical decoders. The proposed encoder’s specific weighting scheme (unnormalized exp(aff(h_t)) with tanh on the sum) is intriguing but insufficiently justified or compared against strong alternatives (e.g., softmax/attention-like normalization, CNN encoders, gated pooling). An ablation isolating the contributions of (i) the second RNN for weights and (ii) the tanh aggregation is needed.
- Efficiency claims are not entirely apples-to-apples. Training time and epochs are compared across different systems drawn from the literature with potentially differing hardware, optimization setups, and maximum sequence lengths (e.g., 50 BPE tokens vs 300–600 characters). The model itself requires 7–19 days for 1–2.8 epochs on Titan X, which is nontrivial. A controlled wall-clock and throughput analysis (tokens/s, characters/s) against in-house baselines (BPE, char2char) on identical hardware and hyperparameters is needed to substantiate the “more efficient” claim.
- Evaluation gaps and missing controls. There are no statistical significance tests; beam search configurations (beam size, length normalization, coverage/penalties) are unspecified; regularization details (dropout, gradient clipping) are missing. The BPE baseline and other baselines differ in max length and preprocessing, and in some cases are reported from other papers rather than reimplemented, which weakens the comparison.
- Morphology learning evidence is anecdotal. While the energy plots and PCA are compelling, there is no quantitative evaluation against gold morpheme or subword segmentation (e.g., MorphoChallenge datasets), nor a comparison to BPE or CNN encoders on morphological tasks. Claims that the model “learns morphology” would be strengthened with segmentation F1 or probing tasks.
- Clarity issues in the decoder and training procedure. The HGRU formulation depends on an auxiliary delimiter sequence a_t; how delimiters are defined and propagated during training/inference is only briefly mentioned. The unfolding matrix R is central to efficiency but lacks complexity analysis or memory overhead discussion. It is also unclear how the target word encoder feedback r_Y is computed at inference time when characters, not words, are being produced; the paper states an ordinary RNN encodes the previous target word, but this relies on correct delimiter detection during decoding.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder weighting: Why use unnormalized weights w_t = exp(aff(h_t)) and tanh on the weighted sum instead of a normalized attention/pooling? Did you try softmax normalization or gating? Any stability issues or saturation with tanh? Ablation results?
- Decoder details: What beam size and length normalization did you use at character level? How are delimiters handled during decoding to trigger state resets reliably? What happens when the model omits a delimiter?
- Efficiency: Can you report tokens/second (source chars, target chars), memory usage, and wall-clock for training and decoding vs in-house BPE and char2char baselines on the same GPU, same batch sizes?
- Ablations: Beyond CNMT vs DCNMT, can you provide (i) removing the weighting RNN (use last state), (ii) replacing the encoder with a CNN, (iii) replacing HGRU with a standard GRU+explicit reset, and (iv) training the same model with a normalized attention over character positions?
- Morphology: Can you evaluate induced segmentations quantitatively against a standard morphological segmentation resource and/or present probing tasks (e.g., predicting inflectional features from word embeddings)?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the target feedback r_Y during inference and how you buffer the last generated word’s characters to re-encode it on the fly.
- Some references lack complete bibliographic details; Table 1 mixes in-sourced and external numbers and uses varying max lengths, which should be harmonized.
- Minor writing issues (e.g., “settable state,” “intractable to conditionally pick outputs”) could be clarified; figures would benefit from higher resolution and consistent notation.

Overall, this is a solid and practically useful architecture with promising results and insightful analyses, but it would benefit from stronger ablations, controlled efficiency measurements, and more rigorous evaluation of morphology learning.
