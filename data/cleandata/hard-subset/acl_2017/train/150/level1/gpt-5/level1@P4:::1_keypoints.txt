- Proposes DCNMT: a deep character-level NMT with a morphology-aware word encoder and a hierarchical decoder that composes character outputs from word-level states.

- Word encoder: two RNNs — one accumulates prefix-sensitive character states, the other produces position-dependent weights intended to highlight morpheme-like boundaries.

- Hierarchical decoder: a word-level decoder produces word states; a resettable character-level HGRU generates characters until a delimiter and then resets to the next word state.

- Engineering trick: an “unfolding” matrix to broadcast word-level outputs to character time steps for efficient batched training.

- Empirical results: competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En, slightly below some recent char models, with faster convergence than some prior systems.

- Analyses show learned morphological regularities (PCA plots, energy peaks) and qualitative robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

- Contribution 1: hierarchical decoder with resettable HGRU and unfolding mechanism for efficient end-to-end character training with word-level planning.

- Contribution 2: morphology-learning word encoder combining prefix-aware states with position-specific weights, enabling segmentation-like behavior without supervision.

- Contribution 3: empirical study demonstrating competitive BLEU, faster training, and qualitative OOV robustness.

- Strength: clear architectural decomposition addressing char-level NMT pain points (sequence length, attention stability) with a practical batching solution.

- Strength: qualitative evidence that the word encoder captures morphemic structure via PCA and energy-peak analyses.

- Strength: ablation vs. ordinary char-RNN encoder showing DCNMT improves BLEU (e.g., +1.2 BLEU on En–Fr).

- Strength: competitive performance with relatively compact parameter counts and reported faster training to comparable BLEU.

- Weakness: comparisons and speed/BLEU claims are insufficiently controlled — baselines use different preprocessing, sizes, and budgets.

- Weakness: notational and technical clarity problems in the word encoder and HGRU definitions hinder reproducibility.

- Weakness: limited quantitative evidence isolating the hierarchical decoder’s benefits (no ablation vs. strong flat char decoder).

- Weakness: morphology analysis is qualitative; lacks evaluation against gold-standard morpheme segmentations.

- Weakness: incomplete decoding/training details (beam size, delimiter handling, length penalties, char vocab decisions) affecting reproducibility and BLEU.

- Question 1: Clarify weighting: is wt used unnormalized in r_word = tanh(sum_t wt r_t), and why use exp(aff(ht)) without normalization versus softmax/sigmoid?

- Question 2: Provide corrected HGRU equations, state initialization at boundaries, training delimiter usage, and inference reset behavior when delimiters are mispredicted.

- Question 3: Specify beam search settings, how hypotheses with differing delimiter counts sync with word-level states, and pruning at delimiter boundaries.

- Question 4: Did you train a controlled BPE baseline with identical settings; if not, please provide such controlled comparisons for fairness.

- Question 5: Provide ablations: (a) morphology-aware encoder only, (b) hierarchical vs. flat char decoder, and (c) alternative weighting functions (softmax/tanh/sigmoid).

- Question 6: Quantify morphology alignment against gold segmentation (e.g., MorphoChallenge) to support morphology-learning claims.

- Question 7: Specify delimiter vocabulary, target-side tokenization, delimiter error rates, and their impact on BLEU and decoding stability.
