Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture that (i) learns morphology via a character-based word encoder using two RNNs—one to produce prefix representations and another to weight them—yielding a compositional word embedding without an explicit subword inventory; and (ii) decodes with a hierarchical, two-level decoder where a word-level decoder conditions a character-level HGRU whose state is reset at word boundaries, with an unfolding scheme enabling efficient batching. Empirically, the model reports BLEU competitive with state-of-the-art character models and better than BPE baselines after limited training, with qualitative evidence of morphology learning and robustness to misspellings.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A morphology-aware word encoder that composes characters into words via weighted prefix states, learning morphemes implicitly.
2) A hierarchical character decoder (HGRU + unfolding) that enables efficient character-level decoding conditioned on word-level states.
3) An empirical finding that such a model can match strong baselines with fewer parameters and fewer epochs, and handle OOVs/misspellings.

Strengths
- Novel encoder design grounded in morphology: The weighted sum over prefix RNN states (with BiRNN-derived weights) is an interesting, linguistically motivated alternative to CNN/C2W encoders, and PCA/energy visualizations suggest the encoder separates stems and affixes better than an ordinary RNN encoder.
- Practical, efficient character decoding: The settable-state HGRU and unfolding matrix are clever engineering to bridge word- and character-time scales, enabling single-pass batching where prior hybrids needed multiple passes. Reported training time/params are favorable.
- Competitive performance quickly: Achieving higher BLEU than BPE after one epoch (En–Fr, En–Cs) and final BLEU close to SOTA character models is a strong pragmatic result, especially given smaller models.
- Robustness to OOV/misspellings: Qualitative examples (recovering misspellings; translating “convenienter”) demonstrate genuine advantages of character-level modeling beyond BLEU.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation fairness and rigor: Comparisons reuse results from different setups/hardware, and some baseline models are not trained to the same convergence criterion. No significance tests; limited metrics beyond BLEU.
- Insufficient ablations: The paper does not isolate gains from the morphology-aware encoder vs. the hierarchical decoder vs. batching trick (CNMT vs. DCNMT is only one cut). Normalization choice (tanh vs. softmax) for weights is not justified experimentally.
- Clarity on decoder mechanics: The HGRU equations and the unfolding matrix are introduced tersely; memory/runtime implications of Ty × T unfolding are not analyzed, and delimiter supervision/definition (tokenization, multilingual scripts) is under-specified.
- Scope: Experiments focus on En–Fr and (Cz) En–Cs; generalization to other scripts and highly agglutinative languages is unclear.

Questions to Authors
- What is the exact delimiter supervision during training for target words, and how robust is the approach to tokenization errors or languages without whitespace?
- Can you provide ablations: (a) encoder without learned weights (uniform/softmax), (b) HGRU replaced by standard GRU with resets, (c) removal of unfolding?
- What are peak GPU memory and tokens/sec vs. Lee et al. (2016) under identical hardware? Any scaling issues with the unfolding matrix for long sequences?
- Do results hold on an agglutinative language (e.g., Turkish, Finnish) and on non-Latin scripts (e.g., Chinese, Japanese with character vocab >120)?
