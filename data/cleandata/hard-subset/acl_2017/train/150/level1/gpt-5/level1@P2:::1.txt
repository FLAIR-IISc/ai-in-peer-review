Summary:
The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that (a) composes word representations from characters via a two-RNN word encoder intended to learn morphology and (b) decodes with a hierarchical decoder: a word-level RNN whose outputs condition a character-level HGRU that produces characters until a delimiter. A batching “unfolding” trick replicates word-level states across character positions to enable efficient training. The model uses six RNNs in total and is claimed to be more efficient than prior char-level approaches, to avoid large-vocabulary issues, and to learn morphology, with BLEU comparable to state of the art on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En.

Main contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
- A practical hierarchical character decoder with a “settable-state” HGRU and an efficient batching/unfolding scheme that avoids two-pass training and enables end-to-end character-level decoding conditioned on word-level states.
- A character-to-word encoder that computes a weighted sum over prefix RNN states, with weights predicted by a BiRNN over characters, intended to capture morpheme boundaries and composition.
- Empirical evidence (BLEU and qualitative analysis) suggesting the approach is competitive with state-of-the-art char-level NMT while being smaller and faster to train, and can handle misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths:
- Addresses a real bottleneck with character-level training efficiency: The hierarchical decoder and the unfolding matrix R are a clear, implementable idea to reconcile different granularities within batch training. This is a useful engineering contribution beyond prior hybrids that required multiple passes (e.g., Luong & Manning 2016).
- Competitive performance with smaller models: Reported model size (~54M) is lower than other char-level baselines (~69–76M), yet BLEU is in the same ballpark on En–Cs and Cs–En, and within a few BLEU on En–Fr. This suggests a favorable parameter-efficiency/quality trade-off.
- Qualitative handling of OOVs and nonce words: The demonstrations of recovering misspellings and compositional generalization (“convenienter”) illustrate a practical benefit of character-level generation that word/subword systems lack without copy/lexicon mechanisms.
- Attempt to learn morphology: The word encoder’s weighting over character prefixes is a plausible mechanism for learning morpheme-like units. The PCA plots and energy visualizations, while anecdotal, are consistent with the claim that the encoder separates stems and affixes better than a plain char-RNN.

Weaknesses:
- Claims vs. results misalignments and unclear baselines: The abstract claims “higher BLEU than BPE after one epoch on En–Fr and En–Cs,” but Table 1 shows En–Fr one-epoch DCNMT (28.13) below the BPE baseline (29.70). Comparisons draw on numbers from different papers with potentially different preprocessing, data sizes, and hardware; “training days” are thus not strictly comparable. Please standardize baselines (same data, tokenization, BLEU script, hardware) and clarify the discrepancy.
- Methodological clarity and correctness: The word encoder weighting is confusing. You define wt = exp(aff(ht)) but later say “instead of normalizing by ∑t exp(aff(ht)), we use tanh as it performs best.” However ran = tanh(∑ wtrt) is not a normalization of w; it is a nonlinearity on the weighted sum, and the scaling of wt remains unconstrained and length-dependent. This raises questions about stability and interpretation (e.g., “energy” as morpheme indicator). Similarly, the HGRU equations (2)–(6) introduce gating with an auxiliary delimiter signal; the notation mixes indices (gt−1 reused on both sides in (2)), and the role of qt, zt, and the elementwise operations is underexplained. A more careful derivation and ablations are needed.
- Limited and partly anecdotal morphology evaluation: The evidence relies on PCA plots for selected word sets and a few segmentation examples (e.g., “consum-er-s”), without quantitative evaluation against morphological segmentation or probing tasks. It is hard to conclude the model “learns morphology” beyond generic char-compositionality.
- Missing experimental details affecting reproducibility: Important settings are not specified (beam width, length normalization or penalties in char-level decoding, gradient clipping, dropout/regularization, early stopping criteria). There is no code link. Max lengths vary across sections (300 vs. 450). Dataset splits across WMT14/15 and dev/test choices are not always consistent with referenced baselines.
- Fairness of efficiency claims: Training-time comparisons pool numbers from different works with different hardware and optimization. The claim that DCNMT is “more efficient than word-based models and conventional character-based models” needs a controlled study (same GPU, batch size, sequence lengths, early stopping) and clear throughput metrics (tokens/sec, chars/sec) and convergence curves.

Questions to Authors:
- Please reconcile the En–Fr “one epoch beats BPE” claim with Table 1. What exact BPE baseline was reproduced on your setup? Did you re-train it or copy numbers from a different system?
- How are weights wt used exactly? Are they normalized (e.g., softmax) anywhere? If not, why exp(·) instead of, say, ReLU? Did you try a proper attention/softmax over character positions?
- Can you provide ablations: (a) ordinary char-RNN word encoder vs. your weighted encoder; (b) hierarchical decoder with/without the unfolding trick; (c) HGRU vs. standard GRU/LSTM with reset at delimiters?
- Decoding details: beam size at character level, any length normalization or coverage penalty? How do you prevent over/under-generation at character granularity?
- Morphology evaluation: Can you report quantitative segmentation quality on a morphological dataset (e.g., MorphoChallenge, CELEX) or probing tasks (predicting inflectional features), and compare to CNN/RNN char encoders and BPE?
- Efficiency: Please report training throughput (chars/sec), steps to reach specific BLEU on dev, and wall-clock on a fixed GPU for your model and your re-implemented baselines.

Additional comments:
- The paper would benefit from proofreading; several typos and notation inconsistencies make technical sections hard to parse.
- The idea is promising and practical; with clarified methodology, stronger controlled baselines, and quantitative morphology analysis, the work could be significantly strengthened.

Overall: Novelty is moderate (hierarchical char decoders exist), but the batching/HGRU engineering and compact model are interesting. However, clarity and evaluation gaps currently weaken the case.
