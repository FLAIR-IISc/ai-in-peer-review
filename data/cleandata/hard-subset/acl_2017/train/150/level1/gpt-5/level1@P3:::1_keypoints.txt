- Summary: Proposes DCNMT — character-level NMT with a weighted-sum RNN word encoder, a hierarchical settable-state character GRU decoder (HGRU), and an "unfolding" trick for efficient batched training; reports competitive BLEU and robustness to misspellings.

- Contribution 1: Efficient hierarchical character decoder (HGRU) + unfolding that conditions char generation on word-level states in single-pass batched training.

- Contribution 2: Two-RNN word encoder that learns morpheme-like weights over character-prefix embeddings to induce subword structure.

- Contribution 3: Empirical claim of robustness to misspellings/nonce words and competitive BLEU with relatively small parameter count and fast early training.

- Strength: Practical hierarchical decoder design and engineering ideas (settable-state GRU, unfolding) that reduce training complexity compared to earlier multi-pass methods.

- Strength: Competitive results with compact models (~54M params) and faster early learning (beats BPE after one epoch in some setups).

- Strength: Qualitative analyses (PCA, energy peaks, example segmentations) suggest the encoder learns subword/morphological structure.

- Strength: Demonstrated robustness to misspellings and nonce morphology, highlighting an advantage of char-level decoding.

- Weakness: Limited novelty relative to prior character/hierarchical architectures; main novelties appear to be the weighted-sum encoder and the unfolding trick.

- Weakness: Methodological clarity issues and equation inconsistencies (ambiguous equations, missing mappings, under-specified decoding/beam search and delimiter handling).

- Weakness: Experimental comparability problems — baselines use different preprocessing/training budgets, ablations limited, and no statistical significance or variance reported.

- Weakness: "Open vocabulary" claim weakened by restricting to top ~120 characters and mapping others to <unk>, especially problematic for languages with diacritics like Czech.

- Weakness: Morphology evidence is anecdotal and lacks quantitative evaluation against gold segmentation or truly morphologically rich languages.

- Question: Provide precise pseudocode/specification of HGRU and unfolding, fix Eq. (2), and clarify initialization and delimiter handling during decoding.

- Question: How is beam search implemented across word/character levels — are delimiters part of hypotheses and how are word-level states synchronized with emitted characters?

- Question: What is the full Czech character vocabulary, how many chars are mapped to <unk>, and what is the impact on BLEU?

- Question: Report runtime metrics (tokens/sec or chars/sec), peak memory, and wall-clock time vs baselines trained on the same hardware.

- Question: Provide controlled ablations: replace word encoder (e.g., CNN/RNN), remove unfolding, and use a pure character decoder to isolate contributions.

- Additional: Clarify whether source/target word encoders share parameters, and whether the target word encoder sees gold vs predicted chars at test time (exposure bias).

- Additional: Ensure BLEU computation/tokenization/casing is consistent with baselines and report statistical significance.

- Additional: Consider evaluations on more morphologically rich languages (e.g., Turkish/Finnish) or morphological segmentation benchmarks to strengthen claims.

- Additional: Improve reproducibility by releasing code and configuration files.
