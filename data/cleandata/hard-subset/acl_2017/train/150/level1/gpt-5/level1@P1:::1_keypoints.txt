- Paper proposes DCNMT: a deep character-level NMT with a morphology-aware character-to-word encoder and a hierarchical character decoder (HGRU) with an unfolding batching trick.
- Key contributions: (1) decoupled RNN word encoder with position-wise energy weights to capture morphemes, (2) HGRU hierarchical decoder with reset and efficient unfolding matrix, (3) empirical results showing competitive BLEU and faster early convergence vs BPE and prior char models.
- Strength: encoder explicitly separates prefix composition and position weighting, aligning with morpheme boundary intuition and potentially highlighting morpheme endpoints without external segmentation.
- Strength: practical HGRU design and unfolding matrix enable end-to-end batched training for hybrid word/character models without complex control flow.
- Strength: competitive empirical performance — surpasses BPE early on for En–Fr, matches recent char-level systems for En–Cs and Cs–En with a modest ~54M parameter model.
- Strength: informative qualitative analyses (PCA clusters, per-character energy profiles, robustness to misspellings/nonce words) support morphology learning and generalization claims.
- Strength: end-to-end character decoding avoids explicit target-side segmentation and OOV handling, generating plausible unseen forms.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental relative to prior char-aware/hierarchical NMT work; encoder weighting scheme is insufficiently justified and lacks comparison to strong alternatives.
- Weakness: efficiency claims are not controlled — training times compared across disparate setups and the reported 7–19 days for limited epochs on Titan X is substantial.
- Weakness: evaluation lacks statistical significance tests, beam/search details, and consistent baseline reimplementations or preprocessing controls.
- Weakness: morphology evidence is mainly anecdotal; no quantitative morpheme segmentation or probing evaluations are provided to substantiate “learns morphology.”
- Weakness: decoder/training clarity issues — delimiter handling, unfolding matrix complexity/memory, and on-the-fly target-word encoding at inference are under-specified.
- Question: why use unnormalized exp weights and tanh aggregation instead of normalized attention/gating, and are ablations available for these design choices?
- Question: what beam size, length normalization, and delimiter-handling strategy were used at decoding, and what happens if delimiters are omitted?
- Question: please report controlled efficiency metrics (tokens/sec, memory, wall-clock) vs in-house BPE and char2char baselines on identical hardware and settings.
- Question: provide ablations: remove weighting RNN, replace encoder with CNN, replace HGRU with standard GRU reset, and try normalized attention over character positions.
- Question: can induced segmentations be evaluated quantitatively against morphological segmentation datasets or via probing tasks for morphology?
- Additional comment: clarify how target feedback r_Y is computed online and how the last generated word is buffered/re-encoded during inference.
- Additional comment: harmonize Table 1 baselines, max lengths, and bibliographic details; improve figure resolution and notation consistency.
- Overall: solid, practical architecture with promising results and analyses, but needs stronger ablations, controlled efficiency comparisons, and quantitative morphology evaluations.
