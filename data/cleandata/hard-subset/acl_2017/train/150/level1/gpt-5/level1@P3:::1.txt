Summary and main contributions
The paper proposes a character-level NMT system (DCNMT) with (i) a word encoder that composes characters into word representations by a weighted sum of prefix RNN states, where weights are produced by a second (bi)RNN intended to capture morpheme boundaries; and (ii) a hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” GRU (HGRU) at the character level that is initialized from a word-level decoder state and generates characters until a delimiter. A training trick (“unfolding” matrix) broadcasts each word-level state across its character span to enable efficient batched training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report BLEU competitive with strong character models and better than a BPE baseline after one epoch; qualitative analyses suggest the encoder learns morphology and the model handles misspellings/nonce forms.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) An efficient hierarchical character decoder design (HGRU + unfolding) that conditions character generation on word-level states in a single-pass batch setup.
2) A two-RNN word encoder that aims to induce morphemic structure via learned weights over character-prefix embeddings.
3) Empirical evidence that the model is robust to misspellings/nonce words and reaches competitive BLEU with relatively small parameter count.

Strengths
- Tackles open-vocabulary translation with a practical hierarchical character decoder. The settable-state GRU and unfolding/broadcasting mechanism are appealing engineering ideas that plausibly reduce the training complexity seen in earlier hierarchical or hybrid char decoders (e.g., Ling et al., Luong & Manning) which required multi-pass training or supervision for the attention component.
- Competitive results with relatively compact models. Reported BLEU is on par with strong character-level systems (Lee et al., Luong & Manning) while using ~54M parameters and substantially shorter training (days/epochs). The “after one epoch” En–Fr/En–Cs results surpass the BPE baseline, suggesting faster early learning.
- Qualitative analysis connects to morphology. PCA visualizations, energy peaks at likely morpheme boundaries, and reasonable segmentations (e.g., consum-er-s, mon-day, foot-ball) are consistent with the claim that the encoder learns subword structure without explicit segmentation.
- Demonstrates robustness to misspellings and nonce morphology. The examples showing recovery of misspelled words and “convenienter” are convincing and highlight an advantage of character-level decoding over word/subword systems that rely on limited vocabularies.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work and insufficient positioning. Hierarchical decoders and character composition for words are well explored: C2W + hierarchical decoding (Ling et al. 2015a,b), hybrid word–char decoders (Luong & Manning 2016), and fully character-level encoder–decoder (Lee et al. 2016; Chung et al. 2016b). The main new elements appear to be (a) the specific weighted-sum word encoder and (b) the unfolding trick for batch training. The paper should more rigorously contrast these with prior architectures (what fails in layer-wise training; what exactly is new in HGRU vs standard GRU/LSTM resets).
- Clarity and correctness issues in the methodology. Key equations are inconsistent or ambiguous: w_t is defined with exp(aff(h_t)) but later “tanh instead of normalization” is mentioned; Eq. (2) seems to have g_{t-1} on both sides (likely g_t = (1−a_{t−1}) g_{t−1} + a_{t−1} s_i); the mapping from g_t to logits in Eq. (7) omits any linear layer. The decoding procedure with the hierarchical setup and beam search is under-specified (how delimiters are handled within the beam; when the next word-level state is consumed).
- Experimental rigor and comparability. The baselines are drawn from different papers with different preprocessing, model capacities, and training budgets. The BPE baseline (from Firat et al.) is not apples-to-apples with your code/hyperparameters, so the “one epoch beats BPE” claim is hard to interpret. CNMT vs DCNMT ablation is shown only for En–Fr; it is unclear which component (word encoder vs hierarchical decoder) drives gains. No statistical significance or run-to-run variance is reported.
- Open vocabulary claim is weakened by a restricted character set. Limiting to the 120 most frequent characters and mapping others to <unk> contradicts the “radically avoids large vocabulary” and “open vocabulary” narrative, especially for Czech with extensive diacritics. The paper should quantify character OOV rates and their impact.
- Morphology learning evidence is anecdotal. Visualizations and a few examples are suggestive but not conclusive. There is no quantitative evaluation against gold morphological segmentation or inflectional generalization tasks, and no analysis on truly challenging morphologies (e.g., Finnish/Turkish/Arabic) to substantiate the central claim.

Questions to authors
- Please provide a precise specification (or pseudocode) of HGRU and the unfolding matrix construction. Correct Eq. (2) and clarify how you handle initialization and delimiter detection during decoding.
- How is beam search implemented across the two levels? Are delimiter decisions part of the hypothesis, and how do you synchronize consumption of word-level states with emitted characters?
- What is the full character vocabulary for Czech (including all diacritics)? What fraction of training/test characters are mapped to <unk>, and how does that affect BLEU?
- Can you report tokens/sec (or chars/sec), peak memory, and wall-clock time versus strong baselines trained with your code on the same hardware?
- Please include controlled ablations: (i) replace your word encoder with CNN or simple RNN last-state; (ii) remove unfolding (naive training); (iii) character-only decoder without hierarchical state resets.

Additional comments
- Clarify whether source and target word encoders share parameters; detail how the target word encoder is trained and whether it receives predicted or gold characters at test time (exposure bias).
- The WMT14 En–Fr training with evaluation on newstest2015 is nonstandard; ensure BLEU computation (tokenization, case) is consistent across systems and report statistical significance.
- Consider adding evaluations on morphologically rich languages beyond Czech (e.g., Turkish/Finnish) and/or a morphological segmentation benchmark to strengthen the morphology claim.
- Reproducibility: please release code and configuration files.
