Summary
The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that seeks to address large-vocabulary issues and exploit morphology without explicit segmentation. It introduces (a) a word encoder that composes character representations via two RNNs: one accumulates prefix-sensitive character states and another assigns position-dependent weights intended to reflect morpheme boundaries; and (b) a hierarchical decoder: a word-level decoder produces word states, and a character-level HGRU “with settable state” generates characters until a delimiter is produced, after which the state is reset to the next word-level state. A batching trick (an unfolding matrix) broadcasts word-level outputs to character time steps for efficient training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En report BLEU comparable to or slightly below recent char-level models, while being faster to train than some prior char-level systems. Analyses (PCA plots, energy peaks) suggest learning of morphological regularities and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A hierarchical character decoder with an HGRU that can be reset by word-level states and a batching “unfolding” mechanism to train end-to-end efficiently on characters while maintaining a word-level planning signal.
2) A word encoder that learns morphology by combining prefix-aware character RNN states with position-specific weights produced by a (bi)RNN, yielding better word representations than an ordinary char-RNN encoder and enabling segmentation-like behavior without explicit supervision.
3) An empirical study on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En showing competitive BLEU to contemporary char-level models, faster convergence than some baselines, and qualitative robustness to misspelled/nonce words.

Strengths
- Clear architectural decomposition that targets known pain points of char-level NMT. The hierarchical decoder design, with a resettable character RNN driven by word-level states, directly addresses sequence length and attention stability issues in character decoding. The unfolding matrix provides a concrete, practical solution for batching and conditioning on word-level states at character time, which is a nontrivial engineering contribution.

- Evidence that the word encoder captures morphological regularities. The PCA visualizations and the learned “energy” peaks at plausible morpheme boundaries support the claim that the dual-RNN encoder learns subword structure. The qualitative examples (e.g., “convenienter”) and detected boundaries (e.g., “consum-er-s”, “mon-day”) align with expected morphemic splits.

- Ablation against an ordinary char-RNN word encoder (CNMT). The DCNMT variant improves BLEU over the ordinary RNN word encoder in the same framework (e.g., +1.2 BLEU on En–Fr test), isolating the benefit of the proposed morphology-aware word encoder.

- Competitive performance with relatively compact models and training time. Despite six RNNs, parameter counts are similar or smaller than some prior char/char2char systems. Reported training time to reach comparable BLEU (e.g., ~19–22 days vs. 30–90 days in cited systems) highlights efficiency advantages in this design.

Weaknesses
- Insufficiently controlled comparisons and overstated claims about speed and BLEU. Several baselines are taken from different papers with different preprocessing, model sizes, and training budgets. The paper claims “obtains a higher BLEU than the BPE-based model after training for one epoch on En–Fr and En–Cs,” but Table 1 shows En–Fr test BLEU after one epoch (28.13) below the BPE baseline (29.70), and Cs–En similarly lags after one epoch. Without replicated, same-code BPE/char baselines under identical settings, efficiency and accuracy advantages are not conclusively demonstrated.

- Notational and technical clarity issues in the word encoder and HGRU. The weight definition wt = exp(aff(ht)) combined with a final tanh on the summed representation is underspecified: no normalization, unclear rationale for exp followed by tanh, and ambiguity about allowing negative/relative weights. The HGRU update (Eq. 2) reassigns g at t−1 in-place, mixes indices (sit vs. si), and does not clearly separate initialization from recurrence. These issues hinder reproducibility and raise questions about exact behavior. Key details of delimiter handling in beam search and error propagation if delimiters are mispredicted are not analyzed.

- Limited quantitative analysis of the hierarchical decoder’s benefits. While the unfolding trick is described, there is no ablation comparing the hierarchical decoder to a strong single-level char decoder using the same encoder, nor measurements of delimiter prediction accuracy, attention stability, or length-normalized speedups. The claim that the approach stabilizes/accelerates character decoding would be stronger with such evidence.

- Morphology analysis is qualitative and lacks rigorous evaluation. The paper provides PCA plots and hand-picked examples, but does not evaluate segmentation quality against a morphological gold standard or measure correlation with morpheme boundaries quantitatively. It is unclear how robust the learned “energy” peaks are across languages and word types.

- Decoding and training details are incomplete. The paper states beam search at character level but does not specify beam width, length penalty, delimiter handling in beams of different word boundaries, or character vocabulary coverage when mapping rare characters to <unk>. These choices can significantly impact BLEU and the claimed OOV robustness.

Questions to Authors
1) Word encoder weighting: Please clarify the exact formulation. Is wt used unnormalized in rword = tanh(∑t wt rt)? Why use exp(aff(ht)) without normalization, and why is tanh applied on the sum? Did you try softmax-normalized weights or direct scalar gates (e.g., sigmoid) and compare?

2) HGRU specification: Could you provide a corrected, unambiguous set of equations for the HGRU, including the state initialization at word boundaries, the role of the auxiliary delimiter sequence during training, and index conventions (sit vs. si)? How is the reset handled during inference when delimiters are mispredicted?

3) Beam search: What beam size and length penalty were used? How are multiple partial hypotheses with different numbers of produced delimiters synchronized with the word-level decoder states? Is pruning or reordering applied at delimiter boundaries?

4) Fairness of comparisons: Did you train your own BPE2BPE baseline with the same encoder/decoder depth, attention, optimizer, and training budget? If not, can you provide such controlled results to substantiate the one-epoch and final BLEU claims?

5) Ablations: Can you report (a) DCNMT with the ordinary RNN word encoder replaced by your morphology-aware encoder only (already partly shown), (b) hierarchical decoder replaced by a flat char decoder with identical encoder, and (c) variants of the weighting function (softmax vs. tanh vs. sigmoid)? This would isolate where the gains come from.

6) Morphology evaluation: Can you quantify the alignment of energy peaks to morpheme boundaries on a dataset with gold segmentations (e.g., MorphoChallenge) for English/Czech? This would strengthen the morphology-learning claim.

7) Delimiter vocabulary: What delimiter(s) are used, and how is tokenization on the target side defined? How often are delimiter predictions erroneous, and what is their effect on BLEU and decoding stability?

Overall, the paper introduces a plausible, structured approach to character-level NMT with interesting engineering ideas and some positive results. Tightening the technical specification and providing controlled, reproducible comparisons and quantitative morphology evaluations would significantly strengthen the submission.
