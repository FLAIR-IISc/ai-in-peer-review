

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a novel architecture. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a word encoder using two recurrent networks to learn morphemes and their combinations, producing meaningful word representations without a fixed vocabulary; (2) a hierarchical decoder that efficiently handles character-level translation without the two-pass training required by previous character-level models; and (3) empirical evidence that this approach achieves competitive BLEU scores while avoiding the out-of-vocabulary issue.

Strengths: First, the paper demonstrates a significant efficiency advantage—DCNMT achieves higher BLEU scores than BPE-based models after just one training epoch, which is a compelling practical advantage. Second, the morphological analysis in Figure 3 provides strong visual evidence that the word encoder produces meaningful representations where words with similar meanings cluster together, unlike standard RNN word encoders. Third, the hierarchical decoder design is cleverly implemented to avoid the inefficiencies of prior character-level approaches, making character-level translation more practical.

Weaknesses: First, the paper's claim of "higher BLEU score than the BPE-based model after one epoch" lacks sufficient detail about the specific BPE baseline used (e.g., which BPE model, hyperparameters), making it difficult to assess the significance. Second, while the model size is comparable to other approaches (Table 1), the paper doesn't clearly explain why DCNMT trains more efficiently despite having six RNNs, which undermines this key claim. Third, the paper doesn't adequately address how this approach would generalize to morphologically complex languages beyond English-Czech, which limits the perceived broader impact.

Questions to Authors: Could you provide more details about the specific BPE baseline used for comparison in the one-epoch results? Additionally, how would your model handle morphological phenomena in agglutinative languages like Turkish, and what modifications would be required?
