

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A word encoder using two recurrent networks to learn morphemes and their combinations, providing a meaningful representation of words without relying on a lookup table.
2. A hierarchical decoder that generates translations at character level but is guided by a word-level decoder, enabling efficient training and better handling of morphological variations.
3. Empirical demonstration that the model achieves competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art systems, outperforms BPE-based models after just one training epoch, and can handle misspelled words and nonce words through morphology learning.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a fundamental problem in NMT**: The paper successfully tackles the OOV problem that has plagued word-level NMT systems. By learning morphology rather than relying on a large vocabulary, the authors provide a practical solution that eliminates the need for vocabulary size limitations while maintaining translation quality. This is a significant contribution to the field, as OOV handling is a persistent challenge in neural machine translation.

2. **Strong empirical validation of morphology learning**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The PCA projections in Figure 3 clearly show that words with similar meanings but different morphologies are represented more coherently by the proposed word encoder than by an ordinary RNN. The analysis in Figure 4 demonstrates how the model assigns energy to morpheme boundaries, validating the core claim that the model learns morphology.

3. **Practical advantage for handling unknown words**: The ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (Table 2) is a significant practical contribution. This demonstrates that the model's morphology learning capability has real-world utility beyond just theoretical interest, as it can handle linguistic variations that would cause standard word-level models to fail.

4. **Efficiency claims supported by results**: The paper demonstrates that their model achieves better performance than BPE-based models after just one training epoch (Table 1), which is a strong efficiency claim. The training time comparison (1 day vs. 2.8-90 days for other models) is a meaningful advantage for a model with comparable BLEU scores.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: While the paper combines existing ideas in a novel way, the core concept of learning morphology for NMT has been explored in prior work. Luong and Manning (2016) proposed a hybrid word-character model, and Ling et al. (2015) explored character-to-word modeling. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from these prior approaches, particularly in terms of how their morphology learning mechanism differs from the hybrid approach.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against BPE-based models and other character-level models, but doesn't compare against the most recent state-of-the-art NMT models. For example, the paper doesn't address how their model performs against newer architectures like the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), which was published after the paper's reference date. This weakens the claim that their model is competitive with "state-of-the-art systems."

3. **Insufficient analysis of training efficiency**: While the paper claims their model is more efficient to train than other character-level models, the comparison in Table 1 lacks crucial details about computational resources. The training times (1 day vs. 2.8-90 days) don't specify whether the models were trained on the same hardware, which significantly impacts the comparison's validity.

4. **Limited exploration of language-specific challenges**: The paper demonstrates results on English-French and English-Czech, but doesn't analyze how the model performs on languages with more complex morphology. For example, Czech is morphologically rich, but the paper doesn't explore whether the model's performance improves or degrades with languages that have even more complex morphological structures.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to the latest state-of-the-art NMT models (e.g., Transformer-based models) that have emerged after 2016, particularly in terms of BLEU scores and training efficiency?

2. Could you provide more details about the computational resources used for training each model in Table 1, including GPU specifications, to better validate the training efficiency claims?

3. The paper demonstrates morphology learning on English, but how would this approach perform on languages with significantly different morphological structures, such as agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish?
