

# Peer Review: "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem through a novel architecture incorporating morphology learning. The authors present two key innovations: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes using two recurrent networks (one for character-level representation and one for weighting these representations), and (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining training efficiency. The main contributions as I see them are:

1. **Morphology-aware word encoding**: A novel approach to learning morphemes and their combinations through two RNNs, which generates meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships.
2. **Efficient character-level translation**: A hierarchical decoder architecture that enables character-level translation with training efficiency comparable to word-level models.
3. **Demonstrated OOV handling**: Empirical evidence showing the model's ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") that would be OOV in standard word-level models.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to morphology learning**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their word encoder learns meaningful morphological representations. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 demonstrate that words with similar morphological structures (e.g., "notable" and "notability") are grouped together in the embedding space, unlike ordinary RNN word encoders. This is a significant contribution to understanding how neural models can implicitly learn linguistic structure.

2. **Training efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model trains significantly faster than previous character-level models while achieving comparable BLEU scores. Table 1 shows their model trains in 1 day (vs. 27-90 days for other character-level approaches), which is a major practical advantage. The hierarchical decoder's design (HGRU) effectively addresses the computational bottleneck of character-level translation.

3. **Practical OOV handling**: The ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (as shown in Table 2) is a tangible benefit of their character-level approach. This demonstrates a clear practical advantage over word-level models, which would simply output <unk> for such inputs. The example of "convenienter" is particularly convincing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims to "learn subword units automatically" but this is largely similar to BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) in spirit—just without the explicit segmentation step. The authors don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing subword-level methods. The paper cites Sennrich et al. (2016) but doesn't clearly articulate how their method differs in practice from BPE-based models, which are the de facto standard in the field.

2. **Misleading OOV claim**: The paper states their model "avoids the large vocabulary issue radically," but this is misleading. While they work at character level with a 120-character vocabulary, this is still a vocabulary (just much smaller than word-level vocabularies). The real advantage is the automatic segmentation of morphemes rather than the vocabulary size itself.

3. **Limited experimental scope**: The experiments are limited to three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), with no analysis of how the model performs on languages with radically different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish). The comparison against state-of-the-art models is also limited to those from 2015-2016, missing more recent advances.

4. **Questionable training efficiency claim**: The paper claims "our model obtains a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch," but this is a minor advantage that doesn't necessarily translate to better performance after full training. The final BLEU scores are comparable to BPE models, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach would scale better with more training.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component versus the hierarchical decoding. It's unclear whether the improved performance is due to the morphology learning, the hierarchical decoder, or both.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model's performance compare against more recent state-of-the-art NMT systems (e.g., those presented at ACL 2016 or WMT 2016)? The paper compares against models from 2015-2016 but doesn't discuss how it would fare against more recent advances.

2. The paper claims "our model is able to learn morphology" but doesn't provide a formal metric for measuring morphological learning. How would you quantify the quality of morphological learning in your model?

3. The paper states that "our model is able to translate the misspelled words or the nonce words" but doesn't provide comprehensive analysis of how often this occurs or how it compares to other models. Could you provide more data on this?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonable approach to character-level NMT with a novel morphology learning component. The empirical results are generally convincing, and the model does demonstrate a practical advantage in handling OOV words. However, the novelty is overstated, the experimental scope is limited, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing subword-level methods like BPE. While the training efficiency is a tangible contribution, the paper doesn't clearly establish why their approach would be preferred over BPE-based models in practice, which dominate the field.

The paper should be accepted with minor revisions to better contextualize their contribution relative to existing work, clarify their claims about vocabulary size and OOV handling, and provide a more comprehensive analysis of their model's strengths and limitations. The core idea of learning morphology is interesting, but the paper needs to better articulate how it advances beyond existing subword approaches.
