Summary: Proposes DCNMT, a deep character-level NMT with a word encoder that learns morphemes via two RNNs and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters from word representations to avoid large vocabularies.

Main contributions:
- Novel word encoder using two RNNs to learn character-level morpheme representations and weighted morpheme composition into meaningful word embeddings.
- Hierarchical decoder that generates character sequences efficiently from word-level representations, removing need for explicit segmentation.
- Empirical claim that character-level morphology learning yields competitive translation performance and fixes OOV/misspelling issues.

Strengths:
- Training efficiency: achieves strong BLEU quickly (e.g., ~30.56 En–Fr after one epoch), far faster than some prior character models.
- Morphology learning validation: PCA visualizations show morphologically related words cluster together, indicating linguistic structure in embeddings.
- Robustness to OOVs: examples demonstrate correct handling of misspelled and nonce words unlike word-level models.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: builds on prior hierarchical/character/morphology work without clearly distinguishing fundamental advances.
- Inadequate SOTA comparison: omits comparisons to recent 2016–2017 top models (e.g., Transformer, Google NMT), leaving true standing unclear.
- Insufficient efficiency analysis: training-time/resource trade-offs (epochs, GPU hours, memory) are not clearly quantified or justified.
- Limited language coverage: evaluated only on En–Fr and En–Cs, not on typologically diverse or agglutinative languages to test generality.
- Unclear hierarchical decoder novelty: HGRU modifications seem minor and not convincingly argued as superior to prior architectures.

Questions to authors:
- How is 30.56 BLEU considered "comparable to state-of-the-art" given some baselines are similar or better?
- How does the claimed elimination of large vocabularies compare quantitatively to typical word-level vocab sizes (e.g., 50k)?
- How would the character-level approach scale to non-alphabetic languages with different character sets (e.g., Chinese, Japanese)?
- Can you provide systematic analyses of which morphological phenomena (affixation, compounding, etc.) the model handles well or poorly?
