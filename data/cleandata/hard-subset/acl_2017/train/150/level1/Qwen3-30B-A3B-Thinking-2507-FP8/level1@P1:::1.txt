

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that learns morphology through a novel architecture. The authors address the vocabulary bottleneck in word-level NMT by introducing a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two recurrent networks, and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations. The model operates entirely at the character level without requiring a large vocabulary, while maintaining the ability to learn meaningful word representations.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphology through two RNNs (one for character-level representations, one for weighting morpheme contributions), producing meaningful word embeddings that capture morphological relationships.
2. A hierarchical decoder architecture that efficiently generates character sequences based on word-level representations, eliminating the need for explicit segmentation while enabling efficient training.
3. Empirical demonstration that character-level modeling with morphology learning can achieve competitive translation performance (comparable to state-of-the-art) while solving the out-of-vocabulary problem and handling misspelled/nonce words.

## Strengths
1. **Training efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results after just one epoch of training (e.g., 30.56 BLEU on En-Fr), significantly outperforming conventional character-level models that require months of training. This is a major practical advantage over existing approaches.

2. **Morphology learning validation**: The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 provide strong evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations, with semantically related words (e.g., "notable" and "notability") clustering together in the embedding space. This is a clear demonstration of the model's linguistic understanding.

3. **Handling of out-of-vocabulary words**: The examples in Table 2 compellingly show the model's ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, which is impossible for standard word-level models. This demonstrates a practical advantage with clear real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: While the combination of ideas is interesting, the approach builds directly on existing work (e.g., hierarchical decoders from Serban et al., 2015; character-level modeling from Ling et al., 2015b; morphology learning from Luong and Manning, 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these prior works or explain why this specific combination is fundamentally better.

2. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares primarily to older models (e.g., bpe2bpe, C2W) but fails to compare against more recent SOTA models from 2016-2017 (e.g., Transformer, Google's neural MT). This makes it difficult to assess the true standing of their approach in the current landscape.

3. **Lack of detailed efficiency analysis**: While the paper claims training efficiency, the comparison in Table 1 is misleading. The model requires 1 training day (vs. 3 months for Luong and Manning) but also requires 300 epochs (vs. 50 for bpe2bpe). The authors don't explain why this trade-off is acceptable or provide a clear resource consumption analysis (e.g., GPU hours, memory usage).

4. **Limited language coverage**: The experiments are restricted to English-French and English-Czech, both Indo-European languages with relatively similar morphological structures. The paper doesn't evaluate the model on languages with very different morphology (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish), which would better test the generalizability of the morphology learning approach.

5. **Insufficient explanation of hierarchical decoder**: The paper doesn't clearly articulate how the hierarchical decoder differs from existing approaches (e.g., Serban et al., 2015). The HGRU mechanism appears to be a minor modification rather than a substantial innovation, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this specific implementation is superior.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that the model achieves "comparable to state-of-the-art" performance, but Table 1 shows that the final BLEU score (30.56) is still lower than some word-level baselines (e.g., bpe2bpe at 29.70). Could you clarify how this is considered "comparable to state-of-the-art"?

2. The paper states that the model "avoids the large vocabulary issue radically," but the vocabulary size for characters (120 most frequent characters) is still substantial. How does this compare to the vocabulary size of a standard word-level model (e.g., 50k words)?

3. The model uses a character-level representation, but the paper doesn't address how it handles languages with very different character sets (e.g., Chinese, Japanese). How would this approach scale to non-alphabetic languages?

4. The paper claims that the model can "learn morphology," but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how well it learns different types of morphological rules (e.g., affixation vs. compounding). Could you provide examples of morphological phenomena it handles well versus poorly?
