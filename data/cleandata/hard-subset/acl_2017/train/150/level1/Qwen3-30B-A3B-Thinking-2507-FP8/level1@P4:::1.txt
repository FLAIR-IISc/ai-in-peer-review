

Summary: This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a novel architecture. The main contributions are: (1) a morpheme-aware word encoder that learns subword units and their combinations without external segmentation, (2) a hierarchical decoder (HGRU) for efficient character-level translation, and (3) empirical evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations while achieving competitive BLEU scores.

Main Contributions:
1. A morpheme-aware word encoder that learns subword units and their combinations through two RNNs, eliminating the need for external segmentation methods like BPE.
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates character sequences based on word-level representations through the HGRU mechanism.
3. Empirical demonstration that the model learns meaningful morphological representations, as evidenced by PCA projections of word embeddings and successful translation of nonce words.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses the critical out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in NMT through a novel morphological learning approach. The PCA analysis in Figure 3 provides strong evidence that words with similar meanings are represented closer together in the embedding space, demonstrating the model's ability to learn meaningful morphological relationships.

2. The model achieves competitive BLEU scores (comparable to state-of-the-art) and notably outperforms BPE-based models after just one training epoch. This demonstrates significant training efficiency, particularly for the En-Fr and En-Cs tasks, where their model achieves higher scores than BPE-based models after one epoch (27.02 vs. 26.91 BLEU on En-Fr development set).

3. The hierarchical decoder design (HGRU) provides an elegant solution to the inefficiency of character-level decoding. The unfolding technique for batch training (Figure 2) is well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in character-level NMT, making the model practical for real-world applications.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a rigorous definition of "learning morphology" and doesn't demonstrate that the model has learned grammatical morphological rules. The evidence provided (Figure 3) shows words with similar meanings are closer in embedding space, but this doesn't necessarily indicate the model has learned morphological structure (e.g., prefixes/suffixes) rather than just semantic similarity.

2. The training efficiency claim is misleading. While the model trains faster than some character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016), it doesn't compare to the most efficient state-of-the-art approaches (BPE-based models, which are known to be highly efficient). The model size (54M parameters) is comparable to or larger than some BPE models, contradicting the claim of being "more efficient."

3. The paper doesn't isolate the contribution of morphological learning to the improved performance. The one-epoch advantage could be due to the hierarchical decoder rather than morphological learning, but the authors don't provide ablation studies to verify this. The analysis in Section 5.2 focuses on the word encoder but doesn't show how morphological learning specifically improves translation quality.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you define "learning morphology" in this context, and what specific morphological features (e.g., prefixes, suffixes) does your model learn? Can you provide a quantitative analysis of the morphological features captured by the word encoder?

2. The model achieves better performance after one epoch than BPE-based models after many epochs. Can you provide ablation studies to determine whether the morphological learning component or the hierarchical decoder is primarily responsible for this advantage?

3. The experiments focus on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En. How does the model perform on languages with extremely rich morphology (e.g., Finnish, Turkish), which would provide a stronger test of the morphological learning capability?
