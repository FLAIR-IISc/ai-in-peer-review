- Proposes a novel deep character-level NMT (DCNMT) with a morphology-aware word encoder and hierarchical character-level decoder.
- Architecture uses six RNNs in four layers to avoid large vocabulary bottlenecks and enable efficient training.
- Demonstrates higher BLEU than BPE models after one epoch and comparable performance to state-of-the-art character models on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En.
- Shows the model effectively learns morphemes and their combinations, aiding generalization and faster convergence.
- Robust to misspelled and nonce words, making it practical for noisy or user-generated text.
- PCA and other analyses provide evidence of meaningful morphological representations learned by the model.
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple language pairs with detailed comparisons to word- and character-level baselines.
- Does not consistently outperform state-of-the-art character-based models in final BLEU scores; trade-offs between efficiency and peak performance need clarification.
- Potential scalability issues for longer sequences or larger datasets; impact of deeper RNNs and longer training not explored.
- Lacks qualitative translation examples to substantiate claims about handling rare or morphologically complex words.
- Claims about generalization to other tasks (speech recognition, summarization) are unsubstantiated by experiments or discussion.
- Asks whether longer training or deeper architectures could close the performance gap with state-of-the-art models.
- Requests more qualitative case studies showing behavior on rare, misspelled, or morphologically complex words.
- Suggests exploring effects of hyperparameters (embedding size, number of layers) on performance and efficiency.
- Overall assessment: well-motivated and innovative approach, but would benefit from addressing scalability, qualitative evidence, and broader comparisons.
