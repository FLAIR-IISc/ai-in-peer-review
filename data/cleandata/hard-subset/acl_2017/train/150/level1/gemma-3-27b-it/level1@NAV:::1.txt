## Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the large vocabulary problem and improve training efficiency. The core idea is to utilize two recurrent networks to learn morphology – one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation – coupled with a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation. The model consists of six recurrent networks and aims to learn subword units automatically, rather than relying on techniques like byte-pair encoding.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the *architecture* itself – the combination of the morphology-aware word encoder and the hierarchical decoder. While character-level NMT isn’t novel, the specific architecture and the attempt to explicitly model morphology through dedicated recurrent networks are interesting. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the *demonstration of comparable performance* to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, despite a simpler model size and training process. The provided experimental results, particularly the BLEU scores, support this claim. Finally, the *analysis showing the model’s ability to learn morphology* – via visualization of character weights and successful translation of morphologically complex words – is a useful and insightful addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed architecture is a potentially promising approach to character-level NMT, integrating morphology learning in a structured manner. This moves beyond simply applying RNNs to characters and attempts to capture linguistic structure.
2. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems, especially with a smaller model and faster training (as indicated in Table 1), demonstrates the viability and efficiency of the approach.
3. **Empirical Evidence of Morphology Learning:** The visualizations and examples provided lend credence to the claim that the model successfully learns morphological information, which is a key design goal.
4. **Addressing OOV issue:** The model inherently handles OOV words, a significant advantage over word-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* is interesting, individual components (hierarchical decoders, RNNs) are well-established in the NMT literature. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this specific architecture distinguishes itself from existing character-level models.
2. **Limited Ablation Studies:**  The paper would be strengthened by more thorough ablation studies. For example, it's not entirely clear how much each part of the architecture (morphology-aware encoder vs. hierarchical decoder) contributes to the overall performance.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reported results do not provide a clear sense of how sensitive the model is to hyperparameter choices. A discussion of hyperparameter tuning would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) compared to other character-level models (e.g., BPE-based models)?
2.  How does the performance of the model vary across different morphological richness of languages? Were other language pairs tested besides En-Fr and En-Cs?
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of tanh activation for normalization in the word encoder, and how this impacts performance compared to other normalization methods?
