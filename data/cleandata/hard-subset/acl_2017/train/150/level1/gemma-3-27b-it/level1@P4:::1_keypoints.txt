- Proposes DCNMT: a six-layer character-level NMT with two RNNs in the encoder and a hierarchical decoder to handle large vocabularies and morphology.  
- Primary contribution is the architecture that aims for efficient, character-level translation and morphology learning.  

Strengths:
- Tackles NMT large-vocabulary and rare-word problems by operating at the character level.  
- Empirical results (En→Fr, En→Cs) show competitive BLEU scores versus state-of-the-art systems.  
- Architecture and design choices (two encoder RNNs, hierarchical decoder, HGRU) are clearly explained and illustrated.  
- Qualitative analyses (Figures 3–4) provide initial evidence the model captures morphological information.  
- Demonstrates relatively efficient training compared to other character-level models (Table 1).  

Weaknesses:
- Individual components (RNNs, hierarchical decoders, attention) are not novel; novelty is mainly in their combination and application.  
- Morphology analysis is superficial, relying on PCA visualizations and few examples without rigorous quantitative evaluation.  
- No ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each architectural component.  
- Comparisons lack strong word-level NMT baselines that use rare-word handling (e.g., copy mechanisms).  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity (hidden sizes, embedding dims) and robustness are not reported.  

Questions to authors:
- Can you provide quantitative evaluations of morphology learning (e.g., performance on morphologically complex words or targeted tests)?  
- How does DCNMT compare to a strong word-level NMT baseline with rare-word handling like copy mechanisms?  
- How sensitive are results to hyperparameters such as RNN hidden size and embedding dimension?  
- How does your hierarchical decoder differ from and improve upon existing hierarchical decoder approaches?
