This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the large vocabulary problem common in word-based NMT. The core idea is to learn morphology using two recurrent networks – one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation – coupled with a hierarchical decoder. The authors claim their model avoids the large vocabulary issue, is more efficient to train than word-based and conventional character-based models, achieves competitive BLEU scores, and learns morphology.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hierarchical Character-Level NMT Architecture:** The primary contribution is the proposed architecture. Combining two RNNs for morphological encoding with a hierarchical decoder to generate text at the character level is a well-defined and potentially valuable approach. This is a non-trivial combination of ideas, addressing limitations of purely character-level models.
2. **Morphology Learning Focus:** The explicit focus on learning morphology within an NMT system, rather than relying on subword units like BPE, is a significant aspect of the work. The analysis in Section 5.2 attempts to demonstrate this learning, providing valuable insights.
3. **Efficient Training of Deep Character-Level Models:** The authors address the training difficulties associated with deep character-level models and report efficient training, achieving comparable results to state-of-the-art systems with a relatively small model size (as shown in Table 1).

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the issues with word-level NMT (large vocabulary, OOV words) and provides a strong motivation for exploring character-level alternatives that explicitly model morphology.
2. **Novel Architectural Components:** The two-RNN word encoder and the hierarchical decoder are well-described and represent innovative components. The use of the gate recurrent unit (HGRU) in the decoder, specifically designed for character-level generation, is a practical approach.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide experimental results on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) and demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods, including BPE-based models and other character-level approaches.  The efficiency comparison presented in Table 1 is supportive.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** Section 5.2 attempts to provide evidence for morphology learning through PCA visualization and analysis of character energies, which strengthens the claim that the model is capturing morphological information. The examples provided in Table 2 further support this claim.
5. **Addressing Training Challenges:** The authors directly address the challenge of training deep character-level models, which has been a common obstacle in previous work. Their model design and training details appear to have successfully mitigated this issue.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, individual elements – character-level NMT, hierarchical decoders, RNNs for encoding – are well-established techniques. The paper doesn't introduce fundamentally new building blocks, and the innovation lies in their specific integration.
2. **PCA Visualization & Interpretation:** The PCA visualization in Figure 3 is somewhat subjective and lacks quantitative evaluation. It’s difficult to definitively conclude from the visualization that the model is learning meaningful morphological representations. Further statistical analysis is needed.
3. **Insufficient Comparison with Strong Baselines:** While comparisons are provided, the reported results lack a comparison against strong, recent subword NMT models that may outperform BPE, like Unigram Language Model (ULM). Comparing to these could better contextualize the model's performance.
4. **Ablation Studies Missing:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to demonstrate the individual contributions of each component of the architecture (e.g., removing the word encoder, simplifying the decoder). This would help to pinpoint which elements are most crucial for performance.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper includes a few examples of successful translation of misspelled or nonce words, but a more comprehensive error analysis would provide a deeper understanding of the model’s strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a quantitative evaluation of the morphological representations learned by the word encoder, perhaps by measuring its ability to predict morphological features (e.g., tense, number)?
2.  How does the performance of your model compare to more recent subword NMT models based on the Unigram Language Model (ULM)?
3.  What were the computational resources (e.g., GPU memory, training time) required for training your model on the different language pairs? A more detailed breakdown of training cost would be helpful.
4.  Can you elaborate on the choice of the tanh activation function in the weight normalization step? Were other activation functions explored, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The motivation and the idea are strong, but the empirical evaluation could be more rigorous.
