This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) designed to address the large vocabulary problem and improve morphology learning. The core idea is to use two RNNs in the encoder to learn representations of preceding characters and the weight of those representations, followed by a hierarchical decoder to translate at the character level.

The primary contribution is the architecture itself – a six-layer recurrent network (two for encoding, four for decoding) specifically designed for character-level translation with a focus on capturing morphological information. The authors argue that this approach avoids the large vocabulary bottleneck of word-based models while being more efficient to train than conventional character-based models. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, showing comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems, and initial evidence suggesting the model *does* learn morphological information. The novelty of the hierarchical decoding scheme, aiming for efficient character-level translation, also constitutes a contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper tackles a significant challenge in NMT: handling large vocabularies and rare words. The character-level approach fundamentally avoids this problem, and the proposed architecture aims to make it computationally feasible and effective.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The BLEU scores presented, while not definitively state-of-the-art, are competitive and demonstrate the potential of the proposed architecture. Achieving comparable results to existing systems *with* a character-level model is a noteworthy achievement, especially considering the training efficiency claims.
3. **Clear Architecture and Explanation:** The architecture is clearly explained and illustrated in Figure 2. The rationale behind the design choices, particularly the two RNNs in the encoder and the hierarchical decoder, is well-articulated. The explanation of HGRU is also understandable.
4. **Initial Evidence of Morphology Learning:** The qualitative analysis, supported by Figure 3 and 4, provides preliminary evidence that the model is indeed learning morphological information, which aligns with the authors' claims and is a valuable demonstration of the approach.
5. **Efficient Training:** The authors claim and demonstrate, through comparisons in Table 1, that their model is relatively efficient to train compared to other character-level models. This is an important practical consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, individual aspects of the architecture (RNNs, hierarchical decoders, attention mechanisms) are well-established in NMT. The novelty lies in the specific application and integration of these components, but this could be more thoroughly discussed in relation to existing work.  The hierarchical decoder seems reminiscent of previous work without clear distinction.
2. **Superficial Morphology Analysis:** The morphological analysis is based on PCA visualization and a limited set of examples. While suggestive, it lacks rigorous quantitative evaluation. A more systematic analysis is needed to convincingly demonstrate that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.  For example, performance on morphologically complex words or ablation studies could be useful.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't include ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the architecture. It's unclear how much each part (e.g., the two RNNs in the encoder, the hierarchical decoder) contributes to the overall performance.
4. **Limited Comparison with Strong Baselines:** The comparisons primarily focus on BPE-based and existing character-level models. A comparison with a strong word-level NMT baseline, trained with a large vocabulary and techniques for handling rare words (e.g., copy mechanisms) would be beneficial. This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the benefits of the proposed approach.
5. **Unclear Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of various hyperparameters (e.g., RNN hidden size, embedding dimension) on performance is not discussed. It is unclear if the results are robust to changes in these parameters.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more quantitative evaluation of the model's ability to learn morphology? Perhaps by evaluating performance on morphologically complex words or performing an ablation study?
2. How does the performance of your model compare to a strong word-level NMT baseline with techniques for handling rare words (e.g., copy mechanisms)?
3. What is the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameters, such as the RNN hidden size and embedding dimension?
4.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the existing hierarchical decoders and how your proposed approach differs and addresses them?
