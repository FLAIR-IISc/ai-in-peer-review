- Six-RNN hierarchical character-level NMT architecture that uses word-level context to initialize character generation.  
- Morphology-aware word encoder using two RNNs (one for preceding characters, one for weighting) to model word structure.  
- Empirical validation on En–Fr and En–Cs with PCA analyses suggesting the model captures morphological information.  
- Directly addresses the large-vocabulary problem by moving to character-level modeling.  
- Strong baseline comparisons (BPE and other char models); reports comparable or better BLEU and faster training.  
- Novel architectural components (hierarchical decoder and morphology-aware encoder) that form a cohesive system.  
- Preliminary evidence of morphology learning (PCA visualizations and example translations like “convenienter”).  
- Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful diagrams.  
- Individual components are not entirely novel; novelty mainly in their specific combination.  
- Morphology analysis is qualitative/insufficient; lacks quantitative validation of learned morphological features.  
- Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and scalability to larger datasets or more complex languages.  
- Missing a detailed error analysis describing typical translation failures and their causes.  
- Some claims are overstated (e.g., “radically avoids the large vocabulary issue”; “comparable” is vague).  
- Request quantitative evaluation of morphological awareness (e.g., predict stems/suffixes from encoder).  
- Ask for ablation on the choice of six RNNs and experiments with different depths.  
- Request detailed computational cost comparison (training time and memory) versus BPE and other char models.  
- Ask for a detailed error analysis with example failure cases.  
- Request sensitivity analysis to hyperparameters (embedding size, hidden size, learning rate).  
- Suggest comparison against newer subword methods like SentencePiece.  
- Ask for clarification on the specific WMT'15 datasets used.  
- Clarify the difference between “char” and “character” in Table 1.  
- Overall: solid, well-executed paper with promising results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
