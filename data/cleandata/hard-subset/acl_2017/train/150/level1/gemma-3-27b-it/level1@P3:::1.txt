This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model aiming to address the large vocabulary problem in word-level NMT and the training difficulties of purely character-level models. The core idea is to learn morphology using two RNNs – one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation – followed by a hierarchical decoder that translates at the character level. The authors demonstrate comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks and provide analyses suggesting the model learns morphological information.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hierarchical Character-Level Architecture:** The most significant contribution is the design of a six-RNN deep character-level NMT architecture with a novel hierarchical decoder. The decoder's ability to leverage word-level context to initialize character-level generation is a reasonable approach to address the length and training challenges of purely character-based systems.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** The proposed word encoder, utilizing two RNNs to learn character representations and their weights, is a worthwhile exploration. The idea of explicitly modelling morphology within the encoder shows promise, particularly for morphologically rich languages.
3. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:**  The paper presents reasonably thorough experimental results on multiple language pairs, including morphologically rich Czech. The inclusion of analyses investigating whether the model learns morphological information (through PCA visualization) adds to the paper’s credibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Problem:** The large vocabulary issue in NMT is a well-recognized bottleneck. This work directly addresses it by moving to a character-level model, offering a potentially elegant solution.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against both BPE-based models and other character-level models establishes a clear benchmark for the proposed approach. Achieving comparable or better performance, especially with reportedly faster training, is a positive sign.
3. **Novel Architectural Components:** The hierarchical decoder and the morphology-aware word encoder offer novel contributions to the field of NMT.  These components are well-motivated and contribute to a cohesive system.
4. **Demonstration of Morphology Learning:** The analysis using PCA visualization, while not conclusive, provides preliminary evidence that the model learns something about word structure and morphology.  The examples translating "convenienter" correctly are also compelling.
5. **Clear and Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The diagrams are helpful in understanding the proposed architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, several individual elements have precedents. The use of RNNs for character-level modelling, hierarchical decoders, and attention mechanisms are all established techniques. The core novelty lies in the specific way these are combined, which is not dramatically different from existing character-level approaches.
2. **Insufficiently Detailed Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization is a good start, but it's not a rigorous analysis. It would be much more convincing to see quantitative evaluation of morphological awareness. For example, could the encoder representations be used to predict morphological features (stem, suffix, etc.)? A more detailed analysis would really substantiate the claim of learning morphology.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't provide much discussion about hyperparameter tuning or the scalability of the model to larger datasets or more complex languages. The efficiency gains reported should be substantiated with more rigorous experiments, particularly regarding training time and memory usage as the model scales.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. What types of errors does the model still make? Where does it struggle? An error analysis could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
5. **Overstated Claims:** Phrases like "radically avoids the large vocabulary issue" and "comparable to state-of-the-art systems" are somewhat overstated. While the model *reduces* the vocabulary problem, it doesn't eliminate it entirely (there's still a character vocabulary).  And "comparable" is a vague term.  Precisely how does it compare to the best systems, and under what conditions?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more quantitative evaluation of the model's morphological awareness, such as predicting morphological features from the word encoder’s representation?
2.  How did the choice of 6 RNNs impact performance? Did you experiment with different depths, and if so, what were the results?
3.  What is the computational cost of training and inference compared to BPE-based models and other character-level models (e.g., C2W)? Specifically, how does training time and memory usage scale with larger datasets and model sizes?
4.  Can you provide a detailed error analysis, including examples of typical translation errors and a discussion of their causes?
5.  How sensitive is the model performance to the hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size, RNN hidden state size, learning rate)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be helpful to see a comparison against more recent subword segmentation techniques like SentencePiece.
*   The paper references WMT'15, but provides no details about the specific datasets used.
*   Please clarify the difference between "char" and "character" in Table 1.

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a reasonable and well-executed approach to character-level NMT. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and the empirical results are promising. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a more robust analysis of morphology learning and detailing scalability concerns, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
