This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the large vocabulary problem inherent in word-level NMT and the training difficulties of traditional character-level NMT. The core idea is to learn morphology using two recurrent networks—one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation—followed by a hierarchical decoder that translates at the character level. 

The primary contribution is the novel architecture incorporating the morphological learning component within the encoder and a hierarchical decoder. While character-level NMT isn’t new, the specific combination of learning morphology via weighted character representations and the hierarchical decoder provides a potentially effective way to address the challenges of training deep character-level models and capturing subword information without relying on pre-defined segmentation (like BPE). A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of this architecture on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, demonstrating competitive performance (comparable to state-of-the-art) and showing gains over a BPE baseline after just one epoch of training. The analysis suggesting the model learns morphology, while not conclusive, adds support to the design rationale.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NMT Problem:** The paper tackles a significant issue in NMT – the large vocabulary and OOV problem. By moving to a character level and designing a system that explicitly attempts to learn morphological structure, the authors propose a promising solution. This is crucial for morphologically rich languages.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The combination of the weighted character representation learning within the encoder and the hierarchical decoder is a novel architectural choice. The hierarchical decoder approach, setting the character-level decoder’s initial state from the word-level decoder, seems a clever way to leverage word-level information while still operating at the character level. The explicit attempt to model morphemes within the encoder is also a strong point.
3. **Competitive Performance:**  Achieving comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art systems, particularly given the relatively simple training regime (one epoch outperforms BPE), is a significant result. Demonstrating performance on morphologically rich languages like Czech is important.
4. **Efficiency:** The model’s efficiency in training compared to other character-level models, as highlighted in Table 1, is a notable strength. Maintaining a similar encoding length to word-based models while eliminating the large vocabulary is advantageous. The model size being smaller than alternatives is also positive.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, the individual components (RNNs, hierarchical decoders, attention) are well-established in NMT.  The paper needs to more strongly emphasize *how* the specific architecture addresses the inherent challenges of deep character-level modeling that existing techniques fail to resolve. 
2. **Morphology Learning Analysis is Weak:** The PCA visualization and examples showing correct translation of “convenienter” provide some evidence of morphological learning, but this analysis feels superficial. A more rigorous evaluation of morphological understanding would strengthen the claims—e.g., analyzing the learned representations for identifiable morphemes or performing targeted experiments with morphological variations.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies. For instance, what is the impact of removing the weighted character representation learning? How does the performance change with different RNN architectures or hidden layer sizes? These studies would help justify the design choices.
4. **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Comparing against only a BPE baseline and a few older character-level models feels insufficient. Including results from more recent and powerful character-level NMT models (e.g., those using transformers) would provide a more accurate assessment of the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned weights in the word encoder? Specifically, can you identify which characters consistently receive higher weights and whether these correspond to common morphemes?
2.  How sensitive is the model to the choice of character vocabulary size?
3.  What is the computational cost of training this model compared to a similarly performing word-level NMT model, considering both training time and memory usage?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for using tanh for normalization of the weights instead of a softmax function?

**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, but some sections could be more concise. The paper would be strengthened by including an example of the generated character sequences for a particular input sentence, illustrating how the hierarchical decoder operates. Providing code and data would further enhance reproducibility.
