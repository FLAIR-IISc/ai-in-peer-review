## Review of Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the large vocabulary problem and difficulties with rare words. The core idea is to learn morphology through two recurrent networks within a word encoder and a hierarchical decoder that operates at the character level. The model consists of six recurrent networks, aiming for efficient training despite its depth. The authors demonstrate competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, claiming the model learns morphology effectively.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the *architecture combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a hierarchical character-level decoder*. While character-level NMT isn’t novel, the specific design of learning morphological representations *within* the encoder, coupled with the hierarchical decoding strategy, is a significant architectural contribution. The second contribution lies in the *demonstration of competitive performance* with state-of-the-art systems, particularly given the model’s relatively small size and efficient training. The empirical results, while not dramatically surpassing existing methods, show the viability of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The combination of the morphology-aware word encoder and hierarchical decoder is a well-motivated and potentially powerful approach to character-level NMT. The design addresses the challenges of long sequences in character-level models and the need to capture subword information.
2. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems, especially considering the model's size and training efficiency, is a strong indicator of the approach's effectiveness. The comparison with BPE-based models is particularly relevant.
3. **Efficiency:** The authors highlight the efficient training of the deep model, which is a significant advantage. The reported training times and model size are reasonable.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization and examples of handling misspelled/nonce words provide some evidence supporting the claim that the model learns morphological information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Components:** While the combination is novel, individual components (RNNs, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in NMT. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this specific combination addresses limitations of previous approaches.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Morphology:** The analysis of morphology learning is somewhat superficial. The PCA visualization is helpful, but more quantitative analysis (e.g., analyzing learned weights, ablation studies) would strengthen the claim.
3. **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to two language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs). Evaluating on a wider range of languages, particularly those with more complex morphology, would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model's capabilities.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to existing character-level models is limited. Comparing against more recent and powerful character-level NMT systems would provide a clearer benchmark.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the architecture (e.g., the morphology-aware word encoder, the hierarchical decoder)?
2. What specific design choices in the word encoder contribute most to the model's ability to learn morphology?
3. How does the performance of the model vary across different morphological complexities of languages? Would you expect the benefits of this approach to be more pronounced in highly inflected languages?
