**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder to translate at the character level. The model, called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT), consists of six recurrent networks and is trained end-to-end. The authors claim that their model avoids the large vocabulary issue, is more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models, and achieves a higher BLEU score than the byte pair encoding (BPE)-based model.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Architecture**: The authors propose a novel architecture that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder to translate at the character level.
2. **Efficient Training**: The authors claim that their model is more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models.
3. **Improved Performance**: The authors claim that their model achieves a higher BLEU score than the BPE-based model and is comparable to the state-of-the-art systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Addressing the Large Vocabulary Issue**: The authors propose a novel architecture that avoids the large vocabulary issue, which is a major challenge in NMT.
2. **Efficient Training**: The authors claim that their model is more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models, which is a significant advantage.
3. **Improved Performance**: The authors claim that their model achieves a higher BLEU score than the BPE-based model and is comparable to the state-of-the-art systems, which demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Complexity of the Model**: The authors propose a complex architecture that consists of six recurrent networks, which may be difficult to train and optimize.
2. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on two language pairs (En-Fr and En-Cs), which may not be sufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach.
3. **Lack of Comparison with Other Character-Level Models**: The authors only compare their model with the BPE-based model and do not provide a comparison with other character-level models, which may be a limitation of their evaluation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other language pairs and tasks?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the training procedure and hyperparameter tuning for their model?
3. How do the authors plan to address the complexity of their model and make it more efficient to train and optimize?
