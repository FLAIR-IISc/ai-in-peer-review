- Proposal: a deep, fully character-level NMT with a hierarchical architecture (character → subword → word) using six RNNs to address OOVs and improve training efficiency.
- Word encoder: novel two-stage design—one RNN over characters and a secondary BiRNN that computes weights to form a weighted-sum word representation.
- Hierarchical decoder: word-level RNN produces states that initialize a character-level HGRU to generate words character-by-character, with an "unfolding" mechanism for batching.
- Empirical claim: competitive BLEU on En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En, faster convergence (strong performance after one epoch), and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.
- Contribution 1: new word encoder architecture aimed at learning morphological representations via BiRNN-weighted character composition.
- Contribution 2: hierarchical character-level decoder with an efficient batching/unfolding mechanism to speed training.
- Contribution 3: empirical demonstration that a fully character-level NMT can be both effective and efficient, especially for morphologically rich languages.
- Strength: architecture novelty—structured morphological modeling and a sophisticated decoder design.
- Strength: strong empirical results and notable training efficiency compared to several baselines.
- Strength: thorough qualitative analysis (PCA of embeddings, character energy visualizations, examples on misspellings).
- Weakness: decoder description lacks clarity (HGRU equations contain a likely typo, and batching/unfolding details are under-specified).
- Weakness: design choices (e.g., tanh for weighting) lack justification or ablation; efficiency claims rely on wall-clock days rather than normalized metrics.
- Weakness: baseline comparisons are indirect (taken from other papers) and need controlled reimplementation; differences vs. Lee et al. (2016) are minimal and under-discussed.
- Question: why use tanh for weighted character sums instead of softmax or unweighted sum, and are ablations available?
- Question: clarify the HGRU state update (possible typo) and detail how the unfolding matrix R is constructed and applied efficiently for variable-length batches.
- Question: can the encoder’s weighted summation distinguish true morpheme signals from positional (primacy/recency) biases observed in Figure 4a?
