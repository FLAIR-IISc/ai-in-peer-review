### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation model. The core idea is to structure the model hierarchically to manage the long sequences inherent in character-level processing while explicitly modeling word morphology. The architecture consists of four main components: (1) a novel character-level source word encoder composed of two RNNs that compute a weighted sum of character representations to form a word embedding, designed to capture morphology; (2) a standard BiRNN sentence encoder that operates on these word embeddings; (3) a hierarchical decoder with a word-level RNN that generates a context vector for each target word; and (4) a character-level RNN decoder that uses this context vector to generate the characters of that word until a delimiter is produced. The entire model, comprising six RNNs, is trained end-to-end.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Efficient Hierarchical Decoder Architecture:** The primary contribution is the two-level decoder that operates at a word-level pace but generates at a character-level granularity. This design cleverly mitigates the main drawback of character-level NMT—the computational burden of attending over and decoding extremely long sequences. The proposed mechanism for resetting the character-level decoder's state with the word-level decoder's output, along with the matrix-based unfolding trick for efficient batch training, is a significant and practical architectural innovation.

2.  **An Effective Character-based Word Encoder for Morphology:** The proposed source word encoder, which uses an attention-like mechanism to weigh character-level hidden states, is shown to be effective at learning representations that reflect morphological relationships. While intra-word attention is not entirely new, its specific implementation and the compelling qualitative analysis demonstrating its ability to identify morpheme-like boundaries and create a structured embedding space is a solid contribution.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results Demonstrating Training Efficiency:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that this architecture not only achieves competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art systems but does so with remarkable training efficiency. The result showing that the model outperforms a BPE-based subword model after just a single epoch of training is a powerful argument for the architecture's effectiveness and its ability to quickly learn meaningful linguistic structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The paper's core weakness of character-level NMT (inefficiency due to sequence length) and word-level NMT (OOV issues) is clearly stated. The proposed hierarchical architecture is a very elegant solution to this trade-off. By having the main attention mechanism and decoder operate at the word-level, it avoids the quadratic complexity of attending over long character sequences. At the same time, by generating outputs character-by-character, it completely eliminates the OOV problem and the need for a large softmax layer. This is a principled and effective design.

2.  **Convincing Qualitative Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of supporting their central claim that the model "learns morphology." The analyses in Section 5.2 are particularly strong. The PCA plot in Figure 3 provides a clear visual demonstration that their word encoder produces more semantically coherent embeddings than a standard RNN encoder. Furthermore, the "energy" plots in Figure 4, which highlight peaks at plausible morpheme boundaries (e.g., after 'y' in 'anyway'), and the segmentation examples in Figure 5, provide compelling evidence that the encoder is learning meaningful subword structures without explicit supervision.

3.  **Strong and Well-Rounded Experimental Results:** The paper is evaluated on multiple language pairs, including a morphologically rich one (Czech), which is crucial for validating its claims. The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with or superior to several strong baselines, including BPE-based models and other character-level systems (Table 1). The most striking result is the model's performance after just one epoch, suggesting it learns much faster than subword models. The examples in Table 2, showing the model's ability to handle misspelled and nonce words ("convenienter"), effectively demonstrate the practical advantages of a fully character-level approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The proposed DCNMT model introduces several novel components simultaneously: the two-RNN word encoder, the hierarchical decoder, and the HGRU cell. The paper's main comparison is between the full model (DCNMT) and a version with a simpler word encoder (CNMT). While this highlights the benefit of their encoder, it doesn't fully disentangle the contributions of the encoder and the decoder. A more thorough ablation would have been valuable. For instance:
    *   How does the proposed word encoder perform with a standard, non-hierarchical character decoder?
    *   How does the hierarchical decoder perform with a simpler encoder, like a CNN over characters or just the standard RNN from the CNMT baseline?
    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to ascertain how much of the performance gain comes from the encoder versus the novel decoder structure.

2.  **Clarity and Framing of the Word Encoder:** The description of the word encoder in Section 3.1 is functional but could benefit from better contextualization. The mechanism of computing weights (`wt`) for each character's hidden state (`rt`) is fundamentally a form of self-attention over the characters within a word. Framing it as such and briefly discussing its relationship to other attention mechanisms would have made the novelty and design choices clearer. The claim that it is better than a CNN encoder because it "is able to generate a meaningful representation" is quite strong and is supported only by qualitative evidence; a more direct comparison or nuanced phrasing would be appropriate.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on Inference Speed and Complexity:** The paper rightfully emphasizes training efficiency in terms of "days to train." However, inference speed is also a critical metric for practical NMT systems. The hierarchical generation process, which involves running the target word encoder for each generated word before feeding it back to the first-level decoder, seems potentially slower than a standard word-level or even a flat character-level model at inference time. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the computational complexity at test time and empirical measurements of decoding speed (e.g., words/second) compared to the baselines.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The proposed word encoder is an interesting design. Could you elaborate on its connection to intra-word attention mechanisms? Have you experimented with more standard attention formulations (e.g., dot-product attention) for calculating the character weights, and if so, how did they compare to your proposed method using a separate BiRNN?

2.  Thank you for the insightful qualitative analysis. The energy plots in Figure 4 show high weights for both the beginning and ending characters of morphemes. Is this a consistent pattern you observed? Does this suggest the model is learning something akin to prefix/suffix representations?

3.  Regarding the hierarchical decoder, the feedback loop requires generating a full word, encoding it with the target word encoder, and then using that representation for the next word-level step. How does this impact decoding speed compared to models like BPE or flat character-level systems? Could you provide any metrics on inference speed (e.g., sentences per second)?
