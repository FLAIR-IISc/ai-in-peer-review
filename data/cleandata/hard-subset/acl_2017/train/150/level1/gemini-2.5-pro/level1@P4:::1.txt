### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model. The architecture is hierarchical, operating at character, subword, and word levels. The main goal is to solve the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-based models while improving training efficiency and performance compared to previous character-level approaches. The model consists of six recurrent neural networks (RNNs) in total. The key components are a novel word encoder designed to learn morphology and a hierarchical decoder that generates translations character by character. The word encoder uses one RNN to process characters sequentially and a second bidirectional RNN to compute weights for each character's hidden state, creating a weighted-sum representation of the word. The hierarchical decoder uses a word-level RNN to generate a sequence of states, where each state initializes a second, character-level RNN (termed HGRU) responsible for generating the characters of a single word. The authors evaluate their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, showing competitive results with state-of-the-art systems and demonstrating benefits in terms of training speed and the ability to handle misspelled or nonce words.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel word encoder architecture for learning morphological representations.** The model explicitly attempts to learn subword composition by using a secondary BiRNN to assign importance weights to the hidden states of a primary character-level RNN. This provides a structured way to compose character information into a word vector, distinct from standard character RNN/CNN encoders.
2.  **A hierarchical character-level decoder with an efficient batching mechanism.** The two-level decoder (word-level controller and character-level generator) provides a structured generation process. The proposed mechanism for "unfolding" the word-level states to align with the character sequence aims to make this hierarchical structure efficient for batch training.
3.  **An empirical demonstration of an efficient and effective fully character-level NMT system.** The paper shows that its model achieves competitive BLEU scores, particularly on morphologically rich languages, while claiming significant improvements in training speed over other character-level models and even subword-based models (e.g., reaching strong performance after only one epoch).

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Proposed Architecture.** The paper presents a thoughtfully designed architecture. The word encoder (Figure 1) is a clear and novel contribution. Instead of simply taking the final hidden state or using a standard attention mechanism over characters, it employs a separate BiRNN to learn dynamic, context-aware weights for each character's representation. This is a plausible and well-motivated approach to learning morphology. The hierarchical decoder is also a sophisticated design that attempts to blend the efficiency of word-level generation with the flexibility of character-level output.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Training Efficiency.** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with or exceed those of several contemporary models, including subword-based (BPE) systems and other character-level approaches (Table 1). The most compelling argument is the model's reported training efficiency. The claim that the model surpasses the final BPE baseline performance after just one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs is a significant practical advantage. This suggests that the model's architectural biases (i.e., the explicit morphological modeling) may lead to faster convergence.

3.  **Thorough Qualitative Analysis.** The paper provides strong qualitative evidence to support its claims. The analysis in Section 5.2, including the PCA projection of word embeddings (Figure 3) and the visualization of character "energy" weights (Figure 4), offers valuable insight into the model's internal workings and supports the assertion that it learns meaningful morphological representations. Furthermore, the examples of translating misspelled words and nonce words (Table 2) are highly effective in demonstrating a key practical advantage of this fully character-level approach over models reliant on a fixed vocabulary.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in the Decoder Description.** The description of the hierarchical decoder in Section 3.2 is difficult to parse. The mechanism of the "HGRU" and its state update (Equations 2-6) is not explained with sufficient clarity. Specifically, Equation (2) appears to have a typographical error, defining `gt-1` in terms of itself. The purpose of the "unfolding" matrix R is explained with a very simple example, but it is not clear how this is generalized and implemented efficiently for batches of variable-length sentences without becoming a computational bottleneck itself. The description would benefit greatly from pseudo-code or a more detailed algorithmic walkthrough.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Certain Design Choices.** Several architectural choices lack rigorous justification. For instance, in the word encoder, the paper states that a `tanh` activation is used on the weighted sum of character representations "as it performs best in experiments." Using `tanh` instead of a standard normalization like softmax is an unusual choice for a weighting mechanism, as it allows for negative weights, the interpretation of which is unclear. No ablation study is presented to quantify this performance difference or compare it against other alternatives, making the choice seem arbitrary. Similarly, claims of efficiency are based on training days, which can be affected by implementation and hardware; a more direct metric like tokens/second would provide a more robust comparison.

3.  **Baseline Comparisons Could Be Stronger.** The results for the BPE baseline and other models are cited from external papers. While common practice, this makes direct comparison difficult due to potential differences in data preprocessing, hyperparameter tuning, and model implementation. For a stronger claim of superiority, especially in efficiency, a baseline model (e.g., BPE-based) trained by the authors under identical experimental conditions would be more convincing. Additionally, the comparison against the fully character-level model of Lee et al. (2016) shows very similar performance on Cs-En (22.48 vs 22.46). A more in-depth discussion is needed to differentiate the proposed model and clearly articulate the advantages of its explicit morphological modeling over the implicit approach in Lee et al.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In the word encoder (Section 3.1), you combine character representations `rt` using weights `wt`, followed by a `tanh` activation. Could you elaborate on the motivation for `tanh` over a standard softmax? Does the `tanh` allow for negative weights, and if so, what is the intuition behind a character representation contributing negatively to a word's meaning? Could you provide ablation results comparing `tanh`, softmax, and a simple unweighted sum?
2.  Could you please clarify the HGRU state update in Equation (2)? As written, `gt-1` is defined in terms of `gt-1`. Is this a typo? Furthermore, could you provide more detail on the implementation of the unfolding matrix `R` for batch processing? How is this matrix constructed and applied efficiently for a batch of sentences with varying numbers of words and characters per word?
3.  The analysis in Figure 4a suggests the model assigns high energy to both the beginning and end of morphemes. How does the weighted summation in the word encoder differentiate between these signals? Is it possible that the model is simply learning a positional bias (primacy/recency) rather than genuine morpheme boundaries?
