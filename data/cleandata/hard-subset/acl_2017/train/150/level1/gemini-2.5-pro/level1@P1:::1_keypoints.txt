- Presents a deep, fully character-level NMT with a hierarchical design: character-level source word encoder (two RNNs), BiRNN sentence encoder on word embeddings, hierarchical word-level decoder, and character-level decoder — trained end-to-end.

- Contribution: novel two-level decoder that generates at character granularity but decodes/attends at word pace, with state-reset and matrix unfolding for efficient batching.

- Contribution: character-based word encoder using attention-like weighting over character hidden states that captures morphology and produces structured embeddings.

- Contribution: strong empirical results showing competitive BLEU and much faster learning (outperforms BPE after one epoch), demonstrating training efficiency.

- Strength: elegant, well-motivated architecture resolving the trade-off between character-level OOV handling and word-level attention efficiency.

- Strength: convincing qualitative analyses (PCA, energy plots, segmentation) showing the encoder learns morpheme-like boundaries and coherent embeddings.

- Strength: robust experimental validation across multiple languages including morphologically rich Czech; handles misspellings and nonce words in examples.

- Weakness: missing ablation studies to disentangle the effects of the two-RNN encoder, hierarchical decoder, and HGRU cell—unclear which component drives gains.

- Weakness: encoder description lacks framing as intra-word/self-attention and needs clearer comparisons (quantitative) to CNN or other encoders.

- Weakness: insufficient analysis of inference-time complexity and decoding speed; hierarchical feedback loop may slow test-time decoding.

- Question: please elaborate on the connection to intra-word attention and whether standard attention variants (e.g., dot-product) were tried for character weighting.

- Question: are the high energy weights at morpheme beginnings and ends a consistent pattern, suggesting prefix/suffix-like representations?

- Question: how does the hierarchical decoder affect decoding speed compared to BPE or flat character models — any words/sentences-per-second metrics?
