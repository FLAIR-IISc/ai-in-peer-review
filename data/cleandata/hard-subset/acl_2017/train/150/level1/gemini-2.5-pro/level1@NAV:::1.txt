This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) model designed to learn morphology explicitly. The architecture tackles the out-of-vocabulary problem of word-level models and the inefficiency of typical character-level models. It introduces a novel word encoder composed of two RNNs to create word representations from characters, and a hierarchical two-level decoder that generates translations character-by-character, conditioned on word-level states. The model, comprising six RNNs, is trained end-to-end.

**Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel and computationally efficient hierarchical decoder. By having a word-level RNN guide a character-level GRU variant (HGRU), the model maintains structural coherence while generating character sequences. The proposed batching method makes this deep architecture practical to train.
2.  A second significant contribution is the character-based word encoder that explicitly models subword composition. The design, which learns to weight character prefix representations, provides a compelling method for learning morphology dynamically, supported by strong qualitative evidence.

**Strengths:**
1.  The model demonstrates impressive training efficiency. Despite its deep, six-RNN architecture, it trains significantly faster than other character-level models and achieves strong results after only a single epoch, suggesting it learns more effectively from the data.
2.  The paper provides excellent qualitative analysis that supports its central claim of learning morphology. The PCA plots of word embeddings and visualizations of the internal encoder weights offer convincing evidence that the model captures meaningful subword structures.
3.  The model shows clear practical benefits in its ability to handle misspelled words and morphologically novel nonce words (e.g., “convenienter”), a key advantage of its fully character-level approach that subword-unit models like BPE cannot replicate.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The paper lacks crucial ablation studies. With multiple novel components (the word encoder and the hierarchical decoder), it is difficult to ascertain the individual contribution of each to the final performance. Experiments isolating these components are needed to validate the architectural design choices.
2.  The design of the word encoder, specifically the use of an unnormalized exponential for weighting character representations, is not well-justified. The authors state this "performs best" without further analysis, but this could lead to unstable training dynamics. A comparison to a standard softmax-based attention would be more principled.
3.  The evaluation is incomplete as it omits inference speed. Character-by-character generation is auto-regressive and can be very slow. A report on decoding speed is essential to assess the model’s practicality compared to faster subword-based systems.
