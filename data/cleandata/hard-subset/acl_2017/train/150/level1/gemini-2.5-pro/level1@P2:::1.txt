### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep, purely character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to overcome the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and training inefficiencies associated with large vocabularies. The model introduces a novel hierarchical architecture composed of six recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The two main contributions are: 1) A source-side word encoder that uses two RNNs to learn a weighted combination of character-prefix representations, intended to capture word morphology. 2) A hierarchical two-level decoder, where a word-level RNN predicts a sequence of word representations, and each of these representations is used to initialize the state of a character-level RNN (a custom "HGRU") that generates the corresponding word's character sequence. The authors evaluate their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, showing that it achieves BLEU scores competitive with state-of-the-art character and subword-level models, while claiming significantly improved training efficiency. Qualitative analyses are provided to support the claim that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Decoder for Character-Level NMT:** The primary contribution is the two-level decoding architecture. This design elegantly bridges the gap between word-level and character-level modeling. It allows the high-level attention mechanism to operate over a sequence length proportional to the number of source words (as in word-level NMT), which is computationally advantageous, while still generating output at the character level to eliminate the OOV problem. The proposed "HGRU" and the matrix-based "unfolding" technique for efficient batch training are clever engineering solutions to make this complex architecture practical.

2.  **A Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** The second contribution is the source word encoder, which composes a word vector from its constituent characters using a dual-RNN, attention-like mechanism. Unlike simpler character-level encoders (e.g., taking the final RNN state or using a CNN), this method explicitly models the contribution of different morpheme-like segments to the overall word meaning. The analysis provided suggests this approach produces more structured representations than a standard RNN encoder.

### Strengths
1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in NMT at the time: the trade-off between vocabulary size, OOV handling, and computational feasibility. Character-level models were known to be slow and difficult to train due to extremely long sequence lengths. The proposed hierarchical architecture is a conceptually sound and effective solution. By decomposing the generation process into "what word to say" (word-level decoder) and "how to spell it" (character-level decoder), the model maintains the structural benefits of word-level processing while reaping the open-vocabulary benefits of character-level generation.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are highly competitive with other contemporary state-of-the-art character-level systems (e.g., Chung et al., 2016b; Lee et al., 2016) and surpasses the strong BPE-based subword baseline on several tasks. More impressively, the paper highlights a significant advantage in training efficiency. For instance, the claim that their model outperforms the BPE baseline after just one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs is a powerful argument for its faster learning capability. This efficiency makes a complex, deep character model much more practical for researchers and practitioners.

3.  **Convincing Qualitative Analysis and Error Analysis:** The paper provides strong supporting evidence beyond just BLEU scores. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 effectively contrast their proposed word encoder with a simpler RNN, suggesting it learns more semantically meaningful representations. The analysis of internal weights in Figure 4 provides plausible evidence for the model's ability to identify morpheme boundaries. Most compellingly, the examples in Table 2, showing the model's ability to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words with valid morphology ("convenienter"), provide a concrete demonstration of the practical benefits of their approach over models reliant on a fixed vocabulary. This goes a long way toward validating the "learning morphology" claim.

### Weaknesses
1.  **High Model Complexity and Lack of Ablation Studies:** The proposed DCNMT model is very complex, involving six separate RNNs. While the performance is strong, it is difficult to ascertain which components are most critical to its success. The paper only compares the full model (DCNMT) with a version using a simpler RNN word encoder (CNMT). A more thorough ablation study is needed to disentangle the contributions of the novel word encoder and the hierarchical decoder. For instance, how would a standard character-CNN encoder (like Kim et al., 2016) perform when paired with the authors' hierarchical decoder? Conversely, how would their novel word encoder perform with a simpler, flat character decoder (like Chung et al., 2016b)? Without this, the performance gains are attributed to the entire complex system, not its specific innovations.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important architectural details are introduced with minimal justification beyond "it performs best in experiments."
    *   In the word encoder, the weights `wt` are passed through a `tanh` activation function rather than a `softmax`, which is standard for attention-like mechanisms. This means the weights do not sum to one and can be negative, which is counter-intuitive for a weighted sum. The rationale for this choice is not explained.
    *   The HGRU is a custom GRU variant. The state-resetting mechanism is central to the hierarchical decoder, but it is not clear if alternative methods for injecting the word-level state `st` (e.g., concatenating it to the character embedding at each step within a word) were explored.

3.  **Limited Evaluation on Morphologically Diverse Languages:** The model's central claim is that it learns morphology. While comparing English-French (morphologically poor) with English-Czech (morphologically rich) is a good start, the evaluation would be much stronger if it included languages with more complex and diverse morphological systems. For example, agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish, where single words can correspond to entire phrases in English, would serve as a more rigorous test bed for the model's morphological capabilities.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the individual contributions of the proposed word encoder and the hierarchical decoder? An ablation study replacing one of these components with a more standard alternative (e.g., a CNN word encoder or a flat character decoder) would be very insightful for understanding where the performance and efficiency gains originate.

2.  The use of `tanh` instead of a standard `softmax` for the weights in the word encoder (Section 3.1) is an unconventional choice for an attention-like mechanism. Could you provide more intuition or analysis on why this was more effective than a standard normalized attention mechanism? Does this allow the model to "down-weight" certain prefixes in a way that softmax cannot?

3.  Regarding the HGRU in the hierarchical decoder, have you experimented with alternative ways to condition the character-level generation on the word-level state `st`? For example, instead of fully resetting the HGRU state at word boundaries, one could concatenate `st` to the input of the HGRU at every character-level step. How does your proposed state-resetting mechanism compare to such alternatives?
