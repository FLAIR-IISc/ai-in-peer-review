**Reviewer for ACL_2017**

**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to overcome the limitations of fixed, large vocabularies. The architecture is hierarchical, operating on characters, subwords, and words. On the source side, a novel word encoder uses two RNNs to compute a weighted sum of character prefix representations, aiming to explicitly learn morphological structure. This produces a vector for each word. A standard sentence-level BiRNN then encodes the sequence of these word vectors. On the target side, a hierarchical decoder is employed. A first-level, word-based decoder with attention generates a sequence of state vectors, one for each target word. A second-level, character-based decoder (using a custom "HGRU" unit) is initialized with these word-level states to generate the actual character sequence for each word. The authors demonstrate that this complex six-RNN model achieves competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, trains efficiently, and provides qualitative evidence of learning morphology and handling OOV words like misspellings.

**Main Contributions**
1.  A novel, fully character-level NMT architecture that is hierarchical on both the encoder and decoder sides. This design cleverly avoids the long-sequence problem of pure char-to-char models by maintaining a word-level "scaffolding," which appears to be key to its training efficiency and performance. The efficient batch-training mechanism for the hierarchical decoder is a notable engineering contribution.
2.  Strong empirical validation of a character-level approach. The paper shows that a fully character-based model can be competitive with state-of-the-art subword (BPE) and other character-level systems on standard WMT tasks, including a morphologically rich language pair (En-Cs). The model's rapid convergence (achieving strong results after only one epoch) is particularly impressive.
3.  A novel source-side word encoder designed to capture morphology from characters, supported by compelling qualitative analysis. The visualizations and examples of translating nonce words suggest the model is learning meaningful sub-word representations, moving beyond simple character composition.

**Strengths**
1.  **Elegant and Principled Architecture:** The core idea of a hierarchical model that bridges the character and word levels is well-motivated and elegantly executed. By using word-level RNNs to process sequences of character-derived representations, the model keeps the primary sequence lengths manageable, mitigating the vanishing gradient and computational cost issues that plague naive char-to-char models. The symmetric hierarchical structure of the encoder and decoder is conceptually appealing and seems to be effective.

2.  **Strong and Efficient Empirical Performance:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are on par with or exceed contemporary state-of-the-art character- and subword-level systems on multiple language pairs. The most striking result is the model's training efficiency. As shown in Table 1, the DCNMT model outperforms the final BPE baseline after just one epoch of training on En-Fr and En-Cs. This rapid learning suggests the architectural priors (i.e., the explicit modeling of word structure) are highly effective and provide a significant advantage over less structured approaches.

3.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides strong supporting evidence for its claims beyond BLEU scores. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 compellingly argue that the proposed word encoder produces a more semantically structured representation space than a standard RNN encoder. Furthermore, the analysis of the encoder weights in Figure 4 provides a plausible, interpretable story for how the model learns morpheme-like units. Finally, the examples in Table 2, showing successful translation of misspelled and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"), are a powerful demonstration of the practical benefits of a truly open-vocabulary, morphologically-aware model.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The proposed model has two main novel components: the morphological word encoder and the hierarchical decoder. However, the paper does not provide an ablation study to disentangle their respective contributions. The comparison between CNMT (using a simple RNN for words) and DCNMT isolates the benefit of the source-side encoder, which is a good start. However, it is unclear how much of the overall performance gain comes from the hierarchical decoder structure itself. An experiment pairing a simpler word encoder (e.g., a CNN as in Kim et al., 2016) with the hierarchical decoder would have made the contribution of each component much clearer.

2.  **Unclear Justification for Word Encoder Design:** The specific formulation of the word encoder feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks theoretical grounding. The weights `wt` are calculated as `exp(aff(ht))`, which are unnormalized positive values. The final representation is `tanh(sum(wt * rt))`. Why is this specific combination of unnormalized weights and a final tanh squashing function chosen over more standard alternatives, such as using a softmax to create a proper attention distribution over character positions? The authors state that this "performs best in experiments," but this justification is insufficient for such a core component of the model. This opacity makes the mechanism less interpretable and harder to build upon.

3.  **Clarity and Complexity:** While the high-level idea is clear, the low-level details of the model are quite complex and at times hard to follow. The description of the HGRU and the "unfolding matrix" `R` for efficient batching is very dense. A more detailed explanation with a small concrete example would significantly improve clarity. The claim that the model is "the simplest" (Sec 5.1) seems questionable, given that it involves six interconnected RNNs and several custom components. Its parameter count may be smaller, but its architectural complexity is high.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the design choices for the source word encoder? Specifically, why did you opt for unnormalized exponential weights instead of a standard softmax, and what is the intuition behind applying `tanh` to the final weighted sum? Have you explored other aggregation functions?
2.  The HGRU update in Equation (2) seems to implement a hard reset of the character-level state `g_t-1` with the new word-level state `s_it` at every word boundary. Could you confirm this interpretation? Did you experiment with "softer" updates, such as gating or addition, that might allow information from the previous character-level state to persist across word boundaries?
3.  Given the impressive one-epoch results, could you comment on the model's final convergence? How many epochs did the final models in Table 1 train for, and did you observe significant overfitting or instability in later stages of training?
