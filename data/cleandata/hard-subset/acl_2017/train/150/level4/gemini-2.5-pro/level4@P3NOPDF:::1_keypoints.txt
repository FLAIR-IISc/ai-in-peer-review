- Paper proposes a novel character-to-character encoder-decoder that internally learns morpheme-like subword representations to combine open-vocabulary benefits with richer subword structure.
- Experiments on French-English, Czech-English, and English-Czech show the model learns plausible morphological segmentations and is competitive with other character-based systems.
- Strength: innovative hybrid architecture that handles rare/unseen words by operating on characters while using learned morpheme units internally.
- Strength: strong reproducibility—authors released source code and final trained models for all three language pairs.
- Strength: efficient training and good generalization—reasonable quality after one epoch and robustness to OOV and nonce words via morpheme backing-off.
- Weakness: experimental clarity is lacking—paper does not state which specific WMT/newstest sets were used (only discoverable in README).
- Weakness: comparisons are too narrow—omits broader state-of-the-art systems (e.g., BPE-based NMT and top phrase-based SMT) and does not contextualize the performance gap.
- Weakness: figure clarity—text describes a 6-level architecture but Figure 2 shows fewer layers; a complete diagram (possibly in an appendix) is needed.
- Weakness: software usability could be improved—README lacks copy-paste training/testing examples and the code should provide a standard --help flag.
- Weakness: minor presentation issues—need proofreading to fix grammatical errors, misspellings, and LaTeX formatting problems.
- Overall: interesting and promising contribution with exemplary open-sourcing, but impact is reduced by omissions in experimental reporting and comparisons; addressing dataset clarity and SOTA context would strengthen the paper.
