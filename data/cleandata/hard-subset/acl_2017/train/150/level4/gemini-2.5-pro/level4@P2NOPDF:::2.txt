### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel deep architecture for character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT), named DCNMT. The model extends the standard encoder-decoder framework by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer and a corresponding deep word-decoding layer. The encoder transforms character-level inputs into intermediate sub-word representations, which are then processed by the main NMT model. The decoder performs the reverse operation, converting target-side sub-word representations back into a final character sequence. The stated motivation for this architecture is to combine the advantages of character-level NMT, such as a small vocabulary and the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words, with the efficiency of sub-word models that operate on shorter sequences. The authors also claim that the proposed model is capable of learning morphological information more effectively than other state-of-the-art approaches.

**Strengths**

*   The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making the complex architecture relatively easy to follow.
*   The proposed deep character-to-subword architecture is a novel and interesting contribution to the field of character-level NMT.
*   The results presented are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses**

*   The empirical evaluation does not provide a conclusive case for the superiority of the proposed model over strong existing baselines. The reported performance gains are marginal and lack statistical significance analysis.
*   The claims regarding the model's ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently substantiated with rigorous experimental evidence, relying instead on a small set of qualitative examples.

**General Discussion**

My primary concern relates to the experimental evaluation and the comparison with other state-of-the-art systems. The authors compare their DCNMT model against several baselines on two key metrics: training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of DCNMT over a very strong baseline like `bpe2char` is not clearly established.

*   **BLEU Score:** The performance difference between DCNMT and `bpe2char` is minimal (0.04 BLEU on Cs-En and 0.1 BLEU on En-Cs). Without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to conclude that one model definitively outperforms the other. I strongly recommend that the authors conduct significance tests to validate their results.
*   **Training Time:** The training time comparison is also inconclusive. For the Cs-En task, `bpe2char` is reportedly eight days faster than DCNMT. Training time for `bpe2char` is not provided for En-Cs, and the model is not evaluated at all on En-Fr. To make a compelling argument for the proposed architecture, a more complete and direct comparison with `bpe2char` across all tasks is necessary.

My second major concern is with the analysis in Section 5.2, which discusses the model's ability to learn morphology. While the authors present several well-chosen and insightful examples, these qualitative illustrations are not a substitute for a formal evaluation. The claims made in this section are strong, but they are not backed by quantitative experiments. I would encourage the authors to expand this part of the work with a more systematic analysis. This could involve using morphological analysis tools to evaluate the learned segmentations or designing specific probes to test morphological awareness. For the current submission, if such an evaluation is not feasible, I suggest toning down the claims about morphological learning and potentially moving the current qualitative analysis to an appendix.

**Other Comments, Doubts, and Suggestions**

*   **Acronyms:** Several acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) are used without being defined. Others (e.g., RNN, BPE) are defined only after their first use. For clarity, please ensure all acronyms are defined upon their first appearance.
*   **Concept of "Energy":** The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but is used extensively in the analysis in Section 5.2. It would be beneficial to briefly reiterate its meaning in Section 5.2 and provide more intuition on its interpretation. For instance, does a high energy value on a character suggest a likely morpheme boundary? Additionally, the term "peak" used in the context of Figure 5 should be explicitly defined.
*   **Acronym Consistency:** The acronym for Byte Pair Encoding is introduced in uppercase (BPE) but is subsequently written in lowercase (`bpe`). Please use consistent capitalization.
*   **Monolingual Data:** The statement in Section 4.1 that "no monolingual corpus is used" seems unnecessary and could perhaps be omitted.
*   **Figure 4a:** There appears to be a rendering issue with Figure 4a, as the color-coded energy values are not visible for all characters.
*   **Table 1 Results:** The results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) on the Cs-En task are not reported in the original paper. If the authors reproduced these results themselves, this should be explicitly stated.
*   **Morphological Richness:** Describing French as "morphologically poor" is an oversimplification. A more accurate characterization would be that it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
*   **Data Links:** A link is provided for the WMT'15 training corpora but not for WMT'14. For consistency, please provide links for all corpora used.
*   **References:** Several references in the bibliography are incomplete. Please review and complete them.
