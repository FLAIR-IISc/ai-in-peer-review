### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder architecture for Neural Machine Translation (NMT) that operates on a character-to-character basis. The key innovation is an internal architecture that learns to represent and process text through a combination of characters and learned morpheme-like units. The model aims to leverage the open-vocabulary advantages of character-level models while capturing the richer linguistic structure of subword units. The authors provide results for French-English, Czech-English, and English-Czech translation tasks, demonstrating the model's ability to learn plausible morphological segmentations and achieve competitive results against other character-based systems.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Architecture:** The primary strength of this work is its innovative hybrid architecture. By starting and ending with characters but operating internally on learned morphological representations, the model offers an elegant approach to handling rare and unseen words, a common challenge in NMT. The qualitative analysis suggests the model is successful in learning meaningful morphological segmentations.

2.  **Reproducibility and Openness:** The authors should be highly commended for their commitment to reproducible research. They have released not only their source code but also the final trained models for all three language pairs. This greatly facilitates validation of their results and enables other researchers to more easily replicate and extend this work.

3.  **Training and Generalization:** The model appears to be efficient, achieving reasonable translation quality after just a single training epoch, with continued improvement in subsequent epochs. Furthermore, the paper provides compelling qualitative evidence that the system can handle out-of-vocabulary words (including nonce words) by implicitly backing off to its learned morpheme representations, demonstrating strong generalization capabilities.

### **Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a promising direction, several weaknesses should be addressed to strengthen the submission.

1.  **Lack of Experimental Clarity:** A significant weakness is the failure to specify the datasets used for development and testing. The paper reports results on WMT data but does not state which specific `newstest` sets were used. This is critical information required for readers to compare the reported BLEU scores with existing literature and public benchmarks (e.g., matrix.statmt.org). I was only able to identify the datasets (newstest2013 for development and newstest2015 for testing) by consulting the README file in the supplementary code. This essential detail must be explicitly included in the main body of the paper.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The experimental comparison is too narrow. The authors compare their model against several other character-level NMT systems but omit any comparison to the broader state-of-the-art for these language pairs. On these standard WMT datasets, top-performing systems (including phrase-based SMT and other NMT architectures like BPE-based models) have achieved significantly higher scores. Acknowledging and discussing this performance gap is crucial for properly contextualizing the paper's contribution and understanding the current trade-offs of the proposed model.

3.  **Clarity of Figures:** There is a confusing discrepancy between the architecture described in the text and the accompanying diagram. The paper states the model has a 6-level architecture, but Figure 2 appears to illustrate fewer layers. The caption should be revised to clarify whether the figure is a simplified view. To avoid ambiguity, a complete diagram of the full architecture should be provided, even if placed in an appendix.

4.  **Software Usability:** While providing the code is a major plus, its usability could be improved. The README file lacks explicit, copy-pasteable examples for running the training and testing commands, which would lower the barrier to entry for other researchers. Furthermore, the software should adhere to standard command-line conventions by implementing a `--help` flag to display usage information.

5.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading pass to correct a handful of minor grammatical errors, misspellings, and LaTeX formatting issues (such as reverse quotation marks).

### **Overall Recommendation**

This paper makes a nice contribution to the literature on character-level and subword-based Neural Machine Translation. The proposed architecture is interesting, and the model's ability to learn morphology from raw text is a promising result. The commitment to open-sourcing code and models is exemplary.

However, the paper's impact is currently diminished by the significant omissions in the experimental reporting and comparison. The lack of clarity on test sets and the absence of a comparison to the overall state-of-the-art make it difficult to assess the practical performance of the system. If the authors can address these points—particularly by clarifying the datasets in the paper and discussing the results in the context of the wider state-of-the-art—this would become a much stronger and more impactful submission.
