### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model. The architecture features a novel word encoder that learns sub-word representations from character sequences and a hierarchical decoder that generates target words character-by-character. The goal is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT, such as eliminating out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words and reducing vocabulary size, while using an intermediate word-level structure to manage the long sequence lengths that typically challenge pure character-level models.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture:** The primary contribution is the design of an end-to-end, fully character-level NMT system that internally operates on a word-like structure. The model, comprising six recurrent neural networks, effectively bridges character-level inputs and outputs with word-level encoding and decoding steps, which is an original and well-motivated approach to the problem.
2.  **A Character-Based Word Encoder:** The proposed word encoder, which uses two RNNs to compute a weighted sum of character prefix representations, is a notable component. This mechanism is designed to automatically learn sub-word units and their compositions without relying on pre-processing steps like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE).
3.  **Empirical Demonstration of Viability:** The paper demonstrates that a complex, deep character-level model can achieve BLEU scores competitive with strong subword-based baselines on both morphologically rich (En-Cs) and less rich (En-Fr) language pairs. The qualitative analysis showing the model's ability to handle misspelled and nonce words is also a valuable contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Ambitious Architecture:** The proposed model is an elegant and non-trivial attempt to combine the strengths of character-level and word-level NMT. The hierarchical design, which processes information at character, sub-word, and word levels, is a creative solution to mitigate the respective weaknesses of each approach (e.g., long dependencies for character models, OOV issues for word models). The end-to-end differentiability of such a complex system is a significant engineering achievement.

2.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured, making the complex architecture relatively easy to follow. The explanation of the model components is logical, and the figures—particularly Figure 2 illustrating the overall architecture and data flow—are effective and greatly aid in understanding the system's mechanics.

3.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** The qualitative analysis in Section 5.3 provides compelling evidence for a key advantage of the character-level approach. The examples in Table 2, where the model correctly translates misspelled words and morphological neologisms (e.g., "convenienter"), effectively showcase the model's flexibility and its superiority over word-level systems in handling such inputs. This is a strong argument for the practical utility of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Comparison to Baselines:** The performance claims are not fully supported by the results in Table 1. The BLEU score gains over the strong `bpe2char` baseline are marginal (0.1 BLEU on En-Cs and 0.04 on Cs-En) and may not be statistically significant. Moreover, the claimed training efficiency is questionable; on the Cs-En task, the proposed DCNMT model takes 22 days to train, which is significantly longer than the 14 days reported for `bpe2char`. The training time for En-Cs is not provided for the baseline, and `bpe2char` is not evaluated on En-Fr, making a complete assessment of the efficiency trade-offs difficult.

2.  **Unsupported Claims about Learning Morphology:** The claims that the model learns morphology are not substantiated with rigorous, quantitative evidence. The analysis in Section 5.2, while insightful, is anecdotal and relies on a few well-chosen examples and PCA visualizations. This does not constitute sufficient scientific proof. While the visualizations are interesting, a more formal evaluation is needed to support such strong claims. For example, the authors could test the learned representations on dedicated morphological analogy tasks or perform a more systematic analysis of the morpheme segmentation. As this analysis is not central to the main contribution of the paper, I would suggest either toning down these claims or moving this section to an appendix.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Details:** Several aspects of the paper could be clarified to improve reproducibility and understanding.
    *   Many acronyms are used without prior definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA). While some are common knowledge, defining them would improve clarity for a broader audience.
    *   The concepts of "energy" and "peak energy" used to describe the word encoder's behavior in Section 5.2 are not formally defined, making their interpretation ambiguous. A more precise explanation of how these values are derived and what they signify would be beneficial.
    *   In Table 1, the provenance of the Cs-En results for the `bpe2char` model is unclear, as the cited paper (Chung et al., 2016b) does not appear to report them. If the authors ran this experiment themselves, this should be explicitly stated.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Have you performed statistical significance tests for your BLEU score comparisons, particularly against the `bpe2char` model? Given the small differences, this information is crucial for evaluating whether your model provides a meaningful improvement.

2.  Could you clarify the trade-off between the marginal BLEU improvements and the increased training time observed on the Cs-En task? Your abstract claims the model is "more efficient in training," but the data in Table 1 seems to contradict this when compared to the `bpe2char` baseline.

3.  The results for the `bpe2char` model on Cs-En in Table 1 are not found in the cited paper (Chung et al., 2016b). Did you run this baseline yourselves? If so, could you provide the implementation details to ensure a fair comparison? Furthermore, could you provide the missing training time for the En-Cs baseline?
