### Strengths

*   The authors present a novel adaptation of the encoder-decoder NMT architecture. The proposed model operates on characters at the input and output layers but utilizes intermediate representations of both morphemes and characters, which is an interesting and well-motivated approach.
*   The public release of the source code and the final trained models for French-English, Czech-English, and English-Czech is a commendable practice. This transparency facilitates the validation of the reported results and allows other researchers to more easily replicate and extend this work.
*   The system demonstrates promising performance early in the training process, achieving reasonable translation quality after just a single epoch and showing continued improvement in subsequent epochs.
*   The model appears to learn plausible morphological segmentations and effectively handles out-of-vocabulary words, including nonce words, by leveraging its subword-level representations.

### Weaknesses

*   The paper does not specify which WMT development and test sets were used for evaluation. This omission makes it difficult for readers to compare the reported results with existing work, such as the scores compiled on matrix.statmt.org. This reviewer had to consult the README file in the supplementary code to discover that the experiments used `newstest2013` for development and `newstest2015` for testing. This crucial information should be stated explicitly in the paper.
*   While the inclusion of a software README is helpful, the instructions could be improved. The training and testing sections would benefit from explicit command-line examples. Additionally, the software currently lacks a standard `--help` flag for command-line usage, which would improve its usability.
*   There is a discrepancy between the model description and its visualization. The paper describes a 6-level architecture, but the diagram in Figure 2 appears to illustrate fewer than six layers. The figure caption should be more explicit about what is being shown. If the figure is a simplified view, a complete diagram of the full architecture should be provided, perhaps in an appendix.
*   The experimental results are compared only to other character-based neural systems. The paper does not situate its performance relative to the broader state-of-the-art in machine translation, which includes other architectures. Publicly available WMT results for these language pairs suggest that the overall state-of-the-art is significantly higher than the results reported here. The authors should acknowledge this gap and ideally discuss how their system compares to top-performing MT systems more generally.
*   The manuscript contains a handful of minor errors in English usage, spelling, and LaTeX formatting (e.g., incorrect quotation marks). These should be corrected in the final version.

### General Discussion

This paper is a solid contribution to the literature on character-level and subword-level neural machine translation, presenting a novel architecture with promising capabilities.
