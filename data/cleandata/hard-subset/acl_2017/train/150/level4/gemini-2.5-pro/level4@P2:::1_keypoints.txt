- Proposes a hierarchical character-to-character NMT model with a two-RNN word encoder and a two-level decoder (word representations then characters).

- Contribution: novel hierarchical architecture that bridges word- and character-level NMT, keeping open vocabulary while adding useful structure.

- Contribution: shows effective unsupervised morphological learning via weighted-prefix encoder, clustering, and correct handling of nonce words/misspellings.

- Contribution: an efficient, high-performing character-level model that converges quickly and can outperform BPE baselines after one epoch.

- Strength: model design is novel and well-motivated, learning subword units implicitly through an interpretable weighted-prefix encoder.

- Strength: provides compelling qualitative and quantitative analyses (PCA projections, weight visualizations, translation examples) explaining why the model works.

- Strength: strong empirical results across language pairs, including morphologically rich languages, with notable training efficiency.

- Strength: authors released code and trained models, supporting reproducibility.

- Weakness: lacks comparison to the broader WMT/state-of-the-art systems, making overall performance positioning unclear.

- Weakness: architectural diagram (Figure 2) is too high-level and does not clearly show the six RNN components and their interactions.

- Weakness: minor presentation issues (grammar, formatting) and missing dataset details in table captions that reduce clarity.

- Question: how do the reported results compare to overall SOTA on WMT14 En–Fr and WMT15 En–Cs?

- Question: please clarify the mapping between the "six recurrent networks" in the text and the components shown in Figure 2.

- Question: why apply tanh to the weighted sum in the word encoder instead of normalizing weights (e.g., softmax), and what effect does this choice have?
