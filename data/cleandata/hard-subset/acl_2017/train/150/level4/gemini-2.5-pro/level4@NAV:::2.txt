- Strengths: The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written. The explanations are largely easy to follow, the proposed ideas are novel, and the results are compelling.

- Weaknesses: The interpretation of the results raises some questions. Furthermore, the claims about the proposed method's ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by rigorous scientific evidence.

- General Discussion:
This paper presents a sophisticated architecture for character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT). The proposed model builds upon the conventional encoder-decoder framework by introducing a deep word-encoding layer. This layer processes character-level input to generate sub-word representations for the source sentence. Symmetrically, a deep word-decoding layer is incorporated on the output side to convert target-language sub-word representations back into a final character sequence. The motivation for this architecture is to harness the advantages of character-level NMT—namely, a reduced vocabulary size and the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words—while simultaneously boosting system performance by using an intermediate sub-word representation, which shortens the effective length of the input character sequence. The authors also assert that their deep word-encoding model surpasses other state-of-the-art methods in its capacity for morphological learning.

My primary concern pertains to the experimental evaluation. The comparison against the `bpe2char` baseline is not entirely convincing. The reported differences in BLEU score are marginal (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), making it difficult to conclude that one model outperforms the other without a statistical significance test. Was such an analysis performed? Regarding training time, the `bpe2char` model is noted to be eight days faster for the Cs-En task. The training time for En-Cs is conspicuously absent, and the `bpe2char` model is not evaluated on En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison with this baseline is necessary to substantiate the claimed advantages of the proposed model.

A second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate the system's ability to learn morphology. However, this section consists primarily of a few well-chosen examples and qualitative discussion, rather than formal experiments or quantitative evaluation. While the examples are illustrative and didactically presented, they do not constitute sufficient evidence to support the authors' claims. This line of investigation is very promising and could potentially form the basis of a separate publication. For the current work, given that this analysis is not central to the paper's main contribution, I recommend moving this section to an appendix and moderating the claims regarding the system's morphological learning capabilities.

Other comments, doubts and suggestions:
 - Many acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) are used without prior definition, while others (e.g., RNN, BPE) are defined only after their first use. For the sake of clarity, and although many are common in the field, it is recommended that all acronyms be explicitly defined upon their first appearance.
 - The concept of 'energy' is introduced in Section 3.1 but is used again extensively in Section 5.2 without further explanation. It would be beneficial to revisit this concept in Section 5.2, perhaps clarifying its interpretation. For instance, does a high energy value on a character suggest a morpheme boundary? Additionally, the term 'peak' used in the context of Figure 5 is not defined.
 - Please clarify the inconsistent capitalization of the 'BPE' acronym, which is capitalized at its introduction but lowercased thereafter.
 - In Section 4.1, the statement that no monolingual corpus was used seems superfluous. Consider removing it for conciseness.
 - There appears to be a rendering issue in Figure 4a, as the color-coding for energy values is missing for some characters.
 - The results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) in Table 1 for Cs-En are not reported in the original paper. If these results were reproduced by the authors, this should be explicitly stated.
 - Characterizing French as 'morphologically poor' may be an overstatement. It would be more accurate to describe it as less morphologically rich than a Slavic language like Czech.
 - A hyperlink is provided for the WMT'15 training corpora but not for WMT'14. For consistency, please provide links for both.
 - Several bibliographic entries are incomplete.
