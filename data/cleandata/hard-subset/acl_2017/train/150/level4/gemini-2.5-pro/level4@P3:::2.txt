### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a deep, hierarchical neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that operates purely at the character level. The model aims to mitigate the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-level models while improving upon the training efficiency of previous character-level systems. The proposed architecture consists of six recurrent neural networks (RNNs) organized into several layers. On the source side, a novel word encoder first processes characters to form subword and word representations, which are then passed to a sentence-level encoder. On the target side, a hierarchical decoder first predicts a word-level representation, which then initializes a second-level RNN to generate the corresponding character sequence for that word. The authors evaluate their model on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, claiming competitive performance with state-of-the-art systems and superior training efficiency.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The primary contribution is the design of a complex, fully end-to-end character-level translation system. The architecture's specific components, including the weighted RNN-based word encoder and the two-level hierarchical decoder (HGRU), represent a novel approach to structuring the translation process from source characters to target characters while retaining intermediate word-level abstractions.

2.  **Qualitative Demonstration of Handling Morphological Nuances and OOV Words:** The paper provides a compelling qualitative analysis of the model's ability to translate misspelled words and morphologically complex nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"). This effectively showcases a key practical advantage of character-level modeling that is often lost in word- or subword-based systems.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Ambitious and Well-Motivated Model Design:** The proposed six-RNN architecture is an ambitious and principled attempt to combine the benefits of different levels of linguistic granularity (character, subword, word). The design, particularly the hierarchical decoder that separates the prediction of "what to say" (word-level) from "how to write it" (character-level), is conceptually elegant and addresses a known challenge in long-sequence character modeling.

2.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Non-Standard Words:** The examples in Table 2 are a strong argument for the model's practical utility. Its ability to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and invented but morphologically plausible words ("convenienter") demonstrates a robustness that is highly desirable in real-world applications and is a clear advantage over models reliant on fixed vocabularies.

3.  **Promising Training Efficiency in Early Stages:** The results in Table 1 show that the proposed DCNMT model can outperform a BPE-based baseline after only a single epoch of training on the En-Fr and En-Cs tasks. This suggests the architecture is capable of learning effective representations quickly, lending credence to the authors' claims about improved training efficiency, at least in the initial phases.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unsubstantiated Claims Regarding "Learning Morphology":** The paper's central claim that its word encoder "learns morphology" is not supported by rigorous scientific evidence. Section 5.2 relies entirely on a few hand-picked examples, PCA visualizations, and an analysis of internal "energy" weights. While this qualitative analysis is insightful, it does not constitute a formal evaluation. To validate such a strong claim, the learned representations should be evaluated on a dedicated morphological analysis task (e.g., morpheme segmentation or part-of-speech tagging for rare words). Without this, the claims should be significantly softened, and this section might be better framed as qualitative analysis in an appendix.

2.  **Inconclusive Final Performance and Efficiency Comparison:** The empirical comparison with other state-of-the-art character-level models is not entirely convincing. For both En-Cs and Cs-En, the BLEU score difference between DCNMT and the bpe2char model is marginal (0.1 and 0.04 BLEU, respectively), a difference that is likely not statistically significant. Furthermore, the claim of superior efficiency is challenged by the training times in Table 1: for Cs-En, the DCNMT model took 22 days, whereas the bpe2char model took only 14. For En-Cs, the training time for the competing model is not provided, making a full comparison impossible. This weakens the argument that the proposed model offers a clear advantage over existing methods.

3.  **Minor Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The paper could be improved with better attention to detail. Several widely-used acronyms (e.g., LSTM, CNN, PCA) are used without definition, while others are defined after their first use. The concept of "energy" is central to the analysis in Section 5.2 but is not clearly defined or interpreted for the reader. There are also minor inconsistencies (e.g., capitalization of "BPE" vs. "bpe") and formatting problems (e.g., missing colors in Figure 4a, incomplete references) that detract from the overall clarity.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance gap between your DCNMT model and the `bpe2char` model is very narrow on the Czech translation tasks. Have you performed any statistical significance tests to confirm whether your model's performance is demonstrably different? Furthermore, given that your model takes significantly longer to train on the Cs-En task (22 days vs. 14 days), could you elaborate on the claimed efficiency benefits of your architecture?

2.  The qualitative analysis in Section 5.2 is interesting, but the title and claims focus heavily on "learning morphology." Would you be willing to either provide quantitative evidence to support this claim (e.g., via an extrinsic evaluation task) or soften the claims to better reflect the qualitative nature of your analysis?

3.  In Table 1, the results for the `bpe2char` model on the Cs-En task appear to be new, as they are not in the cited paper. Could you confirm if you ran these experiments yourselves? Additionally, why is the training time for `bpe2char` on the En-Cs task missing?
