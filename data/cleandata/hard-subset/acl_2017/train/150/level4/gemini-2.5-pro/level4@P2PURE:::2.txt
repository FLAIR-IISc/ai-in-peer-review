**Strengths:**
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making most of the technical explanations easy to follow. The proposed ideas are novel, and the results are interesting.

**Weaknesses:**
The interpretation of the results is not entirely convincing. Furthermore, the claims regarding the model's ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence.

**General Discussion:**
This paper proposes a novel architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The model extends the standard encoder-decoder NMT framework by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer, which maps character-level input to sub-word-level representations of the source sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer transforms the target-language sub-word representations into a final character sequence output. This architecture aims to combine the benefits of character-level NMT (e.g., reduced vocabulary, handling of unknown words) with the efficiency of sub-word models, which shorten the input sequence length. The authors also claim that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than other state-of-the-art approaches.

The empirical evaluation raises some concerns. The advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over baselines such as bpe2char is not clearly established. The reported BLEU score differences are marginal (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), and without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to conclude that DCNMT truly outperforms bpe2char. Regarding training time, the comparison is also inconclusive; for Cs-En, the bpe2char model trains 8 days faster than DCNMT. Training time for En-Cs is not provided for bpe2char, nor is bpe2char evaluated on the En-Fr task. A more thorough comparison with the bpe2char system is needed to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed model.

A second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate the model's ability to learn morphology. However, this section relies on a few selected examples rather than a systematic, quantitative evaluation. While the examples are illustrative and well-explained, they do not constitute sufficient empirical evidence to support the claims about the model's morphological awareness. I encourage the authors to expand on this interesting analysis, which could potentially form the basis of a separate publication. Since this analysis is not central to the paper's main contribution, I suggest that for the current version, the authors soften these claims and consider moving this section to an appendix.

**Other Comments and Suggestions:**
*   Several acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) are used without definition, while others (e.g., RNN, BPE) are defined after their first use. For clarity, all acronyms should be defined upon first mention.
*   The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1. It would be helpful to briefly recap this concept in Section 5.2, where it is used frequently, and clarify its interpretation. For example, does a high energy value on a character suggest a morpheme boundary? Additionally, the term "peak" used in Figure 5 should be defined.
*   The acronym BPE is defined in uppercase but subsequently used in lowercase. This should be made consistent.
*   Is it necessary to state that no monolingual corpus was used in Section 4.1? This is typically the default assumption.
*   Figure 4a appears to be rendered incorrectly, as the energy value colors are missing for some characters.
*   In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) for Cs-En are not reported in the original paper. If the authors computed these results themselves, this should be explicitly stated.
*   The description of French as "morphologically poor" is an overstatement. It would be more accurate to describe it as less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
*   Why is a link provided for the WMT'15 training corpus but not for WMT'14?
*   Several references in the bibliography are incomplete.
