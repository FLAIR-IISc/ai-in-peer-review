**Strengths**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The explanations are generally easy to follow, the ideas presented are original, and the results are quite interesting.

**Weaknesses**

I have some concerns about the interpretation of the results. Additionally, the claims regarding the proposed method's ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently backed by empirical evidence.

**General Discussion**

This paper proposes a novel, complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The architecture extends a standard encoder-decoder model by adding a deep word-encoding layer to transform character-level input into sub-word representations. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added on the output side to convert target-language sub-word representations back into a final character sequence. The goal is to combine the benefits of character-level NMT (e.g., reduced vocabulary size, ability to handle unseen words) with the efficiency of sub-word models, which shorten the effective input sequence length. The authors also claim that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than other state-of-the-art approaches.

I have some concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. The authors compare their approach to other state-of-the-art systems based on two metrics: training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over competing approaches, such as bpe2char, is not clearly demonstrated. The differences in BLEU score are very small (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), and it is difficult to conclude that one model significantly outperforms the other. Have statistical significance tests been performed? Regarding training time, the bpe2char model for Cs-En is noted to take 8 fewer days to train than DCNMT. Crucially, the training time for En-Cs is not provided, and the bpe2char baseline is not evaluated at all for En-Fr. A more complete comparison with this system is necessary to substantiate the claimed advantages of the proposed model.

My second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors investigate the system's ability to learn morphology. However, the analysis in this section is purely qualitative, consisting of a few examples and accompanying commentary. While these examples are well-chosen and illustrative, no formal experiments or quantitative evaluation are presented to support the authors' claims. This is a very interesting line of inquiry, and I would encourage the authors to expand on it in future work; it could even form the basis of a separate paper. Since this analysis is not central to the paper's main contribution, I suggest that for the current submission, the authors move this section to an appendix and soften the claims made about the system's ability to learn morphology.

**Other comments, doubts and suggestions:**

*   Several acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) are used without being defined, while others (e.g., RNN, BPE) are defined only after their first use. While many of these are standard in the field, defining all acronyms upon first use would improve clarity.
*   The concept of 'energy' is introduced in Section 3.1. It would be helpful to briefly recap this concept in Section 5.2, where it is used extensively. Providing some intuition on its interpretation would also be beneficial (e.g., does high energy on a character indicate a likely morpheme boundary?). Additionally, the term 'peak' used in the context of Figure 5 is not defined.
*   The acronym BPE is defined in uppercase but subsequently written in lowercase ('bpe'). This inconsistency should be corrected.
*   The statement in Section 4.1 that no monolingual corpus was used seems unnecessary.
*   In Figure 4a, the color-coding for energy values appears to be missing for some characters.
*   In Table 1, the Cs-En results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) do not appear in the original paper. If the authors of this submission computed these results themselves, this should be explicitly stated.
*   The paper describes French as "morphologically poor." It would be more accurate to state that it is less morphologically rich than a Slavic language like Czech.
*   A URL is provided for the WMT'15 training corpora but not for the WMT'14 corpora. This should be made consistent.
*   Several bibliographic references are incomplete.
