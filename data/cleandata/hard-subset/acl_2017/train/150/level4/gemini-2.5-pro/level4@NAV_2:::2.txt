**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture, named DCNMT. The model aims to combine the advantages of character-level NMT, such as handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words, with the efficiency of subword-level models. The proposed architecture features a novel word encoder composed of two recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to learn subword representations from character sequences. On the decoding side, it employs a hierarchical structure where a word-level decoder's state initializes a character-level decoder (HGRU) to generate the final translation. The authors claim this hierarchical approach (character→subword→word→sentence→word→character) enables efficient end-to-end training and allows the model to learn morphology. Experiments on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks show that the model achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel, deep hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT. The core innovations are the dual-RNN word encoder designed to learn subword representations automatically from characters, and the hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates character sequences from word-level states.
2.  An empirical demonstration of the model's effectiveness and training efficiency. The proposed system achieves competitive BLEU scores on multiple language pairs. Notably, it outperforms a BPE-based baseline after only a single epoch of training on two tasks, and it shows a strong qualitative ability to translate misspelled words and unseen morphological variants.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The paper presents a sophisticated and original architecture for character-level NMT. The hierarchical design is a thoughtful approach to mitigate the challenges of pure character-level models (e.g., long sequence lengths and training difficulty) while avoiding the fixed vocabularies of word-level models. The proposed word encoder and hierarchical decoder are interesting and non-trivial contributions to the field.
2.  **Promising Empirical Results and Efficiency:** The experiments show that DCNMT achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with other state-of-the-art character- and subword-level systems. The result that the model can outperform a strong bpe-based baseline after just one epoch of training (on En-Fr and En-Cs) is particularly compelling evidence for its training efficiency and ability to learn useful representations quickly.
3.  **Effective Handling of Open-Vocabulary Phenomena:** The paper provides strong qualitative evidence of the model's ability to handle words not seen during training. As shown in Table 2, the model can correctly translate misspellings and nonce words with novel morphological inflections (e.g., "convenienter"). This highlights a clear advantage over standard word-level NMT and demonstrates that the character-level modeling is capturing meaningful sub-word regularities.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. The authors do a good job of explaining the complex architecture, and the accompanying diagrams (Figures 1 and 2) are helpful in understanding the data flow and model components.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison to Baselines:** The quantitative comparison with the state-of-the-art `bpe2char` model is not fully convincing. The reported differences in BLEU score on En-Cs and Cs-En are very small (0.1 and 0.04, respectively) and are presented without statistical significance tests, making it impossible to determine if DCNMT offers a tangible improvement. Furthermore, the training efficiency claims are undermined by the Cs-En results, where DCNMT requires 8 more days of training than `bpe2char`. The omission of the training time for `bpe2char` on the En-Cs task further complicates a fair assessment of efficiency.
2.  **Unsupported Claims on Learning Morphology:** Section 5.2 claims to investigate the model's ability to learn morphology, but the analysis is based solely on a few curated examples and visualizations. While these examples are illustrative, they do not constitute rigorous scientific evidence. The paper lacks any formal experiments or quantitative metrics to systematically evaluate the morphological competence of the word encoder. As a result, the claims in this section are overstated and should be toned down or framed as preliminary, qualitative observations.
3.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Technical Details:** The paper is unclear on several points, which hinders reproducibility and full understanding.
    *   Several widely-used but important acronyms are used without being defined (e.g., LSTM, CNN, PCA), or are defined after their first use (RNN, BPE).
    *   The concept of "energy" is central to the analysis in Section 5.2, but it is not clearly defined or interpreted. The meaning of the "peak energy" markers in Figure 5 is not explained at all.
    *   Figure 4a, which visualizes character energies, appears to be corrupted or rendered incorrectly, as the color values are missing for many characters, making it difficult to interpret.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the marginal differences in BLEU scores between DCNMT and `bpe2char` in Table 1, have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate that your model's performance is superior?
2.  Could you clarify the results on training time? Specifically, why is the training time for the `bpe2char` model on En-Cs missing? And how do you reconcile the claim of greater training efficiency with the fact that your model took significantly longer to train (22 vs. 14 days) than `bpe2char` on the Cs-En task?
3.  Regarding the morphology analysis in Section 5.2, could you provide a more precise definition of "energy"? How should the "peak energy" markers in Figure 5 be interpreted, and do they consistently correspond to morpheme boundaries across a larger, more diverse set of examples?
4.  The results for the `bpe2char` model (Chung et al., 2016b) on the Cs-En task do not appear in the original publication. Did you reproduce these results by training the model yourselves? If so, this should be explicitly stated in the paper.
