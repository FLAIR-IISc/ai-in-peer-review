### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to learn morphology implicitly. The architecture avoids the large vocabulary limitations of word-level models by operating directly on characters for both input and output. The core of the model consists of a novel source-side word encoder, which uses two recurrent networks to generate weighted representations of character sequences, and a hierarchical decoder that generates target words character-by-character.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Effective Character-Level Architecture:** The paper introduces a well-motivated, deep hierarchical model that successfully learns representations of words from their constituent characters. This approach elegantly handles the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and shows a promising ability to interpret and translate unseen morphological variations, including nonce words.
2.  **Demonstration of High Training Efficiency:** A significant practical contribution is the model's ability to achieve strong translation performance after only a single training epoch. This rapid learning curve compares favorably to other character-level models, which are often cited as being slow and difficult to train.
3.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and final pre-trained models publicly available. This is a valuable contribution to the community, as it facilitates the verification of their results and enables other researchers to build upon this work.

### Strengths

1.  **Effective Morphological Learning and OOV Handling:** The paper's primary strength is its model's demonstrated ability to learn meaningful sub-word representations without explicit segmentation. The analysis in Section 5.2, particularly the PCA plots and the examples of segmenting words like "consumers" into "consum-er-s," provides compelling evidence that the word encoder is capturing useful morphological structure. This capability directly translates into robust performance on rare words, misspelled words, and even nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"), which is a significant advantage over standard word-level and subword-level (BPE) models.

2.  **Impressive Training Speed:** The claim that the model achieves competitive BLEU scores after just one epoch of training (as shown in Table 1 for En-Fr and En-Cs) is a strong argument for its acceptance. Character-level models are notoriously data-hungry and slow to converge. The efficiency of this architecture makes it a more practical alternative, lowering the barrier to entry for training effective character-based NMT systems.

3.  **Clear and Well-Supported Analysis:** The authors provide a good analysis to support their claims. The qualitative examples in Table 2 clearly illustrate the benefits of their approach over systems that treat unknown words as atomic `<unk>` tokens. Furthermore, the comparison of word embeddings between the ordinary RNN encoder and the proposed encoder (Figure 3) effectively visualizes the improved representational quality achieved by the model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inadequate Comparison to the Overall State of the Art:** The paper's claim of achieving performance "comparable to the state-of-the-art systems" is not fully substantiated. While the model is compared against several other character-level and BPE-based NMT systems, it is not benchmarked against the top-performing systems for these language pairs and test sets from the WMT campaigns. For example, results on public leaderboards (such as matrix.statmt.org) for newstest2015 show that phrase-based SMT and other NMT architectures achieve significantly higher BLEU scores. The absence of this broader context could be misleading and overstates the model's performance relative to the field as a whole. This should be acknowledged and discussed.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Reporting and Model Visualization:** The paper omits critical details about the experimental setup. The specific development and test sets used (newstest2013 and newstest2015) are not mentioned in the main body of the paper and could only be found by inspecting the supplementary code. This information is essential for comparison and reproducibility and should be stated explicitly. Additionally, the architecture diagram in Figure 2 is confusing. It is difficult to reconcile the illustration with the textual description of a six-RNN, four-layer model, which hinders a clear understanding of the full system architecture.

3.  **Minor Issues with Manuscript and Software Polish:** The paper suffers from a number of minor but distracting English language errors, typos, and LaTeX formatting issues (e.g., incorrect quotation marks). These should be corrected in a final version. While the release of the code is commendable, its usability could be improved. The README file lacks explicit examples of the commands needed for training and testing, and the software does not respond to a standard `--help` flag, making it more difficult for others to use.
