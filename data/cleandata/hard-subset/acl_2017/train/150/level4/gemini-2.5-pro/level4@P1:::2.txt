### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The model extends the standard encoder-decoder framework by introducing a novel word encoder that processes character-level input to create intermediate word representations. This is followed by a sentence-level encoder and a hierarchical decoder that generates the final translation at the character level. The primary goal is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT (e.g., a small, fixed vocabulary and the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words) while mitigating the challenges of processing very long character sequences by using an intermediate word-level representation. The authors also claim their word encoder is capable of learning morphology.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel, deep hierarchical architecture for end-to-end character-level NMT, composed of six recurrent neural networks.
2.  A new word encoder model designed to learn morphological representations by computing a weighted sum of character-level RNN hidden states.
3.  An empirical evaluation on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, which shows competitive performance with other state-of-the-art character- and subword-level systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed deep hierarchical model is a novel contribution to character-level NMT. The design, which attempts to combine the strengths of character-level input/output with the efficiency of word-level intermediate processing, is well-motivated and presents an interesting research direction.

2.  **Clear Presentation:** Despite the inherent complexity of the six-RNN model, the paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors do a good job of explaining the architecture and training procedure, making the work largely understandable and reproducible.

3.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** The qualitative examples in Table 2 provide compelling evidence that the model effectively leverages its character-level nature. Its ability to correctly translate misspelled words (e.g., "unconclusive") and morphological variations unseen during training (e.g., "convenienter") is a significant and practical advantage over many word- and subword-level models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Comparison to Baselines:** The paper's primary quantitative results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed DCNMT model over strong existing baselines.
    *   The reported BLEU score improvements over the `bpe2char` model are marginal (0.1 BLEU on En-Cs, 0.04 BLEU on Cs-En). Without statistical significance tests, it is impossible to conclude that DCNMT actually outperforms this baseline.
    *   The comparison of training efficiency is also inconclusive and, in some cases, unfavorable. For Cs-En, the `bpe2char` model is noted as being 8 days faster to train. For En-Cs, the training time for the baseline is missing, and for En-Fr, the `bpe2char` baseline is not evaluated at all. A more thorough and rigorous comparison is needed to justify the considerable added complexity of the proposed architecture.

2.  **Claims on Learning Morphology are Unsubstantiated:** Section 5.2 claims to investigate the model's ability to learn morphology, but the analysis is purely qualitative and anecdotal. While the visualizations in Figure 3 and Figure 4 are interesting, they are based on a few hand-picked examples and do not constitute sufficient scientific evidence to support the strong claims made. The paper lacks any formal experiments or quantitative evaluation to validate that the model is learning morphology in a generalizable way. While this is a promising direction, in its current form, this section overstates its conclusions. I would suggest either conducting a more rigorous analysis or significantly toning down the claims and moving the current discussion to an appendix.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the source for the `bpe2char` (Chung et al., 2016b) results for Cs-En in Table 1? They do not appear to be in the original paper. If you ran these experiments yourselves, this should be explicitly stated. Were statistical significance tests performed for the BLEU score comparisons?

2.  The concept of "energy" is central to your word encoder. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of how to interpret this value in Section 5.2? For instance, does a high energy value at a specific character suggest a potential morpheme boundary? Similarly, the "peak" markers in Figure 5 are used in the visualization but not explained in the text.

#### Additional Comments and Suggestions

*   **Acronyms:** Several acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) are used without being defined, while others (RNN, BPE) are defined after their first use. For clarity, please ensure all acronyms are defined upon their first appearance.
*   **Terminology:** Describing French as "morphologically poor" is inaccurate. It would be more precise to state that it is less morphologically rich than a Slavic language like Czech.
*   **Figures and Tables:** Figure 4a appears to have a rendering error, as the colors for energy values are missing for many characters. In Table 1, the training time for the `bpe2char` model on En-Cs is missing, which makes the efficiency comparison incomplete.
*   **Citations:** Several references in the bibliography are incomplete. Please check them carefully. Additionally, for consistency, a link should be provided for the WMT'14 corpus as is done for the WMT'15 corpus, or neither should be linked.
