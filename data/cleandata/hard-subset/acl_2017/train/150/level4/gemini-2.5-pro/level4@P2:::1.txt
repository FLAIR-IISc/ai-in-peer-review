### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a deep, character-to-character neural machine translation (NMT) model. The core idea is to handle the long dependencies and data sparsity inherent in pure character-level models by introducing a hierarchical architecture. The model uses a novel word encoder composed of two RNNs to learn weighted representations of character prefixes, effectively creating representations for morphemes or subwords without explicit segmentation. It then employs a hierarchical decoder that operates at two levels: a word-level decoder generates a sequence of target word representations, and a character-level decoder then generates the character sequence for each word representation.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The primary contribution is the design of the model itself. The combination of the weighted-prefix source word encoder and the two-level hierarchical decoder is an elegant and well-motivated approach to bridging the gap between word-level and character-level NMT. It maintains the benefits of an open vocabulary while imposing a useful structural bias that appears to facilitate learning.

2.  **Effective Unsupervised Morphological Learning:** The paper provides strong qualitative evidence that the model learns meaningful subword units and representations. The analysis showing how the word encoder assigns weights (Figure 4), the reasonable clustering of morphologically-related words (Figure 3), and the model's ability to correctly translate nonce words and misspellings (Table 2) are significant findings that validate the architectural claims.

3.  **An Efficient and High-Performing Character-Level Model:** The proposed model demonstrates impressive training efficiency, achieving strong results after just one training epoch, in some cases outperforming the final scores of a BPE-based baseline. This suggests the architecture is well-suited to the task and learns quickly, which is a valuable practical contribution for such a deep model.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model Design:** The paper addresses the key challenge of pure character-level models (i.e., handling extremely long sequences) with a thoughtful hierarchical structure. Instead of relying on pre-processing steps like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) to define subword units, this model learns them implicitly. The design of the word encoder, which creates a representation by learning a weighted sum of character prefix RNN states, is particularly novel and provides an interpretable mechanism for discovering morphemes. This is a solid architectural contribution to the field.

2.  **Compelling Qualitative and Quantitative Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of not only reporting BLEU scores but also investigating *why* their model works. The analyses in Section 5.2, including the PCA projections of word embeddings and the visualization of the word encoder's attention-like weights, provide convincing evidence that the model is indeed learning morphology. Furthermore, the examples in Table 2, which show the model's ability to handle misspellings and unseen morphological variants ("convenienter"), are a powerful demonstration of the practical benefits of this approach over standard word-level or even BPE-based models.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with or superior to other character-level and subword-level NMT systems on multiple language pairs, including a morphologically rich one (Czech). The fact that the model achieves very respectable performance after only a single epoch of training (Table 1, En-Fr and En-Cs) is a significant strength, indicating that the architecture's inductive biases lead to faster and more efficient learning.

4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and trained models available. This is a valuable contribution to the community, as it allows other researchers to easily verify the results, build upon this work, and apply the model to new tasks and languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Contextualization of Results:** The paper's primary weakness is its failure to compare against the broader state-of-the-art (SOTA) in machine translation for the given language pairs. The comparisons in Table 1 are restricted to other character-level or subword-based neural models. However, at the time of submission, top-performing systems on WMT tasks often included phrase-based systems or different NMT architectures that achieved significantly higher BLEU scores. Without this context, it is difficult to assess the model's performance against the overall SOTA. The authors should, at a minimum, acknowledge the gap to the top WMT results and discuss the trade-offs (e.g., trading some performance for the benefits of a pure character-level approach like OOV handling).

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Architectural Diagram:** The paper describes a complex architecture with six recurrent networks organized into four layers. However, Figure 2, the main architectural diagram, is a high-level schematic that does not clearly illustrate all six of these components and their interactions. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully grasp the model's data flow. A more detailed diagram, perhaps in an appendix, would greatly enhance the paper's clarity.

3.  **Minor Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The paper suffers from a number of minor grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and formatting mistakes (e.g., incorrect LaTeX quotation marks). While these do not prevent understanding, a thorough proofreading would improve the overall quality of the manuscript. Additionally, while the specific dev/test sets are stated in Section 4.1, this crucial information should also be included in the caption of Table 1 for immediate clarity and to prevent ambiguity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your performance comparison in Table 1 is limited to other character- and subword-level models. Could you please comment on how your results compare to the overall state-of-the-art on the WMT14 En-Fr and WMT15 En-Cs tasks from the corresponding evaluation campaigns?

2.  Could you please clarify the mapping between the "six recurrent networks" described in Section 3.3 and the components shown in Figure 2? For instance, where are the two RNNs of the source word encoder and the target word encoder represented in the diagram?

3.  In the word encoder (Section 3.1), you mention that instead of normalizing the weights `w_t` (e.g., with a softmax), you apply a `tanh` function to the final weighted sum. This is an unconventional choice. Could you provide more intuition on why this might be beneficial and how it affects the representations being learned?
