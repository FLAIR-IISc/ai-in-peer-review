**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper presents a deep neural machine translation (NMT) model that operates entirely at the character level. The proposed architecture is hierarchical, encoding source sentences by first composing character inputs into morpheme-like representations, then words, and finally sentences. Decoding is also hierarchical, with a word-level decoder guiding a character-level generator. The primary goal is to eliminate the need for a fixed vocabulary, thereby solving the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, while maintaining training efficiency.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel word encoder architecture composed of two recurrent networks that learns to weigh character sequences to form meaningful sub-word and word representations, effectively learning aspects of morphology.
2.  An efficient hierarchical decoder that uses a specialized Gated Recurrent Unit (HGRU) to generate character sequences conditioned on higher-level word representations, enabling effective end-to-end training of a deep, multi-level model.
3.  Empirical results on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks demonstrating that the proposed model is competitive with other character- and subword-level systems, and notably, that it can outperform a BPE-based baseline after only a single epoch of training.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Effective Architecture for Learning Morphology:** The core strength of this paper is its well-motivated architecture for character-level translation. The proposed word encoder is an elegant solution for learning distributed representations from characters without explicit segmentation. The analysis in Section 5.2, particularly Figures 3 and 4, provides compelling evidence that the model learns meaningful representations that capture morphological similarities and differences. This learned morphological awareness directly leads to the model's impressive ability to handle misspelled words and unseen morphological variants (nonce words), as demonstrated in Table 2.

2.  **Remarkable Training Efficiency:** The model demonstrates impressive learning speed. As shown in Table 1, the proposed DCNMT achieves a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after just one training epoch on the En-Fr and En-Cs tasks. For a deep, six-RNN model operating on long character sequences, this efficiency is a significant practical advantage and suggests that the hierarchical structure provides a powerful inductive bias that accelerates learning.

3.  **Complete Handling of the OOV Problem:** By operating purely on characters, the model fundamentally solves the OOV problem that plagues word-level and even subword-level models. This allows it to gracefully handle rare words, misspellings, and neologisms without resorting to <unk> tokens or simple copying mechanisms. The qualitative examples in Table 2 effectively showcase this key advantage over competing approaches.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and final trained models available. This is a commendable practice that greatly facilitates the verification of their results and allows other researchers to more easily build upon and compare against this work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Overall State-of-the-Art:** While the paper provides a solid comparison against other character-level and subword-level NMT systems, it fails to benchmark against the broader state-of-the-art for the WMT translation tasks. The abstract's claim that the model is "comparable to the state-of-the-art systems" is not fully substantiated, as results from top-performing systems (which may use different architectures like the Transformer or be phrase-based) on these standard test sets are significantly higher. Including these numbers would provide crucial context for the paper's contributions and a more realistic assessment of the model's performance.

2.  **Clarity of the Model Diagram:** There is a discrepancy between the textual description of the model as a "deep model consisting of six recurrent networks" and the visualization in Figure 2. The figure shows four main layers, and it is not immediately obvious how these map to six distinct RNNs (e.g., that the bidirectional encoders each consist of two RNNs). A more detailed diagram or a clearer caption explicitly mapping the six RNNs to the components in the figure would significantly improve the reader's understanding of the full architecture.

3.  **Minor Presentation and Supplementary Material Issues:** The paper has several minor issues that detract from its polish. The manuscript contains a handful of grammatical errors and awkward phrasings. Additionally, while the provision of code is a strength, the documentation could be improved; the README file would benefit from explicit command-line examples for training and testing, and the software itself should include standard functionality like a `--help` flag.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The empirical evaluation focuses on comparisons with other character-level or subword-level models. Could you please position your results relative to the overall state-of-the-art on the newstest2015 benchmark for the language pairs you tested? Even if your model does not outperform them, acknowledging the gap would strengthen the paper.

2.  Regarding inference speed, character-level decoding can be significantly slower than word- or subword-level decoding due to the much longer output sequences. Could you provide any analysis of the decoding speed (e.g., words per second) of your model compared to the BPE baseline?

3.  Could you clarify the mapping of the "six recurrent neural networks" to the architecture shown in Figure 2? For example, is the source word encoder a single BiRNN (2 RNNs), the sentence encoder a single BiRNN (2 RNNs), and the decoders each a single RNN, for a total of six? Explicitly stating this would resolve the ambiguity.
