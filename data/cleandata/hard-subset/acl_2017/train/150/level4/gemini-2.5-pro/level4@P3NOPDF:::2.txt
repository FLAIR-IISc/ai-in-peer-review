### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel deep architecture for character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT), named DCNMT. The proposed model extends the standard encoder-decoder framework by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer on the source side and a corresponding deep word-decoding layer on the target side. These layers are designed to transform character-level sequences into intermediate sub-word representations. The primary motivation is to combine the benefits of character-level NMT—such as a reduced vocabulary size and the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words—with the efficiency of sub-word models, which operate on shorter sequences. The authors also claim that this architecture is capable of learning morphological features more effectively than other state-of-the-art methods.

### **Strengths**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the complex architecture relatively easy to follow. The central idea of using deep encoding and decoding layers to bridge character-level input/output with an internal sub-word representation is original, and the preliminary results presented are interesting and promising.

### **Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

My main reservations about this paper concern the interpretation of the results and the strength of the evidence supporting some of its central claims.

1.  **Evaluation and Comparison with Baselines:** The empirical comparison to existing state-of-the-art systems, particularly `bpe2char`, is not entirely convincing. The reported BLEU score differences are marginal (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs). Have the authors performed a statistical significance test to validate these small gains? Without it, it is difficult to conclude that DCNMT offers a clear performance advantage over a strong baseline like `bpe2char`.

    Furthermore, the training time comparison raises questions. DCNMT takes significantly longer to train on Cs-En (8 days more than `bpe2char`), while the training time for `bpe2char` is not provided for En-Cs, and the model is not evaluated at all on En-Fr. To substantiate the claims of the proposed model's superiority, a more complete and rigorous comparison is needed, including significance testing and a full account of computational costs across all evaluated language pairs.

2.  **Unsupported Claims on Learning Morphology:** My second major concern relates to the claims in Section 5.2 regarding the model's ability to learn morphology. While the presented examples are insightful and well-explained, they are anecdotal. The paper lacks a formal, experimental evaluation to support these strong claims. This line of inquiry is very promising and could potentially form the basis of a separate publication. For the current work, I suggest two possible revisions: either provide a more rigorous, quantitative analysis of the model's morphological learning capabilities, or, if that is beyond the scope of this paper, move the current qualitative analysis to an appendix and soften the claims in the main body of the text.

### **Minor Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Acronyms:** Several common acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) are used without definition, while others (RNN, BPE) are defined after their first use. For clarity and accessibility, all acronyms should be defined upon their first appearance.

*   **Clarity on "Energy" Concept:** The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but could benefit from a clearer explanation in Section 5.2 where it is heavily used. It would be helpful to refresh the concept and provide guidance on its interpretation. For instance, does a high energy value on a character signal a likely morpheme boundary? Additionally, the term "peak" used in Figure 5 should be explicitly defined.

*   **Figures and Tables:**
    *   Figure 4a appears to be rendered incorrectly, as the colors representing energy values are not shown for every character.
    *   In Table 1, the results for the Chung et al. (2016) baseline on Cs-En are not from the original paper. The authors should clarify if they re-implemented this model themselves.

*   **Wording and Data:**
    *   The description of French as "morphologically poor" could be nuanced. It is perhaps more accurate to describe it as less morphologically rich than a Slavic language like Czech.
    *   For reproducibility, a link to the WMT'14 training corpora should be provided, similar to the one for WMT'15.

*   **Consistency and Formatting:**
    *   There are minor inconsistencies, such as the capitalization of BPE (initially uppercase, then lowercase).
    *   Several references in the bibliography appear to be incomplete and should be checked.

*   **Typos and Grammar:** The paper would benefit from a careful proofread to correct minor grammatical errors. For example:

    *   "Luong and Manning (2016) use..." (not "uses")
    *   "HGRU (it is..." (not "It is")
