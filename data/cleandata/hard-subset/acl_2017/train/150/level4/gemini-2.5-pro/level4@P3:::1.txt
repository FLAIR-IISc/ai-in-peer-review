### **Summary**

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model. The core problem it addresses is the trade-off between word-level models, which suffer from large vocabularies and out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issues, and conventional character-level models, which can be inefficient to train due to long sequence lengths. The proposed architecture consists of six recurrent neural networks (RNNs). On the encoder side, a novel word encoder uses two RNNs to compose a word representation from its characters, aiming to learn morphological structure automatically. On the decoder side, a hierarchical structure is employed where a word-level RNN guides a character-level RNN (an HGRU variant) to generate the translation character by character. The authors claim this deep architecture is efficient to train, eliminates the need for a large vocabulary, and achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks. A key finding is that the model appears to successfully learn and leverage morphology, enabling it to handle OOV words, misspellings, and even nonce words effectively.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Deep Hierarchical Character-Level Architecture:** The primary contribution is the specific six-RNN architecture that operates hierarchically. The combination of a weighted character-RNN for source word representation and a multi-level decoder (word-level state guiding a character-level generator) is a novel approach designed to balance the benefits of character-level modeling (open vocabulary) with the efficiency of word-level processing.

2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Morphological Learning:** The paper provides compelling qualitative and quantitative evidence that its model learns meaningful subword representations. The analyses, including PCA visualizations of word embeddings (Figure 3), morpheme weight visualizations (Figure 4), and the impressive ability to correctly translate misspelled and nonce words (Table 2), serve as a strong demonstration of the model's practical benefits beyond standard BLEU score improvements.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The proposed DCNMT architecture is thoughtfully designed. It directly addresses the known shortcomings of both standard word-level and character-level NMT. By encoding words from characters and then encoding sentences from these word representations, it keeps the sentence encoder's sequence length manageable, a key challenge for flat character-level models. The hierarchical decoder is a clever solution for efficient character-level generation. This design makes a solid contribution to the literature on character-aware NMT.

2.  **Strong Evidence for Learning and Leveraging Morphology:** A major strength of this work is the detailed analysis showing the model is not just a black box. The visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 suggest that the word encoder is learning linguistically plausible representations. More convincingly, the examples in Table 2, where the model correctly translates the misspelled "unconclusive" and the nonce word "convenienter," demonstrate a powerful generalization capability that word-based or BPE-based models would likely fail on. This capability is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Training Efficiency and Competitive Performance:** The authors report impressive training efficiency. As shown in Table 1, the model achieves strong performance, even surpassing a BPE-based baseline, after only a single training epoch. This supports the claim that learning morphology can speed up convergence. The final BLEU scores are shown to be competitive with other recently published character-level and subword-level NMT systems on the chosen language pairs, validating the effectiveness of the approach.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and trained models available. This is a commendable practice that allows for verification of their results and facilitates future research building upon this work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Experimental Details for Reproducibility:** A critical piece of information is missing from the main paper: the specific development and test sets used for evaluation. While the paper mentions using WMT'14 and WMT'15 data, it does not state, for example, that `newstest2013` was used for development and `newstest2015` for testing. This information is essential for readers to compare the reported results with other work (e.g., on `matrix.statmt.org`). Although I located this information in the supplementary code's README file, it is standard and necessary practice to include it explicitly in the experiments section of the paper.

2.  **Limited Comparison to the Overall State-of-the-Art:** The empirical comparison in Table 1 is focused exclusively on other character-based and subword-based neural models. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging and comparing its results to the broader state-of-the-art on these tasks at the time of submission, which may have included phrase-based systems or other NMT architectures. This context is important for accurately positioning the paper's performance contributions within the field of machine translation as a whole, not just within the sub-field of character-level NMT.

3.  **Clarity of Model Architecture Description:** There is a slight disconnect between the textual description of the model and the diagram in Figure 2. The text (Section 3.3) describes a total of six RNNs organized into four layers (source character → source word → source sentence → target word → target character). However, it is not immediately obvious how all six RNNs map onto the components in Figure 2. A more explicit caption or a more detailed diagram (perhaps in an appendix) would improve the clarity and help readers fully grasp the data flow through this complex architecture.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper contains a number of minor grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and LaTeX formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent use of quotation marks). These do not detract from the core scientific contribution but should be corrected in a final version to improve readability.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the mapping between the six RNNs/four layers described in Section 3.3 and the components depicted in Figure 2? A clearer connection between the text and the diagram would be very helpful.

2.  Could you comment on how your results for the En-Fr and En-Cs `newstest2015` tasks compare to the overall top-performing systems from the WMT 2015 evaluation, including non-character-based systems? This would provide valuable context for your results.
