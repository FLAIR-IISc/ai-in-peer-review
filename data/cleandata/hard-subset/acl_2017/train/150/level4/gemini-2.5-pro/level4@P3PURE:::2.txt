### Strengths

The paper is well-structured and generally clear. The core ideas are original, the explanation is easy to follow, and the results are interesting.

### Weaknesses

The interpretation of the experimental results raises some questions. Furthermore, the claims regarding the model's ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by rigorous evidence.

### General Discussion

This paper proposes a deep architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The model builds upon a standard encoder-decoder architecture by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer, which transforms character-level input into sub-word representations. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is used on the output side to convert target-language sub-word representations into the final character sequence. This architecture aims to combine the benefits of character-level NMT (e.g., a smaller vocabulary, robustness to unseen words) with the efficiency of sub-word models by processing shorter intermediate sequences. The authors also claim that their deep word-encoding model captures morphology more effectively than other state-of-the-art approaches.

While the proposed architecture is promising, I have two main concerns regarding the evaluation and the claims made.

First, the experimental comparison between the proposed model (DCNMT) and other state-of-the-art systems warrants further discussion. Specifically, the advantage of DCNMT over the `bpe2char` baseline is not clearly established. The reported BLEU score differences are marginal (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs). Without a statistical significance test, it is difficult to conclude that DCNMT truly outperforms `bpe2char`. Regarding training time, `bpe2char` is significantly faster for Cs-En (by 8 days). The training time for `bpe2char` is not provided for the En-Cs task, and this baseline is omitted entirely for En-Fr. A more complete comparison with the `bpe2char` system is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed model.

Second, Section 5.2 claims to investigate the system's ability to learn morphology. While the provided examples are well-chosen and illustrative, they constitute anecdotal evidence rather than a formal evaluation. The claims about morphological learning are therefore not substantiated by systematic experiments. I would encourage the authors to expand this interesting analysis with a more rigorous evaluation, which could potentially form the basis of a separate publication. For the current work, I suggest either softening the claims about learning morphology or moving this qualitative analysis to an appendix to ensure the paper's core contributions remain the focus.

### Other Comments and Suggestions

*   Several acronyms are used without prior definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined after their first use (e.g., RNN, BPE). While many are well-known, defining all acronyms on their first use would improve clarity.
*   The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but used extensively in the analysis in Section 5.2. It would be helpful to briefly revisit the concept in Section 5.2 and provide guidance on its interpretation. For instance, does a high energy on a character indicate a likely morpheme boundary? Additionally, the term "peak" used in the context of Figure 5 should be explicitly defined.
*   The acronym BPE is capitalized at its definition but subsequently written in lowercase (`bpe`). This should be made consistent.
*   The statement in Section 4.1 that no monolingual corpus was used seems unnecessary.
*   In Figure 4a, the energy value colors appear to be missing for some characters.
*   In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) on the Cs-En task are not reported in the cited paper. If the authors reproduced these results themselves, this should be explicitly stated.
*   The description of French as "morphologically poor" is an oversimplification. A more precise description would be that it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
*   For consistency, a link should be provided for the WMT'14 training corpora, as is done for the WMT'15 corpora.
*   Several references in the bibliography are incomplete.
