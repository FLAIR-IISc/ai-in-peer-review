- Strengths: Generally, the paper is well-structured and clear. Most explanations are possible to follow, the ideas presented are original, and the results obtained are quite interesting.
- Weaknesses: I have some doubts about the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, I feel that some claims about the ability of the proposed method to learn morphology are not well-supported by scientific evidence.
- General Discussion:
This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture, adding a new deep word-encoding layer that is capable of encoding character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to convert the target-language sub-word representation into character sequences as the final output of the NMT system. The purpose of such an architecture is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT (reduced vocabulary size and flexibility to deal with unseen words) and, at the same time, improve the performance of the entire system by using an intermediate representation of sub-words to reduce the size of the input sequence of characters. In addition, the authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is capable of learning morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches.
I have some concerns about the evaluation. The authors compare their approach with other state-of-the-art systems, considering two parameters: training time and BLEU score. However, I do not see any clear advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over other approaches such as bpe2char. The difference between the two approaches with regard to the BLEU score is very small (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs) and it is difficult to say that one is performing better than the other without knowing any statistically significant information: has statistical significance been evaluated? Regarding training time, it is worth mentioning that bpe2char takes 8 days less than DCNMT for Cs-En. Training time for En-Cs is not provided (why not?) and bpe2char for En-Fr is not evaluated. I think a more complete comparison should be made with this system to prove the benefits of the proposed model.
My second concern is on section 5.2, where the authors begin to claim that they have investigated the morphological learning ability of their system. However, the section only contains examples and some comments on them. Even though these examples are very well-chosen and explained in a very didactic way, it is noteworthy that no experiments or formal evaluations have been conducted to support the authors' claims. I would certainly encourage the authors to expand on this very interesting part of the paper, which could even become a separate paper in itself. On the other hand, this section does not seem to be an important point of the paper, so for the current work I can only suggest moving this section to an appendix and softening some of the claims made about the system's capabilities for morphological learning.
Other Comments, Doubts, and Suggestions:
 - There are many abbreviations used that are not defined (such as LSTM, HGRU, CNN, or PCA) or that were defined only after they began to be used (such as RNN or BPE). Even though some of these abbreviations are well-known in the field of deep learning, I would encourage the authors to define them to improve clarity.
 - The concept of energy is first mentioned in section 3.1. Even though the explanation provided at that point is sufficient, it would be good to refresh the idea of energy in section 5.2 (where it is used several times) and provide some hints about understanding it: high energy on a character will indicate that the current morpheme should be divided at that point? In addition, the concept of extreme (in figure 5) is not described.
 - When the abbreviation BPE is defined, capital letters are used, but then, for the rest of the mentions, it is in small letters; is there a reason for this?
 - I am not sure it is necessary to say that a monolingual corpus is not used in section 4.1.
 - It seems that something is wrong in figure 4a because the colors for the energy values are not shown for each character.
 - In table 1, the results for Cs-En for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) were not taken from the paper, as they are not reported. If the authors calculated these results themselves (as it seems), they should mention it.
 - I would not say that French is morphologically poor, but rather that it is not as rich as Slavic languages like Czech.
 - Why is the link given for the WMT'15 training corpus but not for WMT'14?
 - Many references are incomplete
