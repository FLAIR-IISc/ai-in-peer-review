## Review for ACL 2017

This paper presents a novel approach to neural machine translation (NMT) based on an encoder-decoder architecture. The system uniquely combines character-level processing at the input and output with morpheme-level representations in the intermediate layers, offering a promising strategy for handling morphological complexity and rare/unseen words. The authors also demonstrate a clear benefit from continued training, with improvements observed across multiple epochs.

**Strengths:**

The core contribution of this work lies in its innovative hybrid character-morpheme NMT model. By leveraging both character and morpheme information, the system appears capable of learning reasonable morphological tokenizations and, importantly, demonstrating a degree of robustness to out-of-vocabulary words, even nonce words, through implicit fallback to morphemic representations. This is a significant advantage over traditional word-based NMT systems. The authors deserve credit for publicly releasing both the code and pre-trained models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs translation tasks. This facilitates reproducibility, validation, and further research by the community. The reported results suggest that the system achieves reasonable translation quality even after the initial training epoch, with continued improvement as training progresses.

**Weaknesses:**

While the paper presents a valuable contribution, several aspects require attention. A crucial omission is the explicit specification of the WMT test and development sets used for evaluation. Currently, this information is only available in the code’s README file (newstest2015 for test and newstest2013 for development). This lack of clarity hinders direct comparison with established results reported on resources like matrix.statmt.org and makes it difficult for readers to contextualize the performance of the proposed system. 

The instructions provided in the software README, while functional, could be significantly improved. Providing concrete examples of training and testing commands within the README would greatly enhance usability. Furthermore, the software lacks a `--help` flag, which is standard practice and would assist users in understanding available options and parameters.

The paper describes a 6-level architecture, however, Figure 2 presents a diagram with fewer than 6 layers. This discrepancy is confusing and requires clarification. The figure caption should be more explicit in outlining the architecture represented, or, if it does not depict all layers, a more comprehensive diagram – perhaps in the appendix – should be included. 

The results section currently focuses on comparison to other character-based NMT systems. However, it omits a crucial benchmark: comparison to state-of-the-art results achieved by non-character-based systems on the same datasets. WMT data reports significantly higher scores for many systems, and this discrepancy should be acknowledged and discussed.  Positioning the work relative to the broader state of the art is essential for a comprehensive evaluation.

Finally, the paper contains a number of minor errors, including grammatical disfluencies, spelling mistakes, and LaTeX issues such as incorrect quotation marks. A careful proofread would address these issues and enhance the overall polish of the submission.

**General Discussion:**

This paper offers a compelling contribution to the field of character-based neural machine translation. The proposed architecture, which integrates character and morpheme information, presents a promising approach for improving translation quality, especially in morphologically rich languages and for handling rare words. While the paper has some shortcomings regarding clarity, completeness of results, and presentation, the core ideas are strong and the public release of code and models is commendable. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the research community.
