This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture by adding a new deep word-encoding layer capable of encoding the character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. In the same way, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to transform the target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence as the final output of the NMT system. The objective of such architecture is to take advantage of the benefits of character-level NMT (reduction of the size of the vocabulary and flexibility to deal with unseen words) and, at the same time, improving the performance of the whole system by using an intermediate representation of sub-words to reduce the size of the input sequence of characters. In addition, the authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is able to learn morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is a novel deep character-level NMT architecture incorporating a word encoder utilizing recurrent networks to learn morphological structure, and a hierarchical decoder. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of competitive performance on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, achieving BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems. Finally, the analysis suggesting the model's ability to learn morphological information, while preliminary, is a noteworthy aspect.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture with the morphology-aware word encoder is a genuinely interesting approach to address the challenges of vocabulary size and OOV words in NMT. The combination of recurrent networks for learning morphological representations is a creative idea.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The achieved BLEU scores on the En-Fr and En-Cs tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model. Achieving comparable results to established state-of-the-art systems is a strong indicator of the method's potential.
3. **Efficient Training:** Despite the model's depth (six recurrent networks), the authors claim efficient training, which is an important consideration for practical application. This is somewhat supported by the training time comparison in Table 1.
4. **Addressing OOV Problem:** By operating at the character level, the model inherently addresses the out-of-vocabulary problem, a significant advantage over traditional word-based NMT systems.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Statistical Significance:** The performance comparison lacks statistical significance testing. The BLEU score improvements over baselines like bpe2char are small (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and without statistical tests, it’s difficult to conclude whether the gains are truly meaningful.
2. **Incomplete Comparison:** The comparison to the bpe2char baseline is incomplete. Training time for En-Cs is missing, and En-Fr is not evaluated for bpe2char, hindering a fair assessment of the proposed model's efficiency.
3. **Insufficient Evidence for Morphology Learning:** The evidence supporting the claim that the model learns morphology is largely qualitative and based on illustrative examples. A more rigorous, quantitative evaluation of morphological understanding is needed. Moving the current Section 5.2 to an appendix and softening the claims around morphological learning would be advisable.
4. **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper contains numerous minor issues affecting clarity, including undefined acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA), inconsistent capitalization (BPE), and typographical errors (detailed in the original review). The concept of “energy” could be better explained, particularly in Section 5.2.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. Have statistical significance tests been performed to compare the performance of DCNMT with the baseline models? If so, please provide the results.
2. Can you provide the training time for the bpe2char baseline on the En-Cs dataset?
3. What specific metrics or experiments were used to evaluate the model's ability to learn morphology beyond the examples shown in Section 5.2?
4. Can you clarify the meaning of "energy" in the context of the word encoder and its relation to morpheme boundaries?

**Additional Comments:**
- Please ensure consistent capitalization of acronyms throughout the paper.
- Correct all typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies.
- Briefly define all acronyms upon first use.
- Consider rephrasing the statement that French is "morphologically poor" to reflect its morphological richness relative to languages like English, rather than implying it lacks morphology altogether.
- Provide links to both WMT’14 and WMT’15 datasets.
- Please verify the completeness of the references.
