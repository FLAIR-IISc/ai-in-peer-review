This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture by adding a new deep word-encoding layer capable of encoding the character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. In the same way, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to transform the target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence as the final output of the NMT system. The objective of such architecture is to take advantage of the benefits of character-level NMT (reduction of the size of the vocabulary and flexibility to deal with unseen words) and, at the same time, improving the performance of the whole system by using an intermediate representation of sub-words to reduce the size of the input sequence of characters. In addition, the authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is able to learn morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches.

**Summary of Contributions:** The paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) utilizing a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder and decoder. The primary contribution is the word encoder, which leverages recurrent neural networks to learn morphological representations of words, and the hierarchical decoder that enables efficient character-level decoding. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating competitive performance on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art systems, while avoiding a large vocabulary. A further, though less substantial, contribution is the analysis suggesting the model's ability to learn morphological structure.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture, combining recurrent networks for morphological analysis and character-level decoding, is a significant structural innovation. This approach offers a compelling solution to the challenges of large vocabularies in NMT while potentially capturing subword information.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems, particularly considering its character-level operation. This demonstrates the efficacy of the architecture and training methodology. The reported improvements over baseline character-level models are encouraging.
3. **Addressing OOV Issues:** The character-level approach inherently addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, a crucial limitation of word-level NMT systems. The ability to translate misspelled or nonce words, as highlighted in Section 5.3, is a practical benefit of this approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The performance comparison with other models lacks statistical significance testing. The reported BLEU score differences, particularly for Cs-En (0.04) and En-Cs (0.1), may not be substantial enough to confidently claim superiority without statistical validation.
2. **Incomplete Comparison & Training Time Discrepancies:** The comparison to bpe2char is incomplete. Training time for En-Cs is missing, and En-Fr is not evaluated.  The bpe2char model demonstrates faster training times for Cs-En, which is a relevant consideration. A more comprehensive and fair comparison is needed.
3. **Insufficient Evidence for Morphological Learning:** The claims regarding the model’s ability to learn morphology (Section 5.2) are primarily based on illustrative examples and qualitative analysis.  A more rigorous, quantitative evaluation is required to substantiate these claims. The PCA projection and energy visualizations are suggestive, but lack formal metrics or comparisons to baselines specifically designed to assess morphological understanding.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have statistical significance tests been performed to compare the BLEU scores of DCNMT with the baseline models? If so, please report the p-values.
2.  Can you provide the training time for the bpe2char model on the En-Cs dataset?
3.  What quantitative metrics were used, or could be used, to evaluate the model's ability to learn morphological representations? Could you provide any results from such evaluation?
4.  In Figure 4a, the color bar seems to be incomplete, please provide a complete and correct color bar.
