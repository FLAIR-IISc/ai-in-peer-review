- Introduces a hierarchical deep character-level NMT model using six RNNs to address large vocabulary and OOV issues.  
- Core idea: two RNNs in the encoder explicitly model morphology and decoding is at the character level.  
- Contribution 1: novel hierarchical architecture that learns morphological decomposition without predefined subword units.  
- Contribution 2: demonstrates competitive BLEU scores on En→Fr and En→Cs with a relatively small model and fast training.  
- Contribution 3: releases code and trained models to support reproducibility.  
- Strength: the two-RNN word encoder is a conceptually interesting way to integrate morphology into NMT.  
- Strength: achieves strong translation performance despite small model size and limited training epochs.  
- Strength: improves handling of OOV and misspelled words by backing off to morpheme-like representations.  
- Strength: public release of code/models promotes transparency and future research.  
- Weakness: paper omits clear specification of which WMT test/dev sets were used, complicating comparisons.  
- Weakness: software documentation is minimal; lacks examples and a helpful CLI (--help) is missing.  
- Weakness: architectural diagram (Fig. 2) is unclear/incomplete, showing fewer than the claimed six RNN layers.  
- Weakness: comparisons are mainly to other character-based systems; lacks broader SOTA or hybrid system comparisons.  
- Weakness: minor language and LaTeX presentation errors that need proofreading.  
- Q1 to authors: why use tanh for normalization in the word encoder instead of softmax?  
- Q2 to authors: how sensitive is performance to the character vocabulary size (set to 120); were alternatives tested?  
- Q3 to authors: what is the computational cost of the hierarchical decoder versus a standard character-level decoder?  
- Overall: solid contribution with promising morphological modeling; clarifying evaluation, comparisons, and documentation would strengthen the paper.
