This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level approaches, particularly the large vocabulary problem and handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture leveraging recurrent neural networks to learn morphology and decode at the character level. The model consists of six recurrent networks, aiming for efficient training and competitive performance.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are: 1) a novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT that explicitly models morphology via two recurrent networks in the encoder, and 2) a demonstration of competitive translation performance, achieving BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, particularly notable given the relatively simple model size. A third, supporting contribution is the release of code and trained models, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Morphological Modeling:** The proposed architecture, specifically the two RNNs in the word encoder, represents a conceptually interesting approach to integrating morphological information into NMT. By learning representations of preceding characters and weighting their importance, the model effectively decomposes words into morphemes without relying on pre-defined subword units like byte-pair encoding. This is a significant design choice and a key strength of the paper.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The reported BLEU scores, while not necessarily state-of-the-art overall, are strong, especially considering the model’s relatively small size and the fact that competitive results are achieved after only one epoch of training.  The system’s ability to approach the performance of more complex models is a positive indicator of the approach’s effectiveness.
3. **Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** The character-level approach demonstrably improves the model’s ability to translate unseen words and even misspelled words, a common challenge for word-level NMT systems. The ability to implicitly "back off" to morphemes allows for reasonable translation even in the absence of complete word representations.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:**  Releasing the code and trained models is a valuable contribution to the research community, enabling independent verification and facilitating future work building upon this approach. This promotes transparency and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity Regarding Evaluation Datasets:** The paper lacks explicit mention of the specific WMT test and development sets used for evaluation. This omission hinders direct comparison with existing published results. The information is buried in the code README, which is not ideal for readers assessing the paper's contributions.
2. **Insufficient Software Documentation:** While the code is provided, the software documentation (README) is only adequate.  More detailed instructions with concrete examples for training and testing would significantly improve usability.  The absence of a `--help` flag in the software is a minor but noticeable inconvenience.
3. **Ambiguity in Architectural Diagram:** The figure depicting the model architecture (Figure 2) appears incomplete, showing fewer than the claimed six recurrent network layers.  This creates confusion and requires clarification, either by revising the figure to include all layers or providing an additional, more comprehensive diagram.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The results are primarily compared to other character-based systems. A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art word-level or hybrid systems (as reported on resources like matrix.statmt.org) is needed to accurately position the work within the broader NMT landscape. The paper should acknowledge the gap in performance compared to the current best results.
5. **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor English errors (disfluencies, misspellings) and LaTeX inconsistencies (e.g., incorrect quotation marks). A careful proofread would improve readability and professionalism.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind using the tanh activation function for normalization in the word encoder, as opposed to a standard softmax?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the size of the character vocabulary (currently set to 120)? Were different vocabulary sizes explored?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the hierarchical decoder compared to a standard character-level decoder without the hierarchical structure?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of character-level NMT. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly regarding clarity of evaluation details and comparison to state-of-the-art systems, would significantly strengthen the paper. The morphological modeling aspect is promising and the release of code and models is commendable.
