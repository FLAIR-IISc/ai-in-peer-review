This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture by adding a new deep word-encoding layer capable of encoding the character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to transform the target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence as the final output of the NMT system. The objective of this architecture is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT – reduction of vocabulary size and flexibility in handling unseen words – while improving performance by using an intermediate representation of sub-words to reduce the length of the input character sequence. Furthermore, the authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is able to learn morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches.

**Strengths:** In general, the paper is well structured and clear. The explanations are largely understandable, the ideas presented are original, and the results obtained are quite interesting.

**Weaknesses:** I have some doubts about the interpretation of the results. Additionally, I believe that some of the claims regarding the method’s capability to learn morphology are not adequately supported by scientific evidence.

**General Discussion:**

The authors compare their approach (DCNMT) to other state-of-the-art systems using training time and BLEU score as evaluation metrics. However, the advantage of DCNMT over approaches such as bpe2char is not entirely clear. The difference in BLEU score is small (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and it is difficult to determine whether one system outperforms the other without statistical significance information. Has statistical significance been evaluated? Regarding training time, bpe2char takes 8 days less to train for Cs-En. Training time for En-Cs is not provided (why not?), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char is necessary to substantiate the advantages of the proposed model.

My second concern pertains to Section 5.2, where the authors discuss their investigation into the system’s ability to learn morphology. However, the section primarily consists of examples and commentary. While these examples are well-chosen and explained didactically, no experiments or formal evaluation appear to have been conducted to support the authors’ claims. I encourage the authors to expand on this potentially significant aspect of the paper, perhaps even as a separate work. However, this section does not appear to be critical to the core contribution, and I suggest moving it to an appendix and softening the claims made regarding the system’s morphological learning capabilities.

**Other Comments, Doubts and Suggestions:**

*   Many acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined after their initial use (e.g., RNN, BPE). While some of these acronyms are well known in the field of deep learning, defining them would improve clarity.
*   The concept of “energy” is first introduced in Section 3.1. While the initial explanation is sufficient, it would be helpful to revisit the concept in Section 5.2 (where it is used extensively) and provide guidance on its interpretation. For example, does a high energy on a character indicate a suitable point for morpheme splitting? Also, the concept of a “peak” in Figure 5 is not described.
*   The acronym BPE is initially defined using capital letters, but subsequently appears in lowercase. Is there a specific reason for this inconsistency?
*   The statement regarding the lack of monolingual corpus use in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary.
*   Figure 4a appears to have incomplete color mapping for energy values for some characters.
*   In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) for Cs-En are not reported in the original paper. If the authors computed these results themselves, as it seems, they should explicitly state this.
*   I would describe French as not being *as* morphologically rich as Slavic languages like Czech, rather than stating it is morphologically poor.
*   A link is provided for the WMT’15 training corpora, but not for WMT’14.
*   Several references are incomplete.
