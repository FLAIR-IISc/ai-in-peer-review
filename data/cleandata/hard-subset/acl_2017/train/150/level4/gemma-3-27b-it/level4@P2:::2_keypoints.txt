- Proposes a novel six-layer character-level NMT architecture with deep word-encoding and hierarchical decoding to reduce vocabulary issues and sequence length.  
- Claims the model combines character-level advantages (small vocab, handling unseen words) with sub-word representations to improve performance.  
- Reports competitive BLEU scores on En–Fr and En–Cs while keeping model size and training time modest.  
- Presents initial analyses (PCA and energy visualizations) suggesting the model may learn morpheme-like representations.  
- Architectural originality: introduces two RNNs for word encoding and a hierarchical decoder distinct from typical word/subword NMT.  
- Empirical merit: achieves BLEU comparable to baselines (BPE and other character models), showing value despite modest gains.  
- Clear presentation: paper is well-structured with helpful diagrams that clarify the architecture.  
- Practical benefit: character-level approach naturally addresses OOVs and can handle misspellings/nonce words.  
- Lacks statistical significance testing to confirm whether small BLEU differences are meaningful.  
- Incomplete baseline comparisons (e.g., bpe2char missing for En–Fr and missing training-time data for En–Cs).  
- Insufficient quantitative evidence for claimed morphological learning; analyses are primarily qualitative.  
- Uses undefined acronyms and under-explains terms like “energy” and “peak,” hindering accessibility.  
- Asks authors to report significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) for BLEU differences.  
- Requests a more comprehensive comparison including training times, computational cost, and all baselines.  
- Suggests quantitative experiments for morphology (e.g., morphological segmentation tasks) to substantiate claims.  
- Recommends defining acronyms at first use, clarifying analysis terms, moving Section 5.2 to an appendix or tempering morphology claims, fixing typos, completing references, rephrasing “morphologically poor,” and providing a WMT’14 corpus link.
