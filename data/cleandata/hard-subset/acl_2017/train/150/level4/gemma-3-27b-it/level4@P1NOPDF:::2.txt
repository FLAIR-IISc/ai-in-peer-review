- Strengths: Overall, the paper is well-structured and clear. The explanations are generally easy to follow, the ideas presented are original, and the results are interesting.
- Weaknesses: I have some reservations regarding the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the claims concerning the method’s ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by scientific evidence.
- General Discussion:
This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a standard encoder-decoder framework by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer, which encodes the character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to transform the target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence, forming the final output of the NMT system. The goal of this architecture is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT—namely, reduced vocabulary size and the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words—while simultaneously improving performance by using an intermediate sub-word representation to shorten the input character sequence. In addition, the authors assert that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than existing state-of-the-art approaches.

I have some concerns regarding the evaluation. The authors compare their approach (DCNMT) to other systems based on training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of DCNMT over approaches like bpe2char is not clearly demonstrated. The difference in BLEU score is small (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), and determining statistical significance is difficult without that information. Has statistical significance been evaluated? Regarding training time, bpe2char takes 8 days less to train for Cs-En, which is considerable. Training time for En-Cs is not provided (and should be), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the claimed advantages of the proposed model.

My second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to have investigated their system’s ability to learn morphology. However, the section primarily presents examples and commentary. While these examples are well-chosen and clearly explained, no experiments or formal evaluations appear to have been conducted to support the authors’ claims. I encourage the authors to expand this potentially valuable aspect of the work, perhaps even as a separate paper. Nevertheless, this section doesn’t appear critical to the core contribution, so for the present work, I suggest moving it to an appendix and moderating the claims regarding morphological learning. 

Other comments, doubts and suggestions:
 - Many acronyms are used without initial definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined after their first use (e.g., RNN, BPE). While many of these acronyms are common in deep learning, defining them would improve clarity.
 - The concept of “energy” is first mentioned in Section 3.1, with a sufficient explanation at that point. However, it would be helpful to revisit the idea in Section 5.2 (where it's used extensively) and provide guidance on its interpretation: does a high energy on a character indicate a likely split point within a morpheme? Furthermore, the meaning of “peak” in Figure 5 is not described.
 - The acronym BPE is initially capitalized, but is subsequently written in lowercase; is there a reason for this inconsistency?
 - It’s unclear whether mentioning the lack of a monolingual corpus in Section 4.1 is necessary.
 - Figure 4a appears to have missing colour mapping for some characters' energy values.
 - In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) for Cs-En are not reported in the original paper. If the authors computed these results themselves (as it seems), they should state this explicitly.
 - I would describe French as not being *as* morphologically rich as Slavic languages like Czech, rather than “morphologically poor.”
 - A link is provided for the WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14; why?
 - Several references are incomplete.
