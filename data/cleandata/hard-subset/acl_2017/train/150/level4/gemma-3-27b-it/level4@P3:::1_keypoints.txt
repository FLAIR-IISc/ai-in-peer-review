- Proposes a novel character-level NMT architecture that learns morphology via recurrent networks to represent words and a hierarchical character-level decoder.  
- Main contributions: explicit morphological modeling, automatic subword learning to avoid large vocabularies, and an efficient hierarchical decoder.  
- Empirical results: competitive BLEU on En–Fr and En–Cs and analyses showing the model learns morphological structure.  

- Strength: novel architecture bridging word- and character-level NMT, addressing rare/unseen words with interpretable morphological representations.  
- Strength: competitive performance achieved quickly (not state-of-the-art but encouraging after one epoch).  
- Strength: authors release code and trained models, aiding reproducibility.  
- Strength: qualitative analyses (PCA, nonce words) indicate meaningful morpheme representations.  

- Weakness: experimental details unclear—WMT dataset specifics should be stated in the main text.  
- Weakness: code documentation could be improved (more examples, a --help flag).  
- Weakness: Figure 2/architectural diagram lacks clarity about the claimed “6-level” architecture.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to state-of-the-art word/hybrid models on WMT benchmarks.  
- Weakness: minor grammatical/LaTeX issues that need proofreading.  

- Question: provide detailed computational cost comparison versus other character- and subword-level NMTs, especially impact of six recurrent networks on train/inference time.  
- Question: have deeper recurrent architectures been tried, and what were the outcomes?  
- Question: why was tanh chosen for normalizing weights in the word encoder and were alternatives considered?  

- Additional comment: add a clearer discussion of the method’s limitations.  
- Additional comment: include an ablation study to quantify each component’s contribution.  
- Additional comment: discuss generalization prospects to languages with different morphological typologies.
