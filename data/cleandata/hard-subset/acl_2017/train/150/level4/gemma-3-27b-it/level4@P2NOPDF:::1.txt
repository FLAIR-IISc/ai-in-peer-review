This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder neural machine translation (NMT) approach that leverages characters, morphemes, and characters again—a compelling strategy for handling morphology and rare words. The authors’ decision to release both code and trained models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs translation is a significant strength, facilitating both validation of their work and further research by the community. Importantly, the system demonstrates promising performance, achieving reasonable translation quality even after initial training epochs, with continued improvement observed throughout training. The model’s ability to learn plausible morphological tokenizations and effectively handle out-of-vocabulary words, potentially through implicit decomposition into morphemes, is particularly noteworthy.

However, the paper suffers from several shortcomings. Primarily, the specific WMT test and development sets used for evaluation are not clearly stated within the text. This omission hinders direct comparison of the reported results with existing literature, readily available on resources like matrix.statmt.org. The reviewer had to consult the code supplement’s README file to determine the evaluation was performed on newstest2015 and newstest2013, information that should be explicitly stated in the paper itself. 

The documentation accompanying the released code, while functional, could be improved. Providing concrete examples demonstrating how to execute the training and testing commands would greatly enhance usability. Furthermore, the software lacks a `--help` flag, which would provide users with immediate guidance on its functionality. 

A point of confusion arises with Figure 2. The paper describes a 6-level architecture, yet the figure depicts fewer layers. The figure caption requires clarification, and a more comprehensive diagram—perhaps in the appendix—should be included if the provided figure does not illustrate the complete architecture.

The results section focuses on comparison with other character-based NMT systems, but lacks a broader context. The paper does not acknowledge or discuss the state-of-the-art performance achieved by other types of MT systems on the same datasets, as reported by WMT and available on matrix.statmt.org. This comparison is crucial, as current state-of-the-art results significantly exceed those presented in this work. Including this context would provide a more balanced evaluation of the contribution.

Finally, the paper contains a number of minor issues, including grammatical errors, misspellings, and LaTeX inconsistencies (such as incorrect quotation marks). Addressing these would improve the overall polish and readability of the manuscript.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of character-based NMT, and the released resources are a definite asset to the research community. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
