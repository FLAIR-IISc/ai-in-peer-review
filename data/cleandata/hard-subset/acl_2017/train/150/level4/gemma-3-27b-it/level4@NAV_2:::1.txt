This paper presents a novel neural machine translation architecture leveraging character-level processing with an emphasis on learning morphology. The approach utilizes recurrent networks to represent preceding characters and word weights, coupled with a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation, resulting in a six-layer recurrent network model.

The primary contribution is the proposed deep character-level model architecture, effectively addressing the large vocabulary problem in NMT through morphological learning and hierarchical decoding. A second contribution lies in the demonstration of competitive translation performance – achieving BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks, and achieving promising results even after a single training epoch. Finally, the authors provide empirical evidence that the model effectively learns morphological information, enabling it to handle unseen words and perform reasonable translations on nonce words.

Strengths:
The proposed architecture offers an elegant solution to the large vocabulary problem inherent in word-level NMT, offering a compelling alternative to subword-based approaches like BPE. The hierarchical decoder and the learned morphological representations are key innovations.
The authors have generously provided both the code and trained models for En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation, facilitating reproducibility and further research. This is a significant advantage for the community.
The model demonstrates promising initial performance, achieving competitive BLEU scores relatively quickly – even after just one epoch of training, the system shows strong results compared to baseline models.
The model’s ability to learn morphological information is supported by the analyses presented, suggesting the system effectively captures subword units and generalizes to unseen words, including nonce words.

Weaknesses:
The paper lacks clarity regarding the specific WMT test and development sets used for evaluation. While the README file for the code supplement identifies these as newstest2015 and newstest2013, respectively, this information should be explicitly stated in the paper for ease of comparison with existing literature.
The software documentation, while functional, could be improved with more detailed instructions and examples for training and testing. The inclusion of a `--help` flag for the software would enhance usability.
Figure 2, illustrating the model architecture, appears incomplete. The paper describes a six-layer architecture, but the figure does not clearly represent all six layers. A more comprehensive diagram, potentially in an appendix, is needed to clarify the model structure.
The results are primarily compared to other character-based neural systems, but a comparison to state-of-the-art systems employing different approaches (e.g., word-based or subword-based models) is missing. Acknowledging and discussing the gap between the reported results and the current state-of-the-art would strengthen the paper.
The paper contains minor English language issues, including typos, grammatical errors, and incorrect LaTeX formatting (e.g., use of reverse quotation marks). A careful proofread is recommended.

Questions to Authors:
Could the authors elaborate on the computational cost of the morphological learning component compared to standard word embedding lookup?
What specific design choices in the hierarchical decoder were critical for achieving efficient training, and how were these choices determined?
Could the authors provide examples of specific morphological patterns that the model demonstrably learns, beyond the illustrative cases presented in the paper?
