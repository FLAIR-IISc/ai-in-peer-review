**Review for ACL 2017**

**Strengths:**

The authors present a novel adaptation of the encoder-decoder neural machine translation (MT) paradigm. Their approach uniquely combines character-level processing at the input and output with representations of morphemes and characters in intermediate layers. The release of both code and trained models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs translation is a significant contribution, facilitating validation of the work and enabling further research and extensions by the community. The reported system demonstrates encouraging translation quality, achieving reasonable results even after a single training epoch, with continued improvement observed in subsequent epochs. Furthermore, the system appears to learn effective morphological tokenizations and exhibits the capacity to handle out-of-vocabulary words – including nonce words – by implicitly leveraging morphemic information.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper lacks explicit specification of the WMT test and development sets used for evaluation. This omission hinders direct comparison of the reported results with existing work, such as those available at matrix.statmt.org. The reviewer was required to consult the code supplement’s README file to determine that newstest2015 was used for testing and newstest2013 for development; this information should have been clearly stated within the paper itself.

The instructions provided in the software README are adequate but could be improved. The training and testing sections would benefit from explicit examples of command-line execution. The software itself should implement a `--help` flag to provide users with readily accessible documentation, a feature currently absent.

The paper describes a 6-level architecture; however, Figure 2 depicts fewer than six layers. This discrepancy requires clarification. The figure caption should be more descriptive, and if the figure does not represent the complete architecture, an additional figure (potentially in the appendix) illustrating all layers should be included.

The results section provides comparisons to other character-based neural MT systems but omits a comparison to state-of-the-art results from systems employing different approaches. WMT and matrix.statmt.org report results from a variety of systems on these datasets, and the current state-of-the-art performance significantly exceeds the results presented in this paper. This should be acknowledged and discussed. 

The paper contains several minor issues related to English grammar, spelling, and LaTeX formatting (e.g., the use of reverse quotation marks). These should be addressed through careful proofreading.

**General Discussion:**

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the growing body of literature on character-based neural machine translation.
