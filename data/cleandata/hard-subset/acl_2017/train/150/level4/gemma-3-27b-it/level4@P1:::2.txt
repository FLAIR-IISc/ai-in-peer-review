This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture by adding a new deep word-encoding layer capable of encoding the character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. In the same way, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to transform the target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence as the final output of the NMT system. The objective of such an architecture is to take advantage of the benefits of character-level NMT (reduction of the size of the vocabulary and flexibility to deal with unseen words) and, at the same time, improve the performance of the whole system by using an intermediate representation of sub-words to reduce the size of the input sequence of characters. In addition, the authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is able to learn morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches.

**Strengths:** The paper presents an original and well-structured approach to character-level NMT. The proposed architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental results are promising. The combination of recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder is a novel idea, and the reported BLEU scores are competitive with existing methods. The idea of explicitly modeling morphology within the encoder is particularly interesting. The thorough experimentation on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En) strengthens the findings. 

**Weaknesses:** I have some reservations regarding the interpretation of the results and the strength of the evidence supporting the claim of improved morphological learning. The comparison with baseline systems, particularly bpe2char, could be more comprehensive. The BLEU score differences are small, and statistical significance testing is absent, making it difficult to definitively conclude superiority. The training time comparison is incomplete; En-Cs training time for bpe2char is missing, and En-Fr results for bpe2char are not presented. The section on morphological learning (5.2) relies heavily on illustrative examples and lacks formal evaluation. While these examples are insightful, they do not provide conclusive evidence for the claim.

**General Discussion:**

The authors address the challenges of large vocabulary sizes and out-of-vocabulary words in NMT by proposing a deep character-level model. This model learns subword units automatically and utilizes a hierarchical decoder to generate character sequences. The core innovation lies in the word encoder, which leverages two recurrent networks to capture morphological information. 

The paper’s experimental evaluation compares the proposed model (DCNMT) with other state-of-the-art systems using BLEU scores and training time. While DCNMT demonstrates competitive performance, the differences are often marginal. For example, the improvement over bpe2char is only 0.04 BLEU points on Cs-En and 0.1 on En-Cs. Crucially, the lack of statistical significance testing makes it difficult to determine if these differences are meaningful. Furthermore, bpe2char is faster to train for Cs-En, raising questions about the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

Section 5.2 attempts to demonstrate the model’s ability to learn morphology. The visual examples presented are compelling, showing that the word encoder can differentiate words based on morphological similarity. However, this section lacks rigorous evaluation. Quantitative experiments or analyses specifically designed to assess morphological understanding would significantly strengthen this claim. Moving this section to an appendix and softening the claims regarding morphological learning would be a reasonable step.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to determine if the observed differences in BLEU scores are significant?
2.  Could you provide the training time for bpe2char on the En-Cs dataset?
3.  What experiments could be conducted to more formally evaluate the model's ability to learn morphology (e.g., probing tasks, morphological analysis of learned representations)?
4.  Can you elaborate on why the energy values are incomplete in Figure 4a?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Numerous acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are either undefined or defined after first use. Consistent and early definition of these terms would improve clarity.
*   The concept of “energy” requires more context in Section 5.2. A clarification of how to interpret high energy values would be helpful.
*   The capitalization of "BPE" is inconsistent.
*   The statement about not using monolingual data in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary.
*   The results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) for Cs-En in Table 1 appear to be author-computed, which should be explicitly stated.
*   The description of French as "morphologically poor" is debatable. "Morphologically rich" versus "morphologically poor" is relative; Czech is simply *more* morphologically rich.
*   A link to the WMT'14 training corpora should be added, mirroring the link for WMT'15.
*   Several references are incomplete.
