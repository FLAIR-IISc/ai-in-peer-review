Strengths: The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, allowing for easy understanding of the presented ideas, which are original and yield interesting results.

Weaknesses: I have some reservations regarding the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the claims concerning the proposed method’s ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by scientific evidence.

General Discussion:
This paper investigates a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture builds upon a standard encoder-decoder framework with the addition of a deep word-encoding layer designed to map character-level input into sub-word representations of the source sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer transforms target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence, forming the final output of the NMT system. The motivation behind this architecture is to leverage the advantages of character-level NMT – reduced vocabulary size and adaptability to unseen words – while simultaneously enhancing performance through the use of intermediate sub-word representations to shorten input sequence lengths. The authors also posit that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than existing state-of-the-art approaches. 

My primary concern lies with the evaluation. The authors compare their approach (DCNMT) to other systems using training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of DCNMT over alternatives like bpe2char is not immediately apparent. The BLEU score difference is marginal (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and determining statistical significance is crucial. Has statistical significance testing been performed? Regarding training time, bpe2char demonstrates a substantial 8-day advantage for Cs-En. Training time for En-Cs is not provided (and should be), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the proposed model’s benefits.

My second concern pertains to Section 5.2, where the authors discuss the system’s morphological learning capabilities. This section primarily presents examples and accompanying commentary. While the examples are well-chosen and clearly explained, there appears to be a lack of experimental data or formal evaluation to support the stated claims. I encourage the authors to expand on this potentially valuable aspect of their work, perhaps even developing it into a separate paper. However, as it currently stands, this section isn’t central to the paper’s core contribution, and I suggest moving it to an appendix and moderating the claims regarding morphological learning.

Other comments, doubts and suggestions:
- Numerous acronyms are used without initial definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA), or are defined after their first use (e.g., RNN, BPE). While some acronyms are common in deep learning, defining them would improve clarity.
- The concept of “energy” is introduced in Section 3.1. While the initial explanation is sufficient, refreshing this concept in Section 5.2 (where it’s frequently referenced) and providing guidance on its interpretation would be beneficial – for instance, would a high energy value on a character suggest a potential morpheme split point? Additionally, the meaning of "peak" in Figure 5 is undefined.
- The acronym BPE is initially presented in capital letters but is subsequently written in lowercase; is there a specific reason for this inconsistency?
- The statement about not using a monolingual corpus in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary.
- Figure 4a appears to have an issue with color mapping, as not all energy values are represented.
- In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) for Cs-En are not sourced from the original publication. If the authors recomputed these results, they should explicitly state this.
- Describing French as "morphologically poor" is inaccurate; it is more precise to say it’s less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
- A link is provided for the WMT’15 training corpora, but not for WMT’14.
- Several references are incomplete.
