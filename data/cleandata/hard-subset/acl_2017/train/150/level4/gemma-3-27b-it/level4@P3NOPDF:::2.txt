This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed Deep Character Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) architecture extends a standard encoder-decoder framework by introducing deep word-encoding and -decoding layers. These layers aim to encode character-level input into sub-word representations and decode them back into character sequences, respectively. The objective is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT – reduced vocabulary size and improved handling of out-of-vocabulary words – while enhancing performance through intermediate sub-word representations that shorten the input sequence length. The authors also claim their deep word-encoding model exhibits superior morphological learning capabilities compared to existing approaches.

The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the presented ideas. The approach is original, and the reported results are interesting. However, I have some reservations regarding the interpretation of the results and the support for claims about morphological learning.

My primary concern lies with the evaluation. The authors compare DCNMT to state-of-the-art systems using BLEU score and training time. However, the advantage of DCNMT over comparable systems, such as bpe2char, appears marginal. The BLEU score difference is small (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to determine if these differences are meaningful. Moreover, bpe2char exhibits a significantly faster training time for Cs-En (8 days less than DCNMT). Training time for En-Cs is not provided, and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr, hindering a complete comparison. A more thorough evaluation against bpe2char is crucial to substantiate the claimed advantages of the proposed model.

My second concern relates to Section 5.2, which discusses the model’s ability to learn morphology. The section primarily presents illustrative examples and accompanying observations. While these examples are insightful and pedagogically sound, they lack formal experiments or quantitative evaluation to support the authors' claims. I encourage the authors to expand this promising aspect of the work, potentially as a separate paper. Alternatively, for the current submission, I suggest moving this section to an appendix and toning down the assertions regarding morphological learning capabilities.

Several minor points could further improve the paper:

*   **Acronyms:** Many acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are either undefined or defined after their first use. Defining these upfront would enhance clarity.
*   **Energy Concept:** The concept of “energy” is introduced in Section 3.1. A refresher in Section 5.2, coupled with guidance on its interpretation (e.g., does a high energy indicate a likely split point within a morpheme?), would be beneficial. The meaning of “peak” in Figure 5 should also be clarified.
*   **BPE Consistency:** The acronym BPE is initially capitalized but subsequently appears in lowercase. Maintaining consistent capitalization is recommended.
*   **Monolingual Data:** The statement about not using monolingual corpora in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary.
*   **Figure 4a:** The color mapping for energy values appears incomplete in Figure 4a.
*   **Table 1:** The results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) for Cs-En are not reported in the original paper and appear to be recomputed. This should be explicitly stated.
*   **Morphological Richness:** Describing French as “morphologically poor” is inaccurate; it's more appropriate to say it's less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
*   **WMT Links:** Providing a link to the WMT'14 training corpora, in addition to WMT'15, would be helpful.
*   **Incomplete References:** Several references are incomplete.
