This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a standard encoder-decoder framework by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer to map character-level input to sub-word representations, and a deep word-decoding layer to transform target-language sub-word representations back into a character sequence. The goal is to combine the benefits of character-level NMT—reduced vocabulary size and handling of out-of-vocabulary words—with improved performance through an intermediate sub-word representation, thereby shortening the input character sequence length. Furthermore, the authors posit that their deep word-encoding model learns morphological information more effectively than existing methods.

**Strengths:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, and the presented ideas are original. The reported results are interesting.

**Weaknesses:** I have reservations regarding the interpretation of the results. Additionally, the claims concerning the model’s ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence.

**General Discussion:**
The evaluation compares the proposed approach (DCNMT) to state-of-the-art systems using training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of DCNMT over systems like bpe2char is unclear. The BLEU score difference is minimal (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and statistical significance testing is absent. Is the observed difference statistically significant? Regarding training time, bpe2char is notably faster for Cs-En (8 days less). Training time for En-Cs is not reported (and should be), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the benefits of the proposed model.

My second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to have investigated morphological learning. However, the section relies primarily on examples and commentary. While these examples are illustrative, no formal experiments or evaluations support the authors' claims. I encourage them to expand upon this intriguing aspect, potentially as a separate publication. Nevertheless, this section is not crucial to the core contribution, and could be moved to an appendix with more cautious phrasing regarding morphological learning capabilities.

**Other Comments, Doubts, and Suggestions:**
 - Several acronyms are used without initial definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined after their first usage (e.g., RNN, BPE). Although some are common in deep learning, defining them would improve clarity.
 - The concept of “energy” is introduced in Section 3.1. A refresher of this concept in Section 5.2, with guidance on interpretation—for example, does high energy indicate a likely morpheme split point?—would be helpful. Also, the “peak” in Figure 5 is not described.
 - The acronym BPE is initially presented in uppercase, but subsequently in lowercase; the reason for this inconsistency should be clarified.
 - The statement regarding the lack of monolingual corpora in Section 4.1 appears unnecessary.
 - Figure 4a appears incomplete, as energy values are not consistently colored for all characters.
 - In Table 1, the Cs-En results for Chung et al. (2016) were not sourced from their paper. If the authors recomputed these results, this should be explicitly stated.
 - Describing French as “morphologically poor” is imprecise; it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech, but not inherently poor.
 - A link to the WMT'15 training corpora is provided, but not for WMT'14.
 - Several references are incomplete.
