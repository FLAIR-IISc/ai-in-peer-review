This paper presents a novel neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that operates at the character level, incorporating a mechanism for learning morphology. The core idea is to use recurrent networks to represent words based on their constituent morphemes, and then decode at the character level with a hierarchical decoder.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed architecture for deep character-level NMT which explicitly models morphology. By learning subword units automatically through recurrent networks, the model avoids the large vocabulary issue common in word-based NMT and offers potential advantages over existing subword approaches like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE). A second contribution is the hierarchical decoder, which efficiently decodes at the character level while maintaining encoding lengths comparable to word-based models. Finally, the empirical results demonstrate that the model achieves competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, and exhibits an ability to learn morphological structure, as shown through analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architectural Approach:** The proposed architecture is a compelling attempt to bridge the gap between word-level and character-level NMT, and to leverage the benefits of both. The use of recurrent networks to learn morphological representations is a particularly interesting idea that addresses a key challenge in NMT – handling rare and unseen words. The explicit modeling of morphology provides a potentially more interpretable and robust approach than purely data-driven subword segmentation methods.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The reported BLEU scores, while not state-of-the-art overall, are encouraging, especially considering the model's relative simplicity and the fact that competitive performance is achieved after only one epoch of training. The comparison to BPE-based models demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Resource Availability:**  The authors’ decision to release both code and trained models is a significant strength. This enables reproducibility, facilitates further research, and allows the community to benefit directly from this work. This is especially valuable for a complex architecture like the one presented.
4. **Demonstrated Morphological Learning:** The provided analysis, including the PCA visualization in Figure 3, suggests that the model is indeed learning meaningful representations of morphemes. The ability to handle nonce words, as illustrated in Table 2, further supports this claim and highlights a potential advantage of the morphological approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Reporting of Experimental Details:** The paper lacks clarity regarding the specific WMT datasets used for evaluation. While the README provides this information (newstest2015 for test, newstest2013 for dev), it should be explicitly stated in the main text for transparency and ease of comparison with other works.
2. **Software Usability Concerns:** The documentation for the released code is adequate but could be improved. More detailed instructions with specific command-line examples would enhance usability. The absence of a `--help` flag for the software is a minor but noticeable omission.
3. **Architectural Diagram Clarity:** Figure 2, which illustrates the model architecture, is somewhat unclear. The description claims a “6-level architecture,” yet the diagram doesn't explicitly show all six layers. A more comprehensive diagram or a clearer explanation of the architectural components is needed.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The results are primarily compared to other character-based models, but a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art word-based or hybrid models on the WMT benchmarks is needed to fully contextualize the performance. Acknowledging the gap between the reported results and current state-of-the-art scores is crucial.
5. **Minor Linguistic Issues:** The paper contains some minor grammatical errors, typos, and LaTeX formatting issues (e.g., incorrect quotation marks). A careful proofread would improve the overall quality of the presentation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the proposed model compared to other character-level and subword-level NMT systems? Specifically, what is the impact of the six recurrent networks on training and inference time?
2. The paper mentions the possibility of using deeper recurrent networks. Have you experimented with deeper architectures, and if so, what were the results?
3. Could you elaborate on the choice of the tanh activation function for normalizing the weights in the word encoder? Were other normalization methods considered, and if so, why was tanh preferred?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   It would be interesting to see an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component of the architecture (e.g., the morphological encoder, the hierarchical decoder).
*   Consider adding a discussion on how this method might generalize to other language pairs, particularly those with very different morphological structures.
