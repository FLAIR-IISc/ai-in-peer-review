This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture by adding a new deep word-encoding layer capable of encoding the character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. A deep word-decoding layer is similarly added to the output to transform target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence, completing the NMT system. The goal is to combine the benefits of character-level NMT (reduced vocabulary size and handling of unseen words) with improved performance through an intermediate sub-word representation to reduce input sequence length. Furthermore, the authors claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than existing methods.

**Contributions:**

1.  **A novel deep character-level NMT architecture:** The paper introduces a six-layer recurrent network architecture incorporating a word encoder designed to learn morphological structure and a hierarchical decoder to generate character sequences. This architecture effectively addresses the large vocabulary problem in NMT.
2.  **Demonstrated competitive performance:** The proposed model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art systems on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, while maintaining a relatively small model size and reasonable training time.
3.  **Exploration of morphological learning:** The paper presents initial analysis and visualization (through PCA and energy representations) suggesting the model can learn to represent morphemes, offering potential insights into the model’s internal representations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and architectural novelty:** The proposed architecture is a significant departure from typical word-level or subword-level NMT systems. The combination of two RNNs for word encoding and the hierarchical decoder is a noteworthy contribution.
2.  **Empirical results:** The paper demonstrates competitive BLEU scores on multiple translation tasks. The comparison with baselines, including BPE and other character-level models, provides a reasonable benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach. While the improvements aren't dramatic, achieving comparable performance with a different architectural approach is valuable.
3.  **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly explains the core ideas. The diagrams (Figures 1 and 2) are particularly helpful in understanding the architecture.
4.  **Addressing the OOV problem:** The character-level approach intrinsically addresses the out-of-vocabulary issue, which is a significant challenge in NMT. The model’s ability to handle misspelled or nonce words, as demonstrated in Section 5.3, is a practical benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited statistical significance testing:** The performance comparison lacks statistical significance testing. The BLEU score differences reported (e.g., 0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs) are small, and it is unclear whether these differences are statistically meaningful. This weakens the claim of superior performance.
2.  **Incomplete comparison:** The comparison to baselines is incomplete. For instance, bpe2char isn't evaluated on En-Fr, and training time for En-Cs isn’t provided. A more thorough comparison with competing approaches would strengthen the evaluation.
3.  **Insufficient evidence for morphological learning:** While the paper claims the model learns morphology, the evidence presented in Section 5.2 is primarily qualitative. The PCA visualization and energy analysis are interesting, but lack rigorous quantitative evaluation. Demonstrating morphological learning requires more than illustrative examples.
4.  **Acronyms and technical details:** The paper uses numerous acronyms without initial definitions or defines them after their first use. Additionally, certain concepts (e.g., "energy," “peak”) are introduced without sufficient explanation, making it difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific techniques to fully understand the methodology.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you performed statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) to determine whether the observed differences in BLEU scores are statistically significant?
2.  Could you provide a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, including training time for all language pairs and a more detailed analysis of the computational cost?
3.  What quantitative metrics did you consider to evaluate the morphological learning capabilities of the model? Could you propose and perform experiments (e.g., morphological segmentation tasks) to demonstrate the model's ability to learn morphemes more rigorously?
4.  Please define all acronyms upon their first use and provide a clearer explanation of terms like “energy” and “peak” within the context of your analysis.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider moving the material in Section 5.2 to an appendix and toning down the claims regarding morphological learning, unless stronger evidence is provided.
*   Correct minor typographical errors as noted in the original review.
*   Ensure all references are complete.
*   Reconsider the phrasing of "morphologically poor" when describing French; "less morphologically rich" might be more accurate.
*   Provide a link to the WMT’14 training corpora.
