This paper presents a complex character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture designed to leverage sub-word representations. The proposed architecture, extending a standard encoder-decoder framework, incorporates a deep word-encoding layer to map character-level input to sub-word units, and a deep word-decoding layer to generate the final character sequence output. The motivation is to combine the benefits of character-level NMT – reduced vocabulary size and improved handling of out-of-vocabulary words – with the efficiency gains of operating on shorter sequences through the use of an intermediate sub-word representation. The authors also posit that the deep word-encoding model facilitates improved morphological learning.

In general, the paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible. The approach is original, and the presented results are interesting. However, I have some reservations regarding the interpretation of the experimental results and the support for claims about morphological learning.

My primary concern lies with the evaluation. The authors compare their proposed model (DCNMT) to several state-of-the-art systems using BLEU score and training time. While the results are presented, the advantage of DCNMT over approaches like bpe2char is not compelling. The BLEU score difference is marginal (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to determine if the observed difference represents a genuine performance improvement. Furthermore, bpe2char exhibits a significantly faster training time for Cs-En (8 days less), and training time data is missing for En-Cs and the En-Fr language pair. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char, including statistical significance testing and complete training time data, is needed to substantiate the claims of superiority.

My second concern relates to Section 5.2, which discusses the system’s ability to learn morphology. The section relies heavily on illustrative examples and accompanying commentary, but lacks formal experiments or quantitative evaluation. While the examples are insightful and well-explained, they do not provide sufficient evidence to support the authors’ assertions. I encourage the authors to consider a more rigorous investigation of this aspect, which could potentially be developed into a separate publication. Alternatively, for the current paper, it might be prudent to move this section to an appendix and moderate the strength of the claims regarding morphological learning.

Several minor issues detract from the paper's clarity:

*   Numerous acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are either undefined or defined after their initial use. Defining these terms upfront would improve readability.
*   The concept of “energy,” introduced in Section 3.1, is used extensively in Section 5.2 without adequate re-explanation. Clarifying how to interpret energy values – for example, whether a high energy indicates a potential morpheme split point – would be beneficial. The meaning of “peak” in Figure 5 is also undefined.
*   The capitalization of "BPE" is inconsistent; it is initially capitalized but subsequently appears in lowercase.
*   The statement regarding the lack of monolingual corpus usage in Section 4.1 feels unnecessary.
*   Figure 4a appears to have incomplete color mapping for energy values.
*   Table 1 presents results for Chung et al. (2016) for Cs-En that are not reported in the original paper. If these results were recomputed, this should be explicitly stated.
*   The characterization of French as "morphologically poor" is inaccurate; it is more appropriate to describe it as less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
*   A link to the WMT’15 training corpora is provided, but not to the WMT’14 corpora.
*   Several references are incomplete.
