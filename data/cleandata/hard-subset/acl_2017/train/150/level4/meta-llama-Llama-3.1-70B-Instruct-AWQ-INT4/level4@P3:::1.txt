**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder. The model, called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT), translates at the character level and avoids the large vocabulary issue. The authors claim that their model achieves a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks, and its final BLEU score is comparable to state-of-the-art systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel architecture**: The authors propose a hierarchical architecture that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder, which translates at the character level.
2. **Efficient training**: The model achieves higher performance with efficient training, using a variant of the gate recurrent unit (GRU) that used in the second-level decoder.
3. **Learning morphology**: The model is able to learn morphology, which is demonstrated through experiments and analysis.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors present a novel adaptation of encoder-decoder neural MT using an approach that starts and ends with characters, but in between works with representations of morphemes and characters.
2. **Code and model release**: The authors release both their code as well as their final learned models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, which is helpful in validating their work and for others looking to replicate and extend this work.
3. **Reasonable translation results**: The system reported appears to produce translation results of reasonable quality even after the first training epoch, with continued progress in future epochs.
4. **Learning morphological tokenizations**: The system appears to learn reasonable morphological tokenizations and appears able to handle previously unseen words (even nonce words) by implicitly backing off to morphemes.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of explicit test and dev set description**: The authors do not explicitly state which WMT test and dev sets their results are reported on, making it problematic for readers wishing to compare the reported results to existing work.
2. **Instructions and software issues**: The instructions given in the software README are not great, and the software itself should respond to a --help flag, which it currently does not.
3. **Diagram clarity**: The diagram in Figure 2 appears to show fewer than 6 layers, and the caption should be more explicit. If this figure is not showing all of the layers, then there should be a figure somewhere (even if it's in an appendix) showing all of the layers.
4. **Comparison to state-of-the-art results**: The results show comparison to other character-based neural systems but do not show state-of-the-art results for other types of MT system. The state-of-the-art is much higher than any of the results reported in this paper, and this should be acknowledged and ideally discussed.
5. **Minor English disfluencies and LaTeX issues**: There are a handful of minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and minor LaTeX issues, such as reverse quotation marks, which should be corrected.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words, especially when they are not seen during training?
2. How do you plan to improve the model's performance on longer sentences, as the current results are reported on relatively short sentences?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the learned morphology and how it affects the translation quality?
