**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder. The model translates at the character level, avoiding the large vocabulary issue and improving training efficiency. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on English-to-French, English-to-Czech, and Czech-to-English translation tasks, achieving comparable results to state-of-the-art systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Architecture**: The authors propose a hierarchical architecture that learns morphology and translates at the character level, addressing the limitations of traditional word-level neural machine translation models.
2. **Efficient Training**: The model is able to efficiently train on large datasets, achieving reasonable translation results even after the first training epoch.
3. **Morphological Tokenizations**: The system learns reasonable morphological tokenizations and can handle previously unseen words by implicitly backing off to morphemes.

**Strengths**

1. The authors present a novel adaptation of encoder-decoder neural MT that starts and ends with characters but works with representations of morphemes and characters in between.
2. The authors release both their code and final learned models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, facilitating validation and extension of their work.
3. The system produces translation results of reasonable quality even after the first training epoch, with continued progress in future epochs.
4. The system learns reasonable morphological tokenizations and can handle previously unseen words by implicitly backing off to morphemes.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not explicitly state which WMT test and dev sets their results are reported on, making it difficult for readers to compare the results to existing work.
2. The instructions in the software README could be improved with explicit examples of how to run the respective commands.
3. The paper describes a 6-level architecture, but the diagram in Figure 2 appears to show fewer than 6 layers, which may cause confusion.
4. The results do not show state-of-the-art results for other types of MT systems, which should be acknowledged and discussed.
5. There are minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues that should be corrected.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the hierarchical decoder works and how it is able to efficiently train on large datasets?
2. How do you plan to extend your work to other languages and datasets?
3. Can you provide more insights on how the model learns morphological tokenizations and handles previously unseen words?
4. How do you plan to address the limitations of your model, such as the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art results for other types of MT systems?
