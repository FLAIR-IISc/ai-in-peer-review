- Strengths:

The authors present a novel adaptation of encoder-decoder neural machine translation (MT) that operates on characters, morphemes, and characters. This approach is innovative and contributes to the existing literature on character-based neural MT.

The authors have made their code and final learned models for French-English, Czech-English, and English-Czech publicly available. This is highly beneficial for validating their work and facilitating replication and extension of this research by others.

The system reported in the paper produces translation results of reasonable quality even after the first training epoch, with continued progress in subsequent epochs.

The system also appears to learn reasonable morphological tokenizations and can handle previously unseen words (including nonce words) by implicitly backing off to morphemes.

- Weaknesses:

A major limitation of the paper is the lack of explicit information on which WMT test and development sets the reported results are based on. This omission makes it challenging for readers to compare the reported results to existing work (e.g., the results at matrix.statmt.org). This reviewer had to consult the README file in the code supplement to find this information, which indicates that the test set was newstest2015 and the development set was newstest2013. This crucial detail should have been explicitly stated in the paper.

The instructions provided in the software README are satisfactory but could be improved. Specifically, the training and testing sections would benefit from explicit examples of how to run the respective commands. Additionally, the software should respond to a --help flag, which it currently does not.

The paper describes a 6-level architecture, but the diagram in Figure 2 appears to show fewer than 6 layers. This discrepancy should be clarified, and if Figure 2 does not depict all the layers, a supplementary figure (even in an appendix) should be included to show the complete architecture.

The results presented in the paper only compare the proposed system to other character-based neural systems. However, the state-of-the-art results for other types of MT systems, as reported by WMT (and matrix.statmt.org), are significantly higher than the results reported in this paper. This should be acknowledged and discussed.

There are several minor issues with the paper, including English disfluencies, misspellings, and LaTeX issues (e.g., reverse quotation marks). These should be corrected to improve the overall quality of the paper.

- General Discussion:

This paper is a valuable contribution to the existing literature on character-based neural MT. The authors' innovative approach and publicly available code and models make this work a useful addition to the field.
