**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder, which translates at the character level. The model consists of six recurrent networks and is trained end-to-end. The authors claim that their model avoids the large vocabulary issue, is more efficient in training than word-based models and conventional character-based models, and achieves a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel architecture for NMT that learns morphology by using two recurrent networks and a hierarchical decoder, which translates at the character level.
2. The model consists of six recurrent networks and is trained end-to-end, avoiding the large vocabulary issue and improving training efficiency.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, achieving a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch.

**Strengths**

1. The authors present a novel adaptation of encoder-decoder neural MT using an approach that starts and ends with characters, but in between works with representations of morphemes and characters.
2. The authors release both their code as well as their final learned models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, which is helpful in validating their work and for others looking to replicate and extend this work.
3. The system reported appears to produce translation results of reasonable quality even after the first training epoch, with continued progress in future epochs.
4. The system appears to learn reasonable morphological tokenizations and appears able to handle previously unseen words (even nonce words) by implicitly backing off to morphemes.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not explicitly state which WMT test and dev sets their results are reported on, making it problematic for readers wishing to compare the reported results to existing work.
2. The instructions given in the software README are not great and could be enhanced with explicit examples of how to run the respective commands.
3. The paper describes a 6-level architecture, but the diagram in Figure 2 appears to show fewer than 6 layers, which is confusing.
4. The results show comparison to other character-based neural systems but do not show state-of-the-art results for other types of MT system, which should be acknowledged and discussed.
5. There are a handful of minor English disfluencies, misspellings, and minor LaTeX issues that should be corrected.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the hierarchical decoder works and how it is trained?
2. How do you handle out-of-vocabulary words in your model?
3. Can you provide more information on the hyperparameters used in the experiments and how they were tuned?
4. How does your model compare to other state-of-the-art NMT systems on the same datasets?
5. Can you provide more analysis on the learned morphological tokenizations and how they affect the translation quality?
