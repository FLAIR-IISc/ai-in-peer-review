**Review**

**Strengths:** The paper is generally well-structured and clear, allowing for easy comprehension of the presented ideas. The originality of the ideas and the interesting results obtained are notable strengths of the paper.

**Weaknesses:** However, there are some concerns regarding the interpretation of the results and the scientific evidence supporting the claims made about the proposed method's capability to learn morphology.

**General Discussion:** This paper proposes a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT) that extends the classical encoder-decoder architecture by incorporating deep word-encoding and decoding layers. The objective is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT while improving performance by using an intermediate sub-word representation. The authors claim that their deep word-encoding model can learn morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches.

**Concerns and Suggestions:**

1. **Evaluation:** The comparison with other state-of-the-art systems, such as bpe2char, raises concerns. The difference in BLEU scores between the proposed approach (DCNMT) and bpe2char is minimal, and the lack of statistical significance information makes it difficult to determine which approach is superior. Additionally, the training time for bpe2char is significantly shorter for the Cs-En task, and the En-Cs training time is not provided. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char is necessary to demonstrate the advantages of DCNMT.

2. **Learning Morphology:** The section on learning morphology (5.2) lacks formal evaluation and experiments to support the claims made. Although the examples provided are well-chosen and explained, it is essential to conduct experiments to validate the authors' assertions. Consider moving this section to an appendix and softening the claims regarding the system's ability to learn morphology.

3. **Clarity and Consistency:** Several acronyms, such as LSTM, HGRU, CNN, and PCA, are used without definition or are defined after being used. Defining these acronyms would improve the paper's clarity. Additionally, the concept of energy is introduced in Section 3.1 but not revisited in Section 5.2, where it is used extensively. Providing hints on interpreting energy values and explaining the concept of peak in Figure 5 would be beneficial.

4. **Minor Issues:** The acronym BPE is defined with capital letters but used in lowercase throughout the paper. Figure 4a appears to be incorrect, as the energy values are not shown for every character. The results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) in Table 1 were not taken from the original paper, and it is unclear if the authors computed these results themselves. The statement about French being morphologically poor is misleading, and the link provided for WMT'15 training corpora but not for WMT'14 is inconsistent. Several references are incomplete, and there are typos throughout the paper.
