- Strengths: The paper is generally well-structured and clear, allowing the reader to follow most of the explanations. The ideas presented are original, and the results obtained are quite interesting.

- Weaknesses: I have some doubts about the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, I believe that some of the claims regarding the capability of the proposed method to learn morphology are not adequately supported by scientific evidence.

- General Discussion:
This paper explores a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture extends a classical encoder-decoder architecture by incorporating a new deep word-encoding layer capable of encoding character-level input into sub-word representations of the source-language sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to transform the target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence as the final output of the NMT system. The objective of this architecture is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT (reduced vocabulary size and flexibility in handling unseen words) while improving the performance of the entire system by utilizing an intermediate representation of sub-words to reduce the size of the input character sequence. Additionally, the authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is better at learning morphology than other state-of-the-art approaches.

I have some concerns regarding the evaluation. The authors compare their approach to other state-of-the-art systems based on two parameters: training time and BLEU score. However, I do not clearly see the advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over other approaches, such as bpe2char. The difference in BLEU scores between the two approaches is very small (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), making it challenging to determine whether one outperforms the other without information on statistical significance: was statistical significance evaluated? Regarding training time, it is worth noting that bpe2char for Cs-En takes 8 days less than DCNMT. For En-Cs, the training time is not provided (why not?), and for En-Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. I think a more comprehensive comparison with this system should be conducted to demonstrate the advantages of the proposed model.

My second concern is with Section 5.2, where the authors claim to have investigated the ability of their system to learn morphology. However, this section only contains examples and comments on them. Although these examples are well-chosen and explained in a didactic manner, it is worth noting that no experiments or formal evaluation appear to have been conducted to support the authors' claims. I would encourage the authors to extend this interesting part of the paper, which could potentially become a separate paper. On the other hand, this section does not seem to be a critical point of the paper, so for the current work, I suggest moving this section to an appendix and softening some of the claims regarding the system's ability to learn morphology.

Other comments, doubts, and suggestions:
- There are many acronyms used without definition (such as LSTM, HGRU, CNN, or PCA) or defined after being used (such as RNN or BPE). Although some of these acronyms are well-known in the field of deep learning, I would encourage the authors to define them to improve clarity.
- The concept of energy is first mentioned in Section 3.1. Although the explanation provided is sufficient at that point, it would be helpful to refresh the idea of energy in Section 5.2 (where it is used several times) and provide some hints on how to interpret it: would a high energy on a character indicate that the current morpheme should be split at that point? Additionally, the concept of a peak (in Figure 5) is not described.
- When the acronym BPE is defined, capital letters are used, but then it is lowercased in subsequent mentions; is there a reason for this?
- I am not sure if it is necessary to mention that no monolingual corpus is used in Section 4.1.
- It appears that there is an issue with Figure 4a, as the colors for energy values are not shown for every character.
- In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) for Cs-En were not taken from the paper, as they are not reported. If the authors computed these results themselves (as it seems), they should mention it.
- I would not say that French is morphologically poor but rather that it is not as rich as Slavic languages such as Czech.
- Why is a link provided for WMT'15 training corpora but not for WMT'14?
- Several references are incomplete.
