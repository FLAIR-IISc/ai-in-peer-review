**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel architecture for deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) by learning morphology. The model uses two recurrent neural networks to learn the representation of preceding characters and the weight of this representation of the whole word. A hierarchical decoder is then used to translate at the character level. The model is designed to address the issues of large vocabulary and out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words in traditional word-level NMT models.

**Main Contributions**

1. A novel architecture for deep character-level NMT that learns morphology by using two recurrent neural networks and a hierarchical decoder.
2. The model is able to learn the representation of morphemes and the rules of how they are combined, which may be viewed as learning distributed representations of words explicitly.
3. The model is efficient to train and achieves a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel and original architecture for deep character-level NMT that learns morphology.
2. The model is able to learn the representation of morphemes and the rules of how they are combined, which may be viewed as learning distributed representations of words explicitly.
3. The model is efficient to train and achieves a higher BLEU score than the bpe-based model after training for one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs translation tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. The evaluation of the model is not comprehensive, and the comparison with other state-of-the-art systems is not thorough.
2. The authors claim that their deep word-encoding model is able to learn morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches, but this claim is not properly backed by scientific evidence.
3. The paper lacks a formal evaluation of the model's ability to learn morphology, and the examples provided in Section 5.2 are not sufficient to support the authors' claims.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model, including a comparison with other state-of-the-art systems?
2. Can you provide a formal evaluation of the model's ability to learn morphology, including experiments and statistical significance tests?
3. Can you clarify the concept of energy in Section 3.1 and provide hints on how to interpret it in Section 5.2?

**Additional Comments**

* The paper uses many acronyms that are not defined, such as LSTM, HGRU, CNN, and PCA. It would be helpful to define these acronyms to improve clarity.
* The concept of energy is mentioned for the first time in Section 3.1, but it would be nice to refresh the idea in Section 5.2 and provide some hints on how to interpret it.
* The acronym BPE is defined with capital letters, but then used in lowercase throughout the paper. Is there a reason for this?
* It is not necessary to mention that no monolingual corpus is used in Section 4.1.
* Figure 4a seems to be missing color labels for the energy values.
* The results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) for Cs-En were not taken from the papers, and it is not clear if the authors computed these results themselves.
* French is not morphologically poor, but rather not as rich as Slavic languages such as Czech.
* A link is provided for WMT'15 training corpora, but not for WMT'14.
* Several references are incomplete.
* There are several typos throughout the paper, including "coveres" instead of "covers", "both consists of two-layer RNN, each has 1024" instead of "both consist of two-layer RNN, each have 1024", and "the only difference between CNMT and DCNMT is CNMT" instead of "the only difference between CNMT and DCNMT is that CNMT".
