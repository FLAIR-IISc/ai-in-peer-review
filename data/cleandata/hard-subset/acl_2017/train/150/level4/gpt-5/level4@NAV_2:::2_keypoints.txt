- Paper is well organized, readable, with followable technical narrative, novel core ideas, and intriguing empirical findings.
- Uncertainty in interpretation of results and insufficient rigorous evidence for claims about the model learning morphology.
- Proposes a character-level NMT with deep word-encoding and decoding modules that map characters to subword-like representations and back.
- Goal is to combine character-level benefits (small vocabularies, robustness) with shorter effective sequences via intermediate subword representations.
- Authors claim the deep word encoder captures morphology better than prior methods.
- Evaluation concerns: comparisons rely on training time and BLEU, with very small BLEU differences and no reported statistical significance testing.
- Baseline comparison incomplete: bpe2char trains 8 days faster for Cs–En; En–Cs training time omitted; En–Fr lacks bpe2char baseline—need comprehensive, apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Section 5.2’s morphological analysis is illustrative rather than controlled; recommends systematic experiments or moving the material to an appendix and moderating claims.
- Define acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, etc.) on first use and avoid defining some only after first mention.
- Clarify the notion of “energy,” revisit it in Section 5.2, state whether high energy implies morpheme boundaries, and explain the term “peak” in Figure 5.
- Ensure consistent capitalization of BPE throughout the paper.
- The statement that no monolingual data are used may be unnecessary.
- Verify Figure 4a for missing color values in some characters’ energy visualizations.
- In Table 1, if Chung et al. (2016) Cs–En results were computed by the authors, explicitly state this and describe the setup.
- Avoid calling French “morphologically poor”; say it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
- Add a link for the WMT’14 corpora (WMT’15 link is provided).
- Several references are incomplete and should be corrected.
