Summary
The paper proposes a character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that augments a standard encoder–decoder with attention by adding deep word-encoding and -decoding layers. These layers map character sequences into subword-like representations on the source side and back into characters on the target side, aiming to retain the benefits of character-level NMT (open vocabulary, robustness to unseen words) while shortening effective sequence lengths via subword abstractions. The authors further claim that the model captures morphological structure better than comparable approaches.

Strengths
- The paper is generally well structured and clearly written; the main ideas and modeling choices are easy to follow.
- The architectural design is original and thoughtfully motivated.
- The empirical results are interesting and suggest the approach is competitive with strong subword-based baselines.

Weaknesses
- The interpretation of several results is not fully convincing, especially given the very small BLEU differences relative to a strong baseline and the absence of statistical significance testing.
- Claims about the model’s ability to learn morphology are not adequately supported by quantitative evidence.
- The comparative evaluation against bpe2char is incomplete and, in places, asymmetric (missing runtimes or evaluations on certain language pairs).

Detailed comments
- Comparative evaluation and significance: The reported BLEU differences between DCNMT and bpe2char are extremely small (e.g., 0.04 for Cs–En and 0.1 for En–Cs), which makes it difficult to assert a clear advantage without statistical significance analysis. Please report statistical significance (e.g., paired bootstrap resampling) and include confidence intervals or p-values to substantiate claims of improvement or parity.
- Training time and fairness of comparison: For Cs–En, bpe2char trains 8 days faster than DCNMT under your setup. For En–Cs, the training time is not reported, and for En–Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. A more complete and balanced comparison is necessary:
  - Report training times for all models and language pairs on identical hardware and with matched optimization settings.
  - Include throughput (tokens/characters per second) and number of training steps to convergence.
  - If some models or language pairs could not be run, explain why and discuss potential impact on conclusions.
- Morphology analysis (Section 5.2): The section states that the model learns morphology but provides only a few qualitative examples. While the examples are well chosen and instructive, they are insufficient to support strong claims. I recommend:
  - Adding a quantitative evaluation, such as morpheme boundary prediction (e.g., F1 against gold morphological segmentations), morphological tagging accuracy via a probing classifier over learned representations, or correlation between “energy” peaks and linguistically annotated boundaries.
  - Reporting results across multiple languages with varying morphological richness.
  - If such an evaluation is beyond the scope of the current paper, consider moving the qualitative analysis to an appendix, softening the claims, and leaving a thorough investigation for future work.
- Clarity of the “energy” concept: You introduce “energy” in Section 3.1, but it reappears in Section 5.2 without a brief refresher or guidance on interpretation. Please restate the definition when it becomes central to analysis, clarify what constitutes a “peak,” and explain whether high energy on a character suggests a morpheme boundary or another structural signal.
- Scope and framing: The paper’s main contribution is a practical architecture for character-level NMT with subword-like internal structure. The paper would be stronger if the core empirical story focused tightly on translation quality, efficiency (training/inference), and robustness to rare or unseen words, with the morphology story presented as preliminary evidence rather than a central claim unless backed by quantitative evaluation.

Other comments, questions, and suggestions
- Acronyms: Several acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined only after first use (e.g., RNN, BPE). Please define all acronyms at first mention.
- BPE capitalization: Ensure consistent capitalization of “BPE” throughout.
- Monolingual data: It may be unnecessary to emphasize that no monolingual corpora are used in Section 4.1 unless comparing against systems that do; consider clarifying the rationale or omitting.
- Figures: In Figure 4a, the color mapping for energy values appears missing for some characters. Please verify rendering and ensure colorbars/legends are readable.
- Table 1 (Chung et al., 2016 results): You report Cs–En numbers that are not in the original paper. If you reproduced these results, explicitly state that and provide replication details (data, preprocessing, hyperparameters), ensuring a fair comparison.
- Language characterization: Rather than calling French “morphologically poor,” consider phrasing it as “less morphologically rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.”
- Dataset links: You provide a link for the WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14; please add the WMT’14 link for completeness.
- References: Several references appear incomplete; please check author lists, titles, venues, years, and page numbers.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper with a creative architecture and competitive results. However, the empirical evidence currently does not convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over strong baselines, and the claims about morphology learning are not sufficiently supported. I encourage the authors to (1) provide statistical significance testing; (2) complete and balance the comparisons with bpe2char across language pairs with matched setups and full runtime reporting; (3) either add a quantitative morphology evaluation or temper those claims and move the qualitative analysis to an appendix; and (4) address the noted presentation issues. With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger and more compelling contribution.
