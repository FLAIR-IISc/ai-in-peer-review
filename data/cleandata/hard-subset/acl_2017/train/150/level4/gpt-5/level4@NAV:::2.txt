Review-
- Strengths: Overall, the paper is clearly written and well organized. Most of the exposition is easy to follow, the ideas are original, and the resulting findings are quite interesting.

- Weaknesses: I have reservations about how the results are interpreted. In addition, some assertions about the proposed method’s ability to learn morphology are not properly supported by scientific evidence.

- General Discussion:
This paper presents a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The model augments a standard encoder–decoder framework with a deep word-encoding layer that converts character-level inputs into subword representations of the source sentence. Symmetrically, a deep word-decoding layer is added on the output side to map target-side subword representations back to a character sequence as the system’s final output. The goal is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT (reduced vocabulary size and flexibility with unseen words) while improving overall performance by introducing an intermediate subword representation to shorten the character input sequence. The authors also claim their deep word-encoding component learns morphology better than other state-of-the-art methods.
I have some concerns about the evaluation. The comparison against other state-of-the-art systems focuses on two metrics: training time and BLEU. However, I do not see a clear advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over alternatives such as bpe2char. The BLEU differences are minimal (0.04 in Cs–En and 0.1 in En–Cs), making it difficult to conclude that one outperforms the other without statistical significance testing: was statistical significance assessed? Regarding training time, it is notable that bpe2char for Cs–En trains 8 days faster than DCNMT. For En–Cs the training time is not reported (why?), and for En–Fr bpe2char is not evaluated. A more comprehensive comparison with this system is needed to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed model.
My second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors state that they investigated the model’s capacity to learn morphology. The section, however, only presents a few examples with accompanying commentary. While these examples are well chosen and explained in a very instructive manner, there appear to be no experiments or formal evaluations to substantiate the claims. I strongly encourage the authors to expand this very interesting part of the work, which could even stand as a separate paper. That said, this section does not seem critical to the main contribution; for the current submission, I suggest moving it to an appendix and tempering claims about the system’s morphological learning capabilities.
Other comments, doubts and suggestions:
 - Many acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined only after being introduced (e.g., RNN, BPE). Even if these are common in deep learning, defining them would improve clarity.
 - The notion of energy appears first in Section 3.1. Although the explanation there suffices, it would help to revisit the concept in Section 5.2 (where it is repeatedly used) and provide guidance on interpretation: does high energy on a character indicate a morpheme boundary should occur there? Also, the notion of a “peak” (in Figure 5) is not described.
 - When BPE is first defined, it is in uppercase, but subsequent mentions are lowercase; is there a reason for this inconsistency?
 - I am not sure it is necessary to state in Section 4.1 that no monolingual data are used.
 - Figure 4a seems problematic, as the energy colors are not displayed for every character.
 - In Table 1, the Cs–En results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) are not reported in the original papers. If the authors computed these themselves (as it appears), this should be stated.
 - I would not describe French as morphologically poor; rather, it is less rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.
 - Why is there a link for the WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14?
 - Several references are incomplete.
