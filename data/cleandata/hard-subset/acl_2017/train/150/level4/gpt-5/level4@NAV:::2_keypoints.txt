- Strength: Paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow with original ideas and interesting findings.  
- Strength: The proposed architecture (deep word-encoding/decoding layers around a char-level NMT) is novel and aims to combine character-level flexibility with shorter subword representations.  
- Weakness: The interpretation of results is questionable and some claims (notably about learning morphology) lack scientific support.  
- Evaluation concern: Comparisons focus only on BLEU and training time, with minimal BLEU differences that may not be statistically significant—no significance testing reported.  
- Evaluation concern: Training-time comparisons are incomplete and unfavorable in some cases (bpe2char trains 8 days faster for Cs–En; En–Cs training time missing; En–Fr lacks bpe2char baseline).  
- Recommendation: Provide a more comprehensive and statistically robust comparison with strong baselines like bpe2char.  
- Morphology claim concern: Section 5.2 offers only illustrative examples without formal experiments or quantitative evaluation to support claims about learned morphology.  
- Recommendation: Expand the morphology analysis into formal experiments or move the exploratory section to an appendix and temper morphological claims.  
- Clarity issue: Many acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are undefined or defined after use; define them when first introduced.  
- Concept clarity: The notion of “energy” and “peaks” is introduced in Section 3.1 but should be revisited and interpreted clearly in Section 5.2.  
- Notational inconsistency: BPE is inconsistently capitalized (uppercase then lowercase); standardize usage.  
- Minor content suggestion: Stating that no monolingual data are used in Section 4.1 is likely unnecessary.  
- Figure issue: Figure 4a appears problematic because energy colors are not shown for every character.  
- Table/replication note: Cs–En results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) are not in the original papers—if computed by the authors, this should be explicitly stated.  
- Terminology nuance: French should not be called “morphologically poor”; better to say it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.  
- Missing resource links and references: Provide a link for WMT’14 training corpora (WMT’15 is linked) and fix several incomplete references.  
- Typos: Multiple grammatical and typographical errors were noted and should be corrected (several specific examples provided in the review).
