- Strengths: The paper is generally well organized and clear. Most explanations are easy to follow, the ideas are original, and the results are intriguing.

- Weaknesses: I am uncertain about some result interpretations. In particular, several claims about the method’s ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence.

- General discussion:
This work proposes a complex character-level NMT architecture that extends a standard encoder–decoder by adding a deep word-encoding layer to map character-level inputs into subword representations of the source sentence. Symmetrically, a deep word-decoding layer converts target-side subword representations into a character sequence. The goal is to retain the advantages of character-level NMT (smaller vocabulary, robustness to unseen words) while improving efficiency and performance by introducing a subword intermediate that shortens character sequences. The authors also claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than existing approaches.

I have concerns about the evaluation. The comparison focuses on training time and BLEU. I do not see a clear advantage of DCNMT over strong baselines such as bpe2char. BLEU differences are minimal (0.04 for Cs–En and 0.1 for En–Cs), making it difficult to assert superiority without significance testing. Has statistical significance been assessed? Regarding training time, bpe2char is reported to be 8 days faster than DCNMT for Cs–En. Training time for En–Cs is missing (why?), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En–Fr. A more complete and balanced comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the claimed benefits.

My second concern is Section 5.2. The authors state they investigated the system’s ability to learn morphology, but the section presents only a few illustrative examples with commentary. While the examples are well chosen and explained, there is no formal experiment or quantitative evaluation to support the claims. I encourage the authors to substantially expand this line of analysis—potentially as a standalone paper. For the present work, consider moving this section to an appendix and softening claims about morphological learning.

Other comments, questions, and suggestions:
- Many acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined after first use (e.g., RNN, BPE). Even if well known, please define them upon first mention for clarity.
- The notion of energy first appears in Section 3.1. Although the initial explanation suffices, it would help to revisit and briefly re-explain “energy” in Section 5.2 (where it is used repeatedly) and provide guidance on interpretation (e.g., does a high energy at a character suggest splitting a morpheme there?). Also clarify what is meant by “peaks” in Figure 5.
- Ensure consistent capitalization of BPE (it is introduced in uppercase but later appears in lowercase).
- It may be unnecessary to explicitly state in Section 4.1 that no monolingual data were used.
- Figure 4a seems to have an issue: the energy color scale is not rendered for all characters.
- In Table 1, results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) for Cs–En are not reported in the original paper. If you computed them yourselves, please state this explicitly.
- Instead of saying French is “morphologically poor,” consider saying it is “less morphologically rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.”
- A link is provided for WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14; please add the latter for completeness.
- Several references appear incomplete; please check and fix all bibliographic entries.
