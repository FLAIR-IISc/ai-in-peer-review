Summary
This paper proposes a complex character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that extends a classical encoder–decoder by adding a deep word-encoding layer to convert character-level input into subword representations of the source sentence, and a deep word-decoding layer to map target-language subword representations into an output character sequence. The goal is to retain the benefits of character-level NMT (smaller vocabulary and robustness to unseen words) while improving efficiency and performance by leveraging intermediate subword representations to shorten character sequences. The authors further claim that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than existing state-of-the-art approaches.

Strengths
- The paper is generally well structured and clear. Most of the exposition is easy to follow.
- The ideas are original, and the empirical results are interesting.

Weaknesses
- I have doubts about the interpretation of some results.
- Several claims about the model’s ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by scientific evidence.

Detailed evaluation and concerns
- Comparative evaluation: The paper compares the proposed model (DCNMT) against state-of-the-art systems primarily on training time and BLEU. However, I do not clearly see an advantage over bpe2char. The BLEU differences are minimal (0.04 in Cs–En and 0.1 in En–Cs), making it hard to conclude superiority without statistical significance testing. Has statistical significance been evaluated?
- Training time reporting: For Cs–En, bpe2char trains 8 days faster than DCNMT. For En–Cs, the training time is not provided—why not? For En–Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. A more complete and balanced comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the claimed advantages.
- Section 5.2 (morphology): The paper states that the authors investigated the model’s ability to learn morphology, but this section contains only qualitative examples and commentary. While the examples are well chosen and pedagogically clear, there is no formal experiment or quantitative evaluation to support the claims. I encourage the authors to expand this very interesting analysis—potentially as a separate paper. For the current submission, I suggest moving this section to an appendix and softening claims about the system’s morphological learning capabilities.

Other comments, questions, and suggestions
- Acronyms: Several acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined only after being used (e.g., RNN, BPE). Even if well known, they should be defined on first mention to improve clarity.
- “Energy” concept: Energy is introduced in Section 3.1 with an adequate explanation, but it should be briefly revisited in Section 5.2 where it is used repeatedly. Please provide guidance on interpretation (e.g., does high energy on a character indicate a likely morpheme boundary?). The notion of “peak” in Figure 5 should also be defined.
- BPE capitalization: BPE is introduced in uppercase but later appears in lowercase. Please make capitalization consistent.
- Section 4.1: Stating that no monolingual corpus is used may be unnecessary.
- Figure 4a: The color map for energy values appears to be missing or inconsistently shown for some characters.
- Table 1: For model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) in Cs–En, results are not reported in the original papers. If the authors computed these values themselves (as seems likely), this should be explicitly stated.
- Morphology characterization: Rather than saying that French is morphologically poor, please state that it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.
- Data links: A link is provided for WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14. Please add the missing link.
- References: Several references are incomplete and should be corrected.
