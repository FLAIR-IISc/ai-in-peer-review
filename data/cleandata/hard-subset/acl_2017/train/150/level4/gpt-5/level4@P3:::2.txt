Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that avoids large target vocabularies while preserving efficiency. The model has two key components: (a) a word encoder that composes character sequences into word representations using two RNNs with learned, position-dependent weights intended to capture morpheme structure; and (b) a hierarchical decoder that generates characters conditioned on word-level decoder states via a variant of GRU with settable state (HGRU) and a batching trick to unfold word states over character timesteps. Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level systems, with early-epoch performance sometimes surpassing BPE baselines. The authors further provide qualitative analyses suggesting that the word encoder learns morphology and that the system handles nonce and misspelled words.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical character-level NMT architecture with a morpheme-aware word encoder and an efficient hierarchical decoder, enabling end-to-end training without large-vocabulary softmax.
2) Empirical results showing competitive BLEU with substantially smaller model size and promising early-epoch performance versus BPE baselines.
3) Qualitative evidence of morphological sensitivity and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Architectural novelty and motivation: The combination of a two-RNN word encoder with learned position-dependent weights and a hierarchical character decoder is original and well-motivated by the vocabulary bottleneck. The batching/unfolding trick for conditioning the character decoder on word-level states is a practical contribution toward efficient training.

- Competitive performance with smaller models: Despite comprising six RNNs, the model is relatively compact (about 54M parameters) and achieves BLEU comparable to strong character-level baselines across three language pairs. The one-epoch results that surpass BPE baselines are noteworthy and suggest favorable learning dynamics.

- Clear end-to-end character-level translation while mitigating sequence length: By compressing source characters into word representations and decoding characters from word-level states, the approach preserves the open-vocabulary benefits of character models while avoiding very long sequences in the encoder.

- Qualitative robustness to OOV/misspellings: The examples showing correct handling of misspellings and morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”) are compelling demonstrations of practical advantages over word-level systems.

- Generally clear exposition: The high-level narrative, model diagrams, and training details allow readers to follow the architecture and reproduce many aspects.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and inconclusive empirical comparison: The BLEU differences versus bpe2char are very small (e.g., 0.04 in Cs–En, 0.1 in En–Cs), and no statistical significance testing is reported. Training-time comparisons are inconsistent: for Cs–En, bpe2char is 8 days faster than DCNMT; for En–Cs, DCNMT time is reported but bpe2char is missing; for En–Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. Without matched, comprehensive comparisons (same hardware, stopping criteria, beam sizes) and significance tests, claims of superiority are hard to substantiate.

- Morphology claims lack formal evaluation: Section 5.2 presents qualitative plots and examples, but no quantitative analysis (e.g., morpheme boundary F1, correlation between “energy” peaks and gold segmentations, or probing tasks such as morphological tagging from word representations). As written, the evidence does not adequately support the claim that the model “learns morphology.”

- Clarity gaps and missing definitions: Several acronyms are undefined on first use (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA), and some are defined late (RNN, BPE). The notion of “energy” and “peaks” is central to Section 5.2 but is not reintroduced or operationalized there, making interpretation ambiguous.

- Reproducibility and reporting issues: Some table entries appear derived rather than cited (e.g., model (3) for Cs–En is not in the original paper); figures (e.g., Fig. 4a) seem inconsistent in coloring; dataset links are inconsistent (WMT’15 linked, WMT’14 not); several references are incomplete. These issues hinder verification.

- Overstatements and imprecise claims: Describing French as “morphologically poor” is imprecise; it is less rich than Czech but not poor. Some claims about efficiency and morphology are stronger than the evidence presented.

Questions to Authors
- Significance and variance: Please report bootstrap resampling or paired significance tests for BLEU differences across systems. Also provide mean/variance across multiple runs or checkpoints.

- Fair-time comparisons: For each language pair, report training days, epochs, hardware, and early-stopping criteria for all baselines (including bpe2char) to enable apples-to-apples comparisons.

- Decoder efficiency: What is the computational overhead of constructing and multiplying by the unfolding matrix R? How does it scale with sequence length, and what is the wall-clock impact?

- Morphology evaluation: Can you quantify morphology learning? For example:
  - Correlate “energy” peaks with morpheme boundaries against a gold segmentation resource (F1/precision/recall).
  - Run a morphological tagging or inflectional feature prediction probe from word representations.
  - Compare against a CNN word encoder under the same protocol.

- Table 1 provenance: How were the Cs–En results for model (3) obtained? If reproduced, please state your training setup and any deviations from the original paper.

- Figures: Please clarify Fig. 4a coloring, annotate “peak” definitions, and restate the “energy” definition in Section 5.2.

Additional Comments and Suggestions
- Define acronyms (RNN, LSTM, GRU/HGRU, CNN, BPE, PCA) on first use. Keep capitalization consistent for BPE.

- Consider moving Section 5.2 to an appendix or tempering claims unless you add quantitative morphology experiments.

- Minor wording/style:
  - “are the bilingual, parallel corpora provided …”
  - “Luong and Manning (2016) use …”
  - “HGRU (it is …)”
  - “covers”
  - “both consist of two-layer RNNs; each has 1024 …”
  - “the only difference between CNMT and DCNMT is that CNMT …”
  - Prefer “French is less morphologically rich than Czech.”

- Dataset and references: Provide a link to WMT’14 parallel corpora; complete missing bibliographic fields (venues, years) and fix citation formatting.

- Reproducibility: If possible, release code and scripts or provide enough detail (tokenization, character inventories, beam sizes, length penalties) to replicate results.

Overall Recommendation
This is a well-motivated and thoughtfully designed architecture with competitive results and practical advantages. However, the empirical comparisons need to be more complete and statistically grounded, and the morphology learning claims should be supported quantitatively or softened. With these additions and fixes, the paper would make a solid contribution.
