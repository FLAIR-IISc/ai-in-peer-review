Strengths:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. Most of the technical narrative is followable, the core ideas are novel, and the empirical findings are intriguing.

Weaknesses:
- I remain uncertain about some aspects of the results interpretation. Moreover, several assertions about the model’s ability to learn morphology are insufficiently supported by rigorous evidence.

General Discussion:
This work investigates a sophisticated architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The model augments a standard encoder–decoder with attention by introducing a deep word-encoding module that converts character inputs into subword-like representations of the source sentence. Symmetrically, a deep word-decoding module converts target-side subword representations into the final character sequence. The intent is to retain the advantages of character-level NMT (smaller vocabularies and robustness to unseen forms) while improving overall performance via an intermediate subword representation that shortens the effective character sequence. The authors also claim their deep word encoder captures morphology more effectively than prior methods.
I have concerns about the evaluation. The comparison against other state-of-the-art systems relies on training time and BLEU. It is not evident that the proposed DCNMT is preferable to, for instance, bpe2char. The BLEU differences are very small (0.04 in Cs–En and 0.1 in En–Cs), and without statistical significance testing it is difficult to conclude one system outperforms the other. Was significance assessed? Regarding training time, bpe2char for Cs–En trains 8 days faster than DCNMT. For En–Cs, training time is not reported (why is this omitted?), and for En–Fr, bpe2char is not included. A more comprehensive, apples-to-apples comparison with this baseline is needed to substantiate claimed advantages.
My second concern is Section 5.2. The section states it investigates morphological learning, but it provides illustrative examples with commentary rather than controlled experiments or formal evaluation. While the examples are insightful and clearly explained, the claims would benefit from systematic experimental validation. I encourage the authors to expand this promising thread—potentially as a standalone contribution. For the current paper, consider moving this material to an appendix and moderating claims about morphological learning.

Other comments, doubts and suggestions:
- Many acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined only after first use (e.g., RNN, BPE). Even if common in the field, define them on first mention to improve clarity.
- The notion of “energy” appears first in Section 3.1. Although the initial explanation suffices there, please briefly revisit it in Section 5.2 (where it is used repeatedly) and clarify its interpretation: does a high energy on a character imply a morpheme boundary at that point? Also, the term “peak” in Figure 5 is not explained.
- BPE is introduced in uppercase but later appears in lowercase; ensure consistent capitalization.
- The statement in Section 4.1 that no monolingual data are used may be unnecessary.
- Figure 4a appears to have missing color values for some characters’ energy; please verify.
- In Table 1, results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) on Cs–En are not reported in the cited paper. If you computed them, explicitly state this and describe the setup.
- Rather than calling French “morphologically poor,” it may be more accurate to say it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.
- You provide a link for the WMT’15 corpora but not for WMT’14; consider adding one.
- Several references are incomplete and should be corrected.
