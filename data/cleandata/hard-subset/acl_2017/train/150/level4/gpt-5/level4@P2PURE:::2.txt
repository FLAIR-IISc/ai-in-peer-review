- Strengths: Overall, the paper is well structured and clear. Most explanations are easy to follow, the ideas are original, and the results are quite interesting.

- Weaknesses: I have reservations about the interpretation of the results. In addition, some claims about the method’s ability to learn morphology are not adequately supported by scientific evidence.

- General Discussion:
This paper presents a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). It extends the standard encoder–decoder framework with a deep word-encoding layer that converts character-level input into subword representations of the source sentence. Symmetrically, a deep word-decoding layer converts target-side subword representations into a character sequence as the final system output. The goal is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT (smaller vocabulary and robustness to unseen words) while improving overall performance by introducing an intermediate subword representation to shorten the input character sequence. The authors further claim that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than other state-of-the-art approaches.
I have some concerns about the evaluation. The authors compare their approach to other state-of-the-art systems using two metrics: training time and BLEU. However, I do not see a clear advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over alternatives such as bpe2char. The BLEU differences are very small (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), and without statistical significance testing it is difficult to conclude that one system outperforms the other. Was statistical significance evaluated? Regarding training time, it is notable that bpe2char for Cs-En trains 8 days faster than DCNMT. For En-Cs, training time is not provided (why not?), and for En-Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. A more complete comparison with this system is needed to substantiate the claimed advantages of the proposed model.
My second concern relates to Section 5.2, where the authors state that they investigated the system’s ability to learn morphology. However, this section presents only a few examples and commentary. Although these examples are well chosen and explained in an informative manner, there appear to be no experiments or formal evaluations to support the authors’ claims. I encourage the authors to expand this very interesting part of the work, which could even become a separate paper. That said, this section does not seem critical to the current contribution; for the present manuscript, I suggest moving it to an appendix and softening the claims regarding the system’s ability to learn morphology.

Other comments, doubts, and suggestions:
- Many acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined only after being used (e.g., RNN, BPE). Even though many are common in deep learning, I recommend defining them upon first use to improve clarity.
- The concept of energy is introduced in Section 3.1. While the explanation is sufficient there, it would help to revisit and briefly explain the notion of energy again in Section 5.2 (where it appears multiple times), including guidance on interpretation. For instance, does high energy on a character indicate a morpheme boundary at that position? Also, the notion of a “peak” (in Figure 5) is not described.
- When BPE is first defined, it appears in uppercase, but subsequent mentions are lowercase. Is there a reason for this inconsistency?
- I am not sure it is necessary to state in Section 4.1 that no monolingual corpus is used.
- Figure 4a appears to have an issue: the colors for the energy values are not shown for every character.
- In Table 1, the results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) for Cs-En were not taken from the original paper, as they were not reported there. If the authors computed these results themselves (as seems likely), they should say so.
- I would not describe French as morphologically poor; rather, it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.
- Why is there a link to the WMT’15 training corpora but not to WMT’14?
- Several references are incomplete.
