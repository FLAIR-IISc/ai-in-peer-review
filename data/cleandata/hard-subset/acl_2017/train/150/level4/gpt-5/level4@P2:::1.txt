Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture that learns morphology via a two-RNN word encoder and uses a hierarchical decoder that generates characters conditioned on word-level states (HGRU). This design aims to remove large-vocabulary bottlenecks while retaining efficient training and stable attention.
- Secondary: The model yields competitive BLEU scores after only one epoch and shows robustness to OOVs, misspellings, and nonce forms by leveraging learned morphemes.
- Tertiary: The authors release code and trained models for En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, facilitating replication and further research.

Strengths
- Tackles the vocabulary and OOV problem with an elegant character-in/character-out approach that still exploits word-level structure. The two-RNN word encoder decouples character composition from morpheme weighting, and the hierarchical decoder avoids the inefficiencies of two-pass training seen in prior hybrid systems. The paper supports these claims with evidence of competitive BLEU and qualitative analyses of morpheme boundaries.
- Fast learning and training efficiency. The reported results show reasonable BLEU after a single epoch, with continued improvement in subsequent epochs. Compared to prior character-only models that required months of training, the proposed system reaches comparable performance in days, aided by a compact parameter count.
- Morphological generalization and robustness. The encoder appears to learn meaningful subword segmentations (e.g., consistent treatment of suffixes) and the system handles unseen or misspelled words (including nonce morphology like “convenienter”) by backing off to morphemes, a practical advantage over word-level systems.
- Reproducibility. Releasing both code and trained models for multiple language pairs meaningfully lowers the barrier to replication and extension.

Weaknesses
- Dataset splits not clearly specified in the paper. The dev and test sets (newstest2013 and newstest2015, respectively) are only stated in the code README, not in the main text. This omission hinders direct comparison with existing work (e.g., WMT results on matrix.statmt.org). The paper should explicitly document all evaluation sets and any filtering or preprocessing choices.
- Software usability could be improved. The README’s training and testing instructions would benefit from end-to-end command examples, including data paths, hyperparameters, and decoding settings. The binaries/scripts do not respond to a --help flag, which would aid users in reproducing results.
- Architectural clarity. The text describes “six recurrent networks” across a hierarchical stack, but Figure 2 appears to depict fewer layers. The caption is too terse, and the paper should either revise Figure 2 to show all components or add an additional schematic that enumerates each RNN (source character RNN, morphological weighting RNN, bidirectional sentence encoder, word-level decoder, character-level decoder/HGRU, target word encoder used for feedback).
- Limited baseline coverage and positioning vs. state-of-the-art. While the comparisons to character-based models are useful, the paper does not contextualize results against strong subword/word-level NMT or phrase-based WMT systems reported on the same test sets, where the SOTA is notably higher. The paper should acknowledge this gap, discuss likely causes (e.g., training budget, architecture depth, search settings), and ideally report stronger baselines under matched conditions.
- Minor presentation issues. There are several English disfluencies, typos, and LaTeX problems (e.g., reversed quotation marks). These detract from readability and should be corrected.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in the response)
- Please confirm the exact dev/test sets and any filtering or length constraints used for each language pair. Were there maximum word/character lengths applied at training and inference time?
- Could you provide the precise training and decoding commands (with all flags), including optimizer settings, beam size, length normalization/penalty, batch size, and early stopping criteria, so that Table 1 can be reproduced?
- Please enumerate the six RNNs and clarify how they map to Figure 2. If not all components are shown, can you include a complete architecture diagram (even as an appendix)?
- How does training and inference speed compare to your strongest baselines in tokens/sec or chars/sec, and what are the memory footprints during training and decoding?
- Could you add comparisons to stronger BPE-based or word-level attentional models trained under similar budgets on the same data, and discuss the remaining gap to the best WMT systems?

Additional comments
- Editorial: fix punctuation, spelling, and LaTeX quote marks; ensure consistent notation; standardize references (years and venues).
- Software: add a --help option and example scripts for training, validation, and decoding; specify required preprocessing/tokenization and character set handling.
