- Strengths: The paper is generally well structured and clear. Most explanations are easy to follow, the ideas are original, and the results are interesting.

- Weaknesses: I have doubts about the interpretation of the results. In addition, some claims about the method’s ability to learn morphology are not properly supported by scientific evidence.

- General discussion:
This paper presents a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). It extends a classical encoder–decoder model with a deep word-encoding layer that converts character-level input into subword representations of the source sentence. Symmetrically, a deep word-decoding layer converts target-side subword representations into the final character sequence. The goal is to combine the advantages of character-level NMT (smaller vocabulary, robustness to unseen words) with improved performance by introducing an intermediate subword representation that shortens character sequences. The authors also claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than state-of-the-art approaches.

I have several concerns about the evaluation. The comparisons focus on training time and BLEU. However, I do not see a clear advantage of the proposed model (DCNMT) over alternatives such as bpe2char. The BLEU differences are minimal (0.04 in Cs–En and 0.1 in En–Cs), making it difficult to conclude which system is better without statistical significance testing. Has statistical significance been assessed? Regarding training time, bpe2char for Cs–En is 8 days faster than DCNMT. For En–Cs, training time is not provided (why not?), and for En–Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. A more complete and balanced comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the claimed advantages.

My second concern is Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate the system’s ability to learn morphology. The section presents only a few examples and commentary. While these examples are well chosen and clearly explained, there appear to be no experiments or formal evaluations to support the claims. I strongly encourage the authors to extend this very interesting analysis—potentially as a standalone paper. For the current submission, I suggest moving this section to an appendix and softening claims about the system’s morphological learning capabilities.

Other comments, doubts, and suggestions:
- Many acronyms are used without definition (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA) or are defined only after they are first used (e.g., RNN, BPE). Even if common in deep learning, please define all acronyms on first use to improve clarity.
- The concept of “energy” is introduced in Section 3.1. While the initial explanation is sufficient, it would help to briefly restate it in Section 5.2 and provide guidance on interpretation. For instance, does high energy on a character indicate a likely morpheme boundary? Also, the notion of a “peak” (Figure 5) is not defined.
- When BPE is first defined, it appears in uppercase, but subsequent mentions use lowercase. Please make capitalization consistent.
- It may be unnecessary to explicitly state in Section 4.1 that no monolingual corpus is used.
- Figure 4a appears to have an issue: colors for the energy values are not shown for every character.
- In Table 1, the Cs–En results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) were not reported in the original paper. If the authors reproduced these results themselves (as seems likely), this should be clearly stated.
- I would not describe French as morphologically poor; rather, it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages such as Czech.
- Why is a link provided for the WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14?
- Several references are incomplete.
