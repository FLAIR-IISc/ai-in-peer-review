- Proposes a character-level NMT with deep word-encoding/decoding layers that map characters to subword-like representations and back, claiming reduced sequence length and better morphological capture.
- Paper is well structured and clearly written.
- Architectural design is original and thoughtfully motivated.
- Empirical results are competitive with strong subword-based baselines.
- BLEU differences versus bpe2char are very small and lack statistical significance testing.
- Claims about learned morphology are supported only by a few qualitative examples, not quantitative evidence.
- Comparative evaluation against bpe2char is incomplete and asymmetric (missing training times/evaluations for some language pairs).
- Recommend reporting statistical significance (e.g., paired bootstrap) with confidence intervals or p-values.
- Training-time comparisons are unfair/incomplete; report training times, throughput, and steps to convergence for all models/pairs on identical hardware and settings.
- If some models/pairs weren’t run, explain why and discuss impact on conclusions.
- Morphology analysis needs quantitative evaluation (morpheme boundary F1, probing classifier accuracy, correlation with annotations) across multiple languages or move qualitative analysis to appendix and soften claims.
- Clarify and restate the definition and interpretation of “energy,” specify what constitutes a “peak” and its linguistic meaning.
- Tighten scope: focus main empirical story on translation quality, efficiency, and robustness; present morphology as preliminary unless supported quantitatively.
- Define all acronyms at first mention and ensure consistent capitalization of “BPE.”
- Clarify the role or omission of monolingual data in experiments.
- Fix Figure 4a color mapping/legends so energy values render clearly.
- If Table 1 reproduces prior results (Chung et al., 2016), explicitly state reproduction and provide replication details (data, preprocessing, hyperparameters).
- Rephrase language characterization (e.g., “less morphologically rich than Czech” rather than “morphologically poor”).
- Add missing WMT’14 dataset link and complete/verify reference bibliographic details.
- Numerous minor typos and phrasing edits are needed (examples listed by reviewer).
- Overall: creative, well-written work with competitive results but needs statistical testing, balanced runtime comparisons, quantitative morphology evaluation or softened claims, and presentation fixes to be more convincing.
