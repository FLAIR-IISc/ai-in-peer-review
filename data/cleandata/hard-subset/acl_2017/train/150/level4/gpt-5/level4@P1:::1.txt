Summary and Contributions
- This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model that learns morphology and generates translations character by character. The encoder uses two RNNs to build word representations by weighting prefix-based character states with a second BiRNN (“energy”) signal. The decoder is hierarchical: a word-level decoder produces states that initialize a character-level decoder (HGRU) to emit the characters of each target word until a delimiter.
- The approach removes the large-vocabulary bottleneck (no word softmax), keeps encoder sequence lengths close to word-level models, and introduces an efficient batching/training scheme via an unfolding matrix for the character decoder.
- Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show the model surpasses a BPE baseline after one epoch and achieves final BLEU scores comparable to recent character-based systems. Analyses (PCA visualizations, energy plots) suggest the model learns morphemes and handles misspellings and nonce words.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An efficient hierarchical character-level NMT architecture that eliminates large-vocabulary softmax and trains faster than prior character-only systems while remaining competitive in BLEU.
2) Empirical evidence that the model quickly reaches strong performance (e.g., surpassing a BPE baseline after one epoch) and remains compact (~54M parameters).
3) Qualitative demonstrations that the word encoder captures morphology and that the overall system is robust to OOVs, misspellings, and nonce words.

Strengths
- Efficient, open-vocabulary architecture: By learning word representations from characters and decoding at the character level, the model sidesteps large word softmaxes and OOVs. The unfolding/batching strategy for the character decoder is practical and appears to enable end-to-end training without the multi-pass tricks sometimes needed in earlier character-level work.
- Strong early learning and compactness: Table 1 shows the model exceeds the BPE baseline after just one epoch on En–Fr and En–Cs and remains competitive at convergence, with fewer parameters (~54M) than several comparator character models. Reported training on a single GTX Titan X and shorter time-to-quality are convincing practical advantages.
- Morphology-aware representations: The word encoder’s energy weighting produces more semantically coherent clusters than an ordinary RNN encoder (PCA plots), and the energy profiles align with plausible morpheme boundaries. The qualitative segmentation examples (e.g., “consumers,” “football”) and the “any*/every*” analyses support the intended behavior.
- Robustness to misspellings and nonce forms: The model translates misspelled inputs and unseen morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”) correctly, where word-level systems would emit <unk> or copy. This is a clear benefit of pure character decoding and offers practical robustness.
- Reproducibility: Code and trained models for fr–en, cs–en, and en–cs are released, facilitating validation and extension.

Weaknesses
- Test/development sets not stated in the paper: The paper does not specify which WMT dev/test sets were used; this information appears only in the code README (newstest2013 for dev, newstest2015 for test). Without this in-paper disclosure, it is hard for readers to compare results against the WMT leaderboard (e.g., matrix.statmt.org). This should be explicit in the main text.
- Documentation quality: The software README is serviceable but could be clearer. Concrete, copyable command-line examples for training and decoding are missing, and the binaries/scripts should respond to a --help flag. Improving these would significantly ease replication.
- Figure mismatch and missing detail: The text describes an architecture with six RNNs, but Figure 2 appears to depict fewer layers. The caption should explicitly indicate what is shown and what is omitted, and a complete architecture diagram (even in an appendix) would resolve the ambiguity.
- Limited context vs. broader SOTA: Results are compared to several character-based baselines, but not to the broader state of the art on these WMT benchmarks (including non-character and non-neural systems). Public WMT results for these tasks are higher than the numbers reported here. The paper should acknowledge this gap and discuss how the proposed model trades off simplicity/efficiency vs. absolute BLEU.
- Minor language and formatting issues: There are scattered English disfluencies, typos, and LaTeX quirks (e.g., reversed quotation marks). A careful editing pass would improve readability.

Questions to the Authors
- Please state explicitly in the paper the dev/test sets used (e.g., “dev: WMT newstest2013; test: WMT newstest2015”), along with tokenization/preprocessing choices for all systems compared.
- Can you provide a complete architectural diagram showing all six RNNs and their connections, including where the HGRU state is set/reset, and how the unfolding matrix interfaces with batching?
- How does your best final BLEU compare to reported WMT state-of-the-art for these directions on the same test sets? A brief discussion of the performance gap and the efficiency/robustness benefits your model provides would be valuable.
- Could you add concrete example commands in the README for training and decoding, and a --help option describing arguments? This would greatly aid reproducibility.
- Is HGRU (with settable state and delimiter-triggered resets) novel relative to prior hierarchical decoders, or a tailored variant of GRU? A short comparison/ablation would clarify its contribution.

Overall Evaluation
This is a solid and practical contribution to character-level NMT: the architecture is thoughtful, the efficiency gains are compelling, and the qualitative morphology/OOV handling is convincing. With clearer dataset specification, a complete figure of the full architecture, improved documentation, and a brief discussion situating results against broader WMT SOTA, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
