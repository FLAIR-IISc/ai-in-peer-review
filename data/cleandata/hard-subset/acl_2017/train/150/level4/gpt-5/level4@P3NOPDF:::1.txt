Summary
This paper presents a novel encoder–decoder neural MT architecture that operates on characters at the input and output, while learning intermediate representations at the morpheme and character levels. The approach shows promising performance and robustness to unseen and nonce words. The authors also release code and trained models for fr–en, cs–en, and en–cs, which substantially aids reproducibility.

Strengths
- Novel design: The character-to-morphology-to-character pipeline is a meaningful contribution to character-based NMT, offering a principled way to capture morphology while maintaining open-vocabulary translation.
- Reproducibility: The public release of code and final trained models (fr–en, cs–en, en–cs) is valuable for validation and for enabling follow-up work.
- Training dynamics: The system achieves reasonable translation quality after the first epoch, with continued improvements in subsequent epochs.
- Morphological handling: The model learns plausible morphological segmentations and handles unseen or nonce words by effectively backing off to morphemes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dataset specification: The paper does not state which WMT development and test sets are used. This omission makes it difficult to compare results with prior work (e.g., on matrix.statmt.org). According to the code supplement README, the test set is newstest2015 and the dev set is newstest2013. These details must be clearly reported in the main text, ideally in the experimental setup section, to enable fair comparisons and reproducibility.
- Software usability: While the README is serviceable, it would benefit from concrete, end-to-end examples of training and inference commands (including configuration, data preprocessing, and expected outputs). The command-line interface should also support a --help flag describing arguments and defaults. Improving these aspects would lower the barrier to replication and adoption.
- Architectural clarity: The paper describes a 6-level architecture, but Figure 2 seems to depict fewer than six layers. Please clarify this discrepancy. The figure caption should explicitly indicate which components are shown and which are omitted for brevity. If space is limited, include a complete schematic (even in an appendix or supplementary material) showing all layers and data flow.
- Comparative results: The paper compares primarily against other character-based NMT systems, but does not situate results relative to state-of-the-art systems reported by WMT (and summarized on matrix.statmt.org). Since those results on the same datasets are substantially higher, the manuscript should acknowledge this and discuss the performance gap. Where possible, include comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., subword-based NMT and competitive SMT/NMT entries from the corresponding WMT years) to contextualize the contribution.
- Presentation and copyediting: There are minor English disfluencies, spelling errors, and LaTeX issues (e.g., reversed quotation marks). A careful proofreading pass will improve clarity and polish.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution to character-based NMT that thoughtfully integrates morphological structure and demonstrates practical robustness to OOVs. The release of code and trained models is a strong plus for the community. To maximize the paper’s impact and clarity, I recommend: explicitly reporting the dev/test sets, improving software documentation and CLI support, resolving the architectural figure discrepancy, and situating results against stronger baselines from WMT. With these revisions and minor language cleanup, the paper will be significantly stronger and easier to evaluate and reproduce.
