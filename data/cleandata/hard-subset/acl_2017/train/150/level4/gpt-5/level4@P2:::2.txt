Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a deep, character-level NMT architecture that avoids large target vocabularies. It introduces (i) a character-to-word encoder intended to learn subword structure/morphology, and (ii) a hierarchical decoder that generates characters conditioned on word-level states (via an HGRU), enabling efficient character-level decoding.
- Empirically, the model achieves BLEU scores comparable to strong character/subword baselines on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, with notably strong performance after only one epoch on some pairs. The model is smaller than some recent character-level systems and claims faster training than pure character models.
- The paper argues that the encoder captures morphology and subword boundaries, supported by case studies and visualizations.

Strengths
- Clear architectural motivation: The hierarchical design addresses key drawbacks of character-level NMT (sequence length, attention difficulty) while removing the large-vocabulary bottleneck. The two-RNN word encoder and the HGRU decoder are well motivated by linguistic structure (morphemes) and training efficiency.
- Competitive performance with efficient training: Table 1 shows that DCNMT reaches strong BLEU in fewer epochs than some character-level baselines, and after one epoch surpasses a BPE baseline on En–Fr and En–Cs. This suggests practical benefits when training time is constrained.
- Robustness to misspellings/nonce words: Section 5.3 presents qualitative examples where DCNMT correctly translates misspellings and morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”), highlighting a genuine advantage of character-level models over word-level systems that rely on unknown tokens.
- Compact model: Despite using six RNNs, the total parameter count (~54M) is smaller than several compared character models, which is a non-trivial engineering contribution given the depth and hierarchical structure.
- Evidence of subword-aware encoding: Visualizations (Figures 3–5) indicate the encoder differentiates morphologically related forms better than an ordinary character-to-word RNN and can identify plausible subword boundaries without explicit supervision.

Weaknesses
- Comparative evaluation is inconclusive: The BLEU gaps with bpe2char are small (e.g., 0.04 in Cs–En, 0.1 in En–Cs), and no statistical significance testing is reported. Without significance tests and variance estimates, it is difficult to claim superiority. Moreover, the comparison coverage is uneven: bpe2char is absent for En–Fr, and En–Cs training time for bpe2char is not provided.
- Morphology-learning claims lack formal evaluation: Section 5.2 relies on selected examples and PCA plots. While instructive, there is no quantitative assessment (e.g., unsupervised segmentation scores, probing tasks, or morphological reinflection tests) to substantiate the claim that the model “learns morphology” better than baselines.
- Incomplete or unclear reporting: Some training-time numbers are missing; the provenance of certain baseline results appears mixed (e.g., Table 1, model (3) for Cs–En). Important implementation details (e.g., how the unfold matrix R interacts with batching and whether it affects decoding latency) could be clearer.
- Presentation issues reduce clarity: Many acronyms are used before being defined (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA), the “energy” and “peak” terminology is introduced informally and then reused without redefinition in Section 5.2, and Figure 4a appears to have missing color values. These detract from readability and interpretability.
- Overstated claims: Statements such as “French is morphologically poor” are imprecise; French is less morphologically rich than Czech, but not “poor.” Some reference entries are incomplete, and dataset links are inconsistent.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Did you run statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) for BLEU differences in Table 1? Please report p-values or confidence intervals.
- For Table 1, model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) in Cs–En: did you reproduce these results? If so, please state settings and confirm they are comparable.
- Can you provide En–Cs training time for bpe2char and an En–Fr bpe2char baseline to complete the comparison?
- Could you quantify morphology learning (e.g., MorphoChallenge-style segmentation metrics, or probing classification of suffixes/prefixes) to support Section 5.2?
- Please clarify the “energy” measure: how should peaks be interpreted in relation to morpheme boundaries? Also define “peak” in Figure 5 and fix the color rendering issue.

Additional comments
- Define all acronyms on first use (RNN, LSTM, GRU, HGRU, CNN, BPE, PCA) and use consistent casing for BPE throughout.
- Consider moving Section 5.2 to the appendix if no quantitative evaluation can be added; temper claims accordingly.
- Provide a link for WMT’14 (as for WMT’15) and complete all references.
- It may not be necessary to state that no monolingual data is used, unless contrasting with systems that do.
