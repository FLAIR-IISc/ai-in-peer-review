Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture that augments a standard attention-based encoder–decoder with two additional components: (i) a word encoder that composes characters into subword/word representations using two RNNs intended to capture morphology, and (ii) a hierarchical decoder that conditions a character-level decoder (HGRU) on word-level states to generate target words as character sequences. The goals are to avoid large vocabularies, keep sequence lengths manageable, and improve training efficiency relative to fully character-based models. The paper also claims that the model learns morphology and can handle misspellings and nonce forms.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical character→subword→word architecture with a novel RNN-based word encoder and a settable-state character-level decoder (HGRU), enabling end-to-end character-level NMT with reduced sequence lengths.
2) An efficient training strategy (unfolding first-level decoder outputs over character positions) that yields competitive BLEU with a relatively small model and fewer epochs than prior character-level systems.
3) Qualitative analyses suggesting the model captures morphological regularities and handles OOV/misspelled words.

Strengths:
- Clarity and organization: The paper is generally well structured and readable. The model description is detailed enough to follow, and the qualitative demonstrations are intuitive and pedagogical.
- Architectural originality: The combination of a morphology-aware word encoder with a hierarchical character decoder is interesting and goes beyond standard BPE or pure character approaches. The HGRU with settable state and the batching trick for unfolding word-level states are practical contributions.
- Competitive performance and efficiency: Despite using six RNNs, the model size is modest (~54M parameters). On En–Fr and En–Cs, one-epoch training already outperforms a BPE baseline, and final BLEU is comparable to strong character-level systems. The ability to translate misspellings and nonce words (e.g., “convenienter”) is a compelling qualitative strength that word-level systems lack.

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation and significance: The reported BLEU gains over bpe2char are very small (e.g., +0.04 on Cs–En and +0.1 on En–Cs), making it hard to claim superiority without statistical significance testing. No significance results are reported. Moreover, training-time comparisons are incomplete or inconsistent: for Cs–En, bpe2char trains ~8 days faster than DCNMT; for En–Cs, training time is missing; for En–Fr, bpe2char is not evaluated. A fuller, apples-to-apples comparison with bpe2char (and other competitive baselines) is needed to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Morphology claims lack formal validation: Section 5.2 presents well-chosen qualitative examples and PCA plots, but no formal experiments (e.g., morphological segmentation accuracy, probing tasks, or controlled generalization tests) are provided. The evidence is illustrative rather than conclusive. Given space, this section could be moved to an appendix and the claims about morphology learning softened, or supplemented with quantitative evaluation.
- Presentation issues:
  - Many acronyms are undefined at first use (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA), and some are defined after being used (e.g., RNN, BPE).
  - The notion of “energy” introduced in Section 3.1 is used repeatedly in Section 5.2 without reintroduction or guidance on interpretation; “peak” in Figure 5 is also undefined.
  - BPE capitalization is inconsistent.
  - The statement that French is “morphologically poor” is overstated; better to say “less morphologically rich than Czech.”
  - Figure 4a appears to have missing color values for some characters.
  - In Table 1, the Cs–En results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016b) are not reported in that paper; if reproduced by the authors, this should be stated.
  - A link is given for WMT’15 but not for WMT’14; several references are incomplete.
  - Stating that no monolingual data is used in Section 4.1 may be unnecessary.

Questions to authors:
- Have you run statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) for BLEU differences, especially against bpe2char? Please report them.
- Can you provide complete and consistent training-time numbers for all settings, including En–Cs, and include bpe2char for En–Fr?
- For the Cs–En results of model (3) in Table 1, did you reproduce them yourselves? If so, please document the setup and note this explicitly.
- Please clarify the “energy” notion and “peak”: how are energies computed and normalized, and how should peaks be interpreted with respect to morpheme boundaries?
- Please define all acronyms at first use and standardize BPE capitalization.
- Can you fix or explain the color mapping issue in Figure 4a?
- Please add a link for the WMT’14 corpora and complete missing reference details.

Additional comments and typos:
- Prefer “French is less morphologically rich than Czech” over “morphologically poor.”
- Several references are incomplete; please provide full venue, year, and page numbers where appropriate.

  - “..is the bilingual, parallel corpora provided...” → “..are the bilingual, parallel corpora provided...”

Overall, this is a clear and interesting paper with a promising architecture and competitive results. Strengthening the empirical comparison (significance and training-time parity) and adding more rigorous morphology evaluation would substantially improve the paper.
