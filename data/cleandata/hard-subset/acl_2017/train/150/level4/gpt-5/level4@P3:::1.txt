Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture that avoids large target vocabularies while leveraging morphology. A two-RNN word encoder computes character-compositional word representations with learned position-dependent weights intended to capture morphemes. A hierarchical decoder performs word-level planning and character-level generation via a modified GRU (HGRU) whose state is periodically reset from the word-level decoder and “unfolded” across character positions for efficient batched training. Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show faster early convergence than a BPE baseline and final BLEU scores competitive with recent character-level systems. Qualitative analyses suggest the model learns meaningful subword structure and handles misspellings and nonce words. Code and pretrained models are released.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, end-to-end character-level NMT architecture that combines a morphology-aware word encoder with a hierarchical, batched character decoder (HGRU + unfolding), eliminating large-vocabulary softmaxes while keeping encoder sequence lengths near word-level.
2) Empirical evidence of efficient training and competitive BLEU with a relatively small model, including faster gains in the first epoch than a BPE baseline.
3) Analyses that indicate emergent morphological segmentation and robustness to OOVs, misspellings, and productive morphology.

Strengths
- Addresses OOV and large-vocabulary bottlenecks with a coherent design. The two-RNN word encoder decouples character accumulation from position-dependent weighting, plausibly modeling morpheme boundaries. Combined with character decoding, this removes the target softmax over large vocabularies while maintaining short encoder sequences.
- Training efficiency and model size. Despite using six RNNs, the reported model is compact (≈54M parameters) compared to other character-based systems (≈69–76M), and reaches strong BLEU quickly (e.g., after one epoch exceeds BPE on En–Fr/En–Cs). Reported training days are fewer than several recent char baselines.
- Decoder engineering is useful to the community. The HGRU with resettable state and the unfolding matrix enable efficient batched training of a hierarchical character decoder without multiple forward passes. This is a practical contribution beyond the specific model.
- Qualitative evidence of morphology learning and robustness. PCA plots, per-character “energy” visualizations, and automatic segmentations (e.g., consum-er-s, mon-day) are convincing and align with linguistic intuition. The system translates misspellings and nonce words (“convenienter”) correctly, highlighting a clear practical benefit of character-level modeling.
- Release of code and pretrained models facilitates verification and reuse.

Weaknesses
- Dataset splits and evaluation details are not clearly stated in the paper. The specific WMT dev/test sets (newstest2013/newstest2015) appear only in the README, not in the main text. This hinders comparability and should be explicit, along with preprocessing, filtering, and tokenization choices.
- Limited comparison to strong non-character baselines and to the WMT state of the art. The paper emphasizes comparisons among character-based systems, but WMT leaderboards for the same years/data often report substantially higher BLEU with other architectures. The claim of being “comparable to the state-of-the-art systems” is too strong without broader baselines and context; at minimum, this gap should be acknowledged and discussed.
- Clarity and consistency issues in the model description. The paper states a six-RNN architecture, but Figure 2 does not clearly depict all six components, and the caption/text are ambiguous. Some equations contain typographical/notation errors (e.g., bracketed superscripts, undefined symbols), and terms like “Trgt,” “start-of-the-art” appear. These issues make it harder to precisely reproduce the architecture.
- Insufficient ablation to isolate where gains come from. The CNMT vs. DCNMT comparison is briefly mentioned, but there is no systematic ablation of the word encoder’s weighting mechanism (two RNNs + tanh weights) versus simpler alternatives (last-state encoder, CNN, softmax-normalized weights), nor of the target-side word encoder feedback. Without this, it is hard to attribute improvements to morphology learning versus other factors.
- Reproducibility and completeness of experimental details. The README is usable but could be much stronger (e.g., command-line examples, --help). Test-time search details (beam size, length normalization, stopping, character penalties) are missing. Character vocabulary choices (120 chars, handling of <unk>) and their coverage on dev/test are not quantified. Training hyperparameters differ by task (minibatch sizes), and it is unclear whether baseline systems were matched on hardware/epochs/updates.

Questions to Authors
- Please specify in the paper, for each language pair, the exact train/dev/test splits (e.g., WMT newstest2013/newstest2015), preprocessing pipeline (tokenization only?), and any filtering.
- What beam size, length normalization, and stopping criteria did you use at test time? Were these tuned on the dev set?
- Can you provide a schematic that clearly enumerates all six RNNs and their connections, including when the HGRU resets vs. continues across characters?
- What is the exact novelty of HGRU relative to a standard GRU with an external reset signal? Could a conventional GRU with a learned reset input replicate this behavior? Any ablation?
- Please add ablations: (a) your word encoder vs. last-state RNN vs. CNN; (b) tanh weighting vs. softmax-normalized attention-like weights; (c) with/without target-side word encoder feedback.
- How many dev/test characters are mapped to <unk> under the 120-character vocabulary, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- Can you evaluate your induced segmentations against a morphological analyzer (for English/Czech) to quantify the “learns morphology” claim?
- Please contextualize your BLEU relative to the strongest WMT systems for the same year/data, and temper claims about “state-of-the-art” accordingly.

Additional Comments
- Please proofread for English and LaTeX (e.g., quotes, equation notation, typos).
- Table 1 should include consistent training settings (epochs, updates, hardware), sentence-length cutoffs, and explicit beam sizes for all systems compared.
- Consider releasing end-to-end scripts and adding a --help option with example commands to improve reproducibility.
