- Strengths: The paper is generally well-structured and clear, with most explanations being easy to follow. The ideas presented are original, and the results are interesting.
- Weaknesses: I have some reservations regarding the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the claims concerning the method’s ability to learn morphology are not sufficiently supported by scientific evidence.
- General Discussion:
This paper investigates a complex architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The proposed architecture builds upon a standard encoder-decoder framework by incorporating a deep word-encoding layer designed to map character-level input to sub-word representations of the source sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output, transforming target-language sub-word representations into a character sequence, completing the NMT system. The goal of this architecture is to leverage the advantages of character-level NMT—reduced vocabulary size and improved handling of out-of-vocabulary words—while enhancing performance through an intermediate sub-word representation that shortens the input character sequence. The authors also assert that their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than existing state-of-the-art methods. 

My primary concern lies with the evaluation. The authors compare their approach (DCNMT) to other systems based on training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of DCNMT over approaches like bpe2char is not readily apparent. The BLEU score difference is small (0.04 for Cs-En and 0.1 for En-Cs), and determining statistical significance is crucial. Has statistical significance been assessed? Regarding training time, bpe2char takes 8 days less for Cs-En, while En-Cs training time is not reported (and should be). Furthermore, bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison with bpe2char is needed to substantiate the proposed model’s advantages.

My second concern pertains to Section 5.2, where the authors discuss their system’s morphological learning capabilities. The section primarily presents examples and commentary, which, while well-chosen and clearly explained, lack experimental or formal evaluation to support the authors’ claims. I encourage the authors to expand this potentially impactful aspect of the work, perhaps even as a separate paper. However, this section isn’t critical to the core contribution, and could be moved to an appendix with a more cautious framing of the morphological learning claims. 

Other comments, doubts and suggestions:
 - Many acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are used without initial definition or are defined after their first use. While some are common in deep learning, defining them would improve clarity.
 - The concept of “energy” is introduced in Section 3.1. While the initial explanation is sufficient, revisiting the concept in Section 5.2, with guidance on its interpretation (e.g., high energy indicating a morpheme split point), would be beneficial. Additionally, the meaning of “peak” in Figure 5 should be clarified.
 - The acronym BPE is initially capitalized but subsequently appears in lowercase; the reason for this inconsistency should be explained.
 - The statement regarding the lack of a monolingual corpus in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary.
 - Figure 4a appears incomplete, as energy values are not color-coded for all characters.
 - In Table 1, the Cs-En results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) are not sourced from the original paper. If the authors recomputed these results, they should state this explicitly.
 - Describing French as “morphologically poor” is inaccurate; it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
 - A link is provided for the WMT’15 training corpora but not for WMT’14.
 - Several references are incomplete.
