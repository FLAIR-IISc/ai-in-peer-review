- Strength: Paper is well-structured, clearly written, presents original ideas and interesting, easy-to-follow results.  
- Weakness: Interpretation of results is questionable and claims about morphology learning lack sufficient scientific evidence.  
- Contribution: Proposes a character-level NMT with deep word-encoding and word-decoding layers to produce intermediate sub-word representations.  
- Motivation: Aims to combine character-level benefits (smaller vocab, handling unseen words) with shorter input sequences via sub-word representations.  
- Claim: Authors assert the deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than state-of-the-art methods.  
- Main concern (evaluation): BLEU differences vs. baselines are minimal (0.04 Cs–En, 0.1 En–Cs) and lack statistical significance testing.  
- Main concern (training time): bpe2char requires 8 fewer training days for Cs–En; En–Cs training time is omitted and En–Fr bpe2char results are missing—comparison is incomplete.  
- Recommendation: Provide statistical tests and a more comprehensive, fair comparison to substantiate DCNMT benefits.  
- Concern (morphology analysis): Section 5.2 relies only on illustrative examples and descriptive commentary without formal experiments or quantitative evaluation.  
- Recommendation (morphology): Either expand Section 5.2 into a proper empirical study or move it to an appendix and tone down morphology claims.  
- Comment: Define all acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) on first use.  
- Comment: Clarify the concept of "energy" in Section 5.2 and explain what constitutes a "peak" in Figure 5.  
- Comment: Standardize BPE capitalization consistently across the paper.  
- Comment: The statement that no monolingual corpus was used (Section 4.1) is unnecessary.  
- Figure issue: Figure 4a shows inconsistent energy color mapping across characters and should be fixed.  
- Table/attribution: In Table 1, indicate/cite if Chung et al. (2016) Cs–En results were computed by the authors rather than taken from the original paper.  
- Language caution: Avoid calling French "morphologically poor"; instead say it is less morphologically rich than languages like Czech.  
- Data links: Justify why a link is provided for WMT'15 but not for WMT'14 training corpora.  
- References: Complete all references.  
- Typos to fix: several grammatical/typographical errors (list provided by reviewer, e.g., "are the bilingual...", "use" vs "uses", "it is" lowercase, "covers", subject-verb agreement in RNN sentence, add "that" in CNMT/DCNMT sentence).
