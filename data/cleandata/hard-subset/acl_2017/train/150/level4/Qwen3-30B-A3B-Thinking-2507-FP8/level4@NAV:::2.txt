

Strengths: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with original ideas and interesting results that are generally easy to follow.  
Weaknesses: I have concerns regarding the interpretation of the results, and certain claims about the method’s capability to learn morphology lack sufficient scientific evidence.  

General Discussion:  
This paper proposes a complex character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture that extends a classical encoder-decoder framework by adding a deep word-encoding layer to convert character-level input into sub-word representations of the source sentence, and a deep word-decoding layer to transform target-language sub-word representations into character sequences. The goal is to leverage character-level NMT benefits (reduced vocabulary size, better handling of unseen words) while improving performance via intermediate sub-word representations that shorten the input character sequence. The authors further claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than state-of-the-art approaches.  

My primary concern is the evaluation. The authors compare their DCNMT model to state-of-the-art systems (e.g., bpe2char) using training time and BLEU score. However, the BLEU differences are minimal (0.04 in Cs-En, 0.1 in En-Cs), and without statistical significance testing, it is unclear whether DCNMT outperforms alternatives. Additionally, bpe2char requires 8 days less training time for Cs-En; En-Cs training time is omitted (please justify), and En-Fr results for bpe2char are missing. A more comprehensive comparison is needed to substantiate DCNMT’s advantages.  

My second concern pertains to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate morphology learning. The section relies solely on illustrative examples and descriptive comments without formal experiments or evaluation. While the examples are well-chosen and clearly explained, the lack of empirical support weakens the claims. I recommend either expanding this into a standalone study or moving the section to an appendix and tempering the morphology-related assertions.  

Other comments:  
- Define all acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) upon first use.  
- Clarify the concept of "energy" in Section 5.2 (e.g., high energy indicating morpheme boundaries) and explain "peak" (Figure 5).  
- Standardize BPE capitalization (consistent use of "BPE" or "bpe").  
- The statement about no monolingual corpus being used (Section 4.1) is unnecessary.  
- Figure 4a has inconsistent energy color mapping across characters.  
- Table 1: Cite if Chung et al. (2016) results for Cs-En were computed by the authors (not from the original paper).  
- Avoid stating French is "morphologically poor"; instead, note it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.  
- Justify why a link is provided for WMT'15 but not WMT'14 training corpora.  
- Complete all references.
