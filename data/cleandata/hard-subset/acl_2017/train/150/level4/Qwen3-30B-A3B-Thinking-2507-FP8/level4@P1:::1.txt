

Summary: This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) model that addresses the large vocabulary bottleneck in traditional word-based neural machine translation (NMT) systems. The authors introduce a hierarchical architecture that learns morphology through a specialized word encoder and a character-level hierarchical decoder. The main contributions, in order of significance, are: (1) a novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through two recurrent networks, (2) a hierarchical decoder that efficiently translates at character level while maintaining the same encoding length as word-based models, and (3) empirical demonstration that the model achieves competitive BLEU scores with significantly reduced training time compared to existing character-level approaches.

Strengths:
The paper presents a compelling approach to character-level NMT that effectively addresses the out-of-vocabulary problem while maintaining translation quality. The authors' key insight—that learning morphology through a dedicated word encoder enables efficient character-level translation—leads to a model that achieves state-of-the-art results with minimal training (just one epoch) on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks. Their empirical results demonstrate that the model not only matches but exceeds the performance of BPE-based models after a single training epoch, and achieves competitive BLEU scores with significantly reduced training time compared to other character-level approaches. The model's ability to handle unseen words (including nonce words like "convenienter") through morphological decomposition is particularly noteworthy, as it solves a critical limitation of standard word-based NMT systems.

The authors have made their code and trained models publicly available for the three language pairs (fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs), which greatly enhances reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area. This is a significant contribution to the community beyond the technical merits of the approach itself.

The model's efficiency in training is well-documented, with the authors demonstrating that their six-recurrent-network architecture trains faster than other character-level models despite its complexity. Their analysis showing that the model learns meaningful morphological representations (evidenced by the PCA projections in Figure 3) provides strong support for their approach's theoretical foundation.

Weaknesses:
The paper fails to explicitly state which WMT test and development sets were used for evaluation, which is critical for readers to properly contextualize the results. The only way to determine this was by examining the code repository's README, which indicates newstest2015 for testing and newstest2013 for development. This information should have been clearly stated in the main text to facilitate direct comparison with other systems reported on matrix.statmt.org.

The paper describes a "six-level architecture" but Figure 2 appears to show fewer than six layers, creating confusion about the model's structure. The caption should be more explicit about how the six layers map to the architecture, or a supplementary figure should be provided showing all layers. This discrepancy between the textual description and visual representation undermines the clarity of the technical contribution.

The results section compares the model to character-level approaches but fails to acknowledge that the state-of-the-art results for these language pairs on WMT benchmarks are substantially higher than those reported in the paper. For instance, the best results on En-Fr (as reported on matrix.statmt.org) exceed the 30.56 BLEU score reported for DCNMT. This lack of context makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the claimed results.

The software documentation, while functional, could be improved with explicit examples in the README for both training and testing commands. The absence of a --help flag in the software itself is a minor but unnecessary inconvenience for potential users.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the exact WMT test and development sets used in your experiments (newstest2015 and newstest2013), and explicitly state this in the paper?
2. Please provide a detailed explanation of how the six-layer architecture maps to the components shown in Figure 2, or include a supplementary figure showing all layers.
3. Given that the state-of-the-art results on these language pairs are substantially higher, could you elaborate on the specific conditions under which your model achieves competitive performance compared to these stronger baselines?
