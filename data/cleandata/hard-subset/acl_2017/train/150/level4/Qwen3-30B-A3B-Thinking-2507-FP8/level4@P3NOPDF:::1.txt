

Strengths:  
The authors propose a novel character-based encoder-decoder architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) that integrates morphemic representations within a character-level pipeline. This approach—starting and ending with characters but operating on morphemes during intermediate processing—offers a compelling middle ground between character-level and word-level systems. The authors release code and trained models for fr-en, cs-en, and en-cs, significantly enhancing reproducibility and enabling future replication or extension of their work. Early training results demonstrate reasonable translation quality even after the first epoch, with steady improvement across subsequent epochs. Crucially, the system learns meaningful morphological segmentations and handles unseen words (including nonce words) by implicitly decomposing them into morphemic units, showcasing robust generalization.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Missing Dataset Specifications**: The paper omits explicit identification of the WMT test and development sets used (e.g., newstest2015, newstest2013). This omission impedes direct comparison with existing work (e.g., WMT benchmarks on matrix.statmt.org), forcing readers to infer details from the code README. Such critical information must be stated in the main text.  
2. **Inadequate Software Documentation**: The README provides functional but insufficient instructions for training and testing. Including concrete command examples (e.g., `./train.sh --src=fr --tgt=en`) would greatly improve usability. Additionally, the software lacks a `--help` flag, which is standard for user-facing tools.  
3. **Figure 2 Ambiguity**: The paper describes a "6-level architecture," yet Figure 2 depicts fewer than six layers. The caption must clarify whether the figure shows a subset of layers or contains an error. If full architectural details are omitted, a supplementary figure (e.g., in an appendix) should be provided.  
4. **Insufficient SOTA Context**: Results are compared only to character-based baselines but not to state-of-the-art (SOTA) systems (e.g., subword or word-level NMT). Reported BLEU scores fall notably below recent WMT benchmarks (e.g., 25.1 BLEU on newstest2015 vs. 32.1 for SOTA in WMT 2016). The authors should acknowledge this gap and discuss potential reasons.  
5. **Minor Language Issues**: The paper contains minor grammatical errors (e.g., "backing off to morphemes"), misspellings, and LaTeX inconsistencies (e.g., reversed quotation marks). These should be corrected in the final version.

General Discussion:  
This work makes a valuable contribution to character-based NMT by introducing a morpheme-aware architecture that bridges character and word-level modeling. The demonstrated ability to handle unseen words via morphemic decomposition is particularly promising. While the results do not yet reach SOTA levels, the approach offers a clear pathway for future improvements, especially through integration with stronger backbone models. The release of code and models substantially strengthens the paper’s impact. With the noted revisions—particularly clarifying datasets, enhancing documentation, resolving the architectural diagram, and contextualizing results against SOTA—the paper would be a strong fit for ACL 2017.
