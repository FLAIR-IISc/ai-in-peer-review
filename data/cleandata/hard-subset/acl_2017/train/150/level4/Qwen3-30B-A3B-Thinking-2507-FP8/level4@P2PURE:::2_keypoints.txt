- Strength: Paper is well-structured, clearly written, presents original ideas and interesting, easy-to-follow results.
- Weakness: Reviewer doubts interpretation of results and finds claims about learning morphology insufficiently supported.
- Contribution: Proposes DCNMT — a character-level NMT with deep word-encoding and deep word-decoding layers that map between characters and sub-word representations.
- Motivation: Aims to combine character-level benefits (small vocab, OOV handling) with shorter input sequences via intermediate sub-word representations.
- Claim: Authors assert the deep word-encoding model learns morphology better than state-of-the-art methods.
- Evaluation concern: Advantages over bpe2char are unclear due to minimal BLEU differences (0.04 Cs→En, 0.1 En→Cs) and no statistical significance testing.
- Training-time concern: DCNMT takes 8 more days than bpe2char for Cs→En; En→Cs training time not reported and bpe2char not evaluated for En→Fr, so comparisons are incomplete.
- Recommendation: Provide more comprehensive comparisons (statistical tests, missing training times, broader baselines) to validate claimed advantages.
- Morphology-claim concern: Section 5.2 uses illustrative examples only and lacks formal experiments or quantitative evaluation to support morphology-learning claims.
- Suggestion: Move the morphology example section to an appendix and temper claims, or expand into a separate paper with formal experiments.
- Acronyms: Many acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are undefined or inconsistently defined; define on first use and maintain consistency.
- Energy concept: “Energy” introduced in Sec. 3.1 is not re-explained in Sec. 5.2 where used repeatedly; clarify interpretation (e.g., high energy → morpheme boundary) and define “peak” in Figure 5.
- BPE capitalization: Inconsistent capitalization of BPE; maintain consistent usage after first definition.
- Unnecessary statement: The note that no monolingual corpus is used in Sec. 4.1 is unnecessary.
- Figure issue: Figure 4a appears to have missing color coding for energy values across characters.
- Table 1 issue: Results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) in Cs→En are reported though not present in original paper — authors must state if they reimplemented these results.
- Linguistic claim: Describing French as “morphologically poor” is inaccurate; it is less rich than Slavic languages like Czech but not morphologically poor.
- Data citation: WMT’15 training corpora link provided but WMT’14 link omitted — clarify why.
- References: Several references are incomplete and need fixing.
- Typos and wording errors: Multiple grammatical and typographical errors (list provided by reviewer) need correction for clarity.
