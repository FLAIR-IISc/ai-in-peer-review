

Strengths: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with original ideas and interesting results that are generally easy to follow.

Weaknesses: I have significant concerns about the interpretation of results and the empirical support for claims regarding the method’s ability to learn morphology.

General Discussion: This paper proposes a complex character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture extending the standard encoder-decoder framework. A deep word-encoding layer processes character inputs into sub-word representations, while a deep word-decoding layer converts target-language sub-word outputs back to character sequences. The authors argue this approach combines the benefits of character-level NMT (reduced vocabulary size, better handling of unseen words) with improved performance through intermediate sub-word representations that shorten input sequences. They further claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than state-of-the-art alternatives.

My primary concern is the evaluation methodology. The authors compare their DCNMT model against systems like BPE2Char using training time and BLEU score. However, the BLEU improvements are marginal (0.04 for Cs-En, 0.1 for En-Cs) without statistical significance testing—critical for determining if differences are meaningful. Training time comparisons are also incomplete: BPE2Char requires 8 fewer days for Cs-En, but En-Cs training times are omitted (why?), and En-Fr results for BPE2Char are absent. A more comprehensive comparison—including statistical validation and full training time data—is essential to substantiate DCNMT’s advantages.

My second concern involves Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate morphology learning. This section relies solely on illustrative examples and descriptive commentary, lacking any formal experiments or quantitative evaluation. While the examples are well-selected and clearly explained, the absence of empirical validation undermines the strong claims made. I recommend relocating this section to an appendix and tempering the morphology-related assertions in the main text. This part is intriguing and could form the basis of a separate paper, but it currently lacks the rigor expected for the current work.

Additional Comments:
- Define all acronyms upon first use (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE). Some are defined after initial usage.
- Clarify the "energy" concept (introduced in Section 3.1) when revisited in Section 5.2. Explain its interpretation (e.g., high energy indicating morpheme boundaries) and define "peak" (referenced in Figure 5).
- Standardize BPE casing: Use "BPE" consistently (not "bpe" after initial capitalization).
- The statement about "no monolingual corpus used" (Section 4.1) is unnecessary and could be omitted.
- Figure 4a’s color scheme for energy values appears inconsistent—ensure all characters have visible color coding.
- Table 1 reports Chung et al. (2016) results for Cs-En, but these are not from the original paper. If the authors computed them, this must be stated.
- Avoid stating French is "morphologically poor"; instead note it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
- Explain why WMT'15 training corpora are linked but WMT'14 is not.
- Correct incomplete references.
