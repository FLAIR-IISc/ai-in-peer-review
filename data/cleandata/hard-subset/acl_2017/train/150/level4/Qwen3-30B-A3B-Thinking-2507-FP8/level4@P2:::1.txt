

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses vocabulary limitations in word-based approaches by learning morphology through a novel architecture. The primary contributions are: (1) a morphology-aware word encoder using two recurrent networks to learn morpheme representations and their combinations, (2) a hierarchical decoder enabling efficient character-level translation without large vocabularies, and (3) empirical demonstration that the model handles out-of-vocabulary words and nonce words through morphological reasoning. The authors show the model achieves competitive BLEU scores with minimal training (one epoch) and better efficiency than comparable character-level models.

**Strengths**  
1. The model's morphology learning capability represents a significant contribution. The word encoder explicitly learns morpheme boundaries and their combinations, producing meaningful word representations that cluster semantically similar words (e.g., "reliable" and "reliability" are closer in embedding space than in standard RNN encoders). This enables the model to handle unseen words (e.g., "convenienter") by decomposing them into morphemes, a capability absent in word-based systems. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 compellingly demonstrate this advantage over standard approaches.

2. The training efficiency is impressive despite the model's complexity (six recurrent networks). The paper demonstrates DCNMT achieves higher BLEU scores after one training epoch than BPE-based models after full training. The hierarchical decoder avoids the computational bottlenecks of prior character-level approaches (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016), which required months of training. The model's compact size (54M parameters) and speed (training in ~7 days vs. ~90 days for comparable systems) make it practical for real-world use.

3. The authors release both code and trained models for three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), greatly enhancing reproducibility and enabling future research. This transparency is rare in NLP and significantly strengthens the paper's impact. The supplementary material provides additional examples of morphological generalization, further validating the approach.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper lacks explicit description of the WMT test and development sets used, which is critical for fair comparison. The reviewer had to infer from the code's README that newstest2015 was used for testing and newstest2013 for development. This should be clearly stated in the main text, as matrix.statmt.org and other benchmarks require this information for meaningful comparison.

2. The claimed 6-layer architecture conflicts with Figure 2, which appears to show fewer layers. The caption states "four layers" but the text describes six recurrent networks. The figure should be revised to explicitly show all six networks (or clarified that Figure 2 is a simplified schematic), as this discrepancy undermines confidence in the model's complexity claims.

3. The results fail to acknowledge state-of-the-art performance for other MT systems. While the paper compares to character-level models, it omits comparison to current word-level SOTA systems (e.g., Google's Transformer, which scores 39.8 BLEU on En-Fr). The paper's best result (30.56 BLEU) lags significantly behind these systems, which should be addressed to properly contextualize the contribution.

4. The software documentation is insufficient. The README lacks concrete examples for training and testing commands, and the software does not support a --help flag. This creates unnecessary barriers for reproducibility, despite the valuable code release.

5. Minor grammatical errors and LaTeX issues (e.g., reverse quotation marks, inconsistent spacing) detract from professionalism. While not critical, these should be corrected in the final version.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims "comparable to state-of-the-art systems" but does not specify which. Please clarify whether this refers to character-level or word-level SOTA, and provide the exact BLEU scores for the strongest baselines (e.g., Google's Transformer).

2. Figure 2 shows "four layers" but the text describes six recurrent networks. Please clarify the discrepancy and either revise the figure or explain why Figure 2 is a simplification.

3. The model's ability to handle nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") is impressive, but how does it perform on truly novel morphological structures (e.g., "unconvenienter")? Please provide additional examples if available.
