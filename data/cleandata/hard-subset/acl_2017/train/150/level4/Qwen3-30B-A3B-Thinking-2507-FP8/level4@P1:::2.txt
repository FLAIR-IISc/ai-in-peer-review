

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that learns morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The primary contributions are: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations without explicit segmentation, enabling meaningful word representations; (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates from word-level representations to character sequences, avoiding vocabulary bottlenecks while maintaining efficiency; and (3) empirical validation that the model learns morphology effectively, outperforming subword-level baselines in training efficiency and handling unseen words.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel architecture for character-level NMT**: The proposed DCNMT model elegantly addresses vocabulary size limitations by learning morphology implicitly rather than relying on external segmentation (e.g., BPE). This avoids the "out-of-vocabulary" problem while maintaining translation quality comparable to state-of-the-art systems. The hierarchical decoder efficiently bridges word-level and character-level representations, enabling end-to-end training without the inefficiencies noted in prior work (e.g., Ling et al., 2015b).  

2. **Training efficiency**: The model achieves competitive BLEU scores after just one training epoch, outperforming BPE-based systems (e.g., +1.3 BLEU on En-Fr) and training significantly faster than comparable character-level models (e.g., 7 days vs. 90 days for Luong and Manning, 2016). This efficiency stems from the model’s compact size (54M parameters) and the hierarchical decoding mechanism, which avoids the batch inefficiencies of prior approaches.  

3. **Morphology learning capability**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that the word encoder generates meaningful representations of morphologically related words (e.g., "notable" vs. "notability" in Fig. 3). The PCA projections and energy analysis (Fig. 4) show the model correctly identifies morpheme boundaries (e.g., "any" in "anyone" and "anyway"), suggesting it captures linguistic structure beyond simple character sequences. This is further validated by the model’s ability to translate nonce words like "convenienter" (Table 2), which would be impossible for word-level systems.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of statistical significance in BLEU comparisons**: The paper claims DCNMT outperforms baselines (e.g., +0.1 BLEU on En-Cs), but these differences are marginal and lack statistical significance testing. For instance, the 0.1 BLEU gain over bpe2char (Chung et al., 2016b) on En-Cs is negligible without confidence intervals or significance tests. The training time advantage (7 days vs. 15 days for DCNMT vs. bpe2char on Cs-En) is also underemphasized relative to the small BLEU gains.  

2. **Overstated morphology claims**: Section 5.2 asserts the model "learns morphology," but the evidence is limited to illustrative examples (Fig. 3–5) without formal evaluation. While the PCA and energy analyses are insightful, they do not quantify *how well* the model learns morphology compared to baselines (e.g., CNN-based encoders, Kim et al., 2016). The section should either include a controlled experiment (e.g., morphology-aware metrics) or be moved to an appendix to avoid overstating the contribution.  

3. **Incomplete evaluation of training time**: The paper omits training times for En-Cs (Table 1) and fails to compare DCNMT against bpe2char on En-Fr, which weakens the efficiency claims. For example, the 7-day training time for DCNMT on En-Fr is presented, but bpe2char’s time for the same task is not provided, making it impossible to assess relative efficiency.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Were statistical significance tests performed for BLEU differences between DCNMT and baselines? If not, please include them in the revision.  
2. How does the model’s morphology learning compare quantitatively to baselines (e.g., CNN word encoders)? Could you add a simple metric (e.g., morpheme boundary accuracy) to support your claims?  
3. Why is training time for En-Cs missing in Table 1? Please clarify or provide the missing data.  

**Additional Comments**  
- Define acronyms (e.g., HGRU, PCA, BPE) upon first use to improve clarity.  
- Clarify the "energy" concept in Section 5.2 (e.g., "High energy indicates morpheme boundaries").  
- Correct inconsistent capitalization of BPE (e.g., "BPE" vs. "bpe").  
- Avoid stating French is "morphologically poor"; instead, note it is less morphologically rich than Czech.  
- Fix minor typos (e.g., "coveres" → "covers," "uses" → "use" for Luong and Manning, 2016).  
- The reference to WMT'15 without a link for WMT'14 is inconsistent; add a link for both.
