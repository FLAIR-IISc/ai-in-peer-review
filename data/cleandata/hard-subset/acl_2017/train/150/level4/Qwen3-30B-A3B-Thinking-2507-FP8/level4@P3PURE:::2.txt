

Strengths: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a generally accessible exposition. The core ideas are original, and the obtained results are interesting.

Weaknesses: I have concerns regarding the interpretation of results. Specifically, some claims about the method's ability to learn morphology lack sufficient scientific evidence.

General Discussion:
This paper proposes a complex character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture. The approach extends a classical encoder-decoder framework by introducing a deep word-encoding layer that transforms character-level input into sub-word representations of the source sentence, and a deep word-decoding layer that converts target-language sub-word representations into the final character sequence. The objective is to leverage character-level NMT benefits (reduced vocabulary size, better handling of unseen words) while improving overall performance through intermediate sub-word representations that shorten the input character sequence. The authors additionally claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than state-of-the-art approaches.

I have significant concerns about the evaluation. The authors compare their method (DCNMT) to other state-of-the-art systems using training time and BLEU score. However, the BLEU score differences between DCNMT and bpe2char are minimal (0.04 in Cs-En, 0.1 in En-Cs), making it difficult to assert superiority without statistical significance testing. Has statistical significance been evaluated? Regarding training time, bpe2char requires 8 days less than DCNMT for Cs-En. Training time for En-Cs is not provided (why?), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison is needed to substantiate the claimed advantages of DCNMT.

My second concern pertains to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate their system's morphological learning capability. The section only presents illustrative examples and descriptive commentary, lacking any formal experiments or evaluation metrics to support these claims. While the examples are well-chosen and clearly explained, they do not constitute evidence. I encourage the authors to expand this section into a dedicated study, but for the current paper, I recommend moving it to an appendix and softening the claims about morphological learning capability, as this is not a critical component of the main contribution.

Other Comments:
- Several acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are used without definition or defined after initial use. Please define all acronyms upon first appearance for clarity.
- The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but not revisited in Section 5.2 (where it is used repeatedly). A brief refresh of this concept and its interpretation (e.g., high energy indicating a morpheme boundary) would be helpful. The term "peak" (Figure 5) is also undefined.
- BPE is capitalized upon first definition but later used in lowercase; please maintain consistent capitalization.
- The statement about no monolingual corpus being used in Section 4.1 appears unnecessary.
- Figure 4a exhibits an issue: energy color mapping is missing for some characters.
- Table 1 reports results for model (3) (Chung et al. 2016) in Cs-En. Since these results are not reported in the original paper, the authors should clarify if they computed them and mention this.
- The characterization of French as "morphologically poor" is inaccurate; it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
- A link is provided for WMT'15 training data but not for WMT'14; please provide consistency.
- Several references are incomplete.
