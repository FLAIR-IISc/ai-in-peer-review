

Summary: The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that introduces a novel word encoder capable of learning morphology and a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) the deep character-level architecture that avoids vocabulary bottlenecks while maintaining translation performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems; (2) the word encoder that effectively learns morphological structures, enabling better handling of unseen words and morphological variations; and (3) the training efficiency of the proposed model compared to other character-level approaches, despite its complexity.

Strengths: The paper presents a well-structured approach to character-level neural machine translation that addresses a significant limitation of current word-based systems (vocabulary bottleneck). The model architecture is clearly explained with sufficient technical detail. The experimental results demonstrate that the model achieves competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art systems, particularly on morphologically rich languages like Czech. The model outperforms baseline approaches when trained for just one epoch, suggesting potential training efficiency benefits. The analysis of morphology learning provides compelling visual evidence that the model learns meaningful representations of words with similar morphological structures.

Weaknesses: The paper does not provide statistical significance tests for the reported BLEU score differences, making it difficult to assess whether the small improvements (e.g., 0.1 BLEU points in En-Cs) are meaningful. The comparison with bpe2char shows minimal performance gains but longer training times, which undermines the claimed advantages of the DCNMT approach. The claims about the model's ability to learn morphology (Section 5.2) are largely based on illustrative examples without formal evaluation metrics or experiments to validate these claims quantitatively. The comparison with other state-of-the-art systems is incomplete, as training times for some language pairs are not provided (e.g., En-Cs), and the model comparison with bpe2char shows longer training times with only marginal performance gains.

Questions to Authors: Have statistical significance tests been performed for the reported BLEU score differences between DCNMT and other models? Could the authors provide more formal evaluation metrics to support their claims about morphology learning, beyond the illustrative examples shown in Section 5.2? Why are training times not provided for the En-Cs language pair, and how do they compare to the other language pairs? The paper claims French is morphologically poor; could the authors clarify this characterization, as French is often considered to have moderate morphological complexity compared to languages like Czech?
