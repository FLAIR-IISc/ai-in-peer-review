

Summary: The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that learns morphology through a novel architecture. The primary contribution is a hierarchical approach that encodes source sentences at the character level while learning subword representations through a specialized word encoder. This avoids vocabulary bottlenecks while maintaining competitive performance. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that the model can learn morphological patterns, which enables translation of misspelled or nonce words. The third contribution is the efficient training process, as the model achieves comparable BLEU scores after just one epoch.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant problem in NMT: vocabulary size limitations. By learning morphology rather than relying on subword tokenization (like BPE), the model eliminates the vocabulary bottleneck while maintaining competitive performance. The architecture is well-motivated and clearly explained, with the hierarchical decoder providing an elegant solution to character-level translation challenges.

2. The experimental results demonstrate that the model achieves competitive BLEU scores with significantly fewer training epochs than comparable approaches. The paper shows that the model outperforms BPE-based models after just one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks, which is a compelling efficiency advantage. The demonstration of translating misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") is particularly convincing evidence of the model's linguistic capabilities.

3. The analysis of morphology learning is insightful, particularly the PCA visualizations showing how words with similar morphological structures cluster together. The examples in Section 5.2 (e.g., "anyone" vs. "everything") effectively illustrate how the model captures morphological patterns. These findings suggest broader applicability beyond NMT to other language processing tasks.

Weaknesses:
1. The claimed advantages over state-of-the-art approaches are not sufficiently supported by evidence. The BLEU score differences between DCNMT and bpe2char are minimal (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and the paper does not provide statistical significance tests. Without this, it's difficult to claim superiority over competing approaches. The training time comparison is also incomplete, with missing data for En-Cs and inconsistent reporting of training days.

2. The claim that the model "learns morphology" lacks rigorous evaluation. Section 5.2 contains illustrative examples but no formal experiments or quantitative measures of morphological learning. The paper does not compare the morphological learning capability against alternative approaches or provide metrics to evaluate this claim. This weakens a central contribution of the paper.

3. The paper contains several clarity issues that hinder understanding. Many acronyms (RNN, BPE, HGRU, PCA) are used without definition or inconsistently defined. The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but not properly explained for later sections where it's used extensively. Figure 4a has missing color coding for energy values, making interpretation difficult. The paper also incorrectly states that French is "morphologically poor," which is inaccurate.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please provide statistical significance testing for the BLEU score comparisons between DCNMT and other models, especially for the small differences noted in Table 1.
2. How does the model's morphological learning capability compare quantitatively to other approaches (e.g., CNN word encoders)? Can you provide metrics or experiments to support the claim that your model "learns morphology"?
3. Why was the training time for En-Cs not reported in Table 1, and why is the training time comparison incomplete for En-Fr?
4. Could you clarify the role of "energy" in the word encoder and how it relates to morpheme boundaries? The concept is crucial to the paper's claims but lacks sufficient explanation.

Additional Comments:
- Please define all acronyms upon first use (RNN, BPE, HGRU, etc.).
- Address the inconsistent capitalization of BPE (sometimes capitalized, sometimes not).
- Correct the statement about French being "morphologically poor" - it's more accurate to say it's less morphologically rich than languages like Czech.
- Fix the figure issues (missing energy color coding in Figure 4a, unclear description of "peak" in Figure 5).
- Clarify why no monolingual corpus was used (this is not necessary to mention).
- Ensure consistent use of "the" in phrases like "the only difference between CNMT and DCNMT is CNMT" (should be "is that CNMT").
- Correct typos: "coveres" → "covers," "both consists" → "both consist," "uses" → "use" (Luong and Manning 2016).
