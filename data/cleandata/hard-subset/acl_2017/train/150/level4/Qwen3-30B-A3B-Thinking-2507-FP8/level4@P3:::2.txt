

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that learns morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors claim three key contributions: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes by using two RNNs to weight character-level representations, (2) a hierarchical decoder that generates target characters conditioned on word-level representations, and (3) empirical evidence that the model captures morphological structure. As I see them, the strongest contribution is the architecture itself (particularly the word encoder), followed by the efficient training performance, with the morphological learning claims being the weakest due to insufficient evidence.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel architecture for morphology learning**: The word encoder (with two RNNs) is a genuinely new approach to handling morphology in NMT. Unlike BPE-based methods, it learns subword units implicitly rather than relying on heuristics. The design avoids the large vocabulary bottleneck while maintaining a compact model (54M parameters), and the empirical results (Table 1) show that training for just one epoch yields competitive BLEU scores (28.13 on En-Fr vs. 29.31 for the final model), suggesting faster convergence than competing character-level models.

2. **Efficiency in training**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that their model trains faster than alternatives. While Luong and Manning (2016) required 3 months for a +0.5 BLEU improvement, DCNMT achieves comparable results in 1 day (En-Fr) or 5 days (En-Cs), with a significantly smaller model size. This is a practical contribution for resource-constrained settings.

3. **Intuitive morphological analysis**: The qualitative analysis in Section 5.2 (Figures 3-5) provides compelling visual evidence that the model produces meaningful word representations. The PCA projections (Figure 3) show that words with similar morphemes cluster together, and the energy analysis (Figure 4) demonstrates that the model identifies morpheme boundaries (e.g., "any" in "anyone" and "anyway"). This supports the core idea of morphology learning, even if formal evaluation is lacking.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient evidence for morphology claims**: The paper’s strongest claim—that the model "learns morphology"—is not rigorously supported. Section 5.2 relies solely on qualitative examples (Figures 3-5) without quantitative metrics (e.g., morpheme boundary detection accuracy, analogy tests, or comparison to explicit morphology tools). Without statistical validation, the claim remains speculative. The authors should either provide rigorous evaluation or temper their assertions.

2. **Incomplete and ambiguous comparison**: The BLEU comparisons (Table 1) are misleading. For Cs-En, DCNMT’s final BLEU (16.96) is only 0.04 higher than bpe2char (16.92), yet the paper presents this as a significant advantage. Crucially, no statistical significance tests are reported. Additionally, training times are incomplete (En-Cs time is missing), and bpe2char is not evaluated on En-Fr despite being a key baseline. This undermines the claim of "efficiency" and "superior performance."

3. **Overstated novelty**: The model’s novelty is overstated. The hierarchical decoder resembles Ling et al. (2015b) and Luong & Manning (2016), while the word encoder is a minor variation of RNN-based word representations (e.g., Cho et al., 2014). The paper should more clearly distinguish its contributions from prior work.

4. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent capitalization for acronyms (e.g., "BPE" vs. "bpe") and omits definitions for key terms (HGRU, PCA, energy) on first use. This reduces clarity for readers outside the immediate NLP community.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do you statistically validate that the BLEU improvements over baselines (e.g., +0.04 in Cs-En) are significant?  
2. Why is the training time for En-Cs omitted in Table 1, and how does DCNMT compare to bpe2char on En-Fr?  
3. Can you provide quantitative evidence (beyond visual examples) that the model learns morphology, such as a morpheme boundary detection accuracy score or a comparison to a linguistic analysis tool?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The claim that French is "morphologically poor" is inaccurate; it is moderately inflected. Czech is richer, but French is not "poor."  
- Typos: "coveres" → "covers," "consists" → "consist," "uses" → "use" (Luong & Manning 2016).  
- The paper should define "energy" and "peak" (Figure 5) earlier to avoid confusion.  
- The analysis of "convenienter" (Table 2) is a strong example but should be framed as a qualitative observation rather than evidence of "learning morphology."
