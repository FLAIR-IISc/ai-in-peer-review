

The paper presents a character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture extending a standard encoder-decoder with deep word-encoding and decoding layers. This approach aims to leverage character-level benefits (vocabulary size reduction, handling out-of-vocabulary words) while improving performance via intermediate sub-word representations. The core contribution lies in the claim that the proposed deep word-encoding model better learns morphology compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible. The technical approach is original, and the results demonstrate interesting potential for character-level NMT. The examples illustrating morphology learning in Section 5.2 are particularly well-chosen and didactic.

**Major Weaknesses:**  
The primary concern lies in the evaluation and the evidence supporting the paper’s key claims. The comparison with baselines (notably bpe2char) is insufficiently rigorous. The marginal BLEU gains (0.04 for Cs-En, 0.1 for En-Cs) lack statistical significance testing, making it impossible to determine if these differences are meaningful. Crucially, the training time advantage claimed for DCNMT is undermined: bpe2char requires 8 days *less* training time for Cs-En, while En-Cs training time for DCNMT is omitted (please explain why), and bpe2char is not evaluated for En-Fr. This incomplete comparison fails to demonstrate a clear advantage for DCNMT. The authors must provide a more comprehensive analysis, including statistical significance and full training time comparisons, to justify their claims.

A second major weakness is the unsupported claim that DCNMT learns morphology better than other approaches. Section 5.2 presents illustrative examples and commentary but lacks any formal evaluation or empirical validation. The absence of metrics, ablation studies, or comparative experiments (e.g., comparing morphological accuracy against baselines) renders the claim speculative. While the examples are compelling, they are insufficient evidence. The authors should either provide rigorous experimental support for this claim or significantly temper the language (e.g., "suggests potential for morphology learning" instead of "learns morphology better"). Given the current evidence, this section should be moved to an appendix, as it is not critical to the core contribution of the NMT architecture.

**Minor Issues & Suggestions:**  
*   **Clarity & Consistency:** Define all acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) upon first use. The inconsistent capitalization of "BPE" (defined uppercase, used lowercase later) needs correction. The phrase "French is morphologically poor" should be revised to "French is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech" for accuracy. The reference to "no monolingual corpus" in Section 4.1 is unnecessary.  
*   **Figure & Table Issues:** Figure 4a’s energy color mapping is incomplete for all characters. Table 1’s model (3) results for Cs-En appear to be computed by the authors (not from the original paper) and should be explicitly stated. The WMT'15 corpus link is provided but not WMT'14 (please clarify the reason).  
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The term "energy" (introduced in Section 3.1) is used extensively in Section 5.2 without refresh. A brief clarification (e.g., "high energy indicates a likely morpheme boundary") would improve understanding. The concept of a "peak" in Figure 5 requires explicit definition.  
*   **Typos & Grammar:** Correct grammatical errors: "corpora provided" (plural), "Luong and Manning (2016) use", "HGRU (it is", "coveres" → "covers", "both consists" → "both consist", "the only difference... is CNMT" → "the only difference... is that CNMT".  
*   **References:** Ensure all references are complete.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting architectural extension to character-level NMT with a clear motivation. However, the evaluation is flawed due to the lack of statistical significance for BLEU gains and incomplete training time comparisons, and the key claim about superior morphology learning lacks empirical support. Addressing these core weaknesses through rigorous evaluation and revised claims is essential for publication. The current evidence does not justify the stated advantages of DCNMT over baselines like bpe2char. While the morphology examples are valuable, they require formal validation to support the claim or should be relegated to an appendix. The minor issues (acronyms, figures, typos) are easily fixable but do not overshadow the major evaluation concerns.

**Summary:** The core architecture is novel and well-motivated, but the evidence for its advantages and its claimed morphology learning capability is insufficient. The evaluation must be strengthened with statistical significance testing, complete comparisons (including training times), and empirical validation of the morphology claims. Without these, the paper's main contributions are not convincingly supported. The authors should prioritize addressing these critical weaknesses before publication.
