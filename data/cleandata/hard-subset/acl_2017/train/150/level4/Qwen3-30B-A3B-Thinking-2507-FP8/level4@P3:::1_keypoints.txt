- Proposes a deep character-level NMT with a morpheme-based word encoder, hierarchical character-level decoder, and empirical evidence of learned morphology and handling of unseen words.  
- Morpheme-based word encoder uses two recurrent networks to learn meaningful word representations without a fixed vocabulary.  
- Hierarchical decoder translates at character level while maintaining training efficiency.  
- Shows empirical evidence that the model captures morphological patterns and handles unseen/misspelled words effectively.  
- Strength: PCA visualizations and nonce-word translations indicate the encoder clusters morphologically related words better than standard embeddings.  
- Strength: Achieves competitive BLEU scores quickly (notably after one epoch), claiming faster training than prior character-level models.  
- Strength: Naturally handles OOVs by generating character-level outputs, avoiding <unk> tokens and producing morphologically valid words.  
- Weakness: Fails to state which WMT test/dev sets were used, harming reproducibility and comparison.  
- Weakness: Omits comparisons to strong word-level baselines (e.g., BPE models with ~30.56 BLEU), making "state-of-the-art" claims unclear.  
- Weakness: Figure/description of a "6-layer" architecture is misleading or unclear and needs explicit mapping of networks to the diagram.  
- Weakness: Minor issues (grammar, LaTeX formatting, unclear phrasing) that detract from professionalism.  
- Question: Clarify the reported BLEU discrepancy—one-epoch DCNMT score appears lower than BPE's final score.  
- Question: Provide direct comparison to best-performing word-level systems (e.g., BPE’s En–Fr results).  
- Question: How does learned morphology compare to explicitly segmented/morphological-analyzer-based models?  
- Additional: Recommend comparing OOV handling against strong word-level baselines, provide clearer context for training-efficiency claims, and expand README with training/inference examples.
