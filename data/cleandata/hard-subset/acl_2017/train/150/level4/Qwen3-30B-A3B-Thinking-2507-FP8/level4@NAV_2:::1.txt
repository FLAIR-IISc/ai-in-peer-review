

Strengths
The paper introduces a novel character-level neural machine translation architecture that integrates morphological learning through a dual-recurrent word encoder and hierarchical decoder, offering a principled solution to vocabulary bottlenecks. This approach demonstrates strong empirical performance, achieving competitive BLEU scores after just one training epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks—surpassing BPE-based baselines and matching state-of-the-art character-level models. The model’s ability to implicitly learn morphemes enables robust handling of unseen words (including nonce words like "convenienter") and misspellings, as validated through extensive analysis and sample translations.

Weaknesses
The paper lacks explicit specification of the WMT test and development sets used (newstest2015 and newstest2013), which is essential for reproducibility and fair comparison with established results on matrix.statmt.org. The software documentation, while functional, requires clearer examples for training and testing commands and should implement a --help flag for user guidance. The claim of a "six-layer architecture" conflicts with Figure 2, which visually depicts fewer layers; the figure caption must clarify this discrepancy or provide a complete architectural diagram. Crucially, the results omit comparison to leading word-based neural MT systems (e.g., those reporting >30 BLEU on En-Fr), making it difficult to assess relative progress against the current state-of-the-art. Minor grammatical errors and LaTeX inconsistencies (e.g., reverse quotation marks) require correction.

General Discussion
This work makes a meaningful contribution to character-level neural machine translation by demonstrating that morphology-aware encoding enables both high performance and efficient training, while resolving out-of-vocabulary challenges inherent in word-level approaches.
