

Strengths: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with original ideas and interesting results that are generally easy to follow.

Weaknesses: I have reservations regarding the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, certain claims about the method's ability to learn morphology lack sufficient scientific evidence to support them.

General Discussion:
This paper proposes a complex character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture. The approach extends a standard encoder-decoder framework by introducing a deep word-encoding layer that transforms character-level input into sub-word representations of the source sentence. Similarly, a deep word-decoding layer is added to the output to convert target-language sub-word representations into the final character sequence. The objective is to leverage the benefits of character-level NMT (reduced vocabulary size and improved handling of unseen words) while enhancing overall system performance through an intermediate sub-word representation that reduces the input sequence length. The authors additionally claim their deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than state-of-the-art approaches.

I have concerns about the evaluation. The authors compare their method (DCNMT) to other state-of-the-art systems using training time and BLEU score. However, the advantage of DCNMT over approaches like bpe2char is unclear. The BLEU score differences are minimal (0.04 in Cs-En and 0.1 in En-Cs), and without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to determine if one model outperforms the other. Regarding training time, bpe2char requires 8 fewer days than DCNMT for Cs-En. Training time for En-Cs is not provided (please explain why), and bpe2char was not evaluated for En-Fr. A more comprehensive comparison is needed to validate the proposed model's advantages.

My second concern pertains to Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate their system's morphology learning capability. This section only provides examples and descriptive comments, lacking any formal experiments or evaluation to support the claims. While the examples are well-chosen and explained clearly, the absence of empirical evidence is problematic. I recommend expanding this section into a separate paper due to its potential, but for the current work, I suggest moving it to an appendix and tempering the claims about morphology learning capabilities.

Other Comments:
- Many acronyms (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) are used without prior definition or defined after first use. Please define all acronyms upon first appearance to improve clarity, even if common in deep learning.
- The concept of "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but not revisited in Section 5.2 (where it is used repeatedly). A brief refresh of the concept and its interpretation (e.g., high energy indicating a morpheme boundary) would be helpful. The concept of "peak" in Figure 5 is also undefined.
- The acronym BPE is capitalized upon first definition but later used in lowercase; please maintain consistent capitalization.
- The statement that no monolingual corpus is used in Section 4.1 seems unnecessary.
- Figure 4a appears to have missing color coding for energy values across all characters.
- Table 1 reports results for model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) in Cs-En. Since these results are not reported in the original paper, if the authors computed them, they must state this explicitly.
- The description of French as "morphologically poor" is inaccurate; it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
- A link is provided for WMT'15 training corpora but not for WMT'14; please clarify the reason.
- Several references are incomplete.
