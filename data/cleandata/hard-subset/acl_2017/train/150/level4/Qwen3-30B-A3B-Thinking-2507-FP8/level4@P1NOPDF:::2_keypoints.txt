- Strength: Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and presents original ideas with interesting, easy-to-follow results.
- Strength: Proposes a complex character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) extending encoder-decoder with deep word-encoding and deep word-decoding layers.
- Claim: DCNMT combines character-level benefits (small vocab, OOV handling) with intermediate sub-word representations to shorten input sequences and improve performance.
- Claim: Authors assert the deep word-encoding model learns morphology more effectively than state-of-the-art alternatives.
- Major concern: Evaluation methodology is weak — reported BLEU improvements are marginal (0.04 Cs→En, 0.1 En→Cs) with no statistical significance testing.
- Major concern: Training time comparisons are incomplete or inconsistent (missing En→Cs times, missing BPE2Char En→Fr results); full training-time data needed.
- Recommendation: Provide comprehensive comparisons including statistical validation and complete timing data to substantiate DCNMT’s advantages.
- Concern about morphology claims: Section 5.2 relies solely on illustrative examples and descriptive commentary without formal experiments or quantitative evaluation.
- Recommendation: Move exploratory morphology analysis to an appendix and temper morphology-learning claims in the main text or perform rigorous empirical validation.
- Comment: Define all acronyms upon first use (e.g., LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) — some are defined after initial usage.
- Comment: Clarify the "energy" concept from Section 3.1 when revisited in 5.2, explain interpretation (e.g., energy indicating morpheme boundaries) and define "peak" (Figure 5).
- Comment: Standardize BPE casing to "BPE" consistently.
- Comment: Omit the unnecessary statement about "no monolingual corpus used" in Section 4.1.
- Comment: Fix Figure 4a color scheme so all characters have visible energy color coding.
- Comment: Table 1 reports Chung et al. (2016) Cs→En results not from the original paper—state if the authors computed them.
- Comment: Avoid calling French "morphologically poor"; instead say it is less morphologically rich than Slavic languages like Czech.
- Comment: Explain why WMT'15 training corpora are linked but WMT'14 is not, and correct incomplete references.
- Typos to fix: several examples given (e.g., "is the bilingual, parallel corpora provided" → "are the bilingual, parallel corpora provided"; "Luong and Manning (2016) uses" → "use"; "coveres" → "covers"; others listed).
