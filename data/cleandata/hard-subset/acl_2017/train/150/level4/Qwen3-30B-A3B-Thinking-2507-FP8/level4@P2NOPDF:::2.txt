

Strengths: The paper presents a well-structured, clear exposition of an original approach to character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The core ideas are compelling, and the results demonstrate interesting potential for the proposed architecture.

Weaknesses: Two key concerns require attention. First, the interpretation of results lacks sufficient rigor, particularly regarding the claimed advantages of the method. Second, assertions about the model's superior ability to learn morphology are not adequately supported by empirical evidence.

General Discussion: The paper introduces DCNMT, a character-level NMT architecture extending a standard encoder-decoder framework with deep word-encoding and word-decoding layers. These layers convert character sequences into sub-word representations (input) and back to character sequences (output), aiming to combine character-level benefits (vocabulary size reduction, handling unseen words) with improved performance via intermediate sub-word representations. The authors further claim DCNMT better learns morphology than state-of-the-art methods.

My primary concern is the evaluation methodology. The comparison with systems like bpe2char focuses solely on BLEU score and training time, yet the reported BLEU differences are minimal (0.04 for Cs-En, 0.1 for En-Cs) without statistical significance testing. Without this, it is unjustified to claim superiority. Regarding training time, bpe2char requires 8 fewer days for Cs-En, while En-Cs training time is omitted (justified?); En-Fr results for bpe2char are also absent. A more comprehensive comparison, including statistical validation and full training time data, is essential to substantiate the proposed model's advantages.

A second major issue is Section 5.2, where the authors claim to investigate morphology learning. This section relies solely on illustrative examples and descriptive commentary without any formal evaluation (e.g., morpheme segmentation accuracy, ablative studies). While the examples are well-chosen and explained, the absence of empirical validation weakens the claims. This section holds significant potential as a standalone contribution but currently lacks the necessary evidence for the current paper. I recommend either: (1) conducting rigorous morphology experiments to support the claims, or (2) moving the section to an appendix and softening the assertions about morphology learning capability.

Additional Comments:
- **Acronyms**: Define all acronyms (LSTM, HGRU, CNN, PCA, RNN, BPE) upon first use. For instance, "RNN" is introduced before definition.
- **Energy Concept**: The term "energy" is introduced in Section 3.1 but reused in Section 5.2 without refresh. Clarify its interpretation (e.g., "high energy at a character indicates morpheme splitting") and define "peak" (Figure 5).
- **BPE Capitalization**: Inconsistent capitalization (e.g., "BPE" vs. "bpe")—standardize throughout.
- **Monolingual Corpus**: The statement "no monolingual corpus is used" (Section 4.1) is unnecessary; this is standard practice.
- **Figure 4a**: Energy color mapping is incomplete—ensure all characters have corresponding color values.
- **Table 1**: Model (3) (Chung et al., 2016) results for Cs-En are not from the original paper. If re-computed, explicitly state this.
- **Morphological Complexity**: Avoid stating "French is morphologically poor." Instead, note "French exhibits less morphological complexity than Slavic languages like Czech."
- **WMT References**: Provide a link for WMT'14 training corpora to match WMT'15.
- **References**: Correct incomplete citations.
