

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that learns morphology through a novel architecture. The primary contributions are: (1) a morpheme-based word encoder using two recurrent networks to learn meaningful word representations without a fixed vocabulary, (2) a hierarchical decoder that translates at character level while maintaining training efficiency, and (3) empirical evidence that the model learns morphological patterns and handles unseen words effectively. Unlike prior work, the authors avoid vocabulary bottlenecks while achieving competitive performance with fewer training epochs.

**Strengths**  
1. **Effective morphological learning**: The word encoder produces semantically meaningful representations, as demonstrated by PCA visualizations (Figure 3) showing that morphologically related words cluster together. This is a significant advancement over standard word embeddings, which fail to capture such relationships (e.g., "notable" and "notability" are separated in ordinary RNN encoders but grouped in the authors' model). The model's ability to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" (Table 2) further validates this contribution.  

2. **Training efficiency**: The model achieves competitive BLEU scores after just one epoch (e.g., 28.13 on En-Fr vs. 29.38 for BPE-based models trained for 50 epochs), outperforming prior character-level models that require months of training (e.g., Luong & Manning, 2016). This efficiency stems from the hierarchical decoder's design, which avoids the slow training typical of character-level approaches.  

3. **Practical handling of OOV words**: The model inherently solves the out-of-vocabulary problem by translating at character level. It correctly handles misspelled words (Table 2a) and generates morphologically valid nonce words (Table 2b), a capability absent in word-level systems that map such words to <unk>. This demonstrates a meaningful practical advantage over existing NMT approaches.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of clear experimental details**: The paper fails to explicitly state which WMT test and development sets were used (newstest2015 and newstest2013), requiring readers to consult supplementary code. This omission impedes reproducibility and comparison with other systems (e.g., on matrix.statmt.org). The authors should clarify this in the main text.  

2. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art**: The results (Table 1) compare only to character-level models but omit state-of-the-art word-level baselines (e.g., BPE models achieving 30.56 BLEU on En-Fr). Given that the paper claims "comparable to state-of-the-art," the authors must address how their model stacks up against the best-performing systems on these datasets.  

3. **Misleading architecture description**: Figure 2 claims a "6-layer" model but appears to show fewer layers. The caption should explicitly state how the six networks map to the diagram, or a supplementary figure should clarify the architecture. This confusion undermines the paper's technical claims.  

4. **Minor but distracting issues**: The paper contains grammatical errors (e.g., "it is possible to use" instead of "it is possible to use"), inconsistent LaTeX formatting (e.g., reverse quotation marks), and unclear phrasing (e.g., "the number of outputs of the first-level decoder is much fewer"). These should be corrected for professionalism.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims the model "obtains a higher BLEU score than the BPE-based model after training for one epoch." However, Table 1 shows DCNMT's one-epoch score (27.02) is lower than BPE's final score (29.70). Please clarify this discrepancy.  

2. The model is compared to character-level systems but not to strong word-level baselines (e.g., BPE models). How does DCNMT compare to the best-performing word-level systems (e.g., 30.56 BLEU for En-Fr)?  

3. The PCA visualizations (Figure 3) suggest the word encoder learns morphology, but how does this compare to explicitly segmented morphological models (e.g., those using morphological analyzers)?  

**Additional Comments**  
The authors should address the OOV handling claim by comparing to a strong word-level baseline (e.g., BPE). The training efficiency claim is compelling but needs stronger context regarding state-of-the-art. The code release is a major strength, but the README should include concrete examples of training and inference commands.
