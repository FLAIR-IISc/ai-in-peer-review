## Review for ACL: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the open vocabulary problem. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two RNNs, coupled with a hierarchical decoder. The authors claim their model achieves competitive translation performance across multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) and is more efficient to train than previous character-level approaches. They also highlight the model's ability to learn morphological structure and handle misspelled/nonce words.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:**  The proposed architecture combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a hierarchical decoder is a reasonable and well-motivated approach to tackling the challenges of character-level translation, specifically length and training instability.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:**  The design of the word encoder, utilizing two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combinations, is a novel and potentially insightful way to incorporate morphological information into the NMT process.
3. **Demonstrated Performance:** Showing competitive BLEU scores on multiple translation tasks, especially morphologically rich languages like Czech, validates the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.


**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core NMT Problem:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem, a significant limitation of word-level NMT, and proposes a viable solution based on character-level modeling.
2. **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture is logically structured and justified. The authors clearly explain the motivations behind each component, linking it back to the challenges of character-level translation. The introduction does a good job of setting the stage.
3. **Strong Experimental Validation:**  The model is evaluated on multiple language pairs, including a morphologically rich language (Czech).  The quantitative results (BLEU scores) demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance, comparable to state-of-the-art approaches.  The comparison with bpe-based methods is also valuable.
4. **Interesting Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides compelling evidence that the word encoder is indeed learning meaningful representations related to morphemes. The authors’ analysis of the energy weights within the encoder (Figure 4) further supports this claim.
5. **Practical Advantage: Handling Misspellings/Nonce Words:** The demonstrated ability to translate misspelled words or nonce words is a practical benefit of character-level modeling and a notable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of components is sensible, the individual components themselves (RNNs, BiRNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are all well-established techniques in NMT. The novelty lies primarily in *how* these components are combined, rather than the components themselves.  The paper needs to better articulate *where* the significant innovation resides.
2. **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:**  The paper lacks a thorough ablation study to dissect the contribution of each component within the proposed architecture. For example, how much does the morphology-aware word encoder *specifically* contribute to performance compared to a simpler word encoder? Without this, it’s hard to assess the true value of the proposed approach.
3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper mentions efficiency, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational cost (training time, memory usage) with other methods, especially BPE. A more rigorous analysis of the trade-offs between performance and computational efficiency would be valuable.  The statements about training days seem a bit vague.
4. **Presentation could be more Concise:** The introduction is a bit lengthy and could be streamlined. Some explanations feel repetitive.


**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ablation Study:** Could you provide an ablation study comparing the performance of your full model to variants *without* the morphology-aware word encoder, and with a simpler word encoder (e.g., a standard embedding layer)?
2. **Computational Cost:** Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the training time and memory usage of your model compared to BPE-based NMT?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive is your model's performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., RNN hidden size, learning rate)?
4. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative error analysis of your model’s translations? What types of errors does it still make, and are they related to morphological complexity or other factors?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The figures are well-designed and help to illustrate the model architecture and analysis.
*  The reference list is comprehensive.
*  Minor edits for clarity and conciseness would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of character-level NMT. The proposed architecture is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While the novelty of the individual components may be limited, the combination and the analysis of morphological learning are valuable. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (specifically, the lack of ablation studies and detailed computational cost analysis) would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
