This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level NMT, particularly the OOV problem and the difficulty of training character-level models. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture with a novel word encoder that learns morphological structure and a hierarchical decoder that generates translations character by character. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hierarchical Character-Level NMT Architecture:** The most significant contribution is the proposed hierarchical architecture integrating a morphology-aware word encoder and a character-level decoder. This allows for effective character-level translation without the computational burden of purely character-based models.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** The design of the word encoder, utilizing two RNNs to learn representations of preceding characters and their weights, is a key innovation. This effectively captures morphological information, leading to improved handling of unseen words and potentially better generalization.
3. **Efficient Character-Level Decoding:** The HGRU-based hierarchical decoder, with its state-setting mechanism and unfolding strategy, enables efficient training of a deep character-level model, overcoming the challenges associated with long sequences and computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Problem:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem in NMT, a persistent challenge that impacts translation quality, especially for morphologically rich languages. The character-level approach inherently mitigates this issue.
2. **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture is a logical and well-motivated approach to combining the benefits of word-level and character-level modeling. The design choices, such as the two RNNs in the word encoder and the HGRU decoder, appear carefully considered.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art character-level and subword-level NMT models. The improvements on En-Cs and Cs-En, where morphology plays a larger role, are particularly encouraging.
4. **Clear Explanation and Analysis:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the model architecture and training procedure. The analysis regarding learning morphology, including the PCA visualizations and discussion of weight distributions, provides valuable insights.
5. **Demonstration of Morphology Learning:** The examples in Section 5.2, showing how the model learns to represent morphemes and handles morphological variations (e.g., "convenienter"), provide compelling evidence of the model's ability to capture morphological information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, the individual components themselves (RNNs, BiRNNs, GRUs) are well-established in the field. The novelty lies primarily in their specific arrangement and application within the hierarchical architecture. A stronger statement of the distinctiveness of the HGRU unit, beyond being a modified GRU, would be beneficial.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions some hyperparameter tuning but lacks a detailed discussion of sensitivity analysis. It's unclear how robust the model's performance is to variations in hyperparameters, which could be important for reproducibility and generalization.
3. **Ablation Studies are Limited:** The paper does not include extensive ablation studies to isolate the contributions of individual components. For example, it would be valuable to assess the impact of removing the morphological weighting mechanism in the word encoder or replacing the HGRU with a standard GRU.
4. **Qualitative Error Analysis is Missing:** While the examples in Section 5.3 showcase the model's ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words, a broader qualitative error analysis would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to bpe-based models is useful, but comparing against more recent and powerful subword segmentation techniques (e.g., SentencePiece) might strengthen the evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of your model compared to other character-level NMT approaches, specifically addressing the memory requirements and training time?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of the character set size (e.g., the 120 characters used in the experiments)?
3.  What is the impact of the tanh activation function used for normalization in the word encoder compared to other normalization methods (e.g., softmax)?
4.  Could you provide examples of cases where the model fails to correctly translate a word due to its morphological complexity? This would help understand the limits of the approach.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 1 is a bit dense and could be improved for readability.
*   The reference list appears incomplete (e.g. missing Sennrich et al., 2016).
*   The link to Google Translate in Table 2 is a time-sensitive comparison, and the results may change over time.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of character-level NMT. The proposed hierarchical architecture, with its morphology-aware word encoder and efficient decoder, addresses a significant challenge and achieves competitive performance. While the individual components are not entirely novel, their combination and application within this framework are well-motivated and effective. Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides convincing evidence of the model's capabilities. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
