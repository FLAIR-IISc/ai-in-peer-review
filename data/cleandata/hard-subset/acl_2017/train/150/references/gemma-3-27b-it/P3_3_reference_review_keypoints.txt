- Proposes a hierarchical deep character-level NMT with a dual-RNN word encoder and character-level hierarchical decoder to learn subword units and handle OOVs.
- Shows competitive BLEU results on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En and presents evidence the model captures morphological information.
- Novel combination: hierarchical architecture and dual-RNN encoder that learns morpheme-like subword representations without pre-defined segmentation.
- Demonstrates the model’s ability to handle morphology quantitatively and qualitatively (BLEU, PCA visualization, example translations).
- Successfully trains a deep character-level model (six RNNs) and introduces an efficient hierarchical decoder (HGRU).
- Addresses the OOV problem and offers an alternative to word-level and BPE-based subword methods.
- Clear motivation and well-explained design with helpful figures illustrating the architecture.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly on morphologically rich language pairs.
- Compelling analyses showing handling of misspellings and nonce words, supporting learned morphological representations.
- Efficient implementation mitigates common computational concerns of character-level models.
- Many individual components (RNNs, attention, GRUs) are standard; novelty is primarily in their arrangement.
- Comparisons with strong baselines, including recent BPE or transformer-based models, are insufficient.
- Lacks thorough ablation studies to quantify each component’s contribution (e.g., dual-RNN vs single RNN or embedding lookup).
- Missing analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (RNN sizes, layers, learning rates) to assess robustness.
- Claim of “learning morphology” is somewhat overstated; PCA and examples are suggestive but not definitive.
- Requests clearer differentiation from prior character-level NMT work and explanation of how limitations are overcome.
- Asks for ablation experiments to measure performance impact of encoder components.
- Requests reporting of hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness of results.
- Suggests more quantitative morphological evaluations (representation similarity, probing tasks).
- Wants detailed computational cost comparisons (training/inference time, memory) versus other models.
- Notes writing needs minor proofreading, recommends discussing limitations and future work, and points out incomplete references (e.g., Firat et al.).
- Overall recommendation: solid, well-motivated contribution with promising results; accept with minor revisions pending stronger comparisons, ablations, and morphological evaluation.
