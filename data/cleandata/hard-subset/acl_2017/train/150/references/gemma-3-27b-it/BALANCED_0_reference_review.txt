This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model aiming to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the OOV problem and the difficulty of capturing morphology. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder based on recurrent neural networks to learn subword units (morphemes) and a hierarchical decoder.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed architecture that combines character-level modeling with a learned morphological structure, effectively bridging the gap between purely word-based and character-based NMT. The method for learning subword units without relying on pre-defined segmentation algorithms like BPE is also a valuable contribution. Finally, the comprehensive experiments on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) and comparison with existing models provide strong empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture with the RNN-based word encoder and character-level decoder is a well-motivated and innovative approach. The decomposition of word representation learning into morpheme representation and combination rules is a key strength. This allows the model to handle OOV words and capture morphological similarities effectively.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments on three different language pairs demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed model. The model achieves competitive results compared to state-of-the-art NMT models, including character-level and subword-level approaches.  The inclusion of morphologically rich languages (Czech) is particularly valuable.
3. **Learning Morphology:** The analysis presented (Figure 3 & 4, PCA projection & energy analysis) provides compelling evidence that the model indeed learns morphological information. The visualization of learned representations and energy weights supports the claim that the model captures meaningful subword units.  The ability to translate "convenienter" correctly serves as a concrete example.
4. **Efficiency:** Despite the complexity of the architecture (six RNNs), the paper reports reasonable training times and a relatively small model size compared to other character-level models. The efficient hierarchical decoder design contributes to this efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study. For example, it would be beneficial to analyze the performance of the model without the BiRNN in the word encoder, or with a simpler word encoder (e.g., a lookup table). This would help isolate the contribution of each component and provide more insight into the model’s behavior.
2. **Decoder Detail:** The description of the HGRU decoder, while presented, is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanation. Specifically, the "unfolding" process using the matrix R is not immediately clear and requires careful reading to understand. A visual illustration of this process alongside Figure 2 would be helpful.
3. **Comparison Baselines:** The comparison against other character-level models is somewhat limited. While the paper compares to Luong & Manning (2016), it would be helpful to include results from more recent and competitive character-level NMT models.  Also, the reported BLEU scores for some baselines are simply stated without citation to the exact experimental setup, making direct comparison challenging.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of various hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensions, RNN hidden sizes) on performance is not discussed. An analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity could reveal important insights into the model's robustness and optimal configuration.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the matrix R used in the HGRU decoder, perhaps with a simplified example to illustrate its function?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time) of the unfolding process in the HGRU decoder compared to alternative approaches like those used by Luong and Manning (2016)?
3.  Have you experimented with different initialization strategies for the RNNs within the hierarchical architecture, and if so, what impact did they have on performance?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of tanh as the normalization function for the weights in the word encoder? What alternatives were considered, and why was tanh preferred?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation is generally clear, but some equations could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   The paper is well-written and organized, but some sections could be streamlined for better readability.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.



Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and effective approach to character-level NMT. The strengths of the paper – the innovative architecture, comprehensive experiments, and evidence of morphological learning – outweigh the weaknesses. With some minor revisions addressing the points raised above, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
