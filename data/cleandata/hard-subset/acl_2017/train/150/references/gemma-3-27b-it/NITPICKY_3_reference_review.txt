This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model with a hierarchical architecture, aiming to address the OOV problem and improve translation performance, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The core idea involves a word encoder learning morphological representations and a hierarchical decoder translating at the character level. The claimed contributions are a novel architecture exploiting word structure, automatic subword unit learning, and efficient training of a deep model with six recurrent networks.

However, while the paper attempts a nuanced approach, several issues detract from its overall impact. The primary contribution – the architectural novelty – feels incremental. The combination of character-level encoding with a hierarchical decoder isn't conceptually groundbreaking, building heavily on prior work (Luong & Manning, 2016; Ling et al., 2015a/b). The described word encoder, while intended to capture morphology, lacks a clear theoretical justification beyond empirically observed benefits.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The OOV problem remains a significant challenge in NMT, especially for morphologically rich languages. This work directly tackles that issue, which is commendable.
2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) demonstrating some generalization capability, even if the gains aren't substantial across the board.  The inclusion of morphologically rich languages like Czech is a positive aspect.
3. **Efficiency Claims:** The authors assert efficient training despite the model's depth, and provide some comparative training time metrics (Table 1). This is an important practical consideration for complex models.
4. **Morphological Analysis:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides a suggestive, albeit limited, analysis indicating the model learns some morphological similarities, even if not rigorously quantified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of combining character-level processing with hierarchical decoding, and utilizing RNNs to learn morphological structures, feels evolutionary rather than revolutionary.  The connections to existing work (Luong & Manning, Ling et al.) are not sufficiently highlighted in terms of *what is different* and *why the proposed approach is superior*.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the impact of individual components. For instance, what is the performance gain *specifically* from the word encoder’s morphological learning component?  Removing it and comparing to a standard character-level model is critical. The effect of the six RNNs is not broken down.
3. **Ambiguous Implementation Details:** The description of the HGRU (Hierarchical GRU) is vague. The role of the "auxiliary sequence" {a0, a1, . . . , at−1} feels ad-hoc and isn't thoroughly explained. The unfolding step described in Section 3.2 with the matrix R is confusing and requires more clarity with a simpler, illustrative example.
4. **Overstated Claims and Minor Errors:** The claim of “comparable to state-of-the-art” needs strong justification. The BLEU scores in Table 1, while respectable, aren't decisively superior to existing methods, and the performance improvement over a baseline CNMT is relatively small. There's a minor inconsistency in the referencing (e.g., “Firat et al., 2016” without complete citation information).
5. **Formatting & Style:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, extra spaces, hyphenation).  The writing style is occasionally awkward and verbose. The figures require better labeling and clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the HGRU and the role of the auxiliary sequence? A clearer example would be helpful.
2. What is the computational cost of the matrix unfolding step in Section 3.2? How does this affect scalability to longer sequences?
3. Please present a comprehensive ablation study showing the contribution of each component (word encoder, HGRU, model depth) to the overall performance.
4. Can you provide a more rigorous analysis of the morphological representations learned by the word encoder? Beyond the PCA visualization, are there metrics (e.g., similarity scores between representations of morphologically related words) that can quantify this learning?
5.  Please clarify how the results compare to current state-of-the-art models, providing a more precise comparison and highlighting specific advantages of your approach.

**Additional Comments:**

* The references section is incomplete and lacks full citation details.
*  The text could benefit from a thorough proofread to address minor grammatical and stylistic errors.
* Consider including error analysis examples to showcase the model's strengths and weaknesses.



Overall, while the paper addresses an important problem and presents a reasonably well-executed experiment, the lack of significant novelty, insufficient ablation studies, and minor technical issues lead me to recommend **rejection**. The contribution feels incremental, and a substantial revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above is needed for reconsideration.
