This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that attempts to address the limitations of both word-level and naive character-level approaches. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture leveraging recurrent networks to learn morphology and combine character representations into word representations, effectively bridging the gap between character and word levels. The authors claim a novel architecture, efficient training of a deep model, and comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on multiple translation tasks.

The primary contribution is the proposed hierarchical architecture, specifically the word encoder that learns morphological structure. While character-level NMT isn't new, the explicit modeling of subword units *without* relying on pre-defined segmentation algorithms like BPE is a significant and interesting direction. The model shows a potential to capture morphological information, a known weakness of standard NMT.  A second, notable contribution is demonstrating that deep character-level models *can* be effectively trained, mitigating the vanishing gradient issues often associated with long character sequences.  Finally, achieving competitive performance on multiple language pairs—including morphologically rich Czech—further validates the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The method of learning morphological structure through the coupled RNNs in the word encoder is a genuinely novel idea. It moves beyond simply using characters as the basic unit and attempts to *understand* word structure. This is a substantial departure from existing character-level or subword-level methods.
2. **Efficient Training:** The authors successfully train a relatively deep model (six RNNs) which is an accomplishment in itself, given the challenges of training character-level models. This suggests the architecture is well-designed and doesn’t suffer from severe gradient issues.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The model achieves comparable BLEU scores to strong baselines on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En tasks. Demonstrating success on a morphologically rich language like Czech is particularly encouraging and supports the claim that the model is indeed learning morphological information.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides compelling (though preliminary) evidence that the model is learning meaningful representations related to morphemes.  The ability to translate "convenienter" correctly is also a strong indication.
5. **Addressing OOV:**  The inherent character-level nature of the model elegantly handles out-of-vocabulary words, a chronic problem in word-level NMT.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares against baselines, there’s a lack of detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, what’s the performance gain from the BiRNN in the word encoder? How does the hierarchical decoder compare to a simpler character-level decoder?  Without this, it’s hard to pinpoint exactly what’s driving the performance improvements.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameters were similar to Bahdanau et al. (2015), but doesn't detail the search process or provide any sensitivity analysis. Character-level models are known to be sensitive to hyperparameters, and understanding this is crucial.
3. **Decoder Complexity:** The hierarchical decoder, while innovative, appears complex. The explanation of the HGRU and the unfolding process (Section 3.2) is a bit convoluted.  Further clarification and simplification would be beneficial.
4. **Qualitative Error Analysis is Limited:**  The paper lacks a thorough qualitative analysis of translation errors. Providing examples of where the model succeeds and fails would strengthen the conclusions. The analysis of misspelled words is a good start, but more comprehensive examination is needed.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Character-Level Baselines**: The comparison to Luong and Manning (2016) notes their long training time. However, it would be useful to include a more direct comparison to other recent character-level architectures, even if it means using slightly older results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, specifically isolating the contribution of the BiRNN in the word encoder and comparing different decoder architectures?
2. What was the hyperparameter tuning process? Were any specific strategies employed to address the sensitivity of character-level models?
3. Can you provide more examples of successful and unsuccessful translations, with a focus on morphological complexities?
4. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training your model compared to the baselines? (Beyond just stating the training days)
5.  The "tanh" normalization in the word encoder seems unconventional. Could you provide more justification for this choice, and perhaps compare its performance to a more standard normalization method (e.g., softmax)?



**Additional Comments:** The paper is well-written and the idea is compelling. The experimental setup is reasonable. Despite some minor methodological shortcomings, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach are significant. I lean towards acceptance, as the core idea is strong enough to warrant further investigation.
