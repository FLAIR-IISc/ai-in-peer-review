- Summary: Proposes DCNMT, a hierarchical character-level NMT with a dual RNN encoder for morphology and a hierarchical character decoder, claiming competitive results and better OOV handling.

- Contribution 1: Novel dual-RNN word encoder that models morphological structure by representing and weighting preceding characters.

- Contribution 2: Practical hierarchical decoder (HGRU) that decodes at the character level while mitigating computational costs of pure character models.

- Contribution 3: Empirical demonstration of competitive BLEU scores on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, including morphologically rich languages.

- Strength 1: Directly addresses OOV and morphology issues important for robust NMT.

- Strength 2: Sound, well-motivated architecture combining encoder and decoder components coherently.

- Strength 3: Empirically validated performance with favorable results and compact model size compared to baselines.

- Strength 4: Analysis (PCA visualizations, energy weights) provides evidence of learned morphological representations.

- Strength 5: Shows robustness to misspelled and nonce words, demonstrating practical benefits of character-level modeling.

- Weakness 1: Individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoding) lack strong novelty; contribution is mainly architectural integration.

- Weakness 2: Baselines are insufficiently comprehensive—needs stronger, more recent baselines (e.g., advanced BPE methods).

- Weakness 3: Limited error analysis beyond BLEU; lacks detailed examples and diagnostics, especially where morphology matters.

- Weakness 4: No discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity or robustness to different training settings.

- Weakness 5: Figure 2 (architecture diagram) is dense and hard to parse; needs clearer visualization.

- Question 1: Provide quantitative analysis of energy weights (e.g., average energy on last morpheme character vs. others) to support morphological boundary claims.

- Question 2: Have you compared with stronger BPE-based NMT systems (e.g., subword regularization), and if not, why?

- Question 3: Can you include specific translation error examples and analyze failures where morphology was critical?

- Question 4: Describe the hyperparameter selection process and whether a systematic search was performed.

- Question 5: Provide detailed computational cost comparisons (training time and memory) versus other character-level models.

- Additional comment 1: Writing is generally clear but could be more concise in places.

- Additional comment 2: Include discussion of limitations and future research directions.

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper with valuable contributions and promising results; recommend accept with minor revisions.
