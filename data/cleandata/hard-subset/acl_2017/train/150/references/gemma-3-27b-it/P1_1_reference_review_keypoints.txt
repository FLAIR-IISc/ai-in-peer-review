- Proposed a deep character-level NMT with a hierarchical architecture combining a character-based word encoder and hierarchical decoder to handle open-vocabulary and rare words.
- Introduced a morphology-aware word encoder using two RNNs to learn character representations and their weighting.
- Showed competitive performance on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, arguing benefits for morphologically rich languages and improved training efficiency.
- Addresses the critical open-vocabulary/rare-word problem in NMT by moving to character-level modeling.
- The hierarchical architecture and morphology-aware encoder are a thoughtful, potentially significant combination.
- Empirical validation across three language pairs, including Czech, supports generalizability.
- Paper is well-written with helpful diagrams and a solid related-work context.
- Authors consider efficiency and report reasonable training times for a character-level approach.
- Many individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are standard, so novelty of parts is limited.
- The core innovation (morphology-aware encoder) feels incremental and gains are not dramatically large relative to complexity.
- Evidence that the model truly learns morphology is limited; PCA visualization is unconvincing.
- Lacks rigorous analyses such as morpheme visualization, targeted ablations, or direct morphological accuracy measures.
- Comparisons to prior character-level baselines are limited; needs fairer comparison to well-optimized state-of-the-art baselines.
- Paper omits discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and tuning, hurting reproducibility.
- Lacks detailed error analysis to illustrate specific translation strengths and failure modes.
- Requests more detailed analysis of learned morpheme representations and how morphemes are combined.
- Asks for sensitivity data on hyperparameters (hidden sizes, learning rate) and tuning process.
- Requests justification/experimental rationale for using tanh instead of normalization in weight calculation.
- Asks for clarification on the comparison to Luong & Manning (2016), especially regarding training time vs. gains.
- Requests concrete examples showing successful translation of misspellings or nonce words and explanations of the mechanism.
- Notes the need to clarify distinction between "subword units" and "morphemes."
- Flags the claim of efficiently training six recurrent networks as bold and asks for more training strategy detail.
- Suggests adding an ablation study to quantify each architectural component's impact.
- Overall recommendation: promising contribution but requires major revisions (reviewer leans toward Accept with major revisions).
