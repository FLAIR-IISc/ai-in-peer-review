This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, aiming to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and improve translation quality, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture featuring a word encoder learning morphological representations and a character-level decoder. The authors claim novelty in automatically learning subword units, generating meaningful word representations, and efficiently training a deep model with six recurrent networks.

The primary contribution is the proposed hierarchical architecture combining morphological analysis with character-level decoding. While character-level NMT isn’t novel, the specific method of learning morphological structure via a dual-RNN word encoder appears reasonably innovative. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, suggesting the approach is viable. Finally, the analysis attempting to demonstrate the model’s ability to learn morphology, through PCA visualization and analysis of character-level energies, is a potential contribution, though the evidence is limited.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a relevant problem:** The OOV issue in NMT is a well-known challenge, and exploring character-level models is a valid approach to mitigation. The focus on morphology is particularly pertinent for languages with rich morphological structures.
2. **Hierarchical architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture is well-motivated. The separation of morphological learning and character-level decoding offers a potentially effective way to combine the benefits of both word- and character-level approaches.
3. **Competitive performance:** The reported BLEU scores are competitive with existing character-level models, indicating the proposed model is performing at a comparable level to the state of the art.
4. **Thorough experimentation:** The authors evaluate their model on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), demonstrating its generalizability. The comparison with baseline models, including BPE and CNN-based encoders, provides context for the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of substantial novelty:** While the combination of techniques is reasonable, the individual components—RNNs, character-level modeling, attention mechanisms—are all well-established in NMT research. The morphological learning component, while interesting, relies on standard RNNs and doesn't introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic approach.
2. **Insufficient ablation study:** The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture. For example, what is the performance gain from the morphological word encoder compared to a simple embedding lookup table?  Without this, it’s difficult to ascertain which parts of the architecture are truly contributing to the improved performance.
3. **Ambiguous morphological analysis validation:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides limited evidence of actual morphological learning. Words with similar morphology *appear* closer, but this could be due to other factors, and a more rigorous quantitative analysis is needed. The analysis of character energies, while intriguing, is also not particularly convincing. The observation that ending letters have higher energy is expected and doesn’t necessarily indicate meaningful morphological understanding.
4. **Minor inconsistencies and imprecise language:** Several minor issues detract from the paper’s clarity. For example, the statement "we can regard the weight wi as the energy that determines whether ri is a representation of a morpheme" is somewhat vague. The sentence structure is occasionally awkward, and the paper contains typos ("effect" instead of "affect" in the introduction).
5. **Incomplete experimental details:** More details about the training process would be helpful. For instance, the authors state they use a minibatch size of 56/72, but don’t mention how the sentences are padded or truncated to achieve this. The learning rate annealing schedule could also be described more precisely.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a detailed ablation study, comparing the performance of your full model to variants without the morphological word encoder, with a standard embedding lookup table instead, and with different RNN architectures in the word encoder?
2. Could you provide a more quantitative evaluation of the morphological learning, such as analyzing the learned weights to identify morphemes or evaluating the model's ability to translate morphologically related words?
3.  Please clarify the training procedure regarding padding and truncation of sentences to achieve the stated minibatch sizes. Provide details about the learning rate annealing schedule.
4. Figure 2 uses the abbreviation "UNK". Is this referring to unknown characters, or an unknown word token? This should be clarified.



**Additional Comments:**

The phrase "efficiently train the deep model" is somewhat subjective. While the authors claim efficiency, they don’t provide specific training times or resource usage compared to other models. The reference to WMT’15 in the dataset section appears to be a typo, as the data is used from WMT’14. The final paragraph contains a grammatical error ("...the idea behind our approach could be applied...").
