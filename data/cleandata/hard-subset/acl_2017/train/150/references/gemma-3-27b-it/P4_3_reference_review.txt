This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model addressing the limitations of word-level models, particularly the OOV problem and the challenges of training character-level models. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder learning morphology and a hierarchical decoder. The word encoder utilizes two RNNs to represent preceding characters and their weights within a word, effectively learning subword units automatically. The decoder employs a GRU variant (HGRU) to generate character sequences conditioned on word-level states.

The primary contributions are: (1) A novel word encoder leveraging RNNs to learn morphological representations of words without relying on pre-defined subword units like BPE. This allows the model to capture meaningful subword information, even for unseen words. (2) A hierarchical decoder architecture effectively decoding at the character level, efficiently managing the sequence length and facilitating training. (3) Demonstration of competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, showing the efficacy of the proposed model. The experimental results show that the model can achieve comparable results with state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Morphological Encoder:** The proposed word encoder is a strong point. Unlike BPE which requires pre-training, this method learns subword units directly from the data during training, guided by the morphological structure of words. The use of two RNNs—one for character representation and another for weighting—is a clever approach to capture morphemes and their combinations.
2. **Efficient Hierarchical Decoder:** The hierarchical decoder with the HGRU unit is well-designed. The gate mechanism efficiently handles the transition between word-level and character-level decoding, reducing computational complexity compared to purely character-level approaches. The unfolding strategy for the first-level decoder output makes batch training efficient.
3. **Competitive Results:** The experimental results are solid, demonstrating that the proposed model achieves comparable performance to existing state-of-the-art character-level NMT models on multiple language pairs. The result on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs, Cs-En) is particularly noteworthy, highlighting the advantage of incorporating morphological information.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The paper provides some analysis to suggest that the model actually learns morphology, using PCA visualization and showing the correct translation of "convenienter". These analyses, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence supporting the claim.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Architecture:** While the combination of components is novel, the individual building blocks (RNNs, GRUs, attention mechanisms) are well-established. The core innovation lies in the specific arrangement and interaction of these components rather than fundamentally new algorithmic ideas.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component. While the authors compare with existing models, it’s unclear how much each aspect of the proposed architecture (e.g., the morphological encoder vs. the hierarchical decoder) contributes to the overall performance.
3. **Qualitative Analysis Could Be Expanded:** The analysis of morphology learning is limited. More detailed analysis, for example, examining the learned weights in the word encoder or performing a more systematic evaluation on morphological variations, would strengthen the claim that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.
4. **Training Details & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper provides some training details, but a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. This is important for reproducibility and for understanding the robustness of the model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more detailed ablation studies, specifically analyzing the performance impact of removing the morphological encoder or the hierarchical decoder?
2. Could you provide further examples of morphological variations that your model correctly translates, along with visualizations of the learned weights in the word encoder to illustrate the identified morphemes?
3. What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) compared to existing character-level models like Luong & Manning (2016) with similar performance?
4. How sensitive is the performance to the size of the character vocabulary? Is there a significant performance drop when using a smaller vocabulary?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* The related work section is comprehensive.
* Table 1 could benefit from reporting standard deviations for the BLEU scores.
* Consider including a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential areas for future research.
