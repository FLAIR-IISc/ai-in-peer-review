## Peer Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the challenges of open-vocabulary translation and rare words. The authors propose a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder that learns morphological structure from characters, and a hierarchical decoder. The model is evaluated on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, demonstrating competitive performance compared to existing approaches. The key claim is that by explicitly learning morphology, the model achieves better translation quality, especially for morphologically rich languages, and improves training efficiency.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The proposed architecture, combining a character-based word encoder with a hierarchical decoder, provides a practical and effective framework for character-level translation. This structure helps mitigate the length issues typically associated with pure character-level models.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** The design of the word encoder, leveraging two RNNs to learn character representations and their weighting, shows promise in capturing morphological information within words.
3. **Demonstrated Performance on Morphologically Rich Languages:**  The experiments on En-Cs and Cs-En provide evidence that the proposed model performs well in scenarios where morphological complexity is a significant challenge for traditional NMT models.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper directly tackles the open vocabulary and rare word problem in NMT, a persistent issue hindering real-world application. The shift to character-level modeling is a logical step towards a more robust solution.
2. **Novel Architectural Design:** The hierarchical architecture and the morphology-aware word encoder represent a thoughtful and potentially significant contribution. The idea of explicitly learning morpheme representations within the encoder is well-motivated and appears promising.
3. **Empirical Validation on Multiple Language Pairs:** Evaluation on three different language pairs, including morphologically rich languages like Czech, strengthens the claims and demonstrates the model’s generalizability. The inclusion of Cs-En is particularly valuable.
4. **Clear Explanation and Structure:**  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed model. The diagrams and illustrations are helpful for understanding the architecture.  The related work section provides sufficient context.
5. **Efficiency considerations:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to address the computational demands of character-level models, striving for a model that balances performance with efficiency. The reported training times are reasonable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, many of the individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in the NMT literature.  The core innovation – the morphology-aware word encoder – feels incremental, and the quantitative gain isn’t dramatic enough to fully justify the architectural complexity.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Morphological Learning:** The paper *claims* the model learns morphology, but the analysis supporting this claim is limited. While Figure 3 shows some evidence in PCA projection, it's not very convincing.  A more rigorous analysis, such as visualizing learned morpheme representations, ablation studies focusing on morphological features, or direct assessment of morphological accuracy, would be crucial.
3. **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to existing character-level models (Table 1) is somewhat limited. The cited “Luong and Manning (2016)” model took 3 months to train, and yielded only a small improvement, but the authors dismiss it somewhat quickly. A fairer comparison with a similarly well-optimized, state-of-the-art character-level baseline would strengthen the results.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't provide much discussion of hyperparameter tuning or sensitivity.  Given the complexity of the model, understanding the impact of different hyperparameter settings is important for reproducibility and practical application.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Examining specific translation errors could provide insights into the model’s strengths and weaknesses, and suggest areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations within the word encoder? Specifically, can you visualize or quantify the representations of known morphemes, and demonstrate how the model learns to combine them?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the hidden layer sizes and the learning rate? Could you provide some information on the tuning process?
3.  Can you elaborate on the reasons for using `tanh` instead of normalization in the word encoder's weight calculation?  What experiments led to this decision?
4.  Regarding the comparison to Luong & Manning (2016), could you provide more detail on why their longer training time yielded only a small improvement? Were their results comparable given the computational resources used?
5.  Could you provide some specific examples of cases where the model successfully translates misspelled words or nonce words, and explain *how* the model achieves this?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper mentions "subword units" but does not clearly differentiate these from "morphemes".  Clarifying this distinction would be helpful.
*  The claim regarding efficiently training six recurrent networks is a bit bold - training deep RNNs is still challenging, and more detail on the training strategies employed would be useful.
* Consider adding an ablation study to show the impact of each component of your architecture.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable contribution to the field of character-level NMT.  While the individual components aren't revolutionary, the combination of techniques and the focus on morphological learning are promising.  Addressing the weaknesses, particularly providing more rigorous analysis of morphological learning and comparing against stronger baselines, would significantly strengthen the paper. I lean towards **Accept**, but with major revisions.
