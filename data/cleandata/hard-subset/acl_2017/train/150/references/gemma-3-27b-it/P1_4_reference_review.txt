## Peer Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture (DCNMT) designed to address the limitations of both word-level and purely character-level models. The key idea is to leverage morphological structure by incorporating recurrent networks to learn representations of preceding characters and their weights within words. A hierarchical decoder is then employed to generate the translation at the character level. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, claiming their model learns morphology and addresses OOV issues effectively.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel hierarchical character-level NMT architecture:** The combination of a morphology-aware word encoder with a character-level decoder, particularly the proposed HGRU unit for efficient decoding, is a significant architectural contribution.
2. **Empirical demonstration of morphology learning:** While not entirely novel to attempt to learn morphology in NMT, the authors provide evidence (PCA visualization and translation examples) suggesting their model *does* learn meaningful subword units, particularly in morphologically rich languages.
3. **Competitive performance:** Achieving BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art models, while eliminating a large vocabulary, is a valuable result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a crucial problem:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem in NMT, a persistent challenge. Moving away from large vocabularies is a worthwhile goal, and the proposed approach is a compelling attempt to achieve this.
2. **Well-motivated architecture:** The motivation behind leveraging morphological structure is sound. The explicit attempt to model subword units and their combinations, rather than relying on implicit learning via BPE or character-level models, feels principled.
3. **Clear explanation and thorough experimentation:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the proposed architecture, including the novel HGRU unit. The experiments on three different language pairs (including a morphologically rich one) provide good coverage and strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Demonstrated Efficiency:** The authors point out that the model is efficient to train despite having six recurrent networks. This is a significant point and a practical contribution.
5. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The visualization and examples presented in section 5.2 provide some evidence for the model learning morphemes, and demonstrate the model’s ability to translate misspelled or nonce words.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, individual elements (character-level NMT, hierarchical decoders, RNNs) have all been explored previously. The paper needs to better emphasize the synergistic effect of its components. The HGRU unit is a good addition, but its improvement over a standard GRU isn't thoroughly quantified.
2. **Insufficiently strong evidence for morphology learning:** The PCA visualization is suggestive but not conclusive. While examples of translating misspelled words are impressive, a more systematic evaluation of morphological generalization would be beneficial. For instance, testing on unseen morphological variations would strengthen this claim. 
3. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn’t provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the architecture. For example, how much does performance degrade when removing the morphology-aware word encoder, or when replacing the HGRU with a standard GRU? This information is crucial for understanding the importance of each design choice.
4. **Comparison to stronger baselines:** While comparisons to BPE and character-level models are made, the comparison to state-of-the-art *word-level* NMT systems with subword segmentation (e.g., Byte Pair Encoding) is somewhat limited. A more direct comparison to a strong BPE baseline using a modern NMT framework (e.g., Fairseq, OpenNMT) would be valuable.
5. **Presentation of Results:** The table formatting is somewhat difficult to read. The table could be improved by standardizing the column headings and making the results more easily comparable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more quantitative evaluation of the HGRU unit’s performance compared to a standard GRU, perhaps by reporting training time and BLEU scores with both configurations?
2.  Would you be willing to perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the morphology-aware word encoder and the HGRU unit?
3.  Can you provide examples of instances where the model correctly translates words it has never seen during training, but that are composed of known morphemes?  Could you present a quantitative assessment of such cases?
4.  How sensitive is the performance to the size of the character vocabulary?
5.  Are there any challenges or limitations to scaling this model to even larger datasets or more complex language pairs?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed model compared to word-level and character-level alternatives.
*   The reference list is formatted inconsistently.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a well-motivated and promising approach to character-level NMT. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through more comprehensive ablation studies and a more rigorous evaluation of morphology learning, would significantly strengthen the submission. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
