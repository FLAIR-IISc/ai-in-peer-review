- Proposes DCNMT, a hierarchical deep character-level NMT model to address OOVs and instability in character-based models.  
- Uses a morphology-aware word encoder with two RNNs (preceding-character RNN + weighting RNN) feeding a standard encoder-decoder and a character-level decoder.  
- Demonstrates competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En, matching strong baselines with a relatively small model.  

- Hierarchical architecture (char->subword->word->sentence->target) effectively leverages character information without pure character-level computation costs.  
- Dual-RNN word encoder captures morphological structure and subword units, beneficial for morphologically rich languages.  
- Architecture is novel in integration and well-motivated; hierarchical decoder with state-setting for character generation is clever.  
- Experimental results across three language pairs are strong and training time is relatively efficient.  
- Visualizations show the model learns meaningful morphological representations, reducing black-box concerns.  

- Many individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are not novel; main novelty is their integration.  
- Lacks ablation studies to identify which components (e.g., weighting RNN, HGRU) drive performance.  
- Insufficient discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and whether results are robust to tuning.  
- Error analysis is limited—no systematic breakdown of remaining error types or specific morphological failure modes.  

- Request ablation study removing components (weight-learning RNN, HGRU vs standard GRU).  
- Request discussion/results on hyperparameter sensitivity and any sweeps performed.  
- Request detailed error analysis identifying common and morphological error patterns.  
- Ask whether HGRU decoder complexity is essential or if a simpler character decoder could match performance with tuning.  

- Paper is well-written with helpful figures and comprehensive references.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on providing ablation studies, hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and deeper error analysis.
