## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the challenges of OOV words and improve translation quality, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture that learns subword units (morphemes) automatically via a novel word encoder based on two RNNs, and then decodes at the character level using a specialized hierarchical decoder. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, and provide evidence suggesting the model learns morphological information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT:** The proposed architecture, particularly the dual-RNN word encoder, offers a principled approach to learning subword representations without relying on pre-defined segmentation algorithms like BPE.  This is a valuable contribution to the field of character-level NMT.
2. **Demonstrated ability to handle morphology:** The paper provides both quantitative (BLEU scores) and qualitative (PCA visualization, example translations) evidence suggesting the model *does* learn morphological structure, which is crucial for languages with complex morphology.
3. **Efficient training of a deep character-level model:**  The authors successfully train a model with six RNNs, which is a significant achievement given the known difficulties of training deep character-level models. Their efficient decoder implementation (HGRU) is a key enabler.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical problem:** The OOV issue in NMT is a major bottleneck, and this paper offers a strong alternative to standard word-level approaches and existing subword segmentation techniques.
2. **Clear and well-motivated design:** The rationale behind the hierarchical architecture is well-explained and logically connects to the challenges of character-level modeling and the importance of morphology. The figures (especially Figure 1 and Figure 2) are clear and helpful in understanding the model.
3. **Strong empirical results:** The BLEU scores demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance, even outperforming some existing character-level models.  The inclusion of En-Cs and Cs-En experiments, with their inherent morphological complexity, is a strong point.
4. **Compelling analysis:** The PCA visualization and the example translations showcasing correct handling of misspelled/nonce words provide convincing evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful representations related to morphology.
5. **Efficient implementation:**  The authors highlight the efficiency of their decoder, addressing a common concern with character-level models – computational cost.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, many of the individual building blocks (RNNs, attention, GRUs) are well-established in the NMT literature. The core innovation lies in *how* these components are arranged and used within the hierarchical framework, and the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this differs from prior work.
2. **Insufficient comparison with strong baselines:**  The comparison with other character-level models is somewhat limited.  While the authors include some results, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art character-level models (e.g., more recent BPE-based approaches, models leveraging transformers) would strengthen the paper.
3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity & Ablation studies:**  The paper lacks a thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity or ablation studies.  How crucial are the specific choices of RNN size, number of layers, etc.?  What is the contribution of each component of the word encoder (the two RNNs vs. a simpler embedding lookup)? This information is important for understanding the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
4. **Overstated claim of "learning morphology"**: While the evidence presented supports *some* learning of morphological information, the claim is somewhat strong. The examples presented are compelling, but a more rigorous quantitative evaluation of morphological awareness (e.g., analyzing the representations of related words, probing for morphological features) would be more convincing. The PCA visualization, while suggestive, is not definitive proof.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Novelty:** Could you provide a more detailed discussion of how your approach differs from existing character-level NMT models, specifically addressing how your architecture overcomes the limitations of previous approaches?
2. **Ablation Studies:**  Could you perform ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component in the word encoder (the two RNNs)?  For example, what performance drop occurs when using only a single RNN or a simple embedding lookup?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are your results to changes in key hyperparameters, such as RNN size, number of layers, and learning rate?
4. **Morphological Evaluation:**  Could you perform a more quantitative evaluation of morphological awareness, such as analyzing the similarity of representations for morphologically related words or probing for morphological features?
5. **Computational Cost:**  While you mention the efficiency of your decoder, could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, inference time, memory usage) of your model versus other character-level and word-level NMT models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but there are some instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors. A careful proofread would be beneficial.
*   It would be helpful to include a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.
* The references are somewhat incomplete (e.g., the Firat et al. reference is missing details).




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of character-level NMT.  The proposed hierarchical architecture is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising.  However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing a more thorough comparison with strong baselines, conducting ablation studies, and providing a more rigorous evaluation of morphological awareness – would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.
