## Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of both word-level and purely character-level approaches. The core contribution is a hierarchical architecture featuring a novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations, coupled with a hierarchical decoder that generates text character by character. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, claiming their model learns morphological information effectively and offers a more efficient training process than existing character-level models.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Word Encoder Architecture:** The proposed word encoder, leveraging two RNNs to learn morpheme representations and their combinations, is the most significant contribution. This attempts to bridge the gap between purely character-level and word-level models by explicitly modeling subword structure.
2. **Hierarchical Decoder with Efficient Training:** The hierarchical decoder, paired with the HGRU unit, offers a practical approach to character-level decoding without the computational bottlenecks often encountered in purely character-based NMT. The described training efficiency improvements are valuable.
3. **Empirical Validation on Morphologically Rich Languages:** Evaluating the model on Czech (a morphologically rich language) alongside French provides a stronger assessment of the benefits of the morphological modeling approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper tackles the critical issue of handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words and morphological complexity in NMT, a long-standing problem. The proposed solution is a logical and well-motivated attempt to address these challenges.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The reported BLEU scores demonstrate competitive performance, particularly on the morphologically rich Czech-English tasks. The model achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art models while being computationally more efficient (as indicated by the table on model size and training time).  The improvement over a standard character-level model (CNMT) is a positive indicator.
3. **Detailed Architecture & Implementation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed architecture, including the word encoder, hierarchical decoder, and the HGRU unit. This facilitates reproducibility and understanding. The inclusion of figures is helpful.
4. **Qualitative Analysis Supporting Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) and analysis of character-level energy weights (Figure 4) offer compelling, though not definitive, evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful morphological information. The examples in Table 2 highlighting correct translation of misspelled and nonce words are also persuasive.
5. **Efficient Decoding:** The hierarchical decoder and the HGRU unit represent a practical solution to the challenges of character-level decoding.  The described method to efficiently handle batch processing is a significant improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, many individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in NMT literature. The novelty lies primarily in the specific arrangement and the morphology-focused word encoder, which, while promising, doesn't represent a fundamentally new architectural paradigm.
2. **Analysis of Morphology Learning Could Be More Rigorous:** The qualitative analysis presented (PCA, energy weights) is suggestive but not conclusive. A more rigorous quantitative evaluation of morphological awareness is needed.  For instance, probing the learned representations to see if they capture known morphological features would strengthen the claims.  Ideally, there would be some analysis beyond visualization and error case studies.
3. **Comparison to Subword Models:** The comparison to subword models (like BPE) is somewhat superficial. While the paper mentions BPE, it lacks a detailed comparison and justification for why the proposed approach is superior, particularly in terms of ease of training or handling of unseen morphology. A more direct comparison including the same hyperparameters would be valuable.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters.  A discussion of how different choices for hidden unit size, learning rate, etc., affect performance would provide a more complete understanding of the model’s capabilities.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more quantitative analysis of the morphological information learned by the word encoder? For instance, could you probe the learned representations to identify whether they encode information about prefixes, suffixes, or other morphological units?
2.  How does the performance of your model compare to a state-of-the-art BPE-based NMT system on the same datasets, using comparable hyperparameters? Specifically, what are the computational costs of training and inference for both models?
3.  The paper mentions the model can translate "convenienter" correctly. Are there other examples of successful morphological generalization? Can you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the types of morphological errors the model *still* makes?
4.  The unfolding of the decoder outputs via the matrix R seems crucial for efficiency.  Could you elaborate on the memory footprint of this unfolding step, especially for longer sentences? Does this approach scale well to very long target sequences?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   Consider including an ablation study to analyze the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of NMT, particularly in addressing the challenges of morphology and OOV words. The proposed architecture is well-motivated and shows promising results. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and the focus on morphological learning are valuable. The weaknesses identified are not critical, and I believe the authors can address them with further analysis and experimentation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
