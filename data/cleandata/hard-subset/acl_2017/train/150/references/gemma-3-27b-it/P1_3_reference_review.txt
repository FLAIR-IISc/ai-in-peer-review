## Peer Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) designed to address the limitations of word-level models (large vocabulary, OOV issues) and previous character-level models (difficulty in training, computational cost). The core idea is a hierarchical architecture employing two RNNs in the encoder to learn morphology and a hierarchical decoder to generate translations character by character. The authors claim their model achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks while also being more efficient to train and demonstrably learning morphological information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The proposed hierarchical structure, combining word-level and character-level decoding, offers a practical approach to character-level NMT. It avoids the computational burden of purely character-based models while retaining the benefits of handling OOV words and potentially capturing morphological information.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:**  The dual-RNN encoder, designed to implicitly learn morphemes and their combinations, is a novel idea. The use of weights to represent the significance of preceding characters in forming a word demonstrates an attempt to explicitly model morphological structure.
3. **Efficient Training of a Deep Character-Level Model:**  The authors demonstrate successful training of a relatively deep model (six RNNs) in an end-to-end fashion, which is a notable achievement given the documented difficulties in training deep character-level models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the well-known OOV problem in NMT and offers a compelling solution via character-level modeling.  The motivation for moving beyond simple word-level or subword-level (BPE) approaches is clearly articulated.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The hierarchical decoder with the HGRU unit is a clever design choice that appears to address the challenges of efficiently decoding character sequences.  The idea of initializing the character-level decoder with the word-level decoder output is intuitive.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance compared to other character-level and subword-level approaches.  The BLEU scores presented are encouraging.
4. **Demonstrated Morphology Learning:** The visualization in Figure 3 and the analysis in Section 5.2 provide evidence that the model is indeed learning morphological representations. This is a significant contribution, as it supports the design principles of the word encoder.
5. **Efficient Training:** The paper showcases that the architecture is trainable without resorting to complex layer-wise pre-training or attention supervision, which is a substantial benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the overall architecture is interesting, individual components (RNNs, attention-like weighting in the encoder, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in the NMT literature. The novelty lies primarily in the *combination* of these components, and the paper could better emphasize the synergy achieved through this combination.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:**  The comparison against other character-level models (Table 1) feels somewhat uneven.  The authors compare against models from different years and with varying degrees of complexity.  A more direct comparison to more recent and similarly-sized character-level models would strengthen the results. It would be beneficial to include results against a strong BPE baseline using a modern implementation and comparable model size.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the paper showcases some examples of correct translations for misspelled/nonce words (Table 2), a more comprehensive error analysis would be beneficial.  Specifically, it would be helpful to understand *when* the model fails to handle OOV words or morphological variations and *why*.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that "tanh" performs best in experiments, but doesn’t provide a detailed ablation study regarding the impact of different hyperparameter choices on performance. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
5. **Clarity of Explanation:**  The description of the HGRU unit and the "unfolding" process for the decoder (Section 3.2) is a bit difficult to follow.  A more visual or step-by-step explanation would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more direct comparison to a state-of-the-art subword-level NMT model (e.g., using a modern BPE implementation) with a comparable number of parameters?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of cases where the model fails to correctly translate OOV words or morphologically complex words, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?
3.  What was the rationale for choosing "tanh" as the activation function for normalizing the weights in the word encoder, and have you explored other activation functions (e.g., sigmoid, ReLU)? If so, what were the results?
4.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of the "unfolding" process in the decoder? Does it significantly impact training or inference time?
5. Could you explain the impact of choosing different numbers of layers in the RNNs, and why 2 layers were chosen for the bidirectional RNN sentence encoder and the hierarchical decoder?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, despite the complexity of the model.
*   The reference list is comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   The visual aids (Figures 1, 2, 3, 5) are helpful in understanding the model architecture and the experimental results.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of NMT.  The proposed model is innovative, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are encouraging.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I lean towards acceptance with minor revisions.
