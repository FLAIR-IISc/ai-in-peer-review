## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) aimed at addressing the OOV problem and improving performance on morphologically rich languages. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture leveraging two RNNs in the encoder to learn morphology and a hierarchical decoder to generate character sequences. The authors demonstrate competitive results on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, claiming to achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art models while being computationally efficient.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel character-level word encoder leveraging RNNs to explicitly model morphology.** This is the most significant contribution. The architecture attempts to decompose words into morphemes and learn representations accordingly, addressing a core limitation of existing character-level models.
2. **A practical and efficient hierarchical decoder.** The proposed HGRU-based decoder, coupled with the unfolding strategy, allows for efficient batch training in a character-level translation setting, overcoming a common bottleneck in such models.
3. **Demonstration of competitive performance on multiple language pairs.** The empirical evaluation showing results comparable to existing state-of-the-art models, including on morphologically rich languages, lends credibility to the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of word-level NMT (OOV, large vocabulary) and character-level NMT (computational cost, difficulty in capturing meaningful structure). The motivation for explicitly modeling morphology is well-established and relevant.
2. **Novelty of the Word Encoder:** The dual-RNN encoder attempting to learn morphemes and their combinations is a genuinely novel contribution.  The idea of using weights to represent the importance of each character is insightful and, if effective, could significantly improve character-level representation learning.  The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides a compelling initial validation of this concept.
3. **Efficient Decoder Design:**  The HGRU decoder and the matrix unfolding technique demonstrate practical considerations for making character-level models trainable in batch.  This is a substantial improvement over previous approaches requiring multiple forward passes (Luong & Manning, 2016).
4. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation across three language pairs, including morphologically rich languages like Czech, is a strength.  The comparison with several baselines (BPE, C2W) is thorough and provides a good benchmark for the proposed model.
5. **Demonstrated ability to handle OOV and morphological variations:**  The examples in Table 2 (though limited) demonstrate a qualitative advantage in translating misspelled words and nonce words, which are known challenges for word-level models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Morphological Learning:** While the PCA visualization provides *some* evidence of morphological learning, a more rigorous quantitative analysis is needed. The paper lacks explicit metrics to evaluate whether the model actually learns meaningful morphemes. What types of morphemes are consistently identified? How does this learning affect translation quality for morphologically complex words?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component. For example, what is the impact of the BiRNN in the word encoder? How does the choice of activation function (tanh) affect performance? What are the optimal hidden unit sizes for different components? Without these insights, it's difficult to assess the robustness of the proposed architecture.
3. **Comparability to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, the comparisons could be strengthened by including more recent and stronger NMT models, particularly those incorporating subword segmentation techniques (e.g., SentencePiece).  The claim of "comparable" performance needs to be contextualized against the current state-of-the-art.
4. **Decoder Complexity & Potential Bottleneck:**  The hierarchical decoder, while efficient, introduces additional complexity.  The potential for information loss during the word-level decoding step should be investigated. Does this multi-level decoding introduce a bottleneck that limits overall performance?
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors the model makes and areas for improvement. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand where the model struggles with morphology and what kinds of morphological variations lead to translation failures.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more quantitative metrics to assess the model's ability to learn morphological representations?  For example, can you evaluate how well the learned representations cluster based on morphological features?
2.  What are the results of ablation studies examining the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the BiRNN, the activation function, different hidden unit sizes)?
3.  How does the performance of your model compare to more recent and stronger NMT models incorporating advanced subword segmentation techniques like SentencePiece?
4.  Could you elaborate on the potential information loss during the word-level decoding step and provide any analysis of its impact on performance?
5.  Can you provide a detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors the model makes when handling morphologically complex words?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of figures and tables is helpful for understanding the proposed architecture and experimental results.
*   The references are up-to-date and relevant.
*   Please provide code for reproducibility.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a promising approach to addressing the challenges of character-level NMT. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I lean towards acceptance, pending author responses to the questions and a more thorough analysis of the morphological learning capabilities of the model.
