## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level NMT (large vocabularies, OOV issues) and previous character-level attempts (training difficulty, computational cost). The core idea is to learn morphological structure within words using a novel word encoder consisting of two RNNs – one for character representation and another to weight these representations. This is coupled with a hierarchical decoder that generates translations character-by-character, initialized by a word-level decoder output. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, and provide analysis suggesting the model learns morphological information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Word Encoder:** The proposed architecture for learning word representations by explicitly modeling character sequences and their weighting is a significant contribution. It attempts to bridge the gap between purely word-based and character-based NMT by incorporating morphological awareness.
2. **Effective Hierarchical Decoder:** The design of the hierarchical decoder, utilizing a GRU variant (HGRU) to efficiently handle character-level generation, addresses a key challenge in character-level NMT – computational efficiency. The use of unfolding for batch training is clever.
3. **Demonstrated Performance & Analysis:**  The paper provides solid empirical results on multiple language pairs, showing competitive performance compared to existing character-level and subword-level approaches. The morphological analysis, while somewhat limited, provides some evidence for the model’s ability to learn morphological features.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in NMT - handling OOV words and leveraging morphological information.  The approach offers a compelling alternative to subword segmentation (like BPE) by *learning* subword units instead of relying on a pre-defined algorithm.
2. **Architectural Innovation:** The combination of the character-level word encoder and the hierarchical decoder is well-motivated and offers a potentially advantageous trade-off between representation power and computational complexity. The HGRU is a particularly clever addition.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported BLEU scores demonstrate that the model is competitive with state-of-the-art approaches, especially given its relatively small size (parameter count) compared to some alternatives.
4. **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and the architecture is clearly explained with the help of the diagram in Figure 2.  The motivation for each component is also clearly stated.
5. **Demonstrates Practicality:** The authors show that their model can be trained efficiently (compared to some other character-level models) and achieves good performance without excessive computational resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of the proposed components is novel, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in NMT literature. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work builds upon and significantly improves upon these existing techniques.
2. **Morphological Analysis is Insufficient:**  The analysis of morphological learning (Section 5.2, Figures 3 & 4) is rather superficial. While the PCA plots suggest *some* separation of morphologically similar words, it is not a rigorous or convincing demonstration of morphological understanding.  The authors should consider more quantitative analysis (e.g., analyzing the weights learned in the word encoder, testing the model on morphologically complex words).
3. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines is Missing:** The comparison is made with bpe-based models and character models, but lacks a comparison to more recent and advanced subword-level methods (e.g., Unigram Language Model) and potentially larger character-level models. A comparison with a strong Transformer-based character model would also be helpful.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to choices of learning rate, embedding dimension, RNN hidden size, etc. A brief ablation study would be valuable.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** There's limited analysis of *where* the model fails. Knowing the types of errors the model makes would help understand its strengths and weaknesses better.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different components of your architecture (e.g., removing the word encoder, using a standard GRU instead of HGRU)?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the size of the character vocabulary?  Did you experiment with different vocabulary sizes?
3.  Can you provide more quantitative evidence for the claim that your model learns morphological information? For example, can you measure the similarity of learned representations for words sharing morphemes?
4.   How does the training time and resource usage scale with the size of the dataset and the model complexity (e.g., number of layers)?
5. What is the effect of using different RNN variants (LSTM vs GRU) within the word encoder and the decoder?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references several papers but lacks a concise definition of some of the acronyms used (e.g., C2W).
*  The discussion of the unfolding step in the hierarchical decoder is a bit hard to follow.  A clearer illustration or explanation would be helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-written paper that presents a novel and potentially promising approach to character-level NMT. The proposed architecture is innovative and the empirical results are encouraging. While the paper could be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above, I believe it represents a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
