## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) aiming to address the limitations of both word-level and traditional character-level NMT. The authors propose a hierarchical architecture incorporating a word encoder utilizing recurrent neural networks to learn morphology, and a hierarchical decoder to generate translations character by character. The model is evaluated on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, demonstrating competitive BLEU scores and analyses suggesting effective morphology learning.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Hierarchical Decoder for Character-Level NMT:** The proposed hierarchical decoder effectively bridges the gap between word-level encoding and character-level decoding, offering a more efficient approach than previous character-level models. The HGRU mechanism appears to be a practical solution for efficient batch training.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** While the concept of leveraging morphology isn't entirely novel, the specific implementation using coupled RNNs to model morphemes and their combinations is a reasonable and potentially effective approach.
3. **Empirical Validation on Morphologically Rich Languages:** The inclusion of Czech (a morphologically rich language) in the experimental setup provides a stronger evaluation ground for the proposed morphological modeling capabilities compared to solely using languages like French.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The DCNMT model achieves competitive BLEU scores on all three translation tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to existing character-level and subword-level approaches. While not groundbreaking, the performance is solid and suggests the architecture is well-designed.
2. **Addresses a Key Limitation of NMT:** The paper directly tackles the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-level NMT, offering a viable alternative through character-level modeling. The ability to handle misspelled/nonce words (demonstrated in Table 2) is a significant advantage.
3. **Clear and Logical Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture is well-explained and illustrated, making it relatively easy to understand the core components and their interactions.  The rationale behind the design choices (e.g., the HGRU unit) is also adequately provided.
4. **Support for Morphology Learning:** The visualizations (Figure 3 and 4) offer compelling, albeit qualitative, evidence that the word encoder effectively captures morphological information. The PCA projection and energy analysis are insightful.
5. **Efficiency Considerations:** The authors address the computational challenges of character-level modeling and demonstrate that their model is trainable with a reasonable number of parameters and training time.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of the components is novel, the individual elements (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in NMT research. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of how the proposed architecture differs significantly from existing approaches beyond the hierarchical combination.
2. **Qualitative Evidence for Morphology Learning:** The evidence presented for morphology learning is primarily visual (PCA projection, energy analysis).  More quantitative evaluation of the learned representations (e.g., probing tasks, measuring the similarity of morpheme representations) would strengthen this claim.
3. **Ablation Studies are Limited:**  The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study. It’s not clear which aspects of the architecture are most critical to performance.  For example, how much does the morphology-aware word encoder contribute compared to a simpler word encoder?  What is the impact of the HGRU unit?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameters but doesn't provide detailed analysis of their impact on performance. A discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity would be beneficial.
5. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to existing character-level models is reasonable, it would be helpful to compare against more recent and stronger baselines, such as those employing Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) or WordPiece, especially when highlighting the advantage of learning subword units automatically.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed model compared to other character-level and subword-level NMT models (e.g., memory usage, training time per epoch)?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of the number of hidden units in the RNNs? Did you perform a hyperparameter search to optimize this value?
3.  Can you elaborate on the choice of `tanh` activation for normalizing weights in the word encoder? Were other normalization methods explored?
4.  What specific metrics were used to evaluate the quality of the learned morpheme representations (beyond visual inspection)?
5.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison with BPE or WordPiece based models, specifically addressing the trade-offs between automatic subword learning and pre-defined subword units?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but could be improved by reducing redundancy and clarifying some technical details.
*   The paper would benefit from a more focused discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential areas for future research.
*  The reference list is incomplete (e.g., "Ling et al.,? \Q2015\E").



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of NMT, particularly in addressing the challenges of character-level modeling and incorporating morphological information. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the combination and implementation are well-designed and lead to competitive results. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the depth of analysis and comparison, and I believe addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
