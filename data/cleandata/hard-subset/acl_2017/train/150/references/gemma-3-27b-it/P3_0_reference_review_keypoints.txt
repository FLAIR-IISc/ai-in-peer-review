- Proposes DCNMT: a hierarchical character-level NMT with a morphology-aware word encoder and hierarchical character decoder to address OOVs and training instability.  
- Reports comparable performance to state-of-the-art on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En.  
- Main contribution: a hierarchical architecture that learns morphological structure from characters and decodes at character level.  
- Shows morphological learning via PCA visualizations and translation examples.  
- Provides empirical validation and comparative analysis across multiple language pairs.  
- Strength: directly addresses OOV problem by operating at character level, avoiding vocabulary limits.  
- Strength: innovative coupling of word-level and character-level RNNs with weighted character representations.  
- Strength: HGRU decoder offers an efficient solution for character-level decoding.  
- Strength: competitive BLEU scores and reported training efficiency improvements.  
- Strength: qualitative analyses (PCA, examples) support that the model learns meaningful morphology.  
- Strength: clear writing and helpful architecture diagram.  
- Weakness: individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are not novel; novelty is primarily in their combination.  
- Weakness: lacks ablation studies to isolate the contributions of the weighting mechanism and decoder choices.  
- Weakness: evaluation relies mainly on BLEU; needs human evaluation and targeted morphological error analysis.  
- Weakness: no analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., embedding sizes).  
- Weakness: limited comparison to newer, stronger baselines (e.g., transformer-based models).  
- Question: please provide ablation results for the weighting mechanism and HGRU decoder.  
- Question: what are the computational costs (training time, memory) versus comparable word-level NMT?  
- Question: provide failure cases where morphology causes incorrect translations and explanations.  
- Question: clarify how HGRU improves batch training and quantify efficiency gains.  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to character set size or alternative character/subword choices?  
- Additional: clarify what “RNNsearch” refers to for unfamiliar readers.  
- Additional: discuss potential limitations for languages with very complex morphology (e.g., Turkish, Finnish).  
- Overall: strong paper with promising results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing ablations and more comprehensive evaluation.
