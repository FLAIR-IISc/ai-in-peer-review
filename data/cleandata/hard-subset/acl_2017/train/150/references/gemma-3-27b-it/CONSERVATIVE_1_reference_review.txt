This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, aiming to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly regarding out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder leveraging morphological information and a character-level decoder. The authors claim to achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, and demonstrate the model's ability to learn morphology.

The primary contribution of this work is a relatively complex architecture attempting to integrate morphological information into a character-level NMT system. While the individual components – RNN-based word encoder and hierarchical decoder – are not entirely novel, their combination and the attempt to explicitly model morphology at the character level are noteworthy. A secondary, potentially useful contribution is the demonstration that deep character-level models *can* be trained effectively, a claim often made difficult by vanishing gradients and computational expense.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The OOV problem in NMT is well-established, and exploring character-level approaches is a valid direction. The motivation for modeling morphology is sound, as it directly addresses the limitations of flat word embeddings.
2. **Comprehensive Architecture:** The proposed architecture is reasonably detailed and attempts to address common challenges in character-level modeling (e.g., sequence length, training stability). The hierarchical decoder with the settable state and the HGRU unit are interesting design choices intended to mitigate those challenges.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides experimental results on three translation tasks (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) with comparisons to several baselines, including BPE-based and other character-level models. The reported BLEU scores suggest competitive performance, although the margin of improvement isn't substantial.
4. **Morphology Analysis:** The visualizations in Figure 3 and the discussion around them offer some preliminary evidence that the model *is* learning to represent morphological units, even if the analysis is somewhat limited.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Significant Improvement:** While the model achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art, the gains are not compelling. The BLEU score improvements are marginal, particularly considering the increased architectural complexity. It is unclear whether the added complexity is justified by the performance increase. The claim of "comparable" performance needs to be viewed cautiously.
2. **Insufficient Baselines:** The comparison with baselines feels incomplete. More modern and robust baselines, such as Transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017), are missing. Without a comparison to these stronger baselines, it's hard to assess the true contribution of this work. Relying on older results makes the claims less convincing.
3. **Limited Analysis:** The analysis of the learned morphology is superficial. The PCA plots in Figure 3 are interesting, but more rigorous evaluation is needed to demonstrate that the model is genuinely capturing meaningful morphological information. A more systematic analysis of the learned representations – perhaps by examining the model's behavior on morphologically complex words or using probing tasks – would be beneficial.
4. **Training Details and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The training details are somewhat sparse. The impact of various hyperparameters on performance is not explored, and it is unclear how sensitive the results are to these choices. The claim of efficient training, while supported by the reported training time, lacks context regarding computational resources and dataset size.
5. **Overly Complex:** The model appears to be relatively complex with six RNNs. Complexity should be justified by substantial performance gains; here, the gains are modest, raising concerns about overfitting and generalization.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the results change when compared against a Transformer-based NMT model?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned morphological representations? For example, can the model correctly predict the meaning of unknown words based on their morphology?
3. What is the sensitivity of your model's performance to different hyperparameter settings, particularly the learning rate, embedding dimension, and hidden unit size?
4.  Could you provide a more quantitative analysis of the “efficiency” of the model? For example, what is the training time per epoch compared to simpler baselines?
