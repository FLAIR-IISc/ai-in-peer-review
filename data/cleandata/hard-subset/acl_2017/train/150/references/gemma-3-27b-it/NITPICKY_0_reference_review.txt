This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, dubbed DCNMT, incorporating a novel word encoder leveraging morphology and a hierarchical decoder. The authors claim to address the OOV problem and improve translation quality, particularly for morphologically rich languages, while maintaining training efficiency despite the model's depth (six RNNs).

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of a specific hierarchical architecture attempting to bridge the gap between word-level and character-level NMT. While the idea of incorporating morphological information isn't entirely novel, the proposed word encoder – utilizing two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combination rules – is a potentially interesting direction. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of relatively efficient training for a deep character-level model, achieved through the proposed hierarchical decoder and HGRU unit. Finally, the experiments on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En provide a reasonable evaluation, although the conclusions drawn require more scrutiny (see weaknesses).

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing OOV:** The core motivation of tackling the OOV problem with character-level modeling is strong and relevant. The authors correctly identify this as a significant limitation of traditional word-level NMT. The proposed model inherently handles OOV words by operating at the character level.
2. **Hierarchical Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical structure—character -> subword -> word -> sentence -> target word -> target character—is a well-defined and logical approach to structuring the NMT process. The attempt to explicitly model morphological structure within this hierarchy is commendable.
3. **Training Efficiency:** The authors highlight the relatively efficient training of their six-RNN deep model, which is a notable achievement given the typical computational challenges of character-level NMT.  Table 1 showcases a relatively short training time (1 epoch ~7 days, final ~19 days).
4. **Morphological Analysis:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) and the analysis of character energies (Figure 4) provide some preliminary evidence suggesting that the model *is* learning morphological information. While not conclusive, it’s a positive indicator.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Concepts:** While the *combination* of techniques is presented as novel, many of the individual components are not. Character-level NMT is established (Luong & Manning, 2016).  Hierarchical decoders have been explored.  The RNN-based morpheme learning is conceptually similar to existing compositional models. The authors don't sufficiently emphasize how their approach uniquely advances the state-of-the-art beyond a combination of existing techniques.
2. **Insufficient Comparison & Analysis:** The comparison in Table 1 is limited. While DCNMT achieves competitive BLEU scores, the descriptions of the compared models are vague (“C2W(2) ∼ 54 M char char…”).  Crucially, the differences in training data size, hyperparameters, and training length between DCNMT and the baselines aren't adequately controlled for or discussed, making a fair comparison difficult. The claim of being “comparable to the state-of-the-art” is not convincingly demonstrated.
3. **Overstated Claims & Limited Error Analysis:** The claims about translating misspelled words and nonce words (Section 5.3 and Table 2) are based on very limited examples and lack rigorous error analysis. The provided examples are cherry-picked and don't demonstrate consistent success. The model likely benefits from character-level similarity, but this isn't a novel capability.
4. **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** The description of the HGRU unit is somewhat unclear.  The rationale behind using `tanh` for normalization instead of `softmax` is asserted without sufficient justification. More detail on the specific implementation choices (e.g., initialization, regularization) is needed for reproducibility.
5. **Formatting & Presentation:** Numerous minor formatting issues detract from the paper's professionalism.  There are instances of hyphenated words awkwardly broken across lines ("automati-cally"), inconsistent citation styles, and generally messy formatting of equations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of your model (e.g., the word encoder, the HGRU unit)? Specifically, how much does performance degrade when removing the word encoder or replacing the HGRU with a standard GRU?
2.  How do the hyperparameters of your model compare to those used in the baseline models? Please provide a table detailing the key hyperparameters (embedding size, hidden unit size, learning rate, batch size, etc.) for all models presented in Table 1.
3.  Can you provide a more comprehensive error analysis of the model’s performance on translating misspelled or nonce words? Please provide quantitative results (e.g., the percentage of misspelled words correctly translated).
4.  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the HGRU unit compared to a standard GRU unit?




**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit greatly from a thorough proofread to address the numerous formatting and grammatical errors. The discussion of related work should be more comprehensive, acknowledging the limitations of the proposed approach in relation to existing methods. The presentation of results needs to be more rigorous and less reliant on cherry-picked examples.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea, the lack of novelty, insufficient comparative analysis, overstated claims, and minor technical/formatting issues lead me to recommend **rejection**. The core contribution doesn’t sufficiently advance the state of the art to warrant acceptance given the number of weaknesses. A substantial revision, addressing the issues outlined above, might be considered for resubmission.
