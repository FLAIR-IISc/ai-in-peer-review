## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) designed to address the limitations of word-level NMT, particularly the OOV problem and the difficulty of training character-level models. The core innovation lies in a hierarchical architecture incorporating a word encoder that learns morphological structure from characters and a hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates translations at the character level. The authors demonstrate comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A well-designed hierarchical architecture for deep character-level NMT:** The proposed architecture, combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a character-level decoder, offers a practical and efficient solution for character-level translation, mitigating common issues like computational cost and training instability.
2. **Demonstration of morphological learning within the encoder:** The paper provides evidence, through PCA visualizations and translation examples, suggesting that the proposed word encoder effectively captures morphological information from character sequences.
3. **Empirical Validation & Comparative Analysis:** The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the proposed model across multiple language pairs, demonstrating its competitiveness with existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a core NMT challenge:** The paper directly tackles the critical issue of OOV words in NMT, offering a potentially more robust solution than methods relying on subword units (like BPE) or dictionary lookups. By operating at the character level, the model fundamentally avoids the vocabulary limitation.
2. **Innovative architecture:** The hierarchical structure, particularly the coupling of word-level and character-level RNNs, is a clever design.  The use of weighted character representations within the word encoder allows the model to focus on relevant morphemes, which is a significant improvement over simple character embeddings.  The HGRU decoder is an elegant solution for efficient character-level decoding.
3. **Strong empirical results:** The BLEU scores presented are competitive with existing state-of-the-art models, and the reported training efficiency (especially in comparison to Luong & Manning, 2016) is a definite plus.
4. **Qualitative Analysis:**  The PCA visualization and examples of handling morphological variations (“convenienter”) provide convincing evidence that the model *is* learning morphological information, rather than simply memorizing sequences.  This addresses a common criticism of character-level models – that they lack meaningful representation learning.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the architecture is clearly explained, aided by the helpful diagram (Figure 2).



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of these components is novel and effective, many individual elements (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in the NMT literature. The paper needs to more strongly articulate *how* these components are synergistically combined to achieve significant improvement.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:**  The paper would benefit greatly from ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, what happens if you remove the weighting mechanism in the word encoder?  Or if you simplify the decoder?  Without such analysis, it's hard to determine which aspects of the architecture are truly essential.
3. **Evaluation Primarily Based on BLEU:** While BLEU is a standard metric, it has limitations.  A more nuanced evaluation, including human evaluation or analysis of translation errors, would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s strengths and weaknesses.  Specifically, error analysis focusing on morphological errors would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameters but doesn’t discuss their sensitivity. A more thorough analysis of how different hyperparameters affect performance would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.  For example, what is the impact of varying the embedding dimension?
5. **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons are reasonable, the field is rapidly evolving. Comparing against more recent and powerful NMT models, perhaps incorporating transformers, would provide a more rigorous benchmark.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide ablation study results demonstrating the impact of the weighting mechanism in the word encoder and the HGRU decoder?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of your model compared to word-level NMT with a similar vocabulary size and model complexity?
3.  Can you provide examples of cases where your model *fails* to translate correctly due to morphological complexity, and discuss why those failures occur?
4.  You mention that the HGRU helps with batch training.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges overcome, and quantify the improvement in training efficiency (e.g., training time per epoch)?
5.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of the character set size?  Did you explore using different character sets or subword units at the character level?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "RNNsearch" is used frequently, but a more concise explanation of what RNNsearch *is* would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with that specific work.
*   Consider discussing the potential limitations of the model for languages with highly complex morphology (e.g., Turkish, Finnish) where simply learning character sequences might not be sufficient to capture the intricate morphological rules.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of NMT. While the individual components aren’t entirely novel, the synergistic combination of a morphology-aware word encoder and a character-level decoder is a well-designed and effective approach. The empirical results are promising, and the qualitative analysis provides convincing evidence of morphological learning.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through ablation studies and more comprehensive evaluation, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
