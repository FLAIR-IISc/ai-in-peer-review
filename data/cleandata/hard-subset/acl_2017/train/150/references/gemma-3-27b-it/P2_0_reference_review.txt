## Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) designed to address the limitations of both word-level and purely character-level NMT. The core idea is to introduce a hierarchical architecture that learns morphological structure within words using two recurrent neural networks – one to represent preceding characters and another to weight their importance. A hierarchical decoder then translates at the character level. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, and provide analysis suggesting the model effectively learns morphological information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT:** The proposed architecture, combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a character-level decoder, presents a well-structured approach to leveraging both word-level and character-level information.
2. **Empirical demonstration of character-level morphology learning:** The experiments, alongside the PCA visualization (Figure 3), provide evidence that the model learns meaningful representations of morphemes, allowing it to handle unseen words and potentially improve translation quality for morphologically rich languages.
3. **Efficient training of a deep character-level model:** The authors successfully train a relatively deep model (six RNNs) and report competitive performance, addressing a common challenge in character-level NMT.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a critical problem in NMT:** The paper tackles the well-known out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in NMT in a compelling way. By operating at the character level and explicitly modelling morphology, the model mitigates the limitations of fixed-vocabulary word-level systems.
2. **Well-motivated architecture:** The hierarchical structure is logically sound and intuitively addresses the challenges of character-level translation, particularly the long sequence lengths. The separation of morphological analysis (word encoder) and translation (hierarchical decoder) seems like a sensible design choice.
3. **Strong empirical results:** The BLEU scores reported in Table 1 are competitive with state-of-the-art character-level models, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The improvement shown in the En-Cs task is particularly encouraging, given the rich morphology of Czech.
4. **Analysis supports claims:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) and examples provided in Section 5.2 offer reasonable support for the claim that the model learns morphological representations.  The ability to translate misspelled words (Table 2) is a particularly strong demonstration of the benefits of character-level modeling.
5. **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally easy to follow and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, the individual building blocks (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in the NMT literature. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of what specifically differentiates their approach from existing work beyond the architecture's overall structure.
2. **Insufficient comparison to subword models:** The comparison to a BPE-based subword model is present, but somewhat brief. Given the widespread adoption of BPE, a more thorough comparison – potentially including different BPE vocabulary sizes or variants – would strengthen the paper's contribution. Why does learning morphology *automatically* outperform learned subword units?
3. **Decoder details are somewhat lacking:** The explanation of the HGRU and the matrix unfolding technique (Section 3.2) is a bit dense and could be clarified with a more intuitive explanation and perhaps a simplified diagram. While the authors claim efficiency gains, quantifying the computational savings compared to alternative approaches (like Luong & Manning, 2016) would be valuable.
4. **Limited ablation studies:** The paper doesn’t include ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components (e.g., the two RNNs in the word encoder, the hierarchical decoder) on the overall performance. This would help to understand which parts of the architecture are most critical.
5. **Training details are sparse:** The training details are summarized, but lack specifics. What optimizer parameters were used?  More information here would improve reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of your model versus a comparable BPE-based NMT system and the Luong & Manning (2016) hybrid model?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the size of the character vocabulary? Did you experiment with different vocabulary sizes, and what were the results?
3.  Can you provide more insights into the weights learned by the second RNN in the word encoder (the one computing *wt*)?  Are there clear patterns that indicate it's learning to identify morpheme boundaries?
4.  What is the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the model? Specifically, what is the impact of changing the size of the hidden states, the number of layers, and the learning rate?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its potential sensitivity to noisy or ambiguous morphological structures.
*   The reference to “WMT’151” in Section 4.1 seems like a typo – presumably it should be WMT’15.
*   The paper is well-written, but some sentences are a bit lengthy and could be broken down for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a well-motivated and effective approach to character-level NMT. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are relatively minor and addressable through further experimentation and clarification. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
