- Summary: Proposes a deep character-level NMT with a hierarchical architecture and a novel morphology-aware word encoder using two RNNs, claiming competitive BLEU and efficiency while handling misspellings/nonce words.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a hierarchical character-level NMT architecture combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a hierarchical decoder to address sequence length and training instability.

- Contribution 2: Proposes a novel word encoder that uses two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combinations, aiming to incorporate morphological structure.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates competitive translation performance (BLEU) across En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, especially for morphologically rich languages.

- Strength 1: Directly addresses the OOV/open-vocabulary problem inherent in word-level NMT via character-level modeling.

- Strength 2: Architecture is well-motivated and clearly explained, with components justified relative to character-level challenges.

- Strength 3: Strong experimental validation across multiple language pairs with comparisons to BPE-based methods.

- Strength 4: Analysis (PCA and energy weights) provides evidence that the encoder learns meaningful morphological representations.

- Strength 5: Practical advantage demonstrated in handling misspelled and nonce words.

- Weakness 1: Core components (RNNs, BiRNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are established techniques; novelty is mostly in their combination and needs clearer emphasis.

- Weakness 2: Lacks thorough ablation studies to quantify each component’s contribution, particularly the morphology-aware encoder versus simpler encoders.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient detailed analysis of computational cost (training time, memory) compared to BPE and other methods; efficiency claims are vague.

- Weakness 4: Presentation could be more concise; introduction is somewhat lengthy and repetitive.

- Question 1: Provide an ablation study comparing full model to variants without the morphology-aware encoder and with simpler word encoders.

- Question 2: Provide detailed comparisons of training time and memory usage versus BPE-based NMT.

- Question 3: Report sensitivity of model performance to hyperparameters (RNN sizes, learning rate, etc.).

- Question 4: Provide qualitative error analysis describing remaining error types and their relation to morphology.

- Additional comment 1: Figures are well-designed and help illustrate architecture and analyses.

- Additional comment 2: Reference list is comprehensive.

- Additional comment 3: Minor edits for clarity and conciseness recommended.

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-motivated contribution with valuable morphological analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions contingent on ablation and computational-cost clarifications.
