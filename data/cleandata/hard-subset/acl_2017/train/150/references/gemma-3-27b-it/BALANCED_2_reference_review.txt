This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) aiming to address the limitations of word-level models, specifically the OOV problem and the need for large vocabularies. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a word encoder based on two RNNs—one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation—and a hierarchical decoder. The authors claim their model achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks and, importantly, learns morphological information.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed hierarchical architecture combining a character-level word encoder with a character-level decoder, enabling effective deep character-level NMT. This addresses a key challenge in character-level modeling – maintaining reasonable sequence lengths and efficient training – by leveraging morphological structure. A second contribution is the novel word encoder, which learns subword units automatically using two RNNs, allowing for a meaningful representation of words without relying on pre-defined subword segmentation algorithms like BPE. The empirical evaluation on multiple language pairs and the analysis demonstrating the model’s ability to learn morphology constitutes a third contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem inherent in word-level NMT, which is a significant practical issue. By moving to a character-level model, the reliance on a fixed vocabulary is drastically reduced. This is a compelling motivation and a clear benefit.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture with the two-RNN word encoder is a creative approach.  The idea of explicitly modeling morphological structure within the encoder is insightful and sets this work apart from simpler character-level models. The decoder’s use of a GRU-variant (HGRU) to handle delimiters efficiently is also well-conceived.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The BLEU scores reported in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance on the tested language pairs, and in some cases (En-Cs), surpasses existing character-level models. The comparison against bpe-based models further highlights the benefits of the proposed approach.
4. **Morphological Analysis:** The visualizations in Figures 3 and 5 provide strong evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful representations of words and their morphemes. This is a crucial validation of the underlying design philosophy.  The qualitative examples in Table 2 further support this claim.
5. **Training Efficiency:** Despite the increased model complexity (six RNNs), the authors report reasonable training times and a relatively small model size, making the approach more practical compared to other character-level approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Components:** While the combination of components is novel, many individual components (RNNs, attention, GRUs) are standard building blocks in NMT. The paper doesn't present fundamentally new RNN architectures or attention mechanisms, meaning the core novelty lies in the *arrangement* of existing techniques.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to state-of-the-art models is somewhat limited. While comparisons to BPE and other character-level models are present, the paper lacks a direct comparison against a strong, recently published word-level NMT model with a sophisticated subword handling technique (e.g., SentencePiece). This makes it difficult to definitively assess the performance gains attributable *solely* to the proposed architecture.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not extensively discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices. The optimal configuration of RNN hidden sizes, learning rates, and other parameters can significantly impact performance.  A more thorough ablation study exploring the influence of these parameters would strengthen the results.
4. **Analysis Depth:** While the morphological analysis is a strong point, it could be expanded. Quantitatively measuring the model’s ability to generalize to unseen morphological variations (e.g., novel suffixes or prefixes) would provide stronger evidence for its morphological understanding.
5. **Clarity of Figure 2:** Figure 2 is complex and difficult to follow.  A clearer, more visually organized diagram would improve the paper’s readability.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a direct comparison to a recent, strong word-level NMT model utilizing a state-of-the-art subword segmentation technique (e.g., SentencePiece)? This would clarify the performance benefits of your character-level approach.
2. How sensitive is the model’s performance to different RNN hidden sizes and learning rates? Could you provide an ablation study exploring the impact of these hyperparameters?
3. Can you quantify the model’s ability to generalize to unseen morphological variations (e.g., translating words with novel suffixes or prefixes)?
4. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the activation function `tanh` used for weight normalization in the word encoder? Why was this choice preferred over other normalization methods?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost associated with character-level modeling, particularly in terms of memory usage and training time, compared to word-level models.
*   The phrase "delimter" appears repeatedly. Please correct to "delimiter" throughout the paper.
*   The reference list is incomplete, missing some relevant citations.



Overall, this is a solid and well-motivated paper that presents a novel and promising approach to character-level NMT. While the individual components are not entirely new, the architecture and the morphological analysis are significant contributions. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
