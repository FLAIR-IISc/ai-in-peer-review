This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, addressing the limitations of word-level models (large vocabulary, OOV issues) and previous character-level attempts (computational cost, difficulty in training). The core idea is a hierarchical architecture leveraging morphology – a word encoder based on two RNNs to learn subword units, and a hierarchical decoder to generate character sequences.

The primary contribution is the proposed hierarchical architecture combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a character-level decoder. While character-level NMT is not new, the method of learning subword units *automatically* through the bi-directional RNN encoder and its integration with the hierarchical decoder is a novel approach to capture morphological information. The architecture effectively bridges the gap between word-level and character-level modeling, enabling translation without a large vocabulary while maintaining reasonable computational efficiency.

A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of the model’s effectiveness on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks. The results show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art character-level and subword-level models, demonstrating the practicality of the proposed approach. The performance gain, particularly on morphologically rich languages like Czech, supports the claim that learning morphology improves translation quality.

Finally, the analysis showing the model’s ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words, and the visualization of the learned subword representations (Fig. 3 & 5) offer insights into the model's internal workings, further validating its capacity to learn morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The combination of the bi-directional RNN based morphology-aware word encoder and the hierarchical decoder represents a novel architectural choice. This approach allows for efficient learning of subword units without relying on predefined segmentation algorithms like BPE, unlike other character-level approaches.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The reported BLEU scores on the three language pairs demonstrate that the proposed model achieves performance comparable to other state-of-the-art character-level and subword-level NMT systems. This empirical validation is crucial for establishing the effectiveness of the method.
3. **Morphological Analysis:**  The qualitative analysis, particularly the PCA visualization of word embeddings (Fig. 3) and subword segmentation (Fig. 5), provides strong evidence that the model indeed learns meaningful morphological representations. The ability to translate “convenienter” correctly strengthens this claim.
4. **Handles OOV and Misspellings:** The model’s inherent character-level nature effectively addresses the OOV problem and, as demonstrated in Table 2, shows improved robustness towards misspelled words, a significant advantage over word-based models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares the proposed model against several baselines, a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial. Specifically, the contribution of each component – the bi-directional RNN encoder versus a simpler embedding layer, the hierarchical decoder versus a standard character-level decoder – needs more detailed investigation.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges that the model has six RNNs, implying a large number of hyperparameters. The sensitivity of the model’s performance to these hyperparameters is not discussed. A discussion of hyperparameter tuning and potential challenges in this regard is necessary.
3. **Computational Cost:** Although the authors claim efficiency gains, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) compared to other character-level models is needed. The statement that training is efficient is somewhat subjective without quantitative support. The claim that it’s simpler than other models based on parameter count (Table 1) doesn’t necessarily translate to faster or less memory intensive training.
4. **Decoder Details:** The description of the HGRU unit and the matrix unfolding process for efficient batch training (Section 3.2) is dense and could benefit from a clearer explanation with a simplified example.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, specifically isolating the impact of the bi-directional RNN word encoder and the hierarchical decoder on overall translation performance?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the proposed model compared to other character-level NMT models with similar performance, such as the model by Luong & Manning (2016)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? Were any specific strategies employed to optimize the numerous hyperparameters in the model?
4.  Can you provide more intuition behind the tanh activation function used for normalization in the word encoder (instead of a softmax)? How does this contribute to improved performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, although some sections (e.g., Section 3.2) could benefit from more clarity.
*   The figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture and the learned representations.
*   The inclusion of examples of translations with misspelled/nonce words in the main text is effective in highlighting the model's strengths.
