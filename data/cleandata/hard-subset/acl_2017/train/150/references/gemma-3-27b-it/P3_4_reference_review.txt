## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and improve translation quality, especially for morphologically rich languages. The core idea is to learn morphological structure within a word encoder, using two RNNs – one to represent preceding characters and another to weigh their contributions. A hierarchical decoder then translates at the character level, conditioned on the word-level representation.  The authors demonstrate competitive results on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT:** The proposed architecture, combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a character-level decoder, offers a compelling approach to leverage the benefits of both word and character-level modeling.
2. **Empirical demonstration of morphology learning:** The paper provides evidence, through visualizations and translation examples, that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.
3. **Competitive performance:** The model achieves strong BLEU scores on multiple translation tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in practice.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant problem:** The OOV problem is a fundamental challenge in NMT, and this paper presents a promising solution that moves beyond simple copy mechanisms or subword segmentation.
2. **Intuitive and well-motivated architecture:**  The idea of explicitly modeling morphology is logically sound, especially for morphologically rich languages. The two-RNN word encoder is a clever mechanism to achieve this.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate their model on three different language pairs, including morphologically rich languages like Czech, providing a robust assessment of its performance.  The comparison with existing character-level and subword-level models is valuable.
4. **Clear and detailed explanation:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. Figures are helpful in understanding the model.
5. **Demonstrated ability to handle OOV and nonce words:** The example translations presented in Section 5.3 showcase a clear benefit of the character-level approach – the ability to translate unseen words or misspelled terms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the *combination* of ideas is interesting, the individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in NMT literature. The novelty lies more in the specific arrangement and application to morphology learning rather than fundamentally new building blocks.  The paper could benefit from more explicitly acknowledging this and focusing on the specific contributions of the architecture as a whole.
2. **Lack of ablations regarding the word encoder:** The paper does not provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the impact of the proposed word encoder.  For instance, how does performance change when replacing the two-RNN encoder with a simpler embedding lookup table, or a CNN-based encoder?  A more detailed analysis of the word encoder’s contribution is needed.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** The model consists of six RNNs, which adds complexity and potentially computational cost. While the authors claim training is efficient, a more in-depth analysis of training time and resource requirements compared to simpler models would be beneficial.
4. **Reliance on pre-defined character set:** While understandable, the choice of a fixed-size character set (120 characters) could be a limitation, especially for languages with very large character spaces. The authors should discuss the potential impact of this choice.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to demonstrate the specific contribution of the proposed two-RNN word encoder? Specifically, how does the performance change when using a simpler word encoding mechanism (e.g., embedding lookup table)?
2.  The paper mentions a "gate recurrent unit (GRU)" variant (HGRU). Can you elaborate on the differences between HGRU and a standard GRU, and why these changes were necessary for effective character-level decoding?  Provide details on the design choices that lead to the proposed modification.
3.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the size of the character set?  Have you experimented with larger character sets, or methods for handling OOV characters within the character-level representation?
4.  The visualization in Figure 3 seems to show good separation of morphological variations. Could you provide quantitative results to support these observations (e.g., measuring the distance between embeddings of morphologically related words)?
5.  While you mention the model being efficient, could you provide detailed information on training time and memory usage for different model configurations and dataset sizes?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears incomplete (e.g., missing citation details for some papers mentioned in the text).
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations and future work, such as exploring alternative word encoder architectures or applying the model to other sequence-to-sequence tasks.
*   The explanation of the matrix unfolding in Section 3.2 could be clarified with a more detailed example.




Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of NMT, presenting a well-motivated and empirically validated approach to character-level translation. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
