This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model with a novel architecture designed to learn morphology and address the limitations of both word-level and purely character-level approaches. The core idea is a hierarchical encoder-decoder structure leveraging recurrent neural networks to represent words based on their constituent characters and then decode at the character level. The authors claim to achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, while also demonstrating the model's ability to learn morphological structure.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the exploration of a specific hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT that attempts to explicitly model morphological structure through a dual-RNN word encoder. The presentation is clear, and the motivation for addressing OOV issues and leveraging morphological information is well-established. A secondary, albeit less significant, contribution is the empirical evaluation on multiple language pairs, demonstrating reasonable performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of word-level NMT (large vocabulary, OOV words) and the shortcomings of naive character-level approaches (computational cost, difficulty in training). The proposed approach directly addresses these concerns.
2. **Well-Defined Architecture:** The hierarchical encoder-decoder architecture, particularly the dual-RNN word encoder, is described in a logical and understandable manner, supported by a helpful diagram (Figure 1 and 2).  The concept of learning morphological representations within the encoder is intuitively appealing.
3. **Reasonable Empirical Results:** The reported BLEU scores, while not dramatically exceeding state-of-the-art, are competitive, particularly given the model complexity. The performance on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs, Cs-En) suggests the approach *may* be capturing some morphological information.
4. **Initial Evidence of Morphological Learning:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) provides some preliminary evidence that the word encoder differentiates words based on morphological features, which is a promising indication.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to existing models is incomplete. While results are reported alongside those of several baselines, the experimental setup does not appear fully equivalent. Specifically, the reported results for existing models are pulled from different sources and may utilize different training procedures or data splits. A direct, head-to-head comparison with strong, contemporary baselines (e.g., Transformer models, more recent subword NMT approaches like SentencePiece) is crucial.  Simply stating the model is “comparable” isn’t sufficient without establishing statistical significance and controlled experimentation.
2. **Limited Analysis of Morphological Learning:** While the PCA visualization is a good start, the analysis of morphological learning is superficial. The authors claim the model learns morphemes, but there’s little quantitative evidence to support this. More rigorous analysis is needed, such as examining the learned representations for known morphemes or evaluating the model's performance on tasks specifically designed to assess morphological awareness (e.g., morphologically inflected word recognition). The examples provided in Section 5.2 are qualitative and not particularly compelling.
3. **Complexity and Scalability:** The model architecture is relatively complex (six RNNs).  While the authors claim the model is efficient to train, the training times reported in Table 1 are not convincing, and the lack of detailed computational resource reporting hinders assessment. The scalability of this approach to larger datasets and more complex languages is questionable.
4. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to justify the design choices. For instance, what is the impact of the BiRNN in the word encoder? What is the contribution of the tanh activation function for normalization? A thorough ablation study would help demonstrate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed architecture.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a direct, head-to-head comparison with stronger baseline models, such as Transformer-based NMT systems or more recent subword models (e.g., SentencePiece), trained under identical conditions (data splits, hyperparameters)? Please include statistical significance testing.
2. Please provide a more quantitative analysis of the morphological learning capabilities of your model. For example, can you demonstrate the model's ability to correctly predict morphological inflections or to cluster words based on their morphological features?
3. Can you provide detailed computational resource information (e.g., GPU type, batch size, training time per epoch) for all experiments, including the baselines?
4. What is the impact of the tanh activation function in normalizing the weights?  Have you experimented with other normalization methods, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.  While the authors acknowledge some challenges, a more honest assessment of the model's shortcomings would strengthen the work.
