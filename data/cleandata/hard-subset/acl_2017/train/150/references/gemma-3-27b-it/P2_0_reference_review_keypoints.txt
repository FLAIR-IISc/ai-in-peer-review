- Proposes DCNMT: a hierarchical character-level NMT with a morphology-aware word encoder (two RNNs) and a character-level hierarchical decoder.
- Demonstrates competitive BLEU on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En and shows the model handles unseen/misspelled words.
- Provides PCA and qualitative analysis suggesting the model learns meaningful morpheme-like representations.
- Novelty lies in the combination of components; individual building blocks (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are standard.
- Insufficient comparison to BPE/subword models; needs experiments with different BPE sizes and explanations why learned morphology outperforms subwords.
- Decoder/HGRU and matrix-unfolding descriptions are dense and need clearer, more intuitive explanations and diagrams.
- Claims efficiency gains but lacks quantified comparison of computational cost (training time, memory) versus BPE and prior hybrid models (e.g., Luong & Manning 2016).
- Lacks ablation studies to isolate the contributions of key components (two RNNs, hierarchical decoder).
- Training details (optimizer settings, hyperparameters) are too sparse for reproducibility.
- Questions: provide computational cost comparisons, sensitivity to character vocabulary size, analysis of wt weights for morpheme boundaries, and hyperparameter impact.
- Strength: effectively addresses OOV issues by explicitly modeling morphology and operating at character level.
- Strength: well-motivated hierarchical architecture that mitigates long sequence issues in character-level NMT.
- Strength: clear writing and reasonable empirical/qualitative support for claims, especially for morphologically rich languages (notably En–Cs).
- Additional comments: discuss limitations (sensitivity to noisy/ambiguous morphology), fix typo WMT’151 → WMT’15, and simplify some lengthy sentences.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-motivated, and effective approach; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
