This paper introduces a deep character-level NMT model, DCNMT, designed to address OOV issues and leverage morphological information. The core idea is a hierarchical encoder-decoder architecture with a novel word encoder based on two RNNs – one for character representations and another for weighting them – effectively learning subword units automatically. The decoder is also hierarchical, using a character-level decoder conditioned on a word-level representation. 

The primary contribution is the proposed hierarchical architecture and the morphology-aware word encoder. While character-level NMT isn’t new, the explicit attempt to model morphology within the encoder, without relying on pre-defined subword segmentation (like BPE), is a noteworthy departure. A second contribution lies in the efficient training of this deep model (six RNNs), a common challenge with character-level approaches. Finally, the demonstration of comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on multiple translation tasks, while not a breakthrough, lends credibility to the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Morphology-Aware Encoder:** The approach to learning subword units *within* the encoder, based on character weighting, is genuinely interesting. It sidesteps the need for pre-processing steps like BPE, allowing the model to discover meaningful units directly from the data. This is a bold move and a valuable contribution to the field, potentially offering a more adaptable approach than fixed subword segmentation.
2. **Efficient Deep Model Training:** Character-level models often struggle with computational complexity and training instability. The authors claim and demonstrate (through training efficiency metrics) an ability to train a relatively deep model (6 RNNs) effectively. This practical result is crucial for the viability of the approach.
3. **Demonstrated Competitive Performance:**  Achieving performance comparable to established models, including character-level ones, provides a strong signal that the proposed architecture is viable and holds promise. The inclusion of morphologically rich languages (Czech) in the evaluation further strengthens this claim.
4. **Interesting Analysis of Learned Morphology:** The visualization of character energies (Figure 4) and the PCA projection (Figure 5) offer some initial evidence supporting the claim that the model is learning meaningful morphological units. While not definitive, this is a good step towards understanding the model’s internal workings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Morphological Learning:** The analysis in Section 5.2, while suggestive, is somewhat superficial. Showing that the model *can* translate "convenienter" correctly is a good example, but a more systematic evaluation of morphological generalization (e.g., testing on unseen morphological variations) is needed. A quantitative metric for measuring morphological understanding would be valuable.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, how much does performance degrade when removing the weighting mechanism in the word encoder?  How does the performance compare to a simpler character-level encoder without the hierarchical structure?  These experiments would help to justify the complexity of the proposed architecture.
3. **Hierarchical Decoder – Unclear Benefit:** The benefit of the hierarchical decoder isn't fully convincing. The paper states it avoids the computational bottleneck of a large softmax layer, but doesn’t fully quantify this benefit. Is the performance gain worth the added complexity of the two-level decoding process? The motivation for the HGRU is also not entirely clear.
4. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons are made, the baselines used feel somewhat dated. Recent, strong character-level models (beyond Luong & Manning) and state-of-the-art subword models should be included to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more systematic evaluation of the model’s ability to generalize to unseen morphological variations (e.g., novel affixes)?
2. Could you present ablation studies that isolate the impact of the weighted character representation in the word encoder and the hierarchical decoder?
3.  What is the computational cost (training time and memory usage) comparison between your model and a standard character-level NMT model of similar size?  Specifically, how much does the hierarchical structure contribute to the computational efficiency?
4. How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality of the character embeddings and the size of the hidden states in the RNNs?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from clearer diagrams illustrating the flow of information through the hierarchical architecture.
*  The writing is generally clear, but some sections (e.g., the description of the HGRU) are dense and could be simplified.
* Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations and future work, such as exploring different RNN architectures or incorporating attention mechanisms within the word encoder.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of character-level NMT. The novelty of the morphology-aware encoder and the demonstrated ability to train a deep model efficiently are particularly appealing. Despite some weaknesses in the analysis and evaluation, the boldness of the approach and the promising results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
