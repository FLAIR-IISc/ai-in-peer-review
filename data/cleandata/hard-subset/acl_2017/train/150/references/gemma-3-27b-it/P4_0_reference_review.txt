This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and the difficulties in training deep character-level networks. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture with a novel word encoder leveraging morphology and a hierarchical decoder that generates character sequences.

The primary contributions of this work are: 1) A word encoder based on two RNNs designed to learn morphological structure, allowing the model to represent words based on their constituent morphemes rather than relying solely on a vocabulary lookup. 2) A hierarchical decoder employing a modified GRU (HGRU) that efficiently decodes at the character level by setting the decoder state based on the output of a word-level decoder, addressing challenges in training deep character-level models. 3) Demonstrating competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, alongside analyses supporting the model’s ability to learn morphology.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Word Encoder:** The proposed word encoder, with its dual RNN structure to capture morpheme representations and combination rules, is a noteworthy contribution. This approach moves beyond simple subword units (like BPE) and attempts to *learn* meaningful subword structure directly from the data. The presented PCA visualizations (Figure 3 and 5) provide initial evidence supporting this claim. This design attempts to bridge the gap between word-level and character-level modeling, a potentially valuable direction.
2. **Efficient Hierarchical Decoder:** The HGRU-based hierarchical decoder is well-motivated and addresses a key practical challenge in character-level NMT – efficient training.  The mechanism for initializing the character-level decoder state based on the word-level decoder output, coupled with the state-setting mechanism within the HGRU, offers a clever way to maintain manageable sequence lengths during training. The claimed efficiency gains in training (Table 1) are significant.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), including a morphologically rich language (Czech).  This is a positive aspect, as it provides a more robust assessment of the model’s capabilities, especially its ability to handle morphological complexity. The comparison against baseline models, including BPE and other character-level approaches, helps contextualize the results.
4. **Demonstrated Ability to Handle OOV and Morphology:** The examples provided in Table 2 showcase the model’s ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words, a direct benefit of the character-level approach. The qualitative analysis of morpheme learning (Section 5.2) further reinforces this point.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gains Over State-of-the-Art:** While the model achieves competitive performance, the BLEU score improvements over strong baselines (especially the character-level models) are relatively modest. The paper states the Luong & Manning (2016) model took 3 months to train to achieve +0.5 BLEU points compared to their result.  It is unclear if the gains justify the architectural complexity of the proposed model. A more detailed ablation study isolating the impact of each component (word encoder vs. hierarchical decoder) would strengthen the analysis.
2. **Insufficient Detail in Hyperparameter Tuning:** The training details are somewhat limited. While hyperparameters are mentioned, a more thorough discussion of the tuning process and the sensitivity of the model to different parameter settings would be beneficial. It’s also unclear if the hyperparameters were optimized specifically for each language pair or if a single set was used across all experiments.
3. **Subjectivity in Morphology Analysis:** The PCA visualizations and examples presented to demonstrate morpheme learning are persuasive but somewhat subjective. A more quantitative evaluation of the learned morpheme representations would provide stronger evidence. For instance, could the learned representations be used to predict morphological features or identify cognates across languages?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors the model still makes (e.g., morphological errors, syntactic errors, semantic errors) would provide valuable insights and suggest directions for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to quantify the contribution of the word encoder and hierarchical decoder independently? Specifically, what performance difference is observed when removing each component from the full model?
2.  What were the specific criteria used for selecting the 120 most frequent characters for each language? Was this number optimized, and how sensitive is the model’s performance to this choice?
3.  Can you elaborate on the design choices related to the HGRU, particularly the activation functions and gating mechanisms? Were other variations explored, and why was the current configuration chosen?
4.  Would you be able to provide more quantitative evidence supporting the claim that the model learns meaningful morpheme representations (e.g., correlation with external morphological lexicons)?
