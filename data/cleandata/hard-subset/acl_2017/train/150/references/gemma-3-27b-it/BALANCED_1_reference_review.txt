This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a word encoder that learns morphology and a hierarchical decoder operating at the character level. The word encoder utilizes two RNNs – one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation – to capture subword units automatically. The decoder employs a modified GRU (HGRU) to efficiently generate character sequences, conditioned on the output of the word-level decoder.

The main contributions, in my assessment, are:

1. **The Hierarchical Architecture:** The proposed combination of a morphology-aware word encoder and a character-level decoder presents a novel structure for character-level NMT, effectively bridging the gap between word and character representations. This allows for better handling of rare and unseen words without drastically increasing vocabulary size.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** The design of the word encoder, with its dual RNN structure for capturing character sequences and their weighting, demonstrates a sensible approach to learning subword units automatically, unlike methods relying on byte-pair encoding. The empirical evidence presented suggests it does, indeed, learn meaningful representations of morphemes.
3. **Efficient Character-Level Decoding:** The HGRU decoder, along with the unfolding technique, addresses the computational inefficiencies often associated with character-level decoding, making the model more practical to train.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problems with traditional word-level NMT (OOV words, large vocabulary) and positions character-level modeling as a potential solution, highlighting its challenges. This provides a solid foundation for the proposed work.
2. **Novelty of the Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture combining morphology-aware encoding and character-level decoding is a genuinely interesting approach. While character-level NMT isn't new, the specific way morphology is incorporated is a significant contribution.
3. **Empirical Results:** The experimental results, particularly on morphologically rich language pairs (En-Cs, Cs-En), demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model. Achieving comparable or superior BLEU scores to existing character-level models is a strong indicator of success. The comparisons with BPE and other models strengthen the validity of the results.
4. **Analysis of Learned Morphology:** The PCA visualization and analysis presented in Section 5.2 provides insightful evidence that the word encoder learns meaningful representations of morphemes.  The example translations of misspelled and nonce words further support this claim.
5. **Efficiency Considerations:** The authors acknowledge and address the computational challenges of character-level modeling, proposing the HGRU decoder and unfolding technique to improve efficiency. The reported training times and model size are valuable for reproducibility and comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper provides a comparison with existing models, it lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture. For instance, the benefit of the dual RNN structure in the word encoder, or the impact of the HGRU, is not fully quantified through controlled experiments.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices. A more thorough investigation of different hyperparameter settings and their impact on performance would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
3. **Dataset Size and Domain:** The experiments are limited to WMT datasets. Evaluating the model on a wider range of datasets, particularly those with different characteristics (e.g., domain-specific corpora, low-resource languages), would provide a more comprehensive assessment of its generalizability.
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 1 is difficult to parse and lacks clear formatting. The inclusion of epoch and day numbers is helpful but could be presented more concisely. It would also be beneficial to include confidence intervals for the BLEU scores.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While examples are shown in Table 2, a systematic error analysis, identifying common translation errors and their causes, would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, examining the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture (e.g., the dual RNN in the word encoder, the HGRU decoder)?
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., hidden unit size, learning rate)?  Can you provide a sensitivity analysis?
3. Have you experimented with different strategies for initializing the HGRU state (beyond the output of the first-level decoder)?
4. What is the computational cost overhead of the unfolding step in the HGRU decoder, compared to a standard GRU?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "learned subword unit" is used frequently. Clarify whether these units are consistently morphemes or more general subword segments.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the attention weights to provide further insights into how the model aligns source and target sequences.
*   The reference to "Google translate" in Table 2 is not a formal citation.




Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and effective approach to character-level NMT. The proposed architecture and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the identified weaknesses through more thorough ablation studies, hyperparameter analysis, and error analysis would further strengthen the contribution and increase the impact of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
