## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:** This paper proposes a deep character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) addressing the challenges of OOV words and training instability often encountered in purely character-level models. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture with a word encoder that learns morphological information using two RNNs – one for preceding characters and another to weight their importance. This encoder feeds into a standard encoder-decoder architecture with a character-level decoder.  The authors demonstrate comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Hierarchical Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture – character -> subword -> word -> sentence -> target word -> target character – provides a practical and effective way to leverage character-level information without the computational bottlenecks of purely character-based models.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:**  The designed word encoder, explicitly aiming to learn morphological structure through its dual RNN setup, shows promising results in capturing subword units and potentially improving translation, particularly for morphologically rich languages.
3. **Demonstrated Competitive Performance:** Achieving BLEU scores comparable to strong baselines, including subword and character-level models, demonstrates the viability and effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper tackles the crucial problem of OOV words and the limitations of large vocabulary word-level models, offering a solid alternative.  The hierarchical structure provides a compelling solution.
2. **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The architecture is thoughtfully designed. The rationale behind the dual RNN encoder to capture morphology is clear and intuitive.  The hierarchical decoder with the state-setting mechanism for character generation is a clever way to manage the decoding process.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** The results across three language pairs (including morphologically rich Czech) are encouraging. The comparison against strong baselines demonstrates the effectiveness of DCNMT. The fact that it achieves competitive results *with* a relatively small model size is a notable achievement.
4. **Analysis of Learned Morphology:** The visualization in Figure 3 and the accompanying explanation provide strong evidence that the word encoder *is* learning morphological information, adding support to the core idea. This isn’t just a black box; the authors attempt to understand *how* the model is working.
5. **Efficient Training:** The authors address a key concern with character-level models—training efficiency—and claim (backed by the "Days" column in Table 1) that their model is relatively fast to train, especially given its complexity.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:**  While the *combination* of ideas is valuable, most individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are not entirely novel. The significant contribution is how these components are integrated specifically to address morphology at the character level.  This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it should be acknowledged.
2. **Ablation Studies are Missing:** The paper lacks a thorough ablation study.  It's unclear *which* parts of the architecture are most crucial for performance. For example, how much performance is lost by removing the weight-learning RNN in the word encoder? What's the impact of the HGRU decoder versus a simpler character-level decoder?  This makes it harder to assess the true value of each component.
3. **Insufficient Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameters but doesn't delve into how sensitive the model is to their choices.  Were any significant hyperparameter sweeps performed?  Understanding this would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the examples in Table 2 are illustrative, a more systematic error analysis would be valuable.  What types of errors does the model still make? Are there specific morphological patterns it struggles with?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ablation Study:** Please provide an ablation study, evaluating the performance of DCNMT with different components removed (e.g., removing the weight-learning RNN, using a standard GRU decoder instead of HGRU).
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Can you discuss the sensitivity of the model to key hyperparameters (e.g., RNN hidden size, embedding dimension, learning rate)? Were any systematic hyperparameter sweeps performed?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns and potential areas for improvement?  Are there specific types of morphological errors the model frequently makes?
4. **Decoder Complexity:** Is the complexity of the HGRU decoder essential for performance?  Could a simpler character-level decoder achieve comparable results with appropriate tuning?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed architecture.
* The figures are helpful in visualizing the model and the learned representations.
* The reference list is comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of NMT.  The proposed architecture is well-motivated, the experimental results are encouraging, and the analysis of learned morphology provides valuable insights. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, their combination and application to character-level NMT with a focus on morphology are significant. Addressing the weaknesses through the requested ablation studies and error analysis would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
