This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, addressing the limitations of word-level models, particularly regarding out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. The core contribution lies in a hierarchical architecture incorporating a novel word encoder that learns morphology and a hierarchical decoder for character-level decoding. The encoder uses two RNNs—one for preceding characters and another for weighting their representation—to implicitly learn subword units. The decoder employs a modified GRU unit (HGRU) to efficiently handle character generation.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The proposed architecture provides a structured approach to character-level translation, combining word-level and character-level processing, addressing the computational and training challenges of pure character-level models.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** The novel word encoder leverages RNNs to learn representations of morphemes and their combinations, allowing the model to generalize to unseen words and improve translation accuracy, especially for morphologically rich languages. This differentiates it from simpler subword approaches like BPE.
3. **Efficient Character-Level Decoder (HGRU):** The introduction of the HGRU unit offers an efficient method for character decoding, preventing the computational bottlenecks associated with standard character-level decoders. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem, a significant limitation of word-level NMT, with a well-motivated and technically sound approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrates competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art character-level and word-level models. The results in Table 1 provide clear evidence of the model’s effectiveness.
3. **Novel Encoder Design:** The morphology-aware word encoder represents a significant contribution. The analysis presented in Figure 3 and Section 5.2 convincingly demonstrates the model's ability to learn meaningful representations based on morphemic structure. The PCA visualization is a particularly strong point.
4. **Efficient Decoding:** The HGRU mechanism is a clever solution to the computational challenges of character-level decoding, making the model trainable with a relatively large number of recurrent layers.
5. **Ablation and Analysis:** The paper provides detailed analysis demonstrating how learning morphology affects performance, bolstering the claim that the word encoder is contributing to the results. The analysis in Section 5.3 provides examples where the model correctly translates misspelled words and nonce words.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons are made, they don’t always include the very latest, strongest baselines. For example, while the paper mentions Luong & Manning (2016), citing a training time of 3 months, it does not detail a direct replication or comparison. A more comprehensive benchmark against current state-of-the-art approaches would strengthen the claims.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. The impact of different RNN sizes, learning rates, and embedding dimensions on performance is not fully explored, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the model.
3. **Detailed Architectural Explanation:** While the general structure is described, some components, like the specific affine function `aff()`, are not described in enough detail, making reproduction slightly harder.
4. **Qualitative Examples are Limited:** While examples of successful translations of misspelled and nonce words are provided, a broader qualitative analysis of translation errors and their relation to morphological complexity would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison to the strongest character-level NMT models available at the time of submission, including training times and computational costs?
2.  What was the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameters, particularly the RNN hidden state size and the learning rate?
3.  Can you provide more details on the choice of the affine function `aff()` used in the word encoder, and potentially explore alternative functions?
4.  Can you show a few examples of failure cases and analyze what causes the model to struggle with certain translations?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
* The related work section is comprehensive.
* The supplementary material should be reviewed carefully to understand the broader qualitative performance.
