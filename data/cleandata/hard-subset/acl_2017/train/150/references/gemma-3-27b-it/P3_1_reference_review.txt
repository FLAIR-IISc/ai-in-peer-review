## Review for Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) designed to address the limitations of both word-level and purely character-level NMT. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture that learns morphological structure within words using a dual RNN encoder, and then decodes at the character level using a novel hierarchical decoder. The authors claim their model achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art NMT systems on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation, while effectively learning morphological information and offering advantages in handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel word encoder leveraging RNNs to model morphology:** The approach of using two RNNs – one to represent preceding characters and another to weight their importance – to learn subword units is a conceptually interesting way to inject morphological awareness into the encoder.
2. **A practical hierarchical decoder for character-level NMT:** The proposed HGRU mechanism with a settable state provides an efficient way to decode at the character level, addressing the computational challenges of purely character-based models.
3. **Demonstration of competitive performance on multiple translation tasks:** The paper shows strong results on three language pairs, including morphologically rich languages, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a critical challenge in NMT:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem and the limitations of existing word and character-level approaches.  Morphology is undeniably a vital component for robust NMT, particularly for morphologically rich languages.
2. **Sound technical design:**  The proposed architecture is well-motivated and logically structured. The combination of the dual RNN encoder and the HGRU decoder appears to be a clever way to balance morphological modeling with decoding efficiency. The explanation of the HGRU mechanism and the unfolding procedure are clear.
3. **Empirically validated performance:** The results presented in Table 1 are encouraging. Achieving competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En, while maintaining a relatively small model size, is a significant achievement.  The comparison with BPE and other character-level models provides a good benchmark.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualizations (Figure 3) provide some evidence that the model is indeed learning to represent words based on morphological constituents.  The analysis on the energy weights assigned to characters is insightful.
5. **Handling of Misspelled and Nonce Words:** Demonstrating the ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words effectively showcases a practical benefit of character-level modeling and the model's robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, the individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoding) are not entirely novel. The contribution lies more in the specific architecture and how these components are integrated to address morphological modeling. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but weakens the argument for a breakthrough contribution.
2. **Insufficient baselines:** While the paper compares to a few baselines, it would be beneficial to include more recent and strong baselines, especially those incorporating subword segmentation techniques like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) with more sophisticated training strategies.  A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claims of superiority.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper primarily relies on BLEU scores.  A more detailed error analysis, including examples of translation failures and a discussion of their causes, would provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model.  Specifically, analyzing cases where morphology plays a crucial role in correct translation would be valuable.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices. It’s unclear how robust the performance is to different settings of learning rates, hidden unit sizes, and other key parameters.  A sensitivity analysis would enhance the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
5. **Clarity of Figure 2:** Figure 2, illustrating the full architecture, is quite dense and difficult to parse quickly. A more visually clear and simplified diagram would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the energy weights in Figure 4:** Could you provide a quantitative analysis of these weights? For instance, what is the average energy assigned to the last character of a morpheme compared to other characters?  This would strengthen the claim that the model is learning morphological boundaries.
2. **Baseline Comparison:**  Have you experimented with more recent and stronger BPE-based NMT systems (e.g., those utilizing subword regularization)? If so, how does your model compare to these? If not, why not?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a few specific examples of translation errors made by your model, along with a discussion of the potential reasons for those errors?  Specifically, where did morphology play a key role?
4. **Hyperparameter Search:** What was the process for selecting the hyperparameters used in your experiments? Was a systematic search performed, and if so, what were the results?
5. **Computational Cost:** While you mention the model's efficiency, a more detailed comparison of training time and memory usage compared to other character-level models would be valuable.




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized, but could benefit from a more concise presentation in some sections.
* It would be helpful to include a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of character-level NMT.  The proposed architecture is innovative and the empirical results are promising. While the novelty of the individual components is somewhat limited, the effective integration of these components and the demonstrated performance on challenging translation tasks warrant acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and its impact. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.
