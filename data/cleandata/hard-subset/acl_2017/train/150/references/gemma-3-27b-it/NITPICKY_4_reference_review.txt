This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, DCNMT, incorporating a novel word encoder leveraging morphological information and a hierarchical decoder. The primary claimed contribution is a new architecture addressing the OOV problem and improving translation performance, particularly for morphologically rich languages, while maintaining computational efficiency. A secondary contribution is demonstrating the model’s ability to learn morphological structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of techniques – character-level modeling, a morphological word encoder, and a hierarchical decoder – is presented as novel, each component draws heavily from existing work. The word encoder’s use of RNNs to weigh preceding characters feels reminiscent of attention mechanisms, and the hierarchical decoder echoes previous character-level decoding strategies. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight *how* this specific combination surpasses existing approaches beyond empirical results. It feels more like an engineering effort combining existing pieces rather than a fundamentally new theoretical advance.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper presents results for the full DCNMT model but lacks a comprehensive ablation study to isolate the contributions of each component. For instance, what is the performance gain from the morphological word encoder *alone* compared to a standard character-level encoder? Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the added complexity of the word encoder is truly justified, or if the performance gains are primarily driven by the character-level decoding. The mention of tanh normalization instead of sum normalization lacks justification – is this performance critical, or an arbitrary choice?

3. **Overstated Claims Regarding Efficiency:** The paper claims efficiency, particularly in comparison to Luong & Manning (2016), but the provided table only compares model size and training days.  A detailed comparison of training *speed* (e.g., seconds per epoch) and memory usage is crucial to substantiate this claim.  Simply stating it’s “efficient” is insufficient. The statement about being the “simplest and smallest” model is also potentially misleading – “simplest” is subjective and doesn't necessarily equate to efficiency.

4. **Presentation of Results & Hyperparameter Details:** The tables lack clarity.  The meaning of "Size" is ambiguous (number of parameters?). Providing the number of training steps alongside epochs is crucial for evaluating training convergence. The hyperparameters are briefly mentioned but lack detail – the dimensions of embedding layers beyond character embedding, dropout rates, optimizer details beyond the learning rate schedule. This hinders reproducibility.

5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper mentions the ability to translate misspelled words, but this is presented anecdotally. A more systematic error analysis, comparing the types of errors made by DCNMT versus baseline models, would provide a more robust evaluation of its strengths and weaknesses. The PCA visualization is interesting but its connection to actual translation quality is not explicitly demonstrated.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The reported BLEU scores are competitive with state-of-the-art character-level NMT models, particularly on the morphologically rich En-Cs and Cs-En tasks. This suggests the proposed architecture has merit and can achieve high translation quality.

2. **Addressing the OOV Problem:**  The character-level approach inherently mitigates the OOV issue, a significant advantage over word-based NMT. The authors correctly identify this as a key motivation for their work.

3. **Demonstration of Morphological Learning (Potentially):** The analysis in Section 5.2, while needing more rigor, provides some evidence that the model learns morphological information. The PCA visualization and analysis of character energies are promising initial steps.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed ablation study, evaluating the performance of DCNMT with and without the morphological word encoder, and with different normalization schemes for the character weights.
2.  Provide a more detailed comparison of training speed and memory usage between DCNMT and the baseline models, including metrics like seconds per epoch and peak memory consumption.
3.  Can you elaborate on the choice of tanh activation for the weight normalization? What experiments were conducted to determine its superiority?
4.  Provide a more comprehensive error analysis, comparing the types of errors made by DCNMT with those of the baselines.
5.  What is the specific size (number of parameters) of the word encoder and the decoder components individually?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but there are several minor grammatical errors and stylistic inconsistencies.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the model, such as potential difficulties with very long sentences or languages with extremely complex morphology.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a promising approach to character-level NMT, the lack of thorough ablation studies, the overstated claims about efficiency, and the incremental nature of the novelty lead me to recommend **rejection**. The empirical results are encouraging, but further investigation is needed to definitively establish the benefits of the proposed architecture and justify its complexity. The authors need to provide more convincing evidence that their combination of techniques delivers substantial improvements over existing methods.
