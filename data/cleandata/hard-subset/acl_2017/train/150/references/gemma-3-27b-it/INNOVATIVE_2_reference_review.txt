This paper introduces a novel, hierarchical character-level NMT architecture designed to address the limitations of both word-level and existing character-level models. The core contribution lies in the word encoder, which learns morphological structure via two RNNs – one for character representations and another for weighting those representations. This allows the model to implicitly learn subword units without relying on techniques like byte-pair encoding. The hierarchical decoder further refines the process, operating at both word and character levels.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of learning morphological structure *within* the encoder using weighted character representations is genuinely novel. While character-level NMT isn’t new, this approach attempts to bridge the gap between purely character-based models and traditional word-level approaches in a principled way. This is a bold idea, and a departure from prevalent subword segmentation techniques.
2. **Promising Results:** The BLEU scores presented, while not overwhelmingly superior to existing methods, are competitive, especially considering the model's complexity. Achieving comparable performance with a character-level model, and *without* explicit subword segmentation, is a significant achievement. The gains on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs, Cs-En) are particularly encouraging.
3. **Intriguing Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The visualizations in Figure 3 and the discussion around energy weights in Figure 4 provide some evidence that the model *is* learning morphological patterns. While more rigorous analysis would be ideal, this preliminary exploration is promising and suggests the core mechanism is functioning as intended. The qualitative examples in Table 2 further support this claim.
4. **Addressing OOV & Misspellings:** The ability to handle OOV words and even misspellings, stemming directly from the character-level approach, is a practical advantage and highlights the potential of the model for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Thorough Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares to existing models, there's a noticeable lack of ablation studies. For example, what is the contribution of the BiRNN in the word encoder compared to a unidirectional RNN? How much does the specific weighting scheme contribute compared to a simple average of character representations? Such studies are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of individual components.
2. **Complexity and Scalability:** The model contains six RNNs, which increases computational cost and training time. While the paper claims it is efficiently trainable, a more detailed analysis of training time and memory usage compared to simpler character-level models would be valuable. Scaling to larger datasets and more complex language pairs might pose challenges.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper mentions handling misspellings but lacks a systematic error analysis. Identifying common failure cases (e.g., specific morphological patterns the model struggles with) would strengthen the evaluation and provide directions for future work.
4. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is densely written and can be difficult to follow in places. The diagrams, while helpful, could be more clearly labeled and explained. Some of the mathematical notation requires careful reading.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detailed ablation studies, specifically examining the contribution of the BiRNN in the word encoder and the weighting scheme used for character representations?
2. What is the training time and memory footprint of your model compared to a simpler character-level NMT model (e.g., a single-layer character-level RNN)?
3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of errors the model makes, and relating those errors to morphological complexity or linguistic phenomena?
4. What are the limitations of the current model? Are there specific language families or morphological structures where you anticipate it will perform poorly?



Despite the weaknesses, the novelty of the approach and the promising results warrant strong consideration. The core idea – learning morphology within the encoder – is a significant contribution, and the paper provides a compelling starting point for future research in character-level NMT. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses in a future iteration of the work. The flaws aren’t critical, but strengthening the experimental validation and clarifying the presentation would greatly enhance the paper’s impact.
