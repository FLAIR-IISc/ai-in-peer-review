This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model with a novel word encoder designed to learn morphology and a hierarchical decoder. The core idea is to decompose words into subword units automatically via recurrent networks, rather than relying on pre-defined segmentation like byte-pair encoding. The model aims to address the OOV problem and improve translation quality, particularly for morphologically rich languages. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of a recurrent neural network-based morphological analyzer *within* an end-to-end NMT system. While character-level NMT isn't new, integrating a learnable morphological component and demonstrating its impact is a reasonable undertaking. A secondary contribution lies in the hierarchical decoder architecture, designed to efficiently decode character sequences. The reported results on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En demonstrate comparable or slightly improved performance over existing character-level baselines, which constitutes a minor contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the well-known OOV issue in NMT and attempts to address it in a principled manner by explicitly modeling morphology. This is a long-standing problem, and innovative solutions are valuable.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture and, in particular, the word encoder utilizing RNNs to learn morphological structure are conceptually interesting. The attempt to move beyond simple embedding lookups and toward a more structured representation of words is commendable.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to other character-level models, especially on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs and Cs-En). This provides some evidence that the proposed approach is viable.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) attempts to demonstrate the model’s ability to learn morphological features, providing a qualitative, albeit limited, insight into the model’s inner workings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Strong Baseline Comparison:** While the authors compare against existing character-level models, the comparison is incomplete. Critically, they do not compare against strong *word-level* baselines with large vocabularies and effective OOV handling techniques (e.g., BPE, subword NMT with copy mechanisms). The claim of "comparable to state-of-the-art" is weakened without this crucial comparison. It's unclear whether the gains are significant enough to justify the increased complexity.
2. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the individual contributions of the word encoder and hierarchical decoder. For instance, what is the performance of the model *without* the morphological encoder? How much does the hierarchical decoder contribute compared to a standard character-level decoder? Without these experiments, it’s difficult to assess the effectiveness of each component.
3. **Overstated Novelty:** The use of RNNs to learn representations is not novel in itself. The specific application to morphological analysis within an NMT system is a worthwhile exploration, but the paper sometimes overstates the originality of the individual components. The method resembles compositional approaches to morphology learning in other contexts.
4. **Training Efficiency Concerns:** The authors mention training for only one epoch to achieve competitive results. While they claim the model is efficient, this raises concerns about convergence and generalization. A more thorough analysis of training curves and hyperparameter sensitivity is needed.
5. **Qualitative Analysis is Limited:** While the PCA visualization is a good start, the analysis of morphology learning is superficial. A more detailed analysis of the learned representations – perhaps by examining specific morpheme representations or performing linguistic analysis of translation errors – would strengthen the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a direct comparison against strong word-level NMT baselines (e.g., using BPE with a large vocabulary) on all translation tasks.
2.  Provide ablation studies demonstrating the individual contributions of the word encoder and hierarchical decoder.  Specifically, what is the performance when the morphological encoder is removed, or replaced with a simpler embedding lookup?
3.  Please provide training curves (BLEU score vs. epoch) to demonstrate convergence and generalization ability.
4.  Can you provide more concrete examples of how the learned representations reflect morphological structure, beyond the PCA visualization? For instance, can you identify specific RNN hidden states that correspond to known morphemes?
