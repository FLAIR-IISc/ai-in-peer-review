This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model with a novel word encoder designed to learn morphology and a hierarchical decoder. The primary contribution is the architecture itself – a six-layer recurrent network aiming to bridge the gap between word-level and character-level NMT, purportedly addressing the challenges of OOV words and training instability associated with purely character-level models. A secondary contribution is demonstrating comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks. A tertiary contribution lies in the analysis attempting to show the model learns morphological structures.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant problem:** The issue of OOV words and the difficulties of training purely character-level NMT models are well-established. The attempt to find a middle ground, leveraging morphological information, is a reasonable approach.
2. **Sound experimental setup:** The authors compare their model to several baselines (though some are cited rather than directly replicated – see weaknesses). The inclusion of morphologically rich languages (Czech) is a positive step toward a more comprehensive evaluation. The use of BLEU as a metric is standard and appropriate.
3. **Hierarchical Decoder is a plausible approach:** The hierarchical decoder, setting the character-level decoder state from the word-level decoder, appears to be a logical way to guide character generation and potentially address the long-sequence problem of character-level models.
4. **Demonstrated Competitive Performance:** Achieving BLEU scores comparable to existing state-of-the-art systems, particularly given the complexity of the model, is a noteworthy result, even if not a substantial improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core ideas—using RNNs to encode morphology and a hierarchical decoder—are not entirely novel. Compositional character-to-word models (Ling et al., 2015a,b) and hybrid word-character models (Luong & Manning, 2016) have already explored similar concepts. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate *how* its approach significantly advances the field beyond these prior works.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While baselines are included, several are cited rather than implemented and evaluated directly (e.g., bpe2bpe, char). This makes it difficult to assess the true benefit of the proposed model. A direct, head-to-head comparison with well-implemented baselines is crucial. The authors state that the Luong and Manning (2016) model took 3 months to train, using this as justification for their quicker training time – this is not a valid comparison.
3. **Morphological Analysis is Weak:** The analysis attempting to show the model learns morphology (Figures 3 and 4) is superficial. While the PCA plot suggests some separation of words based on morphology, it's not particularly compelling evidence. A more rigorous analysis, perhaps involving probing the learned representations or examining the model's behavior on morphologically complex words, is needed. The detection of subword units (Figure 5) is similarly unconvincing; the results appear limited.
4. **Model Complexity and Scalability:** The model contains six RNNs, which increases complexity. The paper mentions computational challenges but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost. The training efficiency claims seem overstated given the model size. While the authors claim their model is the simplest and smallest, the parameter count is comparable to other models in Table 1.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Examining the types of errors the model makes (e.g., incorrect morphological inflections, handling of rare words) would provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your word encoder to existing compositional character models (Ling et al., 2015a,b) and hybrid word-character models (Luong & Manning, 2016), specifically highlighting the key differences and advantages of your approach?
2.  Will you commit to running the cited baselines (bpe2bpe, char) yourself and presenting the results in a revised version of the paper?
3. Can you provide a more rigorous analysis of the learned representations, perhaps by probing them with morphological features or examining the model's ability to generalize to unseen morphological variations?
4. What is the memory footprint and training time per epoch for your model compared to the baselines? A detailed computational analysis would be beneficial.
5. Can you provide examples of translation errors, specifically focusing on cases where the model struggles with morphology or rare words?
