This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of word-level models, particularly the OOV problem and the difficulty of handling morphology. The core idea is a hierarchical architecture incorporating a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations, coupled with a hierarchical decoder that generates output at the character level. 

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed hierarchical architecture combining a morphology-aware word encoder and a character-level decoder. This allows the model to circumvent the need for a large vocabulary while still capturing subword information relevant for translation, particularly in morphologically rich languages. A second, supporting contribution is the design of the word encoder itself, which utilizes two RNNs – one for character representation and another for weighting those representations – to effectively learn morphemes. Finally, the efficient training of a deep model (six recurrent networks) and demonstration of competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks are valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NMT Challenge:** The paper directly tackles the well-known OOV problem in NMT, and proposes a compelling solution leveraging character-level modeling. This is a significant issue, particularly for low-resource or morphologically rich languages, making the research relevant and impactful.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture with the dedicated morphology-aware word encoder is a well-motivated and novel design. The separation of morpheme representation learning from word combination weighting is a clever idea that appears to be effective, as supported by the results and analysis.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive BLEU scores on three translation tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness. Importantly, the results on the morphologically rich Czech-English tasks suggest the model *is* successfully leveraging morphological information, which is crucial. The comparison to other character-level and subword models provides a good benchmark.
4. **Efficient Training:** Despite the model’s depth (six RNNs), the authors demonstrate efficient training. The comparison of training time and model size to other approaches highlights a practical advantage of their method.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) and analysis of character energies (Figure 4) provide compelling evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful morphological units. The example of correctly translating “convenienter” further supports this claim.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Hierarchical Approach:** While the *combination* of the proposed components is novel, hierarchical character-to-word approaches are not entirely new (e.g., Luong & Manning, 2016). The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how their approach differs significantly from prior work in terms of architecture and training methodology, beyond simply mentioning the differences.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of the architecture. For example, how much performance is gained by including the morphology-aware word encoder *vs.* a simpler word encoder?  What is the impact of the specific RNN architecture within the encoder and decoder? This would strengthen the argument for the design choices.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The training details section is relatively brief. It is unclear how sensitive the results are to various hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensions, learning rate, RNN hidden unit size). A brief analysis of hyperparameter tuning would increase confidence in the robustness of the model.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the examples in Table 2 are insightful, a more systematic error analysis would be valuable. What types of errors does the model still make? Are there specific morphological phenomena that it struggles with? Identifying these limitations would provide directions for future research.
5. **Presentation & Clarity:** Some parts of the explanation are dense and could benefit from clearer phrasing. For instance, the description of the HGRU unit and the matrix unfolding step in Section 3.2 are difficult to follow without carefully reading multiple times.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your architecture to previous hierarchical character-to-word NMT models (e.g., Luong & Manning, 2016), specifically highlighting the key differences and advantages of your approach?
2.  What was the impact of different activation functions in the weight computation ( *wt* in Section 3.1)? Why was *tanh* chosen over other options like *sigmoid* or ReLU?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the vocabulary size of characters used during training? Would increasing the number of characters significantly impact results?
4.  Can you provide examples where the model *fails* to correctly translate words due to morphological complexity?  This would help understand the current limitations of the approach.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears to be incomplete, with some citations missing full details.
*   Consider adding a diagram visualizing the entire model architecture in a single figure, rather than breaking it up into Figures 1 and 2. This would aid understanding.
*   The supplementary material mentioned in Table 2 should be included with the submission.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and well-motivated approach to character-level NMT. The proposed architecture demonstrates promising results, particularly on morphologically rich languages. While some weaknesses remain, the strengths of the work outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the concerns raised in the weaknesses section and providing answers to the questions posed. The authors should focus on clarifying the novelty of their approach, conducting a more thorough ablation study, and providing a more detailed error analysis.
