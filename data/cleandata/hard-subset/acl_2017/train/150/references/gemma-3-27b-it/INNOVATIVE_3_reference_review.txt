This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) leveraging a novel word encoder based on recurrent neural networks to learn morphological structure, coupled with a hierarchical decoder. The core contribution is the architecture itself, aiming to bridge the gap between purely word-based and character-based NMT, offering the benefits of both without the vocabulary limitations of the former or the computational hurdles of the latter. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, alongside analysis suggesting the model learns morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Architecture:** The hierarchical architecture – character -> subword -> word -> sentence – is a genuinely interesting and potentially impactful approach. The attempt to *learn* subword units (morphemes) rather than relying on pre-defined segmentation algorithms like BPE is a significant departure and a bold move. This learning of morphological structure within the encoder is the most compelling aspect of the work.
2. **Addressing a Core NMT Problem:** The paper directly tackles the OOV problem in NMT, a persistent challenge. The character-level decoding elegantly sidesteps the need for massive vocabularies and the associated complexity.
3. **Promising Empirical Results:** Achieving competitive BLEU scores, particularly on morphologically rich languages like Czech, suggests the approach is viable. While not a dramatic leap over existing SOTA, the performance is respectable given the inherent challenges of character-level modeling. The results demonstrate the method isn't simply a theoretical exercise.
4. **Qualitative Analysis Supports Claims:** The PCA visualization and analysis of character energies in Figure 3 and 4 are valuable in supporting the claim that the model is, in fact, learning something about morphology. This provides insight beyond purely quantitative metrics.
5. **Efficiency Considerations:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to address the computational challenges often associated with character-level models. The discussion of efficient training and the model size comparison (Table 1) is important, showing awareness of practical limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper demonstrates the model *works*, it lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. How much does the morphological encoder *actually* improve performance compared to a simpler character-level encoder? What is the impact of the hierarchical decoder design choices? Without these analyses, it’s hard to definitively say which parts are essential.
2. **Superficial Error Analysis:** The example translations in Table 2 are suggestive, but a more systematic error analysis would strengthen the paper. A quantitative assessment of how well the model handles OOV words, morphological variations, or misspelled words would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss hyperparameter tuning in detail. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to changes in learning rate, RNN size, or other crucial parameters. This leaves open the possibility that the reported performance is contingent on specific, potentially brittle, settings.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to BPE models is relevant, but the paper could benefit from a more direct comparison to other recent character-level NMT approaches, especially those that also attempt to address the efficiency issue.
5. **Presentation Could Be More Concise:** The paper is somewhat verbose, and the explanation of the hierarchical decoder feels a little convoluted. Streamlining the presentation would improve clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide ablation study results demonstrating the impact of the morphological encoder compared to a baseline character-level encoder *without* the learned morphology?
2.  What hyperparameter tuning was performed, and what sensitivity analyses were conducted to ensure the reported results are robust?
3. Could you perform a more quantitative error analysis, specifically focusing on how the model handles OOV words, morphological variations, and misspelled words compared to a standard word-level NMT model?
4.  The unfolding of the first-level decoder outputs to create the input for the second-level decoder feels a bit ad-hoc. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind this specific approach, and explore alternative methods?



**Additional Comments:**

The discussion of related work is adequate, but could be more focused. The reference to Ling et al. (2015a) seems somewhat tangential. The use of Theano and Blocks is noted but feels dated given the prevalence of PyTorch and TensorFlow.



Overall, despite some methodological shortcomings, I find this paper presents a novel and promising approach to character-level NMT. The boldness of the architecture and the initial empirical results warrant acceptance. The weaknesses are addressable in a follow-up version, and the potential impact of the work justifies overlooking them at this stage. I lean towards **accept** with minor revisions.
