This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model incorporating a novel word encoder based on recurrent neural networks to learn morphological structure, coupled with a hierarchical decoder. The authors aim to address the limitations of both word-level (large vocabulary, OOV issues) and traditional character-level models (computational cost, difficulty in training). The primary contribution is the proposed architecture which attempts to learn subword units *automatically* and integrate them into a hierarchical encoder-decoder framework. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation on En-Fr, En-Cs and Cs-En tasks, demonstrating comparable performance to state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a long-standing challenge in NMT: effectively handling morphology and the open-vocabulary problem. While not a novel problem, the authors propose a potentially valuable architectural approach.
2. **Clear Architecture Description:** The proposed model architecture is clearly explained and illustrated with diagrams (Figure 1 & 2), making it easy to understand the key components and their interactions. The explanation of the hierarchical decoder and its integration with the word encoder is particularly well done.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The reported BLEU scores are competitive with other character-level and subword-level models, suggesting that the proposed approach is viable and demonstrates practical performance gains. The claim of comparable performance to SOTA models, while needing careful scrutiny given the rapid advancements in the field, is a positive indicator.
4. **Intriguing Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The visualization in Figure 3 and associated discussion regarding energy weights demonstrate a potentially insightful understanding of morphological segmentation, suggesting the model *is* learning something about subword structure. The attempt to analyze the learned representations is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of components is presented as novel, the individual components themselves (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoders) are well-established in the NMT literature. The paper fails to adequately justify *why* this specific combination should outperform simpler alternatives or existing methods. The claims regarding automatic subword learning are weakened by the existing success of byte-pair encoding (BPE) which is not sufficiently addressed. The authors state their method learns subword units "automatically" but don't rigorously compare this process to BPE or other unsupervised subword segmentation techniques.
2. **Insufficient Baselines and Ablation Studies:** The comparison to baseline models is limited. While BLEU scores are reported, there's a lack of detailed comparison against strong, readily available baselines, particularly BPE-based NMT systems with comparable model sizes and training regimes. Furthermore, the paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture. For example, what is the performance gain from the morphological word encoder alone? What is the impact of the hierarchical decoder? This makes it difficult to assess the true effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper mentions some examples of correct translation of misspelled words (Table 2), but a more systematic error analysis would be highly beneficial. A thorough investigation of the types of errors the model makes (e.g., morphological errors, syntactic errors) would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
4. **Training Details and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The training details, while present, are somewhat sparse. It’s unclear how sensitive the performance is to hyperparameter choices. A discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would strengthen the paper. The extremely short training time for the reported best results (1 epoch) is also concerning.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison to strong BPE-based NMT baselines, including model size and training time?
2.  Please provide the results of ablation studies to assess the individual contributions of the morphological word encoder and the hierarchical decoder.
3.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process and the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings?
4.  Could you provide a more comprehensive error analysis, focusing on the types of errors the model makes, and how these errors relate to morphological complexity?
5. What is the computational cost (training time, inference time, memory usage) of your model compared to a standard word-level NMT system with a similar number of parameters?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper’s conclusions are a bit strong given the lack of definitive evidence and the limited comparison to baselines. The supplementary material should include a more detailed analysis of the learned representations and error cases.
