This paper introduces a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model aiming to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and improve translation quality, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The core contribution lies in a hierarchical architecture combining a novel word encoder learning morphemes and a character-level decoder. The word encoder utilizes two RNNs – one for character representation and another for weighting these representations – to implicitly learn subword units without relying on byte-pair encoding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed architecture is genuinely interesting. Moving beyond simple character-level models or subword segmentation (like BPE) to *learn* morphology via a dual-RNN word encoder is a bold and potentially impactful idea. This is not a mere incremental improvement; it explores a different path for handling morphology in NMT.
2. **Addressing a Core Problem:** OOV words remain a significant bottleneck in NMT. This work directly tackles this issue with a theoretically sound approach – learning internal word structure. The ability to translate misspelled words or nonce words, demonstrated in the experiments, highlights the potential benefit.
3. **Empirical Validation:**  The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, achieving comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art models. While the gains aren’t massive, they’re substantial enough given the inherent difficulty of character-level modeling and the model’s complexity. The included analysis regarding morphology learning (PCA visualization in Figure 3) is encouraging.
4. **Efficient Training Despite Complexity:** Despite having six RNNs, the authors claim efficient training. The details in Section 3.2 outlining the HGRU and the matrix unfolding for efficient batch processing suggest a thoughtful engineering effort.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Morphology Learning:** While the PCA visualization provides *some* evidence of morphology learning, it's somewhat superficial. The authors need to provide more rigorous analysis—e.g., probing for morpheme boundaries in unseen words, analyzing attention weights, or examining representations of related words—to convincingly demonstrate the model truly "learns" morphology in a meaningful way. The examples given are helpful, but not conclusive.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning. The optimal configuration might be heavily dependent on the language pair and dataset. A more thorough ablation study exploring the influence of different hyperparameters (e.g., RNN sizes, learning rates) would strengthen the claims.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparisons primarily focus on existing character-level models. A more direct comparison to strong word-level or subword-level models (e.g., Transformer-based architectures) would provide a clearer picture of the model's strengths and weaknesses. The argument that it is "comparable" to SOTA feels a little weak without a proper comparison.
4. **Presentation could be tighter:** The writing, while clear, is somewhat repetitive. Condensing some sections and streamlining the explanations would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned representations to demonstrate how the model captures and utilizes morphological information? Specifically, could you share examples of attention weights focusing on morpheme boundaries?
2. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of hyperparameters? Please present a table summarizing the results of an ablation study exploring different configurations.
3.  Have you experimented with larger datasets or deeper recurrent networks? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the computational limitations preventing you from doing so?
4.  Why was tanh used for normalization in the word encoder, rather than other normalization techniques? What impact does this choice have on the model’s performance?

**Overall:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a novel and promising approach to neural machine translation. The idea of learning morphology implicitly through a dual-RNN word encoder is a significant contribution, and the initial results are encouraging. I'm willing to overlook the limited analysis and presentation issues given the potential impact of the work. I lean towards **Accept**.
