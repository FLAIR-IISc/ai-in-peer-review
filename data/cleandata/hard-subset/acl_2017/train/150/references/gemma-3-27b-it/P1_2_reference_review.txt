## Review for ACL Submission: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

**Summary:**

This paper presents a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the limitations of traditional word-level NMT, particularly the OOV problem. The core idea is to incorporate morphological information by using a novel word encoder based on two RNNs: one to represent preceding characters and another to weight their importance. A hierarchical decoder then translates at the character level, utilizing the word-level encoder's output. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, claiming their model achieves comparable results to state-of-the-art systems while being more efficient and capable of learning morphological structures.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Hierarchical Character-Level Decoding:** The proposed hierarchical decoder, leveraging a settable state mechanism (HGRU), offers a more efficient approach to character-level decoding than previous methods, particularly avoiding the costly forward passes described in Luong & Manning (2016). This contributes to the practical feasibility of deep character-level NMT.
2. **Morphology-Aware Word Encoder:** While not entirely novel in its use of RNNs, the architecture of the word encoder – specifically the weighting mechanism for preceding characters – represents a sensible attempt to explicitly model morphology within the encoder, potentially improving generalization to unseen words.
3. **Demonstrated Feasibility of Deep Character-Level NMT:** The authors successfully train a relatively deep model (6 RNNs) end-to-end, demonstrating that complex character-level models *can* be effectively trained, a point that has historically been a challenge.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles the well-known OOV problem in NMT in a principled and potentially effective manner. Focusing on character-level modeling is a strong approach, and the proposed architecture aims to improve upon existing character-level models.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented are competitive with state-of-the-art models, particularly considering the model's relative simplicity (in terms of parameter count - see Table 1). The improvement on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs, Cs-En) suggests the morphological awareness is indeed beneficial.
3. **Efficient Decoder Design:** The HGRU decoder is a clever innovation that significantly improves decoding efficiency compared to alternatives. The explanation of how the unfolding process works is clear and demonstrates a thoughtful approach to batch training.
4. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The visualizations (Figures 3 & 5) offer compelling, though not entirely conclusive, evidence that the model is learning something about morphological structure. The analysis of character weights in the word encoder is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** The individual components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical decoding) are not fundamentally new. The novelty lies in the *combination* and specific design choices, which, while sensible, might not be substantial enough for a top-tier conference.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to fully understand the contribution of each component. For instance, how much does the weighted character RNN encoder *specifically* contribute compared to a simple character-level embedding lookup? What is the impact of the HGRU versus a standard GRU? Without this, it's hard to assess the true value of the proposed innovations.
3. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the authors compare to relevant baselines, some of these baselines (e.g., BPE) seem a bit dated.  A comparison against more recent subword segmentation techniques (e.g., WordPiece) would strengthen the evaluation. Furthermore, a comparison to a simple character-level model without the morphological encoder would be valuable.
4. **Subjectivity in Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis of morphology learning (Figures 3 & 5) is somewhat subjective. More rigorous quantitative metrics for evaluating morphological awareness would be desirable.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ablation Studies:** Please provide ablation studies demonstrating the individual contribution of the weighted character RNN encoder and the HGRU decoder to the overall performance.
2. **Subword Baselines:** Could you compare your model to more recent subword segmentation techniques like WordPiece?
3. **Character-Level Baseline:**  How does your model perform compared to a standard character-level NMT model *without* the proposed morphology-aware word encoder?
4. **Morphology Evaluation:** Can you propose and implement quantitative metrics to measure the morphological awareness of your model more rigorously?
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality of the RNN hidden states?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear, but some sentences are awkwardly phrased. A careful review for grammatical errors and clarity would be beneficial.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The figures are helpful, but could benefit from clearer labeling and potentially more examples.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of character-level NMT. The proposed architecture is well-motivated and demonstrates promising results. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, their combination and the resulting efficiency gains are valuable. However, the lack of comprehensive ablation studies and comparison to stronger baselines weakens the paper's claims.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the ablation studies and baseline comparisons.
