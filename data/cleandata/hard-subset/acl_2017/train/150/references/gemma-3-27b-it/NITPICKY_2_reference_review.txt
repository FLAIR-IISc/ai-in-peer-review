This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and leverage morphological information. The core idea involves a hierarchical encoder-decoder architecture with a novel word encoder based on two RNNs – one for preceding character representations and another for weighting these representations. A hierarchical decoder then generates translations at the character level. The authors claim to achieve competitive performance on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks while efficiently training a deep model.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of a specific hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT that attempts to explicitly model subword units (morphemes) without relying on pre-defined segmentation algorithms like byte-pair encoding. While not entirely novel in its components (RNNs, attention, hierarchical structures), the combination and specific implementation details present a reasonable approach to the problem. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of relatively efficient training for a deep character-level model, a known challenge. Finally, the empirical evaluation on multiple language pairs, including a morphologically rich language (Czech), provides some evidence of the model’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The OOV problem in NMT is a significant bottleneck. Exploring character-level models is a valid direction to mitigate this issue, and this paper tackles it directly.
2. **Clear Architectural Design:** The proposed hierarchical architecture is clearly described and logically motivated. The word encoder's two-RNN structure, intended to capture morphological information, is a well-defined component. The diagrams (Figures 1 and 2) are helpful in understanding the model’s flow.
3. **Comparative Results:** The paper presents results on three language pairs, comparing against several baselines, including BPE-based and other character-level models. While not overwhelmingly superior, the reported BLEU scores are competitive.
4. **Efficiency Claim:** The authors claim and, to some extent, demonstrate efficient training compared to other character-level models. The reported training times and model sizes are relevant and support this claim.
5. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The visualization in Figure 3 attempting to show separation of words with similar morphologies provides some qualitative evidence supporting the claim of learning morphological structure. The observation regarding the weight assigned to the beginning and end of morphemes is interesting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** While the combination of components is reasonable, the individual components themselves are not novel. RNNs, attention mechanisms, and hierarchical decoders are all well-established in the NMT literature. The claimed "novel architecture" feels more like a specific arrangement of existing techniques.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to justify the design choices. For example, what is the impact of the BiRNN in the word encoder? What is the benefit of the tanh activation for normalization compared to other options? Without such analysis, it's difficult to assess the contribution of each component.
3. **Qualitative Analysis is Limited:** The morphology analysis, while intriguing, relies on a two-dimensional PCA projection. This is a rather simplistic representation and doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the model has truly learned meaningful morphological representations. A more robust analysis, such as examining the learned weights or performing a controlled experiment on morphologically related words, would be more persuasive.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters.  Were the hyperparameters carefully tuned?  Are the results robust across different settings? This is crucial for evaluating the practical applicability of the model.
5. **Formatting and Presentation:** The paper suffers from several formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing after periods, hyphenation issues) and minor grammatical errors. While not major, these detract from the overall professionalism of the presentation. Equations are not always clearly explained in the surrounding text.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study to quantify the contribution of each component in your proposed architecture, particularly the BiRNN in the word encoder and the tanh activation for normalization?
2.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process and provide insights into the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings?
3.  Beyond the PCA visualization, can you present additional evidence demonstrating that the model has learned meaningful morphological representations, perhaps through a controlled experiment involving morphologically related words?
4.  The paper mentions the "HGRU" as a variant of GRU. Can you elaborate on the specific modifications made to the standard GRU and provide justification for these changes?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is not formatted consistently.
* The sentence "Thus learning morphology in a word encoder might speedup training" is weak phrasing. It should be stated with more confidence if the authors believe it to be true.
* Table 1 is difficult to read due to the use of abbreviations and inconsistent formatting.



Overall, while the paper presents a reasonable approach to character-level NMT and addresses a relevant problem, the lack of substantial novelty, the limited ablation studies, and the superficial qualitative analysis raise significant concerns. The numerous minor flaws in formatting and presentation further detract from the paper’s impact. I lean towards **rejection** due to these shortcomings. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, along with a more compelling demonstration of the model's benefits, would be necessary for reconsideration.
