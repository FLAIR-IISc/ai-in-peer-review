This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model (DCNMT) with a hierarchical architecture, aiming to address the challenges of OOV words and improve translation performance, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The core idea is to learn morphology via a word encoder composed of two RNNs, followed by a hierarchical decoder. The authors claim to achieve comparable performance to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed hierarchical architecture combining a morphology-aware word encoder with a character-level decoder. While character-level NMT is not new, the specific way of incorporating morphological information through the dual-RNN word encoder and the hierarchical decoding process with a modified GRU (HGRU) is a novel approach. A second contribution is the empirical demonstration of the model’s performance on multiple language pairs, including morphologically rich languages like Czech. The experiments provide evidence that the model can handle OOV words effectively and learn morphological representations, shown by the PCA visualization and translation of misspelled/nonce words.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Architecture:** The combination of the dual-RNN word encoder and the HGRU-based hierarchical decoder presents a novel architectural design for character-level NMT. This approach attempts to bridge the gap between word-level and character-level modeling by explicitly incorporating morphological information. The detailed explanation of the HGRU mechanism and its efficient implementation is commendable.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide extensive experimental results on three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), demonstrating competitive performance compared to other character-level and subword-level models.  The inclusion of morphologically rich languages (Czech) is a strong point, as it highlights the potential benefits of morphology-aware modeling.
3. **Analysis of Morphology Learning:** The PCA visualization and examples of translating misspelled/nonce words provide some qualitative evidence that the model successfully learns morphological representations.  The comparison with a standard RNN word encoder further supports the benefit of the proposed approach.
4. **Efficient Implementation:** The authors address the training efficiency concerns often associated with character-level models. They present a method for efficiently decoding at the character level, avoiding the computational overhead of previous approaches. The model’s relatively small size compared to other models is also a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* of components is novel, individual components (RNNs, GRUs, attention) are well-established in the NMT literature. The contribution stemming solely from using these components feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Morphology:** Although the authors demonstrate *some* evidence of morphology learning, the analysis is limited.  A more thorough quantitative analysis, such as evaluating the model’s ability to predict morphological features or decompose words into morphemes, would strengthen the claims. The visual inspection via PCA, while helpful, is not conclusive.
3. **Lack of Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** The comparison with baseline models lacks a strong contemporary baseline. While comparisons are made to existing papers, it is unclear if the latest and strongest models for each language pair have been considered. Including results from more recent, state-of-the-art models (e.g., Transformer-based models) would provide a clearer understanding of the DCNMT’s performance.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis. The optimal hyperparameters may be crucial to achieving the reported performance, and the robustness of the model to different hyperparameter settings remains unclear.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed quantitative analysis of the morphology learning capabilities of your model? For example, can the model accurately predict morphological features (e.g., gender, number) of words?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the size of the RNN hidden states and the learning rate? Did you perform a thorough hyperparameter search?
3.  Have you compared your model against more recent and stronger baseline models, such as Transformer-based NMT systems, on the same datasets? If so, what were the results?
4.  The paper mentions applying the model to speech recognition and text summarization. Could you elaborate on the potential adaptations and challenges of applying the DCNMT architecture to these tasks?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The organization of the paper is generally clear, but some sections could benefit from more concise explanations.
*   The figures are helpful but could be improved with more detailed annotations.
*   The notation is generally consistent, but some equations could be clarified.
