- Summary: Proposes a deep character-level NMT with a novel word encoder (two RNNs) and a hierarchical decoder to learn morphology and handle OOVs and misspellings.

- Contribution: Novel word encoder that learns morphological structure from character-level representations.

- Contribution: Hierarchical decoder (HGRU) for efficient character-level generation.

- Contribution: Empirical evidence that learned word representations capture meaningful morphological relationships.

- Strength: HGRU design enables efficient batch training without extra forward passes, addressing a practical decoding bottleneck.

- Strength: PCA and representation analyses show convincing morphological clustering (e.g., "notable" vs. "notability").

- Strength: Practical OOV/misspelling handling demonstrated (e.g., correctly translating misspelled or rare forms).

- Weakness: Novelty is questionable—word encoder resembles attention/composition approaches like Ling et al. (2015b) and lacks clear differentiation.

- Weakness: Comparative baselines are inadequate; missing comparisons with transformer-based state-of-the-art models.

- Weakness: Training efficiency claims (1 epoch vs. 3 months) are unsubstantiated due to missing hardware/batch/training details and unfair comparisons.

- Weakness: Limited analysis of character-level NMT limitations, such as longer sequence costs and performance on morphologically poor languages.

- Question: How does your word encoder theoretically differ from the attention mechanism in Ling et al. (2015b)?

- Question: Please provide detailed training settings (GPU type/number, batch size) and clarify whether baselines were trained on the same hardware.

- Question: Why were transformer-based approaches not compared, and how would your architecture integrate with transformers?

- Overall assessment: Interesting practical contributions (hierarchical decoder, morphology evidence, OOV handling) but overstated novelty and limited evaluation reduce impact; acceptable but not groundbreaking.
