- Summary: Proposes DCNMT, a character-level NMT with a morpheme-learning word encoder (two RNNs + weighting) and a hierarchical decoder that predicts words then characters to handle OOVs and misspellings while maintaining competitive performance.

- Contribution 1: Novel word encoder that explicitly learns morphology by decomposing words into morphemes and learning their composition.

- Contribution 2: Hierarchical decoder that decodes efficiently at the character level while preserving word-level modeling benefits.

- Contribution 3: Empirical demonstration that character-level modeling with explicit morphology learning can address OOVs and achieve competitive BLEU.

- Strength 1: Effectively handles OOVs, misspellings, and nonce words that word-level models output as <unk>.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical evidence (PCA, analysis) that the model learns meaningful morphological representations grouping related words.

- Strength 3: Claims much faster training than prior character-level models due to hierarchical decoder and HGRU enabling efficient batching.

- Strength 4: Achieves competitive translation quality (e.g., ~30.56 BLEU En-Fr) at the character level.

- Weakness 1: Unclear novelty over BPE; BPE baselines outperform DCNMT, so superiority is not demonstrated.

- Weakness 2: Training efficiency claims are misleading (likely confusion between epochs and days), undermining the fast-training argument.

- Weakness 3: No ablation study to separate the effects of morphology learning versus hierarchical decoder, leaving contributions conflated.

- Weakness 4: Insufficient analysis of when morphology learning is most beneficial (e.g., highly inflected languages vs morphologically poor languages).

- Weakness 5: Weak theoretical justification for why morphology learning should improve translation; the connection to translation quality is assumed rather than proven.

- Question 1: How do the learned subword units qualitatively compare to BPE in quality and interpretability?

- Question 2: How does DCNMT performance after 1 epoch compare to BPE after 1 epoch for a fair training-efficiency comparison?

- Question 3: How exactly does the model generate nonce words like "convenienter"—what morphological rules or representations enable this?

- Question 4: Why is performance better on Cs→En than En→Cs—directionality or other factors?

- Overall assessment: Promising architecture with strong empirical points on morphology and OOV handling, but issues (novelty vs BPE, misleading efficiency claims, missing ablations, limited analysis/theory) weaken the contribution — recommendation: Weak Reject.
