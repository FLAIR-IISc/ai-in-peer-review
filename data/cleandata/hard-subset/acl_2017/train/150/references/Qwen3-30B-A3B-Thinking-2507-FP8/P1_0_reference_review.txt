

# Peer Review: "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural machine translation (NMT) model called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology explicitly. The key contributions are:

1. A novel word encoder that processes character sequences using two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combinations, rather than relying on a fixed vocabulary.
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates characters conditioned on word-level representations.
3. Demonstrated effectiveness in handling OOV words, misspelled words, and nonce words, with competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art models.

The authors claim their model achieves the best of both worlds: the flexibility of character-level models without the OOV problem, and the efficiency of word-level models without the need for large vocabularies.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to morphology learning**: The paper's most significant contribution is the word encoder that explicitly learns morphemes through character processing. This is a principled approach to the OOV problem, as demonstrated by the PCA projections in Figure 3 showing meaningful word representations where semantically similar words (e.g., "reliable" and "reliability") are closer together. The energy weights in Figure 4 further validate that the model learns morpheme boundaries (e.g., "reliability" is segmented as "reli" + "ability").

2. **Effective handling of OOV and misspelled words**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, while word-level models would fail. Table 2 shows that DCNMT correctly translates "convenienter" as "plus pratique" (more practical), while word-level models produce "UNK" tokens. This is a significant practical advantage for real-world translation systems.

3. **Competitive performance with lower training cost**: The paper demonstrates that DCNMT achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models but with significantly lower training time. As shown in Table 1, DCNMT trains in approximately 7 days for En-Fr (vs. 3 months for Luong and Manning, 2016), while achieving better BLEU scores (30.56 vs. 29.31). This efficiency makes the model more practical for research and deployment.

4. **Clear analysis of the word encoder**: The authors provide detailed analysis of how their word encoder learns morphology through energy weights and PCA projections, which strongly supports their claim that the model learns meaningful morphological structure. The analysis of words like "anything" and "everything" (Figure 4) demonstrates the model's ability to generalize to unseen words.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent character-level models**: The paper compares with several character-level models but misses comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art approaches (e.g., 2017-2018 work). For instance, it cites Lee et al. (2016) as a character-level model but doesn't compare with more recent character-level models that might have improved performance. This limits the ability to assess how truly competitive DCNMT is in the current landscape.

2. **Lack of ablation studies on key components**: The paper claims that learning morphology is crucial, but provides no ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component. For example, they don't compare their model to a version without the word encoder (e.g., using a standard character-level model) to quantify how much of the performance gain comes specifically from morphology learning.

3. **Unclear theoretical justification for the word encoder design**: While the word encoder design is described in detail, the paper lacks a theoretical justification for why this specific architecture (with the energy weights and two RNNs) is superior to alternatives. The authors don't explain why they chose to use tanh activation instead of softmax normalization (as mentioned in Section 3.1) or why the BiRNN was necessary for ht.

4. **Limited analysis of scalability and practical deployment**: The paper shows good results on standard datasets, but doesn't analyze how the model scales to larger datasets, more languages, or longer sequences. The training efficiency comparison in Table 1 is helpful, but the authors don't discuss how the model would perform with much larger training corpora or on more morphologically complex languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model "learns morphology" but doesn't provide a quantitative metric to measure this. Could the authors provide a metric to quantify how well the model learns morphology compared to other approaches?

2. The paper states that their model is efficient to train, but doesn't specify how the training time scales with the size of the training data. How would the training time and memory usage change when scaling to larger datasets (e.g., 10x more training data)?

3. The paper mentions that the word encoder can detect subword boundaries (Figure 5), but doesn't explain how this contributes to translation quality. Could the authors elaborate on how the detected subword boundaries specifically improve translation?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to NMT by addressing the OOV problem through explicit morphology learning. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are some limitations in the comparisons and analysis, the core contribution of learning morphology for better translation is significant and could have broader applications beyond NMT. The paper would be strengthened by more recent comparisons and ablation studies, but as it stands, it presents a solid contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**
