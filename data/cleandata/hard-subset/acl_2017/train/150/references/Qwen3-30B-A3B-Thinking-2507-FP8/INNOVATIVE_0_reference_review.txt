

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to neural machine translation (NMT) that directly incorporates morphology into the model architecture, addressing the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem without relying on subword units like BPE. The key innovation is a word encoder that learns morphemes and the rules of how they combine, using two recurrent neural networks: one to learn representations of preceding characters and another to learn weights for these representations. The paper also introduces a hierarchical decoder that first generates target words at a higher level and then decodes each word at the character level. The authors claim their model can handle OOV words, misspelled words, and nonce words better than standard word-level models, while maintaining competitive BLEU scores.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel architecture for learning morphology in NMT**: The paper introduces a fundamentally different approach to handling word structure in NMT by learning morphemes and their combination rules explicitly through a two-RNN word encoder. This is a bold departure from both word-level and subword-level approaches.

2. **Practical ability to handle OOV, misspelled, and nonce words**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their model can correctly translate words it has never seen before (like "convenienter"), a significant practical advantage over standard word-level models that would map such words to <unk>.

3. **Efficient character-level translation with hierarchical decoding**: The hierarchical decoder design provides a novel and efficient way to implement character-level translation without the computational burden of previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Genuinely novel approach to morphology**: The core idea of learning morphology through a two-RNN word encoder is a bold and novel concept in NMT. Unlike previous work that uses subword units (BPE) or character-level models with limited morphology awareness, this paper directly tackles the problem of morphological structure in the model architecture. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 provide strong evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations, with words sharing morphemes clustering together in the embedding space.

2. **Practical demonstration of handling OOV and nonce words**: The paper provides concrete examples (Table 2) showing how the model can correctly translate misspelled words and nonce words like "convenienter" that word-level models would fail to handle. This is not just a theoretical advantage but a practical benefit for real-world applications where OOV words are common.

3. **Competitive performance with efficiency**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models while being more efficient to train than previous character-level approaches (e.g., it trains in 7 days compared to 3 months for Luong and Manning's model). The paper shows that their model even outperforms BPE baselines after just one epoch of training, suggesting their approach truly speeds up learning.

4. **Clear motivation and problem statement**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of current NMT approaches (word-level models' OOV problem, character-level models' training difficulties) and positions their approach as a solution to these specific challenges.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with more recent character-level models**: The paper compares primarily with older character-level models (Luong and Manning, 2016; Lee et al., 2016) but doesn't include more recent state-of-the-art character-level approaches (e.g., Wang et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018). This weakens the claim that their model is "competitive with state-of-the-art" as they're not comparing with the most recent advances.

2. **Lack of systematic analysis of morphology learning**: While the paper shows some examples of morphological learning (Figures 3-5), there's no systematic analysis of how well the model learns morphological rules. How many morphemes does it learn? How does it handle complex morphological phenomena in agglutinative languages? The paper focuses mainly on English examples, which have relatively simple morphology compared to many other languages.

3. **Marginal performance gains**: The BLEU improvements over the BPE baseline are relatively small (e.g., +1.2 BLEU on En-Fr, +0.9 BLEU on En-Cs), which in NMT is often not considered significant. The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why these marginal gains are meaningful given the novelty of the approach.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component. Without these, it's hard to determine how much of the performance gain comes from the morphology learning versus the character-level translation approach itself.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically handle agglutinative languages with complex morphology (like Turkish or Finnish), where morphology is central to the language? The examples provided are mostly English, which has relatively simple morphology.

2. Can you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of the morphology learning component to overall performance? For example, what happens if you replace your word encoder with a standard RNN word encoder?

3. How does your model compare with more recent character-level NMT approaches (e.g., Wang et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018)? The paper seems to be comparing with older approaches.

4. The paper claims the model can "guess" the meaning of words it has never seen before (like "everything"). Can you provide more examples of this, and what is the actual frequency of such correct predictions in practice?

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to incorporating morphology into neural machine translation, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The idea of learning morphemes and their combination rules directly in the model architecture is bold and innovative, moving beyond the standard subword or character-level approaches. The practical demonstration of handling OOV, misspelled, and nonce words is compelling and addresses a real-world problem that word-level models struggle with.

While the performance gains are marginal, the novelty of the approach and its practical advantages justify acceptance. The paper's focus on a significant problem (morphology in NMT) with a fresh perspective aligns with ACL's interest in bold new directions. The weaknesses noted are important but do not outweigh the novelty and practical significance of the contribution.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NMT literature, and its core idea has the potential to inspire further research on incorporating linguistic structure into neural models. The paper's strengths in novelty and practical advantage outweigh the weaknesses in comparison with more recent work and lack of systematic morphology analysis.
