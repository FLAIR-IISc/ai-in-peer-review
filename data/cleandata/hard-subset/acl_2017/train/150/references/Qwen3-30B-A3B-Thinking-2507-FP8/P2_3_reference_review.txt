

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a novel word encoder. The main contributions are:

1. A word encoder that learns morphemes and their combination rules using two RNNs, enabling meaningful word representations
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates character sequences based on word-level representations
3. Demonstration that their character-level model handles OOV and misspelled words better than word-level models while achieving competitive BLEU scores

The paper claims their model (DCNMT) achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art models while addressing the OOV issue through explicit morphology learning.

## Strengths

1. **Effective handling of OOV and morphological challenges**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, which word-level models cannot handle. Table 2 provides compelling examples where DCNMT outperforms Google Translate and LISA, while word-level models fail completely with <unk> tokens. This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where OOV is common.

2. **Efficient training compared to prior character-level models**: The authors provide concrete evidence that their model trains significantly faster than previous character-level approaches. As shown in Table 1, their model achieves competitive BLEU scores in just 1 epoch (7 days) compared to Luong and Manning's (2016) 3 months of training. This efficiency makes character-level translation more practical for real-world applications.

3. **Meaningful morphological representations**: The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 demonstrate that their word encoder produces representations where words with similar morphology cluster together, unlike the ordinary RNN encoder. The analysis in Section 5.2 (Figures 4 and 5) provides evidence that the model learns morpheme boundaries and can generalize to unseen words like "everything" based on morphological rules, validating their core claim.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty and incremental contribution**: While the approach is interesting, the core ideas aren't substantially new. The two-RNN word encoder resembles prior work (e.g., Ling et al., 2015), and the hierarchical decoder concept is similar to existing approaches. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from Luong and Manning's (2016) hybrid word-character model, which also aims to handle OOV through morphological awareness. The novelty appears incremental rather than transformative.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks rigorous ablation studies to isolate the contribution of morphology learning. The comparison between DCNMT and CNMT (which uses a single RNN for word encoding) doesn't clearly show how much of the performance gain comes from morphology learning versus just using character-level processing. Without ablation studies, it's difficult to determine the specific value of the morphology learning component.

3. **Limited evaluation of morphological learning**: While the paper shows some examples of morphological learning, it lacks systematic evaluation. There's no quantitative metric for morphological accuracy, and the examples shown (Figures 3-5) are limited to a few words. The paper claims the model "learns morphology" but doesn't provide evidence that this learning is robust across different morphological structures or languages.

4. **Comparison with contemporary approaches**: The paper compares with a limited set of character-level models (C2W, CNMT, DCNMT) but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches. For example, it doesn't compare with the recent work by Lee et al. (2016) or more modern subword approaches like SentencePiece. The comparison with BPE-based models (bpe2bpe) is somewhat superficial, as it doesn't address the fact that BPE is the dominant approach for handling OOV in NMT.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically handle languages with very different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish) compared to the English-Czech examples shown? What modifications would be needed for such languages?

2. Can you provide more detailed ablation studies showing the contribution of the morphology learning component versus just using a character-level model without the specialized word encoder? What is the performance impact of removing the morphology learning aspect?

3. Why does your model have a lower BLEU score than the BPE-based models on the En-Fr task (28.13 vs 29.70), despite handling OOV better? How would you address this performance gap while maintaining the OOV benefits?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical approach to addressing the OOV problem in NMT through character-level processing with explicit morphology learning. The strengths in handling OOV words and efficient training are significant contributions with real-world applicability. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the paper lacks sufficient ablation studies and systematic evaluation of the morphological learning component. While the results are promising, the paper would benefit from more rigorous comparison with contemporary approaches and clearer evidence of the specific value of morphology learning.

The paper is well-written and the experiments are generally well-designed, but the incremental nature of the contribution makes it difficult to justify as a major advance in the field. For ACL, this work represents a solid technical contribution with practical value, but it doesn't push the boundaries of the field significantly. I would lean toward acceptance with minor revisions addressing the ablation studies and comparison with more recent approaches.
