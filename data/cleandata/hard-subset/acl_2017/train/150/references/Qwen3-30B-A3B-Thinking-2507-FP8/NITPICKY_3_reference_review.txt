

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a hierarchical architecture for character-level neural machine translation (NMT) that learns morphology through a novel word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The authors claim their model can handle out-of-vocabulary words, learn meaningful word representations based on morphology, and achieve performance comparable to word-level models.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through a weighted RNN architecture
2. A hierarchical decoder that operates at character level while maintaining efficient training
3. Demonstrated ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words through character-level modeling

## Strengths

1. **Novel word encoding approach**: The idea of using a weighted RNN to learn morphemes and their combinations is interesting and potentially valuable. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 provide some evidence that the model learns meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships.

2. **Handling of misspelled words**: The examples in Table 2 demonstrate a clear advantage of the character-level approach for handling misspelled words and nonce words that wouldn't be recognized by word-level models. This addresses a real problem in NMT.

3. **Theoretical foundation**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of word-level models (vocabulary size, OOV problem) and character-level models (training difficulties, resource requirements), and provides a clear motivation for their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical mathematical error in weight normalization**: The paper claims "Instead of normalizing it by ∑t exp(aff(ht)), we use an activation function tanh as it performs best in experiments." This is a severe methodological flaw. Using tanh instead of proper normalization (like softmax) means the weights do not sum to 1, which is required for a weighted average. This fundamentally undermines the proposed word encoding mechanism. The authors should have used a softmax or similar normalization to ensure the weights sum to 1.

2. **Misleading performance claims**: The paper claims "our model is comparable to the state-of-the-art character-level models" but Table 1 shows that for En-Fr, their final model (after ~7 days of training) achieves 28.13 BLEU vs the BPE baseline's 29.70 BLEU. This is a significant performance gap, not a "comparable" result. The paper's claim that "our model is the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" is also misleading since Table 1 shows DCNMT has ~54M parameters, matching the BPE model size.

3. **Inconsistent and poorly explained model architecture**: The paper claims "there are totally six recurrent neural networks in our model, which can be divided into four layers as shown in Figure 2" but fails to clearly explain how these six RNNs are distributed across the four layers. The description of the hierarchical decoder (Section 3.2) is confusing, with inconsistent equation notation (e.g., "gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit" which uses g_{t-1} on both sides) and unclear unfolding procedure.

4. **Numerous formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains multiple typos and formatting inconsistencies throughout (e.g., "automati-cally", inconsistent spacing in equations, inconsistent reference formatting like "Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015" instead of "Bahdanau et al., 2015", and inconsistent use of mathematical notation). These make the paper difficult to follow and appear unprofessional.

5. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims to "learn the subword unit automatically" without using BPE, but their approach is very similar to other character-level models that have been proposed. The comparison to existing work (Table 1) shows that their model is not significantly better than existing character-level approaches (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016), which took 3 months to train and yielded +0.5 BLEU points compared to their result.

## Questions to Authors

1. The weight normalization in Equation (3) uses tanh instead of a proper normalization. Can you explain why you believe this is acceptable and how it affects the model's performance? What would happen if you used softmax instead?

2. The final BLEU scores for your model are consistently lower than the BPE baseline (En-Fr: 28.13 vs 29.70, En-Cs: 16.96 vs 17.5, Cs-En: 22.48 vs 23.38). How do you explain this performance gap, and why does the paper claim your model is "comparable" to state-of-the-art?

3. The paper claims "our model is the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" but Table 1 shows your model has ~54M parameters, matching the BPE model size. Can you clarify this claim and explain why you consider your model smaller or simpler?

4. The unfolding procedure described in Section 3.2 is difficult to follow. Can you provide a clearer explanation of how the matrix R is constructed and used in the batch training process?

## Additional Comments

The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies, including inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "ranyone = tanh( 6∑ t=1 wtrt)"), inconsistent use of notation (e.g., "rYt−1" vs "r_{Y_{t-1}}"), and inconsistent reference formatting. These small issues, while seemingly minor, collectively undermine the paper's credibility.

The most concerning issue is the weight normalization error in the word encoder. This is a fundamental mathematical flaw that invalidates a key component of the proposed approach. Without proper normalization, the model's word encoding mechanism does not work as intended, and the claimed benefits of learning morphology are questionable.

While the paper addresses a real problem (the OOV issue), the methodological flaws and misleading performance claims outweigh the potential contributions. The paper would require significant revisions to address these issues before it could be accepted.

## Overall Recommendation
Reject

The paper contains a critical mathematical error in the weight normalization procedure, misleading performance claims, and numerous formatting inconsistencies. These issues fundamentally undermine the paper's claims and methodology. While the problem addressed (handling OOV words) is important, the proposed solution is not properly validated, and the paper fails to demonstrate that the claimed benefits are actually achieved.
