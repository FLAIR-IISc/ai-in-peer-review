

# Peer Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural machine translation (NMT) model called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a character-level representation. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations from character sequences, enabling the model to handle OOV words naturally and generate meaningful word representations that capture linguistic structure.

2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates characters for each word, avoiding the computational inefficiencies of previous character-level models that required separate training passes for word and character levels.

3. Empirical evidence demonstrating the model's ability to learn morphology (through PCA visualizations of word representations) and its competitive performance on standard NMT benchmarks compared to state-of-the-art word-level and character-level models.

## Strengths

1. **Practical OOV handling**: The model's ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") without requiring a large vocabulary is a significant practical contribution. The paper provides compelling evidence in Table 2 that DCNMT can recover the intended meaning of these words, while word-level models would simply output <unk> tokens. This addresses a real-world problem in NMT where OOV words frequently degrade translation quality.

2. **Strong evidence of morphology learning**: The paper's analysis of word representations (Figures 3 and 4) provides convincing evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological relationships. The PCA visualizations show that words with similar morphemes (e.g., "notable" and "notability") cluster together in the representation space, with the model correctly identifying morpheme boundaries. This is a compelling demonstration that the proposed word encoder successfully captures linguistic structure.

3. **Training efficiency**: The paper demonstrates that DCNMT trains significantly faster than other character-level models (Table 1). While Luong and Manning (2016) took 3 months to train their character-level model, DCNMT achieves comparable results in just 7 days. This efficiency advantage, combined with the model's competitive BLEU scores, represents a meaningful practical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: The paper's core innovations (morpheme-based word encoding and hierarchical decoding) build incrementally on existing work rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts. The word encoder resembles the "compositional character to word" model of Ling et al. (2015), and the hierarchical decoder is similar to Luong and Manning's hybrid model. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from these prior works, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

2. **Marginal performance gains**: The BLEU score improvements over state-of-the-art models are modest (typically 0.5-1 BLEU points). On En-Fr, DCNMT achieves 30.56 BLEU compared to Luong and Manning's 30.56 BLEU, which is essentially the same score. The paper claims "comparable performance" but doesn't convincingly demonstrate that DCNMT represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches, particularly given the more complex architecture.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component from the hierarchical decoder. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from learning morphology versus the hierarchical decoding structure. For instance, the comparison with CNMT (which uses a standard RNN word encoder) doesn't show how much of the improvement is due to the morpheme-based encoding versus the overall architecture.

4. **Inference efficiency not addressed**: While the paper claims training efficiency, it doesn't provide any analysis of inference speed or memory usage. The model uses six RNNs (compared to two or three in other models), which could significantly slow down inference. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the model's practical utility for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses only 120 characters per language. How would the model's performance change with a more comprehensive character set (e.g., including all Unicode characters), and would this affect the model's ability to learn morphology?

2. The paper shows that training for one epoch with DCNMT outperforms the final result of the bpe baseline (Table 1). Could the authors provide more details on why this happens, and whether this indicates a fundamental advantage of character-level modeling or a data-specific artifact?

3. The model appears to handle morpheme boundaries well (Figure 5), but how does it perform with highly agglutinative languages where words can have many morphemes? Would the word encoder's attention mechanism be able to correctly segment such words?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical approach to addressing the OOV problem in neural machine translation through character-level modeling with morphological awareness. While the model demonstrates promising results on standard benchmarks and provides compelling evidence of morphology learning, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to prior work. The marginal performance gains and limited ablation studies make it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. The paper would benefit from clearer differentiation from existing work, more comprehensive ablation studies, and analysis of inference efficiency. 

The paper's strengths in practical OOV handling and evidence of morphology learning are valuable contributions to the field, but the modest performance improvements and incremental nature of the approach make it a marginal contribution to the state of the art. With stronger evidence of novelty and more comprehensive analysis, this work could be more compelling for the ACL community.
