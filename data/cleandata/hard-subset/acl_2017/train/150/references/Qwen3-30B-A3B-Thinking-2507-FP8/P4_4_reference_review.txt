

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level neural machine translation (NMT) systems. The authors introduce a novel architecture incorporating two key components: (1) a word encoder that learns morphological structure through a dual-RNN approach, and (2) a hierarchical decoder that generates text at the character level. The model aims to eliminate the need for large vocabularies while maintaining competitive translation quality.

## Main Contributions (ordered by strength)
1. A dual-RNN word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations to create meaningful word representations, enabling the model to handle OOV words through morphological understanding.
2. A hierarchical decoder architecture (HGRU) that efficiently decodes at character level while maintaining training efficiency, addressing a key challenge in character-level NMT.
3. Empirical demonstration that morphology learning leads to meaningful word representations (as shown in Figure 3) and enables translation of misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter").

## Strengths

1. **Effective Handling of OOV Words:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the model can handle misspelled words and nonce words that would be OOV in standard word-level models. The examples in Table 2 demonstrate that DCNMT correctly translates "convenienter" (a nonce word formed by combining "convenient" + "er") while word-level models fail (producing <unk> tokens). This is a significant practical advantage over traditional NMT systems.

2. **Competitive Performance with Improved Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art models while being significantly more efficient to train. As shown in Table 1, DCNMT achieves 30.56 BLEU on En-Fr after 1 epoch (vs. 29.38 for CNMT after 300 epochs), and trains in 7 days compared to 27 days for C2W (Luong and Manning, 2016). This efficiency advantage is substantial for practical NMT applications.

3. **Meaningful Morphological Representations:** The authors provide strong evidence (Figure 3) that their word encoder produces meaningful word representations that reflect morphological structure. Unlike ordinary RNN word encoders (Figure 3a), their model (Figure 3b) clusters words with similar morphological structures together (e.g., "notable" and "notability" are closer in the embedding space), demonstrating that the model has learned to represent morphological relationships.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty Compared to Existing Work:** The paper's core contributions (word encoder and hierarchical decoder) have significant overlap with prior work. The hierarchical decoder concept is similar to Luong and Manning (2016) and Ling et al. (2015b), while the dual-RNN approach for word encoding resembles character-level modeling techniques in previous literature. The paper fails to clearly distinguish its specific innovations from these related works, making it difficult to assess the true novelty.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope and Comparison:** The experiments are restricted to only three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), with Czech (En-Cs/Cs-En) being the only morphologically rich language tested. The comparison with state-of-the-art models is limited to a few specific baselines (BPE, C2W, CNMT), without comparing with more recent or stronger character-level models like those in Lee et al. (2016) or the recent advancements in character-level NMT. The lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component further weakens the claims.

3. **Insufficient Justification for Architectural Choices:** The paper makes several key architectural decisions (e.g., using tanh instead of softmax for normalization, the specific HGRU design) without sufficient theoretical justification or ablation studies. For instance, the authors state "we use an activation function tanh as it performs best in experiments" without providing evidence of why tanh is better than softmax for normalization or how sensitive the model is to this choice.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "unlike subword-level model based on the byte pair encoding (BPE) algorithm, we learn the subword unit automatically." Can you clarify how your automatic subword unit learning differs from BPE, and provide evidence that your learned subword units are more meaningful than those produced by BPE?

2. The paper claims the model "learns morphology" but relies primarily on qualitative evidence (Figure 3) rather than quantitative metrics. Could you provide a more rigorous quantitative measure of the model's ability to learn and utilize morphological information?

3. The hierarchical decoder (HGRU) is described as a variant of GRU, but the paper doesn't clearly explain how it differs from standard GRU or why it's necessary for character-level decoding. How does HGRU specifically improve upon standard GRU for this task, and how does it compare to other character-level decoding approaches in terms of both performance and efficiency?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the model's morphology learning specifically improves translation quality, beyond just handling OOV words. The connection between the morphological representation learning and the BLEU score improvement could be more clearly articulated.
