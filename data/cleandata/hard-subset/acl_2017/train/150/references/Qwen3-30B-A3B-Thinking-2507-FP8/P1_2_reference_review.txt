

# Peer Review: "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation (NMT) called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level NMT. The key innovation is a word encoder that learns morphology by breaking words into morphemes (using two RNNs with a weighting mechanism) and a hierarchical decoder that first predicts target words at the word level and then generates characters for each word. The authors claim their model achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art models while solving the OOV problem and handling misspelled words and nonce words.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that explicitly learns morphology by breaking words into meaningful morphemes and learning how they combine.
2. A hierarchical decoder design that efficiently decodes at the character level while maintaining the benefits of word-level modeling.
3. Empirical demonstration that character-level modeling with explicit morphology learning can solve the OOV problem while achieving competitive translation quality.

## Strengths

1. **Effective OOV handling**: The paper provides compelling evidence that DCNMT can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, whereas word-level models would simply output <unk> tokens. This is a significant practical contribution to NMT, as OOV remains a persistent challenge in real-world applications.

2. **Strong empirical validation of morphology learning**: The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 and the analysis in Section 5.2 provide convincing evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The model successfully groups words with similar morphology together (e.g., "notable" and "notability"), demonstrating that the proposed architecture effectively captures linguistic structure.

3. **Efficient training compared to prior character-level models**: The paper demonstrates that DCNMT trains significantly faster than previous character-level models (1 day vs. 3 months for Luong and Manning, 2016). The hierarchical decoder design with the HGRU mechanism enables efficient batch training, which is a major practical advantage over prior approaches.

4. **Competitive translation quality**: DCNMT achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art word-level models (e.g., 30.56 BLEU on En-Fr test) while operating at the character level. This demonstrates that character-level modeling need not sacrifice performance when combined with explicit morphology learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to BPE-based approaches**: The paper claims to "learn subword units automatically" without BPE, but doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this differs from or improves upon BPE. Table 1 shows that BPE-based models (bpe2bpe) outperform DCNMT on all tasks, suggesting the advantage of their approach is limited. The paper should better justify why learning morphology is superior to the widely adopted BPE method.

2. **Misleading training efficiency claims**: The paper states DCNMT trains in "1 day" compared to "3 months" for Luong and Manning (2016), but the table shows "Days" as "1" for DCNMT and "27" for C2W. The "1" likely refers to 1 epoch, not 1 day, which misrepresents the training efficiency comparison. This is a significant miscommunication that undermines the paper's claims.

3. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component versus the hierarchical decoder design. Without this, it's impossible to determine how much of the performance gain comes from each component, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the morphology learning approach.

4. **Insufficient analysis of when the approach is most beneficial**: The paper focuses on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En tasks but doesn't analyze when the morphology learning approach would provide the most benefit (e.g., for highly inflected languages). The results on En-Fr (morphologically poor) show only modest gains over word-level models, which weakens the argument for the approach's general applicability.

5. **Theoretical justification for morphology learning is weak**: The paper assumes that learning morphology improves translation quality but doesn't provide a strong theoretical argument or detailed analysis of how morphology learning specifically contributes to better translation. The connection between morphology learning and translation performance is somewhat assumed rather than rigorously established.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "learn subword units automatically" without BPE, but how does this differ from BPE in terms of the quality and interpretability of the learned units? Can you provide a qualitative comparison of the subword units learned by your model versus BPE?

2. In Table 1, the DCNMT model achieves 29.31 BLEU after 1 epoch on En-Fr test, while the BPE baseline achieves 29.70. How does DCNMT's performance after 1 epoch compare to the BPE model after 1 epoch? This would be a fairer comparison of training efficiency.

3. The paper states that DCNMT can translate "convenienter" correctly, but how does the model generate this word? Could you provide more details on the morphological rules or representations that allow the model to generate this nonce word?

4. Why does the model perform better on Cs-En (17.89 BLEU) than on En-Cs (16.96 BLEU)? Is this due to the directionality of the translation task, or are there other factors at play?

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a novel architecture for character-level NMT with a focus on learning morphology, which addresses the OOV problem effectively. The empirical results are promising, and the demonstration of morphology learning is compelling. However, the lack of clear novelty compared to BPE-based approaches, the misleading training efficiency claims, and the absence of an ablation study significantly weaken the paper's contribution. With these issues addressed, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's claims of superiority over existing approaches are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**
