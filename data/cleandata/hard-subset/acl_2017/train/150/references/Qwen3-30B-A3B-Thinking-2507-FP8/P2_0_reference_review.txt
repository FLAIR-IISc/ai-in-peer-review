

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The main contributions are: (1) a morpheme-aware word encoder that learns word representations from character sequences rather than using a fixed vocabulary lookup table; (2) a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations; and (3) empirical evidence demonstrating the model's ability to learn meaningful morphological patterns.

## Main Contributions
1. **Morphology-aware word representation**: The core contribution is the word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through two RNNs, eliminating the need for a fixed vocabulary.
2. **Hierarchical decoder**: The paper introduces a novel character-level decoding architecture that efficiently generates sequences by leveraging word-level representations.
3. **Empirical demonstration of morphological learning**: The paper provides visual and quantitative evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological patterns, as shown through PCA projections and translation examples.

## Strengths

1. **Effective solution to the OOV problem**: The paper successfully addresses a critical limitation of word-level NMT models (OOV words) through morphological learning. For morphologically rich languages like Czech, this is particularly valuable. The paper demonstrates that their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words correctly (Table 2), which is impossible for traditional word-level models. This practical advantage is significant for real-world translation applications.

2. **Strong empirical evidence of morphological learning**: The PCA visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 provide compelling evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The paper shows that words with similar morphological structures are clustered together in the embedding space, and the energy weights (Figure 4) reveal how the model identifies morpheme boundaries. This goes beyond merely claiming "learning morphology" to demonstrating it through visual and quantitative analysis.

3. **Efficiency advantages**: The paper demonstrates that DCNMT is significantly more efficient to train than other character-level models (Table 1), requiring only 1-2 training epochs compared to months for other approaches. The training efficiency is a major practical contribution, as character-level models are often considered computationally expensive.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing work**: While the paper presents a novel architecture, the core idea of learning morphological structure is conceptually similar to BPE-based approaches (Sennrich et al., 2016), which have become the standard in NMT. The paper claims to "learn the subword unit automatically" but doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this differs from BPE in terms of the quality of subword units learned. The comparison with BPE-based models (Table 1) shows competitive BLEU scores but doesn't clearly establish a fundamental advantage.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with some character-level models but doesn't adequately compare with the current SOTA approaches, which are predominantly subword-based (e.g., BPE). The paper mentions that their model is "comparable to the state-of-the-art character-level models" but doesn't show how it stacks up against the best subword models, which would be the relevant comparison point.

3. **Lack of systematic analysis of morphological generalization**: While the paper provides some examples of morphological generalization (Table 2), it lacks a systematic evaluation of how well the model generalizes to unseen morphological patterns. The examples are limited to a few specific cases (e.g., "convenienter"), and there's no quantitative measure of the model's ability to handle morphological variations.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "our model is able to learn the morphology" but doesn't provide a clear metric for how well the model learns morphological patterns compared to BPE or other approaches. Could you provide a more systematic evaluation of morphological generalization capabilities?

2. How does your model handle morphological phenomena that BPE struggles with, such as agglutinative languages with complex morphological structures? Could you provide examples where your approach clearly outperforms BPE?

3. The paper states that "our model is able to generate a meaningful representation of the word" compared to CNN word encoders. Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of your RNN-based approach over CNN-based word encoders for morphological learning?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated approach to character-level NMT that effectively addresses the OOV problem through morphological learning. The empirical evidence for morphological learning is strong, and the model's training efficiency is a practical advantage. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from the now-dominant BPE-based methods, and the claimed advantages aren't clearly demonstrated against current state-of-the-art models.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how morphological structure can be learned in neural translation models, but its impact would be greater with stronger comparisons to contemporary approaches and more systematic evaluation of morphological generalization. With minor revisions to address these limitations, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions)
