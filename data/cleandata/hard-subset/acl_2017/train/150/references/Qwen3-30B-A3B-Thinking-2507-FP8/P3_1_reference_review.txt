

# Peer Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two RNNs, (2) a hierarchical decoder for efficient character-level generation, and (3) empirical demonstration of competitive performance on multiple language pairs while handling OOV words.

## Strengths
1. **Addressing a real problem**: The paper correctly identifies the OOV problem as a significant limitation of word-level NMT, and character-level approaches offer a promising alternative. The authors provide a reasonable solution to this well-known challenge.

2. **Practical hierarchical decoder**: The proposed HGRU-based hierarchical decoder effectively addresses the computational inefficiency of character-level decoding. The unfolding technique for batch training is a practical contribution that could benefit the community.

3. **Competitive results**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art BPE-based models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En tasks. The results demonstrate that character-level modeling can be competitive with the current standard approach.

## Weaknesses
1. **Questionable novelty**: The core components of the model build heavily on prior work without clear differentiation. The word encoder resembles the C2W model by Ling et al. (2015b), and the hierarchical decoder is similar to Luong and Manning (2016). The paper fails to convincingly articulate how their approach differs from these existing methods. The authors claim their model "learns morphology" but don't provide strong evidence beyond a PCA visualization (Fig. 3), which is insufficient to establish a significant methodological contribution.

2. **Misleading efficiency claims**: The authors claim their model is "the simplest and smallest" (Table 1), but the model size (54M parameters) is comparable to BPE-based models (52M-76M parameters). Their training time claim is also questionable—they state their model trains in "1 epoch ~7 days" while Luong and Manning (2016) took "3 months," but they don't specify how many epochs Luong and Manning trained for. This comparison is misleading without context.

3. **Overstated benefits for misspelled words**: Table 2 shows DCNMT correctly translates some misspelled words, but this is a limited demonstration. Google Translate and LISA (the other neural MT system they compare to) also handle some misspellings, and the examples are cherry-picked. The paper doesn't provide systematic evaluation of this capability, making the claim unsubstantiated.

4. **Lack of comparison with modern character-level approaches**: The paper doesn't compare with more recent character-level models like Lee et al. (2016) or the recent work by Gu et al. (2017) on character-level NMT. This omission weakens their claim of being "competitive with state-of-the-art character-based models."

5. **No analysis of computational cost**: The paper claims efficiency but doesn't provide inference time or memory usage comparisons with word-level models. For practical deployment, inference speed is as important as training time, and this critical dimension is missing.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your word encoder fundamentally differ from the character-level word representation approach of Lee et al. (2016) or the morphological approach of Luong and Manning (2016)? Please provide a clear technical distinction.

2. Can you provide a systematic evaluation of how well your model handles misspelled words compared to standard word-level models and other character-level models? The current examples are limited and potentially misleading.

3. Why does your model require six RNNs (compared to two in standard NMT) yet claim to be "the simplest and smallest"? Please clarify this apparent contradiction.

4. Could you provide inference time comparisons between your model and standard word-level models? This is crucial for practical adoption.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a reasonable attempt to address the OOV problem through character-level modeling with a morphology-aware approach. While the hierarchical decoder design is practical, the novelty claim is questionable given the close relationship to prior work. The results are competitive but don't demonstrate a clear advantage over current state-of-the-art methods. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with recent character-level models, systematic evaluation of the morphology learning claim, and clearer articulation of the methodological contribution.

The paper's main weakness is overstating the novelty and benefits without sufficient evidence. It would be more valuable if the authors had demonstrated a significant performance gain or provided stronger evidence of morphology learning. As it stands, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative for the field. I recommend rejection but would reconsider with a substantially revised paper that addresses these concerns and provides more convincing evidence of their claims.
