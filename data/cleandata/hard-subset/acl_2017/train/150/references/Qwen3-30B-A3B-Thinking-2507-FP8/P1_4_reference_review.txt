

# Peer Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphological structure. The authors introduce a word encoder that breaks words into morphemes using two RNNs (one for character representation and one for assigning weights to morpheme representations), and a hierarchical decoder that operates at the character level while being conditioned on word-level representations. The model aims to achieve the benefits of character-level translation (no OOV issues) without the training difficulties and performance drawbacks of pure character-level models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **A novel morphological word encoder** that explicitly learns meaningful word structure through weighted character representations
2. **A hierarchical decoder architecture** that enables efficient character-level decoding while maintaining training efficiency
3. **Demonstrated competitive performance** on translation tasks with a character-level model that solves the OOV problem

## Strengths

1. **Practical solution to the OOV problem**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that DCNMT can translate misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") correctly, while standard word-level models fail completely. This is a significant practical advantage with real-world applications (Table 2). The ability to handle OOV words without requiring external lookup mechanisms is a clear contribution.

2. **Efficiency gains over existing character-level models**: The paper shows that DCNMT trains significantly faster than other character-level approaches (5 days vs. 90 days for Luong and Manning 2016), while achieving comparable BLEU scores. This efficiency is crucial for practical adoption of character-level models, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches.

3. **Demonstrated morphological learning**: The PCA visualizations (Figure 3) provide strong evidence that the word encoder learns meaningful morphological structure, grouping words with similar meanings and morphological patterns more effectively than standard RNN word encoders. The analysis of energy weights (Figure 4) shows the model correctly identifies morpheme boundaries.

4. **Competitive performance**: The BLEU scores in Table 1 show that DCNMT achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models on multiple language pairs, including morphologically rich Czech-English translation, while using a character-level approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty distinction**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work. The "word encoder" is conceptually similar to Ling et al. (2015) and Lee et al. (2016), and the hierarchical decoder resembles Luong and Manning (2016). The paper needs to more clearly articulate what is new and different about their approach compared to these existing methods.

2. **Limited evaluation of morphological benefits**: While the paper claims benefits for morphologically rich languages, the experiments only include Czech (morphologically rich) and English-French (morphologically poor). There's insufficient analysis to demonstrate how the morphology learning specifically benefits translation for morphologically rich languages beyond the general BLEU score improvements.

3. **Inadequate comparison to current state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to older character-level models but doesn't address recent SOTA approaches like Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) or more recent neural machine translation models. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess how competitive DCNMT is in today's NMT landscape.

4. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. For example, it's unclear how much the morphological learning component specifically contributes to performance versus the hierarchical decoder architecture. The comparison between CNMT and DCNMT is minimal and doesn't provide enough insight into the specific contributions of the new components.

5. **Limited analysis of morphological learning**: The paper shows some evidence of morphological learning (Figures 3-4), but doesn't provide systematic evaluation of how well the model learns morphological rules. For instance, it doesn't test how well the model can generate words with novel morphological combinations or handle complex morphological phenomena in Czech.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to recent state-of-the-art NMT models (like Transformer) on the same WMT datasets? The paper compares to older character-level models but doesn't address current SOTA.

2. Could you provide more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component versus the hierarchical decoder architecture?

3. How does your model specifically handle complex morphological phenomena in Czech (a highly inflected language)? Could you provide specific examples where morphology learning leads to better translation?

4. How does your approach differ from the "compositional character to word" (C2W) model by Ling et al. (2015)? The paper mentions this work but doesn't clearly distinguish the approaches.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that addresses a meaningful problem in neural machine translation (the OOV issue) with a novel approach. The proposed model demonstrates practical advantages over word-level models in handling OOV words and achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art character-level models. However, the paper would benefit from clearer articulation of novelty, more comprehensive comparison to current SOTA, and more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each component.

The strengths (practical OOV solution, efficiency, demonstrated morphological learning) outweigh the weaknesses, but the paper needs to better position itself within the current NMT landscape to fully demonstrate its contribution. With appropriate revisions addressing the weaknesses identified, this paper would make a solid contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
