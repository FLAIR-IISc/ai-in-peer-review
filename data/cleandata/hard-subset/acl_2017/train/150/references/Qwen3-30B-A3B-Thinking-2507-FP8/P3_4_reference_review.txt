

# Peer Review: "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that learns morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors claim their model addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level NMT by learning morphemes and their combinations, while maintaining competitive performance with state-of-the-art models and improved training efficiency.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes by weighting character representations through attention mechanisms
2. A hierarchical decoder that generates characters efficiently using word-level representations
3. Empirical evidence that the model learns meaningful word representations capturing morphological relationships

## Strengths

**1. Novel morpheme learning approach (most significant contribution):** The paper's strongest contribution is the word encoder that uses attention over character sequences to learn morphemes. Unlike previous character-level models, this approach allows the model to implicitly segment words into morphemes without explicit segmentation. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 provides compelling evidence that their model learns more meaningful word representations than standard RNN word encoders, with words sharing morphemes clustered together. This is a significant technical contribution that directly addresses the OOV problem in a principled way.

**2. Training efficiency (strong practical contribution):** The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model trains significantly faster than previous character-level approaches. As shown in Table 1, their model trains in approximately 7 days compared to 90 days for Luong and Manning (2016), while achieving comparable BLEU scores. This efficiency gain is particularly important for practical NMT applications.

**3. OOV word translation capability (strong empirical validation):** The paper provides compelling evidence that their model can correctly translate misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter" in Table 2), which word-level models would fail to handle. This is a direct, practical benefit of learning morphology that demonstrates the model's robustness.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited comparison with BPE-based models (most critical weakness):** The paper claims character-level models are better than subword-level models (Table 1), but this contradicts the current state of the art where BPE-based subword models (Sennrich et al., 2016) have become the standard. The authors should have compared their model against these models, which would provide a more meaningful evaluation of their approach. The current comparison is misleading since BPE-based models are the dominant approach in modern NMT.

**2. Superficial morphological analysis (significant methodological weakness):** While the paper claims to learn morphology, the analysis in Section 5.2 is limited to a few examples. The authors don't provide systematic evaluation of how well their model learns different morphological patterns (e.g., prefixes, suffixes, compounding) across multiple languages. A more thorough analysis would strengthen their claim of morphological learning.

**3. Questionable novelty (methodological concern):** The core idea of learning morphemes from character sequences has been explored before. Kim et al. (2016) and Lee et al. (2016) used CNNs to learn character-level word representations. The authors' RNN-based approach is not entirely novel, and the paper doesn't adequately differentiate their method from these prior works. The novelty claim needs strengthening.

**4. Overstated comparison to word-level models (interpretation issue):** The claim that "the deep character-level model is able to learn the similar embedding of the words with similar meanings like the word-level models" is overstated. The PCA visualization shows some clustering but doesn't demonstrate that the embeddings are as effective as those learned by standard word-level models. The paper should provide a direct comparison of embedding quality between their model and word-level models.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to BPE-based subword models (Sennrich et al., 2016) in terms of BLEU scores, training efficiency, and OOV handling? The paper's current comparison is misleading as BPE-based models are the current standard.

2. Can you provide a more systematic analysis of how well your model learns different morphological patterns (e.g., prefixes, suffixes, compounding) across multiple languages, rather than just a few examples?

3. How does your model's performance compare to recent state-of-the-art NMT models (e.g., Transformer, M2M) that have largely superseded RNN-based approaches?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the NMT field by proposing a novel character-level approach that learns morphology implicitly. The word encoder design is innovative and the empirical results on OOV words are compelling. However, the paper's claims about superiority over subword-level models are misleading, and the novelty is overstated. The training efficiency claim is strong, but the comparison with the current standard (BPE) is inadequate.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with BPE-based models, a deeper analysis of morphological learning, and clearer differentiation from prior work on character-level representations. As it stands, the paper makes a useful technical contribution but overstates its significance relative to the current state of the art.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept (with major revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above). The core idea is promising but requires more careful positioning within the current NMT landscape.
