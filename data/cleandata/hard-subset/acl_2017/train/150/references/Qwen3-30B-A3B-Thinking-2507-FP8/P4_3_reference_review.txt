

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) model that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in NMT by learning morphology explicitly. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel word encoder that implicitly learns morphemes and the rules for combining them using two RNNs, without requiring explicit segmentation. This allows the model to generate meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships.

2. A hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations, enabling character-level translation while maintaining the same encoding length as word-based models, thus avoiding the need for a large vocabulary.

3. Demonstrated practical advantages including the ability to handle misspelled words, nonce words, and OOV words correctly, which is a significant improvement over standard word-level models.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Morphology Learning Approach**: The paper introduces a distinct method for learning morphology through a two-RNN architecture for word encoding. Unlike previous approaches that rely on explicit segmentation (BPE) or pure character-level modeling, this method learns morphemes implicitly. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 provide compelling evidence that the model produces meaningful word representations where morphologically related words cluster together, which is a significant technical contribution.

2. **Practical OOV Handling Advantage**: The paper demonstrates a concrete, practical advantage of their approach through examples (Table 2) where their model correctly translates misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") that would be OOV in standard word-level models. This is a substantial practical contribution that addresses a well-known limitation of current NMT systems.

3. **Training Efficiency**: The paper shows their model trains significantly faster (1 day vs. 3 months) than comparable character-level models while achieving competitive BLEU scores. This efficiency gain is substantial for practical applications and is well-documented in Table 1 with clear comparisons to prior work.

4. **Competitive Performance**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art word-level and character-level models across three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En). The results demonstrate that character-level modeling with morphology learning can match the performance of more conventional approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Distinction from Prior Work**: The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing word representation methods. The CNN-based word encoders by Kim et al. (2016) and Lee et al. (2016) also learn meaningful word representations, and the hierarchical decoder resembles Luong and Manning (2016). The paper claims novelty but doesn't clearly establish how their method is different from or better than these existing approaches.

2. **Lack of Quantitative Morphology Evaluation**: While the paper provides qualitative evidence (PCA visualizations and examples) that the model learns morphology, it lacks quantitative metrics to measure morphology learning. Without systematic evaluation of how well the model learns morphological rules compared to other methods, the claim that the model "learns morphology" remains largely unsupported.

3. **Insufficient Ablation Study**: The paper does not provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component versus other architectural choices (e.g., the hierarchical decoder). The claim that morphology learning speeds up training (based on one-epoch results) could be due to other aspects of the architecture rather than morphology learning specifically.

4. **Limited Comparison with Explicit Morphology Approaches**: The paper compares with BPE-based and pure character-level models but doesn't compare with approaches that explicitly model morphology (e.g., using morphological analyzers). This makes it difficult to assess whether their implicit approach is superior to explicit morphology handling.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the word encoder's ability to learn morphology specifically compare to other word representation approaches (like CNN-based encoders)? What quantitative metrics could be used to measure this, and how would they be calculated?

2. The paper claims that morphology learning speeds up training (evidenced by better performance after one epoch). However, the architecture has several components that could contribute to this efficiency (word encoder, hierarchical decoder, etc.). Could you provide an ablation study showing the specific contribution of morphology learning to this efficiency gain?

3. The paper demonstrates that the model can implicitly segment words (Figure 5), but doesn't discuss how this implicit segmentation compares to explicit segmentation methods like BPE in terms of quality and stability. Could you provide more details on this comparison?

4. The paper states that the model is efficient to train (1 day vs. 3 months for similar models), but doesn't specify which architectural choices contributed most to this efficiency. Could you elaborate on the specific design decisions that enabled this efficiency?
