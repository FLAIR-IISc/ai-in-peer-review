

# ACL Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in neural machine translation by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and using a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation. The authors claim their model eliminates the need for a large vocabulary, handles misspelled words and nonce words better than standard approaches, and achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel character-level NMT architecture with a word encoder that learns morphology and a hierarchical decoder
2. Demonstration of meaningful word representations that capture morphological structure
3. Claimed ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words correctly

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a genuine challenge in NMT: the OOV problem, and proposes a potentially interesting solution that could be valuable for morphologically rich languages.

2. The technical description of the model architecture is detailed and well-structured, with clear explanations of the word encoder and hierarchical decoder mechanisms.

3. The experiments are thorough, covering three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) and comparing with multiple baselines, including both word-level and character-level models.

## Weaknesses
1. **Marginal performance gains with no statistical significance**: The paper claims "comparable performance" to state-of-the-art models, but the BLEU scores in Table 1 show only marginal improvements (e.g., 30.56 vs. 29.70 BLEU on En-Fr) or even worse performance (Cs-En: 22.48 vs. 22.46). The paper provides no statistical significance tests to confirm these differences are meaningful, making it difficult to assess if the improvements are substantial.

2. **Unconvincing comparison to standard approaches**: The paper fails to convincingly argue why their approach is better than the widely adopted BPE-based subword models (Sennrich et al., 2016), which have become the standard for handling OOV words in NMT. The paper states "our model eliminates the use of a large vocabulary," but BPE-based models also eliminate the need for a large vocabulary through subword segmentation. The paper doesn't demonstrate clear advantages over BPE in handling OOV words.

3. **Questionable efficiency claims**: The paper claims their model is "efficient to train" (Table 1), but the training time comparison is problematic. The paper states that their model takes "1 epoch ~7 days" while Luong and Manning (2016) took "3 months to train." However, the paper doesn't specify the number of epochs for Luong and Manning's model, making the comparison misleading. Additionally, the training time for their model is not significantly better than other character-level models (e.g., ~7 days vs. ~5 days for the "char" model).

4. **Lack of clear link between morphology learning and performance**: The paper demonstrates that the model learns meaningful word representations (Figures 3-5) but fails to convincingly show how this morphology learning directly improves translation quality. The examples in Table 2 (misspelled words and nonce words) are limited and not representative of the broader translation task. The paper claims that morphology learning "speeds up training," but doesn't provide evidence (e.g., training time comparisons with standard word-level models).

5. **Failure to compare with recent strong baselines**: The paper compares with older models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016) but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches like the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), which has become the standard in NMT. This omission significantly weakens the claim of "comparable performance to state-of-the-art."

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims "comparable performance" to state-of-the-art models, but the BLEU scores show marginal or even worse performance than the best baselines (e.g., En-Fr: 30.56 vs. 29.70 for the BPE baseline). Could you clarify why you claim "comparable" performance when the results show only marginal improvements?

2. The paper states "our model is the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" (Table 1), but the model size (54M) is similar to other models (52-76M). Could you clarify what you mean by "simplest" and "smallest," and how this is a meaningful advantage?

3. The paper claims that the model "eliminates the use of a large vocabulary," but BPE-based models also eliminate the need for a large vocabulary. Could you provide a clear comparison of how your approach handles OOV words better than BPE-based models?

4. The paper states that the model "speeds up training" because it learns morphology, but doesn't provide evidence (e.g., training time comparisons with standard word-level models). Could you provide such evidence?

5. The paper doesn't include statistical significance tests for the performance differences. Could you provide such tests to confirm if the observed differences are meaningful?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting idea for character-level NMT that learns morphology, but the evidence provided to support the claims is insufficient. The performance gains over baselines are marginal and not statistically significant, the comparison to standard approaches (BPE) is unconvincing, and the paper fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art models. While the technical description is clear, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify the novelty and practical value of the proposed approach. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence for the claimed contributions and failure to demonstrate clear advantages over established methods.
