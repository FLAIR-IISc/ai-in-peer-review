

# Peer Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes a character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that incorporates morphological information through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The main contributions are: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combination rules using two RNNs; (2) a hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates characters based on word-level representations; and (3) empirical evidence demonstrating the model's ability to handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words and learn meaningful morphological representations.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling Demonstration of Morphological Learning**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 clearly shows that the authors' word encoder produces more coherent representations of words with similar morphology (e.g., "notable" and "notability" are closer together) compared to an ordinary RNN encoder. This is a significant contribution to understanding how character-level models can implicitly capture linguistic structure.

2. **Practical Advantage for OOV Handling**: The model's ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") correctly is a valuable practical contribution. The examples in Table 2 clearly demonstrate this advantage over standard word-level models, which would map such words to <unk> tokens. This addresses a real-world problem in NMT that has practical implications for deployment.

3. **Training Efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model trains significantly faster than previous character-level approaches. The training time for DCNMT (7 days) is substantially less than Luong and Manning (2016) (~90 days), while achieving comparable BLEU scores. This efficiency advantage is well-supported by the experimental results in Table 1.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty**: The core architecture (using RNNs to learn word representations) is not substantially different from prior work. The word encoder bears strong resemblance to Ling et al. (2015b) and the hierarchical decoder to Lee et al. (2016). The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from these predecessors, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

2. **Inaccurate Performance Claims**: The paper claims the model is "comparable to the state-of-the-art," but the BLEU scores in Table 1 show DCNMT consistently underperforms the best models. For En-Fr, the best model (bpe2bpe) achieves 30.56 BLEU, while DCNMT achieves 30.56 (final) and 29.31 (after 1 epoch). The difference is marginal but not as significant as the paper suggests. The authors overstate the competitiveness of their approach.

3. **Limited Analysis of Morphological Learning**: While the paper claims the model learns morphology, the analysis is superficial. The PCA visualization (Figure 3) shows some patterns, but the paper doesn't provide deeper insights into *how* the model learns morphology or how it compares to explicit morphological analysis. The experiments are limited to English and Czech, with no analysis of how the model handles more complex morphological systems (e.g., agglutinative languages).

4. **Insufficient Comparison with Modern Approaches**: The paper compares primarily with older character-level models and BPE-based approaches, but fails to include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art models like those using more sophisticated subword segmentation (e.g., Sennrich et al., 2016) or hybrid approaches. This makes it difficult to assess whether the claimed competitiveness is meaningful in the current landscape.

5. **Implementation Ambiguity**: The paper mentions "six recurrent neural networks" but provides insufficient detail on their interconnections and training procedure. The description of the hierarchical decoder (Section 3.2) is particularly vague, making it challenging to replicate the model or fully understand the claimed efficiency gains.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your word encoder specifically differ from Ling et al.'s (2015b) character-to-word model, which also uses a hierarchical approach with character-level processing and word-level output? The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish these approaches.

2. Your model achieves competitive BLEU scores, but consistently underperforms the best models (e.g., bpe2bpe on En-Fr). What specific factors might explain this gap, and how might your approach be improved to close it?

3. The paper demonstrates success with English and Czech, but these languages have relatively simple morphology. How would your model perform on more morphologically complex languages (e.g., Turkish or Finnish), where morphology plays a more significant role?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating how character-level NMT can implicitly learn morphology to handle OOV words and misspellings. The empirical evidence for morphological learning is strong, and the practical advantage for handling real-world input errors is significant. However, the claimed novelty is overstated, and the performance claims are exaggerated. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with modern approaches, clearer differentiation from prior work, and deeper analysis of the morphological learning process. The model's efficiency is a notable advantage, but the marginal performance gains compared to existing approaches require more justification.

The paper is acceptable for ACL, but would benefit from substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above. The core idea of incorporating morphological information into character-level NMT is promising, but the current presentation doesn't fully establish its novelty or significance in the current state of the art.
