

# Peer Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes DCNMT, a deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a novel architecture. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two RNNs, (2) a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations, and (3) a character-level model that solves the OOV problem while achieving competitive performance with word-level models.

## Strengths

1. **Practical demonstration of morphology learning**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their word encoder learns meaningful morphological representations. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 demonstrate that their model clusters words with similar morphology (e.g., "notable" and "notability") more effectively than an ordinary RNN encoder. This is particularly convincing as it directly addresses a key challenge in NMT.

2. **Effective handling of OOV and nonce words**: The experiments in Table 2 demonstrate a significant practical advantage over word-level models. The model correctly translates misspelled words and nonce words like "convenienter" (which never appeared in training), while word-level models would simply replace them with <unk> tokens. This is a tangible benefit with clear real-world applications.

3. **Competitive performance with efficient training**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models while training significantly faster (1 day vs. 3 months for Luong and Manning, 2016). The paper convincingly demonstrates that their hierarchical architecture avoids the training inefficiencies that typically plague character-level models, making the approach more practical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing work**: While the paper claims to learn morphology "automatically," this approach bears significant resemblance to BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) and other subword-level methods. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish how their morphological learning differs from BPE's subword learning or why it's superior. The word encoder's use of BiRNNs for weighting character representations isn't sufficiently novel compared to prior work on attention mechanisms.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art subword methods**: The paper compares primarily to character-level models but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with BPE-based models (which are the current standard in NMT). The results in Table 1 show DCNMT's BLEU scores are competitive with character-level models but don't demonstrate that it outperforms BPE-based approaches, which would be the more relevant comparison for a practical NMT system.

3. **Unclear necessity of the complex architecture**: The paper claims their model is "the simplest and smallest" but uses six RNNs. It's unclear why this complexity is necessary when simpler character-level models (like Lee et al., 2016) achieve comparable results. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show that removing components (e.g., the BiRNN for weighting) would degrade performance, making it difficult to assess whether the architecture is truly optimal.

4. **Lack of detailed analysis on morphological learning**: While the paper shows some evidence of morphology learning (Figures 3-5), it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the model learns specific morphological rules. For example, it doesn't demonstrate how the model handles agglutinative languages with complex morphology, which would be a stronger test of the approach's capabilities.

5. **Overstated claims about "solving" the OOV problem**: The paper claims to solve the OOV problem, but character-level models still face challenges with extremely rare words or proper nouns. The paper doesn't discuss how their model handles these cases, which are significant in real-world translation scenarios.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with BPE-based models (which are the current standard in NMT) to demonstrate that your approach is superior to the existing state-of-the-art?

2. In Figure 5, you show subword segmentation for words like "consumers" and "monday." How does this compare to the segmentation produced by BPE, and what specific morphological advantages does your method offer?

3. The paper claims your model is "the simplest and smallest" but uses six RNNs. Could you provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost (FLOPs, memory usage) compared to standard word-level models and BPE-based models?

4. Could you elaborate on why your morphology learning approach is better suited for morphologically rich languages like Czech compared to BPE? The paper doesn't provide specific evidence of this advantage.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a thoughtful approach to character-level NMT that demonstrates promising results, particularly in handling OOV and nonce words. The morphology learning aspect is well-validated with compelling visualizations. However, the work lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing subword-level approaches, and the claimed advantages over BPE-based models aren't adequately demonstrated. The model architecture complexity isn't convincingly justified, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how this approach compares to the current standard in NMT (BPE).

The paper would be strengthened by more thorough comparisons with BPE-based models, a clearer justification of the architecture's necessity, and more detailed analysis of the morphological learning process. While the results are promising, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. I recommend rejection, but would encourage the authors to address these concerns in future work with more comprehensive comparisons and a clearer distinction from existing approaches.
