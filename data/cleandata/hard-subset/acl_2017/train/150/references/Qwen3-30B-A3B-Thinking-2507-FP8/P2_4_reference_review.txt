

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel neural machine translation model that operates at the character level but incorporates morphology learning through a specialized word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors aim to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in word-level NMT while avoiding the computational inefficiency of pure character-level models. Their model uses two RNNs to learn morphological representations of words and a hierarchical decoder that combines word-level and character-level processing.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through a two-RNN architecture (one for character-level representations, one for weighting these representations)
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently combines word-level and character-level processing
3. Empirical demonstration that character-level modeling with morphology learning achieves competitive performance while solving the OOV problem

## Strengths

**1. Novel and Practical Approach to Character-Level NMT**  
The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to character-level NMT that directly addresses the key challenges preventing character-level models from being practical: computational inefficiency and difficulty training the attention mechanism. The word encoder that learns morphology is particularly innovative, as it explicitly models how morphemes combine to form words. This is a significant contribution that moves beyond the typical "character-level" models that treat text as simple sequences of characters without considering linguistic structure.

**2. Strong Empirical Evidence of Morphology Learning**  
The paper provides compelling evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 clearly show that words with similar morphological structures are clustered together in the representation space, unlike the ordinary RNN word encoder. The ability to correctly translate "convenienter" (a nonce word formed by combining "convenient" and "er") demonstrates the model's ability to generalize beyond training examples based on morphological knowledge.

**3. Competitive Performance with Improved Efficiency**  
The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art word-level models (e.g., 30.56 BLEU on En-Fr) while solving the OOV problem. Crucially, the training efficiency is significantly better than previous character-level models—while Luong and Manning (2016) required 3 months of training, this model achieves similar results in just a few days. This practical advantage is a major contribution to the field.

**4. Practical Demonstration of Handling Misspellings and Nonce Words**  
The paper convincingly demonstrates the model's ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words (Table 2), which is a significant practical advantage over word-level models. This capability stems directly from the character-level processing combined with morphology learning, and it's a compelling real-world application.

## Weaknesses

**1. Inadequate Comparison with Modern Subword Models**  
The paper claims character-level models are better than subword-level models (Table 1), but it fails to compare with the current state of the art in subword modeling (e.g., BPE, SentencePiece). Recent work (e.g., Sennrich et al., 2016; Kudo, 2018) has shown that subword models can achieve excellent results while handling OOV words effectively. Without comparison to these methods, the claim that character-level models are better is unsupported. The paper should include comparisons with recent subword models.

**2. Lack of Ablation Studies**  
The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (word encoder vs. hierarchical decoder). Without this, it's unclear how much each component contributes to the overall performance. For example, it's difficult to determine whether the morphology learning is truly beneficial or whether the hierarchical decoding alone would have sufficed.

**3. Limited Analysis of Model Complexity**  
While the paper mentions the model has "six recurrent neural networks," it doesn't analyze the impact on model complexity, inference speed, or memory usage compared to standard word-level models. For practical deployment, these are critical considerations that the paper neglects.

**4. Insufficient Discussion of Failure Modes**  
The paper doesn't discuss cases where the model fails, particularly for highly morphologically complex words or languages. Understanding the model's limitations would strengthen the paper's claims about morphology learning.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare with the latest subword-level models (e.g., using SentencePiece or other advanced tokenization) that have achieved state-of-the-art results? The paper claims character-level models are better than subword-level models, but this hasn't been demonstrated with current approaches.

2. Could you provide more details on the inference speed and memory requirements of your model compared to standard word-level models? The paper focuses on training efficiency but doesn't address inference, which is critical for real-world deployment.

3. Have you tested your model on languages with even richer morphology than Czech (e.g., Finnish, Hungarian)? This would further validate the generalizability of your approach.

4. How does your model handle ambiguous morphological segmentation (e.g., words that can be segmented in multiple valid ways)? This is a common challenge in morphology learning.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to character-level NMT that effectively addresses key challenges in the field. The morphology learning mechanism is well-designed and empirically validated, and the model achieves competitive performance with improved training efficiency. However, the lack of comparison with modern subword models and insufficient analysis of model complexity are significant weaknesses that undermine the paper's claims. With revisions addressing these issues, this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to NMT research, particularly in the area of character-level modeling with morphology awareness. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above.
