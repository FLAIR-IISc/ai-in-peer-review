- Proposes DCNMT: a deep character-level NMT with a two-RNN word encoder that learns morphemes and combination rules.
- Introduces a hierarchical decoder that generates character sequences from word-level representations.
- Claims improved handling of OOV and misspelled words while achieving competitive BLEU scores.

- Strength: Demonstrates effective handling of OOV/misspelled/nonce words, outperforming word-level models in examples (Table 2).
- Strength: Trains much faster than prior character-level approaches, achieving competitive BLEU in 1 epoch vs months for prior work (Table 1).
- Strength: Produces meaningful morphological representations with PCA and analyses showing morpheme boundary learning and generalization (Figures 3–5).

- Weakness: Novelty is limited and seems incremental; the two-RNN encoder and hierarchical decoder resemble prior work (e.g., Ling et al., 2015; Luong & Manning).
- Weakness: Lacks rigorous ablation studies to isolate the contribution of morphology learning versus general character-level processing.
- Weakness: Evaluation of morphological learning is limited and anecdotal, with no quantitative metrics or broad language validation.
- Weakness: Comparisons are incomplete, missing recent alternatives (e.g., Lee et al., 2016) and more thorough analysis versus BPE/Subword methods.

- Question: How would the model handle languages with very different morphological typologies (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish)?
- Question: Can the authors provide ablation studies showing the performance impact of removing the morphology-learning word encoder?
- Question: Why does DCNMT score lower than BPE-based models on En–Fr, and how can the performance gap be closed while retaining OOV advantages?

- Overall: Practical and efficient approach with real-world OOV benefits but incremental novelty and insufficient ablations/comparisons; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address evaluations and comparisons.
