

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in NMT by learning morphology. The authors introduce a novel word encoder that decomposes words into morphemes using two RNNs, and a hierarchical decoder that first predicts target words and then generates characters for each word. The paper claims their model achieves competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art models while handling OOV words, misspelled words, and nonce words.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A word encoder that learns morphology by decomposing words into morphemes
2. A hierarchical decoding approach that combines word-level and character-level processing
3. Empirical evidence that the model can handle misspelled words and nonce words

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a genuine problem**: The paper correctly identifies the OOV problem as a significant challenge in NMT, and handling misspelled words and nonce words is a practical benefit for real-world applications.

2. **Provides empirical results**: The paper presents BLEU scores on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En) that show competitive performance compared to other character-level models. The results in Table 1 demonstrate that the model achieves reasonable BLEU scores.

3. **Provides evidence for morphology learning**: The PCA visualization in Figure 3 shows that the model produces representations where words with similar morphology cluster together, which partially supports the authors' claim about learning morphology.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of rigorous comparison with strong baselines**: The paper compares with some character-level models (C2W, char2char) and a weak subword baseline (bpe2bpe), but fails to compare with the most recent and strong baselines in the field. The paper doesn't compare with state-of-the-art BPE-based models (e.g., those using subword units with a large vocabulary) or transformer-based models that have become the standard in recent NMT. The comparison with "bpe2bpe" appears to be a weak baseline, as it's not using the standard BPE approach.

2. **Questionable novelty**: The core idea of learning morphology through RNNs is not novel. Similar approaches have been explored in prior work (e.g., morphological analysis in NLP), and the hierarchical decoder resembles the hybrid model proposed by Luong and Manning (2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work in this space.

3. **Insufficient evidence for key claims**: The paper claims "our model is able to learn the morphology" but the evidence provided (Figures 3 and 4) is limited and not compelling enough to support this claim. The PCA visualization shows some clustering of words with similar morphology, but it's not clear whether this is significantly better than what a standard word embedding model would produce. The example of "convenienter" is interesting but could be a special case.

4. **Overstated efficiency claims**: The paper claims their model is more efficient to train than character-level models (citing Luong and Manning, 2016), but doesn't provide a fair comparison of training times. The Luong and Manning (2016) model might have been trained on different hardware or with different hyperparameters, making the comparison invalid. The paper also doesn't provide a thorough comparison of training times with other recent character-level models.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to determine how much of the performance gain comes from the morphology learning versus the hierarchical decoding structure. Without these studies, it's impossible to assess the true contribution of each component.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims your model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art models, but it doesn't compare with the most recent and strongest baselines (e.g., Transformer models or the latest BPE-based models). How do your results compare with these more recent state-of-the-art approaches?

2. The paper states "our model is the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" but doesn't provide a thorough comparison of model sizes with other character-level models. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of model sizes and training times with recent character-level models?

3. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 shows some clustering of words with similar morphology, but it's not clear how much better this is than what a standard word embedding model would produce. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of the quality of word representations between your model and a standard word embedding model (e.g., using word similarity tasks)?

4. The paper claims the model can handle misspelled words and nonce words, but in Table 2, Google Translate also handles the misspelled words correctly. How does your model's performance on misspelled words compare with other state-of-the-art models?

## Overall Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper presents an interesting approach to handling the OOV problem, but the claims of novelty and effectiveness are not sufficiently supported by rigorous evidence and comparison with strong baselines. The paper fails to demonstrate that their method provides a significant improvement over existing approaches, and the evidence for their key claim (learning morphology) is limited. The lack of thorough comparison with state-of-the-art models and insufficient ablation studies make it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work.

The paper would need to be substantially revised to address these weaknesses, including providing a more comprehensive comparison with the strongest baselines, conducting proper ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each component, and providing more rigorous evidence for the morphology learning claim before it could be considered for acceptance.
