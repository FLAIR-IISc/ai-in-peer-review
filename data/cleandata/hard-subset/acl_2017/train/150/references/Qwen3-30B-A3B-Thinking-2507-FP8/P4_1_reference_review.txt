

# Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model called DCNMT that learns morphology to represent words. The model consists of a word encoder that learns morphemes and a hierarchical decoder that generates text character-by-character. The authors claim their approach solves the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, handles misspelled words and nonce words effectively, and achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art word-level and character-level models.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphology through a two-RNN architecture, generating meaningful word representations that cluster semantically similar words together (evidenced in Figure 3).
2. A hierarchical decoder that decodes at the character level using a HGRU (hierarchical GRU) that can generate character sequences based on word-level representations.
3. Demonstration that the model can correctly translate misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"), a significant practical advantage over word-level models.

## Strengths
1. **Novel morphology learning approach with empirical validation**: The paper's most significant contribution is the word encoder that learns morphology through a two-RNN architecture. The authors provide strong empirical evidence (Figure 3) that their representation clusters words with similar meanings more effectively than an ordinary RNN word encoder. This is a clear technical advancement over standard word embeddings and provides a meaningful representation of words based on morphological structure rather than just lexical statistics.

2. **Practical advantage for handling OOV and misspelled words**: The paper demonstrates a compelling real-world application of the character-level approach. Table 2 shows DCNMT correctly translating misspelled words and nonce words like "convenienter" (a made-up comparative form of "convenient"), which would be OOV in word-level models. This addresses a genuine limitation of standard word-level NMT systems and provides a practical benefit for real-world applications.

3. **Competitive performance with efficient training**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art word-level and character-level models (Table 1), while being more efficient to train than some character-level baselines. The paper shows that their model can reach competitive results in just one epoch (27.02 BLEU on En-Fr dev set), whereas the bpe baseline requires 50 epochs to reach its best performance (26.91 BLEU). This efficiency advantage is significant for practical applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clear novelty distinction from existing work**: The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from closely related work like Ling et al. (2015b) and Luong and Manning (2016). The word encoder resembles the C2W model (Ling et al., 2015b), and the hierarchical decoder is similar to the hybrid approach of Luong and Manning (2016). The paper doesn't clearly establish how the specific architecture (e.g., the use of BiRNN for weight computation) is novel compared to existing approaches.

2. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art models**: The paper compares with models from 2015-2016 (e.g., C2W, BPE models), but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art NMT models from WMT 2016-2017 competitions. For instance, the paper doesn't compare with the Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017) or other recent advances that have significantly improved NMT performance. This weakens the claim that the model is competitive with "state-of-the-art" approaches.

3. **Insufficient analysis of computational trade-offs**: While the paper claims the model is "the simplest and the smallest one in terms of model size" (Table 1), it lacks a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between model complexity, training time, inference time, and performance. The paper doesn't provide metrics on inference speed or memory usage compared to word-level models, making it difficult to assess the practical viability of the approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to learn morphology "automatically" but doesn't clearly differentiate this from existing subword tokenization methods like BPE. Could the authors clarify how their approach differs from BPE in terms of learning morphology, particularly in terms of the mechanism for identifying morpheme boundaries?

2. The paper demonstrates that the model can segment words into morphemes (Figure 5), but doesn't provide quantitative analysis of segmentation accuracy. Could the authors provide metrics on how well the model identifies morphological boundaries compared to other methods?

3. The paper claims the model is efficient to train but doesn't provide detailed computational complexity analysis (e.g., FLOPs, memory usage) compared to word-level models. Could the authors provide such analysis to better understand the practical trade-offs of their approach?
