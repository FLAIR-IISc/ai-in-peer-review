

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The key claim is that their approach can achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art models while eliminating the need for large vocabularies, and it can handle misspelled words and nonce words that word-level models cannot.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two RNNs, producing meaningful word representations.
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates character sequences while maintaining the benefits of word-level modeling.
3. A demonstration of the model's ability to handle morphological variations and translate misspelled words.

## Strengths

### 1. Novel Approach to Morphology Learning
The paper's most significant contribution is the word encoder that learns morphology through a two-RNN architecture. Unlike previous approaches that rely on explicit segmentation (like BPE) or simple character-level models, this encoder learns to identify morphemes and their combinations. The visualizations in Figure 3 compellingly show that the model produces more meaningful word representations than an ordinary RNN word encoder, with words sharing morphological patterns clustering together. This is a meaningful contribution to understanding how neural models can implicitly capture linguistic structure.

### 2. Practical Utility for Handling OOV Words
The paper convincingly demonstrates that the model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, which is impossible for standard word-level models. As shown in Table 2, while word-level models map misspelled words to <unk> tokens, DCNMT successfully recovers the intended meaning. This is a significant practical advantage that addresses a real-world problem in translation systems.

### 3. Competitive Performance with Improved Training Efficiency
The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art approaches (Table 1) while being more efficient to train than previous character-level models. The paper shows that their model takes only ~7 days to train for En-Fr (compared to ~27 days for the purely character model of Luong and Manning, 2016), which is a substantial improvement. The hierarchical decoder with the unfolding mechanism effectively addresses the training efficiency issues that plague character-level models.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Misleading "Deep Character-Level" Claim
The paper's title and claims overstate the character-level nature of their approach. The model actually uses a word encoder that learns morphology, making it a hybrid approach rather than a true character-level model. This misrepresentation is problematic because it conflates their morphological learning approach with the character-level paradigm they're trying to improve upon. The comparison with "purely character" models (like Luong and Manning, 2016) is misleading since their approach is fundamentally different.

### 2. Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art Subword Models
The paper compares with outdated BPE models (bpe2bpe) rather than the current state-of-the-art subword approaches like those using BPE with large vocabularies (Sennrich et al., 2016) or more recent subword segmentation techniques. The comparison is not fair or meaningful for the current NMT landscape. For instance, the paper states that "character-level models are better than the subword-level models" (Table 1), but this is not supported by current literature where subword models dominate.

### 3. Insufficient Analysis of Morphological Learning
While the paper shows some visualizations of word representations (Figure 3), it lacks a thorough analysis of how the model actually learns morphology. For example:
- It doesn't demonstrate how the model handles morphological changes in languages beyond English
- It doesn't provide quantitative analysis of the model's ability to generalize to unseen morphological forms
- It doesn't compare with other morphology-aware approaches (e.g., those using morphological analysis)

The claim that "our model is able to learn the morphology" is supported only by qualitative examples, not by systematic analysis.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "character-level models are better than the subword-level models" (Table 1), but this contradicts current NMT practice where subword models are standard. How do you reconcile this with the recent state-of-the-art (e.g., Sennrich et al., 2016; Firat et al., 2016)?

2. The paper mentions that "the model is able to guess the meaning of 'everything' even it had never seen 'everything' before" (Section 5.2). Could you provide more details on how the model generalizes to unseen morphological forms, including quantitative evidence?

3. The hierarchical decoder's unfolding mechanism (Section 3.2) is claimed to improve training efficiency. Can you provide more details on how this compares to the approach used in Luong and Manning (2016), which also uses a hybrid word-character model?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting approach to learning morphology for neural machine translation with practical benefits for handling OOV words. The word encoder design is novel and the demonstrations of handling misspelled words are compelling. However, the paper overstates its contribution by misrepresenting its approach as "deep character-level" when it's fundamentally a hybrid method. The comparison with state-of-the-art models is inadequate, and the analysis of morphological learning is insufficiently rigorous.

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field by demonstrating a more efficient way to handle morphology in neural translation, but it doesn't fundamentally advance the state of the art. The training efficiency claims are promising but not fully supported by the evidence provided. For acceptance, the authors would need to clarify the relationship between their approach and existing hybrid models, provide more thorough comparisons with current state-of-the-art subword models, and conduct more rigorous analysis of their morphology learning capabilities.

Given these weaknesses, I would recommend a borderline decision. If the authors can adequately address the concerns about misrepresentation of their approach and provide more meaningful comparisons with current state-of-the-art models, this could be a good paper. As it stands, it's a marginal submission that doesn't make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field.
