

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel neural machine translation (NMT) model that operates at the character level while learning morphology implicitly. The authors address the limitations of both word-level models (vocabulary size, OOV problem) and pure character-level models (training difficulties, inefficiency). Their approach features a two-RNN word encoder that identifies morphemes and their combinations, and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations. The model is designed to achieve competitive performance without relying on predefined subword units like BPE, while solving the OOV problem.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel character-level NMT architecture that learns morphology through a two-RNN word encoder, implicitly identifying morphemes and their combinations without requiring explicit segmentation.
2. An efficient hierarchical decoder (HGRU) that enables end-to-end training of character-level models while maintaining reasonable computational efficiency.
3. Demonstrated effectiveness in handling OOV and nonce words, with competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art models on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling Evidence of Morphology Learning**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed word encoder learns meaningful morphological representations. Figure 3 clearly shows that the model's representations cluster words with similar morphemes (e.g., "reliable" and "reliability") in a more linguistically meaningful way than an ordinary RNN word encoder. This is a significant contribution to understanding how character-level models can capture linguistic structure.

2. **Practical Advantage for OOV and Nonce Words**: The experiments in Section 5.3 (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate the model's ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") correctly, which is impossible for standard word-level models. This addresses a real-world problem in translation where out-of-vocabulary words are common, and it's a practical advantage that could be valuable in applications.

3. **Efficient Character-Level Training**: The paper successfully addresses a key challenge in character-level NMT - the difficulty of training these models efficiently. Table 1 shows that their model trains significantly faster than Luong and Manning's (2016) character-level model (1 day vs. 3 months), while achieving comparable BLEU scores. The HGRU design (Equations 2-7) appears to be a clever solution to the inefficiency problem that has plagued previous character-level approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Current State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares with older character-level models (C2W, CNMT, Luong and Manning 2016) and BPE-based models, but fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art NMT models (e.g., Transformer-based architectures). The current standard in NMT is not BPE-based RNNs but rather Transformer models, which have largely surpassed RNN-based approaches. Without comparing with these newer models, the paper's claim of "competitive with state-of-the-art" is questionable.

2. **Lack of Clear Practical Advantage Over BPE**: While the paper demonstrates the model can handle OOV words, it doesn't convincingly argue why a character-level approach is worth the increased complexity over the more efficient BPE-based approach, which is the current standard in NMT. The BLEU improvements over BPE are marginal (e.g., +0.5 BLEU for En-Fr), and the paper doesn't provide evidence that these marginal gains outweigh the computational costs of the character-level approach.

3. **Unclear Novelty of the Core Approach**: The paper's main contribution is an implementation of a character-level model with morphology learning, but this idea has been explored before. Luong and Manning (2016) proposed a hybrid model, and Ling et al. (2015) proposed a character-to-word model. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing works in terms of both methodology and results. The novelty appears to be in the specific implementation details rather than a fundamentally new approach.

4. **Insufficient Analysis of the Model's Limitations**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of when the model fails or where it performs worse than other approaches. For example, it doesn't analyze whether the model struggles with languages that have different morphological structures than English or Czech. Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess the model's generalizability and limitations.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be competitive with state-of-the-art models, but doesn't compare with recent Transformer-based models (e.g., Google's Transformer). How would your model perform compared to these newer architectures, and why might the character-level approach be advantageous over the Transformer?

2. The paper demonstrates that the model handles OOV words better than word-level models, but BPE-based models also handle OOV words reasonably well (by breaking words into subwords). How does your model's OOV handling compare to BPE-based models in terms of BLEU scores, and is the marginal improvement worth the increased complexity?

3. The paper states that "the model almost keeps the same encoding length for encoder as word-based models," but doesn't explain how this is achieved or what the exact encoding length is. Can you provide more details on how the encoding length compares to standard word-based models?

4. The paper mentions that "it is possible to use deeper recurrent networks to make the model deeper," but doesn't provide any analysis of how depth affects performance. What is the optimal depth for this architecture, and how does it compare to the depth used in standard word-level models?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **reject** for this paper. While the paper presents a technically sound approach with some interesting results on morphology learning and OOV handling, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work, and the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed model offers meaningful advantages over the current state-of-the-art in NMT. The comparison with relevant recent work (Transformer-based models) is missing, and the practical advantages over the standard BPE approach are marginal.

## Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the comparison with state-of-the-art models**: The paper should compare with Transformer-based models to properly position its contribution in the current NMT landscape. Without this, the claim of being "competitive with state-of-the-art" is unsubstantiated.

2. **Provide more detailed analysis of when the model excels and when it doesn't**: A thorough analysis of the model's limitations would strengthen the paper and help readers understand the contexts where this approach would be most beneficial.

3. **Clarify the practical advantages over BPE**: The paper should provide a more compelling argument for why the character-level approach is worth the complexity over the more efficient BPE approach, possibly by analyzing the trade-offs between BLEU gain, training time, and inference speed.

4. **Include more linguistic analysis**: The paper shows that the model learns meaningful representations, but it could be strengthened with more detailed linguistic analysis of the morphemes it learns and how they relate to linguistic theory.

The paper has some interesting ideas about learning morphology, but the current presentation doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that these ideas lead to a meaningful advancement in the field of neural machine translation.
