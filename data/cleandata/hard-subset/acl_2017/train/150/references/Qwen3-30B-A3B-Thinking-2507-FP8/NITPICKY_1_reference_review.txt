

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that aims to solve the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in neural machine translation by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and using a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation. The authors claim their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words, and achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art models.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphology by breaking words into morphemes (though the implementation details are confusing and the benefits are not clearly established)
2. A hierarchical decoder that decodes at the character level (though the technical details contain significant errors)
3. A demonstration of character-level modeling that claims to solve the OOV problem (though the claim is misleading)

## Strengths
1. The idea of learning morphology to improve translation is conceptually interesting and potentially valuable for morphologically rich languages.

2. The hierarchical decoder design is novel compared to previous character-level models, and the paper does provide some evidence of meaningful word representations through the PCA plots in Figure 3.

3. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art models on the En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, though the comparison is flawed due to misrepresentation of previous work.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Major Technical Error in Model Description (Critical)
The paper contains a clear mathematical error in the unfolding matrix explanation in Section 3.2. The paper states: "When the target output is 'g o ! ' and the output of the first-level decoder is [s1, s2], the unfolding step will be: [s1, s2] [1 1 1 0 0 0 0 0 1 1] = [s1, s1, s1, s2, s2]." This is mathematically impossible - the dimensions don't match (a 1×2 matrix multiplied by a 1×10 matrix is undefined). The correct operation would require a 2×10 matrix, but the paper doesn't provide the correct matrix. This is a fundamental error that suggests the model might not work as described, undermining the entire paper's credibility.

### 2. Misrepresentation of Previous Work (Critical)
The paper incorrectly describes Luong and Manning (2016) as a "purely character model" when it's actually a hybrid model that uses both words and characters. The paper states: "the purely character model of (5) (Luong and Manning, 2016) took 3 months to train." This is a significant mischaracterization that undermines the paper's claim of being a character-level model. Luong and Manning's model is explicitly a hybrid model, not purely character-based, so comparing it to a character-level model is misleading and inaccurate.

### 3. Inconsistent and Confusing Notation (Major)
The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, making it difficult to understand the model. Examples include:
- "gjt" vs "g^j_t" in equations
- "t" vs "t" in subscripts
- "EOS" vs "</s>" for end-of-sentence token
- "ranyone = tanh(∑_{t=1}^6 w_t r_t)" without justification for the number 6
- "w_t = exp(aff(h_t))" without defining "aff"

This lack of consistency in mathematical notation is a serious flaw in a technical paper.

### 4. Misleading Claims about OOV Problem (Major)
The paper claims to "eliminate the use of a large vocabulary" but still uses a vocabulary of 120 characters (which is a small vocabulary, but not eliminated). Character-level models still have OOV issues for characters not in the vocabulary, which the paper doesn't address. The claim is misleading and inaccurate.

### 5. Inconsistent References and Formatting (Minor but Significant)
The references have numerous formatting errors:
- Inconsistent use of "et al." (with stray backslashes like "et al\.")
- Strange characters like "\Q2015\E" in citations
- Inconsistent citation styles (some have "? 2015" while others have "2015")
- Missing or inconsistent years in citations
- The reference list includes "Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015" which has a question mark and backslash

These formatting errors suggest a lack of attention to detail, which is unacceptable for an ACL paper.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the unfolding matrix operation in Section 3.2? The matrix multiplication described is mathematically impossible (dimensions don't match). How is this operation actually implemented in your code?

2. Why do you describe Luong and Manning (2016) as a "purely character model" when it's actually a hybrid model that uses both words and characters? This mischaracterization significantly undermines your paper's credibility.

3. Can you provide more evidence that the learned subword units are meaningful and different from BPE? The segmentation in Figure 5 looks similar to BPE segmentations.

4. How does your model handle words with no morphological structure (e.g., "the", "a")? The paper doesn't address this.

5. The paper claims to "eliminate the use of a large vocabulary" but still uses a vocabulary of 120 characters. Can you clarify what you mean by "eliminate the use of a large vocabulary"?

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper contains a major technical error in the unfolding matrix explanation (Section 3.2) that is mathematically impossible, which suggests the model might not work as described. This is a critical flaw that cannot be easily fixed in a revision. Additionally, the paper misrepresents Luong and Manning (2016) as a purely character model when it's actually a hybrid model, which undermines the paper's main claim of being a character-level model.

While the idea of learning morphology is interesting, the paper's technical flaws, misrepresentations, and lack of clarity are too severe to recommend acceptance. The numerous minor inconsistencies in notation, references, and formatting further undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with such fundamental technical errors.
