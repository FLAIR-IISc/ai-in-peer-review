

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper

The paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in neural machine translation by learning morphology. The key idea is to replace traditional word-level encoders with a novel word encoder that learns morphemes through a two-RNN architecture, and to use a hierarchical decoder that operates at the character level. The authors claim their model achieves competitive translation performance while eliminating the need for large vocabularies.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes implicitly through a two-RNN architecture (one for character-level representation and one for weighting these representations)
2. A hierarchical decoder (HGRU) that generates characters based on the output of a word-level decoder
3. Experimental evidence that the model can translate misspelled words and nonce words correctly, demonstrating the benefits of learning morphology

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to morphology learning**: The paper proposes a way to learn morphemes implicitly through a two-RNN architecture, which is different from explicit segmentation methods like BPE. This is potentially valuable for morphologically rich languages. The ability to translate "convenienter" correctly (a nonce word) is a compelling demonstration of the model's morphology learning capability.

2. **Demonstrated efficiency**: The paper claims their model is more efficient to train than character-level models like Luong and Manning (2016), which took 3 months to train. The training time of their model is reported as 7 days for En-Fr and 5 days for En-Cs, which is reasonable compared to other character-level models.

3. **OOV problem solution**: The model addresses the OOV problem by learning morphology, as shown by the ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words correctly, which word-level models cannot do.

4. **Competitive results**: The BLEU scores reported are competitive with state-of-the-art models, especially considering the model's simplicity (only 54M parameters vs. 76M for bpe2char models).

## Weaknesses

1. **Missing figures**: The paper references "Figure 4(a) and Figure 4(b)" in section 5.2 but these figures are not included in the submission. Without these figures, it's impossible to verify the key analysis of the morphology learning process. This is a critical omission that undermines the paper's main claim.

2. **Inconsistent notation and equations**: The paper has multiple inconsistencies in mathematical notation. For example, in equation (2), "gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit" uses 'g' for two different variables (previous state and new state), which is confusing. In equations (3) and (4), the notation for 'q' and 'z' is inconsistent, with some equations using 'q' as an index and others as a variable.

3. **Missing details on key components**: The paper claims to use a "target word encoder" but doesn't describe it in detail (section 3.3). Similarly, they mention "the word encoder may be able to guess the meaning of 'everything' even it had never seen 'everything' before" but don't provide any quantitative evidence for this claim. The paper also doesn't explain how the model detects word boundaries (delimiters) in the target language.

4. **Inconsistent BLEU score reporting**: Table 1 reports BLEU scores as "27.02 28.13" for DCNMT on En-Fr, but doesn't specify which number corresponds to the one-epoch training and which is the final score. This makes it difficult to assess the model's performance gain from additional training.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper claims their model is efficient because it consists of six recurrent networks, but doesn't provide an ablation study showing how removing some of these networks affects performance. This is a significant omission that weakens their argument about model efficiency.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "we use an activation function tanh as it performs best in experiments" for the weights wt = exp(aff(ht)), but doesn't normalize by the sum of weights. Why did you choose not to normalize the weights (which is standard in attention mechanisms), and how does this affect the model's ability to learn meaningful morphemes?

2. The paper claims "the number of outputs of the first-level decoder is much fewer than the target character sequence" but doesn't explain why this is the case. Can you clarify the relationship between the number of target words and target characters, and how this affects the model's performance?

3. The paper references Figure 4(a) and 4(b) but these figures are missing. Can you provide these figures to support your analysis of how the model learns morphology?

4. The paper doesn't specify the beam size used for inference. What beam size was used, and how did it affect the BLEU scores reported in Table 1?

5. How did you handle words with characters outside the 120 most frequent characters? What was the percentage of such words in the training data, and how did this impact translation quality?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting idea for learning morphology in neural machine translation, it contains multiple critical flaws that undermine the paper's credibility. The most serious issue is the missing figures (Figure 4(a) and 4(b)) that are essential to support the key claims about morphology learning. The inconsistent notation, missing details about key components, and unclear reporting of results further diminish the paper's quality.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find that the accumulation of these minor but significant flaws (missing figures, inconsistent equations, omitted details) outweighs the potential novelty and value of the proposed approach. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
