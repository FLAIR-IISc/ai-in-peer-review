- Proposes DCNMT: a word encoder using two RNNs to learn morphemes from characters, avoiding a fixed vocabulary.  
- Introduces a hierarchical decoder that generates characters conditioned on word-level representations.  
- Shows effectiveness on OOV, misspelled, and nonce words while achieving competitive BLEU scores.  

- Novel encoder explicitly learns morphology; PCA and energy-weight visualizations show meaningful morpheme-aware representations.  
- Robust handling of OOV/misspelled words, translating nonce forms correctly where word-level models yield UNK.  
- Competitive BLEU with substantially lower training cost (e.g., ~7 days vs. 3 months in prior work).  
- Provides clear analyses (energy weights, examples) demonstrating encoder’s generalization to unseen words.  

- Lacks comparisons to more recent (2017–2018) character-level models, limiting assessment of current competitiveness.  
- No ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the morphology-learning components.  
- Limited theoretical justification for specific encoder design choices (tanh vs softmax, need for BiRNN).  
- Insufficient analysis of scalability and deployment behavior on larger corpora, more languages, or longer sequences.  

- Requests a quantitative metric to measure how well the model learns morphology versus alternatives.  
- Asks how training time and memory scale with much larger datasets (e.g., 10× data).  
- Asks for clarification on how detected subword boundaries concretely improve translation quality.  

- Overall: strong, novel contribution addressing OOV via explicit morphology learning; recommended for acceptance but would benefit from newer comparisons and ablation studies.
