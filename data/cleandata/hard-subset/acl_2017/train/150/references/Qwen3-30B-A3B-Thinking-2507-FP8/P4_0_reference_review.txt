

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel architecture for neural machine translation that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by explicitly learning morphology through a word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The approach uses two RNNs in the word encoder to learn morphemes and their combinations, and a hierarchical decoder that operates at the character level while maintaining the same encoding length as word-based models. The authors claim their model can handle OOV words, misspelled words, and nonce words (new words formed by morphological rules) while achieving competitive BLEU scores.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphology by using two RNNs to represent morphemes and their combinations, rather than using a lookup table for word embeddings.
2. A hierarchical decoder that decodes at the character level while maintaining the same encoding length as word-level models, eliminating the need for a large vocabulary.
3. Demonstrated ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words correctly, which is not possible with standard word-level models.

## Strengths
1. **Effective Morphology Learning**: The paper provides strong visual evidence (Figure 3) that their word encoder produces more meaningful word representations compared to standard RNN word encoders. The PCA projections clearly show that words with similar meaning (e.g., "reliable" and "reliability") are closer together in the embedding space, demonstrating successful morphology learning. This is a significant contribution as it directly addresses the morphological structure of words, which is often ignored in standard word-level NMT.

2. **Practical OOV Handling**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, which is a major limitation of standard word-level models. The examples in Table 2 are compelling evidence that their approach solves a real-world problem that word-level models struggle with, as these words would be mapped to <unk> tokens in standard systems.

3. **Competitive Performance with Efficiency**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models (Table 1), while being significantly more efficient to train than some competing approaches (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016). The authors show that their model can outperform the bpe baseline after just one training epoch (Table 1), suggesting that the morphology learning accelerates the learning process.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Clear Novelty Over Prior Work**: The paper claims novelty for the word encoder, but the approach bears significant similarities to Ling et al. (2015b), which proposed a character-to-word (C2W) model that also learns morphology. The authors cite Ling et al. but fail to clearly differentiate their approach, making it difficult to assess the true novelty. The paper states "Compared with the CNN word encoder (Kim et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2016), our model is able to generate a meaningful representation of the word," but doesn't address how their approach differs from the C2W model by Ling et al., which is more directly related to morphology learning.

2. **Superficial Evaluation of Morphology Learning**: While the paper provides some evidence of morphology learning (Figures 3 and 4), it lacks quantitative evaluation of this capability. The authors don't provide a separate task to measure how well their model learns morphology (e.g., morpheme segmentation accuracy, inflection prediction), which would strengthen their claim. The PCA visualizations are suggestive but not conclusive evidence of successful morphology learning.

3. **Misleading "Deep" Claim**: The paper's title and claims emphasize "deep" character-level translation, but the architecture only uses six RNNs, which is not particularly deep compared to modern NMT systems. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the model's depth is a key factor in its performance, and the "deep" aspect appears to be more of a marketing claim than a substantive contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to learn morphology explicitly, but how does this compare to other approaches that explicitly model morphology (e.g., Firat et al., 2016; Sennrich et al., 2016)? Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the morphology learning capabilities?

2. The paper states that the model can translate "convenienter" correctly as a nonce word. Could you provide additional examples of nonce words with multiple morphological changes (e.g., "unbelievable" vs "unbelievability") to demonstrate the generalizability of this capability?

3. The paper describes a "deep" character-level model, but the architecture only has six RNNs. How does this compare to other deep NMT models in terms of performance and efficiency? What are the limitations of the current architecture for building deeper models?

## Recommendation
The paper presents a meaningful approach to learning morphology in neural machine translation, with strong evidence of improved handling of OOV words and nonce words. While the core idea of learning morphology through a word encoder is interesting, the paper would benefit from a clearer differentiation from existing work, particularly Ling et al. (2015b), and more rigorous evaluation of the morphology learning capability. The efficiency claims are well-supported, and the practical benefits of handling misspelled words are compelling. The paper makes a modest but valuable contribution to the field, particularly for morphologically rich languages. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty and evaluation concerns.
