

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new neural machine translation (NMT) architecture called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level NMT systems. The authors claim their model learns morphology through a novel word encoder that uses two RNNs to represent words as combinations of morphemes, and a hierarchical decoder that generates text at the character level. They claim their approach eliminates the need for large vocabularies while maintaining competitive performance with state-of-the-art models.

## Main Contributions Claimed (as seen by authors)

1. A novel word encoder that learns morphology to represent words as combinations of meaningful morphemes
2. A hierarchical decoder that enables character-level translation without requiring large vocabulary
3. A model that can translate misspelled or nonce words correctly by leveraging learned morphology
4. An efficient training process that requires fewer training epochs than competing character-level models

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement**: The paper effectively identifies the OOV problem as a significant limitation of word-level NMT systems, which is a well-established challenge in the field. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work on this issue (Sutskever et al., 2014; Cho et al., 2015; Luong and Manning, 2016).

2. **Practical implementation details**: The paper provides sufficient technical details about the architecture and implementation (using Theano and Blocks), which would enable replication of the work. The training details (hyperparameters, dataset preprocessing) are well-specified.

3. **Demonstration of OOV handling**: The paper provides some evidence that their model can handle misspelled words better than standard word-level models (Table 2), which is a practical benefit that addresses a real-world limitation of current NMT systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty and insufficient comparison to strong baselines**: The paper claims to introduce a "novel" architecture for learning morphology, but it fails to convincingly distinguish its approach from existing subword-level models (like BPE) or hybrid word-character models (Luong and Manning, 2016). The comparison to baselines is limited to a few character-level models and doesn't include the strongest word-level baselines (like Sennrich et al., 2016). The paper's claim of being "comparable to state-of-the-art" is unsubstantiated since the comparison doesn't include the best-performing word-level models.

2. **Insufficient evidence for key claims**: The paper's central claim that their model "learns morphology" is supported only by weak evidence (Figures 3-5). The PCA projections in Figure 3 are not particularly convincing, and the authors don't provide statistical evidence or rigorous linguistic analysis to support their claim. The examples in Table 2 showing translation of "convenienter" are anecdotal and don't demonstrate systematic learning of morphological rules.

3. **Misleading efficiency claims**: The paper claims their model is "efficient to train" with "1 epoch" compared to "3 months" for other models, but this is misleading. The comparison is made between a model trained for only 1 epoch versus others trained for many more epochs (300 epochs for C2W). The paper doesn't show convergence curves or compare models trained to the same number of epochs, making the efficiency claim unsubstantiated.

4. **Overstated advantages**: The paper claims their model "achieves a high translation performance which is comparable to the state-of-the-art neural machine translation model," but the BLEU scores in Table 1 show that their model is actually slightly behind the best-performing baselines (e.g., 30.56 vs. 30.75 for the best baseline). The paper also overstates the benefits of morphology learning without providing convincing evidence.

5. **Unnecessary complexity**: The paper describes a model with "six recurrent neural networks" (Section 3.3), which seems unnecessarily complex compared to simpler subword-level approaches. The paper fails to justify why this complexity is necessary or how it provides a clear advantage over existing methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your word encoder's "morphology learning" capability fundamentally differ from the subword segmentation learned by BPE (Sennrich et al., 2016)? What specific linguistic properties does your approach capture that BPE does not?

2. The paper claims your model is "the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" (Table 1), but the model size (54M parameters) is similar to other character-level models. What specific architectural simplifications or innovations lead to this claim?

3. The paper states that "when we train our model just for one epoch, the obtained result even outperforms the final result with bpe baseline." This is a surprising claim that requires stronger evidence. Can you provide statistical significance testing to support this claim?

4. The paper claims that your model can "translate the misspelled words or the nonce words" correctly. Can you provide a more comprehensive evaluation of this capability, including quantitative metrics on a larger set of such examples?

5. The paper claims that "the deep character-level model is able to learn the similar embedding of the words with similar meanings like the word-level models." Can you provide more rigorous evidence of this beyond the PCA projections in Figure 3?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the problem addressed (OOV words) is important and the paper provides some practical implementation details, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or rigorously validated. The paper makes several unsupported claims about the advantages of learning morphology, and the evidence provided is anecdotal and insufficient to justify the proposed approach. The comparison to baselines is inadequate, and the efficiency claims are misleading. The paper fails to demonstrate that their approach offers meaningful advantages over established subword-level methods that have been thoroughly validated in the literature. Given the field's emphasis on rigorous validation and clear incremental contributions, this paper does not meet the necessary standards for publication at ACL.
