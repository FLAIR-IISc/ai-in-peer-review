

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level NMT by learning morphology implicitly. The core idea is to use a novel word encoder that processes words as sequences of characters to learn morphemes and their combinations, combined with a hierarchical decoder that generates characters at the word level.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them (ordered by strength):

1. **A novel word encoder that learns morphology implicitly** through two RNNs (one processing preceding characters, another computing morpheme weights), enabling the model to handle OOV words and learn meaningful word representations without explicit segmentation.
2. **An efficient hierarchical decoder architecture** that decodes at character level while maintaining the benefits of word-level modeling, avoiding the need to process entire sentences at character level.
3. **Demonstrated effectiveness and efficiency** of the approach through experiments on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, showing competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art models while addressing the OOV issue.

## Strengths

1. **Tackling a meaningful practical problem**: The paper addresses the OOV problem in NMT, which is a significant issue in real-world applications. The approach of learning morphology implicitly rather than relying on subword segmentation (like BPE) is valuable and addresses a genuine pain point in NMT.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art models, including character-level models. The paper provides compelling evidence that the model can handle misspelled words and nonce words better than word-level baselines (Table 2), which is a practical advantage that should be highlighted more prominently.

3. **Clear demonstration of morphology learning**: The paper provides strong evidence through PCA projections (Figure 3) that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The analysis in Section 5.2 showing that words with similar meanings and morphological structures are embedded closer together is particularly convincing.

4. **Efficiency advantages**: The paper demonstrates that the model is more efficient to train than other character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016, which took 3 months to train). The authors show that their model with six RNNs is actually simpler and smaller than other character-level models (Table 1).

5. **Well-structured presentation**: The paper provides a clear description of the model architecture with helpful figures (Figures 1-2) and equations, making it easy to understand the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: The paper mentions that Luong and Manning (2016) proposed a hybrid neural machine translation model leveraging both words and characters, but doesn't clearly differentiate the novelty of their approach. The core idea of using morphological information for NMT has been explored before, and the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how their specific implementation differs from prior work.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art baselines**: The paper shows that character-level models outperform BPE-based models, but the current state-of-the-art for NMT is BPE-based (e.g., Sennrich et al., 2016), so the most relevant comparison would be with the best BPE-based models. The paper doesn't show how their model compares to the best BPE models in terms of BLEU score, which is a significant omission.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show how much each component (word encoder, hierarchical decoder, etc.) contributes to the overall performance. Without this, it's hard to determine which aspects of the model are truly driving the improvements.

4. **Limited analysis of the model's limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, how does the model perform on languages with very different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish)? The current experiments are limited to English-French and English-Czech, which are morphologically different but not extreme cases.

5. **Questionable efficiency claims**: The paper claims their model is more efficient to train than other character-level models, but the comparison in Table 1 is based on training days without accounting for computational resources. It's possible that their model is faster on a single GPU but might require more GPUs or more time on a different setup, making the efficiency claim potentially misleading.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to the best BPE-based models (e.g., those using subword units with more sophisticated segmentation) in terms of BLEU score? The paper shows character-level models outperform BPE-based models, but the current state-of-the-art for NMT is BPE-based, so it would be important to show how your model compares to the best BPE models.

2. Could you provide ablation studies to show how much each component (word encoder, hierarchical decoder, etc.) contributes to the overall performance? This would help understand the relative importance of the key innovations.

3. How does your model perform on languages with different morphological properties (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish)? The current experiments are limited to English-French and English-Czech, which are morphologically different but not extreme cases.

4. The paper claims the model can translate "convenienter" correctly. Could you provide more examples of how the model handles morphological changes beyond the examples given in Table 2?

5. In the hierarchical decoder, the authors mention using a matrix to unfold the outputs of the first-level decoder for batch training. Could you provide more details on how this unfolding works and how it affects the model's ability to handle variable-length sequences?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper addresses a meaningful problem in NMT with a novel approach that shows promising results. The core idea of learning morphology implicitly is interesting and the empirical results are convincing for the specific tasks tested. However, the paper has significant weaknesses in terms of novelty and comparison with the current state-of-the-art, which are critical for acceptance at ACL.

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has interesting ideas and strong empirical results, but the lack of clear novelty compared to prior work and inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art BPE-based models are significant weaknesses. If the authors can address these issues by:
1) Providing a more thorough comparison with the best BPE-based models
2) Adding ablation studies to show the contribution of each component
3) Clarifying the novelty of their approach relative to prior work
4) Expanding the experimental evaluation to more morphologically diverse languages

the paper could be significantly strengthened and potentially accepted at ACL.
