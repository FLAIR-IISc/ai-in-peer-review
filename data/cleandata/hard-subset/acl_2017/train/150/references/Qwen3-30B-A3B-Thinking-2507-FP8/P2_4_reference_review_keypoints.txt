- Paper proposes a character-level NMT model that learns morphology via a specialized two-RNN word encoder and a hierarchical decoder to balance OOV handling and efficiency.  
- Main contribution: a two-RNN word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations (one RNN for character reps, one for weighting).  
- Main contribution: a hierarchical decoder that combines word-level and character-level processing for efficiency.  
- Main contribution: empirical evidence that morphology-aware character modeling achieves competitive BLEU while solving OOVs.  
- Strength: novel, practical approach addressing character-level NMT inefficiencies and attention training issues by explicitly modeling morpheme composition.  
- Strength: strong empirical evidence of meaningful morphological representations (PCA visualizations, nonce-word translation example).  
- Strength: competitive BLEU scores (e.g., 30.56 on En–Fr) with much faster training than prior character-level work.  
- Strength: robust handling of misspellings and nonce words, demonstrating practical advantages over word-level models.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison with modern subword methods (BPE, SentencePiece), so claims versus subword models are unsupported.  
- Weakness: no ablation studies to isolate contributions of the word encoder versus hierarchical decoder.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of model complexity, inference speed, and memory usage relative to word-level models.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of failure modes, especially for highly morphologically complex languages or cases.  
- Question: how does the model compare to current subword-level models (SentencePiece, BPE) in performance and OOV handling?  
- Question: what are the inference speed and memory requirements compared to standard word-level models?  
- Question: has the model been tested on morphologically richer languages (e.g., Finnish, Hungarian) to validate generalizability?  
- Question: how does the model handle ambiguous morphological segmentation where multiple segmentations are valid?  
- Overall: technically sound, well-written, and a meaningful contribution, recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing comparisons to subword models and complexity analysis.
