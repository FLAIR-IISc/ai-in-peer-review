

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary
The paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issue in neural machine translation by learning morphology directly from character sequences. The key components are a word encoder that learns morphemes and combines them to form word representations, and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters at the second level based on word representations. The authors claim their model achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art models while avoiding the OOV problem and handling misspelled words better than word-level models.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. A word encoder that learns morphemes and combines them to form word representations through a weighted sum of character-level representations
2. A hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations
3. An analysis of how the model learns morphology and handles misspelled words

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem statement**: The paper effectively identifies the OOV problem as a key limitation of word-level NMT, and the motivation for exploring character-level approaches is well-justified.

2. **Novel architecture**: The hierarchical architecture with a word encoder that learns morphology (rather than using a lookup table) represents an interesting approach to character-level NMT. The idea of using weights to determine morpheme boundaries is conceptually novel.

3. **Empirical evidence of morphology learning**: The PCA visualization in Figure 3 provides some evidence that the model learns meaningful word representations based on morphology, which is a valuable contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Major factual error in Table 1**: The paper claims "when we train our model just for one epoch, the obtained result even outperforms the final result with bpe baseline." However, Table 1 shows that for En-Fr, DCNMT at one epoch has a test score of 28.13, while the bpe baseline has a test score of 29.70. This is a clear factual error that undermines the paper's credibility. The authors incorrectly claim their model outperforms the baseline when the data shows the opposite.

2. **Misrepresentation of the OOV problem**: The paper states "we have solved the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issue that word-level models suffer from." However, character-level models inherently solve the OOV problem by definition, as they operate at the character level rather than the word level. This is a misleading claim that misrepresents the nature of the problem and the solution.

3. **Inconsistent and inaccurate technical descriptions**: 
   - The paper states "the first layer is a source word encoder which contains two RNNs" but Figure 1 shows only one RNN (with a BiRNN for hidden states).
   - The paper claims "the only difference between CNMT and DCNMT is CNMT uses an ordinary RNN to encode source words (takes the last hidden state)," but both models are character-level in Table 1, making this comparison confusing.
   - Equations (2)-(6) have multiple typos (e.g., "gt−1" should be "gt", and missing brackets).
   - The paper refers to "Figure 4(b)" but doesn't provide it, making it impossible to verify their claims about morpheme learning.

4. **Lack of novelty**: The core idea of using a character-level model with morphological awareness is not significantly different from prior work like "Fully character-level neural machine translation without explicit segmentation" (Lee et al., 2016) or "Character-aware neural language models" (Kim et al., 2016). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from these prior works.

5. **Inconsistent terminology and formatting**: 
   - The paper uses "char" for character-level and "bpe" for subword-level, which is inconsistent with standard terminology.
   - The references have numerous formatting errors (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015" should be "Bahdanau et al., 2015").
   - The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "coveres" instead of "covers," "effect" instead of "affect").

## Questions to Authors

1. The claim in Section 5.3 that "when we train our model just for one epoch, the obtained result even outperforms the final result with bpe baseline" contradicts the data in Table 1. For En-Fr, DCNMT at 1 epoch has a test score of 28.13, while the bpe baseline has a test score of 29.70. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

2. The paper claims to "solve the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issue," but character-level models inherently solve this issue by definition. How is your approach different from other character-level models that also solve the OOV problem?

3. Figure 4(b) is referenced in Section 5.2 but not provided in the paper. Could you provide this figure to verify the claim about "the word encoder may be able to guess the meaning of 'everything' even it had never seen 'everything' before"?

## Additional Comments

- The paper contains multiple typos and grammatical errors that need to be fixed (e.g., "coveres" instead of "covers," "effect" instead of "affect").
- The reference list has numerous formatting inconsistencies that need to be corrected.
- The paper's claim about learning morphology is not strongly supported by the evidence provided. The PCA visualization is not convincing, and there's no quantitative analysis of how well the model learns morphemes.
- The paper claims to have "a novel architecture" but doesn't clearly explain how it differs from prior work. The comparison to existing character-level models is insufficient.
- The claim that "our model is efficient to train" is contradicted by the training times in Table 1, where their model takes longer to train than some other models (e.g., "C2W(2)" takes 27 days while DCNMT takes 7 days for En-Fr).

## Recommendation
Reject

This paper has significant technical flaws, factual errors, and inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The major factual error in Table 1 (claiming to outperform the baseline when the data shows the opposite) is particularly concerning. Additionally, the paper's claims about novelty and solving the OOV problem are misleading or inaccurate. The numerous formatting errors, typos, and inconsistencies further diminish the paper's quality. While the core idea of learning morphology is interesting, the paper fails to properly support its claims or clearly differentiate itself from prior work. These issues collectively make the paper unsuitable for publication at ACL.
