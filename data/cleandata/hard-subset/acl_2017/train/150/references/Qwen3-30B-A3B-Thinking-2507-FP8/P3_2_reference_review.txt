

# Peer Review: "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that claims to address out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issues by learning morphology explicitly. The authors introduce a novel word encoder that uses two RNNs to represent morphemes and their combinations, and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations. The model claims to achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art approaches while solving OOV problems and handling misspelled words.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphological structure through character-level representations
2. A hierarchical decoder for efficient character-level generation
3. Evidence that the model learns meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships

## Strengths

1. **Novel Hierarchical Decoder Design**: The HGRU mechanism (Section 3.2) is a clever solution to the character-level decoding problem. By unfolding the first-level decoder outputs to feed the second-level decoder, the authors achieve efficient batch training without requiring two separate forward passes as in Luong and Manning (2016). This is a significant practical contribution that addresses a genuine pain point in character-level NMT.

2. **Strong Evidence for Morphology Learning**: The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 and the analysis of word representations in Figure 4 provide compelling evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological relationships. The observation that words with similar meanings cluster together (e.g., "notable" and "notability") is particularly convincing and demonstrates the model's ability to capture linguistic structure beyond simple character sequences.

3. **Practical OOV Handling**: The paper's demonstration of handling misspelled words (Table 2) is a strong practical contribution. Unlike word-level models that fail completely on OOV words, the DCNMT model correctly translates "convenienter" and handles misspelled words like "howeve" in the source text. This addresses a real-world problem that affects translation quality.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Novelty**: The core idea of using an attention-like mechanism to learn morphological structure within words is not substantially novel. The word encoder (Section 3.1) is essentially an attention mechanism applied to character-level representations within a word. This is very similar to the "composition" approach in Ling et al. (2015b), which the authors cite but don't adequately distinguish from their work. The paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from existing attention-based approaches.

2. **Inadequate Comparative Baselines**: The paper compares primarily with older character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016) rather than state-of-the-art approaches. The most significant omission is the lack of comparison with transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017), which have since become the standard in NMT and would provide a more meaningful benchmark for the proposed approach.

3. **Exaggerated Training Efficiency Claims**: The paper claims their model trained in "1 epoch" (7 days) versus Luong and Manning's "3 months" for comparable results. However, the training details don't specify hardware (the authors use a single GTX Titan X), batch size, or whether the Luong and Manning model was trained on the same hardware. Without these details, the efficiency claim is unsubstantiated and potentially misleading. In reality, modern transformer models can train on similar datasets in far less time.

4. **Limited Analysis of Model Limitations**: The paper doesn't address key limitations of character-level NMT, such as the increased computational complexity for long sequences and the potential degradation in performance on languages with shallow morphology. The paper focuses exclusively on morphologically rich languages (Czech) but doesn't demonstrate the approach's performance on morphologically poor languages like English where the benefits might be less pronounced.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your word encoder's "morphology learning" specifically differ from the attention mechanism used in Ling et al. (2015b) for their C2W model? Can you provide a more precise theoretical distinction?

2. The training efficiency claim (1 epoch vs. 3 months) is compelling but lacks critical details. Please clarify the hardware specifications (GPU type, number of GPUs), batch size, and whether Luong and Manning's model was trained on the same hardware for a fair comparison.

3. Why didn't you compare your model with recent transformer-based approaches, which have become the new standard in NMT? How would your architecture integrate with transformer-based models?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to character-level NMT with a novel hierarchical decoder design that addresses a genuine practical problem. The evidence for morphology learning is compelling, and the OOV handling capability is a valuable practical contribution. However, the novelty claim is overstated, the comparative evaluation is limited, and the training efficiency claims require verification. The paper makes a modest contribution to the field but fails to demonstrate significant advantages over more recent approaches. With stronger comparisons to current state-of-the-art models and clearer articulation of the novelty, this work could be more impactful. As it stands, the paper is acceptable but not particularly groundbreaking for ACL.
