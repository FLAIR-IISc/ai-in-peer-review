

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a "deep character-level neural machine translation" model that aims to address out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issues in neural machine translation by learning morphology. The authors claim to introduce a novel architecture with a word encoder that learns morphemes and a hierarchical decoder for character-level translation.

The main contributions claimed by the authors, in order of strength (as I see them):

1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes by using two RNNs to represent preceding characters and compute weights for morpheme boundaries.
2. A hierarchical decoder that generates character sequences conditioned on word-level representations.
3. Demonstration that character-level translation can achieve performance comparable to word-level models while solving the OOV problem.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical motivation for character-level translation**: The paper provides a clear motivation for character-level translation by addressing the OOV problem and the limitations of word-level models. The authors correctly identify that word-level models struggle with rare words and morphological variations, which is a valid concern in machine translation. The connection between morphology and translation quality is well-articulated.

2. **Novel approach to character-level modeling**: The hierarchical architecture with a word encoder that learns morphemes (rather than relying on explicit segmentation or subword units) represents a different approach from existing character-level methods. The idea of using weights to determine morpheme boundaries is conceptually interesting, even if the implementation details have issues.

3. **Demonstration of morphology learning**: The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 provide evidence that the word encoder learns meaningful representations of words with similar morphological structures. This is a valuable contribution that supports the authors' claim about learning morphology.

## Weaknesses

1. **Significant notational and mathematical inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous mathematical inconsistencies that undermine the technical quality of the work. For example:
   - In Equation (2), the recursive definition "gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit" is mathematically invalid as written (it should be gt = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit).
   - The weight calculation "wt = exp(aff(ht))" is not normalized (unlike standard attention mechanisms), and the paper incorrectly claims they "use an activation function tanh as it performs best in experiments" rather than normalizing by the sum.
   - The notation for the RNNs is inconsistent (ht vs st, f1 vs f2), which makes the model difficult to understand.

2. **Questionable novelty**: The authors claim novelty in "learning subword units automatically," but this is largely achieved through the same mechanism as BPE, which was proposed in Sennrich et al. (2016) and has been widely adopted. The paper fails to clearly distinguish their approach from existing subword methods. The Luong and Manning (2016) hybrid model already achieves similar goals, and the authors don't adequately address this prior work.

3. **Inaccurate experimental claims**: The paper makes several inaccurate claims about their results:
   - "the purely character model of (5) (Luong and Manning, 2016) took 3 months to train and yielded +0.5 BLEU points compared to our result." However, Table 1 shows Luong and Manning's model achieved 27.04 BLEU (En-Fr) while DCNMT achieved 30.56 BLEU (final score), meaning DCNMT outperformed by 3.52 BLEU points, not 0.5.
   - The paper claims their model is "the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size," but Table 1 shows DCNMT has the same size (54M) as C2W (54M), and the paper doesn't provide clear evidence that it's simpler.
   - In Table 2, the DCNMT translation of "howeve" as "cependant" is a translation of "however," not "howeve," which misrepresents the model's ability to handle misspelled words.

4. **Insufficient technical details**: The paper lacks crucial implementation details that would allow replication:
   - The exact architecture of the BiRNN used to compute ht in Figure 1 is not specified.
   - The "HGRU" variant is described but not clearly differentiated from standard GRU.
   - The unfolding procedure in Section 3.2 is described in words but not clearly explained mathematically.
   - The model size (54M parameters) for DCNMT is inconsistent with the claim of having six RNNs (which would require more parameters).

5. **Formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous minor errors that undermine professionalism:
   - "automati-cally" (hyphenation error)
   - "coveres" (should be "covers")
   - "both consists" (should be "both consist")
   - Inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "Sutskever et al., 2014" vs "Sutskever et al., 2014;")
   - "the only difference between CNMT and DCNMT is CNMT uses an ordinary RNN to encode source words (takes the last hidden state)" - this is confusingly worded and lacks clarity.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "we learn the subword unit automatically" but doesn't clearly distinguish this from BPE. How is your method different from existing subword approaches like BPE, and why is your approach more effective for morphology learning?

2. In Equation (2), the recursive definition "gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit" appears to be a typo. Could you clarify the correct mathematical formulation for the HGRU update?

3. The paper states "the vocabulary is often filled with many similar words that share a lexeme but have different morphology." This phrasing is confusing - words with different morphology typically have different meanings, not "similar words." Could you clarify what you mean by "similar words" in this context?

4. In Table 1, the DCNMT model is reported to have 54M parameters, but the paper describes six RNNs with varying hidden units (1024, 600, 512). Could you provide a detailed parameter count calculation to verify this number?

5. The paper claims that the DCNMT model can translate "convenienter" correctly. However, the example in Table 2(b) shows the source word "convenienter" being translated to "pratique" (which is "practical" in French), not the comparative form of "convenient." Could you clarify the exact translation and whether this is a correct morphological translation?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in neural machine translation (the OOV issue), it suffers from numerous technical inconsistencies, questionable novelty claims, and significant formatting errors. The mathematical formulations are often unclear or incorrect, which makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. The experimental results are presented with inaccuracies, and the claimed advantages over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance with these issues present. The paper would require substantial revisions to correct the mathematical errors, clarify the novelty claim, and improve the experimental presentation before it would be suitable for publication at ACL.
