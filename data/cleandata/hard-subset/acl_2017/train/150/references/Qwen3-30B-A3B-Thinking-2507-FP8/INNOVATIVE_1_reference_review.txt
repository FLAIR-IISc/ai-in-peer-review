

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to neural machine translation that operates at the character level while incorporating morphological structure through a hierarchical architecture. The authors introduce two key components: (1) a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations using two RNNs, and (2) a hierarchical decoder that generates character sequences based on word-level representations.

The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. **A novel character-level translation architecture that explicitly learns morphology** - This represents a bold departure from standard character-level models (which treat characters as independent units) and word-level models (which rely on large vocabularies). The approach fundamentally incorporates linguistic structure into the model.

2. **Demonstrated ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words and misspellings** - The paper provides compelling evidence that their model can correctly translate nonce words and misspelled words, which is a significant practical advantage over standard word-level models.

3. **Meaningful morphological representations** - The paper provides visual evidence (Figures 3-4) that the model learns meaningful morphological relationships between words, which is a significant contribution to understanding how neural models process linguistic structure.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to character-level translation with morphological awareness**: This is the strongest aspect of the paper. The authors present a genuinely novel way to incorporate linguistic structure into character-level translation, moving beyond simple character sequences to learn morphological patterns. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that ACL reviewers value. The architecture (character → subword → word → source sentence → target word → target character) is innovative and addresses a fundamental limitation of both word-level and pure character-level models.

2. **Practical solution to the OOV problem**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, which is impossible for standard word-level models. As shown in Table 2, this is a significant practical advantage with clear real-world applications. This isn't just a theoretical contribution but addresses a genuine pain point in NMT.

3. **Evidence of meaningful morphological learning**: The PCA visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 provide strong evidence that the model learns meaningful morphological representations. The fact that words with similar meanings cluster together in the 2D projection (Figure 3b) versus the random clustering in the standard RNN (Figure 3a) is compelling evidence that the model is actually learning linguistic structure, not just memorizing.

4. **Competitive performance with efficiency**: The paper shows that their model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art models while training more efficiently than some character-level approaches (e.g., 7 days vs. 90 days for Luong and Manning 2016). This efficiency claim, combined with the practical benefits, makes the approach more appealing for real-world deployment.

5. **Clear demonstration of morphological understanding**: The ability to segment words like "consumers" into "consum-er-s" (Figure 5) shows that the model has learned subword units in a linguistically meaningful way, without explicit segmentation. This is a significant contribution to understanding how neural models process linguistic structure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear distinction from related work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from Luong and Manning (2016), who proposed a hybrid word-character model. While the authors claim their approach is different, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their morphology learning approach differs from this prior work. This is a critical weakness for a paper claiming novelty.

2. **Marginal performance improvements**: While the BLEU scores in Table 1 are competitive, the improvements over existing models are marginal (typically 0.5-1 BLEU points). Given the complexity of the model (six RNNs), it's hard to justify the added complexity with such small gains. The paper doesn't provide a clear ablation study showing which components (morphology learning vs. hierarchical decoder) are most responsible for the performance.

3. **Methodological clarity issues**: The description of the HGRU mechanism (Section 3.2) is confusing and doesn't clearly explain how it differs from standard GRUs. The paper mentions "it is possible to use LSTM instead of GRU," which suggests the architecture might not be as novel as claimed. The mathematical formulation (Equations 2-7) is complex without sufficient intuition.

4. **Limited evaluation on morphologically rich languages**: While the paper uses Czech (a morphologically rich language) as a test case, the BLEU improvements aren't dramatic (e.g., 17.89 BLEU for Cs-En vs. 17.5 for the pure character model). The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate that the morphology learning provides a significant advantage for morphologically rich languages.

5. **Lack of comparison to morphological-aware word-level models**: The paper doesn't compare to approaches that use morphological analyzers or linguistic rules to handle morphology at the word level. This would help establish whether their approach offers a superior solution to the OOV problem.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your morphology learning approach specifically differ from the hybrid word-character model proposed by Luong and Manning (2016)? What are the key advantages of your approach over theirs?

2. The paper claims that your model can correctly translate "convenienter" as a comparative adjective form. Could you provide more examples of how this works for morphologically complex words in Czech, which is a highly inflected language?

3. The paper states that "our model is the simplest and smallest one in terms of the model size," but Table 1 shows similar model sizes to other approaches. Can you clarify what specifically makes your model simpler or smaller?

4. The paper mentions that the model learns subword units without explicit segmentation. How does this compare to the subword units learned by BPE or other subword approaches? Do your learned units align with linguistic morphemes?

5. Could you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (morphology learning vs. hierarchical decoder) to the overall performance?

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a novel and bold approach to character-level neural machine translation that incorporates morphological structure in a way that's fundamentally different from existing approaches. While the performance gains are marginal, the practical benefits of handling misspelled words and nonce words, combined with the clear evidence of morphological learning, make this a significant contribution.

The paper's strength lies in its novel conceptual approach to incorporating linguistic structure into neural translation models, which addresses a real problem (OOV) in a way that's both theoretically interesting and practically useful. The reviewer values novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, and this paper delivers on both counts.

The marginal performance improvements are acceptable given the paper's novel contribution to the field, and the practical benefits of the approach outweigh the methodological shortcomings. The authors have provided compelling evidence of meaningful morphological learning, which is a significant contribution to our understanding of how neural models process linguistic structure.

The paper should be accepted as it introduces a genuinely novel direction in neural machine translation that has both theoretical significance and practical applications.
