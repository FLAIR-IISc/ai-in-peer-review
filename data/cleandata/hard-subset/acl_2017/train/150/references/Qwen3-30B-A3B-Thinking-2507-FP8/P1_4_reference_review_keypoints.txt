- Paper proposes DCNMT: a deep character-level NMT that learns morphology via a weighted-character word encoder and a hierarchical character decoder to avoid OOVs.
- Main contribution: a morphological word encoder that learns meaningful word structure through weighted character representations.
- Main contribution: a hierarchical decoder that enables efficient character-level decoding while maintaining training efficiency.
- Main contribution: competitive character-level translation performance that addresses OOV problems.
- Strength: practical OOV handling—translates misspellings and nonce words correctly without external lookups.
- Strength: training efficiency—significantly faster than prior character-level models while achieving comparable BLEU.
- Strength: evidence of learned morphology—PCA visualizations and energy weights indicate meaningful morpheme discovery.
- Strength: competitive BLEU on multiple language pairs, including morphologically rich Czech-English.
- Weakness: novelty unclear—approach resembles prior work (Ling et al. 2015, Lee et al. 2016, Luong & Manning 2016) and needs clearer distinctions.
- Weakness: limited evaluation of morphological benefits—experiments insufficient to show advantages for morphologically rich languages beyond BLEU.
- Weakness: inadequate comparisons to current SOTA (e.g., Transformer) on the same datasets.
- Weakness: insufficient ablation studies to isolate contributions of morphology encoder vs. hierarchical decoder.
- Weakness: limited systematic analysis of morphological learning (e.g., generation of novel morphological forms, complex Czech phenomena).
- Questions for authors: comparisons to recent SOTA (Transformer), more detailed ablations, specific examples of handling complex Czech morphology, and clearer differentiation from Ling et al. (2015).
- Overall: promising, practical approach with strengths outweighing weaknesses but needs clearer novelty positioning, SOTA comparisons, and deeper analyses; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
