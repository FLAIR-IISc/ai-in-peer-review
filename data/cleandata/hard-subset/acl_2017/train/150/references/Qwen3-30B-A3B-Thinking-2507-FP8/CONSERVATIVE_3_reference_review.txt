

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that claims to address out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issues in neural machine translation by learning morphology through a novel architecture. The model features a word encoder that learns morphemes using weighted RNN states and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations. The authors claim their model achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art models while being able to handle misspelled words and nonce words correctly.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A hierarchical architecture incorporating morphology learning for character-level NMT
2. A word encoder that uses weighted RNN states to learn morphemes and their combinations
3. A hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations

## Strengths

1. **Demonstrated ability to handle morphologically complex words**: The paper provides compelling visual evidence (Figure 3) that their word encoder produces more meaningful representations of morphologically related words compared to standard RNN word encoders. The PCA projections show that words with similar meanings (e.g., "notable" and "notability") are closer in the embedding space, suggesting the model learns meaningful morphological structure.

2. **Practical benefit for misspelled/nonce words**: The paper demonstrates that their model can correctly translate misspelled words (Table 2) and nonce words like "convenienter" that weren't present in the training data. This addresses a real-world problem in NMT that word-level models struggle with, as misspelled words would be mapped to <unk> tokens.

3. **Training efficiency claim**: The paper claims their model trains in just 1 epoch (compared to "3 months" for another character-level model), which is potentially significant for practical deployment. While this needs verification, the training efficiency claim is a notable practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal performance improvements with insufficient evidence**: The paper claims "comparable performance" to state-of-the-art, but the BLEU scores in Table 1 show only marginal improvements (e.g., 30.56 BLEU for DCNMT vs. 29.38 for CNMT on En-Fr). The difference is small (1.18 BLEU points) while the model is more complex (6 RNNs vs. 2 for CNMT). The paper fails to demonstrate statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, and crucially, doesn't compare to the current state-of-the-art Transformer-based models that dominate NMT research.

2. **Questionable novelty of core approach**: The paper claims novelty for learning morphology through weighted RNN states, but this bears significant similarity to existing approaches. For example, the hierarchical decoder resembles Luong and Manning (2016), and the word encoder is a variation of CNN-based word encoders (Kim et al., 2016) with less evidence of superiority. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from established methods.

3. **Inadequate baseline comparisons**: The paper compares only to older character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016) and doesn't compare to modern state-of-the-art NMT systems. Given that the paper was submitted after the Transformer architecture became dominant (2017), the absence of Transformer comparisons is a major oversight that undermines the claimed competitiveness of their model.

4. **Misleading claims about training efficiency**: The paper claims "our model is efficient to train as shown in Table 1" and states "we have trained our model after one epoch" (27.02 BLEU) while the other model took "3 months" (27.04 BLEU). This is misleading because the other model was trained for 3 months (likely hundreds of epochs), while the DCNMT model was trained for just one epoch. The paper doesn't provide training time per epoch or total training time comparisons, making the efficiency claim unsubstantiated.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to determine which components are actually responsible for the marginal improvements. For example, it's unclear whether the morphology learning component is truly contributing to performance gains or if the hierarchical decoding is the main factor.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to recent Transformer-based models (e.g., Google's Transformer, BERT-based NMT) that have become the state-of-the-art in NMT? Given that your paper claims to be "competitive with state-of-the-art," it's surprising not to see these comparisons.

2. The paper claims your model is "the simplest and the smallest one in terms of model size" (Table 1), but you have 6 RNNs while other models have fewer. Can you clarify what you mean by "simplest" and "smallest" in this context?

3. The paper mentions that "when we train our model just for one epoch, the obtained result even outperforms the final result with bpe baseline" (Table 1). Is this a general phenomenon, or was it specific to these experiments? How does this compare to training other models for one epoch?

4. The ability to handle "convenienter" (Table 2) is interesting but very specific. How does your model perform on a broader range of morphological transformations that weren't seen during training?

5. The paper claims "the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issue that word-level models suffer from" is solved, but standard NMT systems using BPE or SentencePiece already handle OOV words effectively. How does your model's OOV handling compare to these established approaches?

## Overall Recommendation
I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. While the paper presents some interesting insights about morphology learning and handles misspelled words better than standard word-level models, the evidence for significant performance improvements over established baselines is lacking. The paper's claims about being "competitive with state-of-the-art" are unsubstantiated given the absence of comparison to current dominant architectures (Transformers). The marginal BLEU improvements (1-2 points) don't justify the increased model complexity. As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation against established baselines, I find the paper's claims to be overstated without sufficient evidence. The paper would need substantial additional experiments comparing to modern state-of-the-art models and providing clearer evidence of the value of the proposed approach before it could be considered for publication.
