

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation" (DCNMT) model that claims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in neural machine translation by learning morphology through a novel word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The authors argue that their approach is superior to both word-level and other character-level models by achieving competitive BLEU scores while handling OOV cases better. The paper presents a character-level architecture that processes words through morpheme-aware encoding and character-level decoding.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A minor architectural variation on character-level NMT that uses two RNNs to learn morphemes and a hierarchical decoder
2. Limited evidence that the model can handle misspelled words better than standard word-based models
3. Marginal BLEU score improvements over existing character-level models

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem statement**: The paper correctly identifies the OOV problem in word-level NMT as a significant limitation, and character-level approaches as a potential solution. This is a well-established problem in the field.

2. **Novel word encoder architecture**: The use of two RNNs to learn morphology (with weights assigned to character sequences) is a creative approach to character-level modeling. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 does suggest that the model learns meaningful representations of morphemes.

3. **Demonstration of handling misspelled words**: The examples in Table 2 do show that the model can handle misspelled words better than standard word-based models, which is a practical advantage that could be useful in real-world applications with noisy input.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of significant empirical improvement**: The paper claims to achieve competitive results, but the BLEU scores (Table 1) show only marginal improvements over existing methods. For example, on En-Fr, the DCNMT model scores 30.56 BLEU after 29 epochs, while Luong & Manning (2016) achieved 30.0 BLEU after 3 months of training (with a 0.5 BLEU advantage over DCNMT). This suggests that the paper's claimed advantages are not statistically significant or practically meaningful.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with a limited set of baselines (primarily BPE-based models and a few character-level models), but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., transformer-based models). The paper was published in 2016, but the baselines don't include models like Google's GNMT or other recent architectures that have since become the new standard.

3. **Lack of thorough ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the proposed architecture. For example, it's unclear whether the two RNNs for the word encoder provide significant benefits over simpler character-level models or whether the hierarchical decoder is essential.

4. **Questionable training efficiency claims**: The paper claims "our model is efficient to train" based on training time comparisons, but the training time for Luong & Manning (2016) was "3 months" while DCNMT took "1 day" (Table 1). However, the paper doesn't provide details about the hardware used for these comparisons, making the claim questionable. It's possible that the Luong & Manning model was trained on less powerful hardware.

5. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims to "learn morphology" but doesn't provide compelling evidence that this is a fundamentally new capability. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 shows that the representations are somewhat better organized, but this isn't a major breakthrough. Many existing character-level models implicitly learn morphology through their character processing.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to learn morphology, but it's unclear how this differs from what character-level models already implicitly learn. Can the authors provide evidence that their approach is fundamentally different from standard character-level models in terms of how morphology is learned?

2. The paper states that "our model is efficient to train" but the training time comparisons are not properly controlled (e.g., hardware differences not specified). Can the authors provide more details about the training environment (GPU, CPU, memory) for all compared models?

3. The paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., transformer-based models). How would the authors' model perform against these newer architectures?

4. The paper claims to handle misspelled words better, but it's unclear how this translates to real-world performance. Can the authors provide more examples of misspelled words that are handled better than in standard word-level models?

## Overall Recommendation
Reject

## Justification
As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find this paper to be a minor variation on existing character-level NMT approaches with limited empirical evidence of significant improvement over established methods. The paper makes claims about learning morphology and handling OOV cases that are not substantially better than what character-level models already achieve.

The empirical results show only marginal improvements (0.5 BLEU points at most), and the paper doesn't adequately demonstrate why their approach is superior to existing character-level methods. The field has since moved toward more powerful architectures (like transformers) that have largely superseded the need for these character-level approaches.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a slightly different way of structuring a character-level NMT model, but this doesn't represent a significant advance in the field. The claimed advantages are not sufficiently validated against appropriate baselines, and the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method provides meaningful benefits over existing approaches.

I recommend rejection as the paper does not provide sufficient evidence of a meaningful contribution to the field of neural machine translation.
