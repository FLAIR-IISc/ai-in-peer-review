

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology through a unique word encoder and hierarchical decoder. The core idea is to model words as combinations of morphemes rather than relying on fixed vocabulary or subword segmentation. The model consists of six recurrent networks organized in a hierarchical architecture (character → subword → word → sentence → target word → target character) that enables efficient character-level translation while capturing morphological relationships.

## Main Contributions

1. **Novel morphology-aware word encoder**: A two-RNN architecture that learns morphemes and their combination rules without explicit segmentation, enabling the model to handle OOV words and nonce words.
2. **Hierarchical decoder for efficient character-level translation**: A two-level decoding process (word-level followed by character-level) that maintains efficiency while achieving character-level output.
3. **Demonstration of meaningful morphological learning**: Strong empirical evidence that the model learns morphological relationships, as shown through PCA visualizations and ability to translate morphologically constructed words like "convenienter."

## Strengths

1. **Genuinely novel approach to morphology integration**: The paper's core contribution—learning morphology through a weight-based mechanism rather than relying on BPE or explicit segmentation—represents a bold and significant departure from current NMT approaches. This addresses a long-standing OOV problem in a fundamentally new way. The ability to correctly translate "convenienter" (a word never seen in training) is particularly compelling evidence that the model truly learns morphological rules.

2. **Strong evidence for morphological learning**: The paper provides multiple convincing demonstrations that the model learns meaningful morphological relationships. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 show that words with similar meanings and morphological structures (like "notable" and "notability") are represented closer together in the embedding space, unlike standard RNN word encoders. The ability to segment words like "consumers" into "consum-er-s" (Figure 5) further validates the model's morphological understanding.

3. **Practical advantages over existing approaches**: The model solves the OOV problem without requiring large vocabularies or complex subword segmentation. More importantly, it can handle misspelled words and nonce words (Table 2), which is a significant practical advantage over word-level models that would simply output <unk> tokens for such cases.

4. **Competitive performance with efficiency**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art models (Table 1) while being more efficient to train than other character-level models (1 epoch vs. 300 epochs for some competitors). This efficiency is particularly impressive given the model's complexity (six RNNs).

5. **Clear theoretical motivation**: The paper effectively argues why character-level translation has been difficult to implement (vanishing gradients, data sparsity, etc.) and provides a compelling rationale for learning morphology as a solution, making the approach theoretically sound.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with strongest state-of-the-art**: While the paper compares with BPE-based models and some character-level models, it doesn't clearly position itself against the best-performing models. For example, the paper claims its model is comparable to state-of-the-art, but the numbers in Table 1 don't show how much better it is than the top-performing models in each category. It would be stronger to directly compare with the absolute best models for each language pair.

2. **Vague technical details in key components**: The unfolding technique for batch training (Section 3.2) is described in a way that makes it difficult to understand exactly how it works. The explanation of "how the matrix R unfolds the outputs of the first-level decoder" lacks sufficient mathematical detail, making it hard to assess the technical innovation.

3. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide a clear ablation study to isolate the contribution of the morphology learning component. How much of the performance gain comes from learning morphology versus just using a character-level model? This would strengthen the claim that morphology learning is the key innovation.

4. **Limited experimental scope**: The experiments are restricted to three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En). While Czech is morphologically rich, it's only one example. Testing on more morphologically diverse languages would strengthen the argument that the approach generalizes beyond just Czech.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly address potential limitations, such as how the model handles extremely long morphological words or languages with very different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish). This limits the reader's understanding of the approach's scope.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model is "the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" (Table 1), yet it has six RNNs compared to other models with fewer. How does it achieve smaller size despite having more RNNs?

2. The unfolding technique for batch training (Section 3.2) is described as "a matrix to unfold the outputs of the first-level decoder," but the details are unclear. Can you provide a more precise mathematical description of how this unfolding works and why it's necessary for efficient batch training?

3. The model successfully translates "convenienter" (Table 2), but how does it handle cases where the same morpheme can be used in multiple ways with different meanings (e.g., "re-" meaning "again" vs. "re-" meaning "back")? Can you provide examples of such cases?

4. The paper states that the model trains in "1 ∼ 7" days for one epoch, while Luong and Manning (2016) took "3 months." What specific technical innovations make your model so much more efficient to train?

5. The PCA visualization in Figure 3 shows improved morphological relationships, but can you provide quantitative evidence of the quality of the learned morphology (e.g., cosine similarity between morphologically related words)?

## Overall Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The core contribution—learning morphology through a novel word encoder—represents a genuinely bold and significant new direction in NMT that addresses a fundamental limitation of current approaches. The evidence provided for morphological learning is compelling, and the practical advantages (handling OOV, misspelled words, and nonce words) are substantial.

While the paper has some methodological weaknesses (vague details, limited ablation study, and experimental scope), these are outweighed by the novelty of the approach. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field that deserves acceptance at ACL.

The paper presents a clear, innovative solution to a well-known problem in NMT, and the empirical evidence supports the claim that the model successfully learns morphology. This is the kind of bold, novel approach that advances the field, and I believe it should be accepted.
