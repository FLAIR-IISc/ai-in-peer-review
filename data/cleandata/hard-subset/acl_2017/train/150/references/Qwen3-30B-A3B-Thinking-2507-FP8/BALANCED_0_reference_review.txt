

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level NMT models. The authors introduce a novel architecture with two key components: (1) a word encoder that learns morphology by using two RNNs to represent words as combinations of morphemes, and (2) a hierarchical decoder that decodes at the character level while maintaining efficiency. The model is designed to handle OOV words and nonce words (like "convenienter") that would be problematic for word-level models. The authors claim their model achieves competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art models while being more efficient to train.

## Main Contributions
1. **Morphology-aware word encoder**: A novel approach that learns word representations by implicitly segmenting words into morphemes using two RNNs, rather than relying on a fixed vocabulary.
2. **Hierarchical decoder architecture**: A character-level decoder that uses word-level information as a controller, enabling efficient training while maintaining the ability to generate character sequences.
3. **Handling OOV words and nonce words**: Demonstrated ability to correctly translate misspelled words and morphologically derived words not seen in training, a significant practical advantage.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to morphology learning**: The paper presents a genuinely novel way to incorporate morphological information into NMT. The word encoder's ability to learn meaningful representations of words based on morphemes (as shown in Figure 3) is a significant contribution. This addresses a key limitation of traditional word-level models that struggle with OOV words and morphological variations.

2. **Strong practical demonstration of OOV handling**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter" in Table 2) correctly, while word-level models would fail. This is a valuable practical contribution that addresses a real-world problem in translation systems.

3. **Efficient training compared to prior character-level models**: The authors demonstrate that their model trains significantly faster than other character-level approaches (e.g., Luong and Manning 2016's model took 3 months, while DCNMT took about 7 days). This efficiency is a meaningful practical contribution that makes character-level translation more feasible.

4. **Competitive performance**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art models on multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), demonstrating that character-level translation can be competitive with word-level approaches while solving the OOV problem.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear distinction from prior work**: The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing character-level models like Ling et al. (2015b) and Lee et al. (2016), which also claim to handle morphology implicitly. The authors need to provide a more thorough comparison to establish the novelty of their specific architecture.

2. **Limited evidence for the claimed benefits of morphology learning**: While the paper claims that learning morphology "speeds up training" (Table 1), the evidence is limited. The authors show that their model achieves better results after one epoch than the bpe baseline after many epochs, but they don't provide a thorough analysis of why this happens. More importantly, they don't compare against a model that explicitly uses morphological information (e.g., a model that takes morphological analysis as input).

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks detailed ablation studies to show how much each component contributes to the overall performance. For example, it doesn't demonstrate how much the morphology learning component contributes versus the hierarchical decoder. Without this, it's hard to assess which aspects of their approach are truly novel and valuable.

4. **Limited comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with some character-level models but doesn't include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., Gu et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017). This weakens the claim that their model is competitive with the "state-of-the-art."

5. **Weak theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for the specific design choices (e.g., why two RNNs for word encoding, why the hierarchical decoder structure). The motivation for the architecture is somewhat ad-hoc, which makes it difficult to evaluate the necessity of their specific approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically differ from the "Character-based neural machine translation" (Ling et al., 2015b) and "Fully character-level neural machine translation without explicit segmentation" (Lee et al., 2016) models? The authors of those works also claim to handle morphology implicitly. Can you provide a detailed comparison of your approach with these prior works?

2. The paper claims "our model is able to learn the morphology," but doesn't provide a quantitative measure of how well the model learns morphology. How do you measure the quality of the morphology learning? Could you add a quantitative evaluation of the morphology learning component?

3. The paper states that training for one epoch with DCNMT outperforms the bpe baseline after many epochs (Table 1), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this happens. Is this primarily due to the morphology learning component, or are there other factors at play? Could you provide more detailed analysis of this phenomenon?

4. The paper doesn't include a comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art character-level models (e.g., Gu et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017). Could you add these comparisons to strengthen the claim that your model is competitive with the state-of-the-art?

## Recommendation

**Decision: Weak Reject**

While the paper presents a novel approach to incorporating morphology into NMT and demonstrates practical benefits in handling OOV words, it has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being accepted at ACL. The lack of clear distinction from prior work, limited evidence for the claimed benefits of morphology learning, and insufficient ablation studies undermine the paper's contribution claims. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with prior work, additional ablation studies, and a more rigorous analysis of why their approach works better than alternatives.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Conduct a more thorough comparison with prior character-level models, especially Ling et al. (2015b) and Lee et al. (2016), to clearly establish novelty.
2. Add ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (morphology learning vs. hierarchical decoder).
3. Provide more detailed analysis of why their model trains faster and achieves better results with fewer epochs.
4. Include comparisons with the most recent state-of-the-art models.
5. Add a quantitative measure of the morphology learning quality to strengthen the claim that the model "learns morphology."

With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field of neural machine translation.
