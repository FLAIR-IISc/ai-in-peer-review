

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel deep character-level neural machine translation model that learns morphology through a specialized word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The key innovation is a two-RNN word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations by assigning attention weights to character-level representations, and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level states. The authors claim this approach solves the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, handles misspelled words and nonce words better than word-level models, and achieves competitive BLEU scores.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel approach to learning morphology in NMT**: The word encoder that uses attention over character-level representations to identify morphemes is a bold conceptual contribution that differs from standard word-level or subword-level approaches.
2. **Hierarchical decoder architecture**: The two-level decoding approach (word-level state guiding character-level generation) is a novel architectural innovation that addresses efficiency concerns with pure character-level models.
3. **Demonstrated OOV handling capability**: The practical ability to translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly is a compelling real-world advantage over standard word-level models.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Core Concept**: The paper's central idea of learning morphology through attention over character-level representations is a genuinely bold and novel approach to the OOV problem. Unlike BPE-based approaches that segment words in a fixed way, this method learns meaningful morphological boundaries automatically. This represents a significant conceptual shift from standard NMT approaches and could inspire new directions in the field.

2. **Compelling Practical Advantage**: The ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") is a strong practical demonstration of the model's utility. The examples in Table 2 clearly show that the model can translate words not seen during training, which is impossible for standard word-level models. This is a real-world problem that has practical implications for NMT systems in the wild.

3. **Competitive Performance with Efficiency**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models while being more efficient to train than some prior approaches (e.g., ~7 days vs. 3 months for Luong and Manning's model). The efficiency gains are particularly notable given the complexity of the architecture (six RNNs).

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Prior Work**: The paper doesn't clearly articulate how its approach differs from existing character-level and morphological modeling work. The Ling et al. (2015) C2W model and Lee et al. (2016) character-based model both use similar character-to-word concepts, and the paper doesn't adequately explain the key distinctions. The claim that their approach "learns the subword unit automatically" is misleading since BPE (Sennrich et al., 2016) also automatically learns subword units, just through a different method.

2. **Superficial Evidence for "Morphology Learning"**: The paper claims to learn morphology, but the evidence (PCA visualizations in Figure 3) only shows that words with similar endings have similar embeddings, which is a common property of many word embeddings. There's no concrete evidence that the model has learned morphological rules (e.g., that "convenient" + "er" = "convenienter") rather than simply capturing statistical correlations.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder. The comparison with CNMT (which uses a regular RNN for word encoding) is a start, but more detailed ablation would strengthen the claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your word encoder that learns morphology fundamentally differ from Ling et al. (2015) and Lee et al. (2016), who also used character-level encoders to build word representations? What specific morphological rules does your model learn that theirs doesn't?

2. Can you provide more concrete evidence that your model has learned morphological rules (beyond showing similar words have similar embeddings)? For example, can you demonstrate that your model correctly infers the meaning of "convenienter" based on the morphemes it learned?

3. Your model achieves better BLEU scores than Luong and Manning (2016) despite having a more complex architecture (six RNNs vs. their two-level model). What specific architectural components or training techniques are responsible for this improvement?

4. How much of the performance gain comes from the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder? Can you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance for this paper. While the methodological presentation has some weaknesses, the core idea of learning morphology through a two-RNN word encoder is sufficiently novel and bold to justify acceptance under ACL's criteria. The practical advantage of handling OOV words and nonce words is compelling, and the competitive performance demonstrates the approach's viability.

The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to NMT by addressing the OOV problem in a fundamentally different way from existing approaches. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper meets the threshold for acceptance despite some limitations in the evaluation. The potential impact of this approach on future NMT research and applications is substantial.

The authors should address the questions above in their response to strengthen the paper's claims about novelty and the nature of the morphology learning.
