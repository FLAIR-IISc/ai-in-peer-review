

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel architecture for character-level neural machine translation that incorporates learned morphology to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem. The main contributions are:

1. **Morphology-aware character-level translation**: A novel approach to character-level NMT that learns morphological structure rather than relying on subword units like BPE. This is achieved through a word encoder that uses two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combinations.

2. **Hierarchical decoder for efficient character-level translation**: A two-level decoder architecture that first predicts target words at a higher level and then decodes those words into characters, enabling efficient character-level translation.

3. **Demonstrated learning of morphological structure**: Strong evidence that the model actually learns meaningful morphological relationships, as shown through PCA projections of word representations and analysis of the energy weights for morpheme boundaries.

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual approach to character-level NMT**: The paper introduces a fundamentally different approach to character-level translation by learning morphology rather than using subword units. This is a bold and novel idea that addresses a core limitation of character-level NMT (performance) while solving the OOV problem. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art models while being able to handle words not seen during training.

2. **Strong evidence of morphology learning**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the model actually learns meaningful morphological structure, not just a technical workaround. The PCA projections in Figure 3 show that words with similar morphological structures cluster together, and the analysis of energy weights in Figure 4 demonstrates that the model identifies morpheme boundaries. This is a significant contribution beyond just another NMT model.

3. **Practical advantage for handling OOV and novel words**: The paper demonstrates the model's ability to correctly translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter"), which is a clear practical advantage over standard word-level models. This is not just a theoretical benefit but has real-world implications for translation systems.

4. **Competitive performance with efficient training**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art character-level models while being more efficient to train than some alternatives (e.g., the Luong and Manning 2016 model that took 3 months to train). The model size is also relatively small compared to other character-level approaches.

5. **Potential for broader application**: The authors suggest that the core idea could be applied to other tasks like speech recognition and text summarization, which increases the significance of the contribution beyond just NMT.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison to the current state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to some character-level models and BPE-based models, but the most relevant baseline is the current SOTA, which is typically a BPE-based model. The paper claims their model is "comparable to the state-of-the-art," but the baselines they use (like BPE2BPE) may not represent the strongest possible comparison. The paper should directly compare to the most recent BPE-based models that achieve the highest BLEU scores on these tasks.

2. **Lack of thorough ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide a clear ablation study to show how much the morphology learning component contributes to the performance. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the key novelty (morphology learning) is actually driving the improvements or if the model would perform similarly with just a standard character-level approach.

3. **Limited analysis of when the approach provides the most benefit**: The paper shows results for English-French (morphologically poor) and English-Czech (morphologically rich) pairs, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of when this approach is most effective. The authors should explore whether the benefits are primarily for morphologically rich languages or if there are other language properties that make this approach particularly advantageous.

4. **Questionable efficiency claims**: While the paper claims their model is "efficient to train," the comparison to the Luong and Manning 2016 model (which took 3 months to train) lacks important details about hardware specifications and training epochs, making it difficult to verify this claim.

5. **Insufficient analysis of model generalization**: The paper shows one example of translating "convenienter" correctly, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how well the model generalizes to morphologically novel structures. How often does it successfully translate morphologically novel words, and under what conditions does it fail?

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model achieves "comparable performance to the state-of-the-art," but the baselines used (BPE2BPE) are not the current SOTA. What is the performance of the best BPE-based model on these tasks, and how does your model compare to it?

2. The paper shows that the model can translate "convenienter" correctly, but this is a single example. How often does the model successfully translate morphologically novel words that it hasn't seen before? Can you provide a systematic analysis of the model's generalization to unseen morphological structures?

3. The paper mentions that the model uses "six recurrent neural networks" but doesn't explain why six is optimal. Can you provide more insight into the model's architecture choices and whether more layers would improve performance?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to character-level neural machine translation that addresses a fundamental limitation of current NMT systems (the OOV problem) by incorporating learned morphology. The core idea is significant and potentially impactful, and the authors provide strong evidence that their model actually learns meaningful morphological structure.

While the paper has some methodological weaknesses (particularly regarding the comparison to the current SOTA and the lack of a thorough ablation study), I believe the novelty and conceptual strength of the approach outweigh these concerns. The paper introduces a fundamentally different way of thinking about character-level translation that could potentially influence the field.

Given my review criteria (prioritizing novel ideas over methodological perfection), I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The core contribution of learning morphology for character-level translation is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance, even with the minor methodological issues that could be addressed in the camera-ready version or through author response.

This paper represents a significant step toward making character-level translation practical and effective, which is a major challenge in the field. The authors have provided compelling evidence that their approach works in practice, not just theoretically, which is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that ACL values.
