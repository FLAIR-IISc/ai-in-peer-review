

# Peer Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary
This paper proposes a character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that explicitly learns word morphology to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem. The approach uses a novel word encoder with two RNNs to identify morphemes through attention weights, and a hierarchical decoder that first predicts words at the word level before generating characters for each word. The authors claim their model solves the OOV issue, handles misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter"), and achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art models.

## Main Contributions
1. A word encoder that learns morphemes implicitly through attention over character sequences, rather than relying on pre-defined subword units (e.g., BPE)
2. A hierarchical decoder enabling character-level generation while maintaining efficient training
3. Demonstration that character-level models can learn meaningful word representations capturing morphological relationships

## Strengths

**1. Addressing a genuine NMT challenge with a principled approach.** The OOV problem is a well-known limitation of word-level NMT, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The paper's approach of learning morphology rather than using heuristics like BPE is theoretically sound and addresses a practical limitation of current systems. The ability to correctly translate "convenienter" (a nonce word formed by adding "er" to "convenient") demonstrates a valuable capability that word-level models fundamentally lack.

**2. Compelling empirical evidence for morphological learning.** The PCA visualization (Figure 3) clearly shows that their word encoder produces more meaningful word representations than standard RNN encoders, with morphologically related words clustered together. This analysis provides strong evidence that the model is learning actual linguistic structure, not just statistical patterns.

**3. Practical advantage for handling misspelled words.** The examples in Table 2 (e.g., translating "howeve" as "cependant" instead of <unk>) demonstrate a significant real-world advantage over standard word-level models. This is particularly valuable for applications where input text may contain typos or non-standard spellings.

## Weaknesses

**1. Questionable novelty compared to prior work.** The core idea of using attention over characters to identify morphemes is very similar to Ling et al. (2015) and Lee et al. (2016), which the authors cite but don't sufficiently distinguish from. The "word encoder" is essentially a character-level attention mechanism with a different interpretation. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from these prior works in a way that justifies a new paper.

**2. Marginal performance gains with no clear advantage over BPE.** The BLEU scores (Table 1) show only minor improvements over baselines, with the model often performing slightly worse than state-of-the-art BPE-based models. For example, on En-Fr, the paper's model achieves 30.56 BLEU compared to 30.71 for the best baseline. The paper fails to convincingly argue why this marginal improvement justifies the more complex architecture.

**3. Training efficiency claims are overstated.** While the authors claim their model is "the simplest and smallest" (54M parameters), this is comparable to Lee et al. (2016) at 69M parameters. The training time (5 days) is also not significantly better than other approaches (e.g., Luong and Manning (2016) took 3 months but achieved +0.5 BLEU). The paper doesn't provide a clear efficiency advantage that would justify the architectural complexity.

**4. Lack of deeper analysis on why the approach works.** The paper shows that the model can learn morphology but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how it does so. For instance, the energy weights in Figure 4 are interesting, but the paper doesn't explain how these weights correspond to actual morphological rules or how they generalize beyond the examples shown.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your word encoder specifically differ from the "compositional character-to-word" (C2W) model in Ling et al. (2015)? The abstract mentions C2W but doesn't clarify the distinction.

2. The paper claims "the model achieves a high translation performance which is comparable to the state-of-the-art," but Table 1 shows the model actually performs slightly worse than some baselines (e.g., 30.56 BLEU vs. 30.71 for the best En-Fr baseline). Can you clarify this discrepancy?

3. The paper states the model can translate "convenienter" correctly, but doesn't explain the specific morphological rules it learned. Could you elaborate on how the model processes this nonce word?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a modest contribution to NMT by presenting a character-level model that learns morphology. While the approach is theoretically sound and the empirical results on misspelled words are compelling, the novelty is limited, the performance gains are marginal, and the training efficiency claims are overstated compared to existing work. The paper would be stronger with clearer differentiation from prior work, more convincing evidence of superiority over BPE, and deeper analysis of the morphological learning process.

The paper should be accepted, but it's a marginal acceptance that would benefit from clearer articulation of its unique contributions and more rigorous comparison with the state-of-the-art. The practical advantage for handling misspelled words is valuable, but the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this model is better than simply using BPE with a larger vocabulary.
