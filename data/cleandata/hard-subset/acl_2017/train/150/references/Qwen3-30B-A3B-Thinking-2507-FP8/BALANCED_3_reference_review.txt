

# ACL Review: Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (DCNMT) model that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology implicitly through a novel word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. Instead of using a large vocabulary or explicit subword segmentation (like BPE), the model learns morphemes and their combination rules through a two-RNN architecture. The model achieves competitive translation performance while being more efficient to train than existing character-level approaches. The authors demonstrate the model's ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words, which word-level models cannot handle.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **A novel word encoder that learns morphology through a two-RNN architecture** - This is the primary contribution, as it enables the model to implicitly learn subword units (morphemes) without explicit segmentation.
2. **A hierarchical decoder that enables efficient character-level generation** - The second-level decoder (HGRU) generates characters based on word-level representations, maintaining efficiency while allowing character-level translation.
3. **Demonstration of meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships** - The paper provides evidence that the model learns representations where words with similar meanings and morphological structures are closer in the embedding space.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to morphology learning with meaningful word representations**: The paper's most compelling contribution is the word encoder that learns morphology implicitly through two RNNs. The PCA projections in Figure 3 provide strong evidence that the model learns meaningful representations where morphologically related words cluster together (e.g., "notable" and "notability"), unlike the ordinary RNN encoder. This addresses a key challenge in NMT by showing how character-level models can achieve word-level semantic relationships. The analysis in Section 5.2 is thorough and convincing, making a strong case for the value of learning morphology.

2. **Practical advantage in handling OOV and nonce words**: The experiments in Table 2 demonstrate a significant practical advantage over word-level models. The model correctly translates misspelled words (e.g., "howeve" → "cependant") and nonce words like "convenienter" that were never seen during training. This is a genuine contribution to real-world NMT applications where spelling errors and novel words are common. The ability to handle these cases without requiring a large vocabulary is a valuable practical benefit.

3. **Efficiency and competitive performance**: The model achieves comparable BLEU scores to state-of-the-art models (Table 1) while being significantly more efficient to train (1 day vs. 3 months for Luong and Manning 2016). This efficiency is impressive given the model's complexity (six RNNs) and demonstrates that the authors have successfully addressed a key challenge in character-level NMT (training efficiency).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear differentiation from prior work**: The paper's approach is too similar to existing work without sufficient differentiation. The hierarchical architecture resembles Ling et al. (2015) and Lee et al. (2016), and the word encoder is similar to CNN-based word encoders. The authors cite these works but fail to clearly articulate how their approach is novel and superior. For example, they don't explain why their RNN-based word encoder is better than the CNN-based approaches they reference, or how their hierarchical decoder differs from the C2W model. This lack of clear novelty weakens the contribution claim.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component. For instance, there's no comparison between using the morphology learning word encoder versus a standard RNN word encoder. Without these studies, it's impossible to determine how much of the performance gain is due to the morphology learning component versus the hierarchical decoder. The paper claims "our model is able to learn the morphology," but without ablation studies, it's unclear how much the morphology learning actually contributes to the performance.

3. **Superficial analysis of morphology learning**: While the paper claims the model learns morphology, the analysis in Section 5.2 is somewhat superficial. The PCA projections and examples (Figures 3-5) are suggestive but don't provide systematic evidence of morphology learning. For instance, they don't evaluate the model on a separate morphological task (e.g., morphological similarity or word inflection) or show how the learned morphemes correspond to actual linguistic morphemes. A more rigorous evaluation would strengthen the claim that the model truly learns morphology.

4. **Limited baseline comparison**: The comparison against state-of-the-art models is limited, particularly to BPE-based approaches. The paper compares against a few character-level models but doesn't provide a thorough comparison against modern BPE-based models (e.g., Sennrich et al. 2016), which are the dominant approach in the field. Without showing how DCNMT compares to these more common approaches, it's hard to assess the practical significance of the proposed method.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your word encoder differ from the C2W model by Ling et al. (2015), and why is your approach better for learning morphology? The paper cites Ling et al. but doesn't clearly distinguish the two approaches.

2. Can you provide ablation studies to show the contribution of the morphology learning component (the two-RNN word encoder) versus a standard RNN word encoder? This would help assess the true value of the proposed architecture.

3. Have you tried using your word encoder as a standalone component for other NLP tasks that require morphological understanding (e.g., POS tagging, morphological analysis)? This would further demonstrate the value of the morphology learning.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents an interesting approach to character-level NMT with a novel word encoder that learns morphology implicitly. The demonstration of meaningful word representations and the ability to handle OOV and nonce words are strong points. However, the lack of clear novelty compared to prior work, insufficient ablation studies, and superficial analysis of the morphology learning claim prevent this from being a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak reject**. The paper has some valuable insights, but the claims need to be strengthened with clearer differentiation from prior work, more thorough ablation studies, and a more rigorous evaluation of the morphology learning capability. With these improvements, the paper could make a significant contribution to the field.

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the word encoder's morpheme learning relates to linguistic morphology (e.g., how the learned morphemes correspond to actual linguistic morphemes).
- The training efficiency claim (1 day vs. 3 months) is impressive, but the paper should clarify whether this is for a single GPU or distributed training, as this affects the practical significance.
- The supplementary material (mentioned in the paper) should contain additional examples and analyses to support the claims about morphology learning.
