

# Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel neural machine translation (NMT) model called Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation (DCNMT) that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in traditional word-level NMT. The authors introduce a word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through a dual-RNN architecture, and a hierarchical decoder that generates characters based on word-level representations. The model claims to achieve comparable performance to state-of-the-art word-level models while eliminating the need for a large vocabulary.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations through a dual-RNN architecture
2. A hierarchical decoder that enables character-level translation with efficient training
3. Empirical demonstration that character-level translation with morphology learning achieves competitive performance and handles OOV words

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a genuine problem**: The paper correctly identifies the OOV issue as a critical challenge in NMT, and the proposed solution is theoretically sound. The authors effectively demonstrate that their model can translate misspelled words and nonce words (Table 2), which is a significant practical advantage over standard word-level models.

2. **Strong evidence of morphology learning**: The paper provides compelling visual evidence (Figures 3-5) that the word encoder actually learns meaningful morphological representations. The PCA projections clearly show that words with similar morphological structures are grouped together, validating the core claim of the paper.

3. **Efficiency advantage**: The paper demonstrates that their model trains significantly faster than comparable character-level models (7 days vs. 90 days for Luong and Manning, 2016), which is a substantial practical advantage. The hierarchical decoder design (HGRU) enables efficient training without the need for multiple forward passes.

4. **Competitive results**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art models (Table 1), particularly on morphologically rich languages (En-Cs, Cs-En), where the benefits of morphology learning are most apparent.

5. **Clear technical explanation**: The paper provides detailed technical explanations of the word encoder and hierarchical decoder architectures, making the approach understandable and reproducible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: The paper primarily compares to models from 2015-2016 (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2015; Luong and Manning, 2016), but fails to compare with more recent Transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017), which have become the new standard in NMT. This significantly weakens the claim that the model is "competitive with state-of-the-art."

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper only evaluates on three language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En), which is insufficient for a comprehensive evaluation of a new NMT architecture. The authors should have included more language pairs or conducted ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component.

3. **Overstated novelty**: While the approach has merit, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from previous hybrid word-character models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016). The word encoder's mechanism for learning morphology bears similarities to existing work, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this approach is fundamentally different.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper relies heavily on empirical results without providing a strong theoretical foundation for why the morphological learning approach should work better than other character-level models. The connection between morphology learning and translation performance could be better justified.

5. **Inconsistent training efficiency claims**: The paper claims their model is "the simplest and smallest one in terms of model size" (Table 1), but the model actually has six RNNs, which is more complex than many baselines. The training efficiency claim needs to be qualified with respect to the model's complexity.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to recent Transformer-based architectures, which have become the new state-of-the-art in NMT? Have you attempted to incorporate your word encoder into a Transformer framework?

2. Your model achieves better BLEU scores than the bpe2bpe baseline after one epoch but worse after more training (Table 1). Could you explain this behavior and whether it indicates a potential overfitting issue?

3. The paper claims your model can handle misspelled words by "guessing" the meaning based on morphology. How does this mechanism differ from the simple character-level translation that could be achieved without the morphological learning component?

4. In Figure 4, you show the energy of preceding characters. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the "energy" values are calculated and why they correlate with morpheme boundaries?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to character-level NMT that effectively addresses the OOV problem through morphological learning. The evidence for morphology learning is strong, and the model demonstrates competitive performance with efficient training. However, the lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art models and the limited evaluation scope significantly weaken the paper's contribution.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of NMT, particularly for morphologically rich languages. While the novelty claims could be better justified, the technical approach is sound and the results are convincing. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent work and additional evaluation on more language pairs.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the comparison to recent Transformer-based models and to provide a more nuanced discussion of the novelty of the approach. The technical content is strong, and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on NMT.
