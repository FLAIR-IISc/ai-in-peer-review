- Summary: Proposes a character-level NMT with a dual-RNN word encoder (attention over characters) and a hierarchical decoder to learn morphology and handle OOVs, claiming comparable BLEU and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

- Contribution 1: A word encoder that implicitly learns morphemes via attention over character sequences instead of pre-defined subword units.

- Contribution 2: A hierarchical decoder that predicts words at the word level then generates characters, enabling character-level generation with (claimed) efficient training.

- Contribution 3: Empirical demonstration that character-level models can learn meaningful word representations capturing morphological relationships.

- Strength 1: Addresses the OOV problem and can translate nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"), a capability word-level models lack.

- Strength 2: PCA visualization shows morphologically related words cluster, supporting the claim of learned linguistic structure.

- Strength 3: Practical advantage for handling misspellings, producing translations instead of <unk> in examples.

- Weakness 1: Novelty is questionable—approach is very similar to Ling et al. (2015) and Lee et al. (2016) and not clearly differentiated.

- Weakness 2: Marginal BLEU improvements and sometimes worse than BPE-based baselines, undermining claims of superiority.

- Weakness 3: Training-efficiency and model-size claims are overstated; parameter counts and training time are comparable to prior work.

- Weakness 4: Lacks deeper analysis of how the model learns morphology or how attention/energy weights correspond to linguistic rules and generalize.

- Question 1: How does the word encoder concretely differ from the C2W/compositional character-to-word model in Ling et al. (2015)?

- Question 2: Can the authors clarify the claim of comparable state-of-the-art performance given slight BLEU deficits (e.g., 30.56 vs 30.71 on En–Fr)?

- Question 3: What specific morphological rules or processing allow the model to translate nonce words like "convenienter" correctly?

- Overall assessment: Modest but valuable contribution—theoretically sound and useful for misspellings, but limited novelty, marginal empirical gains, and insufficient analysis; recommend marginal acceptance with clearer differentiation and deeper evaluation.
