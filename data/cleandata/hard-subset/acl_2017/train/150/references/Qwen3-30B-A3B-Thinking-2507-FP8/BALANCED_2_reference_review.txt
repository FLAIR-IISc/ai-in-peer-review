

# Review for "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that addresses the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem by learning morphology implicitly. The authors introduce a novel word encoder that uses two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combinations, and a hierarchical decoder that generates text at the character level. The main contributions I identify are:

1. **Novel word encoder for morphology learning**: A two-RNN architecture that implicitly learns morphemes and their combinations without requiring explicit segmentation, producing meaningful word representations (evidenced by PCA visualizations in Figure 3).
2. **Efficient hierarchical decoder**: A modified GRU (HGRU) that enables efficient character-level generation without requiring two separate forward passes, making the model more practical to train than previous character-level approaches.
3. **Ability to handle OOV and nonce words**: Demonstrated through experiments that the model can correctly translate misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") that word-level models would fail to handle.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to morphology learning**: The word encoder architecture is genuinely interesting and addresses a fundamental problem in NMT. The paper provides compelling evidence (Figures 3-5) that the model learns meaningful morphological representations, where words with similar meanings cluster together in the embedding space. This is a significant contribution to understanding how neural networks can implicitly capture linguistic structure. The PCA visualizations (Figure 3) clearly demonstrate that the authors' word encoder produces more linguistically meaningful representations than both ordinary RNN encoders and CNN encoders.

2. **Demonstrated practical utility for OOV handling**: The experiments in Table 2 provide strong evidence that the model can handle misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") correctly, whereas word-level models would fail due to <unk> tokens. This addresses a real-world problem in NMT systems where users often input misspelled words or neologisms. This is a clear practical advantage that makes the work relevant beyond theoretical contributions.

3. **Significant training efficiency improvements**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model trains much faster than previous character-level approaches. As stated in Table 1, their model achieves comparable BLEU scores to Luong and Manning (2016) in about 7 days of training versus 90 days for the character-level model. This efficiency gain is substantial for practical deployment, especially given the computational resources required for NMT training.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to be "comparable to the state-of-the-art neural machine translation model," but the BLEU scores in Table 1 don't show meaningful improvements over existing approaches. For example, on En-Fr, their best model (DCNMT) achieves 30.56 BLEU after 15 epochs, while Luong and Manning (2016) achieve the same score after 90 days of training. The paper doesn't demonstrate that their model is substantially better than existing approaches, only that it's comparable but more efficient. The claim that "character-level models are better than the subword-level models" is questionable given that modern NMT systems overwhelmingly use subword approaches like BPE.

2. **Inadequate comparison to modern subword approaches**: The paper compares primarily to older character-level models and a basic BPE approach (bpe2bpe), but doesn't compare to modern, state-of-the-art subword segmentation methods like SentencePiece or more sophisticated BPE implementations. The paper's claim that character-level models are better than subword-level models is not supported by current NMT literature, where subword approaches are standard and often outperform pure character-level models. The authors should address this gap in their comparison.

3. **Questionable novelty of the hierarchical decoder**: The HGRU decoder is presented as a key contribution, but it's essentially a modified GRU that conditions on word-level representations. The paper doesn't provide strong evidence that this is fundamentally different from previous approaches. The efficiency gains are modest (7 days vs. 90 days for the Luong and Manning model), but the paper doesn't clearly articulate what makes their decoder novel beyond the specific implementation details.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims character-level models are better than subword-level models (Table 1), but recent literature (e.g., Sennrich et al. 2016, 2017) shows that subword approaches often outperform pure character-level models. Can you clarify this claim and provide evidence comparing your model to modern subword approaches like BPE with more sophisticated segmentation?

2. The paper states that "our model is the simplest and the smallest one in terms of the model size" (Table 1), but the model size (~54M parameters) is similar to other character-level models (CNMT is also ~52M parameters). Can you clarify what specifically makes your model simpler in terms of architecture?

3. How does your model perform on languages with very different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish) beyond the English-Czech pair tested? The paper only evaluates on morphologically poor (English-French) and rich (English-Czech) languages, but doesn't provide evidence for broader applicability.

## Overall Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach to learning morphology in NMT and demonstrates practical advantages for handling OOV words, it lacks sufficient novelty and fails to show significant performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art approaches. The comparison to modern subword methods is inadequate, and the claimed advantages of character-level modeling over subword approaches are not supported by current literature.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More comprehensive comparison to modern subword approaches (not just the basic BPE model)
2. Clearer demonstration of why their approach is better than existing methods beyond efficiency gains
3. Better justification for their claim that character-level models are superior to subword approaches

Given the current state of the paper, I recommend rejection, but with the hope that the authors could address these concerns in a revised version.

## Additional Comments
The paper's writing is generally clear and well-structured, and the technical details are well-explained. The experiments are thorough for the scope of the work, but the evaluation lacks context within the current NMT landscape. The supplementary material could provide additional insights into the model's behavior on other language pairs or with different morphological structures.
