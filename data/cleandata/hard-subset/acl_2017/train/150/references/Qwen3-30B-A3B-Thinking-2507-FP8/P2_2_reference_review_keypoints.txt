- Proposes DCNMT: a dual-RNN word encoder that learns morphemes and a hierarchical character-level decoder to eliminate large vocabularies and handle OOVs.

- Main contributions: novel word encoder learning morphemes, hierarchical decoder for efficient char-level generation, and empirical demonstration of competitive performance and OOV handling.

- Strength: Directly addresses the OOV problem and can translate misspelled and nonce words.

- Strength: Provides strong visual evidence (PCA plots) that the encoder learns meaningful morphological representations.

- Strength: Shows substantial training-efficiency gains versus prior character models (7 days vs. 90 days).

- Strength: Achieves BLEU scores comparable to baselines, especially on morphologically rich language pairs.

- Strength: Clearly explained architectures, aiding reproducibility.

- Weakness: Lacks comparison to recent Transformer-based state-of-the-art models.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope—only three language pairs and few ablations.

- Weakness: Novelty is overstated; approach is similar to prior hybrid word-character models.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical justification linking morphology learning to translation gains.

- Weakness: Training-efficiency and simplicity claims are inconsistent given the model’s six RNNs.

- Question: How does the model compare to Transformers and can the word encoder be integrated into a Transformer?

- Question: Why does the model outperform bpe2bpe after one epoch but underperform after more training—possible overfitting?

- Question: How is the model’s handling of misspellings different from plain character-level translation without morphological learning?

- Question: How are the “energy” values in Figure 4 computed and why do they correspond to morpheme boundaries?

- Overall: Solid, well-executed contribution with convincing morphology evidence and efficiency, but needs broader comparisons and evaluation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
