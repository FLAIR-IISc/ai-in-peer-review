- Proposal: DCNMT — a character-level NMT with a morpheme-learning word encoder and hierarchical decoder to address OOVs.  
- Word encoder: learns morphemes from characters to produce linguistically meaningful word representations and handle OOVs.  
- Hierarchical decoder: generates characters per word efficiently, avoiding separate word/character training passes.  
- Empirical evidence: PCA visualizations show morphology learning and DCNMT achieves competitive BLEU on standard benchmarks.  
- Strength — practical OOV handling: can translate misspellings and nonce words where word-level models output <unk>.  
- Strength — strong morphology evidence: representation clusters and morpheme boundary detection convincingly demonstrated.  
- Strength — training efficiency: trains much faster than prior character-level models (7 days vs months) with comparable performance.  
- Weakness — limited novelty: builds incrementally on prior compositional character-to-word and hybrid decoder work without clear differentiation.  
- Weakness — marginal BLEU gains: improvements are small or equal to baselines, questioning practical advancement.  
- Weakness — missing ablations: no isolation of morphology encoder vs hierarchical decoder contributions.  
- Weakness — inference efficiency unaddressed: model uses six RNNs with no analysis of inference speed or memory.  
- Question: How would using a comprehensive character set (e.g., full Unicode) affect performance and morphology learning?  
- Question: Why does one-epoch DCNMT outperform the final BPE baseline — data artifact or inherent advantage?  
- Question: Can the encoder handle highly agglutinative languages with many morphemes and correctly segment them?  
- Overall: Promising, practical approach to OOVs with evidence of morphology learning but incremental, with modest gains and lacking ablations and inference analysis.
