

# Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that incorporates morphology learning to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem in neural machine translation. The authors introduce a novel word encoder that learns morphemes and their combinations, and a hierarchical decoder that operates at the character level.

**Main Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer):**
1. A novel word encoder architecture using two RNNs to learn morphemes and their combinations, producing meaningful word representations that capture morphological relationships.
2. A hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates characters for each word while maintaining the same encoding length as word-based models.
3. Empirical evidence demonstrating the model's ability to handle OOV, misspelled words, and nonce words, and to learn meaningful morphological relationships between words.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Morphology Integration**: The paper presents a clear and novel approach to incorporating morphology learning into character-level NMT. The word encoder using two RNNs (one for character-level representation and another for weighting morpheme contributions) is a significant technical contribution that addresses a real challenge in character-level translation. This architecture allows the model to generate meaningful word representations without a predefined vocabulary.

2. **Strong Evidence of Morphology Learning**: The authors provide compelling empirical evidence that their model actually learns morphological relationships. The PCA projections in Figure 3 clearly show that their word encoder produces representations where words with similar meanings (e.g., "notable" and "notability") are closer together, unlike the ordinary RNN word encoder. The energy weight analysis in Figure 4 further validates their claim about morpheme learning, showing that the model assigns higher weights to morpheme boundaries.

3. **Practical Utility in Handling OOV and Misspelled Words**: The paper convincingly demonstrates the practical advantage of their approach through examples in Table 2, where the model correctly translates misspelled words and nonce words (like "convenienter") that word-level models would fail to handle. This is a significant contribution with clear real-world applicability.

4. **Competitive Performance**: The model achieves BLEU scores comparable to state-of-the-art word-level and character-level models across multiple language pairs (En-Fr, En-Cs, Cs-En). The results in Table 1 show that their model performs well, with the final BLEU scores matching or exceeding those of previous character-level models.

5. **Efficiency Comparison**: The paper provides a detailed comparison of training efficiency (Table 1), showing that their model trains faster than other character-level models despite having more RNNs. This is an important practical consideration for real-world deployment, as they claim to train their model in just 1 epoch compared to 300 epochs for other character-level models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent BPE-Based Models**: While the paper compares with some BPE-based models (citing Sennrich et al., 2016), it doesn't compare with more recent BPE-based NMT systems that have become standard in the field. The paper cites Firat et al. (2016) but doesn't show how their model compares to more recent BPE approaches, which weakens the claim that their model is competitive with state-of-the-art.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder. Without these, it's difficult to assess how much each component contributes to the overall performance. For example, it's unclear whether the morphology learning is the primary factor behind the improved performance or if the hierarchical decoding alone would have been sufficient.

3. **Superficial Analysis of Model Complexity**: The paper mentions that their model consists of six RNNs but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how this complexity affects training time, inference time, or memory usage compared to simpler models. The efficiency claims are somewhat superficial without detailed analysis of inference speed and memory requirements.

4. **Lack of Failure Case Analysis**: The paper only shows examples where the model succeeds (Table 2), but doesn't provide examples where it fails or discuss limitations. This makes it difficult to assess the model's practical utility and potential weaknesses.

5. **Questionable Novelty of Word Encoder**: The word encoder architecture is somewhat similar to previous work (e.g., Ling et al., 2015), and the paper doesn't clearly distinguish how their approach differs from these earlier methods. The distinction between their approach and existing compositional models needs to be more explicit.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the model can handle nonce words like "convenienter" that weren't in the training set. Can you provide more details on how the model generalizes to such cases, and whether this generalization is specifically due to the morphology learning or could be attributed to other factors in the model?

2. How does your model compare to more recent BPE-based NMT systems (e.g., those using more sophisticated subword segmentation)? The paper cites Sennrich et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent work that might have improved upon this approach.

3. How does your model compare to word-level models in terms of inference speed and memory usage? The paper claims efficiency in training, but doesn't discuss inference time or memory requirements, which are critical for real-world deployment.
