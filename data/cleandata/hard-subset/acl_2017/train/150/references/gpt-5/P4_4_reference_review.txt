Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) architecture designed to capture morphology and enable efficient character-level decoding. The encoder builds word representations from characters using two RNNs: one produces prefix states over characters and another (a BiRNN) predicts position-wise weights; a weighted sum of prefix states yields the word vector, intended to reflect morphemic structure. The sentence is then encoded by a bidirectional RNN. The decoder is hierarchical: a word-level RNN produces a state per target word, and a modified GRU (HGRU) generates the sequence of characters for that word, resetting its state at delimiters. An unfolding matrix allows efficient batching across character sequences aligned to word boundaries. The paper reports competitive BLEU scores on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, along with qualitative analyses suggesting learned morphology and robustness to misspellings and nonce words.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A character-based word encoder that uses position-specific weights over character-prefix RNN states to produce word representations, aiming to learn morphemic segmentation without explicit supervision.
2) A hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” HGRU and an unfolding/batching mechanism to train character-level decoders efficiently conditioned on word-level states.
3) Empirical evidence that the proposed model achieves BLEU scores comparable to prior character-level systems with smaller parameter counts and fewer training days, and qualitative demonstrations of robustness to OOVs and misspellings.

Strengths
- Structured character-to-word composition: The encoder introduces a principled mechanism to softly segment words by weighting character-prefix states, contrasting with prior C2W (final state) or CNN encoders. The energy peaks aligning with plausible morpheme boundaries and cross-word consistency of weights (e.g., “any-*”, “every-*”) provide interpretable signals that the model captures subword structure.
- Efficient character-level decoding: The hierarchical decoder with HGRU and the unfolding matrix enables batch training with character-level outputs conditioned on word-level states, avoiding two separate passes used in some hybrid approaches. Reported training times (e.g., 7–22 days on a single GPU) are lower than some prior fully character-level models, at similar BLEU.
- Competitive translation quality across language pairs: On En–Fr and Cs–En the model is within the range of state-of-the-art character baselines, and for Cs–En essentially matches char2char (22.48 vs. 22.46). The En–Fr results (30.56 BLEU) exceed the cited bpe2bpe baseline (29.70), suggesting the approach is viable beyond highly inflected languages.
- OOV and misspelling robustness: The qualitative examples show correct handling of misspelled inputs and nonce morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”). This aligns with the model’s open-vocabulary character decoding and is a practical advantage over word-level systems.
- Model size and simplicity: Despite six RNNs, the parameter count (~54M) is smaller than some competing character/subword systems (~69–76M), supporting the efficiency claim.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior work is incremental: Hierarchical decoders conditioned on word-level states, character-level encoding/decoding, and soft/learned subword composition have been explored (e.g., Ling et al. 2015; Luong & Manning 2016; Lee et al. 2016; Serban et al. 2015). The specific weighting of character-prefix states by a separate BiRNN is a variation, but the paper does not clearly position how this differs from or improves upon attention over character positions or existing C2W/CNN encoders via controlled ablations.
- Limited and partially inconsistent experimental rigor: Many baseline numbers are taken from other papers with potentially different preprocessing, data filtering, and training budgets. The En–Fr setup references WMT’14 data but evaluates on newstest2015; this mismatch and the absence of standardized comparisons (e.g., same training epochs, beam sizes, length limits) weaken claims of superiority. No statistical significance tests are reported, and decoding speed/latency is not quantified.
- Incomplete ablations and sensitivity analyses: The contribution of the word encoder vs. a simpler char-BiRNN/CNN encoder is not isolated beyond a single “CNMT” variant. The effect of the weight normalization choice (using tanh on an unnormalized weighted sum with exp weights) is not analyzed; potential saturation/scale issues and alternatives (softmax normalization, additive attention) are not considered. The necessity and impact of the unfolding matrix R are not quantified.
- Clarity and technical specification issues: Key components (HGRU equations, delimiter handling during training/inference, construction of a_t, resetting behavior, and the unfolding matrix R) are described tersely, making exact reimplementation difficult. Some notation is overloaded (e.g., s_t used both for first-level state and unfolded states) and figures are essential but not fully explained in text. Several language/typo issues hinder clarity.
- Morphology learning evidence is anecdotal: The PCA plots and a few qualitative examples suggest learned morphemic patterns, but there is no quantitative evaluation against a morphological segmentation dataset or probing tasks. Claims that the encoder “learns morphology” are therefore not strongly substantiated.

Questions to Authors
1) Can you provide controlled ablations isolating the word-encoder design? Specifically: (a) replace the weighted-sum encoder with a BiRNN final state and with a CNN+highway encoder; (b) replace exp(aff(ht)) weights+tanh with softmax-normalized attention; (c) analyze BLEU and training speed impacts.
2) Please clarify the data and evaluation setup per language pair: which WMT years/corpora, tokenization, filtering, and exact dev/test sets (e.g., En–Fr newstest2013/newstest2015 vs. WMT’14 newstest2014). Were all systems trained on exactly the same bitext and with the same maximum sequence lengths?
3) How is the auxiliary delimiter indicator a_t obtained during training and inference? Are spaces used as delimiters in all languages? How is punctuation handled? What happens for languages with ambiguous tokenization?
4) Can you report decoding speed (chars/sec and words/sec), average generated word lengths, and beam search configuration at character level? How does the unfolding matrix R affect wall-clock time and memory compared to a naïve implementation?
5) The weighting scheme: why avoid normalization? Did you observe saturation in tanh(r) or instability from large exp weights? Please report training stability and any gradient clipping/regularization employed.
6) Can you quantify morphological competence (e.g., F1 against a morph segmentation benchmark, or probing tasks for suffix/prefix detection) to support the “learning morphology” claim?
7) For fairness, can you re-train a strong BPE baseline under your exact setup (data, model size, training budget) and report results with significance testing?
