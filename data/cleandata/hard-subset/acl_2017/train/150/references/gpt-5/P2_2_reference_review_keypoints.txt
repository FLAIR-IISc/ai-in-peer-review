- Proposes DCNMT: character-level NMT with a morphology-aware weighted-prefix word encoder and a hierarchical word→char decoder with an efficient unfolding/batching trick.  
- Word encoder: two char-RNNs produce prefix representations and weights that are combined into a weighted-sum word embedding intended to discover morpheme boundaries.  
- Hierarchical decoder: word-level decoder initializes a character-level HGRU that generates characters until a delimiter; training uses an unfolding matrix to avoid multi-pass training.  
- Empirical results: evaluated on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En showing BLEU comparable to other char-level models and better than some subword baselines, with smaller model size and faster training.  
- Qualitative analyses: PCA and energy plots show plausible morpheme peaks and grouping; examples demonstrate robustness to misspellings and nonce words.  

Strengths
- Addresses OOV/rare-morphology issues with an interpretable, linguistically motivated weighted-prefix encoder.  
- Visualization and qualitative examples support the claim of subword generalization beyond fixed vocabularies.  
- Practical training/decoding design (HGRU + unfolding) yields favorable training speed and parameter efficiency compared to prior char-level systems.  
- Ablations (comparison to CNMT) isolate encoder contribution and show consistent BLEU gains and faster convergence.  
- End-to-end character modeling eliminates need for external segmentation or BPE rules.  

Weaknesses
- Key methodological details are underspecified: ambiguous weighting formulation (tanh vs. softmax/exp), HGRU dimensions/initialization, and attention interaction.  
- Delimiter handling is unclear: training uses gold delimiters in unfolding, but inference behavior and error propagation are not fully addressed.  
- Baseline comparisons are uneven and potentially unfair; strong, capacity-matched BPE baselines and statistical significance tests are missing.  
- Morphology claims are mainly qualitative; no quantitative evaluation against segmentation/morphology benchmarks or probing tasks.  
- "Open vocabulary" claim weakened by mapping all but top 120 characters to <unk>, with no coverage statistics or analysis of remaining OOV chars.  
- Missing training/decoding details (beam settings, normalization, regularization, batching, throughput) and no code release hinder reproducibility.  

Review criteria summary
- Originality: moderate—variants on known char-based encoders/decoders, but weighted-prefix encoder and batching trick are novel.  
- Soundness/correctness: mixed—plausible results but ambiguous math and incomplete ablations raise concerns.  
- Clarity: below average—typos, inconsistent notation, and missing algorithmic details reduce clarity.  
- Significance: moderate—practical efficiency gains, but not clearly superior to strong subword baselines.  
- Empirical evaluation: mixed—good multilingual coverage and ablations but fairness, detail, and rigor lacking.  
- Reproducibility: weak—critical hyperparameters and implementation specifics are missing and no code provided.  

Key questions for authors
- Clarify the weighting: is wt = tanh(aff(ht)) or wt = exp(aff(ht)) and how is scale controlled across word lengths?  
- Define delimiters precisely: how are word boundaries tokenized in training and how are delimiter prediction errors handled at test time?  
- Provide beam search and regularization details: beam size, length/coverage normalization, dropout, gradient clipping, etc.  
- Report what fraction of chars are mapped to <unk> under the 120-char vocabulary per language.  
- Provide a matched-capacity, matched-budget BPE baseline and throughput metrics (chars/sec or tokens/sec).  
- Quantify morphology learning via segmentation F1 or probing tasks on annotated corpora.  

Additional comments
- Fix numerous typos and inconsistent references/notation.  
- Add algorithmic pseudocode for the unfolding/batching and more complete equations for HGRU and delimiter handling.  

Overall
- Promising, interpretable approach with efficient training and useful qualitative evidence, but clarity, experimental rigor, and reproducibility need substantial improvement before acceptance.
