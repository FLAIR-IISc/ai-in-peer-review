Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes a character-based NMT architecture with two main ideas: (1) a word encoder that composes word embeddings from characters by weighting prefix-encoding RNN states with a second (bi-directional) RNN that scores character positions, intended to learn morphemes and their composition; (2) a hierarchical decoder with a word-level RNN whose outputs initialize a character-level GRU (HGRU) to generate each target word until a delimiter, with a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to efficiently replicate word-level states across characters.
- Empirically, the model is evaluated on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs and Cs–En, reporting BLEU competitive with prior character/subword systems and some qualitative evidence of morphological generalization (e.g., handling misspellings/nonce forms, PCA plots, subword boundary “energies”).
- The paper argues the approach trains faster than some prior pure character models while keeping open-vocabulary benefits.

Main contributions (in order of strength)
1) An efficient hierarchical character decoder that resets character-level state at word boundaries and can be trained in batch via an unfolding replication matrix. This is a practical engineering contribution to make char-level decoding more efficient.
2) A character-to-word encoder with position-dependent weighting from a separate BiRNN, effectively an attention/pooling mechanism over character positions to form word representations, aimed at learning morphology.
3) Qualitative analysis and small-scale demonstrations that the model handles OOV/misspelled/nonce words and appears to capture morphological structure.

Strengths
- Practical hierarchical decoding mechanism: The HGRU with reset-on-delimiter plus the unfolding matrix is a useful technique that appears to reduce training time relative to prior char-level decoders that required two passes or more complex scheduling (e.g., hybrid/char-only models). The reported wall-clock reductions (e.g., En–Fr trained in ~1–3 weeks vs months in cited work) suggest meaningful engineering value, even if more rigorous speed reporting would help.
- Competitive BLEU with fewer parameters: With ~54M parameters, the model achieves BLEU comparable to or slightly better than strong subword baselines and close to state-of-the-art char-based models on the reported setups, indicating the approach is viable without very large models.
- Open-vocabulary robustness: The qualitative examples on misspellings and nonce morphology (e.g., “convenienter”) illustrate the natural advantage of character modeling. This is an important practical benefit over word/BPE systems.
- Attempt to learn morphology explicitly: The word encoder’s position-weighted composition is a clear, interpretable mechanism; the PCA plots and energy visualizations are suggestive that it groups morphologically related forms more sensibly than a simple last-state RNN. The application to PTB shows it can roughly discover subword boundaries without explicit supervision.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior work is limited and under-positioned: The word encoder is essentially attention/gated pooling over character RNN states, conceptually close to prior character composition models with attention or gated CNN pooling. The hierarchical decoder resembles earlier hierarchical decoders/hybrid architectures (e.g., Ling et al. 2015b; Luong & Manning 2016; Lee et al. 2016). The batching trick is useful but incremental. The paper should more precisely delineate what is new versus adaptations of existing ideas and compare to attention-over-characters baselines.
- Evaluation comparability and rigor: The BLEU comparisons are drawn from different papers with possibly different preprocessing, training data filtering, and search settings. There are inconsistencies (e.g., En–Fr “WMT’14” training but evaluation on newstest2015; dev/test mismatches across works). No statistical significance tests, no controlled reimplementations of baselines, and no standardized tokenization/reporting details impede fair comparison.
- Morphology evidence is mostly qualitative: The PCA plots and a few cherry-picked examples are insufficient to support the claim “learns morphology.” There is no quantitative evaluation on morphological segmentation or tagging, no controlled nonce-word generalization tests at scale, and no OOV/noise robustness benchmarks.
- Clarity and correctness issues: Several equations are ambiguous or inconsistent (e.g., wt defined with exp but then “we use tanh instead of normalizing,” unclear normalization; indices j/t; shapes). The HGRU equations are hard to follow, and the role of the delimiter mask a_t and how it is obtained at training vs inference is under-specified. Many language and typographical errors, and references are incomplete or malformed. Figures are referenced but not fully described.
- Missing ablations and training details: No ablation on the word encoder (e.g., last-state RNN vs CNN vs attention softmax vs tanh weighting), the reset mechanism, or the importance of the unfolding matrix. Key hyperparameters (beam size, dropout/regularization, layer norms), training schedule, and inference speed are not reported. It is unclear how much each component contributes to BLEU and speed.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder weighting: Do you use normalized attention over character positions (softmax) or unnormalized weights followed by tanh on the summed vector? The text seems contradictory. Please specify the exact formulation and rationale.
- Delimiter handling: What exactly is the delimiter in training (space? special token) and how is a_t constructed? At inference, do you reset only when the model emits a delimiter, and how do you avoid degenerate behaviors (e.g., missing delimiters)? Any constraints or penalties used?
- Beam search: What beam size is used at character level? Do you synchronize beams at word boundaries or use any structured beam across word/char levels? Any length or delimiter penalties?
- Data and preprocessing: For En–Fr, what exact training set (WMT’14 vs ’15), tokenization, normalization, and vocabulary of 120 characters were used? Are punctuation and diacritics preserved? Please reconcile the dev/test years and ensure comparability to cited baselines.
- Speed measurement: How were “days” measured (hardware, mixed precision, early stopping)? Can you report tokens/sec or characters/sec for training and decoding compared to a reproduced baseline?
- Controlled robustness: Can you provide a systematic evaluation on synthetic misspellings/noisy text and on nonce morphological forms (e.g., productivity tests), beyond isolated examples?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, model configs, and scripts to construct the unfolding matrix and training data?

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct the mathematical definitions (especially the word encoder weighting and HGRU update), add a schematic algorithm for training/inference with a_t and R, and fix reference and language issues.
- Run controlled, apples-to-apples comparisons by re-training key baselines under the same data and preprocessing; include significance tests and report beam sizes and decoding setups.
- Provide ablations: (i) word encoder variants (last-state, CNN, softmax attention); (ii) with/without unfolding trick; (iii) with/without hierarchical reset; (iv) normalization choices (tanh vs softmax).
- Add quantitative morphology/robustness evaluations (e.g., segmentation F1 on Morpho-Challenge, probing tasks, noise robustness benchmarks).
- Report training/decoding speed in standardized units and memory footprint, and release code.

Overall, the paper presents a promising and practical architecture for character-level NMT with an interesting word encoder and an efficient hierarchical decoder, but the novelty is incremental and the empirical validation and clarity need strengthening. I lean toward rejection in the current form, but the work could become competitive with clearer exposition, stronger controlled experiments, and more rigorous evidence for morphology learning and efficiency.
