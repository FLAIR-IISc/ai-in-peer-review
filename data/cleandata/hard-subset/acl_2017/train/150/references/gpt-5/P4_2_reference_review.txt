Summary
The paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model that aims to learn morphology and alleviate large-vocabulary issues in word-level NMT. The approach introduces (a) a character-based word encoder that aggregates prefix-level RNN states using position-dependent weights from a second RNN (a BiRNN), and (b) a hierarchical decoder: a first-level “word-level” decoder that produces vectors per target word, and a second-level character decoder (HGRU) whose state is set by the first-level output and generates characters until a delimiter. An unfolding/batching scheme enables efficient training of the character decoder with variable word lengths. Experiments on WMT14 En–Fr and WMT15 En–Cs/Cs–En report BLEU comparable to published character-level models, with qualitative analyses suggesting learned morphological regularities and robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A novel word encoder that learns morphology by weighting character-level prefix RNN states with position-wise scores from a second (bi-directional) RNN, yielding word representations without a lookup table.
2) A hierarchical character-level decoder with a settable-state GRU (HGRU) and an “unfolding” mechanism to support efficient batch training and inference at the character level.
3) Empirical demonstration that the model achieves competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr/En–Cs/Cs–En with smaller parameter counts and faster training than some prior char-level baselines; qualitative evidence that the encoder captures morpheme boundaries and that the system handles misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary and morphology: By operating at character level and explicitly modeling word-internal structure, the model sidesteps fixed vocabularies and demonstrates robustness to misspellings and unseen morphological variants. The qualitative examples (e.g., “convenienter”) and segmentation visualizations are consistent with the goal of learning morpheme-like units.

- Competitive accuracy with efficiency: On three WMT settings, reported BLEU is comparable to other character-based systems (Lee et al., Luong & Manning) while using fewer parameters (~54M) and fewer training days/epochs. The batching/unfolding approach mitigates typical inefficiencies of hierarchical char decoders, offering a practical contribution.

- End-to-end trainable hierarchical design: The architecture integrates six RNNs into a coherent end-to-end framework without layer-wise pretraining or attention supervision. This addresses known training difficulties in prior char-level NMT and is supported by the reported successful convergence on large datasets.

- Novel word-encoder formulation: The two-RNN word encoder that sums prefix encodings with position-dependent weights is a distinct alternative to CNN-based or C2W encoders. The PCA analyses suggest this mechanism can produce semantically organized word embeddings and highlight morpheme endpoints.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior hierarchical/char work: The hierarchical decoder, word-to-char generation with delimiter boundaries, and leveraging character encoders for word representations are well-studied (e.g., Ling et al., Luong & Manning, Lee et al.). The main novelty is the specific word encoder weighting scheme and the batching trick; the paper would benefit from a clearer differentiation and ablation showing these components’ unique gains.

- Technical clarity and correctness issues: Several equations and descriptions are unclear or potentially inconsistent (e.g., Eqn. (2) reuses gt−1 on both sides; the role of at and how it is set/inferred at test time; the definition and dimensions of the unfolding matrix R; how delimiters are modeled in the target alphabet). The phrase “Instead of normalizing by sum exp(aff(ht)), we use tanh” conflates post-sum nonlinearity with weight normalization; the actual weight normalization is unclear, raising concerns about length bias and stability.

- Evaluation gaps and fairness: BLEU tables combine results from different sources with possibly different preprocessing, training regimens, and hardware; beam sizes are not specified; and some comparisons (e.g., to strong BPE baselines on the same data/hyperparameters) may not be strictly controlled. Reported training time comparisons across papers may be unreliable. No statistical significance testing is reported.

- Morphology claims are largely qualitative: While plots indicate energy peaks near morpheme boundaries, there is no quantitative evaluation against morphological segmentation benchmarks, nor intrinsic metrics (e.g., boundary F1) to substantiate the morphology learning claim.

- “Open vocabulary” qualification: The character set is capped at the 120 most frequent characters with <unk> for others, which undermines a fully open-vocabulary claim, especially for diacritics and non-ASCII characters in En–Cs/Cs–En. The coverage “nearly 100%” is not quantified.

Questions to Authors
1) Word encoder weighting: Are weights wt normalized (e.g., softmax over positions)? If not, how do you control scaling with word length and prevent dominance by longer words? Please clarify the precise formulation and any regularization.

2) HGRU details and inference: How is at determined during inference, given that delimiter decisions are model outputs? Please provide a precise decoding algorithm, including how/when the first-level decoder advances, and beam search settings at the character level.

3) Unfolding matrix R: Please formalize the construction, shape, and use of R in batched training, including how gradients propagate across the two levels.

4) Ablations: What is the BLEU impact of (a) the proposed word encoder vs. an ordinary RNN/CNN char encoder, (b) HGRU vs. a standard GRU with reset, and (c) training with vs. without unfolding? This would help isolate your contributions.

5) Character vocabulary: What exact coverage do the 120-character sets achieve on train/dev/test for each language? How are diacritics handled in Cs and what is the impact on BLEU when mapping rare characters to <unk>?

6) Decoding hyperparameters: Please report beam sizes, length penalties, and any specific heuristics used in character-level search. Are results sensitive to these?

7) Quantitative morphology evaluation: Can you provide results on a standard morphological segmentation dataset or an intrinsic boundary-detection metric to substantiate morphology learning?

Overall, the paper presents a practical hierarchical char-level NMT with a novel word encoder and an efficient training mechanism, achieving competitive BLEU. However, clarity issues, limited ablations, and modest novelty relative to prior char/hierarchical NMT reduce its impact. Clarifications and additional experiments would strengthen the case.
