Summary and contributions
- Proposes a morphology-aware character-to-word encoder that composes a word representation from character-level RNN states using learned, position-dependent “energy” weights produced by a second (bi-directional) RNN. This aims to implicitly discover morphemes and how they combine.
- Introduces a hierarchical decoder with a settable-state HGRU that generates characters conditioned on a word-level state, switching/resetting at delimiter boundaries. An efficient batching trick (“unfolding” matrix) enables training in a single pass.
- Provides qualitative and quantitative evidence that the encoder learns morphology (energy peaks at morpheme boundaries, PCA clustering, plausible segmentations) and that the overall character-level system is competitive with strong subword- and character-level baselines while being relatively parameter-efficient.

Strengths
- Novel encoder that explicitly targets morphology learning: The dual-RNN architecture that separately (i) tracks prefix composition and (ii) predicts per-position weights is a fresh, clearly motivated idea for subword discovery without external segmentation (contrasting with BPE/CNN-based encoders). The qualitative analyses (morpheme-boundary energies; PCA plots showing -ability/-ibility families; reasonable segmentations like consum-er-s, foot-ball) convincingly support the claim that the encoder captures morphology.
- Principled, practical hierarchical decoder: The HGRU with settable state and an unfolding/batching mechanism is a thoughtful contribution that addresses a known bottleneck of character decoders (training inefficiency, attention difficulties). The mechanism to reset at delimiters and to condition character generation on word-level states is elegant and potentially useful beyond MT.
- Competitive performance with smaller models and faster early learning: Despite six RNNs, the model size (~54M) is smaller than other char-level systems reported (69–76M), and it achieves comparable BLEU on En–Fr, En–Cs, Cs–En. Notably, after just one epoch, DCNMT outperforms BPE baselines, supporting the claim that morphology-aware compositionality accelerates learning.
- Robustness to OOVs/misspellings/nonce forms: The examples demonstrating correct handling of misspellings and morphologically novel forms (e.g., convenienter) are compelling and align with the purported benefits of character-level modeling plus morphology-aware composition. This is a meaningful practical advantage over word-level models.
- Clear, modular architecture likely transferable: The character→subword→word→sentence→word→character hierarchy is conceptually clean and could plausibly be adapted to other sequence tasks (speech, summarization), increasing the broader impact potential.

Weaknesses
- Limited and partially inconsistent empirical rigor: The baseline comparisons pull numbers from multiple papers with heterogeneous setups, data filtering, and training budgets. Some DCNMT results are only “comparable” (not superior), e.g., En–Cs trails the best published char baseline. Claims of efficiency (epochs/days) are hard to interpret without standardized hardware/training regimes and variance across runs. A controlled reimplementation/ablation suite is missing.
- Ablations are largely absent: There is no systematic study isolating the contribution of (a) the morphology-aware weights vs an ordinary char-to-word encoder, (b) HGRU vs a standard char decoder with resets, (c) the choice of tanh vs normalized weights, or (d) the target-side word-encoder feedback. Given the architectural novelty, ablations would greatly strengthen causal claims.
- Methodological clarity gaps: The weighting scheme allows negative “energies” (tanh of unnormalized exp(aff(h))) and mixes terminology (energy/weight) without justification; a softmax over positions or a proper gating could be more principled. Beam search on the hierarchical decoder is under-specified (single-level beam at character level? nested beams at word boundaries?), as is the handling of attention across the two levels during inference. The training relies on an auxiliary delimiter sequence a_t; inference details for boundary prediction and stability are not fully spelled out.
- Morphology evaluation is anecdotal: While figures are illustrative, there is no quantitative evaluation of discovered subword units (e.g., segmentation F1 vs. Morfessor/BPE, or correlation with morphological tags), nor extrinsic analysis linking morphological quality to translation gains (e.g., OOV/rare-word subsets, morphologically-rich languages beyond Czech).
- Presentation and referencing issues: Formatting/typos, incomplete citations, and occasional contradictions (e.g., “simplest” model despite six RNNs; “we need explicit segmentation” vs “learn subword units automatically”) reduce clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Weighting scheme: Why use tanh of unnormalized weights (potentially negative contributions) rather than a normalized distribution (softmax) or bounded gate (sigmoid)? Please provide an ablation comparing these options and discuss observed behavior (e.g., does negativity help disambiguate morphemes?).
- Decoder search: How is beam search implemented across the two levels? Is there a single character-level beam that implicitly handles word boundaries, or a nested beam that resets at delimiters? Any pruning heuristics to keep search tractable?
- Training/inference alignment: During training you use the ground-truth delimiter sequence a_t. At inference, how is a_t obtained—purely from generated characters? How sensitive is performance to premature/late delimiters? Please report boundary error rates.
- Efficiency: Can you provide standardized training time comparisons (same hardware, tokens/sec, wall-clock to a fixed BLEU) against bpe2char/char2char baselines? How much does the unfolding matrix reduce time vs a naïve two-pass approach?
- Ablations: Please report (i) replacing the morphology-aware encoder with an ordinary char-RNN/CNN encoder; (ii) replacing HGRU with a standard GRU/LSTM with reset; (iii) removing the target word-encoder feedback. How do BLEU and early-epoch learning curves change?
- Morphology evaluation: Can you quantify segmentation quality on a morphologically annotated corpus or compare discovered units with BPE/Morfessor? Also, provide OOV/rare-word subset BLEU and error analysis.

Overall: This paper introduces two genuinely novel, well-motivated components that push character-level NMT beyond incremental tweaks. Despite evaluation gaps, the direction is bold and promising, with credible qualitative evidence and competitive results. I lean toward acceptance conditioned on clarifying the search/training details and adding minimal ablations.
