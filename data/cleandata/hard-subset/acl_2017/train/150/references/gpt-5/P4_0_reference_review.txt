Summary
This paper proposes a fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) system with a hierarchical architecture designed to learn morphology and improve efficiency. A two-RNN word encoder computes word representations directly from characters via a weighted sum over prefix hidden states, where weights are produced by a second BiRNN over characters. On the target side, a two-level decoder is introduced: a word-level decoder produces states that condition a character-level GRU variant (HGRU) which generates characters until a delimiter is produced. An “unfolding matrix” is used to efficiently broadcast word-level decoder outputs to all characters of the corresponding word during batch training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report BLEU comparable to other character-level systems and sometimes better than BPE baselines. Qualitative analyses suggest the encoder captures morpheme-like units and the model handles misspellings and nonce words.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Character-to-word encoder that aims to learn morphemes and their composition: a weighted sum over character-level RNN states with weights from a separate BiRNN provides a compact word embedding without a lookup table. The qualitative analyses indicate improved clustering by morphology versus a simple RNN encoder.

2) Hierarchical character decoder with a “settable-state” GRU (HGRU) and a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to condition character generation on word-level states efficiently. This removes the need for two-pass training and enables end-to-end training with character-level outputs.

3) Empirical evidence that a fully character-level system can be competitive on WMT-scale data without explicit subword segmentation, alongside qualitative demonstrations of robustness to misspellings and nonce morphological forms.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary and morphological variation directly at the character level. The qualitative examples (e.g., correcting misspellings and translating “convenienter”) illustrate the anticipated benefits of character modeling, which are hard for word-level/BPE systems.

- Encoder design tailored to morphology. The weighted aggregation over character prefixes, with weights from a second RNN, plausibly separates stem and affix contributions. The PCA plots show better organization of derivational pairs (e.g., -ability/-ibility families) relative to a simple character RNN encoder, and the energy plots align with potential morpheme boundaries.

- End-to-end hierarchical decoding at the character level. Conditioning character generation on word-level decoder states is a sensible approach to retain shorter effective time scales while avoiding a large softmax. The proposed unfolding trick makes batched training straightforward, addressing known efficiency issues in prior hybrid/character models.

- Competitive BLEU in multiple directions with relatively compact models. Across En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, the reported BLEU matches or exceeds cited character-level baselines and some BPE baselines, and the parameter count is lower than some alternatives. Early-epoch performance suggests faster learning than a BPE baseline in some cases.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and completeness of the technical description. The weighting mechanism for the encoder is ambiguously specified: w_t = exp(aff(h_t)) is introduced, but the text states “instead of normalizing by sum exp, we use tanh,” which conflates normalization of weights with a nonlinearity applied after summation. It is unclear whether weights are normalized (e.g., softmax) and how scale is controlled. The HGRU equations and the role of a_t (delimiter indicator) also need clearer, complete definitions, including exact training/inference behavior.

- Limited and potentially incomparable evaluation. Many baselines are taken from different papers with different hardware, preprocessing, and stopping criteria; significance tests are absent. Training-time comparisons in “days” and “epochs” do not control for hardware or exact data configurations, and no decoding speed, throughput, or memory measurements are reported. The claim of efficiency would be stronger with controlled measurements.

- Ablation and diagnostic analysis are insufficient. Beyond the “CNMT” variant, there is no systematic isolation of (i) the morphology-aware weighting vs. a standard attention or normalized weighting, (ii) the hierarchical decoder vs. a flat character decoder under identical settings, or (iii) the unfolding trick’s impact. This makes it difficult to attribute the observed gains to specific components.

- Morphology learning is only qualitatively assessed. While PCA plots and energy heatmaps are informative, there is no quantitative evaluation (e.g., morpheme boundary detection accuracy, nearest-neighbor consistency across derivational pairs) or broader analysis across languages with rich morphology (e.g., Czech) using test sets with morphological variation.

- Experimental details and preprocessing choices are under-specified. The restriction to the top 120 characters and mapping others to <unk> may be problematic for Czech diacritics; handling of Unicode/normalization is not discussed. Beam search parameters and stopping criteria for character-level decoding are not reported. The En–Fr dataset/test split description appears inconsistent (WMT’14 training but testing on newstest2015).

Questions to Authors
1) Word encoder weighting: Are the weights w_t normalized (softmax) over positions, or are they unnormalized positive scalars? Please provide the exact final formula for r_word, including any normalization and nonlinearity. Did you compare against a standard attention (softmax-normalized) variant and a simple last-state encoder?

2) HGRU definition and inference: Please provide a complete, unambiguous specification (or pseudocode) of the HGRU, including the role of a_t during training and at test time. At inference, is a_t determined by the model’s own predicted delimiter token? How do you handle errors in delimiter prediction and state resets?

3) Efficiency: Can you report controlled speed and memory metrics (tokens/sec, chars/sec, GPU hours) for training and decoding, compared to reimplemented BPE and character baselines on the same hardware and data? What beam sizes were used, and how does character-level beam search affect latency?

4) Ablations: Can you provide ablations for (i) encoder weighting (softmax vs. unnormalized, with/without the BiRNN weights), (ii) decoder architecture (hierarchical vs. flat character decoder), and (iii) the unfolding matrix trick? How much does each component contribute to BLEU and training speed?

5) Morphology evaluation: Do you have quantitative evidence that the encoder discovers morphemes (e.g., segmentation F1 on a standard dataset or correlation with morphological lexicons), especially for Czech? If not, can you at least report consistent nearest-neighbor analyses across larger vocabularies?

6) Data/preprocessing: How are non-ASCII characters handled within the 120-character limit, particularly for Czech? Are diacritics preserved or mapped to <unk>? Please clarify the En–Fr training/test splits to ensure comparability with cited baselines.

Overall, the paper proposes a plausible morphology-aware character-level NMT with a practical hierarchical decoder. Addressing clarity, providing controlled comparisons and ablations, and strengthening quantitative morphology evidence would substantially improve the paper.
