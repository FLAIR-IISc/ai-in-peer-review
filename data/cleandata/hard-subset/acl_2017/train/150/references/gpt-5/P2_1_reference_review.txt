Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that combines: (a) a character-to-word encoder that aims to learn morpheme-like subword structure via a weighted sum over character-level RNN states, and (b) a hierarchical decoder with a “word-level” RNN whose outputs initialize a character-level GRU (HGRU) to generate each target word until a delimiter is produced. The authors argue this preserves short encoder sequences (word-level) while decoding at character-level, improving OOV handling and training efficiency. They report BLEU on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En and provide qualitative analyses suggesting the encoder captures morphology and that the model can translate misspellings/nonce words.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical hierarchical character decoder with a “settable-state” GRU and a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to train efficiently in one pass.
2) A character-to-word encoder that learns weighted combinations of character-level states intended to capture morphemes, with qualitative evidence (energy plots, PCA) and examples on misspellings/nonce words.
3) Empirical results showing competitive BLEU vs char/subword baselines with smaller model size and faster training than some prior char-only models.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary and morphology explicitly: Decoding at the character level naturally handles OOV, misspellings, and nonce forms. The qualitative examples (e.g., “convenienter”) and energy/boundary visualizations convincingly illustrate behaviors word-level models cannot achieve, and are directly tied to the proposed architecture.

- Practical training innovation for hierarchical char decoding: The HGRU with externally settable state and the unfolding/broadcasting trick for batching make the two-level decoder train in a single forward pass. This is a useful engineering contribution with potential to be adopted by others working on hierarchical decoders.

- Competitive performance with smaller models and moderate training time: Reported BLEU is comparable to state-of-the-art character-level systems on three language pairs, with fewer parameters (~54M) and substantially less training time than some prior char models (e.g., Luong & Manning’s pure char system). Even if some comparisons are imperfect (see below), the numbers suggest the approach is viable and efficient.

- Clear intuition and analysis around morphology: The PCA plots and energy profiles show the proposed encoder clusters related forms more meaningfully than a simple RNN word encoder, and seems to highlight morpheme boundaries (e.g., “any”, “every”, suffixes). While qualitative, this analysis is aligned with the model design and helps interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior character/subword work: Character-aware word encoders (CNNs/RNNs) and hierarchical decoders have been explored (e.g., Ling et al. 2015; Lee et al. 2016; Luong & Manning 2016; Serban et al. 2015). The main novel elements here—the specific weighting scheme for the word encoder and the HGRU with batching trick—feel incremental and are not rigorously compared to strong alternatives (e.g., CNN encoders, BiLSTM encoders with attention over character positions, or standard GRUs with explicit reset). The paper would benefit from ablations to isolate the gains due to the word-encoder weighting and the HGRU reset vs simpler baselines.

- Clarity and rigor of the word encoder formulation: The weighting scheme defines wt = exp(aff(ht)) but then states “instead of normalizing by sum exp(aff(ht)), we use tanh” and applies tanh to the final sum. This conflates scaling and normalization and makes it hard to interpret weights as attention-like probabilities. Why is unnormalized exp preferable? How sensitive are results to normalization or other nonlinearities? Without ablations or clearer motivation, it’s difficult to assess whether the observed “morpheme energies” are robust or incidental.

- Experimental comparability and reporting gaps: Several comparisons draw on results from different datasets/preprocessing/training budgets (e.g., En–Fr trained on WMT’14 but evaluated on newstest2015; baselines taken from different works with possibly different corpora, BPE merges, beam sizes, or hardware). The table is hard to parse and some entries are inconsistent (missing dev scores, different max lengths). Strong claims about efficiency and BLEU superiority should be backed by controlled, same-data, same-preprocessing, same-budget comparisons (e.g., reproduce a BPE2BPE baseline and a char2char baseline under the same setup). Also, decoding speed and training throughput are not reported (only days/epochs), which depend on hardware and implementation.

- Limited quantitative analysis of morphology learning: The paper’s morphology claims are supported by PCA plots and hand-picked examples. There is no quantitative evaluation of segmentation quality (e.g., against Morfessor or gold morpheme boundaries), nor systematic analysis of generalization to unseen morphological variants. An ablation comparing the proposed encoder vs CNN/LSTM alternatives on low-resource/morphologically rich languages would strengthen the claim.

- Presentation issues: The paper contains many typos, occasional unclear notation (e.g., indices in Eqns. 2–6), and incomplete references. Figures/tables could be clearer (Table 1 formatting; Figure axes/labels). Some implementation details are underspecified (beam sizes, delimiter specification, character set and handling of out-of-set chars beyond mapping to <unk>, BPE settings for baselines).

Questions to authors
- Ablations: What is the BLEU impact of (i) removing the word-encoder weighting (use last state or average), (ii) replacing the RNN-based encoder with a CNN, (iii) using normalized attention (softmax over positions) vs the current unnormalized/tanh scheme, and (iv) using a standard GRU with an explicit reset to st vs HGRU?

- Efficiency: Please report tokens/sec or chars/sec, and decoding speed, for DCNMT vs reproduced baselines on the same hardware. How large is the batching speedup from the unfolding trick?

- Comparability: For En–Fr, what exact training data and preprocessing were used? Why evaluate on newstest2015 with WMT14 training? Can you reproduce bpe2bpe and char2char baselines under identical settings?

- Beam search: What beam size at the character level? How do you handle interaction between word boundaries and beam pruning? Any instability compared to word-level beam search?

- Morphology: Can you provide a quantitative segmentation evaluation or at least statistics on OOV handling and generalization to unseen morphological forms?

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem with a sensible hierarchical design and provides practical training ideas. The empirical results are promising and the qualitative analyses are insightful. However, the methodological novelty is modest, and the experimental validation lacks rigor and ablations necessary to isolate contributions and ensure fair comparisons. With stronger controlled experiments and clearer formulation/analysis of the word encoder and HGRU components, this could be a solid contribution. As it stands, it partially meets ACL criteria on significance and soundness but falls short on clarity and rigor of evaluation.
