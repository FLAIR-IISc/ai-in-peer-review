Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that introduces (i) a character-based word encoder intended to learn morpheme-like units via a weighted combination of character-level RNN states, and (ii) a hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” character-level GRU (HGRU) that conditions on word-level decoder states at word boundaries. The authors argue this keeps encoder sequence lengths close to word-level models while decoding at character level, improving OOV handling and efficiency. Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En report BLEU scores competitive with prior character/subword models and show qualitative evidence for morphology learning (PCA visualizations, morpheme boundary energies, anecdotal robustness to misspellings/nonce words).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A character-based word encoder that aggregates character RNN states via learned position-wise weights (intraword attention without normalization), claimed to capture morphemes and improve word representations.
2) A hierarchical character decoder (HGRU) that resets its state using word-level decoder outputs at delimiters, plus a batching trick (R-matrix unfolding) to align word-level outputs with character time steps for efficient training.
3) An empirical study showing competitive BLEU to prior character-level systems with smaller model size and some qualitative evidence of morphology induction and robustness to non-dictionary forms.

Strengths
1) Sensible architectural goal: The hierarchical encoder/decoder design is well-motivated by the desire to keep sequence lengths manageable on the source side while reaping character-level benefits on the target side (open vocabulary, robustness). The settable-state character decoder and the batching/unfolding trick address practical training difficulties common in char-level NMT.

2) Competitive results with smaller models: The reported BLEU for En–Fr and (to a lesser extent) En–Cs/Cs–En appears broadly competitive with prior char/subword systems while using fewer parameters (~54M vs 69–76M), suggesting a favorable accuracy/size trade-off.

3) Qualitative morphology analysis: The PCA plots contrasting the proposed word encoder against a plain char-RNN encoder, the boundary “energy” visualizations, and the segmentation examples (e.g., “consum-er-s”, “foot-ball”) provide intuitive qualitative evidence that the encoder attends to morpheme-like structure.

4) Practical robustness claims: The examples translating misspellings/nonce forms (e.g., “convenienter”) illustrate the expected benefits of character-level decoding. Though anecdotal, they align with the community’s observations and are useful to highlight.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty is limited and not clearly positioned: Char-to-word encoders and hierarchical decoders have prior art (e.g., Ling et al. 2015a/b; Serban et al. 2015; Luong & Manning 2016; Lee et al. 2016). The proposed word encoder reduces to intraword attention over character RNN states; this is not clearly differentiated from existing char-to-word models beyond using unnormalized weights combined with tanh. The “settable-state” HGRU is essentially resetting the initial hidden state at word boundaries, a common tactic; the batching/R-matrix trick is an implementation detail. A clearer novelty claim and ablation contrasting against a softmax-normalized intraword attention or standard C2W encoders is missing.

2) Experimental comparability and clarity: There are inconsistencies and ambiguities:
- Datasets: En–Fr mentions WMT’14 training but evaluates on newstest2015; En–Cs/Cs–En use WMT’15. Preprocessing is “only tokenization”; others often include truecasing/normalization/BPE merges—comparability to cited numbers is questionable.
- Table 1 is hard to parse (misaligned columns, duplicated DCNMT lines “1 ~ 7 days” vs “~2.8 ~19”, unclear what those mean). Beam size, length normalization, early stopping, dropout, and regularization are unspecified. No statistical significance tests.
- “120 most frequent characters cover nearly 100%” is vague; the use of <unk> for characters undermines the “solves OOV” claim.

3) Methodological clarity issues in the decoder:
- The HGRU equations are difficult to follow (indices j/t, notation of sit vs st, q/z/g, φ vs tanh, and gating by a_t). It’s not entirely clear how training/inference handle a_t, especially within-beam synchronization when delimiters differ across hypotheses.
- Search is said to be “directly on character level,” yet the first-level word decoder must advance only when a delimiter is predicted. How are attention/context and s_{t+1} computed for partial beams without a completed word? How is pruning affected? These details are critical for reproducibility and speed claims.

4) Morphology learning claims lack quantitative evaluation: The evidence is almost entirely qualitative (PCA plots, visual “energies”). There is no evaluation against a morphological segmentation benchmark (boundary F1) or intrinsic metrics (e.g., alignment of peaks with morpheme boundaries), nor ablations (e.g., compare to last-state C2W, CNN encoders, normalized intra-word attention). Claims that “learning morphology speeds up training” are not substantiated via controlled ablations.

5) Writing, formatting, and references: Numerous typos and grammatical errors (“effect” vs “affect”, “monolinguality and crosslinguality”), inconsistent equation numbering, undefined variables, and figures not fully described hamper readability. References are malformed (years marked “?”, incomplete venue info), which is below ACL standards. Some claims are overstated (e.g., “solved the OOV issue”).

Questions to authors
1) Datasets and preprocessing: Please clarify training/evaluation splits for each language pair (WMT year, dev/test sets), tokenization/truecasing, filtering (max length in words/chars), and whether you detokenize before BLEU. Are your BLEU numbers case-sensitive? What beam size and length normalization did you use?

2) Fairness of comparisons: For each baseline in Table 1, confirm that the data and preprocessing match yours. If not, please rerun/approximate baselines under your setup or caveat the comparison. Provide statistical significance of BLEU improvements.

3) Decoder/search details: Precisely describe the beam search over characters with word-level resets. How do you handle beams that hypothesize delimiters at different times? When is s_{t+1} computed, and how does attention update? What is the computational complexity vs char2char baselines?

4) Word encoder ablations: Compare your weighted-sum encoder to (a) last-state C2W, (b) CNN encoders, and (c) normalized intraword attention (softmax over positions). Report BLEU and training time to substantiate the morphology/speed claims.

5) Morphology evaluation: Provide quantitative evaluation of learned boundaries (e.g., boundary F1 on a morphologically annotated dataset or Morpho Challenge data). Also report the proportion of boundary “peaks” aligning with known morpheme boundaries.

6) Robustness evaluation: Beyond anecdotes, can you systematically test misspellings/nonce words (e.g., synthetic perturbations) and report accuracy/BLEU degradation vs baselines?

7) Reproducibility: List missing hyperparameters (dropout, weight decay, layer norms, gradient clipping), initialization, training epochs to convergence, and release code if possible.
