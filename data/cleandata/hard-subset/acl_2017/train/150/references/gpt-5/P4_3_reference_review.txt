Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that composes source words from characters and decodes target text at the character level via a hierarchical decoder. The core components are:
1) A morpheme-aware character-to-word encoder: two RNNs over characters produce (i) prefix representations and (ii) position-wise scalar “energies” that weight these prefixes to form a word vector. This aims to learn morphemes and their combination rules, reducing source sequence length while avoiding large word vocabularies.
2) A hierarchical character decoder with a settable-state GRU (HGRU): a first-level (word-level) decoder produces a state per target word; a second-level GRU generates characters until a delimiter, then resets its state to the next word-level state. An “unfolding” matrix maps word-level states to character time steps to enable efficient batched training.
3) Empirical evaluation on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En shows competitive BLEU to recent character/subword baselines with fewer parameters, plus qualitative analyses suggesting morphology learning and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Effective hierarchical design for character-level NMT: The model keeps source sequence lengths comparable to word-based models by composing words from characters, and it removes large target softmaxes by decoding characters. This addresses OOVs while preserving encoder efficiency.
- Practical training mechanism: The settable-state HGRU and the unfolding matrix provide a clear, implementable way to align word-level and character-level time steps in batch training, avoiding multiple passes. The reported fast convergence (notably strong performance after 1 epoch) is promising.
- Competitive results with compact models: On En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, DCNMT achieves BLEU that is competitive with character-level baselines and better than a BPE baseline cited, with fewer parameters (~54M). This suggests a favorable accuracy/complexity trade-off.
- Qualitative evidence for morphology: Visualizations (energy over characters, PCA projections) show plausible morpheme boundary sensitivity and grouping of derivational pairs (e.g., capability/capable). The ability to translate misspellings and nonce words (e.g., “convenienter”) is consistent with the claimed advantages of character-level modeling.
- End-to-end simplicity: All components are standard RNN/GRU/LSTM building blocks, with no reliance on external segmenters or BPE, making the approach conceptually straightforward and potentially broadly applicable.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Character-to-word encoders and hierarchical decoders are established (e.g., C2W, hybrid word-char NMT, char2char). The proposed word encoder’s energy-weighted sum and the HGRU reset are incremental extensions of attention/gating ideas. The paper would benefit from clearer positioning and ablations to isolate what is truly new and impactful.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair comparisons: Several baselines are taken from prior papers with different preprocessing, BLEU computation, and hardware. Training time comparisons in “days” across different setups are not directly comparable. The claim that character-level models outperform subword models is not convincingly established under controlled conditions.
- Missing ablations: Only a limited CNMT vs DCNMT comparison is shown (on En–Fr). There is no systematic assessment of (i) the word encoder versus a CNN or a simple last-state encoder under the same setup, (ii) the benefit of the HGRU versus a standard GRU with resets, or (iii) the effect of normalizing the energy weights (softmax) versus the ad hoc tanh choice.
- Technical clarity and rigor: Key equations and notations are underspecified or ambiguous (e.g., w_t defined as exp(aff(h_t)) but “normalized” via tanh at the end; reset equation with a_t and s_it; definition and computation of a_t during training/inference). Decoding/search details (beam width, length penalties, delimiter handling errors) are not documented. Without code or more precise descriptions, reproducibility is limited.
- Morphology learning claim weakly validated: The evidence is qualitative (PCA plots, example segmentations). No quantitative evaluation on morphological segmentation benchmarks or alignment to gold morpheme boundaries is provided, making the morphology-learner claim tentative.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder weighting: Why are the position weights w_t not normalized (e.g., via softmax)? Did you compare softmax-normalized attention, sigmoid gates, or learned convex combinations against the current tanh-based formulation? How sensitive are BLEU and convergence to this choice?
- HGRU specification: Please provide a complete, unambiguous definition of the HGRU, including variable shapes, the exact reset/update when a_t=1, and the meaning of s_it. How is the auxiliary delimiter sequence a_t derived during training? At inference, how do you mitigate cascading errors if delimiters are predicted too early/late?
- Decoding/search: Do you perform beam search at the character level only? What beam size and length normalization are used? Are there constraints to ensure well-formed word boundaries or to limit pathological long/short words?
- Ablations under identical settings: 
  • Replace the proposed word encoder with (i) last-state char RNN and (ii) CNN-based char encoder; report BLEU and training speed on En–Cs/Cs–En. 
  • Replace HGRU with a standard GRU plus explicit state reset; report impact. 
  • Remove the target word-encoder feedback r_{Y_{t-1}}; report impact.
- Efficiency reporting: Please provide tokens/sec or characters/sec and wall-clock per epoch on your hardware for all compared variants. Training “days” extracted from other papers are not directly comparable.
- Morphology evaluation: Can you evaluate segmentation quality against a benchmark (e.g., MorphoChallenge, CELEX, or English derivational suffix lists) and report precision/recall/F1 for boundary detection?
- Target feedback computation: How do you construct r_{Y_{t-1}} online at inference time? Do you re-encode the generated character sequence after each delimiter, and what is the additional cost? Does this feedback materially improve BLEU?

Overall, the paper presents a coherent hierarchical character-level NMT design with practical training tricks and competitive results. However, clearer technical specification, stronger controlled ablations, and fairer comparisons are needed to substantiate the novelty and the morphology-learning claim.
