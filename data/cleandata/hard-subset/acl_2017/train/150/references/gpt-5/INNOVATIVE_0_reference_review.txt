Summary
This paper proposes a fully character-level NMT model with a hierarchical architecture that explicitly targets morphology. It introduces (a) a character-to-word encoder that composes word representations by weighting prefix-level character RNN states with a second, independent (bi)RNN, and (b) a hierarchical decoder: a word-level RNN whose outputs initialize a modified GRU (HGRU) that generates target words as character sequences until a delimiter is produced. An unfolding trick allows efficient batched training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr and En↔Cs show BLEU comparable to strong character-level baselines and better than BPE in some settings, with qualitative analyses suggesting the encoder learns morpheme boundaries and handles misspellings/nonce forms.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A morphology-aware character-to-word encoder that decouples composition (prefix representations) from selection (per-position weights), effectively an attention over character prefixes to form word embeddings without supervision or BPE.
2) A hierarchical word-to-character decoder with a “settable-state” GRU and an efficient unfolding/batching scheme for training, enabling character-level generation conditioned on word-level states.
3) Evidence (qualitative and some quantitative) that such a model can learn morpheme-like boundaries and improves robustness to OOV/misspellings, while remaining competitive in BLEU and relatively efficient to train.

Strengths
- Novel encoder idea targeted at morphology: The dual-RNN word encoder that learns per-prefix weights is a bold departure from standard char-CNN/LSTM encoders. The visualization of “energy” peaks at morpheme boundaries and PCA plots showing semantically sensible clusters support the claim that the model captures morphology, and the nonce-word examples (e.g., “convenienter”) illustrate generalization beyond the training lexicon.
- End-to-end character-level model with practical decoding: The hierarchical decoder avoids massive softmaxes and aligns word-level planning with character-level realization. The HGRU and unfolding trick address a known bottleneck in batching/efficiency for character decoders, which is valuable in practice.
- Competitive results with smaller models and fewer epochs: On En–Fr and En↔Cs, BLEU matches or exceeds BPE baselines and is in the ballpark of prior char-level systems, with favorable model size and early-epoch performance. This suggests the architecture confers real learning efficiency, consistent with the morphology motivation.
- Robustness to OOV and misspellings: The qualitative case studies convincingly highlight a practical benefit of fully character-level modeling that word/BPE systems often struggle with. This functionality is important for real-world MT.
- Clear architectural direction: The paper articulates a coherent multi-level view (char → subword → word → sentence → word → char) that could inspire further work beyond MT.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient ablations and controls: The paper compares to a variant (CNMT) but lacks systematic ablations to isolate the gains from (i) the weighted word encoder vs a standard BiLSTM/CNN char encoder, (ii) normalized vs unnormalized weights (softmax vs tanh), (iii) the hierarchical decoder vs a plain char decoder, and (iv) the unfolding trick’s contribution. Without these, it’s hard to attribute the improvements to specific innovations.
- Clarity and technical rigor issues: The word-encoder weighting uses exp(aff(ht)) followed by tanh and omits normalization; this is unusual for attention-like mechanisms and may saturate. The HGRU equations are difficult to parse, and several implementation choices (delimiter definition, handling of spaces, how attention over encoder states is integrated) are underspecified. This hampers reproducibility and theoretical understanding.
- Evaluation comparability: BLEU numbers are drawn from different sources with potentially different preprocessing, beam sizes, and training budgets. Claims about training time and efficiency (epochs/days) are not controlled across systems/hardware. A unified training/evaluation protocol is needed to substantiate efficiency claims.
- “Open vocabulary” caveat: Limiting the character set to 120 and mapping the rest to <unk> undermines the open-vocabulary claim, especially for Czech with diacritics. The paper does not quantify coverage, unknown-character rates, or impact on BLEU.
- Morphology analysis is largely anecdotal: While the visualizations are appealing, there is no quantitative evaluation of morpheme boundary detection (e.g., against annotated segmentations) or controlled robustness benchmarks on OOV/misspellings. This weakens the central morphology-learning claim.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder: Why not use a normalized attention (e.g., softmax over positions)? Did you try it, and how did it compare to tanh-weighting? How sensitive is performance to the choice of the second RNN (BiRNN vs uniRNN) and to the use of prefix vs bidirectional character features?
- Decoder: How exactly are delimiters defined in the target (spaces, special token)? Are spaces part of the character vocabulary, and are they predicted? Does HGRU state reset only on delimiters, or can it be learned to continue/reset? Any evidence that the reset operation helps?
- Unfolding/batching: Can you provide pseudo-code or complexity analysis for the unfolding matrix approach? What is the measured training speedup vs a naïve approach and vs Luong & Manning’s two-pass training, on the same hardware?
- Coverage: What fraction of training/test characters fall outside the 120-character set per language, and how does that affect BLEU and qualitative behavior?
- Ablations: Can you add comparisons against (i) a standard BiLSTM/CNN char-to-word encoder, (ii) your architecture but with normalized attention weights, and (iii) a single-level char decoder with the same encoder, all trained under the same setup?
- Evaluation protocol: Please report consistent tokenization/casing, beam size, and significance tests, and, if possible, re-train a strong BPE baseline under your setup for a clean comparison.

Overall, I find the central idea—a morphology-aware character-to-word encoder coupled with a practical hierarchical decoder—novel and promising. Despite clarity and evaluation shortcomings, the conceptual contribution is strong and could open interesting directions. With addressed clarifications and added ablations, I would be inclined to support acceptance.
