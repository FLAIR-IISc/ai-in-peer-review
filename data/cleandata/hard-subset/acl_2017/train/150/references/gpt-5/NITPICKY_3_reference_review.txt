Summary and contributions
- Proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) with (i) a character-to-word encoder that computes a word representation as a weighted sum over character-level RNN states, where weights are produced by a separate (bi)RNN; and (ii) a hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” HGRU that generates characters conditioned on word-level decoder outputs and resets on delimiters.
- Presents a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to align fewer word-level outputs with longer character sequences for efficient training.
- Provides qualitative evidence that the encoder learns morpheme-like segments and demonstrates robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.

Strengths
- Tackle open-vocabulary NMT at the character level while keeping encoder sequence lengths close to word-based models via a word encoder. This is a practical design point with potential efficiency advantages compared to full character encoders of the source.
- The idea of decoupling “content” (forward character RNN) from “weighting” (BiRNN producing per-position energies) is a sensible architectural bias for morphology and is related to attention over characters within words; qualitative plots suggest it focuses on boundaries.
- The hierarchical character decoder with a state-reset mechanism is a useful engineering contribution for batched training; the unfolding trick is interesting and potentially generalizable to other hierarchical sequence models.
- BLEU results are competitive with other char-level systems on WMT En–Fr/En–Cs/Cs–En while reportedly using fewer parameters. Training time appears shorter than some prior purely character models.
- The error-robustness examples (misspellings, nonce morphology) illustrate a genuine advantage of character decoding over word/BPE, and the qualitative visualization is informative.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited relative to prior work. The encoder is essentially attention/gating over character states to build a word embedding; similar ideas exist in char-aware word encoders (e.g., attention or convolution + pooling). The hierarchical decoder with character generation conditioned on word-level states closely resembles prior hybrid/char decoders (Ling et al., 2015; Luong & Manning, 2016), and the “settable state” HGRU resembles resetting hidden states at boundaries. The paper does not clearly differentiate theoretical or empirical advantages over these.
- Experimental rigor and comparability are insufficient. Baselines are pulled from different papers with heterogeneous preprocessing, training budgets, and test sets. The paper says En–Fr uses WMT’14 data but evaluates on newstest2015; En–Cs/Cs–En use WMT’15. Tokenization, truecasing, BLEU variant (case-sensitive vs not), and detokenization for character outputs are not specified. Beam size, length penalty, and stopping criteria for character-level beam search are missing. No statistical significance tests. The reported training days/epochs are difficult to interpret and not directly comparable to cited works. No code is provided.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics. The claimed benefits hinge on (i) the morphology-aware word encoder vs a standard char-RNN word encoder; (ii) tanh-based unnormalized weights vs softmax; (iii) the hierarchical decoder vs a flat char decoder. There are no quantitative ablations isolating these components. The only ablation (CNMT) uses a different word encoder but the definition is vague and results are not systematically compared. No analysis of the unfolding trick’s effect.
- Technical clarity issues. The HGRU equations are opaque/inconsistent: Eq. (2) overwrites g_{t-1} with a mixture using itself and s_t (indexing unclear), φ is undefined, dimensionalities are not specified, and indexing with superscript j is idiosyncratic. The unfolding matrix R example is dimensionally inconsistent and the construction is not precisely defined for batching or backprop. The choice to replace normalization with tanh on weights is unusual; negative weights imply subtractive contributions with no justification. The delimiter definition and handling during training/inference are unclear. Attention mechanism details (Bahdanau-style?) and alignment parameters are under-specified.
- Claims about morphology learning are largely anecdotal. PCA plots and a few examples are not a rigorous evaluation. No quantitative segmentation accuracy vs gold morpheme boundaries or comparison to BPE/BPE-dropout. The robustness experiments are cherry-picked; no controlled noisy-input evaluation is provided.

Questions to authors
- Please formalize the HGRU precisely. Define φ, dimensions of all matrices/vectors, and correct the indexing. How is the state “set” at delimiters implemented in practice during training and inference?
- Provide a mathematically consistent definition of the unfolding matrix R with shapes and a general algorithm for creating it per batch. How do you handle varying numbers of words per sentence? Is R fixed to gold delimiters during training? How does backprop flow to the first-level decoder through R?
- Why use tanh on the per-character weights instead of a softmax or sigmoid gating? Did you try softmax normalization over positions within a word? Please include an ablation.
- What exactly are the delimiter tokens and how are they injected/learned? During inference, how do you choose when to stop word generation vs continue characters? What is the beam size and search strategy (single beam over characters or nested beams)? Any length normalization?
- Preprocessing/evaluation: case sensitivity, tokenization, detokenization of character outputs, BLEU script and options. Are the reported BLEU scores directly comparable to each baseline? If not, please re-run strong baselines (BPE and char2char) under your setup.
- Training details: dropout, layer norm, weight tying, attention type, learning rate schedule beyond one decay, gradient clipping, early stopping, hardware. Please report wall-clock throughput (tokens/sec or characters/sec).
- Ablations: (i) remove the BiRNN weighting and use last-state or mean pooling; (ii) softmax vs tanh weights; (iii) hierarchical decoder vs flat character decoder; (iv) impact of the unfolding trick on speed/quality.

Additional comments
- References are severely malformed (years missing/question marks, duplicated authors, stray LaTeX artifacts). Please fix all citations and ensure correct venue and year.
- Numerous typos/grammar: “automati- cally,” “effect”→“affect,” “monolinguality and crosslinguality,” “It is much difficult,” “settable,” “concluant,” inconsistent capitalization of En-Cs, etc.
- Figures/Tables are hard to follow; Table 1 columns are misaligned and contain unexplained symbols; include clear headers, consistent units, and complete captions. Define CNMT explicitly.
- Character set of 120 “covers nearly 100%” is vague; report exact coverage and handling of accents/diacritics for Czech/French; mapping rare chars to <unk> undermines “open vocabulary.”
- Consider adding quantitative morphology evaluation (e.g., MorphoChallenge, SIGMORPHON) and synthetic noise robustness tests.
