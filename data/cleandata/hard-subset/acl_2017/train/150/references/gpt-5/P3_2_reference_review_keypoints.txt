- Proposes a hierarchical character-based NMT with a morphology-aware word encoder (char→subword-like→word→sentence) and a hierarchical decoder (word→character) using a resettable HGRU.  
- Introduces an efficiency-oriented batching/unfolding trick to train the hierarchical decoder in a single pass over character sequences.  
- Shows competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En and qualitative evidence that the encoder learns morpheme-like units and is robust to misspellings/nonce words.  
- Strength: naturally handles OOVs, misspellings, and nonce forms while keeping encoder sequences short.  
- Strength: simple, practical hierarchical decoding that conditions character generation on word-level states with an efficient batching trick.  
- Strength: attempts explicit morphological modeling via a two-RNN word encoder with per-position scalar “energies”; qualitative analyses suggest morpheme alignment.  
- Strength: competitive results with a relatively modest parameter count (~54M) and reasonable training time.  
- Strength: clear motivation addressing vocabulary/OOV limits of word-level NMT and long-sequence issues of pure char models.  
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to prior hierarchical/char models; HGRU is essentially GRU with external resets and the encoder resembles existing compositional char-to-word methods.  
- Weakness: methodological clarity problems—unnormalized per-position weights, opaque HGRU notation, and reliance on word boundary supervision reduce interpretability and claims of “pure” character modeling.  
- Weakness: empirical comparisons mix results from different setups; missing critical training/hyperparameter details and no statistical significance testing.  
- Weakness: morphology evaluation is qualitative and anecdotal; lacks quantitative segmentation or morphologically focused error analysis.  
- Weakness: reproducibility and presentation issues—references/figures/tables and implementation details (code, tokenization, char inventory handling) are incomplete; fixed 120-char list contradicts open-vocabulary claim.  
- Requests ablation on weight normalization (softmax/gating) and comparisons to C2W/CNN encoders and hierarchical decoders.  
- Asks for clarification on delimiter handling, applicability to languages without whitespace, unfolding matrix costs, and empirical speed comparisons to strong char baselines.  
- Requests in-house matched baselines (BPE and char), beam/decoding details, regularization settings, and statistical significance reporting.  
- Requests quantitative morphology evaluations (segmentation F1, correlation of energies with morpheme boundaries) and robustness analysis to character set size and <unk> mapping.  
- Overall recommendation: promising practical design and batching trick but incremental novelty and insufficient empirical/methodological evidence; leans toward rejection pending stronger baselines, clearer ablations, and quantitative morphology results.
