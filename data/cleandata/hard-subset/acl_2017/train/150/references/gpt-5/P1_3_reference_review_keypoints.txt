- Proposes DCNMT: a hierarchical character-level NMT that keeps word-level encoder lengths while enabling open-vocabulary decoding.  
- Character-to-word encoder: two char-level RNNs produce prefix representations and position-dependent weights; weighted sum yields a word embedding.  
- Hierarchical decoder: word-level decoder emits per-word states; a character HGRU resets to word state at delimiters to generate characters.  
- Introduces an unfolding/batching trick to align per-word states with per-character steps during training.  
- Empirically competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En with smaller parameter counts and fewer training days on a single GPU.  
- Qualitative analyses show the encoder appears to capture morphemic structure and handle misspellings/nonce words.  
- Contribution: practical hierarchical char-level architecture with an efficient batching mechanism (engineering benefit).  
- Contribution: novel char-to-word encoder that softly segments words and composes morpheme-like units (methodological).  
- Contribution: empirical evidence of competitive performance, faster convergence, and OOV/misspelling robustness (experimental).  
- Strength: clear motivation—addresses OOVs and misspellings while avoiding long-sequence issues of full char encoders.  
- Strength: neat, implementable HGRU/state-setting and unfolding matrix trick that reduces training cost despite multiple RNNs.  
- Strength: competitive benchmark results with both one-epoch and final outcome reporting to show faster convergence.  
- Strength: PCA and energy-profile qualitative evidence correlating with morpheme boundaries and correct handling of noisy inputs.  
- Strength: ablation comparing CNMT vs DCNMT attributes gains to the morphology-aware word encoder.  
- Weakness: encoder idea similar to prior char-to-word methods (BiLSTM/CNN/attention); novelty and clarity of weighted-sum formulation are limited.  
- Weakness: unclear weight formulation—wt usage, lack of normalization, and odd application of tanh raise stability/interpretability questions.  
- Weakness: evaluation fairness issues—pooled baselines with differing preprocessing, length limits, hardware, and no significance testing.  
- Weakness: limited quantitative validation of morphology learning—mostly anecdotal analyses, no gold-segmentation or boundary F1.  
- Weakness: reliance on explicit whitespace boundaries and a top-120 character shortlist limits transfer to non-whitespace languages and true open-vocab.  
- Weakness: missing ablations isolating the hierarchical decoder’s contribution versus the word encoder (e.g., simpler char decoder comparisons).  
- Question: are weights wt used without normalization, how do you avoid exploding sums, and did you try softmax attention over characters?  
- Question: at test time are delimiters generated by the char decoder or forced, and how is beam search organized across levels?  
- Question: how would the model handle languages without spaces—can delimiters be learned as latent variables?  
- Question: why choose 120 characters per language and what fraction of chars map to <unk>; impact on diacritics/noisy text?  
- Question: report tokens/sec and decoding speed vs BPE baselines on same hardware; memory footprint impact of unfolding trick?  
- Additional: proofread math (indices/equations) and clarify tanh/energy role.  
- Additional: provide code or pseudocode for the batching/unfolding mechanism and beam search to aid reproducibility.  
- Additional: report BLEU with standard WMT tokenization/case, include significance tests, and add ablation table (normalized vs unnormalized weights; with/without HGRU reset; different char limits).
