Summary and main contributions (my view)
- The paper proposes a hierarchical character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) with (a) a character-to-word encoder that aggregates prefix-level character RNN states using weights produced by a second (bi-)RNN (“intra-word attention”-like pooling), and (b) a two-level decoder where a word-level RNN produces states that initialize a character-level GRU (HGRU) which generates a character string until a delimiter, with a batching trick (“unfolding matrix”) to train efficiently.
- It presents empirical results on WMT14 En–Fr and WMT15 En–Cs/Cs–En showing BLEU competitive with prior character/subword systems and reports smaller model size and shorter training time than some prior character-level systems. It also provides qualitative analyses: robustness to misspellings/nonce words and visualizations suggesting learned morphology.
- Methodologically, the most distinctive ideas are the decoupled intra-word weighting mechanism in the encoder and the practical batching trick for hierarchical character decoding.

Strengths
- Addresses a real pain point of word-level NMT (OOV/rare forms) while avoiding very long character sequences on the encoder side. The hierarchical design is sensible: encode near word granularity; decode characters to preserve open-vocabulary behavior.
- Competitive results on non-trivial benchmarks, including a morphologically rich language (Czech). On Cs–En, DCNMT matches prior char-level results; on En–Cs it narrows the gap to stronger character baselines. Model size (~54M) is modest.
- Efficiency-oriented engineering. The HGRU with settable state and the unfolding/broadcasting trick are pragmatic contributions for batching and speed (particularly compared to two-pass hybrid training in prior work). The “days/epochs” reports, while imperfect, suggest the method is practical.
- Qualitative evidence of morphological generalization. The misspelling/nonce examples (e.g., “convenienter”) are convincing case studies for character-level decoding. Encoder visualizations (PCA and energy plots) are consistent with intra-word segmentation behavior.
- End-to-end character decoding with attention and without explicit external segmentation is attractive for deployment and multilingual extensibility.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to prior work. The encoder is essentially an attention-like pooling over character RNN states (cf. C2W/BiLSTM/CNN encoders), with the new aspect being decoupling the representation and weighting RNNs; the decoder is a hierarchical character decoder with state resets very close to earlier hierarchical decoders/HRNNs (Ling et al., 2015; Chung et al., 2016a). The batching trick, while useful, is an implementation detail rather than a core modeling advance. The paper should more clearly position novelty against C2W, CNN-char encoders, and Hierarchical RNNs.
- Fairness and rigor of evaluation. Many comparisons use numbers copied from different papers with different codebases, hardware, and hyperparameters (and even different maximum lengths). “Training days” are not directly comparable. No statistical significance tests. The stronger contemporary character baselines (e.g., Lee et al.) are not reimplemented under the same setup. This weakens claims about efficiency and superiority.
- Open-vocabulary claim is undermined by character shortlist. Mapping all but the 120 most frequent characters to <unk> contradicts “open vocabulary,” especially for Czech with accented letters. The paper does not report test-set OOV character rates or the impact of <unk> on BLEU.
- Missing ablations and analysis of core design choices. The encoder weighting uses unnormalized exp(aff(h_t)) and tanh on the sum, which is nonstandard; there is no ablation versus softmax-normalized attention or versus simpler pooling (last state, mean/max, CNN). Similarly, the benefit of HGRU vs a standard GRU with explicit reset is not quantified. The CNMT comparison (ordinary RNN word encoder) is helpful but insufficient to isolate which components matter most.
- Decoding/search details are underspecified. The paper states it searches at the character level and advances the word-level decoder on delimiter prediction, but does not describe how beams are managed across the two levels, what beam sizes are used, or how the model copes with erroneous delimiter placement. Complexity and latency implications are not discussed.
- Clarity and reproducibility. The write-up has numerous typos and some confusing equations/descriptions (e.g., weight normalization; attention details; training R definition). Important hyperparameters are missing (dropout, gradient clipping, beam width), and code is not provided. Figures/tables are hard to parse; references are inconsistently formatted.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Encoder weighting: Why choose unnormalized exp(aff(h_t)) with tanh over standard softmax attention? Please provide an ablation comparing softmax vs your choice and report BLEU and training stability.
- Character vocabulary: What is the exact character set per language and the test-set OOV character rate? How often is <unk> generated at test time, and what is its effect on BLEU, especially for Czech?
- Decoding/search: What beam sizes are used at the character level? How is the two-level beam organized when delimiters are hypothesized at different times across beams? Any heuristic penalties for too-short/too-long words?
- Ablations: Can you report (i) your model with a standard BiLSTM or CNN word encoder (no weighting RNN), (ii) standard GRU in the char decoder with manual resets, (iii) DCNMT without the target-side word encoder feedback, all trained under your setup? This would clarify which parts drive gains.
- Efficiency: Please report tokens/sec or chars/sec and GPU model to make speed claims comparable. How sensitive is training to the maximum character length cap?

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem and presents a sensible, practically useful architecture, with solid qualitative evidence and competitive BLEU. However, novelty over prior hierarchical/character NMT is limited, and the empirical evaluation (fairness, ablations, and decoding details) is not yet rigorous enough for a strong acceptance. With clarified comparisons and stronger ablations, this could be a meaningful contribution; in its current form I lean borderline reject.
