Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a hierarchical, deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) designed to capture morphology while retaining training efficiency. The key components are: (i) a “word encoder” that composes character sequences into word representations via two RNNs—one producing prefix representations and another producing scalar “energies” over character positions—which are combined into a weighted sum; (ii) a hierarchical decoder with a word-level RNN driving a character-level “HGRU” that can reset its hidden state to the word-level state at delimiters, with an unfolding trick to enable efficient batched training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show BLEU competitive with recent character/subword models and qualitative analyses suggesting learned morpheme-like segmentations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical character-to-word encoder and word-to-character decoder design that aims to learn morpheme-like units without explicit segmentation and supports efficient training via a settable-state GRU and unfolding for batching.
2) Empirical results showing competitive BLEU with lower parameter count and faster training than some prior character models, plus qualitative evidence of morphology learning and robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.
3) A practical recipe for character-level decoding conditioned on word-level states that avoids dual passes or complex alignment supervision.

Strengths
1) Clear architectural motivation and practical engineering to make character-level NMT trainable and efficient. The HGRU with explicit state resets at delimiters and the unfolding matrix R are simple, effective ideas that address a known bottleneck in hierarchical char decoders. The paper convincingly argues for training efficiency (fewer parameters and days) relative to several prior char-level baselines.

2) Competitive translation quality. On En–Fr, DCNMT surpasses a BPE baseline and matches or approaches strong character models; on Cs–En it is on par with character baselines. These results suggest the approach is viable across morphologically poor and rich settings.

3) Qualitative morphology analysis. The weight/“energy” plots peaking at plausible morpheme boundaries and the PCA projections that group paradigm-related words provide intuitive evidence that the encoder learns reusable subword structure. The misspelling/nonce examples (e.g., “convenienter”) illustrate the practical benefits of open-vocabulary character modeling.

4) End-to-end training without auxiliary supervision. Unlike earlier work that relies on layer-wise training or attention supervision for characters, the proposed model trains end-to-end and integrates character, word, and sentence levels coherently.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty relative to prior character-aware encoders/decoders is incremental and not rigorously positioned. The word encoder is close in spirit to C2W (Ling et al.) and CNN/RNN char encoders (Kim et al., Lee et al.); the decoder resembles prior hierarchical or hybrid character-word decoders (Luong & Manning). The proposed weighting of character-prefix representations via another RNN is interesting, but the paper does not clearly articulate why this specific mechanism is preferable to existing composition functions (e.g., CNN+highway, attention over characters), nor provide ablations against them.

2) Mathematical and algorithmic clarity. Several definitions are confusing or inconsistent:
- Weights w_t are defined as exp(aff(h_t)) yet “not normalized,” with tanh applied only after the weighted sum. This raises scale/gradient concerns and undermines the interpretation of w_t as an “energy” selector. A comparison to normalized (softmax) weighting or attention would be valuable.
- HGRU equations contain typos/notation ambiguities (e.g., sit, indices), and the role of a_t during training vs. inference is under-specified. It appears training uses gold delimiters; exposure bias at word boundaries is not discussed.
- The unfolding matrix R trick is sensible but not fully specified for variable-length batches (padding, masking, efficiency vs. gather operations).

3) Experimental comparability and reporting. Many baselines are taken from different papers under potentially different preprocessing, hardware, and stopping criteria. For En–Fr, using WMT14 training but testing on newstest2015 is nonstandard; BLEU calculation details (tokenization, case, beam size) are not specified; no statistical significance testing or variance across runs is reported. The character vocabulary is capped at 120 symbols with <unk> mapping, which weakens the open-vocabulary claim, especially for Czech with diacritics; coverage statistics by language are missing.

4) Limited quantitative analysis of morphology and robustness. The morphology learning claim rests on qualitative plots and a handful of examples. There is no intrinsic evaluation (e.g., unsupervised segmentation metrics) or controlled OOV/misspelling robustness study (e.g., accuracy vs. edit distance, nonce productivity tests), and no comparison to BPE segmentations.

5) Ablations and component contributions are absent. It is unclear how much each component contributes: word encoder vs. standard char CNN/RNN; HGRU resets vs. a simpler char decoder fed with repeated word-level context; normalization of weights; importance of target-side word-encoder feedback. Without ablations, it’s hard to attribute gains.

Questions to authors
1) Word encoder weighting: Why not normalize w_t with softmax (i.e., attention over character positions)? Did you compare exp-only vs. softmax vs. sigmoid/tanh gating? How sensitive are results to this choice?

2) Character set and <unk>: What exact character inventories were used for En, Cs, Fr? What fraction of training and test characters were mapped to <unk> per language? How does performance degrade if <unk> appears on source/target?

3) Decoding details: What beam sizes (character-level) were used? How are word boundaries handled in beam search (synchronization across hypotheses at delimiters)? Any heuristics to prevent overly long/short words?

4) Training: Do you use gold delimiters (a_t) during training and predicted delimiters at test time? Did you observe boundary exposure bias or train with scheduled sampling? How do you batch sequences with different word/character lengths efficiently?

5) Ablations: Can you report results for (a) replacing the proposed word encoder with a CNN+highway char encoder, (b) using normalized attention over characters, (c) removing target-side word-encoder feedback, and (d) a flat character decoder with the same parameter budget?

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated attempt to make character-level NMT practical by learning morphology-aware word representations and leveraging a hierarchical decoder with efficient batching. The empirical results are promising and the qualitative analyses are compelling. However, the methodological novelty is moderate, several technical details are unclear, and the evaluation lacks rigorous, comparable baselines and ablations. With clarifications and stronger experimental evidence (especially ablations and robustness studies), this could be a solid contribution. In its current form, I lean toward borderline accept.
