Summary
This paper proposes DCNMT, a deep character-level NMT model that explicitly learns morphology and decodes characters efficiently. The key ideas are: (i) a novel word encoder that composes a word from characters via two decoupled RNNs—one producing per-position character-composed states and another producing per-position “energy” weights—to learn morpheme-like units and how they combine; (ii) a hierarchical decoder where a word-level RNN produces word states that condition a character-level GRU (HGRU) which “resets” at predicted delimiters, along with a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to make training efficient. Results on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show BLEU competitive with prior char-level models, smaller model size, and faster convergence; qualitative analyses suggest the encoder learns morphological structure and better handles misspellings/nonce words.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A morphology-aware character-to-word encoder that uses per-character energies learned by a separate RNN to weight subword representations, yielding meaningful word embeddings without explicit segmentation.
- A hierarchical decoder with a settable-state HGRU and a batching/unfolding mechanism, enabling end-to-end character decoding conditioned on word-level context with improved training efficiency.
- Empirical evidence that the approach is competitive in BLEU while offering practical advantages (faster convergence, smaller model, robustness to misspellings/nonce words), plus qualitative morphology analyses.

Strengths
- Novel modeling idea for morphology: The decoupled two-RNN word encoder that assigns learned “energies” to positions is a fresh and elegant way to induce morpheme-like structure. The analysis (energy peaks at morpheme boundaries, PCA plots) is compelling and aligns with linguistic intuition.
- Efficient character decoding: The HGRU with resettable state and the unfolding/batching trick are pragmatic contributions that address a known bottleneck in char-level NMT training, avoiding two-pass decoding and enabling competitive training speed on a single GPU.
- Competitive performance with smaller models: BLEU is on par with strong character-level baselines (slightly below the best on En–Cs, comparable on Cs–En, and better than BPE on En–Fr), while using fewer parameters, and converging quickly (notably strong scores after one epoch).
- Qualitative benefits beyond BLEU: Demonstrations on misspellings and nonce morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”) highlight an important advantage of character-level modeling that word/BPE systems typically struggle with.
- Clear direction-setting: The proposed hierarchy (char → subword → word → sentence → target word → target char) offers a coherent blueprint that could generalize to other sequence tasks where morphology matters.

Weaknesses
- Limited and mostly qualitative validation of “learning morphology”: The paper shows nice visualizations and a few segmentations, but lacks a quantitative intrinsic evaluation (e.g., morpheme boundary F1 on a standard dataset) or a systematic extrinsic study isolating morphology’s impact (e.g., controlled inflectional/derivational generalization tests).
- Incomplete ablations: While CNMT vs. DCNMT suggests the word encoder helps, there is no direct comparison against strong alternative character-to-word encoders (e.g., CNN character encoders, Ling’s C2W) within the same framework, nor ablations of the energy weighting (e.g., softmax-normalized vs. unnormalized exp, or removing the second RNN).
- Clarity and correctness of some technical details: The weighting scheme uses wt = exp(aff(ht)) without normalization and then tanh on the weighted sum, which is unusual; stability/scale control is not well justified. The HGRU equations and the delimiter mechanism are described with confusing notation, and the training/inference boundary handling could be clearer.
- “Open vocabulary” caveat: Restricting to the top 120 characters and mapping others to <unk> undermines the open-vocab claim, especially for diacritics-rich languages (e.g., Czech). It would help to report coverage on dev/test and analyze the impact of this design choice.
- Fairness of comparisons and reporting: Training time/epochs comparisons rely on differing implementations/hardware choices from prior work. More standardized speed metrics (tokens/sec, decoding latency) and beam configurations would strengthen the efficiency claims. BLEU improvements are competitive but not state of the art on all pairs.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Word encoder:
  - Why choose unnormalized exp(aff(ht)) weights followed by tanh on the sum? Did you try softmax-normalized weights or alternative activations? Please provide an ablation.
  - How sensitive is performance to the dimensionalities of the two RNNs and the choice of BiRNN vs. uni?
  - Could you quantitatively evaluate learned segmentations against a morphological gold standard?

- Hierarchical decoder:
  - Please clarify the HGRU equations and indexing (Eqns. 2–6). In particular, how exactly is the reset performed at delimiters during training and decoding? What is the delimiter symbol and how is it predicted at test time?
  - Can you report decoding speed (chars/sec or words/sec) and training throughput, compared against a strong char2char baseline under the same hardware and beam size?

- Data and preprocessing:
  - What fraction of dev/test characters are mapped to <unk> for En–Fr and En–Cs? How do results change with a larger character set?
  - Are the BPE baselines trained/tuned under identical tokenization, data filtering, and architecture depth as your sentence encoder/attention?

- Generalization:
  - Have you tried languages with richer morphology (e.g., Turkish, Finnish) to further test the morphology claim?
  - Could you release code and pretrained models to facilitate reproducibility?

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite imperfect evaluation and some clarity gaps, the paper introduces a genuinely novel morphology-aware word encoder and a practical hierarchical decoding/training mechanism. The approach is bold, conceptually interesting, and shows competitive results with additional qualitative benefits. With clarifications and a bit more quantitative analysis of morphology, this could be an impactful contribution.
