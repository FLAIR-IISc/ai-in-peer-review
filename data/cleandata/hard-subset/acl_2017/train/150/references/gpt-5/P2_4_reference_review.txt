Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) with two key components: (1) a word encoder that composes character sequences into word representations by weighting prefix RNN states with per-character “energies” computed by a second (bi)RNN, purportedly learning morphemes; (2) a hierarchical decoder with a “word-level” decoder producing states that seed a character-level GRU variant (HGRU) to generate target characters until a delimiter. A batching trick (“unfolding” matrix) aligns word-level states to character positions to train efficiently.
- Experiments on En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En (WMT) report BLEU competitive with prior character-level systems and better than a BPE baseline, with fewer parameters and faster convergence. Qualitative analyses (PCA plots, energy peaks) suggest learned morphology, and examples show robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.

Main contributions as I see them
1) An implementation of a hierarchical char-level decoder that conditions a char-GRU on word-level states with an efficient batching/unfolding mechanism. This addresses a known training inefficiency in hybrid/char decoders.
2) A specific word encoder that linearly combines prefix RNN states with learned per-position weights from a BiRNN, aiming to discover morpheme-like units and improve word representations.
3) An empirical study showing competitive BLEU to strong character-level baselines and qualitative evidence of morphological awareness and OOV handling.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical pain point in character-level NMT training: The unfolding/batching solution to map fewer word-level states to more character positions is clear, simple to implement, and plausibly responsible for the reported faster convergence vs. prior char decoders. This is a useful engineering contribution for the community.
- Sensible architecture design: Keeping source encoding length near word-level via a learned word encoder while decoding at characters is a reasonable trade-off between open-vocabulary decoding and efficient source-side attention. The hierarchical decoder with explicit word-state resets matches linguistic structure and simplifies long-range character generation.
- Competitive results with a compact model: Reported BLEU on En–Cs and Cs–En are on par with or slightly better than other char models (Lee et al., Luong & Manning) with fewer parameters (~54M) and fewer epochs, suggesting good parameter efficiency.
- Qualitative morphological analysis: The PCA plots comparing an ordinary char-RNN word encoder vs. the proposed weighted-prefix encoder, energy peaks aligning with morpheme boundaries, and segmentation examples on PTB provide intuitive support that the encoder is learning meaningful subword structure. The robustness examples (misspellings, “convenienter”) illustrate a practical advantage of character-level decoding.
- End-to-end training without explicit segmentation or BPE: This is still desirable in morphologically rich settings; eliminating vocabulary truncation on the target side is valuable.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: The hierarchical decoder idea, character-conditioned word states, and char-based word encoders are well explored (C2W, char-CNN/LSTM encoders, hybrid word-char decoders). The HGRU is essentially a GRU with an externally set state at boundaries; the main innovation is the batching trick. The word encoder’s weighted sum of prefix states resembles a self-attention over character positions. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning vs. prior compositional char encoders and hierarchical decoders, with ablations showing why this specific formulation matters.
- Methodological clarity and correctness issues: Several equations and notations are confusing or inconsistent (e.g., wt = exp(aff(ht)) but later “instead of normalizing by sum we use tanh,” then tanh(sum wt rt) as the word representation; unclear how tanh replaces normalization; undefined φ; mixing indices j and vector notation; Eq. (2) uses gt−1 on both sides). The HGRU description lacks a precise, self-contained definition. Attention mechanism details (score function, normalization, multi-head?) are not specified beyond referencing Bahdanau. These issues reduce reproducibility and make it hard to assess soundness.
- Evaluation rigor and fairness: 
  • Data/preprocessing inconsistencies: The text mixes WMT14 and WMT15, but later says dev=newstest2013 and test=newstest2015 for both En–Fr and En–Cs; this deviates from standard En–Fr WMT14 setups and complicates comparison. It is not clear that all baselines in Table 1 use exactly the same corpora, filtering, and tokenization. 
  • Beam/search details (beam size, length normalization, char-level pruning) are missing. 
  • No statistical significance tests or variance across runs. 
  • The character set is truncated to the 120 most frequent characters, mapping the rest to <unk>, so the method is not fully open-vocabulary; the impact is not quantified.
- Insufficient ablations: Key claims (morphology learning, efficiency) are not backed by controlled ablations (e.g., replace the word encoder with a char-CNN or simple last-state encoder; softmax-normalized weights vs. tanh; remove unfolding trick; word-level vs. char-level decoding). It is thus unclear which component contributes what to BLEU and speed.
- Morphology claims are mostly anecdotal: PCA plots and a few “energy peak” visualizations are interesting but do not constitute a quantitative evaluation of morpheme discovery (e.g., segmentation F1 against a gold standard, or correlation with morphological families). The misspelling/nonce examples are cherry-picked; a systematic robustness evaluation is missing.

Questions to authors
- Data and comparability: Please specify exactly which parallel corpora, filtering, tokenization, and truecasing you used per language pair; confirm that all baselines in Table 1 were trained on the same data with the same preprocessing. Why choose newstest2015 for En–Fr rather than the standard WMT14 test? If different, how should we compare to published numbers?
- Decoding details: What beam size(s) and length normalization/scoring did you use at character level? Any heuristics to prevent overlong or undersegmented outputs? What is the delimiter character and how is it treated during search?
- Word encoder formulation: 
  • What is the precise definition of wt and the final combination? If wt = exp(aff(ht)), how does tanh “instead of normalizing” apply? Did you try softmax-normalized weights across positions? 
  • Please provide ablations comparing: (i) your weighted-prefix encoder vs. last-state char-RNN, (ii) char-CNN encoder, (iii) removing the BiRNN that computes ht.
- HGRU specification and efficiency: Provide a clear, standalone definition of HGRU with all gates and shapes; explain Eq. (2) where gt−1 appears on both sides. How much wall-clock speedup (tokens/sec) does the unfolding matrix yield vs. a naïve two-pass hybrid? Include decoding speed.
- Open vocabulary: With only the top 120 characters retained, how often does <unk> appear on dev/test? How sensitive is performance to this threshold? Can you handle scripts with larger character sets?
- Morphology evaluation: Can you provide a quantitative assessment of learned segmentation (e.g., against MorphoChallenge or UniMorph-derived boundaries) and/or families (nearest neighbors within derivational paradigms)?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate (incremental). 
- Technical quality: Mixed; ideas are reasonable, but mathematical presentation and experimental rigor need strengthening; ablations are missing.
- Significance: Moderate; useful engineering contribution and competitive char-level results.
- Clarity: Below average due to notation/typos and dataset ambiguity.

With clearer method specification, stronger ablations, and tightened, comparable evaluation, this would be a solid contribution. As is, it falls short on rigor despite promising ideas.
