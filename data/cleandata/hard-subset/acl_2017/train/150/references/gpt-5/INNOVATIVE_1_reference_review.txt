Summary and contributions
- A character-driven NMT architecture (DCNMT) that keeps word-length encodings on the source side while decoding at the character level via a hierarchical decoder. The key novelty is a learned, morphology-aware word encoder: two RNNs jointly compute (i) prefix-based character representations and (ii) position-wise weights (“energies”) to combine them into a word vector, aiming to discover morphemes without supervision.
- A hierarchical decoder using a “settable-state” HGRU that conditions a character-level generator on word-level decoder states and an efficient batching trick (an unfolding matrix) to align word- and character-time steps, enabling single-pass batched training.
- Empirical results on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En showing competitive BLEU vs prior character-level and subword baselines, faster training than some prior pure char-level decoders, and qualitative evidence of robustness to misspellings/nonce words and of learned morphology.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: unsupervised morphology-aware word encoder. The decoupling of (i) character prefix representations and (ii) position-specific weighting, learned end-to-end, is a neat idea distinct from C2W (Ling et al.) and char-CNN encoders (Kim et al.; Lee et al.). The qualitative analyses (energy peaks at morpheme boundaries; PCA neighborhoods; segmentations like “consum-er-s”) suggest the encoder captures morphemic structure.
- Hierarchical decoding with efficient batching. The HGRU “settable state” and the unfolding-matrix trick address a practical pain point of character-level decoders (complex batching/two-pass training). This is a useful engineering contribution likely to generalize beyond MT.
- Open-vocabulary and robustness benefits demonstrated. The ability to translate misspellings and nonce forms (e.g., “convenienter”) is compelling and directly tied to the paper’s central thesis of learning morphology. This is a meaningful user-facing advantage over word/BPE systems.
- Competitive accuracy with smaller models and fewer epochs. On En–Fr, DCNMT outperforms a BPE baseline and is within the range of contemporary char-level systems with fewer parameters/training days, supporting the claim that the proposed hierarchy makes char-level models more trainable.
- Clear, ambitious scope. The paper attempts a full character→subword→word→sentence→word→character hierarchy and demonstrates it at WMT scale, which is a bold step beyond small-scale morphology probes.

Weaknesses
- Limited and sometimes unclear evaluation methodology. Comparability across systems is hard to assess: maximum lengths, epochs, and wall-clock days differ by model and language pair; beam search settings, dropout, gradient clipping, and other regularization details are omitted; some referenced numbers are taken from other papers with differing setups (WMT14 vs WMT15, tokenization). Statistical significance is not reported. A unified reimplementation or at least a stronger controlled comparison would strengthen claims.
- Insufficient ablations. We see one variant (CNMT with ordinary RNN word encoder), but no systematic ablation to quantify contributions of (a) morphology-aware weighting vs simple pooling/last-state; (b) hierarchical vs flat char decoder; (c) HGRU vs standard GRU/LSTM; (d) the unfolding/batching trick’s speed/accuracy trade-offs. Given the multiple novel components, isolating their effects is important.
- Morphology evidence is mostly anecdotal. PCA plots and a few energy visualizations are suggestive but not rigorous. A quantitative segmentation evaluation (e.g., against MorphoChallenge, Morfessor baselines, or linguist-annotated segmentations) or probing tasks (e.g., inflectional paradigm consistency, analogy) would substantiate the “learning morphology” claim.
- Ambiguities and technical clarity issues. The weighting mechanism uses tanh on unnormalized energies; the rationale and stability properties are not explained, and the link to attention-like normalization is unclear. The HGRU equations and training signals around delimiters depend on ground-truth a_t; handling of errors at test time (early/late delimiters) and beam interaction isn’t discussed. The training/inference mismatch merits analysis.
- Open-vocabulary claim partly undercut by character set truncation. Mapping all non-top-120 characters to <unk> contradicts the strong OOV claim, especially for diacritics in Czech/French or punctuation/symbol-heavy inputs. Reporting performance sensitivity to character set size would clarify this trade-off.
- Presentation and reproducibility. References are incomplete/inconsistent; several typos; figures are critical but sparsely described. No code link. Important hyperparameters (beam size, length penalties, clipping) are missing, complicating replication.

Questions to authors
- Morphology encoder:
  - Why use tanh on unnormalized energies instead of a softmax or sigmoid gating? Did you try normalized attention over positions, and how did it compare?
  - Do the weights peak specifically at morpheme boundaries across languages? Can you provide a quantitative segmentation metric (even on a small annotated subset)?
- Decoder/HGRU:
  - How do you handle predicted delimiters that are too early/late during inference with beam search? Any observed failure modes?
  - What beam size and search constraints do you use at the character level? Is there a word-level beam as well?
  - Can you provide a micro-benchmark comparing training speed with/without the unfolding trick on identical hardware?
- Ablations:
  - How much BLEU comes from the source morphology encoder vs the hierarchical decoder? Can you report BLEU for: (i) standard char-CNN/biLSTM word encoder + your decoder; (ii) your encoder + standard char decoder?
  - Does replacing HGRU with a vanilla GRU (state reset via masking) change performance/speed?
- Data/processing:
  - What is the effect of expanding the character vocabulary beyond 120 (especially for Czech diacritics)? Any degradation/improvement?
  - Were byte-level models considered as a baseline?
- Robustness:
  - Beyond single-word misspellings, how does the system handle multi-error/noisy inputs (e.g., synthetic noise benchmarks)? Any quantitative robustness results?

Overall assessment
This is an ambitious, creative paper that proposes a fresh take on learning morphology in char-level NMT and a practical hierarchical decoding scheme. Despite evaluation and clarity gaps, the core ideas are novel and promising, and the qualitative demonstrations are compelling. With stronger ablations and more rigorous morphological evaluation, this could be impactful. I lean toward acceptance for its originality and potential to influence future character-level modeling.
