Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that: (1) composes word representations from characters via a novel word encoder that uses two RNNs—one to compute prefix representations and another to produce position-wise “energy” weights—intended to learn morphemes and their combination rules; and (2) decodes with a hierarchical, word-to-character decoder where a “settable-state” HGRU is initialized from a word-level decoder state and generates characters until a delimiter, with a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to enable efficient training.
- Empirically, the model is evaluated on En–Fr (WMT14 data, tested on newstest2015) and En–Cs/Cs–En (WMT15), and is reported to be competitive with prior character-based systems while being smaller and faster to train, with qualitative evidence of handling misspellings/nonce words and inducing morpheme-like segmentations.
- The paper argues that the morphology-aware encoder accelerates training and that the hierarchical decoder makes character-level decoding efficient without explicit subword segmentation.

Strengths
- Interesting encoder design to induce morphology: The two-RNN word encoder with position-wise weighting is a novel twist on character-to-word composition, distinct from pure CNN encoders and prior C2W models. The visualizations (PCA clusters and per-character “energy” peaks) are suggestive that the model learns morpheme-like boundaries and associates related derivational forms more closely than a plain RNN encoder.
- Practical hierarchical decoder and batching trick: The “settable-state” HGRU with a delimiter-triggered reset and the unfolding matrix R are concrete engineering contributions that address a real pain point in training hierarchical decoders efficiently in batch mode. This is useful beyond this paper’s specific architecture.
- Competitive BLEU with smaller model size and reasonable training time: Reported En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En BLEU are close to or better than cited character-level baselines while using fewer parameters (~54M vs ~69–76M) and training in markedly fewer days/epochs on a single GPU. The “after one epoch” numbers surpass a BPE baseline, supporting the claim that the encoder speeds up learning.
- Qualitative robustness to misspellings/nonce words: The examples where the model recovers misspellings and translates a synthetic comparative (“convenienter”) into an appropriate paraphrase are compelling, illustrating a concrete user-facing advantage of character-level generation.
- End-to-end character-level without BPE: Avoiding explicit subword segmentation and still achieving competitive performance is valuable, especially for domains where robust open-vocabulary handling is important.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and rigor of the method description: Key equations and mechanisms are underspecified or inconsistent. For the word encoder, w_t is first defined via exp(aff(h_t)) and later the text says “instead of normalizing… we use tanh,” but it is unclear what is actually used in r = tanh(sum w_t r_t) and how w_t is constrained. For the first-level decoder, Eq. (8) gives a linear combination (W1 c_t + W2 r_{Y_{t-1}} + W3 u_{t-1} + b) instead of a standard GRU/LSTM update, while the text calls it “similar to RNNsearch”; it is unclear how u_t is updated and how attention is computed. The exact HGRU update (Eqs. 2–6) uses nonstandard notation (q_t, z_t) and is hard to follow, and the role of the auxiliary sequence a_t presumes known delimiters. Overall, the paper needs a more precise, self-contained specification to be reproducible.
- Evaluation fairness and completeness: The comparisons mix datasets/test sets and settings (e.g., En–Fr on newstest2015 vs many prior works report newstest2014), and rely on results from different codebases/hardware without controlled re-implementations. Important baselines are missing or weakly tuned (e.g., strong BPE word-level or hybrid char–word models under the same encoder/decoder/optimizer configuration). No statistical significance tests are reported. Without controlled experiments, claims about being “simpler/smaller/faster” are hard to credit.
- Limited ablations: The paper does not isolate the contributions of (a) the morphology-aware encoder vs a CNN or a plain char-RNN; (b) HGRU vs a standard char-level decoder with resets; or (c) the unfolding trick. An ablation suite would substantiate which component drives the gains and the speedups.
- Morphology learning claims are mostly anecdotal: While the PCA plots and energy heatmaps are informative, there is no quantitative evaluation of induced segmentations (e.g., against a morphological analyzer) or downstream evidence (e.g., larger gains on morphologically rich languages, robustness to systematic morphological perturbations). Some shown segmentations (e.g., consum-er-s) and the reliance on whitespace delimiters raise questions about what is being learned beyond word-boundary cues.
- “Open vocabulary” claim is overstated: The model restricts the character set to the top 120 and maps others to <unk>, which undercuts the open-vocab claim, especially for accented scripts or noisy text. The prevalence of mapped characters and the impact on En–Cs are not quantified.
- Reporting and writing quality: There are typos, broken/misaligned table entries, incomplete references, and missing hyperparameters (beam size, dropout, gradient clipping, attention details). These hinder reproducibility and readability.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder weights: Do you normalize w_t (e.g., softmax) or use tanh(aff(h_t)) directly? Please provide the exact formula used in the final model and any constraints on w_t (e.g., non-negativity).
- First-level decoder: Is s_t produced by a GRU/LSTM update with attention as in Bahdanau et al., or by the linear combination in Eq. (8)? If the latter, how is u_t computed? Please detail the attention mechanism, layer counts, and where nonlinearity is applied.
- Delimiters and training: What tokens count as delimiters for the second-level decoder (spaces only, punctuation, EOS)? How is a_t derived during training and how is segmentation handled at inference when beam search explores multiple character paths?
- Unfolding matrix R: How is R computed during batched teacher forcing when target lengths differ? What is the complexity and memory overhead for long sequences?
- Baselines: Can you provide controlled baselines (BPE and hybrid word–char) trained in your codebase on the same data/splits, with the same encoder/decoder sizes and optimizer? Also report beam sizes and decode times to substantiate the efficiency claims.
- Morphology evaluation: Can you quantify the alignment between your induced segmentations and a morphological analyzer on English and Czech, and/or provide systematic robustness tests to morphological perturbations?

Additional comments
- Please include statistical significance tests and length-based BLEU breakdowns. Report OOV character rates given the 120-character vocabulary. Release code and pretrained models. Consider evaluating on additional morphologically rich languages (e.g., Turkish, Finnish) to better stress-test the morphology claims.
