Summary and main contributions
The paper proposes a character-centric NMT architecture (DCNMT) that aims to reap the benefits of character-level modeling while avoiding long input sequences and large softmaxes. The core ideas are:
- A word encoder that composes character sequences into word representations via two RNNs: one produces prefix representations over characters; a second (biRNN) produces position-wise “energies/weights” that select and combine morpheme-like units into a word vector.
- A hierarchical decoder: a “word-level” decoder whose outputs initialize a character-level decoder (HGRU) that generates the target characters until a delimiter is emitted; training uses an unfolding matrix so that the word-level outputs align with character time steps for efficient batching.
- Experiments on WMT14 En–Fr and WMT15 En↔Cs show competitive BLEU vs other character-level systems and better than a BPE baseline; qualitative analyses suggest the encoder learns morpheme boundaries and handles misspellings/nonce words.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Architectural: a word encoder that explicitly reweights character prefix states to approximate morpheme-aware composition, and a settable-state character decoder (HGRU) enabling efficient batch training of hierarchical character decoding.
2) Empirical: competitive BLEU with fewer parameters than some prior char-level systems, and qualitative evidence of morphological structure learning and robustness to misspellings.
3) Engineering: a practical batching strategy (unfolding matrix) to align word-level outputs with character-level steps.

Strengths
- Addresses a real pain point: open-vocabulary and morphology. The character-level decoder naturally handles OOV, misspellings, and nonce formations, demonstrated with clear qualitative examples (e.g., “convenienter”), and visualizations indicating the encoder captures morpheme boundaries. This is a meaningful advantage over purely word-level models.
- Thoughtful hierarchical design for efficiency. The settable-state HGRU and unfolding trick are novel engineering contributions that make character decoding compatible with batched training without requiring two separate passes (unlike some hybrid models). The reported parameter counts are modest (~54M), and the model reaches competitive BLEU in fewer days/epochs than some prior char-level systems.
- Competitive results across language pairs. On En–Fr and En↔Cs, DCNMT matches or improves upon strong character-level baselines and surpasses BPE baselines in the reported comparisons, supporting that the approach is viable across a morphologically poor language pair (En–Fr) and a rich one (Cs).
- Interpretability angle. The energy plots and PCA projections, while qualitative, are compelling and align with linguistic intuition about morphology and the importance of word-beginning and suffix characters.
- Clear articulation of training and inference procedures. The end-to-end joint training without special supervision for attention or layerwise pretraining is a practical advantage relative to some prior character-level NMT works.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning relative to prior char encoders is limited and under-evaluated. The word encoder is a weighted sum of prefix states with learned position weights (akin to attention over character positions). This is conceptually close to existing char-to-word encoders (BiLSTM/CNN+highway), and the novelty rests on the “two-RNN with energy weights” formulation. However, there is no direct, controlled comparison to strong char encoders (e.g., BiLSTM over chars, CNN-char), only to an “ordinary RNN taking the last state.” As such, it’s unclear whether gains come from the weighting mechanism vs simply using a standard BiLSTM/CNN encoder.
- Quantitative claims about morphology are not substantiated. While visualizations are nice, the paper lacks quantitative evaluation of morpheme boundary discovery (e.g., segmentation F1 on a morphology dataset) or downstream ablations tying BLEU gains to the proposed weighting. Without this, the “learning morphology” claim remains speculative.
- Clarity and correctness issues in equations and batching description. The definition of weights wt (exp(aff(ht))) and subsequent tanh applied to the sum is unusual; not normalizing weights (e.g., softmax) raises scale/stability questions. The HGRU equations and symbol shapes aren’t fully specified; the unfolding matrix R’s dimensions and multiplication example are confusing/inconsistent. The training supervision for the “word-level” decoder is implicitly driven by delimiter positions from whitespace tokenization, but this is not clearly formalized.
- Evaluation comparability and efficiency evidence are mixed. Results from other systems are taken from different papers with potentially different preprocessing, training budgets, and decoding settings, limiting the strength of cross-paper comparisons. Reported training times (e.g., 1 epoch in ~5–7 days on a Titan X) do not convincingly establish superior efficiency; there is no throughput (tokens/sec), memory, or wall-clock breakdown vs baselines run under the same environment.
- Missing important experimental details and analyses. No dropout/regularization details, gradient clipping, length normalization in beam search, or sensitivity to maximum length thresholds. No error analysis on when the hierarchical decoding fails (e.g., delimiter mistakes), nor discussion of non-whitespace scripts (Chinese/Japanese), where “word delimiter” is not explicit.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a controlled ablation comparing: (a) last-state RNN, (b) BiLSTM over characters (concatenate last forward/backward states), (c) CNN-char with highway, and (d) your weighted-prefix encoder, under identical settings? This is critical to isolate the contribution of the weighting mechanism.
- Why not use softmax-normalized weights over character positions? Did you observe instability with normalization, or does tanh over the unnormalized sum empirically work better? Please include training stability metrics and ablations.
- Can you clarify the unfolding matrix R with precise dimensions and a worked example matching shapes? Also, please specify the HGRU equations with tensor shapes and how sit is broadcast over character steps.
- How is the number of “word-level” steps determined during training and decoding? Is it strictly tied to gold whitespace segmentation during training? At inference, how do you prevent degenerate behaviors (too many/few delimiters), and do you use any constraints?
- Could you report speed (characters/sec), memory usage, and beam search details (beam size, length normalization) for your runs, and rerun a strong BiLSTM-char encoder baseline under the same setup?

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, and results are competitive; however, the paper needs stronger, controlled comparisons to standard char encoders, clearer and more rigorous presentation of the core mechanisms, and more robust quantitative evidence for the “learning morphology” claim and efficiency. With these addressed, the work would be much stronger.
