Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that aims to learn morphology and avoid large-vocabulary softmaxes. Two main architectural ideas are introduced:
1) A character-to-word encoder that computes a word embedding as a weighted sum of per-prefix RNN states, where the weights are predicted by a separate (bi-directional) RNN over characters. The authors interpret the weights as discovering morpheme boundaries and composition rules.
2) A hierarchical decoder composed of a word-level RNN and a character-level GRU variant (HGRU) whose hidden state can be “set” to the current word-level state at delimiter boundaries. An unfolding matrix is used to enable efficient batched training at character time steps.

Empirically, DCNMT is evaluated on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, showing competitive BLEU compared to subword-level and character-level baselines, with anecdotal demonstrations of robustness to misspellings/nonce words and qualitative morphology analysis.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A practical hierarchical character-level decoder with a resettable character GRU and a batching trick (unfolding matrix), enabling end-to-end training without layer-wise pretraining or attention supervision.
2) A morphology-aware character-to-word encoder that learns to weight character-prefix representations, presented as unsupervised discovery of morphemes.
3) An empirical study indicating that the approach is competitive with prior char/subword NMT and can translate misspellings/nonce forms, with training efficiency claims.

Strengths
1) Sensible hierarchical design with efficient training at the character level. The HGRU with reset-at-delimiter and the unfolding matrix are concrete engineering contributions that plausibly reduce the training complexity of character-level decoders and avoid dual forward passes seen in prior hybrid work.
2) Competitive BLEU across three language pairs with relatively compact models. On En–Fr, DCNMT improves over the C2W baseline and a BPE baseline; on En–Cs/Cs–En, it is comparable to strong character baselines (e.g., Lee et al., Luong & Manning). Reporting both epochs and wall-clock days is appreciated.
3) Clear qualitative evidence that the encoder captures morphological regularities. The PCA plots showing better clustering of related derivatives (e.g., -ability vs. base forms) and the “energy” peaks at plausible morpheme boundaries are informative; the examples with “convenienter” and misspellings illustrate the intended benefits of character modeling.
4) End-to-end character-level generation with a word-level scaffold. The system avoids a large softmax on words and still leverages word-level structure during decoding, striking a reasonable balance between pure char and word-level NMT.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty relative to prior work is not fully established. Hierarchical decoding with word-to-character transitions, compositional char-to-word encoders, and reset mechanisms have been explored (e.g., Ling et al. 2015a,b; Luong & Manning 2016; Lee et al. 2016). The proposed word encoder’s weighted sum over character prefixes resembles attention over positions; the precise novelty and why it materially improves over C2W/char-CNN need clearer positioning and stronger ablations.
2) Technical clarity and correctness issues. The HGRU equations (Eqns. 2–6) are ambiguous (e.g., Eqn. 2 reuses g_{t-1} on both sides; indices for s_i,t vs. s_t are unclear). The definition and use of the auxiliary delimiter sequence a_t in both training and inference need to be specified precisely. The unfolding matrix R is described informally; a rigorous description/pseudocode would help. The choice to not normalize weights w_t (exp followed by tanh of a sum) is unusual—stability and scale control are not discussed.
3) Evaluation and baseline comparability. The BPE baselines appear weaker than contemporary strong BPE systems; beam size, length penalty, and search details are not reported; hardware and training-time comparisons across systems may be inconsistent. CNMT vs. DCNMT is only shown for En–Fr, making it hard to isolate the contribution of the new word encoder across languages. No statistical significance tests, and no human evaluation.
4) Morphology claims are qualitative and limited. While the plots are suggestive, there is no quantitative evaluation of segmentation or morpheme discovery (e.g., against MorphoChallenge, Morfessor outputs, or measured boundary F1). The misspelling/nonce demonstrations are anecdotal; a controlled noise/OOV robustness evaluation would substantiate the claims.
5) Reproducibility gaps. Important details are missing (dropout, layer norm, initialization, gradient clipping, weight tying, beam width/penalties, vocabulary/case/diacritics handling). Mapping all non-listed characters to a single <unk> character contradicts the “open vocabulary” claim and may affect Czech performance; the actual coverage statistics per language should be reported.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Please clarify the HGRU update precisely: provide corrected equations with consistent indexing, a_t definition at training and test time, and a concise pseudocode of the training/inference loop showing when s_t is injected and how R is constructed/applied in batches.
- How is the target word encoder used at inference? Do you re-encode the just-generated characters between delimiters to obtain r_{Y_{t}} for Eqn. (8)? If so, how do you integrate this into beam search, and what is the computational overhead?
- Why choose unnormalized weights for the word encoder (exp + tanh after summation) rather than a softmax attention over positions? Did you compare alternatives (softmax, sigmoid gates, normalization by sum of weights)? Please provide quantitative ablations.
- Report full search settings (beam size, length/coverage penalties) and rerun stronger BPE baselines under matched settings if possible. Can you include CNMT vs. DCNMT on En–Cs and Cs–En to isolate the contribution of the proposed source word encoder?
- Provide quantitative morphology evaluation (e.g., boundary F1 against a morphological analyzer or Morfessor) and a controlled robustness evaluation to misspellings/nonce words (e.g., synthetic noise on dev/test).
- What exact character sets (size and composition) were used for Czech and French? What fraction of training/test characters were mapped to <unk> per language?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a reasonable hierarchical character-level NMT with an interesting encoder and an efficient character decoder. The idea is promising and results are competitive. However, the novelty relative to prior hierarchical and character-aware NMT is not clearly delineated; several technical descriptions are ambiguous; and the empirical validation lacks strong ablations and fair, matched baselines. I lean toward reject in the current form. With clarified math, stronger/matched baselines, quantitative morphology and robustness evaluations, and ablation studies, this work could make a solid contribution.
