Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that (a) learns word representations from characters via a “morphology-aware” word encoder and (b) decodes at the character level via a hierarchical decoder with a settable-state GRU (HGRU). The word encoder uses two RNNs: one to compute prefix states over characters (rt) and a BiRNN to compute positional weights (wt), then forms a weighted sum of rt to obtain a word representation. The decoder uses a word-level RNN to produce states st and a character-level HGRU that is reset to st at word boundaries, with a batching trick (“unfolding matrix”) to align word-level outputs to character sequences. Experiments on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show competitive BLEU, plus qualitative analyses suggesting learned morphemes and robustness to misspellings.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A two-RNN word encoder that computes a weighted combination of character-level prefix states to form a word representation, intended to capture morphology.
2) A hierarchical character decoder with a “settable-state” GRU and a batching/unfolding trick to train efficiently at character level.
3) Empirical evaluation across three language pairs and qualitative morphology/robustness analyses.

Strengths
- Architectural idea linking character-composed word representations to character-level decoding: The decoupling between composition (rt) and positional weighting (wt from a BiRNN) is interesting and plausibly useful for morphology. The hierarchical decoder that resets the char-level state at word boundaries is a reasonable design that can ease long-range credit assignment.
- Training efficiency considerations: The “unfolding matrix” to broadcast word-level states across character positions is a practical batching trick. Reported model size (~54M) is smaller than some char baselines (69–76M), with decent BLEU on En–Fr.
- Competitive performance on En–Fr: The reported 30.56 BLEU (newstest2015) compares favorably to the bpe2bpe baseline (29.70) cited, with fewer parameters. The early-epoch numbers are suggestive of faster learning.
- Qualitative analyses: PCA plots, energy profiles over characters, and the misspelling/nonce examples are intuitive and align with the claim that the model captures some morphological regularities and can handle OOV variants better than word models.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and correctness of the technical description: The HGRU equations are inconsistent/unintelligible. Eq. (2) defines gt−1 in terms of itself and sit, which is nonsensical as written; indices i/t are overloaded (si t vs st), and the auxiliary sequence a is not precisely tied to actual delimiter tokens. Bias terms are omitted, φ is undefined, and the notation mixes element-wise and matrix ops without sufficient care. The unfolding matrix R is described informally; dimensions and mapping are unclear. Overall, key components are too ambiguously specified to reimplement faithfully.
- Evaluation comparability and experimental rigor: The En–Fr setup states WMT’14 training but evaluates on newstest2015, while prior works commonly report newstest2014; cross-paper comparability is therefore questionable. Preprocessing is only “tokenization,” with no casing/normalization detail. Beam size, length penalties, and decoding specifics are missing. Training length limits (“Length”) differ across systems, making comparisons unfair. Statistical significance is not reported. Some baselines are from different data/settings (e.g., Luong & Manning’s char model); the paper acknowledges a 3-month training for that model but still compares BLEU directly.
- Character vocabulary choice undermines “open vocabulary” claims: The model uses only the top 120 characters per language and maps the rest to <unk>. This contradicts the claim of solving OOV at the character level, especially for Czech with many diacritics and for punctuation/symbol diversity. No analysis of how often <unk> characters occur, nor sensitivity to larger char inventories, is provided.
- Lack of ablations and diagnostics: There is no ablation to isolate the benefit of the morphology-weighted word encoder vs. a simpler char-to-word encoder (CNMT is mentioned but the table is confusing and doesn’t cleanly quantify the differential). The choice to not normalize weights wt (use tanh on a sum of exp weights) is asserted to “perform best” without evidence. No comparison to simpler alternatives (e.g., single RNN with self-attention over character positions, CNN encoders) on the same setup is provided.
- Morphology analysis is anecdotal and at times questionable: The PCA visualizations and energy plots are tiny and qualitative; segmentation examples include non-words (“everyway”), and “consumers” → “consum-er-s” is not compelling evidence of systematic morphology discovery. The text admits needing “explicit segmentation indicating the boundary of words,” which runs counter to claims of automatic subword learning. There is no quantitative morphological evaluation (e.g., MorphoChallenge, accuracy of morpheme boundary detection).
- Formatting, references, and numerous minor errors: Many typos and grammar issues (e.g., “conclusive/unconclusive,” “It is much difficult,” “start-of-the-art”). References are malformed (“Bahdanau et al.,? 2015”), missing years, and inconsistent. Table 1 has garbled citations and duplicated/ambiguous entries (“Sennrich et al., )”; CNMT lines). Notation inconsistencies (Yt vs yt), inconsistent delimiter tokens (<unk> vs UNK), equation numbering and cross-referencing errors (Eqn. (8) used to define sit). These issues hinder readability and confidence.

Questions to authors
1) Please provide a precise, complete specification of HGRU (all equations, shapes, activation φ, biases), and correct Eq. (2). How exactly is the state set at boundaries during training and decoding?
2) How is the delimiter defined in training? Is it whitespace or a learned token? How do you handle punctuation around word boundaries?
3) Detail the unfolding matrix R: exact construction, dimensions, and how it maps the Ty word-level outputs to T character steps in batched computation.
4) Ablations: (a) normalized vs unnormalized weights wt; (b) single-RNN vs two-RNN word encoder; (c) replace the BiRNN weight generator with simple positional parameters; (d) character CNN vs your encoder. Please report BLEU and training speed on the same setup.
5) Data and preprocessing: confirm exact training sets (WMT’14 vs ’15) for En–Fr; casing, normalization, tokenization, and filtering. Report beam size, length normalization, and any coverage penalties.
6) Character inventory: why limit to 120 characters? What fraction of characters are mapped to <unk> in train/dev/test, especially for Czech? Does performance degrade if you include the full Unicode ranges needed?
7) Reproducibility: Will you release code and scripts? Without the exact HGRU and batching details, reproduction seems difficult.
8) For the “misspelling” robustness, can you provide a systematic evaluation (e.g., controlled character perturbations at various rates) rather than a few hand-picked examples?

Overall, while the ideas are promising, the paper in its current form suffers from substantial clarity, methodological, and evaluation issues that prevent a confident assessment and likely reproducibility.
