Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that aims to learn morphology and alleviate OOV issues while keeping training efficient. The core ideas are:
1) A morphology-aware word encoder: two character-level RNNs compute (i) prefix representations over characters and (ii) position-wise weights (via a BiRNN + affine), which are combined into a word vector. This is intended to learn morpheme-like units and their composition.
2) A hierarchical decoder: a word-level decoder produces word states, and a character-level GRU variant (HGRU) generates characters, resetting its state at delimiters. An “unfolding” matrix enables efficient batching by repeating each word-level state across its character span.
3) Empirical results on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En showing BLEU competitive with prior character-level models and generally superior to a BPE baseline, plus qualitative analyses suggesting morphology learning and robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.

Strengths
1) Practical engineering for efficient char-level decoding. The hierarchical decoder with a settable-state GRU and the batching/unfolding trick addresses a known pain point in character-level NMT (slow/unstable training). The paper substantiates efficiency with training-time comparisons (epochs/days) and a compact parameter budget (~54M), achieving competitive BLEU. This is a meaningful contribution for practitioners who have struggled to train fully character-level systems.

2) Competitive translation quality across language pairs. On En–Fr, the model outperforms a cited BPE baseline and approaches the strongest reported char-level results; on Cs–En it matches strong char baselines, and on En–Cs it is close to the best reported character model. The results support the claim that the approach is viable for both morphologically poor and rich languages without resorting to word vocabularies.

3) Qualitative evidence for morphology learning and OOV handling. The PCA analyses and “energy” visualizations suggest the encoder attends to morpheme boundaries (e.g., stems/suffixes), and the qualitative examples show robustness to misspellings and compositional nonce forms (e.g., convenient+er). While anecdotal, these case studies align with the model design and motivate its utility.

4) End-to-end character modeling without pretraining or auxiliary supervision. Unlike some earlier hierarchical/char models that required layer-wise training or auxiliary attention supervision, this system is trained end-to-end and integrates morphology learning directly into the encoder, which simplifies training and deployment.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior hierarchical/char-level NMT. The encoder resembles character-to-word composition with attention-like weighting over character positions; the decoder extends earlier hierarchical decoders (e.g., Ling et al., Luong & Manning) with a GRU that resets at boundaries and a batching trick. These are sensible design choices, but the conceptual advance is incremental. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning against closely related methods and a deeper analysis of what is fundamentally new (beyond engineering).

2) Incomplete and partly incomparable experimental baselines. Several baselines are taken from different papers with differing data/hyperparameters; En–Fr data description (WMT14 corpora) versus evaluation on newstest2015 is potentially inconsistent; beam sizes/decoding configs are not specified; no statistical significance is reported. A strong, controlled BPE baseline trained under identical settings is missing, as is a strong char-CNN/BiLSTM word-encoder baseline of similar capacity. These gaps make it difficult to isolate the contribution of the proposed components.

3) Missing ablations. The paper includes a “CNMT” variant with a simple word encoder, but does not ablate: (i) the morphology-aware weighting vs. a standard char BiLSTM/CNN word encoder; (ii) the HGRU vs. a standard conditional char decoder; (iii) the unfolding trick vs. naïve batching; (iv) weight normalization choices in the encoder (the unnormalized exp weights and final tanh are unconventional). Without ablations, it is unclear which parts drive the gains.

4) Morphology claims are largely anecdotal. The PCA and heatmaps are illustrative but subjective; there is no quantitative evaluation on morphological segmentation or boundary detection datasets, nor systematic analysis across many words. Given the paper’s central claim (learning morphology), a quantitative segmentation or derivational/inflectional generalization test would strengthen the case.

5) Clarity and reproducibility issues. Some equations and notations are confusing (e.g., weight computation uses exp(aff(ht)) but “instead of normalizing … we use tanh,” also tanh is used on the final sum; HGRU equations mix indices and gating variables with limited explanation). The construction of the auxiliary delimiter sequence a_t and delimiter/tokenization choices are under-specified, especially for languages with clitics. Decoding/search details, throughput (tokens/sec), and code availability are not provided, which limits reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the precise novelty of HGRU relative to prior hierarchical decoders and standard GRU/LSTM with conditional initialization/reset. Is the primary novelty the explicit state reset via a_t and the unfolding trick? Provide ablations contrasting HGRU with a standard char decoder initialized at word boundaries.
- How exactly are delimiters and the auxiliary sequence a_t defined and obtained during training? At test time, what mechanisms prevent pathological delimiter generation (too many/few word boundaries), and how sensitive is performance to delimiter errors?
- Can you report controlled experiments with: (a) a strong BPE baseline trained on the same data and infrastructure, (b) a char CNN or BiLSTM word encoder with similar parameter count, and (c) significance tests? Include beam size and decoding settings.
- Please provide a quantitative morphology evaluation (e.g., boundary F1 on a segmentation benchmark), or targeted generalization tests on derivation/inflection paradigms, to substantiate the morphology-learning claim.
- What is the runtime and memory overhead of the unfolding matrix R in practice (chars/sec, words/sec, peak GPU memory), compared to a standard char decoder?

Overall assessment
- Originality: moderate (incremental over prior hierarchical/char NMT).
- Quality: promising but needs stronger, controlled baselines and ablations; morphology evidence is qualitative.
- Clarity: mixed; main ideas are understandable, but some technical details are under-specified.
- Significance: moderate; practical efficiency improvements and competitive performance make it of interest.

I lean toward borderline accept/weak accept if the authors can address comparability and ablation concerns and clarify the technical details; otherwise borderline reject due to limited novelty and evaluation rigor.
