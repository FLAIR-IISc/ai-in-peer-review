Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that aims to learn morphology and avoid large word vocabularies. The key ideas are:
1) A word encoder that composes characters via two RNNs: one produces prefix representations and another produces position-wise “energies” to weight these prefixes, yielding a word embedding intended to capture morphemes and their combination rules.
2) A hierarchical decoder: a word-level decoder produces states that initialize a character-level “HGRU” which generates characters until a delimiter; a batching trick (unfolding matrix) enables efficient training with varying numbers of characters per word.
3) Empirical results on WMT’14 En–Fr and WMT’15 En–Cs/Cs–En show BLEU comparable to prior character-level models, with qualitative analyses suggesting the model learns morphology and handles misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary and robustness: Operating at the character level eliminates word-level OOVs and the qualitative examples demonstrate recovery from misspellings and plausible handling of nonce words (e.g., “convenienter”), which traditional word-level models mishandle.
- Practical hierarchical decoding: The HGRU with resettable state and the unfolding-matrix batching are implementation-aware contributions that can make character-level decoding tractable in batched training, a known pain point for hierarchical word/char models.
- Competitive BLEU with moderate model size: Reported BLEU on En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En is in the vicinity of contemporary character-level systems, with smaller parameter counts than some baselines, and seemingly faster convergence in early epochs.
- Qualitative morphology sensitivity: The intra-word weighting mechanism and PCA visualizations suggest the encoder distinguishes morphologically related forms better than a vanilla RNN word encoder, and it produces intuitive subword boundary energies on out-of-domain text.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over established approaches: Character-to-word encoders (C2W), CNN/RNN character encoders for words, and hierarchical/word-initialized character decoders are well-explored. The proposed intra-word weighting resembles self-attention over character positions; the HGRU reset is a variant of standard gating/resetting at word boundaries. The incremental differences are not convincingly isolated or theoretically motivated.
- Insufficiently controlled empirical comparisons: Many results are copied from different papers with differing datasets, preprocessing, length cutoffs, and training regimes. En–Fr uses WMT’14; En–Cs uses WMT’15. Beam sizes, training epochs, regularization, and hardware differ. Claims of efficiency (epochs/days) are not comparable across implementations, and there is no statistical significance testing.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics: The paper asserts the word encoder “learns morphology,” but provides only anecdotal visualizations. There is no quantitative subword segmentation evaluation (e.g., against Morfessor/BPE or gold morph boundaries), no ablation on normalization choice (tanh vs softmax), no study of the contribution of the target-side word encoder feedback, nor of the batching trick’s effect. CNMT vs DCNMT is reported only on En–Fr; more systematic ablations are needed across tasks.
- Methodological gaps and clarity issues: Key details are underspecified or unclear. The HGRU equations and notation are hard to follow; delimiter handling at inference vs teacher-forced training is not analyzed; beam search settings and character-level search complexity are omitted. The character set is capped at 120 frequent characters and everything else maps to <unk>, which weakens the “open vocabulary” claim (especially for morphologically rich languages and named entities). It is unclear how frequent test-time character OOVs are handled.
- Baseline coverage: The paper omits strong subword baselines trained under matched conditions (e.g., BPE with tuned merge operations and comparable parameter budgets), and does not compare to more recent architectures or training strategies that were state-of-the-art even at the time (e.g., better-regularized attentional RNNs). Human evaluation is absent.

Questions to authors
- Novelty and design choices: In what ways is your intra-word weighting different from a standard self-attention over character positions with softmax normalization? Did you try softmax normalization and other pooling schemes, and can you report ablations on En–Cs/Cs–En?
- Inference details: What beam sizes and pruning strategies are used at the character level? How do you prevent pathological long words or failure to emit delimiters? What is the runtime (tokens/sec) at inference vs strong BPE baselines?
- Delimiter handling: During training you use gold delimiters to reset the HGRU; at test time you rely on predicted delimiters. Did you observe error propagation from premature/late delimiters? Any mitigation (e.g., auxiliary loss, coverage on word boundaries)?
- Character vocabulary: Which characters are included in the 120-type set for each language? What is the fraction of test tokens containing an out-of-set character that maps to <unk>? How sensitive are results to this cap?
- Fair comparisons: For each baseline in Table 1, can you provide matched training settings (preprocessing, length limits, batch size, optimizer, learning rate schedule, GPU type) or retrain them under your pipeline? If not, can you qualify the efficiency claims and provide confidence intervals or paired bootstrap significance for BLEU?
- Ablations: Please provide (i) DCNMT with the word encoder replaced by a plain character RNN/CNN on all three language pairs; (ii) DCNMT without target-side word encoder feedback; (iii) DCNMT with and without the unfolding-matrix batching trick; and (iv) segmentation accuracy vs. a morphological analyzer or agreement with BPE merges.
- Error analysis: Do gains concentrate on morphologically rich phenomena (case, number, derivational morphology), OOV words, or rare types? A breakdown by frequency bins and morphological categories would substantiate the “learning morphology” claim.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, configurations, and scripts to reproduce Table 1 and the qualitative analyses?
