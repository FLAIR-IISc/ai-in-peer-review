Summary and main contributions
- Introduces a character-to-word encoder that explicitly targets morphology: a dual-RNN composition where one RNN produces per-position character states and another RNN produces position-wise “energies” that weight those states to form a word representation. This is essentially an intra-word attention mechanism that aims to discover morpheme boundaries and combination rules.
- Proposes a hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” GRU (HGRU) that conditions character-level generation on word-level decoder states, plus a batching/unfolding trick that makes training efficient without separate passes.
- Demonstrates competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En with a smaller model and fewer epochs than prior fully character-level approaches; provides qualitative evidence that the encoder captures morphology and can handle misspellings/nonce forms.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The word encoder’s decoupled “representation RNN” and “weighting RNN” is a clean and bold idea for learning morphology without explicit segmentation. Framing this as word-internal attention distinguishes it from C2W/char-CNN encoders and is a contribution of independent interest beyond MT.
- Hierarchical decoding that stays purely character-level at the surface while keeping a word-level planning signal is elegant. The HGRU with settable state and the unfolding matrix for efficient batching is a practical contribution addressing a real pain point in char-level NMT training.
- Strong efficiency story: Despite six RNNs, the reported parameter count is smaller than competing character models and training converges within a few epochs on a single GPU. The one-epoch results already surpass a BPE baseline in two directions, suggesting the morphology-aware encoder accelerates learning.
- Competitive quality: Results are broadly on par with state-of-the-art character-level systems for En–Fr and Cs–En, and better than a BPE baseline for En–Cs, while using a simpler/lighter architecture than some recent character models.
- Qualitative analyses align with the modeling intent: PCA plots show meaningful clustering by derivational morphology; energy peaks correlate with (pseudo-)morpheme boundaries; the model translates misspellings and nonce morphological variants (e.g., “convenienter”) plausibly—clear functional benefits of character modeling.

Weaknesses
- Validation of “learning morphology” is mostly qualitative. The energy-as-boundary claim would be much more convincing with a quantitative evaluation (e.g., morpheme boundary F1 on a standard dataset, or alignment to BPE/unsupervised segmentations), or probing tasks (e.g., inflectional/syntactic agreement under nonce forms).
- Comparability and fairness of MT results are under-specified. Several baselines are taken from prior work with different preprocessing, training time, and beam settings. Details like BPE merge counts, sentence/char length limits, dropout/regularization, and beam parameters are missing, making it hard to assess apples-to-apples comparisons or the true efficiency advantage.
- “Open vocabulary” is compromised by truncating to the top 120 characters and mapping the rest to <unk>. For multilingual training and many scripts, 120 characters will not “nearly cover 100%,” and even in Latin scripts it weakens the claim of OOV robustness. The paper should report the actual coverage fraction and failure cases, or relax this constraint.
- The HGRU and weighting scheme need stronger ablations. How does HGRU compare to a standard GRU/LSTM with explicit reset at delimiters? Does the tanh-based weighting outperform a softmax normalization consistently? The CNMT variant only swaps the source word encoder; there’s no ablation isolating the hierarchical decoder’s contribution.
- Clarity/rigor issues: Some notation is hard to follow (e.g., gating equations with indices), and the “energy” terminology is confusing given tanh (allowing negative weights) and no normalization. The delimiter handling is not fully specified. Several claims (e.g., decoder simplicity/efficiency vs. prior work) would benefit from measured throughput (tokens/sec) and memory footprints.

Questions to authors
- Morphology evaluation: Can you provide a quantitative assessment of the encoder’s induced segmentations (e.g., against MorphoChallenge or SIGMORPHON resources), or controlled probing tasks (e.g., predicting inflectional features from the word vector)?
- Ablations: 
  - Replace HGRU with a standard GRU/LSTM that resets at delimiters—what is the delta in BLEU and training speed?
  - Swap tanh weighting for softmax-normalized attention; report impacts on learning speed and accuracy.
  - Remove the target-side word encoder feedback rYt−1 to test its necessity.
- Character set: What exact fraction of training tokens are mapped to <unk> under the 120-character cap for each language? How does performance change when using full Unicode coverage or a larger alphabet?
- Decoding details: What beam sizes are used at the character level, and how do beams at word vs character levels trade off speed/quality? How sensitive are results to the delimiter choice and to length penalties?
- Fairness of comparisons: What BPE merge size and preprocessing were used to reproduce the bpe baselines? Can you run an internal bpe2bpe baseline with your pipeline to solidify the efficiency/quality comparison?

Overall, this paper proposes a fresh architectural idea for learning morphology within a char-based NMT encoder and a practical hierarchical decoder that makes end-to-end character decoding efficient. While the empirical validation is not exhaustive and some comparisons are loose, the conceptual contribution is significant and promising. With stronger ablations and a quantitative morphology study, this work would be a compelling addition to the literature.
