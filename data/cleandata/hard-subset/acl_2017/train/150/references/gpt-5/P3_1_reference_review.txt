Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model (DCNMT) that aims to learn morphology explicitly and to decode efficiently at the character level. The architecture has two main ideas:
1) A word encoder that composes a word representation from characters via two RNNs: one RNN builds prefix-based character states r_t; a second (biRNN) produces position-wise “energies” w_t used to weight r_t before summation. The intent is to learn morpheme-like units and their combination rules.
2) A hierarchical decoder: a “word-level” recurrent state s_t is produced at each target word position, and a second-level character decoder (HGRU) emits characters until a delimiter, with a reset mechanism that sets the HGRU state to s_t at word boundaries. An “unfolding” matrix lets them efficiently broadcast word-level states across character time steps in batch training.

Claims are that this model: (a) removes large softmax and OOV issues; (b) trains faster than prior character-only models; (c) yields competitive BLEU on WMT14 En–Fr and WMT15 En–Cs/Cs–En; and (d) exhibits morphology-aware behavior (PCA, energy visualizations, handling misspellings/nonce forms).

As I see it, the contributions are:
- A morphology-aware character-to-word encoder via attention-like weighting over character prefix states.
- A resettable character-level decoder with a batching trick (unfolding matrix) enabling efficient end-to-end training.
- Empirical evidence that the approach is competitive with strong character/subword baselines and may learn morpheme-like structure.

Strengths
- Architectural idea for morphology-aware word representations: The word encoder decouples representation of character prefixes (r_t) from position-wise gating (w_t). This is a plausible way to induce morpheme boundaries and compositionality without explicit segmentation and is conceptually cleaner than fixed BPE or pure last-state char-RNNs. The qualitative analyses (energy peaks at morpheme boundaries, PCA grouping by stems/suffixes) are consistent with learning morpheme-like features.
- Practical hierarchical character decoding: The HGRU with reset-on-delimiter and the unfolding trick is a reasonable, simple solution to the batching difficulty of conditional per-word character generation. Compared to prior hybrid models that required multiple passes (Luong & Manning), the proposed mechanism is straightforward to implement and seems to reduce wall-clock time.
- Competitive results with smaller models: On En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En, the BLEU scores are comparable to or better than BPE baselines and close to character-only models, while reporting fewer parameters and shorter training times (e.g., test BLEU 30.56 on En–Fr vs 29.70 for BPE). The observation that one-epoch DCNMT performance can already surpass BPE baselines suggests useful inductive bias from the morphology-aware encoder.
- Robustness to OOVs and misspellings: The qualitative examples (e.g., “convenienter”, misspellings) demonstrate the expected benefits of character-level decoding over word-level approaches in handling typographical variants and nonce formations. This is practically valuable, and the model does so without explicit post-hoc UNK replacement.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and rigor of the word encoder formulation: The weighting scheme is under-specified and unusual. The weights are defined as w_t = exp(aff(h_t)), but are not normalized (no softmax); instead tanh is applied after summation: r_word = tanh(Σ_t w_t r_t). This conflates scale with word length and makes the representation length-dependent. The rationale for eschewing normalization (“performs best”) is not analyzed, and ablations vs normalized attention (softmax) are missing. More generally, the two-RNN interaction (dimensions, gating stability, training dynamics) is not sufficiently detailed to be reproducible.
- Limited and uneven baselines/ablations: While there is a CNMT baseline (ordinary RNN word encoder), comparisons are incomplete across language pairs (CNMT only shown for En–Fr). There is no ablation isolating the contributions of (i) the word encoder vs (ii) the hierarchical decoder vs (iii) the unfolding trick. No comparison to a CNN-based char-to-word encoder (Kim et al., Lee et al.) or to a single-RNN attention-over-characters variant. Efficiency claims are based on total training days/epochs but lack per-step speed/throughput or controlled budgets.
- Evaluation details are underspecified and results presentation is messy: Table 1 is difficult to parse; beam sizes, length penalties, dropout/regularization, early stopping, and handling of long sequences (e.g., max 300/450 chars) are not clearly documented. It is unclear whether differences in maximum length constraints bias comparisons. Some cited numbers are from different setups or training budgets (e.g., Luong & Manning), complicating fairness.
- Generality concerns: The hierarchical decoder relies on explicit delimiters (spaces) to determine resets and to construct the auxiliary a_t sequence during training. This limits applicability to languages without whitespace tokenization (e.g., Chinese, Japanese) unless additional segmentation is introduced, which would undercut the segmentation-free claim. This limitation is not discussed.
- Analysis is mostly anecdotal: The morphology learning claim is supported by PCA plots and qualitative segmentations, but lacks a quantitative evaluation (e.g., morpheme boundary F1 against a morphology resource, or improvements on morphologically rich test slices). The misspelling/nonce examples are illustrative but small in scope.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder weighting: Why not normalize w_t with a softmax (attention)? Did you try it, and how did it compare? How do you mitigate the dependence of Σ_t w_t r_t on word length when w_t are unnormalized? Any gradient stability issues?
- Delimiter handling: What counts as a delimiter in training and decoding (space only)? How would the model extend to non-whitespace-segmented languages? Does the model ever predict erroneous delimiters, and how sensitive is performance to delimiter errors?
- Efficiency and training details: What beam sizes and decoding settings were used across systems? What regularization (dropout, weight decay) and layer norms, if any? Can you report tokens/sec or characters/sec and GPU utilization to substantiate efficiency claims?
- Ablations: Can you report results for (i) hierarchical decoder with a simpler char encoder (CNN or last-state RNN), (ii) word encoder with normalized attention, and (iii) character-only decoder without resets? This would clarify which component drives gains.
- Comparisons: For En–Cs/Cs–En, can you provide results for CNMT to isolate the benefit of the proposed word encoder? Also, can you align training budgets (epochs, max lengths) across baselines to ensure fairness?

Overall assessment
The paper presents an interesting and potentially useful combination of a morphology-aware character-to-word encoder and an efficient hierarchical character decoder, with competitive results and plausible qualitative analyses. However, the technical formulation (especially the word encoder weighting), clarity, and rigor of evaluation are lacking, and ablations are insufficient to attribute gains. I lean toward rejection in the current form; with clearer formulation, stronger controlled comparisons, and quantitative morphology evaluation, this could be a solid contribution.
