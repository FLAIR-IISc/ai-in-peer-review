Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a character-centric NMT architecture that aims to “learn morphology” while avoiding large softmaxes. The approach comprises (i) a word encoder that computes a weighted sum over character-level RNN states, where weights are produced by a separate (bi)RNN, intended to emphasize morpheme boundaries; and (ii) a hierarchical decoder with a word-level RNN conditioning a character-level GRU (HGRU) that resets on delimiters, plus a batching “unfolding” trick. Experiments on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En report BLEU competitive with prior character-level systems, with qualitative analyses (PCA plots, energy peaks at subword boundaries) and anecdotal robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A character-based word encoder with intra-word weighting (effectively attention) to emphasize morphemic structure.
2) A hierarchical decoder that conditions a character-level GRU on word-level states, with a practical batching “unfolding” mechanism.
3) Qualitative analyses suggesting the encoder captures subword boundaries; anecdotal examples of handling typos/nonce forms.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem (open vocabulary and morphology). Learning subword structure directly from characters is a natural and timely direction, especially for morphologically rich languages. The architectural attempt to keep encoder sequence lengths closer to word-level is reasonable.

- Practical decoding/training idea. The hierarchical decoder with resettable HGRU and the “unfolding” matrix for batching is a practical contribution that could ease training/decoding for character-level systems. Even if incremental, this is useful to practitioners.

- Competitive BLEU against prior char-level baselines. On En–Cs and Cs–En, the reported BLEU is comparable to earlier character-to-character systems, with smaller parameter counts and shorter training time in some settings.

- Qualitative evidence is suggestive. The PCA plots and energy profiles at putative morpheme boundaries are consistent with the claim that the model focuses on morphologically meaningful character spans. The typo/nonce examples are compelling at least anecdotally.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. The word encoder is essentially an intra-word attention over character RNN states, closely related to C2W (Ling et al.) and character-aware encoders (Kim et al., Lee et al.), which also learn to compose characters into word embeddings. The hierarchical decoder that resets a character-level decoder based on a word-level controller resembles prior hierarchical decoders (e.g., Luong & Manning’s hybrid; Serban et al.). The batching “unfolding” trick is an implementation detail rather than a conceptual advance. As a result, the methodological novelty appears incremental.

- Unclear and potentially problematic formulation details. The word encoder uses unnormalized weights wt = exp(aff(ht)) and then applies tanh to the sum. Without normalization, the representation is length-dependent and could saturate the tanh; no analysis or ablation is provided to justify this choice versus standard normalization (softmax) or even simple pooling. The HGRU equations are hard to follow, with insufficient detail about shapes, reset semantics, and stability. Decoding details (beam size at character level, word boundary handling, penalties) are missing.

- Evaluation lacks rigor and controlled baselines. Many baselines are copied from different papers with different preprocessing, data selection, maximum lengths, and training budgets, which undermines fair comparison. For example, BPE baselines are trained with different length limits (50 tokens) than the proposed system (up to 450 characters), and different corpora/splits appear to be mixed (WMT14 vs WMT15, newstest2014 vs 2015). The claim that “character-level models are better than subword-level models” is not supported by a controlled study using the same architecture, data, and training budget. No statistical significance testing, no chrF or COMET, and no human evaluation are provided.

- Insufficient ablations and diagnostics. Beyond comparing to a variant that uses an “ordinary RNN” word encoder (CNMT), there is no systematic ablation: normalized vs unnormalized weights, with/without the second RNN for weights, hierarchical decoder vs flat char decoder, effect of delimiter reset, effect of encoder depth. Efficiency claims are given in days/epochs without tokens-per-second or decode latency; no direct, controlled speed/memory comparison to strong char/BPE baselines.

- Overstated OOV claims and limited robustness evidence. The character vocabulary is truncated to 120 frequent characters with everything else mapped to <unk>, which contradicts the claim of solving OOV at character level. The typo/nonce evaluation consists of a few hand-picked examples; there is no systematic robustness benchmark (e.g., synthetic noise experiments), nor quantitative morphological segmentation evaluation. The cross-lingual generality to truly rich morphology (e.g., Turkish/Finnish/Arabic) is not tested.

- Reproducibility and clarity issues. Implementation relies on Theano/Blocks; no code is provided. Several sections are difficult to follow, with typos and inconsistent notation. Figures are essential to understanding but not sufficiently described. The choice of splits and datasets is not fully aligned with standard WMT practices for the reported years.

Questions to authors
1) Please provide a controlled comparison to a strong BPE-based baseline using the same encoder/decoder architecture, same data/preprocessing, same sentence/character length thresholds, and similar training budget. Do character models still outperform?

2) Why are the intra-word weights not normalized (softmax)? Did you try normalization or other pooling? Please include ablations showing BLEU and training stability for these alternatives.

3) What are the exact decoding settings at character level (beam size, length penalties, word-boundary handling)? How does runtime (tokens/sec, latency) compare to BPE and char2char baselines on identical hardware?

4) How large is the actual character coverage given the 120-character cap? What fraction of training/test characters map to <unk>, and how does performance vary with larger char sets?

5) Can you quantify morphology learning? For example, evaluate subword segmentation against a gold morphological segmentation or Morpho Challenge data, or measure generalization to synthetic inflections.

6) Please clarify data splits (WMT14 vs WMT15), maximum sequence lengths across systems, and whether any filtering was applied. Are BLEU numbers directly comparable across the cited baselines?

7) Could you release code and trained models to support reproducibility?

Overall, while the direction is interesting and the engineering is sensible, the contributions feel incremental and the empirical validation is not sufficiently controlled to support the stronger claims. I would encourage the authors to tighten the methodology and baselines, add ablations, and quantify the morphological benefits.
