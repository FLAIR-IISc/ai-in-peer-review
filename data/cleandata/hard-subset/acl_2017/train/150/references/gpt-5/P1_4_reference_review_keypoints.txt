- Paper proposes a fully character-based NMT with a hierarchical architecture: char → subword-like word vectors → sentence encoder → word-level decoder → char-level decoder.
- Key idea 1: morphology-aware word encoder using two RNNs—one for prefix character encodings and one to produce position-wise “energy” weights emphasizing morpheme boundaries.
- Key idea 2: hierarchical decoder with a word-level RNN and a resettable character-level GRU (HGRU) plus an unfolding matrix for efficient batched training.
- Empirical claim: competitive BLEU on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En, outperforming a reported BPE baseline in their setup and improving over a strong character baseline.
- Contribution 1: decoupling character composition and position weighting to learn morphemes and combination rules.
- Contribution 2: hierarchical character decoder with HGRU and an unfolding trick that avoids two-pass training.
- Contribution 3: empirical and qualitative evidence for learned morphology and robustness to misspellings and nonce words.
- Strength: clear motivation and coherent architecture addressing vocab size and softmax cost while keeping encoder lengths near word-level.
- Strength: novel encoder design using two RNNs to inject morphology awareness without explicit segmentation.
- Strength: practical unfolding matrix trick for efficient, implementation-friendly batched decoder training.
- Strength: reported competitive results with smaller parameter count and faster initial learning versus some baselines.
- Strength: qualitative analyses (PCA, energy visualizations) suggest morpheme-like boundary discovery and robustness to misspellings.
- Weakness: baselines and evaluation lack rigor and comparability (different preprocessing, hardware, training budgets; no significance testing).
- Weakness: limited ablations—insufficient experiments isolating encoder weighting, alternative encoders, and decoder design choices.
- Weakness: HGRU novelty and clarity are limited; equations and the role of delimiter indicator a_t are under-explained for inference.
- Weakness: morphology evidence is anecdotal with no quantitative segmentation or probing evaluations; modest gains on Czech.
- Weakness: reproducibility and presentation issues—missing preprocessing, delimiter definitions, hardware/time metrics, and formatting/typos.
- Questions for authors: what exactly are delimiters and handling of hyphens/clitics; impact on BLEU of delimiter choices?
- Question: why use tanh of exponentiated affine scores instead of softmax for position weights; were alternatives tried?
- Question: provide controlled ablations for weighted encoder vs char-CNN/BiLSTM, HGRU vs reset GRU, and unfolding trick vs two-pass training.
- Question: training specifics—beam size, length normalization, wall-clock epoch/time on GPU, sensitivity to max char length caps.
- Question: Czech setup—handling diacritics/rare chars within 120-char limit and evidence of morphology benefits vs strong BPE under identical training.
- Question: how often are delimiters predicted incorrectly at inference and are any post-edit heuristics used?
- Overall evaluation: coherent and promising approach with useful implementation trick and suggestive qualitative results, but lacks controlled experiments, stronger baselines, clarity, and reproducibility—leaning toward rejection in current form.
