- Paper proposes DCNMT: hierarchical char-level NMT with a weighted-prefix char→word encoder and a batched hierarchical decoder using a settable HGRU and an "unfolding matrix."
- Claimed contributions: efficient batched hierarchical char decoder, novel char→word composition via secondary RNN weights, and competitive BLEU without BPE plus anecdotal robustness to misspellings/OOVs.
- Strength: practical hierarchical design and efficient training that avoids two-pass decoding and reports wall-clock speed advantages.
- Strength: competitive BLEU with a modest parameter budget (~54M) and avoidance of large word softmaxes, appealing for constrained hardware.
- Strength: qualitative analyses (PCA, energy heatmaps, segmentation examples) and misspelling/OOV illustrations suggest learned subword/morphological patterns.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental—similar hierarchical decoding and per-word conditioning exist in prior work; main innovations are HGRU update and batching trick.
- Weakness: word encoder resembles attention over char positions and overlaps with existing char-aware encoders (CNN+highway, BiLSTM+attention); clearer comparisons/ablations are missing.
- Weakness: evaluation fairness concerns—mixed codebases/hardware, different max char length caps, unclear sentence truncation effects, and no statistical significance reported.
- Weakness: decoding details (beam width, length normalization), decoding speed/latency, and hierarchical beam search experiments are not reported.
- Weakness: "open-vocabulary" claim weakened by limiting to top 120 characters and mapping others to <unk>; coverage and impact are unquantified.
- Weakness: morphology claim rests on qualitative visuals without intrinsic evaluations (morph segmentation F1, probing) or rigorous ablation versus strong baselines; interpretation of weights as "energies" is problematic without normalization.
- Weakness: clarity and notation issues—underspecified equations, confusing statements about normalization/tanh, table formatting issues, incomplete refs, and typographical errors.
- Question (encoder): How does the weighted-prefix encoder compare (BLEU/speed) to BiLSTM+attention and CNN+highway baselines, and why not use normalized attention (softmax)?
- Question (decoder): What beam widths/length normalization were used; were hierarchical beam search or simpler GRU-with-reset baselines tried; what are decoding speeds?
- Question (data/preproc): What fraction of characters map to <unk> per language; how many sentences were dropped due to max char lengths and did baselines use the same truncation?
- Question (robustness/morphology): Can you provide systematic robustness tests to controlled misspellings/OOVs and evaluate induced segmentations against standard morphological datasets or probing tasks?
