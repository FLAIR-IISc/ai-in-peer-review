Summary
This paper proposes a fully character-based NMT system with a hierarchical architecture (character → subword-like word representation → sentence encoder → word-level decoder → character-level decoder). The key ideas are: (i) a morphology-aware word encoder that composes word vectors from characters using two RNNs—one producing prefix representations and another producing position-wise “energy” weights to emphasize morpheme boundaries; and (ii) a hierarchical decoder where a first-level (word-level) RNN produces a state per target word and a second-level “HGRU” generates the characters of that word until a delimiter, with an unfolding matrix enabling efficient batched training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En show BLEU competitive with prior character-level models and better than a reported BPE baseline in their setup. Qualitative analyses suggest the encoder identifies morpheme-like units and improves robustness to misspellings and nonce words.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A morphology-aware character-to-word encoder that decouples character composition and position weighting, aiming to learn morphemes and their combination rules.
2) A hierarchical character decoder with a resettable GRU (“HGRU”) and an efficient batching trick (unfolding matrix) that avoids two-pass training.
3) An empirical study showing competitive BLEU and qualitative evidence of learning morphology and handling OOV/misspellings.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and architectural coherence: The model addresses two pain points of word-level NMT (limited vocabulary, expensive softmax) by staying character-level while keeping encoder sequence lengths near word-level. The word encoder and hierarchical decoder match this motivation well.
- Novel encoder design: The use of two RNNs—one for character prefix encodings and one to produce position-wise weights that emphasize likely morpheme boundaries—is a reasonable and arguably novel way (for the cited timeframe) to inject morphology awareness without explicit segmentation.
- Practical decoder training trick: The unfolding matrix that aligns word-level decoder outputs to character time steps for batched training is a useful, implementation-friendly contribution that plausibly explains the reported training efficiency relative to earlier hierarchical decoders.
- Competitive results and efficiency: On En–Fr, the model improves over a strong character baseline (CNMT) and is close to other char-level systems, with smaller parameter count and fewer reported training days. The one-epoch performance surpassing a BPE baseline is suggestive of faster learning.
- Qualitative analyses: PCA plots and “energy” visualizations indicate the encoder discovers morpheme-like boundaries and clusters semantically related derivations (e.g., -ability vs -ibility, -ity vs -able). The misspelling/nonce word examples show realistic benefits of character-level decoding.

Weaknesses
- Comparability and evaluation rigor: The baselines are drawn from different papers with potentially different data pre-processing, hardware, and training budgets. It is unclear whether the comparisons are apples-to-apples (e.g., WMT’14 vs evaluating on newstest2015, differing beam sizes, epochs, or sentence length caps). No statistical significance tests are reported. Stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., stronger BPE systems in the same codebase, or alternative char encoders/decoders trained under identical conditions) are missing.
- Limited ablations: The encoder’s weighting mechanism (tanh of unnormalized exponentiated affine scores) is unconventional and not justified empirically beyond a statement that it “performs best.” There is only a single comparison to an “ordinary RNN word encoder” (CNMT), but no ablations isolating the contributions of (a) the two-RNN weighting vs a standard attention/softmax, (b) different word encoders (CNN, self-attention), or (c) the HGRU vs a simpler reset mechanism. This weakens the methodological claims.
- HGRU novelty and clarity: The hierarchical decoder resembles prior hierarchical character decoders; the novelty appears to be the batched unfolding trick and specific reset equations. The HGRU equations are hard to follow, and the role of the auxiliary delimiter indicator a_t (given by gold segmentation during training) is under-explained for inference. More clarity on stability, gradient flow, and how often incorrect delimiters derail decoding would help.
- Morphology evidence is anecdotal: The PCA plots and a few qualitative boundary detections on PTB are suggestive but not rigorous. No quantitative segmentation evaluation (e.g., against morphological analyzers, Morfessor, or BPE merges) or probing tasks is provided, and the improvement on morphologically rich Czech is modest compared to the strongest character baselines.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Important details are missing or inconsistent: preprocessing beyond “usual tokenization,” delimiter definition (whitespace? punctuation?), character set handling for diacritics, beam size and search specifics, training/inference speed metrics under controlled hardware. The paper contains numerous typographical and formatting issues (figures/tables are hard to parse; references are incomplete), which hinder clarity.

Questions to Authors
- Delimiters: What exactly constitutes a delimiter during training and inference (space only, punctuation, special tokens)? How are hyphenated words and clitics handled? What is the impact on BLEU when changing delimiter definitions?
- Normalization choice: Why use tanh over the sum of exp(aff(h_t)) instead of a softmax to produce normalized weights? Did you try softmax/attention, and what were the differences in training stability and BLEU?
- Ablations: Can you provide controlled ablations isolating (i) the two-RNN weighted encoder vs a standard char-CNN or char-BiLSTM with last state, (ii) HGRU vs a simple GRU with reset-on-delimiter, and (iii) removing the unfolding trick (two-pass training) to quantify efficiency gains?
- Training details: What beam size and length normalization were used? What is the average wall-clock time per epoch and total training time on your GPU? How sensitive are results to the max sequence length caps (e.g., 300 vs 450 vs 600 chars)?
- Czech setup: How do you handle diacritics and rare characters within the 120-char limit? Any evidence that the encoder’s morphology modeling improves Czech beyond what a strong BPE baseline achieves, under identical training?
- Error modes: How often does the model produce spurious or missing delimiters at inference? Any post-edit heuristics used?

Overall evaluation
This work presents a coherent and potentially useful approach to character-level NMT with a morphology-aware encoder and an efficiently trainable hierarchical decoder. The qualitative analyses and some quantitative gains are promising. However, the empirical evaluation lacks rigor and controlled ablations, the novelty over prior hierarchical char models is incremental, and the paper suffers from clarity and reproducibility issues. I lean toward rejection in its current form, but I believe with stronger controlled experiments, clearer exposition, and better baselines, this line of work could make a solid contribution.
