Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a hierarchical, character-based NMT architecture (encoder: character→subword-like→word→sentence; decoder: word→character). The key components are (a) a morphology-aware word encoder that composes a word from characters using two RNNs and learned per-position weights, and (b) a hierarchical decoder with a “settable-state” GRU (HGRU) that generates characters for each target word, resetting its state at word boundaries.
- An efficiency-oriented batching trick (unfolding matrix) enables training the hierarchical decoder in a single pass over character sequences while conditioning on word-level states, aiming to reduce the overhead of two-pass training used in prior hybrid word/char systems.
- Empirical evaluation on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En shows competitive BLEU with prior character/subword systems and qualitative evidence that the encoder learns morpheme-like units, with anecdotal robustness to misspellings/nonce forms.

Strengths
- Addresses open-vocabulary and robustness issues: The architecture naturally handles OOVs, misspellings, and nonce forms, demonstrated by examples (e.g., convenienter). This is an important practical advantage of character-level NMT that is preserved while keeping encoder sequences short.
- Hierarchical decoding that is simple and practical: Conditioning a character-level decoder on word-level states with a reset at delimiters is a clean way to maintain character-level generation while keeping word-level planning and attention. The batching/unfolding trick is a useful engineering contribution and appears to yield faster training than prior two-pass hybrid models.
- Attempt to model morphology explicitly: The two-RNN word encoder with per-position scalar “energies” aims to discover morpheme boundaries and composition rules. The qualitative analyses (PCA plots, energy peaks aligning to plausible morpheme boundaries) are suggestive and align with the paper’s goal.
- Competitive results with relatively modest model size: Reported DCNMT parameter count (~54M) is smaller than some prior char models (e.g., ~69–76M), with training times/epochs that are comparable or better on stated hardware.
- Clear motivation and relevance: The work targets a central limitation of word-level NMT (vocabulary size and OOVs) and the difficulty of pure character models (long sequences, attention training), proposing a middle ground that is timely and relevant.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing hierarchical/char models is limited and under-situated: Hierarchical decoders conditioned on word-level states have been explored (e.g., Ling et al., 2015b; Luong & Manning, 2016). The proposed HGRU is essentially a GRU with external state resets at delimiters; the key difference is the batching trick. The word encoder is a weighted sum over character RNN states, akin to self-attention/segmental composition, but similar ideas exist in compositional char-to-word encoders. The paper should more clearly differentiate from and ablate against C2W/CNN encoders and hierarchical decoders beyond qualitative plots.
- Methodological clarity issues: The word encoder uses weights wt = exp(aff(ht)) but does not normalize (no softmax), and applies tanh after summation. Without normalization or a clear scale control, interpretability of “energy” as morpheme evidence is questionable and could be unstable. The rationale “tanh performs best” is insufficient—please report comparisons (with/without normalization, alternative gating, layer norms). HGRU equations are hard to follow (e.g., the role of the auxiliary sequence a_t, exact delimiter token, and the notation s_it). The decoder depends on word boundary supervision (spaces), which reduces the claim of “pure” character-level modeling.
- Empirical validation and comparisons: The comparisons in Table 1 mix numbers from different papers, datasets, and hardware; training times/epochs are not directly comparable. Critical settings (beam width, dropout, regularization, optimizer details, sentence length limits, character set coverage per language) are missing or only partially specified. No statistical significance testing is provided. Claims like “character-level models are better than subword-level models” are not generally true across tasks and need stronger evidence with carefully matched baselines (e.g., strong BPE models with comparable capacity and training regimen).
- Morphology evaluation is qualitative and anecdotal: The PCA plots and energy heatmaps are interesting but insufficient. There is no quantitative morphological segmentation or analogy evaluation, nor error analysis on morphologically rich phenomena (e.g., agreement, case/number inflection). This weakens the central “learning morphology” claim.
- Reproducibility and presentation: References are incomplete/erroneous in places; figures/tables are hard to parse; several typos and grammar issues remain. Implementation details (code release, exact tokenization, preprocessing, character inventory decisions, handling of unknown characters) are not sufficient to reproduce results. The model relies on a fixed list of 120 characters; mapping others to <unk> contradicts the “open-vocabulary” claim in practice for scripts with larger inventories.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder: Why avoid normalizing the per-position weights? Did you try softmax-normalized attention over character positions, or gating mechanisms (sigmoid) with residual composition? Please provide an ablation comparing normalization choices and their impact on BLEU and on the “energy” interpretability.
- Decoder and batching: What delimiter is used in training/decoding (space, punctuation)? How does the method generalize to languages without explicit whitespace segmentation or with clitics? Please clarify the construction of the unfolding matrix R and its computational/memory cost, and report speed in tokens/sec relative to a strong char baseline.
- Comparisons: Please provide in-house baselines trained on the same data/hardware with matched capacity (strong BPE and char models), including beam sizes and regularization, and report statistical significance. What is the beam size used in character-level decoding? Any length normalization or coverage penalties?
- Morphology: Can you provide quantitative evidence (e.g., segmentation F1 against a morphological analyzer on En/Cs, or improvements on words with rare/complex morphology)? Do the learned “energies” correlate with true morpheme boundaries?
- Robustness: How sensitive is performance to the choice/size of the character set (120)? What fraction of test characters map to <unk>, and how does that affect translation?

Overall recommendation
The paper tackles an important problem with a reasonable hierarchical design and offers a practical batching trick. However, the novelty over prior hierarchical/char approaches is incremental, and the empirical and methodological evidence for “learning morphology” and for efficiency claims is not yet compelling. I encourage strengthening the baselines, clarifying/ablating the encoder/decoder design choices, and adding quantitative morphology evaluations. Pending these improvements, I lean toward rejection.
