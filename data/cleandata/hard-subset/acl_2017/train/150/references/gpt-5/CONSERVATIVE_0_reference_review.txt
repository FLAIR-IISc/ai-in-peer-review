Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) with (a) a character-to-word encoder that aims to learn morphology by weighting prefix character RNN states with a second RNN (“word encoder”), and (b) a hierarchical decoder with a modified GRU (HGRU) whose state is set by a “word-level” decoder and emits characters until a delimiter.
- Empirically, DCNMT is evaluated on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En and reports BLEU comparable to published character-level systems, with faster training than some earlier character models. The authors further show anecdotal advantages on misspellings/nonce words and qualitative analyses suggesting morphological segmentation.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical character decoder with a simple state-reset mechanism and a batching trick (unfolding matrix) to enable efficient training at the character level.
2) A two-RNN character-based word encoder that produces a weighted sum over prefix states, intended to capture morphemes without explicit segmentation.
3) An empirical study showing competitive BLEU to prior char-level models on three WMT directions and qualitative handling of OOV/misspellings.

Strengths
- Competitive results on multiple WMT directions. The model reaches BLEU on par with prior char-level systems (e.g., tying char2char on Cs–En, improving over a BPE baseline in some settings). The cross-language evaluation is appreciated.
- End-to-end character decoding with OOV robustness. The model can generate words unseen in training, including misspellings and nonce morphological forms, a clear practical advantage of character-level approaches. The qualitative examples illustrate this well.
- Practical batching mechanism for hierarchical decoding. The “unfolding” matrix that aligns word-level decoder outputs to character positions is a simple idea that plausibly enables efficient batched training without two-pass decoding. This is a useful engineering contribution.
- Attempt to leverage morphology explicitly in the encoder. The decoupled “representation RNN” and “weight RNN” offer an interpretable handle on subword structure, and the PCA/energy visualizations suggest it captures some morphological regularities.
- Model size comparatively small among char-level systems. Reported parameter counts (~54M) compare favorably to some prior char baselines, which is encouraging from a deployment standpoint.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior art. The “word encoder” is essentially a learned (unnormalized) attention over character prefix states; similar character-to-word composition approaches exist (C2W, CNNs, char2char). The “HGRU” is a GRU with state resets at delimiters, which is a minor variation conceptually akin to hierarchical decoders in Ling et al. (2015). The contribution reads incremental without a clear theoretical or empirical justification for specific design choices (e.g., unnormalized weights, tanh on weighted sums).
- Evaluation fairness and rigor are insufficient. Many comparisons are taken from different papers with different preprocessing, hardware, and training budgets. Reported “days/epochs” are not directly comparable; baselines’ hardware and stopping criteria are unknown. Beam sizes, detokenization, case handling, and BLEU computation are not standardized or fully specified, making it hard to ascribe gains to the method rather than setup differences. Some max-length constraints (e.g., 300–450 characters) could bias datasets; the effect is not analyzed.
- Missing critical baselines and ablations. There is no controlled ablation isolating (i) the word encoder vs. a simpler char-CNN or unweighted char-RNN; (ii) normalized attention vs. unnormalized/tanh weights; (iii) HGRU vs. standard GRU with external resets; (iv) hierarchical decoder vs. a flat char decoder using the same sentence encoder. The single CNMT variant is insufficient and limited to En–Fr. Without these, the claimed benefits (morphology learning, efficiency) are not convincingly attributable to the proposed components.
- Morphology claims are anecdotal. PCA plots and a few “energy” curves on handpicked words are interesting but do not constitute evidence that the model “learns morphology.” No quantitative evaluation (e.g., segmentation F1 vs. gold, analogy tests, affix prediction, or correlation with morphological lexica) is provided. A comparison to BPE/CNN segmentations or to supervised/unsupervised morpheme learners would be appropriate.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues. The HGRU equations mix unusual notation and introduce an auxiliary delimiter sequence and unfolding matrix whose definitions are hard to follow. The choice to not normalize weights wt is asserted to “perform best” but lacks ablation or intuition (and risks scale instability). Numerous typos and inconsistent references (e.g., missing years, “Bahdanau et al.,?”) hinder readability. No code, exact preprocessing pipeline, or evaluation scripts are provided.

Questions to authors
1) Word encoder: Why use unnormalized positive weights wt with tanh on the sum, instead of a conventional attention normalization (softmax)? Please provide ablations comparing normalized vs. unnormalized weighting and the impact on BLEU and on your morphology analyses.
2) Decoder: Is HGRU necessary beyond resetting a standard GRU at delimiters? Please include a baseline that simply reinitializes a vanilla GRU/hard reset at word boundaries with the word-level state, and report BLEU/training speed.
3) Fair comparisons: What are your exact tokenization/detokenization and BLEU computation settings (cased/uncased, SacreBLEU signature)? Beam sizes for character search? Length penalty? Please rerun strong in-house baselines under identical settings (e.g., BPE2BPE with comparable parameter count and training budget).
4) Data filtering: What fraction of training examples are dropped due to the character-length caps (300/450/600)? How sensitive are results to these caps?
5) Morphology evaluation: Can you provide a quantitative segmentation or affix-detection evaluation (e.g., on MorphoChallenge or using CELEX/Morfessor-derived gold), and comparisons to BPE and char-CNN encoders?
6) Efficiency: Report wall-clock training time per epoch and tokens/sec on your hardware, and decoding speed (chars/sec) and memory footprint at inference, compared to your own BPE and char baselines.
7) Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models, and exact hyperparameters (optimizer settings, dropout, layer norms, initialization, gradient clipping), and the vocabulary of 120 characters per language?

Overall, while the idea is reasonable and the results are promising, the novelty is limited and the empirical validation falls short of ACL standards for methodological rigor. Stronger, controlled baselines and ablations, standardized evaluation, and quantitative morphology analysis are needed to substantiate the claims.
