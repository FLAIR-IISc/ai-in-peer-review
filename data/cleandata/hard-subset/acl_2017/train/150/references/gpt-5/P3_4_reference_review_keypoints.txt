- Proposes DCNMT: hierarchical character→subword/word encoder → sentence encoder → word-level decoder → character-level decoder.  
- Introduces a character-based word encoder that weights prefix character RNN states via a second bi-RNN (unnormalized attention-like weights) to learn morpheme-like composition.  
- Presents a hierarchical decoder using a word-level decoder to set/reset a “HGRU” character decoder state at word boundaries and an unfolding/batching trick for efficiency.  
- Evaluates on WMT En–Fr and En–Cs/Cs–En with qualitative analyses suggesting morphological regularities and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.  

- Architectural separation of prefix character representations and position-specific weighting is intuitively aligned with morphology and supported by suggestive qualitative plots.  
- The settable-state hierarchical character decoder is a practical solution to combine character-level generation with shorter encoder sequences, and batching/unfolding makes it trainable in minibatches.  
- Demonstrates open-vocabulary behavior and handling of misspellings/nonce words with concrete examples, a clear advantage over word-only/UNK strategies.  
- Competitive results with prior character-level systems and smaller parameter counts; training time appears reasonable despite model depth.  

- Novelty is limited: core components resemble prior character-to-word encoders and hierarchical decoders, and the weighting is essentially unnormalized attention over prefixes.  
- Key technical details are under-specified or confusing (HGRU equations/notation, role of a_t, interaction with GRU gates).  
- Unnormalized encoder weights (exp(aff(h_t))) and the ad hoc tanh after summation lack justification or ablation comparisons to normalized attention/gating.  
- The batching “unfolding matrix” is described but its correctness, complexity, and advantages are not quantified.  
- Decoding details (beam search across character/word boundaries, beam recombination, penalties) are missing and nontrivial.  
- Experimental comparisons mix baselines with different data/preprocessing/budgets, making claims about superiority over subword models unreliable.  
- Datasets, tokenization, and baseline setups are inconsistent; no significance testing is reported.  
- Training speed/memory claims are not rigorously measured against re-trained baselines on same hardware.  
- Morphology evidence is anecdotal (PCA/energy plots); no quantitative morphology evaluation or broader tests on morphologically rich languages.  
- Some example segmentations are questionable linguistically (e.g., “consum-er-s”).  
- The “open-vocabulary” claim is weakened by restricting the character set to the top 120 characters mapped to <unk>, with no analysis of its impact.  

- Provide a clear, formal definition of HGRU including a_t, reset/set operations, and training/inference selection of s_{t+1}.  
- Explain beam search implementation when beams predict different word-boundary positions and any normalization for variable word counts.  
- Justify and ablate the choice of unnormalized weights vs. softmax/gating and compare tanh/softmax/other combination functions.  
- State whether the BiRNN producing weights uses full-word lookahead and interpret what that implies for morpheme energies.  
- Re-train and report controlled BPE baselines (e.g., 32k) under identical preprocessing, data, and training budgets, with beam sizes, penalties, and significance tests.  
- Provide wall-clock training time per epoch and decoding speed on the authors’ hardware for DCNMT and baselines.  
- Clarify En–Fr data splits, preprocessing pipeline, and exact test sets used.  
- Report fraction of characters mapped to <unk> per language and sensitivity to character vocabulary size.  
- Quantify morphology learning (e.g., boundary F1 vs. gold morphemes, gains on rare/derived forms, Czech inflection analysis).  
- Release code and scripts for the unfolding/batching trick and HGRU to ensure reproducibility.
