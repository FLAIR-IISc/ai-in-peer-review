Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that leverages morphology through a novel word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The word encoder composes word representations from characters using two RNNs: one builds prefix-based character representations and the other predicts position-wise weights (“energies”) that indicate morpheme boundaries and contributions; a weighted sum yields the word vector. The decoder is hierarchical: a first-level “word” decoder produces states that initialize a second-level character decoder (an HGRU with a settable state). An unfolding matrix aligns word-level steps with character-level positions to enable efficient batched training. Experiments on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show BLEU comparable to strong character-level and BPE baselines, with qualitative analyses suggesting learned morphology and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A word encoder that learns morphology by decoupling character composition and morpheme weighting via dual RNNs, yielding subword-aware word representations without external segmentation.
2) A hierarchical character-level decoder with a “settable-state” HGRU and an unfolding trick for efficient batched training across mismatched word/character time scales.
3) Empirical results on WMT datasets showing competitive BLEU with smaller model size and qualitative evidence of morphology learning and OOV robustness.

Strengths
- Architectural novelty in the word encoder: The dual-RNN design to learn both character-composed representations and position-wise weights is a clear, self-contained contribution beyond standard lookup or CNN/RNN word encoders. The analysis (energy peaks at morpheme boundaries; PCA showing meaningful clusters across derivational pairs) provides evidence that the encoder captures morphological structure without supervision.

- Competitive translation performance with modest size: On En–Fr, DCNMT improves over a BPE baseline (30.56 vs. 29.70 BLEU) and outperforms an ordinary RNN word encoder ablation (CNMT: 29.38), indicating the proposed encoder’s utility. On En–Cs and Cs–En, it matches or trails the best character models by small margins while using fewer parameters (~54M) than some comparators (~69–76M).

- Efficient decoding/training design for hierarchical character generation: The “settable-state” HGRU and unfolding matrix provide a practical mechanism to align word- and character-level decoding during batched training, addressing a known efficiency challenge in hybrid/character decoders.

- Robustness to misspellings and nonce words: Qualitative examples show correct handling of typographical variants and morphological variants (“convenienter”). This behavior is consistent with the character-level, morphology-aware design and is practically valuable.

- End-to-end, segmentation-free pipeline: The method avoids external subword segmenters (e.g., BPE), simplifying training and eliminating vocabulary truncation at the word level while keeping encoder sequence lengths close to word-based models.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and mathematical precision: Several equations and definitions are underspecified or inconsistent. For example, the first-level decoder is described as “similar to RNNsearch,” yet Eq. (8) is a linear combination rather than a standard recurrent update; it is unclear how u_t is computed. The word encoder uses unnormalized weights w_t = exp(aff(h_t)) in a tanh of a weighted sum; no justification is provided beyond “performs best,” and stability/scale effects are not analyzed. The HGRU update (Eq. (2)) reuses g_{t-1} on both sides with a gating scalar a_{t-1} without a full derivation. Dimensions in the unfolding example are also unclear. These issues impede reproducibility and assessment of soundness.

- Limited ablations and controlled comparisons: There is no systematic ablation isolating the contributions of (i) the word encoder vs. a CNN/RNN baseline, (ii) the HGRU vs. a standard character GRU/LSTM, or (iii) the unfolding trick. Only one CNMT ablation is shown on En–Fr. Without broader ablations, it is difficult to attribute gains to specific design choices.

- Comparability of experimental results: Training data and setups differ across cited baselines (e.g., WMT14 vs. WMT15, hardware, training epochs, maximum sequence lengths). The claim that prior pure character models took “3 months” is hardware- and implementation-dependent. Beam sizes, normalization, and other decoding details are missing. These factors make the efficiency and BLEU comparisons less conclusive.

- Open-vocabulary claim vs. character vocabulary truncation: The model restricts each language to the 120 most frequent characters, mapping others to <unk>. This limits true open-vocabulary capability and may affect rare scripts/diacritics (especially for Czech). The impact is neither quantified nor discussed.

- Morphology evaluation is qualitative: While figures suggest morpheme sensitivity, there is no quantitative evaluation (e.g., segmentation F1 against a morphological gold standard, or correlation with morphological paradigms). The segmentation visualizations are anecdotal, and the robustness examples are few.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Word encoder weighting: Why use unnormalized weights w_t with tanh rather than a normalized attention (e.g., softmax over positions)? Did you compare normalization variants quantitatively? Please provide ablation results on this choice.

- First-level decoder formulation: Is Eq. (8) the entire recurrence (i.e., a linear unit), or is it part of a GRU/LSTM cell? How is u_t computed? Please clarify the exact architecture and provide a full set of equations.

- HGRU details: Please provide a complete derivation and dimensions for Eqns. (2)–(6), including how the auxiliary delimiter signal a_t is obtained at training and how state resets are handled at inference. How does HGRU compare to a standard character GRU/LSTM initialized with s_t?

- Unfolding matrix R: Please formalize the shapes and construction of R. How does this approach interact with batching across variable-length sequences? What is the computational overhead vs. naive approaches?

- Experimental settings: What are beam sizes, length normalization, and coverage penalties (if any)? What regularization was used (dropout, weight decay)? Can you report tokens/sec and wall-clock training times under a controlled setup vs. your CNMT ablation?

- Character vocabulary: How many characters fall outside the 120 most frequent in each language, and how often do <unk> characters appear in dev/test? What is the impact on performance if increasing the character set?

- Quantitative morphology analysis: Can you provide segmentation metrics against a morphological analyzer or a quantitative study over a larger set (e.g., accuracy on derivational pairs, edit-based perturbations)?
