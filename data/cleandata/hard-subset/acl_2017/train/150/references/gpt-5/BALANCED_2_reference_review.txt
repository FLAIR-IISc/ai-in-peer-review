Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a deep character-level NMT architecture (DCNMT) that aims to learn morphology and avoid large vocabularies. The model has (i) a character-to-word encoder that composes word representations via two RNNs: one computing character-level representations and another producing per-position weights; and (ii) a hierarchical decoder: a word-level decoder whose outputs condition a character-level decoder (an HGRU) that generates characters until a delimiter. An unfolding/broadcasting matrix enables efficient batched training of the hierarchical decoder. Experiments on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En show BLEU comparable to strong character-based baselines and better than some BPE baselines. Qualitative analyses suggest the encoder learns morpheme-like structure and handles nonce/misspelled words.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A hierarchical character-level decoder with a practical batching trick (unfolding matrix) and a GRU variant (HGRU) that resets on delimiters, enabling efficient end-to-end character decoding conditioned on word-level states.
2) A character-to-word encoder that uses bidirectional character RNN features to produce per-position weights (attention-like) over prefix representations, intended to capture morphemes and improve compositional word embeddings.
3) Empirical evidence that the model is competitive with prior character-level systems and can handle OOV/misspelled/nonce words; qualitative analyses that suggest morphological structure is learned.

Strengths
- Addresses a real pain point (open vocabulary) with a coherent, end-to-end character-level solution. The hierarchical design avoids massive softmaxes while retaining word-level structure, and the character decoder naturally handles OOVs, misspellings, and productive morphology. The qualitative examples (e.g., “convenienter”, misspellings) illustrate concrete benefits over word-level/BPE models.
- Efficient batched training of the hierarchical decoder. The unfolding/broadcasting approach is simple, implementation-friendly, and likely useful beyond this paper. Empirically, training speed (days/epochs) appears favorable for the authors’ setup and model size (~54M parameters).
- The word encoder’s design is intuitively appealing and differs from common CNN or simple C2W encoders: a bidirectional character RNN predicts per-position weights over prefix states. The visualization of “energy” peaks aligning to morpheme boundaries and the PCA plots showing better clustering than a vanilla RNN encoder are suggestive.
- Empirical results are competitive with contemporaneous character-level systems on WMT En–Fr/En–Cs/Cs–En, and outperform some BPE baselines. The one-epoch results are relatively strong, supporting the claim that morphology-aware composition can speed learning.
- The paper tackles both directions and multiple language pairs, including a morphologically rich language (Czech), which is appropriate to the morphology motivation.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning relative to prior work are underdeveloped. Hierarchical character decoders (e.g., Ling et al. 2015b; hierarchical RNNs more broadly) and character-to-word composition (Ling et al. 2015a; Kim et al. 2016) are established. The proposed word encoder is essentially attention over characters; this needs clearer differentiation from existing attention/gated pooling within words. Likewise, the HGRU is a modest modification (state reset with a mask), similar in spirit to hierarchical RNNs with boundary-dependent resets. The unfolding trick, while practical, is a batching detail. The paper should more rigorously articulate what is truly new.
- Technical clarity and correctness issues. The word encoder equations are ambiguous/inconsistent: wt is defined via exp(aff(ht)), then the text says “instead of normalizing by sum exp, we use tanh,” but the formula rword = tanh(sum wt rt) is not a normalization and conflates activation with weighting. It is unclear whether weights are normalized, bounded, or how tanh relates to attention. The HGRU equations are dense and notation is inconsistent (e.g., sit vs st; indices; mixing vectors/scalars), making the mechanism hard to reproduce.
- Evaluation setup and reporting are messy, hindering fair comparison. Table 1 formatting is confusing (duplicated DCNMT rows, mixed “Dev/Test” across language pairs), baselines are drawn from different papers with different hardware/training regimes, and not all use the same dev/test splits. The En–Fr test choice (newstest2015) is non-standard for WMT14 and comparability to cited results is unclear. There is no decoding speed or memory comparison, and training time claims for other systems are anecdotal. Beam/search configs and length penalties are not specified.
- Limited ablations and quantitative analysis of the core claims. The only ablation (CNMT) replaces the proposed word encoder on the source; there is no analysis of the HGRU vs a standard GRU, the effect of the unfolding/broadcasting, the impact of weight normalization choices, or comparison to a CNN/C2W encoder under the same architecture. Morphology learning is supported only by qualitative plots and anecdotal segmentations; no quantitative segmentation or OOV generalization tests are provided.
- Writing quality and references need substantial polishing. The paper contains many grammatical errors and typos, inconsistent notation, incomplete/malformed references (years missing, placeholders), and figures are referenced but not fully described. This affects readability and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Word encoder: Are the per-position weights normalized (e.g., via softmax) or not? If not, what prevents unbounded scaling, and why is tanh applied to the summed vector rather than to weights? Please formalize the exact computation and justify the choice vs standard attention.
- HGRU: Is the auxiliary delimiter mask at known word boundary positions during training derived from spaces in the gold target? At test time, do you reset only when the decoder emits a space? How sensitive is performance to delimiter prediction errors?
- Unfolding matrix: What is the computational/memory overhead for long sequences? How does this compare to two-pass training (word pass + char pass)?
- Fairness of comparisons: What beam sizes and length penalties were used for DCNMT vs baselines? Which exact test sets were used for En–Fr/En–Cs/Cs–En, and do your BLEU scripts/tokenization match those cited works?
- Ablations: Can you report results replacing your word encoder with (a) CNN+max-pool and (b) C2W (BiLSTM) under the same architecture? Also, quantify the impact of HGRU vs standard GRU with a hard reset.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Strengthen novelty claims by framing the word encoder as intra-word attention and contrasting empirically against known char encoders. Provide quantitative morphology evaluation (e.g., segmentation F1 vs Morfessor or MorphoLex) and controlled OOV tests.
- Improve clarity: clean up equations/notation, provide pseudocode for decoding, and detail training/inference hyperparameters. Include decoding speed and memory benchmarks.
- Ensure fair, comparable evaluation (standard WMT splits; consistent preprocessing; same beam; BLEU detok vs tok). Release code if possible.

Overall recommendation: Reject in current form due to clarity/evaluation issues and limited novelty, but with promising ideas. With clearer technical exposition, fairer comparisons, and stronger quantitative analysis, this work could make a useful contribution.
