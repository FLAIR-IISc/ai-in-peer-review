Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a deep character-level NMT model with a morphology-aware word encoder and a hierarchical decoder (DCNMT). The word encoder uses two character-level RNNs: one to build prefix representations and another to assign weights to each prefix, producing a weighted sum as a word representation that is intended to discover morphemes. 
- It introduces a hierarchical decoder: a word-level decoder provides states that initialize a character-level decoder (HGRU) which generates characters until a delimiter; an unfolding/batching trick enables efficient training without two forward passes.
- Empirically, the model is evaluated on WMT En–Fr, En–Cs, and Cs–En, showing BLEU comparable to other character-level models and better than some subword baselines, with smaller model size and faster training than some prior char-level systems. Qualitative analyses suggest morphology awareness and robustness to misspellings/nonce words.

Strengths
- Addresses a real limitation of word-level NMT (OOV/rare morphology) with an explicit, interpretable mechanism. The weighted-prefix word encoder is a clear, linguistically motivated idea; the energy plots and PCA visualizations show the model places peaks at plausible morpheme boundaries and groups morphologically related words. The qualitative examples with misspellings and nonce forms (e.g., convenienter) support the claim that the model generalizes via subword structure beyond a fixed vocabulary.
- Practical decoding/training design for fully character-level generation. The HGRU with settable state and the unfolding matrix allow training efficiency that compares favorably (epochs/days and parameter count) to prior char-level systems that required multi-pass training or very long training times.
- Useful ablation and architecture positioning. The comparison to CNMT (same architecture minus morphology-aware encoder) shows consistent BLEU improvements and faster convergence, isolating the contribution of the encoder. The overall model maintains short encoder sequences (word-level) while decoding at character level, a sensible engineering compromise.
- End-to-end character modeling on both sides without external segmentation or BPE rules. This reduces pre-processing complexity and shows the feasibility of learning subword structure from data.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/soundness of the core method is underspecified in places, harming reproducibility. The weighting formulation is inconsistent: wt is defined via exp(aff(ht)), but the text says they “use tanh instead of normalizing by the sum,” which leaves scaling unclear and raises questions about stability and the intended normalization. The HGRU equations are compact but omit some dimensions/initialization specifics and interaction with attention; the role of the auxiliary delimiter indicator at and how it is produced at test time are not fully specified. The training-time unfolding matrix R depends on gold delimiters; how this generalizes at inference (when delimiter predictions may be wrong) is not deeply discussed. These gaps make it difficult to reimplement faithfully.
- Experimental setup and comparability to baselines are uneven. Several baselines are cited from different papers with different architectures/training budgets/datasets, making direct comparison difficult. The evaluation protocol (which “test” sets are used for En–Fr vs En–Cs/Cs–En) is not consistently specified, and some reported numbers are not SOTA for the time of those datasets. Strong, tuned BPE baselines with matched encoder–decoder capacity and training time are missing, so the claim “character-level models are better than subword-level models” is not convincingly established. No statistical significance tests are reported.
- Morphology-learning claims are largely qualitative. PCA plots and a few energy heatmaps are informative but do not quantify segmentation quality or morphological generalization. There is no evaluation against a morphological segmentation benchmark or probing tasks. The example-based evidence is insufficient to substantiate “learning morphology” as a primary contribution.
- The “open vocabulary” claim is weakened by mapping all but the top 120 characters to <unk>. The paper states this covers “nearly 100%,” but does not quantify coverage or analyze the remaining OOV characters’ impact—this matters for realistic multilingual scenarios and rare characters.
- Missing training and decoding details impede reproducibility and assessment. Beam width and normalization (e.g., length, coverage) are not specified; regularization (dropout, layer norm), gradient clipping, and batching details are absent; speed comparisons are given as “epochs/days” on a single GPU without standardized throughput metrics (tokens/sec), which can be confounded by different preprocessing and stopping criteria. No code release is mentioned.

How well it meets reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The idea of learning word representations from characters is not new (C2W, CNN-char encoders), and hierarchical decoders have precedent. The specific weighted-prefix morphology encoder and the batching trick for the hierarchical decoder are novel variants, but incremental.
- Soundness/correctness: Mixed. The approach is plausible and demonstrated to work, but mathematical specification ambiguities (weighting, HGRU updates, delimiter handling) and lack of rigorous ablations raise concerns.
- Clarity: Below average. Numerous typos, inconsistent notation, and incomplete algorithmic details reduce clarity.
- Significance: Moderate. Results are comparable to prior char-level models and show practical advantages (training efficiency), but do not clearly surpass strong subword baselines; impact depends on better empirical support.
- Empirical evaluation: Mixed. Multi-language evaluation is appreciated, as are ablations and qualitative analyses, but baseline fairness, detail, and statistical rigor are lacking.
- Reproducibility: Weak without code, due to missing hyperparameters and ambiguous method descriptions.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the weighting formulation in the word encoder: is wt = tanh(aff(ht)) or exp(aff(ht))? If unnormalized, how do you control scale across variable-length words?
- How exactly are delimiters defined and handled? What tokenization determines “word boundaries” on the target side during training? At test time, how do delimiter prediction errors propagate to the first-level decoder resets?
- What are the beam search settings (beam size, length normalization, coverage penalties)? Did you apply dropout, gradient clipping, or other regularization?
- What fraction of training/dev/test characters are mapped to <unk> under the 120-character vocabulary, per language?
- Can you provide a fair, matched-capacity BPE baseline (same encoder/decoder depth/width, same training budget) and report decoding/training throughput (chars/sec or tokens/sec)?
- Can you quantify morphology learning (e.g., segmentation F1 on a morphologically annotated corpus, or probing tasks for morpheme-aware representations)?

Additional comments
- Numerous typos and inconsistent references/notation should be corrected. Figures are helpful; adding algorithmic pseudo-code for the unfolding/batching would improve clarity.

Overall: promising idea with interesting qualitative evidence and practical training contributions, but clarity and experimental rigor need strengthening before acceptance.
