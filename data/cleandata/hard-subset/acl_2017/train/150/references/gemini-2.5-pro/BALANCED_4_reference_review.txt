### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-to-character neural machine translation model designed to handle morphological variations and the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem. The model's architecture is structured in four layers: (1) a novel character-level word encoder that learns a weighted combination of character prefixes to form a word representation, (2) a standard BiRNN sentence encoder, (3) a word-level attention-based decoder that generates a sequence of target word representations, and (4) a character-level decoder that generates the actual character sequence for each target word, conditioned on the output from the word-level decoder. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive BLEU scores on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, while also being able to translate misspelled or unseen nonce words.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective hierarchical NMT architecture.** The core contribution is the elegant two-level decoding process, where a "slow" word-level decoder provides high-level guidance to a "fast" character-level generator. This intelligently combines the strengths of word-level models (handling long-range dependencies) and character-level models (open vocabulary, morphological awareness). The proposed HGRU and efficient batching mechanism are crucial for making this architecture practical.
2.  **A novel character-based word encoder.** The proposed method of composing a word vector by learning attention-like weights over the hidden states of a character-level RNN is a distinct contribution. The qualitative analysis provides compelling evidence that this encoder is adept at capturing morphological structure, offering a more interpretable alternative to standard RNN or CNN encoders.
3.  **Thorough empirical validation and qualitative analysis.** The paper presents strong results that are competitive with other character-level and subword-level models of the time, particularly on a morphologically rich language pair (En-Cs). The analysis in Figures 3-5 and the translation examples in Table 2 provide convincing qualitative evidence that the model learns useful morphological representations and leverages them to handle novel words.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model architecture.** The hierarchical design (character→word→sentence→word→character) is intuitive and directly addresses a known trade-off in NMT: word-level models are computationally efficient but struggle with OOVs and morphology, while pure character-level models have an open vocabulary but struggle with the extremely long sequences. This model provides a principled compromise. The separation of concerns, with one decoder deciding *what* to say (the word vector) and another deciding *how* to spell it (the character sequence), is a powerful conceptual framework.
2.  **Convincing evidence of learning morphology.** A central claim of the paper is that the model learns morphology, and the authors provide strong support for this. The PCA plot in Figure 3 clearly shows that the proposed word encoder produces more semantically coherent embeddings than a standard RNN encoder. The weight visualization in Figure 4 suggests the model learns to identify morpheme-like boundaries by assigning higher weights to characters at the end of these units (e.g., 'y' in 'any', 'y' in 'every'). This provides valuable insight into the model's inner workings.
3.  **Demonstrated practical benefits for open-vocabulary translation.** The model's ability to handle misspelled and nonce words (Table 2) is a significant practical advantage over word- and subword-based models. Translating "convenienter" correctly, a word absent from the training set, is a powerful demonstration that the model can generalize morphological rules (e.g., adding '-er' for comparatives) rather than simply memorizing vocabulary. This robustness is a key selling point for real-world applications.
4.  **Strong quantitative results and efficiency.** The model achieves BLEU scores that are comparable or superior to contemporary character- and subword-level baselines, especially on the morphologically rich Czech tasks. Furthermore, the authors report impressive training efficiency (e.g., reaching strong performance after just one epoch and finishing in under a week for En-Fr), which is a notable achievement for such a deep and complex character-level architecture.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of clarity in the description of key components.** Some parts of the methodology are difficult to follow.
    *   **Word Encoder:** The justification for using `tanh(sum(w_t*r_t))` instead of a standard softmax-normalized attention mechanism is weak ("it performs best in experiments"). A more principled explanation or comparison would be beneficial. The term "energy" is used for the unnormalized weights, which can be confusing.
    *   **HGRU Decoder:** The state update equation for the HGRU (Eq. 2) appears tautological: `g_{t-1} = (1-a_{t-1})g_{t-1} + ...`. It is likely that the `g_{t-1}` on the right side refers to the state from the previous *character* time step, while the `g_{t-1}` on the left is the input state for the *current* character time step. This notational ambiguity makes a core component of the model hard to understand and reproduce. The description of the unfolding matrix `R` is also very brief and could benefit from a more concrete example.
2.  **Insufficient ablation studies for a complex model.** The proposed model has many moving parts ("six recurrent neural networks"). However, the paper lacks a systematic ablation study to disentangle the contributions of its different components. The CNMT baseline, which uses a simpler RNN word encoder, is only reported for En-Fr. It would be crucial to see this comparison for the morphologically rich En-Cs/Cs-En tasks, where the proposed encoder is expected to provide the largest gains. This would more rigorously validate the central claim that the specific word encoder design is key to learning morphology. Additional ablations, such as using a simpler decoder, would further strengthen the paper's conclusions.
3.  **Positioning with respect to prior work could be stronger.** While the paper cites relevant work on hierarchical models (e.g., Ling et al., 2015b), it could do a better job of situating its specific contributions. The idea of hierarchical decoding is not entirely new. The paper's novelty lies in its specific, efficient implementation (HGRU, unfolding matrix) and the novel word encoder. Clarifying this distinction would help readers better appreciate the paper's incremental and novel contributions over the existing literature.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the word encoder: Could you please elaborate on the choice of using `tanh` over an unnormalized sum for the prefix representations, as opposed to a standard attention mechanism with a `softmax` normalization over the weights `w_t`? Did you experiment with `softmax` and find it performed worse?
2.  Regarding the HGRU decoder: The state update in Eq. (2), `g_{t−1} = (1− a_{t−1})g_{t−1} + a_{t−1}s_{it}`, is confusing as written. Could you please provide the corrected, unambiguous formulation for how the HGRU state is updated at a word boundary, clarifying which time indices refer to the character-level sequence and which refer to the word-level sequence?
3.  The DCNMT model significantly outperforms the CNMT baseline on En-Fr. Did you perform this same comparison on the En-Cs and Cs-En translation tasks? I would expect the performance gap to be even larger on these morphologically richer languages, which would provide the strongest evidence for the effectiveness of your proposed word encoder.
