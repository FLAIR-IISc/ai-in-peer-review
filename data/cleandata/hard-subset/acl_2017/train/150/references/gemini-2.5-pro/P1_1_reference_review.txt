### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT, designed to overcome the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-level models. The model is structured hierarchically from characters to words to sentences. The primary contributions are a novel word encoder that learns morphological representations and an efficient hierarchical decoder for character-level generation. The word encoder uses a character-level attention mechanism to compose a word representation, which is shown to capture morphological properties. The decoder operates on two levels: a word-level RNN proposes a hidden state for each target word, and a character-level RNN uses this state to generate the sequence of characters for that word. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, trains efficiently, and can robustly handle misspelled and nonce words.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Word Encoder for Learning Morphology:** The core contribution is a word encoder that composes a word's representation from its characters using a learned, unnormalized attention mechanism. The qualitative analysis provides compelling evidence that this method learns to identify morphemes and produces semantically meaningful representations that capture morphological relationships (e.g., distinguishing between adjectives like "reliable" and nouns like "reliability").
2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Character-Level Decoder:** The paper introduces a two-level decoder architecture that combines the structural advantages of word-level decoding with the flexibility of character-level generation. The use of a specialized GRU (HGRU) and an "unfolding matrix" is a clever engineering solution that makes this complex hierarchical process efficient and trainable in batches, addressing a key challenge for character-level models.
3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Robustness:** The paper provides a strong practical demonstration of the benefits of its approach. The model's ability to correctly translate misspelled words and morphologically novel nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") is a clear and powerful illustration of its generalization capabilities beyond what is possible with standard word- or subword-based models.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture (character→word→sentence→word→character) is intuitive and directly addresses the limitations of both pure word-level and pure character-level models. The word encoder's design, in particular, is a novel and effective method for explicitly modeling sub-word compositionality.
2.  **Convincing Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5.2 is a major strength. The PCA plots (Fig. 3) effectively visualize the superiority of the proposed word encoder over a standard RNN. Furthermore, the visualization of character "energy" (Fig. 4) and the subword segmentation (Fig. 5) provide strong, interpretable evidence that the model is indeed learning morphology, as claimed, rather than just being a black box that happens to perform well.
3.  **Demonstrated Training Efficiency and Performance:** The paper successfully tackles the notorious difficulty of training deep character-level models. Table 1 shows that DCNMT trains significantly faster (in days and epochs) than other character-level models while achieving competitive or superior BLEU scores. The fact that the model outperforms a BPE baseline after only one epoch on some tasks is a strong testament to its learning efficiency.
4.  **Practical Benefits Beyond BLEU:** The model's ability to handle noisy inputs like misspellings and nonce words (Table 2) is a significant practical advantage. This demonstrates a level of robustness that is highly desirable for real-world applications but is often overlooked in academic benchmarks that use clean test sets. This capability directly stems from the character-level modeling and learned morphology.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important architectural details are not sufficiently justified. For instance, in the word encoder, the character weights are computed via `exp(aff(h_t))` and then combined using `tanh`. The paper states that using `tanh` instead of a standard softmax normalization "performs best in experiments," but this is an insufficient explanation. A standard attention mechanism uses a softmax to create a probability distribution. The choice of an unnormalized sum within a `tanh` is a significant deviation that requires more theoretical or empirical justification, such as an ablation study comparing it to a softmax-based approach.
2.  **Ambiguity in the Hierarchical Decoder Formulation:** The description of the Hierarchical GRU (HGRU) is confusing. Specifically, in Equation (2), the state `gt-1` is updated using `sit`, which is the output from the first-level (word-level) decoder. The notation `sit` is indexed by the character timestep `t`, while the word-level state `s` should be indexed by the word timestep `i`. The "unfolding matrix" explanation clarifies the implementation, but the formal equations are inconsistent and hard to follow. This makes it difficult to fully understand the information flow and mechanics of the decoder.
3.  **Limited Scope of Morphological Analysis:** The excellent qualitative analysis of learned morphology is performed only on English words. While compelling, the model's biggest performance gains are shown on English-Czech and Czech-English, where Czech is a morphologically rich language. Demonstrating that the word encoder can identify morphemes in Czech (e.g., case endings, verb conjugations) would have made the claims about learning morphology much more general and powerful. Without this, it is unclear if the mechanism is simply learning common English suffixes or a more general morphological principle.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the word encoder's weighting scheme? Specifically, what was the performance difference between your `tanh(sum(w_t*r_t))` formulation and a more standard attention mechanism using a softmax to normalize the weights `w_t`? Providing this comparison would help clarify the contribution of this specific design choice.
2.  Could you please clarify the notation in the HGRU update equations (Eq. 2-6)? Is the variable `sit` in Equation (2) constant for all character timesteps `t` corresponding to the i-th target word? If so, would it be clearer to denote it as `s_i`, where `i` is the word index?
3.  Given the strong performance on the Czech language tasks, did you perform any qualitative analysis, similar to that in Figures 4 and 5, to see if the word encoder was learning meaningful morphological units in Czech? Such an analysis would significantly strengthen your claim that the model learns morphology in a language-agnostic way.
