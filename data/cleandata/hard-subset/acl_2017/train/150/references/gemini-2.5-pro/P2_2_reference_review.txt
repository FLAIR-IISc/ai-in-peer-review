### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a deep, hierarchical neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that operates entirely at the character level. The goal is to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-level models while overcoming the training difficulty and long-sequence challenges of traditional character-level models.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A Novel Word Encoder for Learning Morphology:** The primary contribution is a novel word encoder composed of two RNNs. One RNN processes the character sequence, while a second bidirectional RNN computes attention-like weights for each character's hidden state. The authors claim this mechanism allows the model to automatically learn subword units (morphemes) and their compositional rules, moving beyond fixed segmentation methods like BPE.
2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Decoder Architecture:** The paper proposes a two-level decoder to manage the length discrepancy between word-level concepts and character-level generation. A word-level decoder generates high-level state vectors, which in turn initialize and guide a character-level decoder (a modified GRU, termed HGRU) to produce the final character sequence. This hierarchical structure is designed to be more efficient and trainable than a flat character-level decoder.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive BLEU scores on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks compared to strong subword and character-level baselines. Crucially, they provide compelling qualitative analysis, including visualizations and examples, to support their central claim that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.

---

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Architecture:** The proposed architecture is thoughtfully designed to tackle a well-known problem. The idea of learning subword units implicitly through a weighted combination of character states is a clever and powerful concept. It provides a data-driven alternative to predefined segmentation (like BPE) and is more structured than a simple RNN over characters. The hierarchical nature of the model (from characters up to sentences and back down) is conceptually elegant.

2.  **Strong and Compelling Qualitative Analysis:** The paper's strongest aspect is the analysis in Section 5.2, which directly supports the central claim of "Learning Morphology." The PCA visualizations (Fig. 3) effectively show a better-structured representation space compared to a standard RNN encoder. The "energy" plots (Fig. 4) provide intuitive and convincing evidence that the model is identifying morpheme-like boundaries (e.g., separating "any-"/"every-" from "-one"/"-thing"). This kind of analysis is often missing in deep learning papers and greatly increases confidence in the authors' claims.

3.  **Impressive Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** The examples in Table 2 are excellent. Successfully translating misspelled words ("unconclusive") and, more impressively, nonce words that follow morphological rules ("convenienter") is a powerful demonstration of the model's generalization capabilities. This goes beyond simply solving the OOV problem and shows a deeper understanding of language structure, which is a significant advantage over both word-level and BPE-based models.

4.  **Competitive Performance with High Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are on par with or better than several state-of-the-art systems. A key practical strength highlighted is its training efficiency. As shown in Table 1, DCNMT achieves strong results in just one epoch and reaches its final performance significantly faster than other pure character-level models (e.g., claiming 15 days for En-Cs vs. 90 days for Luong & Manning, 2016). This makes the proposed approach much more practical for real-world application.

---

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important architectural details are presented without sufficient justification. For instance, in the word encoder (Section 3.1), the final representation is computed as `tanh(sum(wt*rt))`. It is highly unconventional not to normalize the weights `wt` (e.g., via softmax). The authors simply state that using `tanh` "performs best in experiments." This is a weak justification. Does this imply negative weights are possible? What is the intuition? A brief ablation study or a more detailed explanation would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Clarity of the Hierarchical Decoder Mechanism:** The description of the hierarchical decoder, particularly the unfolding matrix `R` used for efficient batching (Section 3.2), is dense and difficult to parse. The example provided is minimal, and the text alone does not make the mechanism for aligning the shorter sequence of word-level states (`s_it`) with the longer character sequence fully clear. This lack of clarity hinders reproducibility and a complete understanding of a core component of the model.

3.  **Model Complexity:** The full model consists of six RNNs, making it a very deep and complex system. While the authors demonstrate it can be trained efficiently, this complexity might obscure the precise source of the performance gains. The comparison to the "CNMT" baseline (which uses a simpler word encoder) is a good start, but a more in-depth analysis of the performance gap would be beneficial to isolate the contribution of the novel word encoder versus the rest of the hierarchical structure. The paper could be strengthened by discussing whether simpler architectural variants were explored.

---

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the design choice of using an unnormalized, weighted sum passed through a `tanh` function in the word encoder? What is the intuition behind this compared to a more standard attention mechanism where weights are normalized to sum to one?

2.  During inference, how are the word boundaries for the hierarchical decoder determined? Section 3.4 states that a new vector `st+1` is computed once a delimiter is generated. Does this mean the character-level HGRU generates freely until it produces a space or punctuation mark, at which point the word-level decoder is advanced one step? A clarification of the generation process would be very helpful.

3.  The performance on the morphologically rich En-Cs/Cs-En tasks is a key result. Have you analyzed whether the learned subword units correspond to actual Czech morphemes? This would provide even stronger evidence for your central claim.
