### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT, designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The architecture is hierarchical, processing information from characters to subwords, to words, to a source sentence representation, which is then decoded hierarchically from a target word level down to a target character level.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Architecture for Character-Level NMT:** The core contribution is the end-to-end architecture itself. It cleverly combines two key novel components:
    *   A **character-level word encoder** that uses a dual-RNN setup resembling an attention mechanism over characters to dynamically compose a word representation. This is designed to capture morphemic structure.
    *   A **hierarchical decoder** that uses a word-level RNN to generate a sequence of state vectors, which in turn initialize the state of a character-level RNN (HGRU) to generate the characters for each target word. This modular design effectively bridges the gap between word-level semantics and character-level generation.

2.  **Strong Qualitative Analysis of Morphological Learning:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed word encoder learns meaningful, morphologically-aware representations. The visualizations (PCA projections in Fig. 3, morpheme energy plots in Fig. 4, and subword segmentation in Fig. 5) are a significant contribution, moving beyond just BLEU scores to offer insight into what the model has learned.

3.  **Competitive Empirical Performance and Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with other state-of-the-art character-level models on both morphologically rich (Czech) and less rich (French) languages. Crucially, it demonstrates superior training efficiency compared to some contemporaneous fully character-level models, highlighting the architectural benefits.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The architecture is thoughtfully designed to address the primary challenge of pure character-level models: handling long-range dependencies. By structuring the model hierarchically, it retains the efficiency of word-level sequence lengths at the sentence level while reaping the benefits of character-level modeling (open vocabulary, morphological awareness). The proposed HGRU and the matrix-based unfolding trick for efficient batching is a clever engineering solution.

2.  **Convincing Evidence of Morphological Generalization:** The strongest aspect of this paper is its analysis of the learned representations. The ability of the model to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" (Table 2) is a powerful demonstration of true morphological generalization. This, combined with the visualizations showing that words with similar stems but different suffixes ("reliable" vs. "reliability") are clustered meaningfully, provides strong support for the central claim that the model is "learning morphology."

3.  **Effective Solution to the OOV Problem:** By operating at the character level, the model inherently solves the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem that plagues word-based NMT. The authors demonstrate this effectively with examples of misspelled words, which a word-based system would map to `<unk>`, but which their model handles gracefully. This is a significant practical advantage.

4.  **Strong Results and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves impressive BLEU scores, outperforming BPE-based subword models and performing on par with or better than other character-level approaches from the period. The reported training times (e.g., ~19 days for En-Fr vs. ~90 days for a competitor) show that the architectural choices lead to a more practical and efficient model, which is a significant contribution in the context of deep learning research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices:** While the overall architecture is well-motivated, some specific design choices are not well explained.
    *   In the word encoder (Sec 3.1), the weights `w_t` are computed as `exp(aff(h_t))` but are not normalized via a softmax. Instead, the final representation is passed through a `tanh`. The only justification given is that it "performs best in experiments." This is insufficient. A more standard attention mechanism would use a softmax, providing a probability distribution over character positions. The paper would be stronger if it explained the intuition behind this choice or provided an ablation study comparing it to a standard softmax attention.
    *   The feedback mechanism to the first-level decoder (Sec 3.3) uses an "ordinary RNN to encode the target word" because the feedback is "less important." This claim seems arbitrary and is not substantiated with evidence or ablation.

2.  **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The experimental comparison, while good, could be more rigorous. The authors compare their DCNMT model against CNMT (an ablation) and results from other papers. However, a more direct comparison would be more convincing. For instance:
    *   The paper claims the RNN-based word encoder is superior to CNN-based encoders (Kim et al., 2016) in generating "meaningful" representations. This is a strong claim that is not backed by a direct experimental comparison. Including a version of DCNMT with a CNN word encoder as a baseline would have directly tested this hypothesis.
    *   The BPE baseline results are taken from another paper. While common practice, results can be sensitive to implementation details. An in-house BPE baseline with a comparable parameter count would strengthen the claim of superiority.

3.  **Analysis is Limited to English Suffixes:** The excellent morphological analysis is performed exclusively on English words, focusing on suffixation (e.g., "-able" vs "-ability"). While the model is tested on Czech, a morphologically complex language with a rich case system, the paper does not provide any qualitative analysis to show how the word encoder handles Czech morphology (e.g., prefixes, different declensions). Showing the segmentation or PCA plots for Czech words would have made the claims of morphological learning far more general and robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the word encoder's weighting mechanism? Specifically, why was a non-normalized weighted sum followed by a `tanh` activation chosen over a more conventional softmax-based attention mechanism? What was the intuition, and could you provide results from experiments with a softmax-normalized attention to quantify the performance difference?

2.  In Section 3.3, you state that the feedback to the word-level decoder is "less important" and thus use a simpler RNN encoder for the target words. What is the basis for this claim? Did you experiment with using your proposed dual-RNN word encoder for the target-side feedback as well, and if so, how did it impact performance and training time?

3.  The morphological analysis is very insightful but is confined to English. Given that a key motivation is handling morphologically rich languages, could you provide a similar analysis (e.g., segmentation or visualization of word representations) for Czech to demonstrate that the model effectively captures its more complex morphology?
