**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-to-character neural machine translation model (DCNMT) designed to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-level models. The authors identify two primary contributions:
1.  A novel word encoder that composes character representations into a single word vector. This encoder uses two RNNs: one to process the character sequence and a second bidirectional RNN to compute weights for each character's hidden state. The authors claim this architecture learns linguistic morphology.
2.  A hierarchical decoder composed of a word-level RNN that produces high-level states and a character-level RNN (using a custom "HGRU" cell) that generates the character sequence for each word, guided by the states from the word-level decoder.

The main contribution, as I see it, is the engineering of a fully character-level NMT system that combines several existing ideas (character composition, hierarchical decoding) into an architecture that achieves competitive performance with established subword-based methods, while being more robust to spelling errors and morphological variations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Non-Standard Words:** The paper's most compelling strength lies in its qualitative results. The examples in Table 2, showing correct translations for misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words ("convenienter"), provide strong evidence for the practical benefits of a character-level approach. This robustness is a clear advantage over standard models that rely on a fixed vocabulary (word or subword), which would typically map such inputs to an `<unk>` token.

2.  **Competitive Empirical Performance:** The proposed DCNMT model achieves BLEU scores that are on par with, and in some cases exceed, strong contemporary baselines, including BPE-based subword models and other character-level systems on English-French and English-Czech language pairs. Achieving this level of performance with a purely character-level model is non-trivial, as such models have historically been difficult to train and optimize.

3.  **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The high-level design of the model is intuitive. Decomposing the complex task of character-to-character translation into a hierarchical process (character -> word -> sentence -> target word -> target character) is a sensible approach to manage the long dependencies inherent in character sequences and structure the learning problem.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Architectural Complexity vs. Established Baselines:** The primary baseline for open-vocabulary NMT is the subword model based on Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) (Sennrich et al., 2016). While the paper reports BPE results, the comparison is not rigorous. The BPE scores are cited from external work, precluding a controlled comparison of training efficiency, data, and hyperparameters. The proposed DCNMT model is substantially more complex (a deep stack of six RNNs) than a standard BPE-based NMT model. However, the reported BLEU score improvements are modest (e.g., +0.86 BLEU on En-Fr over the cited BPE result). Without a direct, controlled comparison of training/inference speed and a more significant performance gain, the added complexity of this new architecture over a simple and highly effective baseline like BPE is not well justified.

2.  **Claims of "Learning Morphology" are Overstated and Lack Rigorous Evidence:** A central narrative of the paper is that the word encoder "learns morphology." This claim is supported only by qualitative evidence: a PCA plot of a few hand-picked words and a visualization of attention-like weights ("energy"). This evidence is suggestive but insufficient. The "energy" mechanism is functionally a self-attention layer over characters, and it is not surprising that it learns to focus on salient sub-sequences. To claim it is learning linguistic morphemes, rather than just useful compositional features, requires more formal evaluation, such as using the learned representations in an extrinsic morphological analysis task or comparing against baselines on a word similarity benchmark sensitive to morphology.

3.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The proposed model has several novel components, including the specific dual-RNN word encoder and the hierarchical decoder with its custom HGRU cell. The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each of these components. For example:
    *   How does the proposed word encoder compare against simpler, established character composition methods like a BiRNN followed by max-pooling, or the CNN-based encoder from Kim et al. (2016)? The comparison is only made against a weak baseline of taking the final hidden state of a unidirectional RNN.
    *   How critical is the hierarchical structure of the decoder? A comparison to a flat character-level decoder (if trainable to a reasonable performance) would help demonstrate the necessity of the proposed hierarchy. Without these studies, it is difficult to assess which parts of the complex architecture are actually responsible for its performance.

4.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the complete system is new, its core components are variations on well-established ideas. Character-level word composition and hierarchical decoding have been explored previously. The novelty lies in the specific implementation details (e.g., the weighted sum in the encoder, the HGRU cell). The paper could do a better job of positioning its contributions within this existing literature to clarify what is an incremental improvement versus a fundamental new approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The comparison to BPE is critical for evaluating this work. Can you provide results from a BPE baseline that you trained yourself under identical conditions (data, model size, optimizer, hardware, and training time) as your DCNMT model? A direct comparison of training and inference throughput (e.g., in words/sec) would also be essential to validate claims of efficiency.

2.  The word encoder's final representation is computed as `tanh(sum(w_t * r_t))`. Using `tanh` here is unconventional, as attention-style mechanisms typically use a weighted sum where weights are normalized (e.g., via softmax) to sum to 1. Could you provide a justification for this choice beyond "it performs best in experiments"? Did you experiment with a standard softmax-based attention mechanism, and if so, how did it perform in comparison?
