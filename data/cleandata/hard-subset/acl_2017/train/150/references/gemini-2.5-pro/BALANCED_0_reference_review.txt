### Summary of the paper
This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model with a novel hierarchical architecture. The model is structured to mirror linguistic composition: characters to words, and words to sentences. The encoder first computes word representations from their constituent characters using a custom module with two RNNs: one generates character-level hidden states, and a second, bidirectional RNN computes unnormalized weights for these states. The final word vector is a weighted sum of the character states. These word vectors are then fed into a standard sentence-level bidirectional RNN encoder. The decoder is similarly hierarchical: a word-level attention-based decoder generates a sequence of abstract word representations, and a character-level decoder, using a modified GRU (HGRU), generates the character sequence for each representation. The authors demonstrate that this model achieves competitive results on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks, is efficient to train, and can effectively handle OOV, misspelled, and nonce words by learning morphological patterns.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A novel and well-motivated hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT.** The model design (character→word→sentence→word→character) elegantly manages the long-sequence problem of character-level modeling by introducing a word-level abstraction for the sentence encoder and the primary decoder. The hierarchical decoder, which conditions a character-level generator on a word-level generator's state, is a particularly clever contribution.
2.  **A new, morphology-inspired word encoder.** The proposed method of using a dual-RNN setup to learn a weighted combination of character-level hidden states is a novel approach to learning word representations from characters, differing from more common CNN or simple RNN-based methods.
3.  **Strong empirical results and qualitative analyses.** The paper shows competitive BLEU scores against subword and other character-level baselines, particularly on a morphologically rich language pair (En-Cs). The analysis provides compelling evidence that the model learns useful morphological information, enabling it to robustly handle unseen word variations.

### Strengths
1.  **Elegant and Principled Architecture:** The paper's core strength is its hierarchical design. It provides a clear and intuitive solution to the main drawback of character-level models—the computational and learning challenges associated with extremely long sequences. By operating at a word-level for sentence encoding and high-level decoding, the model retains many of the efficiencies of word-based systems while being a true end-to-end character-level system. This hybrid approach is well-motivated and appears effective.
2.  **Novelty in Word Representation Learning:** The proposed word encoder is a creative departure from standard methods like character-CNNs or taking the final hidden state of an RNN. The idea of learning an explicit weighting over character-level states to represent sub-word importance is compelling. The qualitative analysis in Figure 4, which suggests that the learned weights (energy) peak near morpheme boundaries (e.g., after 'any' in 'anyone'), provides strong support for the claim that this architecture encourages the learning of morphology.
3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Character-Level Benefits:** The paper provides a clear and convincing demonstration of the practical advantages of character-level modeling. The results in Table 2, where the model correctly translates sentences with misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words ("convenienter"), are particularly strong evidence. This showcases a tangible benefit over word/subword models that would map such tokens to `<unk>`, highlighting the model's robustness and generalization capabilities.
4.  **Competitive Performance and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are on par with or exceed contemporary state-of-the-art character-level and subword-based models. Crucially, as noted in Table 1, the model appears to be significantly more efficient to train than some competing fully character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016), making the approach more practical and accessible. The design of the HGRU and the unfolding matrix is a clever engineering solution that enables efficient batch training.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Ablation and Comparison for the Word Encoder:** The paper's central architectural novelty is the dual-RNN word encoder. However, its effectiveness is not rigorously isolated. The only baseline within the paper that uses a different character-to-word model is "CNMT" (an ordinary RNN encoder). A more thorough evaluation would compare this encoder against other strong character-composition functions, such as the widely-used character-level CNN (Kim et al., 2016), within the *same* hierarchical framework. Without this comparison, it is difficult to determine how much of the model's success is due to the novel word encoder versus the overall hierarchical structure.
2.  **Lack of Justification for the Word Encoder's Weighting Mechanism:** The computation of the word representation `r = tanh(sum(w_t * r_t))` where `w_t = exp(aff(h_t))` is unusual and not well-justified. The authors state that they do not normalize the weights because using `tanh` "performs best in experiments," but this is insufficient justification. Using an unnormalized `exp` could lead to exploding values, and it is unclear why this would be preferable to a standard attention mechanism (i.e., a softmax over `aff(h_t)`), which would produce a proper probability distribution over character states. This design choice feels ad-hoc and warrants a more detailed explanation or analysis.
3.  **Claims of "Learning Morphology" Rest on Anecdotal Evidence:** While the visualizations in Figures 3, 4, and 5 are compelling and suggestive, they constitute anecdotal evidence based on a few hand-picked examples. The method for detecting subword boundaries in Figure 5 by identifying "peak energy" is subjective and not systematically evaluated. While proving that a neural network has learned a specific linguistic phenomenon is notoriously difficult, the claims could be strengthened by a more quantitative analysis, for example by correlating the learned "energy" peaks with known morphological segmentation boundaries across a larger vocabulary.
4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The description of the hierarchical decoder, specifically the HGRU, could be clearer. In Eq. (2), the state `g_{t-1}` is updated using a gate `a_{t-1}` and the word-level state `s_{it}`. It is not explicitly stated whether the character-level RNN's state is completely reset by `s_{it}` at a word boundary, or if some information from the last character of the previous word (in `g_{t-1}`) is carried over. A more precise formal description of the state update at word boundaries would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the word encoder's weighting mechanism? Specifically, have you experimented with a standard softmax-based attention mechanism for calculating the weights `w_t`? If so, how did it perform compared to the proposed unnormalized `exp` followed by a global `tanh`?
2.  To better isolate the contribution of your novel word encoder, could you provide results (or discuss your findings if you have them) from an experiment where your word encoder is replaced with a character-level CNN encoder, while keeping the rest of the hierarchical NMT architecture the same?
3.  Regarding the HGRU in the decoder: is the character-level hidden state completely reset by the word-level state `s_t` at the beginning of a new word, or is information from the final character of the previous word preserved? Eq. (2) suggests a gated update, but a clarification on how information flows across word boundaries at the character level would be helpful.
