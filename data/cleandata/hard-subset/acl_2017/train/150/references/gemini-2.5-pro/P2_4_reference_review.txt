### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, purely character-level neural machine translation model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The core of the model is a hierarchical architecture that operates at multiple levels of granularity: character, subword, word, and sentence.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Word Encoder for Learning Morphology:** The primary contribution is a unique word encoder composed of two RNNs. One RNN processes the character sequence, while a second bidirectional RNN learns to compute attention-like weights over the character prefixes. This mechanism is designed to automatically identify morpheme-like units and compose them into a single word representation without relying on pre-processing steps like BPE.
2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Character-Level Decoder:** To manage the long sequences inherent in character-level decoding, the authors propose a two-level decoder. A word-level decoder generates high-level state representations, which then initialize a character-level decoder (termed HGRU). This hierarchical structure, combined with a matrix-based unfolding trick for batching, makes the training of a deep character-level model more efficient.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Qualitative Analysis:** The paper demonstrates that its model (DCNMT) achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks compared to other character-level and subword-level models. Crucially, the authors provide compelling qualitative analysis, including visualizations of word embeddings and morpheme-like unit detection, that supports their central claim of learning morphology.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Architecture:** The proposed word encoder is a key strength. It is conceptually elegant and linguistically motivated, attempting to model the compositional nature of words from morphemes. This is a more principled approach to subword modeling than data-driven segmentation algorithms like BPE, which lack linguistic grounding. The hierarchical decoder is also a clever solution to the practical challenges of training long-sequence character models.

2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experimental Analysis:** The evaluation is thorough. The authors test on language pairs with varying morphological complexity (French vs. Czech), which is crucial for a paper claiming to model morphology. The quantitative results in Table 1 show the model is highly competitive. More importantly, the qualitative analysis is excellent and provides strong evidence for the paper's claims. Figure 3 convincingly shows a more structured embedding space compared to a simpler baseline. Figures 4 and 5 provide intuitive visualizations of how the model identifies subword units, lending strong support to the "learning morphology" claim.

3.  **Demonstrated Training Efficiency and Robustness:** A major challenge for pure character-level models is their computational cost. The paper makes a strong case for its model's efficiency, showing in Table 1 that it trains significantly faster (e.g., ~19 days for En-Fr) than competing character-level models (~90 days for Luong & Manning, 2016). The strong performance after just one epoch further suggests the architecture accelerates learning. Furthermore, the examples in Table 2 effectively demonstrate a key benefit of character-level modeling—robustness to OOVs, misspellings, and morphological variations ("convenienter")—which word- or subword-level models struggle with.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Design Choices and Baselines:** Several architectural details lack sufficient justification. For instance, in the word encoder, the weights `wt` are passed through a `tanh` activation instead of a more standard `softmax` for normalization. The authors only state that this "performs best in experiments," which is an unsatisfying explanation. A deeper analysis or intuition behind this choice is needed. Similarly, the "ordinary RNN word encoder" used as a baseline for the PCA plots in Figure 3 is not clearly defined. Is it simply the final hidden state of a character-level RNN? This ambiguity weakens the comparison.

2.  **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares DCNMT to CNMT (replacing the word encoder with an "ordinary RNN"), a more comprehensive ablation study would have strengthened the paper. The proposed model has two main novelties: the word encoder and the hierarchical decoder. It is unclear how much each component contributes to the final performance. An experiment pairing the proposed word encoder with a standard flat character decoder, or a standard word encoder (e.g., CNN over characters) with the proposed hierarchical decoder, would have helped disentangle their respective contributions.

3.  **Complexity and Scalability Questions:** The full model is quite complex, involving six separate RNNs organized into four layers. While the authors demonstrate efficiency on the given tasks, the overall complexity raises questions about scalability. How does this architecture perform on much larger datasets? Does the intricate interplay between the hierarchical components introduce optimization difficulties that are not apparent in these experiments? The paper does not discuss the potential challenges or limitations of scaling such a deep and structured model.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more intuition or justification for using a `tanh` activation function for the character-prefix weights in the word encoder (Section 3.1) instead of a softmax? Does softmax normalization lead to degenerate solutions (e.g., all weight on the final character), and if so, why might that be?

2.  Could you please clarify the exact architecture of the "ordinary RNN word encoder" used as a baseline in the analysis for Figure 3? Is it the final hidden state of the character-level RNN `r_t`?

3.  Regarding the hierarchical decoder, the unfolding mechanism for batch training is clever. However, can you clarify the interaction during inference/generation? As described in Section 3.4, after generating a delimiter, the model computes the *next* state `s_{t+1}` using feedback from the *just-completed* word `r_{Yt}`. How does this feedback mechanism interact with beam search at the character level? Does the beam contain hypotheses of different word lengths, and how is the state `s` managed across these different hypotheses?
