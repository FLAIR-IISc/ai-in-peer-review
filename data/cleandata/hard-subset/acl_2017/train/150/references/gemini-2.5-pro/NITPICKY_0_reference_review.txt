**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT). The model's architecture is structured in four layers (character→subword→word→sentence), comprising six recurrent neural networks. Its main contributions are a novel source word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The word encoder uses two RNNs to compute a weighted sum of character representations, aiming to learn morphological structure automatically. The two-level decoder first operates at a word level and then uses its output to initialize a character-level RNN (HGRU) that generates the target word. The authors claim that this architecture is efficient to train, handles out-of-vocabulary words (including misspelled and nonce words), and achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art models on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks.

**Contributions**
1.  A novel word encoder architecture that learns a weighted representation of a word from its characters, which provides compelling qualitative evidence of learning morphological features.
2.  A hierarchical decoder design that combines word-level and character-level generation, along with a proposed batching mechanism, which appears to be more computationally efficient than prior pure character-level NMT models.
3.  An empirical demonstration that a fully character-level model can achieve competitive BLEU scores on both morphologically poor (En-Fr) and rich (En-Cs, Cs-En) language pairs, while also showing a qualitative ability to handle lexical noise like misspellings.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Architecture:** The core ideas of the paper—the dual-RNN word encoder for learning sub-word composition and the hierarchical decoder—are well-motivated by the desire to model morphology without explicit segmentation (like BPE) while maintaining the computational structure of word-level models. This architectural contribution is distinct from previous character-aware models.
2.  **Strong Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides a strong qualitative analysis to support its claims of learning morphology. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3, the analysis of attention weights over characters in Figure 4, and the subword segmentation examples in Figure 5 are convincing pieces of evidence that the model is indeed capturing useful sub-word linguistic structure. The ability to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" is a particularly strong demonstration.
3.  **Promising Efficiency and Performance:** The model achieves competitive BLEU scores when compared to other character-level and subword-level systems. Crucially, it appears to do so with significantly greater training efficiency than the pure character-level model of Luong and Manning (2016). The results reported after only a single epoch are impressive and lend credence to the claim that learning morphology can speed up convergence.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Technical Details:** Several critical components of the model are described with insufficient clarity and justification, hindering reproducibility and a full assessment of the method.
    *   **Word Encoder Weights (Sec 3.1):** The computation of the word representation `r = tanh(sum(w_t * r_t))` where the weights are an unnormalized exponential `w_t = exp(aff(h_t))` is highly unconventional. Without normalization (e.g., a softmax), the arguments to `tanh` could become extremely large, leading to vanishing gradients. The justification that this "performs best in experiments" is insufficient. An ablation study comparing this to a standard softmax-based attention mechanism is necessary.
    *   **Hierarchical Decoder Unfolding (Sec 3.2):** The description of the unfolding matrix `R` is confusing. The example provided ("g o !") does not logically lead to the output vector shown (`[s1, s1, s1, s2, s2]`). This mechanism is central to the model's claimed batching efficiency, but its current explanation is opaque. A formal definition and a corrected, clear example are required.
    *   **Model Complexity:** The full model is exceedingly complex, featuring six RNNs and a separate target word encoder. The necessity of this target word encoder to provide feedback `r_{Yt-1}` is not justified. A simpler approach, such as using the final state of the character-level decoder for the previous word, seems more direct and parameter-efficient. The paper lacks an ablation study to justify this added complexity.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:**
    *   **Incomplete Baselines:** Table 1, the central results table, is incomplete. It lacks model size and training time information for some key baselines (e.g., `bpe2bpe`), making a fair assessment of the model's parameter and time efficiency difficult. While results are cited from other papers, the comparison would be stronger if all relevant metrics were reported.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper claims its RNN-based word encoder is superior to CNN-based encoders (Kim et al., 2016) in generating "meaningful representation," yet it provides no experimental comparison to substantiate this. This claim remains speculative. Similarly, the CNMT baseline uses a simple RNN encoder (last hidden state), which is a weak baseline for character-composition models; comparison with a CNN or a more standard attention-based character model would be more informative.

3.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of minor errors that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference section is poorly formatted. Citations are incomplete, use inconsistent naming ("Bahdanau et al.,? 2015"), contain formatting artifacts ("\Q2015\E"), and are generally not in a standard academic format. This is a significant oversight for a submission to a top-tier conference.
    *   **Inconsistent Terminology and Figures:** There are inconsistencies between the text, equations, and figures. For instance, the end-of-sentence token is referred to as `<EOS>` in Figure 2 but `</s>` in the text. In Figure 2, the feedback arrow `r_{Yt-1}` appears to be an input to the first-level decoder, but Equation (8) uses it to compute the output `s_t`, creating confusion about the data flow. The PCA plots in Figure 3 are low-resolution, with pixelated and difficult-to-read labels.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the design choice for the word encoder's weighting scheme in Section 3.1? Specifically, why was an unnormalized exponential chosen over a standard softmax, and can you provide quantitative results from experiments that compared these two approaches?
2.  In Section 3.2, could you provide a more precise, formal definition of the unfolding matrix `R` and how it is constructed during training for a batch of sentences where target words have varying character lengths? A clear, step-by-step example would be very helpful.
3.  The model uses a dedicated RNN to encode the previously generated target word for feedback (`r_{Yt-1}`). What was the reasoning for this design instead of a simpler alternative, like using the final hidden state of the HGRU from the previously generated word as feedback? Did you experiment with this simpler variant?
