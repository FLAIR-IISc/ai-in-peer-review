**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model. The architecture processes information from characters to morphemes/subwords, to words, to sentences, and then decodes hierarchically from a word-level representation to a character-level output. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A novel character-based word encoder that uses two RNNs to compute a weighted sum of character representations, intended to capture morphological structure.
2.  A two-level hierarchical decoder combined with an efficient batching mechanism (an "unfolding matrix") that allows a word-level decoder to guide a character-level generator.
3.  An empirical demonstration that a fully character-level model can achieve competitive BLEU scores with subword-based models while being more robust to out-of-vocabulary phenomena such as misspellings and nonce words.

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective Handling of Open-Vocabulary Issues:** The primary strength of the paper is its fully character-level approach, which elegantly circumvents the OOV problem. The qualitative examples in Table 2, where the model correctly translates sentences with misspellings ("unconclusive") and morphological neologisms ("convenienter"), are compelling evidence of the model's robustness, a significant advantage over standard word-level models and a potential advantage over subword models with fixed vocabularies.
2.  **Competitive Performance with Model Efficiency:** The proposed DCNMT model achieves strong BLEU scores on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, performing on par with or better than several strong baselines, including subword-based and other character-level models. Notably, it does so with a smaller model size (~54M parameters) compared to many of its character-level competitors (~69M-76M), as shown in Table 1. This suggests a well-designed, parameter-efficient architecture.
3.  **Novel Architectural Components:** The paper introduces interesting architectural ideas. The word encoder, while similar in spirit to character-level attention, is a specific and novel formulation. The hierarchical decoder with its state-reset mechanism and the unfolding matrix for efficient batching is a clever engineering solution to the challenge of coupling decoders operating at different time scales.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Severe Lack of Clarity and Precision:** The paper is marred by significant clarity issues that hinder reproducibility and understanding.
    *   **Word Encoder (Sec 3.1):** The description of the word representation is critically ambiguous. The formula `wt = exp(aff(ht))` suggests weights can be arbitrarily large. The subsequent sentence, "Instead of normalizing it..., we use an activation function tanh," is confounding. It is unclear if `tanh` replaces the `exp`, is applied to the final sum, or something else. This lack of normalization or a clear activation function for the weights is a major methodological concern.
    *   **Hierarchical Decoder (Sec 3.2):** The mechanism is poorly described. The update in Eq. (2) reuses `gt-1` on both sides of the equation, which is notationally incorrect. The authors label their state-reset mechanism as a novel "HGRU," which is an overstatement; the core GRU equations (3-6) are standard, and the novelty lies only in the conditional state injection. The explanation of the unfolding matrix `R` is opaque, and the example is presented as a flat vector, making the matrix operation impossible to parse.
    *   **Undefined Variables:** Key variables, such as `ut-1` in Eq. (8), are used without any prior definition.

2.  **Unacceptable Formatting and Lack of Professionalism:** The submission demonstrates a profound lack of attention to detail, which is unacceptable for a top-tier conference. The entire reference section is riddled with formatting errors, likely from a failed BibTeX compilation. There are spurious question marks, malformed author lists (`Bahdanau et al\.,?`), inconsistent formatting, and artifacts like `\Q2015\E`. This carelessness severely undermines the credibility of the research and suggests the paper was not proofread. Minor but frequent grammatical errors (e.g., "effect" instead of "affect") are also present.

3.  **Overstated Claims and Weak Qualitative Analysis:** The central claim that the model "learns morphology" is not rigorously substantiated. The supporting analysis is selective and weak.
    *   The PCA plot in Figure 3 only shows a handful of adjective-noun pairs.
    *   The weight visualization in Figure 4 is cherry-picked. The claim that beginning letters also have high energy is not supported by the figure.
    *   The "subword-level boundary" detection in Figure 5 is not explained. It is unclear how "peaks" are defined, and the analysis is inconsistent: "greatest" is presented as a single unit, contradicting the narrative that the model segments morphemes. These analyses feel more like post-hoc justifications than rigorous evidence.

4.  **Inconsistent Results Reporting:** Table 1 reports BLEU scores for the proposed model after both one epoch and at convergence. This is non-standard and clutters the table. The large performance gap between the one-epoch and final scores (e.g., 28.13 to 30.56 on En-Fr) weakens the argument for accelerated learning; it simply shows the model is far from converged, which is expected.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Please provide the precise and unambiguous mathematical formulation for the word encoder weights `w_t` (Section 3.1). Are the weights normalized (e.g., via softmax)? If not, what mechanism ensures stable training?
2.  Please clarify the state update mechanism in the hierarchical decoder (Section 3.2). Specifically, provide a corrected version of Eq. (2) and a clear, dimensionally correct example of the unfolding matrix `R` in operation. What is the definition of `u_{t-1}` in Eq. (8)?
3.  Could you provide a more rigorous, systematic evaluation of the model's ability to capture morphology, beyond the selective examples in Figures 3, 4, and 5? How are the boundaries in Figure 5 detected from the model's weights?
4.  The numerous and severe formatting errors in the reference list are a major concern. Please confirm that the final version of the paper, if accepted, will be professionally proofread and corrected.
