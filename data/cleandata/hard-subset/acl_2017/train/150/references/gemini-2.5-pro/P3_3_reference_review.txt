### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-to-character Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT. The core idea is to better model linguistic structure, specifically morphology, which is often lost in standard word-level or subword-level models. The architecture operates on multiple levels: it first composes word representations from characters using a novel encoder, then encodes the source sentence at the word level, and finally decodes the target sentence via a hierarchical decoder that operates at both word and character levels.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Morphologically-Aware Word Encoder:** The paper introduces an innovative word encoder composed of two RNNs. One RNN creates representations of character prefixes, while a second bidirectional RNN computes weights for these prefixes. This acts as an intra-word attention mechanism that learns to identify and weigh meaningful sub-word units (morphemes), leading to more structured and interpretable word representations compared to standard character-level CNNs or RNNs.
2.  **A Hierarchical Word-Character Decoder:** The proposed decoder elegantly bridges the gap between word-level and character-level generation. A high-level RNN decoder operates on word-level states, providing structural guidance and context, while a low-level character RNN generates the actual output. This design mitigates the long-dependency problem inherent in purely character-level models while retaining their open-vocabulary benefits.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks, showing particular strength on the morphologically rich Czech language pair. The paper is further strengthened by insightful qualitative analyses, including visualizations that support the claim of learning morphology and compelling examples of the model's ability to handle misspelled words and unseen morphological variants (nonce words).

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Architecture:** The overall hierarchical design (character→word→sentence→word→character) is conceptually elegant and directly addresses a known weakness of both pure word-level models (OOV, morphology) and pure character-level models (long dependencies, computational cost). The explicit attempt to learn morphology at the encoder level and use a word-level scaffold at the decoder level is a very strong and intuitive modeling choice.
2.  **Novelty and Interpretability of the Word Encoder:** The proposed word encoder is a significant contribution. Unlike opaque methods like BPE or character-CNNs, the weighting mechanism offers a path toward interpretability. The analysis in Figure 4, which shows that the learned weights (energy) peak at plausible morpheme boundaries, provides compelling evidence that the model is learning meaningful linguistic structure, not just arbitrary character patterns. This is a valuable feature for understanding and improving NMT models.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The paper presents a solid empirical case for the proposed model. It is benchmarked against relevant and strong baselines from its time, including BPE-based and other character-level systems. The results in Table 1 are competitive, but the qualitative results are even more persuasive. The demonstration of robustness to misspelled words and the correct translation of the nonce word "convenienter" (Table 2) are powerful illustrations of the practical advantages of this character-level, morphology-aware approach over traditional methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in the Decoder Mechanism:** The description of the hierarchical decoder, particularly the HGRU and the "unfolding matrix", is difficult to follow and lacks sufficient justification.
    *   The state update rule in Equation (2), `gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit`, describes a hard reset of the character-level state `g` with the word-level state `s` at word boundaries. This seems abrupt and potentially discards useful information. A motivation for this design over a softer, gated update is missing.
    *   The unfolding matrix `R` is presented as a solution for efficient batch training but relies on knowing the target word boundaries in advance. It is unclear how this translates to inference time. The generation procedure (Section 3.4) is described at a high level, but the crucial detail of how the model learns to *produce* a delimiter to trigger the next word-level step is not explicitly stated, leaving a gap in the reader's understanding of the full generation process.
2.  **Absence of Ablation Studies:** The model introduces two primary novelties: the word encoder and the hierarchical decoder. However, their individual contributions are not disentangled through ablation studies. The comparison between CNMT (using a standard RNN word encoder) and DCNMT is a good start and shows the importance of the word encoder. However, a more complete study would also evaluate the novel word encoder with a standard flat character decoder, or a standard word encoder with the novel hierarchical decoder. Such analysis is crucial for understanding which component contributes most to the performance gains.
3.  **Overstated Claims on Simplicity and Efficiency:** The paper claims the model is "the simplest and the smallest one" and "efficient to train" (Section 5.1). While the parameter count may be competitive, the architecture, with six interconnected RNNs, is conceptually quite complex. Comparing training time in "days" is not a reliable metric for efficiency, as it is highly dependent on implementation and hardware. A more standardized metric like training tokens/second would be needed to substantiate the efficiency claim against other complex character-level models.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the HGRU update in Eq. (2): Could you elaborate on the motivation for the hard reset of the character-level hidden state at word boundaries? Did you experiment with alternative, softer update mechanisms (e.g., using a learned gate), and if so, how did they compare?
2.  Regarding the inference process: How does the model decide when to emit a word delimiter? Is the delimiter simply a token in the character vocabulary that the model learns to generate? If so, how is the beam search adapted to handle this, as it must search over a sequence of characters to form a word before it can be fed back into the target word encoder?
3.  The word encoder uses a `tanh` activation on the weighted sum of prefix representations, rather than a more standard normalization like softmax over the weights `w_t`. You note this performed best experimentally. Could you provide more intuition for this design choice? For instance, does avoiding the softmax constraint allow the model to assign high weights to multiple character positions, potentially corresponding to multiple morphemes within a word?
