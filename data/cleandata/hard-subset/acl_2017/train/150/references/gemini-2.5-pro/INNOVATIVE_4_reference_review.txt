### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The architecture is structured in layers, composing character representations into sub-words and words on the encoder side, and decomposing word-level concepts into characters on the decoder side. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel word encoder architecture that learns compositional word representations from characters. It uses a dual-RNN mechanism where one RNN reads the character sequence and a second, bidirectional RNN learns to assign weights to character prefixes, effectively identifying and composing morpheme-like units to form a final word vector.
2.  A hierarchical decoder composed of a word-level RNN that produces high-level semantic states and a character-level RNN (termed HGRU) that generates the character sequence for each word. This structured approach aims to combine the efficiency of word-level models with the open-vocabulary nature of character-level models.
3.  An empirical demonstration that this morphology-aware architecture is not only competitive with state-of-the-art character- and subword-level models in terms of BLEU score but is also significantly more efficient to train and exhibits a remarkable ability to handle morphologically complex phenomena like misspelled and nonce words.

### Strengths

1.  **Bold and Conceptually Novel Architecture:** The paper's main strength lies in its bold departure from prevalent NMT architectures. Instead of treating sentences as flat sequences of words, subwords, or characters, it proposes a structured, hierarchical model that explicitly attempts to mirror the linguistic composition of language (characters -> morphemes -> words -> sentences). This is a highly ambitious and refreshing approach. The idea of learning morphology not as a pre-processing step (like BPE) but as an intrinsic part of the model is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire a new line of research into more linguistically-informed neural models.

2.  **Elegant and Intuitive Word Encoder:** The design of the source word encoder is particularly creative. The use of a weighted sum of character-prefix representations, where the weights themselves are learned by another RNN, is an elegant mechanism for capturing the notion that certain parts of a word (i.e., morphemes) contribute more significantly to its overall meaning. The qualitative analyses in Figures 3 and 4 provide compelling evidence that this mechanism works as intended, producing more structured word embeddings and identifying plausible morpheme boundaries. This is a far more interesting approach than simply using the final state of a character RNN or a generic CNN encoder.

3.  **Impressive Qualitative Performance on Morphological Challenges:** The model's ability to correctly translate sentences containing misspelled words or even nonce words (e.g., "convenienter") is a powerful demonstration of its practical value. This goes beyond the typical OOV problem and shows that the model has learned generalizable morphological rules. While word-based models would fail completely and subword models might struggle, this model appears to correctly infer meaning from composition, which is a key goal of character-level modeling that is rarely achieved so convincingly.

4.  **Excellent Training Efficiency:** Despite the architectural complexity involving six RNNs, the authors demonstrate that their model is remarkably efficient to train. The claim that it achieves strong performance after just one epoch—outperforming fully-trained BPE baselines in some cases—is a strong argument. This suggests that the architectural priors that encourage learning morphology not only lead to better generalization but also provide a powerful learning signal that speeds up convergence, a highly desirable property.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Ad-hoc Design Choices in the Word Encoder:** While the word encoder is conceptually strong, some of its specific design choices feel heuristic and lack deeper justification. For example, the weights `wt` are calculated using an `exp` function but are not normalized into a proper probability distribution (e.g., via softmax). The authors simply state that applying a `tanh` to the final weighted sum "performs best in experiments." While I appreciate prioritizing empirical results, a more principled explanation for these choices, or an ablation study comparing against more standard alternatives like softmax, would make the contribution more robust and easier for others to build upon.

2.  **Decoder's Reliance on Pre-defined Word Boundaries During Training:** The efficiency of the hierarchical decoder during training depends on an "unfolding matrix" `R`, which requires knowing the locations of word delimiters in the target sentence beforehand. This dependency on word-level segmentation on the target side feels slightly at odds with the goal of a pure character-level model. While this is a clever engineering solution for efficient batching, it raises questions about how "segmentation-free" the model truly is and what the implications are for inference, where such boundaries are not known a priori.

3.  **BLEU Score Improvements are Solid but Not Overwhelming:** The quantitative results show the model is highly competitive, but they do not drastically outperform other state-of-the-art character-level or subword-level models on the standard BLEU metric. For instance, the final En-Fr score of 30.56 is a solid improvement over the BPE baseline (29.70) but is on par with other complex character-level models. The main appeal of this paper is not a new SOTA BLEU score, but rather its architectural novelty and qualitative strengths. However, a clearer quantitative edge would have made the paper's case unassailable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the word encoder's weighting mechanism? Specifically, did you experiment with normalizing the weights `wt` using a softmax function? If so, how did that impact performance and the interpretability of the learned morpheme-like segments shown in Figure 4?

2.  During inference, how does the hierarchical decoder operate without the pre-computed unfolding matrix? I assume the character-level decoder is trained to generate a delimiter token, which in turn signals the word-level decoder to produce the next state `s_{t+1}`. Could you confirm this and clarify if this mismatch between training (using the matrix `R`) and inference procedures posed any challenges?

3.  The analysis in Figure 5 showing emergent subword segmentation is very compelling. Have you considered a more quantitative evaluation of this capability? For example, by comparing the model's predicted boundaries against a linguistic gold-standard morphological segmentation to verify if it is truly learning linguistically plausible morphemes.
