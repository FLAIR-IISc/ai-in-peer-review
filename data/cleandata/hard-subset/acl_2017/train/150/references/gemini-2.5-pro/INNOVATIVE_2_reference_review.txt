### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-to-character neural machine translation model. The core idea is to learn morphological structure explicitly within the network architecture. The model is composed of two main novel components:
1.  A source-side word encoder that uses two RNNs: one to generate representations of character prefixes, and a second bidirectional RNN to compute a dynamic weight for each prefix. The final word representation is a weighted sum of these prefix representations, which the authors claim allows the model to discover and represent morphemes automatically.
2.  A hierarchical decoder consisting of a word-level RNN that generates a sequence of states, and a character-level RNN (a modified GRU dubbed HGRU) that uses these states to generate characters for each target word. This design aims to combine the efficiency of word-level decoding with the open-vocabulary nature of character-level generation.

The entire six-RNN architecture is trained end-to-end and evaluated on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks, demonstrating competitive performance, particularly on the morphologically rich Czech language pair.

### Contributions
1.  The primary contribution is a novel and conceptually interesting word encoder that learns compositional word representations from characters. By dynamically weighting character prefixes, the model appears to learn a soft, implicit segmentation of words into morpheme-like units, which is a significant departure from standard character-level RNN or CNN encoders.
2.  The paper presents a complete and ambitious deep character-level NMT system that successfully integrates the novel encoder with an efficient hierarchical decoder. The successful end-to-end training of such a complex architecture is a notable engineering achievement that makes a bold architectural idea practical.
3.  The work provides strong qualitative analysis, including visualizations and targeted examples (e.g., misspelled and nonce words), that compellingly argues the model is indeed learning aspects of morphology as claimed.

### Strengths
1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The proposed word encoder is a genuinely new and bold approach to learning word representations. The idea of using one RNN to build compositional meaning and another to determine the importance of different sub-word chunks is highly creative. It moves beyond simply processing a sequence of characters and attempts to discover the latent hierarchical structure within a word. This kind of work, which proposes a fundamentally different way of modeling a problem, is exactly what drives the field forward.
2.  **Strong Supporting Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of convincing the reader that their model works as intended. The PCA plots showing better-separated word embeddings (Fig. 3) and the visualization of "energy" peaks at plausible morpheme boundaries (Fig. 4) provide strong intuitive evidence for the morphology-learning claim. Furthermore, the model's ability to correctly translate misspelled words and, most impressively, nonce words like "convenienter" (Table 2), provides powerful functional evidence that it generalizes beyond its training vocabulary by leveraging learned morphological rules.
3.  **Ambitious yet Practical Architecture:** Designing and successfully training a six-RNN model is non-trivial. The paper’s proposal for a hierarchical decoder with the HGRU and an efficient batching strategy demonstrates thoughtful engineering to make this complex, multi-level architecture tractable. While hierarchical models have been explored, this implementation appears particularly elegant and addresses practical efficiency concerns that have hampered previous character-level models.
4.  **Empirical Validation on Relevant Tasks:** The model demonstrates its strengths where it matters most. While its performance on the morphologically simple English-French task is only comparable to baselines, it shows clearer gains against a strong subword baseline on the morphologically rich English-Czech task. This result directly supports the central hypothesis that the proposed architecture is particularly beneficial for languages with complex morphology.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The DCNMT system is complex, integrating several novelties at once. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of its key components. For instance, the main novelty lies in the source word encoder; it is unclear how much of the performance gain comes from this encoder versus the hierarchical decoder structure. While the CNMT baseline provides one point of comparison, a more granular analysis (e.g., using a standard encoder with the HGRU decoder, or the proposed encoder with a standard flat character decoder) would help disentangle the contributions of each part.
2.  **Some Hand-wavy Justifications:** A few design choices are not fully justified. The decision to use a `tanh` function instead of a more standard `softmax` for normalizing the weights in the word encoder is explained only as performing "best in experiments." While empirical results are paramount, a deeper intuition or analysis of why this non-standard choice is effective would make the method feel less heuristic.
3.  **Performance is Not State-of-the-Art:** The reported BLEU scores are competitive but do not decisively outperform all baselines, particularly the BPE-based model on the En-Fr task. This is a minor point, as the paper's value lies in its novel approach rather than squeezing out marginal gains on a benchmark. However, the performance gains, even on the morphologically rich language pairs, are modest. This might suggest that while the idea is promising, the current instantiation has not yet unlocked its full potential.

### Overall Recommendation
This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It presents a bold, novel, and conceptually rich approach to a fundamental problem in NMT: representing words and handling morphology. The core idea of the self-weighting word encoder is highly creative and well-motivated. While the empirical results are not groundbreaking, they are solid and provide clear evidence that the model works as intended, especially on morphologically rich languages. The paper's main strength is not in setting a new state-of-the-art BLEU score, but in introducing a new direction for thinking about compositional word representation, backed by compelling qualitative analysis. The minor weaknesses, such as the lack of exhaustive ablations, do not detract from the significance of the core contribution. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking and innovative research that should be highlighted at our conference.
