### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation model with a hierarchical architecture. The core idea is to bridge the character and sentence levels by introducing intermediate representations for words. On the encoder side, a novel word encoder generates a vector for each source word by computing a weighted sum of its character-prefix representations. On the decoder side, a two-level hierarchical decoder uses a word-level RNN to generate a context vector for each target word, which in turn initializes a character-level RNN that generates the individual characters of that word. The authors claim that this architecture allows the model to learn morphology, handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words effectively, and achieve performance competitive with state-of-the-art character- and subword-level models.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The proposal of a novel, fully character-level NMT architecture that imposes a hierarchical structure (character -> word -> sentence) to manage the long dependencies inherent in character-level modeling.
2.  An empirical demonstration that a complex character-level model can achieve competitive BLEU scores against strong subword-based baselines, particularly on translation tasks involving a morphologically rich language (Czech).

### Strengths

1.  **Effective Handling of Open-Vocabulary Issues:** The model's character-level nature provides a principled solution to the OOV problem that plagues word-level models. The qualitative examples in Table 2, showing the model's ability to correctly translate misspelled and nonce words (e.g., "unconclusive," "convenienter"), are a compelling demonstration of this strength. This is a significant practical advantage over models that rely on a fixed vocabulary, including many subword approaches where rare morphemes might still be segmented incorrectly.
2.  **Competitive Performance on Morphologically Rich Languages:** The model achieves strong results on the En-Cs and Cs-En translation tasks, outperforming the reported BPE baseline. This provides evidence that explicitly modeling word structure from characters can be beneficial for languages where morphology plays a critical role, a setting where standard word-level or even subword-level models can struggle.
3.  **Novel Architectural Concepts:** The paper presents an interesting and ambitious architecture. The idea of a hierarchical decoder that separates the prediction of "what to say" (the word-level decoder) from "how to spell it" (the character-level decoder) is conceptually appealing. While the implementation details raise questions, this high-level concept is a valuable contribution to the exploration of character-level sequence-to-sequence models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Excessive and Poorly Justified Model Complexity:** The proposed architecture is exceedingly complex, involving six distinct RNNs and a custom-designed HGRU cell. This complexity is not rigorously justified through ablation studies. It is unclear if all components are necessary for the reported performance. For instance, is the complex weighted-prefix word encoder significantly better than a standard, well-established character-level CNN or BiRNN encoder? Is the hierarchical decoder superior to a deeper, non-hierarchical character-level decoder? Without these ablations, the contributions of the individual novel components cannot be disentangled from the effect of simply having a large, deep model.
2.  **Weak Motivation for Key Design Choices:** Several important design choices lack clear motivation and seem arbitrary. A prominent example is in the word encoder (Section 3.1), where the attention-like weights `w_t` are computed and then passed through a `tanh` activation function rather than being normalized with a softmax. The only justification provided is that it "performs best in experiments." This is unscientific and provides no insight. A principled explanation or a more thorough empirical comparison with alternatives like softmax is needed to validate such an unconventional choice.
3.  **Overstated and Insufficiently Supported Claims of "Learning Morphology":** The central claim of the paper—that the model learns morphology—is supported by weak, qualitative evidence. The PCA projections in Figure 3 compare the proposed encoder to a simple "ordinary RNN," which is a strawman baseline. A comparison to a more standard character-composition model (e.g., using CNNs) would be necessary to demonstrate superiority. Furthermore, the visualizations of internal "energy" weights (Figure 4) are open to interpretation and do not constitute rigorous proof of morphological analysis. The claim is a significant one and requires much stronger, more quantitative evidence than is provided.
4.  **Inadequate Comparison to Baselines:** The primary baseline for practical NMT systems at the time of this work is the subword model based on Byte Pair Encoding (BPE). The paper's comparison relies on BLEU scores cited from other published works (e.g., Firat et al., 2016), which may have used different data preprocessing, model sizes, or training procedures. For a convincing comparison, a strong BPE baseline should have been implemented and trained by the authors under identical conditions (e.g., same data, comparable parameter count, similar training time/computation budget). As presented, it is difficult to conclude that the proposed model is definitively superior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide an ablation study that isolates the impact of your novel word encoder? Specifically, how does DCNMT perform if the word encoder is replaced by a more standard character-level encoder, such as a multi-layer CNN with max-pooling or a simple BiGRU that takes the final hidden state?
2.  Regarding the word encoder, can you provide a more detailed empirical comparison and justification for using a `tanh` activation on the unnormalized weights `w_t` instead of a standard softmax function? What is the performance difference, and can you offer a hypothesis for this result?
3.  The comparison to BPE models is critical for evaluating the practical utility of your method. Could you clarify whether you trained your own BPE baseline system? If not, can you provide more details on why the cited results from other papers are a fair point of comparison, particularly regarding training data, model capacity, and overall training effort?
