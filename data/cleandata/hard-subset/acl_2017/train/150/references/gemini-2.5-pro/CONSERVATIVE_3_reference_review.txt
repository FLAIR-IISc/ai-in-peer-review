**Summary**

This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model built on a hierarchical architecture. The core contributions are two-fold: (1) a novel word encoder that composes a word representation from its characters by learning a weighted sum of character-level RNN hidden states, with the weights themselves learned by a separate BiRNN, and (2) a hierarchical decoder that uses a word-level RNN to provide states to a second-level character-generating RNN. The authors claim this architecture allows the model to learn morphology, efficiently train an end-to-end character-level system, and achieve results comparable or superior to state-of-the-art subword and character-level models, particularly on morphologically rich languages. The model is evaluated on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves competitive BLEU scores on all three language pairs. In particular, it shows a significant improvement over the widely-adopted BPE subword baseline on the morphologically rich Cs-En and En-Cs tasks (+2.16 and +3.12 BLEU, respectively). Achieving results on par with or exceeding other complex character-level models is a notable accomplishment.

2.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Non-Standard Words:** The paper provides compelling qualitative examples (Table 2) demonstrating the model's ability to correctly translate sentences containing misspelled words and nonce words (e.g., "convenienter"). This is a clear advantage over standard word-level or even subword-level models and highlights the practical benefits of a truly character-level approach. This provides strong evidence for the model's robustness to vocabulary variations.

3.  **Interesting Qualitative Analysis:** The authors include visualizations (Figures 3, 4, 5) attempting to interpret what the word encoder has learned. The PCA plots suggest the proposed encoder produces more semantically coherent representations than a vanilla RNN encoder, and the "energy" plots provide an intuitive, albeit anecdotal, look at how the model might be identifying morpheme-like units. This effort to understand the model's internal workings is commendable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Novel Word Encoder:** The primary architectural innovation, the word encoder, is not sufficiently validated against strong, established alternatives. The paper compares its encoder to an "ordinary RNN word encoder" that simply takes the final hidden state. This is a known weak baseline for composing representations, as it suffers from information loss. A thorough comparison should include more standard and powerful baselines, such as:
    *   A bidirectional RNN encoder, using the concatenation of the final forward and backward states.
    *   A CNN-based character encoder (e.g., Kim et al., 2016), which is a very common and effective method for this task.
    The claim that the proposed model "is able to generate a meaningful representation of the word" in contrast to CNN encoders is asserted without evidence. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the added complexity of the proposed dual-RNN encoder provides a meaningful advantage over simpler, well-established methods.

2.  **The "Learning Morphology" Claim is Overstated:** The evidence presented for the model "learning morphology" is based on a few cherry-picked examples and visualizations. While these are suggestive, they do not constitute rigorous proof. For example, the segmentation shown in Figure 5 is interesting but lacks quantitative evaluation. Do the high-energy points consistently align with linguistic morpheme boundaries across a large, annotated dataset? Without such analysis, the claim remains speculative. The model appears to be learning effective compositional representations, but equating this directly with learning linguistic morphology is a strong claim that is not fully substantiated.

3.  **Architectural Complexity vs. Modest Gains over Baselines:** The proposed "DCNMT" model is highly complex, involving six separate RNNs organized in a multi-level hierarchy. While the results are strong, the performance gains over some of the state-of-the-art baselines are not dramatic. For instance, on En-Fr, the gain over the much simpler BPE method is less than 1 BLEU point. On Cs-En, the result is identical to the `char2char` model of Lee et al. (2016). It is unclear if the substantial increase in architectural and conceptual complexity is justified by the performance improvements, especially when a robust and simple method like BPE remains a very strong competitor.

4.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper proposes a complex system with several new components (the specific word encoder, the hierarchical decoder with HGRU). However, there are no ablation studies to disentangle the effects of these components. How much of the performance gain comes from the word encoder versus the hierarchical decoder? How does the proposed HGRU compare to a simpler, non-hierarchical character decoder fed with word-level context? This makes it difficult to understand which parts of the model are actually contributing to its success and whether simpler combinations could achieve similar results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify why you chose to compare your word encoder only against a unidirectional RNN? To better situate your contribution, could you provide results comparing it against more standard character-composition baselines, such as a BiRNN or a CNN encoder, within your NMT framework?

2.  The claim of learning morphology is a central motivation for your work. Beyond the qualitative examples, have you considered a more quantitative evaluation of this claim? For example, by comparing the subword segmentations induced by your encoder's "energy" peaks against a gold-standard morphological lexicon (e.g., Celex or Morfessor)?

3.  To help understand the contribution of each component, would it be possible to provide an ablation study? Specifically, what is the performance if you replace your proposed word encoder with a standard BiRNN encoder, or replace your hierarchical decoder with a standard single-level character decoder conditioned on the same word-level context `st`?
