### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The architecture is hierarchical and consists of two primary novel components. First, a sophisticated word encoder that uses a dual-RNN structure to compute word representations from characters. One RNN processes the character sequence, while a second bidirectional RNN computes an attention-like weight for each character's hidden state, allowing the model to dynamically learn the composition of morphemes. Second, a hierarchical decoder that operates at two levels: a word-level RNN decoder generates a sequence of abstract word representations, and a character-level RNN decoder (using a custom "HGRU" cell) takes each of these representations and generates the corresponding character sequence for the target word. The authors evaluate this model on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation, arguing that it achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art character-level models while being more efficient and capable of learning meaningful morphological representations.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel, structured word encoder for learning morphology.** The paper's most significant contribution is the design of a character-level word encoder that attempts to explicitly model morphological composition. Instead of using a standard RNN or CNN, the proposed dual-RNN architecture with an internal weighting mechanism is a creative and principled attempt to have the model automatically discover and weight sub-word units (morphemes) when forming a word representation. This is a bold architectural choice motivated by linguistic intuition.

2.  **An efficient, end-to-end hierarchical character-to-character NMT model.** The paper presents a complete and elegant architecture that operates purely on characters, thus solving the OOV problem by design. The hierarchical nature of the decoder, which conceptually separates word-level and character-level generation, is a clever way to maintain some of the structural advantages of word-level models while retaining the full flexibility of character-level generation. The proposed HGRU and unfolding matrix are solid engineering contributions that make this complex, six-RNN model tractable and efficient to train.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The central idea of designing a specific neural module to learn morphology is highly innovative and refreshing. It moves away from the trend of applying ever-larger, generic architectures and instead proposes a structured, linguistically-motivated model. The word encoder's design is particularly compelling, as it provides a mechanism for learning sub-word composition in a data-driven way, which is conceptually more satisfying than relying on pre-processing heuristics like BPE. This is precisely the kind of ambitious, idea-driven research that pushes the field in new directions.

2.  **Strong Qualitative Evidence for Morphological Learning:** The authors provide excellent qualitative analysis to back their claims. The PCA visualizations (Fig. 3) convincingly suggest that their proposed encoder creates a more semantically structured embedding space than a standard RNN. More impressively, the analysis of the internal weights (Fig. 4) provides an intuitive glimpse into the model's "reasoning," showing it assigns higher importance to characters at morpheme boundaries. The ability to correctly translate nonce words ("convenienter") and misspelled words is a powerful, practical demonstration that the model has learned generalizable morphological rules rather than simply memorizing vocabulary. This evidence is more persuasive than a minor gain in BLEU score.

3.  **Addresses a Key NMT Problem in a Principled Manner:** The model provides an end-to-end solution to the OOV problem, which has long plagued word-level NMT. By operating entirely on characters, it can handle any input word and generate any output word. The claim that this approach also speeds up initial learning—outperforming a BPE baseline after just one epoch on some tasks—is a significant practical benefit that further strengthens the case for this architectural direction.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Quantitative Results are Competitive but Not Decisive:** While the model performs well, its final BLEU scores do not consistently or significantly outperform other contemporary character-level or subword-level models. For instance, on En-Cs, it is slightly behind the model of Luong and Manning (2016), and on Cs-En, it is on par with Lee et al. (2016). While the authors rightly point to their model's superior training speed, the lack of a clear state-of-the-art BLEU score might be seen as a weakness. A more direct comparison with a CNN-based character encoder would have also helped to better isolate the benefits of the proposed morphological encoder versus other character-level composition functions.

2.  **Architectural Complexity:** The proposed model is quite complex, involving six RNNs organized across four hierarchical layers. The description of the hierarchical decoder, particularly the HGRU state updates and the unfolding matrix `R`, is dense and requires careful reading to fully grasp. While the authors demonstrate its efficiency, this complexity could be a barrier to reproduction and further analysis. An ablation study on the hierarchical nature of the decoder itself would have been welcome.

3.  **Generality of Morphological Claims:** The analysis of morphological learning is performed on English, a language with primarily concatenative morphology. The strong claims about "learning morphology" would be more robust if tested or at least discussed in the context of languages with more complex, non-concatenative morphology (e.g., Semitic languages). This is not a major flaw but rather a limitation on the scope of the current validation.

### Overall Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and exciting direction for neural machine translation. The main contribution—a linguistically-motivated architecture for learning morphology—is a significant conceptual advance. The authors support their bold idea with compelling qualitative evidence that suggests the model is indeed learning meaningful sub-word structures. While the quantitative evaluation is not overwhelmingly superior to all baselines, the performance is strong, and the reported training efficiency is a major plus. The paper's weaknesses, such as its complexity and the lack of a knockout BLEU score, are minor compared to the strength and novelty of its core ideas. This work prioritizes a significant new concept over incremental methodological improvement, and it has the potential to inspire future research into more structured and interpretable neural models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
