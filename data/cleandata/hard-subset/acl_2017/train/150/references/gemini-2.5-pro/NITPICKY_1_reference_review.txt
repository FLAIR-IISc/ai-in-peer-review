### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT. The goal is to leverage the benefits of character-level modeling (e.g., handling rare words and morphology) while maintaining the computational tractability of word-level models.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel character-level word encoder designed to learn morphology. It uses two RNNs: one to build representations of character prefixes and a second (bidirectional) one to compute unnormalized attention-like weights over these prefixes. The weighted sum of prefixes forms the final word representation.
2.  A hierarchical decoder architecture composed of a word-level decoder RNN and a character-level decoder RNN (HGRU). The state of the word-level decoder is used to initialize the state of the character-level decoder at the beginning of each word, which then generates characters until a delimiter is produced.
3.  An experimental evaluation on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation, demonstrating competitive performance against subword- and other character-level models, along with qualitative analysis suggesting the model learns morphological patterns.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Architecture for Morphological Learning:** The proposed word encoder is an interesting and novel attempt to explicitly model the composition of morphemes within a word. By using a weighted combination of character-prefix representations, it provides an inductive bias that appears to be effective, as suggested by the qualitative analyses in Figures 3 and 4. This is a more structured approach than standard character-level CNNs or RNNs.
2.  **Competitive Empirical Performance:** The model achieves strong BLEU scores on multiple language pairs, including a morphologically rich language (Czech). The results are comparable to or better than several strong baselines, including BPE-based and other character-level systems (Table 1), validating the overall effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
3.  **Fast Convergence/Training Efficiency:** The results reported after just one epoch are surprisingly strong, in some cases outperforming fully trained BPE baselines. This suggests that the model's architecture provides a powerful learning signal that allows it to converge much more quickly than competitors, which is a significant practical advantage.
4.  **Effective Handling of OOV phenomena:** The paper demonstrates a key advantage of character-level models by showing the system can successfully translate sentences containing misspelled words and morphological neologisms ("nonce words") like "convenienter" (Table 2). While this is an expected benefit of character-level modeling, the provided examples are compelling.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Justification:** Several core design choices are poorly justified and deviate from standard practice without explanation.
    *   In the word encoder (Section 3.1), the weights `wt` are computed via `exp(aff(ht))` and are not normalized (e.g., via a softmax). The authors state, "Instead of normalizing it..., we use an activation function tanh as it performs best in experiments." This is a critical architectural detail that is justified only by a single, unsubstantiated clause. Without an ablation study or a more detailed explanation, this choice seems arbitrary and makes the model dynamics difficult to reason about (e.g., potential for exploding values before the final tanh).
    *   Key components of the model are not clearly defined. For example, `u_{t-1}` in Equation (8) is used without any prior definition. It is presumably the previous hidden state of the first-level decoder, but this should be stated explicitly.
    *   The claim that the target-side feedback is "less important" and thus justifies a simpler RNN encoder is presented without any supporting evidence.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Details and Ablation Studies:** The experimental setup is not described with enough detail to ensure reproducibility.
    *   The beam size used for decoding is never mentioned. This is a critical hyperparameter that has a major impact on NMT performance and decoding speed, and its omission is a serious flaw.
    *   The learning rate annealing schedule is vaguely described as being "annealed to 10^-4," with no details on the trigger (e.g., validation perplexity plateau, fixed epoch number). The tokenizer used is only described as "the usual tokenization."
    *   The paper lacks crucial ablation studies. The central claim is that the novel word encoder is effective, but it is only compared against a vanilla RNN encoder (CNMT). How does it compare to a more standard character-level CNN encoder? What is the performance impact of using a softmax for the weights `wt` versus the unnormalized `exp`/`tanh` combination? Without these ablations, it is difficult to isolate the source of the performance gains.

3.  **Poor Presentation Quality and Numerous Errors:** The paper is marred by a substantial number of typographical, grammatical, and formatting errors, which detracts from its scientific credibility.
    *   There are grammatical errors (e.g., "which can effect the performance" instead of "affect"; "it is much difficult to train" instead of "more difficult") and typos ("computation resource").
    *   The References section is exceptionally poorly formatted, containing numerous BibTeX errors such as extraneous characters (`?`), failed macros (`\Q2015\E`), and repeated author blocks ("Bahdanau et al.,? 2015, Bahdanau et al.,"). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier publication and suggests a lack of care in the paper's preparation.
    *   Figure 5, intended to show subword segmentation, is cluttered with `<unk>` tokens and lacks a clear explanation of how "peak energy" is used to identify boundaries, making it difficult to interpret.

4.  **Superficial Analysis:** The qualitative analysis, while providing some intuition, relies on cherry-picked examples. The comparison in Table 2 against commercial systems like Google Translate is not a rigorous scientific comparison; the correct baselines would be the authors' own word-level or BPE models to demonstrate that the observed robustness is a direct result of their character-level architecture and not just a general property of any strong NMT system.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a technical justification for using an unnormalized `exp()` followed by a global `tanh` in the word encoder, rather than a standard softmax over the character positions? An ablation study comparing these two options would be highly valuable.
2.  What was the beam size used for decoding in your experiments? Given the character-level search space, this is a critical detail for both performance and computational cost.
3.  Could you please clarify the precise definition of `u_{t-1}` in Equation (8) and provide specific details on the learning rate annealing schedule and tokenization method used?
4.  The numerous and significant formatting errors in the reference list undermine the paper's professionalism. Can you confirm that these will be thoroughly corrected in any final version?
