### **Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a deep, purely character-level neural machine translation model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The architecture is hierarchical, comprising four main layers: (1) a novel character-based word encoder that composes a word representation from its characters, (2) a standard BiRNN sentence encoder operating on these word representations, (3) a word-level RNN decoder that predicts the representation of the next target word, and (4) a character-level RNN decoder that generates the characters for each predicted word representation. The key novelties are the word encoder, which uses two coupled RNNs to learn a weighted combination of character-prefix representations, and the hierarchical decoder, which uses a modified GRU (HGRU) to efficiently switch between word-level and character-level generation. The authors evaluate their model on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, demonstrating competitive BLEU scores against subword and other character-level models, particularly on the morphologically rich Czech language pairs. The paper includes a strong qualitative analysis to support its central claim that the model effectively learns morphological structure.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Character-Level NMT Architecture:** The primary contribution is the end-to-end hierarchical architecture that bridges characters, words, and sentences. The design of the decoder, with a word-level RNN controlling a character-level one via a resettable state (HGRU), is an elegant and effective way to manage the different levels of granularity. The proposed "unfolding matrix" method for efficient batch training is a noteworthy technical contribution.
2.  **A Compositional Word Encoder for Learning Morphology:** The paper introduces a unique dual-RNN word encoder. One RNN computes representations of character prefixes, while a second BiRNN computes unnormalized weights for these representations. The resulting weighted sum is intended to model how morphemes compose to form a word's meaning. The qualitative analysis showing that this model learns plausible morphological segmentations and semantically structured embeddings is a significant finding.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis for a Character-Level Model:** The work provides a thorough evaluation showing that a purely character-level model can be trained efficiently and achieve results comparable or superior to strong subword-based (BPE) baselines. The analysis of the model's ability to handle OOV words, misspelled words, and nonce words with plausible morphological structures ("convenienter") effectively highlights the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The architecture is intuitively appealing and directly addresses a clear shortcoming of word-level NMT: the inability to model the internal structure of words. The hierarchical decomposition of the translation process from sentence-to-word and then word-to-character is a very natural approach. The specific mechanisms, like the HGRU, are clever solutions to the practical challenges of implementing such a hierarchy.
2.  **Compelling Qualitative Evidence for Morphological Learning:** The paper's strongest aspect is its analysis supporting the claim of "learning morphology". Figure 3, showing better-separated word embeddings, Figure 4, visualizing learned weights that align with morpheme boundaries, and Figure 5, demonstrating plausible subword segmentation, collectively provide strong evidence that the model is not just a black box but is learning interpretable, linguistically-grounded representations.
3.  **Strong and Efficient Performance:** The model achieves competitive BLEU scores, outperforming the widely-used BPE approach on the morphologically rich En-Cs and Cs-En tasks. Crucially, it does so with a relatively small parameter count and impressive training efficiency (e.g., strong results after just one epoch), which counters the common criticism that character-level models are too slow and difficult to train.
4.  **Clear Exposition and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is described in sufficient detail, and the figures are highly effective at illustrating the core concepts. The authors provide enough detail about their training setup to facilitate reproduction.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconventional and Under-justified Word Encoder Weighting:** The method for combining character-prefix representations in the word encoder (`r_word = tanh(sum(wt * rt))`) is unorthodox. The weights `wt` are derived from an unnormalized `exp()` function, meaning they are not a probability distribution and can be arbitrarily large. The final `tanh` squashing function is then applied to the entire sum. The authors mention this "performs best in experiments" but provide no further justification, ablation study, or intuition for why this is preferable to a standard attention mechanism (i.e., softmax-normalized weights). This lack of analysis on a core component of their proposed model is a notable weakness.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Competing Character Composition Models:** The paper dismisses CNN-based word encoders (e.g., Kim et al., 2016) with the brief claim that their model "is able to generate a meaningful representation," but provides no direct comparison or evidence to support this. CNNs are a very strong and common baseline for character-level composition due to their efficiency and effectiveness. The lack of a direct comparison with a CNN-based word encoder within their own framework is a missed opportunity and leaves the superiority of their more complex dual-RNN encoder unsubstantiated.
3.  **Ambiguity in the Generation/Decoding Process:** While the training process is clear, the description of generation (Section 3.4) is brief. Specifically, during beam search at the character level, it is unclear how the word-level feedback (`rYt-1`) is handled. After a delimiter is generated for a given hypothesis in the beam, the model must encode the characters generated since the last delimiter. This process and its interaction with maintaining the beam of character-level hypotheses needs more detailed explanation to fully understand the decoding-time dynamics and computational cost.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the design of the word encoder's weighting mechanism? An ablation study comparing your unnormalized `exp()` + `tanh` approach against a more standard softmax-based attention mechanism would significantly strengthen the paper by justifying this unconventional choice.
2.  In your analysis of learned morphology (Fig. 5), you show "peak energy" corresponding to subword boundaries. Could you clarify how these peaks are defined and detected from the model's weights `w_t`? Is it simply a visualization of local maxima in the weight sequence?
3.  Regarding the beam search decoding process, when a hypothesis generates a delimiter, do you immediately run the target word encoder on the preceding characters to generate the feedback vector `rYt` for that specific hypothesis? How does this interact with other hypotheses in the beam that may not have generated a delimiter at that same step?
