### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) designed to learn morphological information automatically and handle the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem. The model is structured as a four-layer hierarchy: (1) a source-side character-to-word encoder, (2) a word-level sentence encoder, (3) a word-level decoder, and (4) a character-level decoder.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Word Encoder for Learning Subword Structure:** The primary contribution is the source word encoder architecture. Instead of using a standard RNN or CNN, it employs two coupled RNNs: one generates character-level representations, and a second bidirectional RNN computes attention-like weights over these representations. This weighted sum forms the final word embedding. The authors provide compelling qualitative analysis suggesting this structure effectively identifies and weights character sequences corresponding to morphemes.

2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Decoder:** The paper introduces a hierarchical decoder that operates at both the word and character levels. To make this complex structure efficient for batch training, it proposes a specialized Gated Recurrent Unit (HGRU) and an "unfolding matrix" mechanism. This is a clever engineering solution that allows a single forward pass to manage the different time scales of word and character generation, addressing a key implementation challenge for such models.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and In-depth Analysis:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed model achieves BLEU scores competitive with state-of-the-art subword and character-level systems on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks. Crucially, the authors provide strong supporting analysis through visualizations (PCA plots of word embeddings, energy plots of intra-word weights) and qualitative examples (handling of misspelled and nonce words) that lend significant credibility to their claim of learning morphology.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Word Encoder:** The design of the word encoder is the paper's strongest point. It is a novel form of intra-word attention that is explicitly designed to model how morphemes compose to form a word's meaning. The visualizations in Figure 3 and Figure 4 provide convincing evidence that the model is learning meaningful sub-structures within words, which is a significant step beyond simply treating a word as a flat sequence of characters. The ability to identify morpheme boundaries (e.g., in "any-one", "every-thing") without explicit supervision is impressive.

2.  **Practical Solution to Hierarchical Decoding:** Character-level decoders are notoriously slow. Hybrid word/character decoders often require inefficient workarounds for batching. The proposed HGRU and unfolding matrix approach is a practical and well-thought-out solution that makes the hierarchical architecture feasible to train efficiently. This technical contribution is valuable for future work on models that operate at multiple granularities.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Analysis:** The paper excels in its analysis section. Beyond just reporting BLEU scores, the authors investigate *what* their model has learned. The PCA projection (Figure 3) effectively shows that their encoder produces more semantically structured representations than a standard RNN encoder. The energy plots (Figure 4) provide a clear, interpretable view into the model's inner workings. Finally, the examples of translating misspelled and nonce words (Table 2) are a powerful demonstration of the practical benefits of their character-level approach over standard word/subword models.

4.  **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves strong BLEU scores that are comparable to or better than several strong baselines, including BPE-based models and other character-level systems on multiple language pairs. This demonstrates that the proposed architecture is not just an interesting idea but a high-performing NMT system. The reported training efficiency is also a notable advantage over some other purely character-level models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Hierarchical Decoder Mechanism:** The description of the HGRU and the unfolding matrix in Section 3.2 is quite dense and could be improved for clarity. For example, in Equation 2, the indexing of `sit` is ambiguous. Since `t` is the character-level timestep, it's unclear how `sit` (the word-level decoder state) is selected. The matrix example helps, but a more formal and step-by-step walkthrough of how the matrix `R` is constructed and used with the word-level states `s` during batched training would greatly enhance the reader's understanding of this core mechanism.

2.  **Lack of Essential Ablation Studies:** The paper introduces a complex architecture with several novel components, but it lacks a thorough ablation study to justify each part. The most critical missing comparison is between the proposed DCNMT and the CNMT baseline (which uses a simpler RNN word encoder). In Table 1, CNMT is only shown for En-Fr, where its performance is mixed compared to DCNMT (better on Dev, worse on Test). CNMT results are completely absent for the morphologically rich En-Cs and Cs-En tasks, where the proposed word encoder is claimed to be most beneficial. A direct comparison on all tasks is necessary to isolate the true performance gain from the novel word encoder versus other architectural choices.

3.  **Overstated Claim of "Learning Morphology":** While the analysis is strong, the model's behavior may be better described as learning "useful subword units" or "effective compositional representations" rather than "learning morphology" in a strict linguistic sense. For instance, in the subword segmentation analysis (Figure 5), the word "greatest" is treated as a single unit rather than being segmented into "great-est". This suggests the model identifies statistically relevant chunks, which may not always align with true morphemes. Softening this claim would make the paper more precise.

4.  **Insufficient Discussion of Complexity Trade-offs:** The model consists of six RNNs and operates on much longer character sequences than word/BPE models. While the authors claim training is efficient (e.g., ~19 days for En-Fr), this is still substantial. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the trade-offs. For a ~1 BLEU point improvement over BPE on En-Fr, is the significant increase in architectural and computational complexity justified for all use cases? A discussion of inference speed would also be highly relevant here.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the mechanism for aligning the word-level decoder states `s` with the character-level HGRU updates during batched training? Specifically, in Eq. (2), `gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit`, how is the word-level index `i` for `s` determined from the character-level index `t`?

2.  Why were the results for the CNMT baseline omitted for the English-Czech and Czech-English experiments in Table 1? A comprehensive comparison between CNMT and DCNMT across all tasks seems crucial for validating the effectiveness of your proposed word encoder, especially on morphologically rich languages.

3.  Regarding the analysis in Figure 5, the model does not segment the word "greatest" into its morphemes "great" and "-est". Does this indicate that the model is learning statistically significant character n-grams rather than true morphological rules in all cases? Could you comment on the limitations of your model's ability to learn morphology in a linguistically precise manner?
