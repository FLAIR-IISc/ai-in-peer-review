### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces a deep, hierarchical character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT, designed to overcome the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and better handle morphology compared to word or subword-level models. The model's key components are a novel word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The word encoder uses two RNNs to compositionally build a word representation from its characters: one RNN generates representations for character prefixes, while a second BiRNN computes weights for these representations, which are then combined in a weighted sum. This is intended to explicitly learn the structure of morphemes. The hierarchical decoder operates on two levels: a word-level decoder generates a state vector for each target word, which in turn initializes a character-level decoder that generates the characters for that word. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive BLEU scores on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation, particularly outperforming BPE-based models on the morphologically rich Czech tasks. They also provide qualitative analyses suggesting the model learns meaningful morphological representations and can successfully translate misspelled or unseen nonce words.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and intuitive word encoder architecture for learning compositional word representations from characters.** The use of a second RNN to learn weights for character-prefix representations is an elegant method for modeling morphology, supported by compelling qualitative visualizations.
2.  **An efficient, fully character-to-character NMT model with a hierarchical decoder.** The two-level decoder design cleverly avoids the computational burden of single-level character models with very long sequences, and the proposed mechanisms for batch training make this deep architecture practical.
3.  **Strong empirical evidence for the benefits of character-level morphological modeling, especially for morphologically rich languages.** The paper shows state-of-the-art results for its time on En-Cs and Cs-En translation and provides excellent examples of handling linguistic phenomena (misspellings, nonce words) that are impossible for standard word-level models.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of the Proposed Architecture.** The core ideas of the DCNMT are well-motivated and technically sound. The word encoder's design to learn a weighted combination of prefixes is a clear and clever way to induce morphological structure. The hierarchical decoder is an effective strategy for managing the sequence length problem in character-level NMT. The combination results in a coherent end-to-end model that directly addresses the weaknesses of both word-level and naive character-level approaches.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Results.** The model achieves strong performance, posting BLEU scores that are competitive with or superior to other character-level and subword-level models on multiple language pairs. The improvements on the morphologically rich En-Cs and Cs-En tasks are particularly significant and provide strong validation for the paper's central hypothesis. Furthermore, the claim of faster learning (e.g., strong performance after just one epoch) is an important practical advantage.
3.  **Excellent Qualitative Analysis.** The authors go beyond BLEU scores to investigate *what* their model has learned. The PCA projections of word embeddings (Fig. 3), the visualization of morpheme-boundary "energies" (Fig. 4), and the examples of translating misspelled and nonce words (Table 2) are highly persuasive. These analyses provide strong evidence that the model is not just a black box but is genuinely learning aspects of morphology, which directly supports the paper's title and claims.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation.** The paper is well-written, and the complex architecture is explained clearly with the help of effective diagrams (Fig. 1 and 2). The experimental setup is thorough, using standard datasets and comparing against relevant, strong baselines.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Design Choices.** Several critical design choices are asserted to be optimal without sufficient explanation or ablation.
    *   In the word encoder (Sec 3.1), the weights `w_t` are not normalized (e.g., via softmax). The authors state that using `tanh` on the final sum "performs best in experiments," but this is a major departure from standard attention mechanisms. Without normalization, the "energy" interpretation is less clear, and potential training instability is a concern. An ablation study or a more detailed hypothesis for this effect is needed.
    *   The feedback mechanism in the decoder (Sec 3.3) uses "an ordinary RNN to encode the target word" because feedback is "less important." This claim is not substantiated and seems counter-intuitive; one might expect the more sophisticated encoder to be used on both source and target sides for consistency.
2.  **Lack of Clarity in the Hierarchical Decoder Mechanism.** The description of the HGRU and the "unfolding matrix" `R` is overly dense and difficult to fully understand.
    *   Equation (2), `gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit`, appears to be a typo, as `gt-1` is defined in terms of itself. This crucial step—how the word-level state is injected into the character-level RNN—needs to be specified correctly and clearly.
    *   The unfolding matrix `R` is a key component for efficient batching, but the explanation is too brief. A more detailed walkthrough, perhaps with a small batch containing words of different lengths, would be necessary for reproducibility.
3.  **Inadequate Comparison with CNN Encoders.** The paper dismisses CNN-based character encoders with the brief statement that their RNN-based model generates "a meaningful representation," but provides no direct evidence or comparison. Given that CNNs are a very strong baseline for character-level composition (as shown by Kim et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2016, cited in the paper), the lack of a direct empirical comparison within their framework is a notable omission. An ablation study swapping their encoder with a standard CNN encoder would have significantly strengthened their claims about the superiority of their specific architecture.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the word encoder's weighting scheme: could you please elaborate on the decision not to normalize the weights `w_t`? What was the empirical performance difference you observed compared to a standard softmax normalization, and can you offer any intuition as to why the unnormalized version performed better?
2.  In the description of the HGRU (Section 3.2), Equation (2) seems to contain a typo. Could you please provide the correct formulation for how the character-level state `g` is updated based on the delimiter indicator `a_{t-1}` and the word-level state `s_{it}`?
3.  The paper's claim that the proposed word encoder is superior to CNN-based alternatives is a strong one. Do you have any results from ablation studies that directly compare your proposed encoder against a standard CNN character encoder within the same DCNMT framework?
