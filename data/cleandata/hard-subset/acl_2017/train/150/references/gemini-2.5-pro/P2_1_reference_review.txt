### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-to-character neural machine translation (NMT) model. The main goal is to effectively handle the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem and leverage morphological information without resorting to explicit subword segmentation methods like BPE. The authors introduce two primary architectural innovations:

1.  **A novel character-level word encoder:** This encoder represents a word by learning a weighted combination of its character prefixes. It uses a standard RNN to process the character sequence and a separate bidirectional RNN to compute an "energy" or weight for each prefix. This is designed to implicitly capture morphemes and their compositional structure.
2.  **An efficient hierarchical decoder:** The decoder operates on two levels. A word-level decoder generates a sequence of hidden states, where each state conceptually represents a target word. A second, character-level decoder then takes each of these states in turn and generates a character sequence, terminating when it produces a word delimiter. This hierarchical structure is made efficient for batched training via a clever "unfolding matrix" mechanism.

The authors evaluate their model (DCNMT) on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation, demonstrating competitive performance against BPE-based and other character-level models. A significant part of the paper is dedicated to qualitative analysis, arguing that their word encoder successfully learns morphological patterns.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model architecture.** The core ideas are intuitive and directly address the stated problems. The word encoder's design, using one RNN for representation and another for weighting, is a clever way to approximate a compositional, morphology-aware word representation without explicit segmentation. Similarly, the hierarchical decoder is a logical approach to manage the computational burden of long character sequences by imposing a soft word-level structure. It combines the planning aspect of word-level models with the flexibility of character-level generation.

2.  **Strong and insightful qualitative analysis.** A major strength of this paper is the effort to validate the central claim of "learning morphology." The PCA projections in Figure 3 provide compelling visual evidence that the proposed encoder produces more semantically structured representations than a baseline RNN encoder. The analysis of character weights ("energy") in Figure 4 successfully illustrates how the model learns to place importance on morpheme boundaries (e.g., after 'any' and 'every'). Furthermore, the examples of translating misspelled and nonce words (e.g., "unconclusive," "convenienter") provide concrete proof of the model's robustness and generalization capabilities, which is a key advantage of character-level modeling that is well-demonstrated here.

3.  **Competitive empirical results.** The model achieves strong BLEU scores that are comparable to or better than several state-of-the-art baselines on multiple language pairs. In particular, its performance on the morphologically rich Czech language pairs is a good validation of the approach. The reported training efficiency, while still requiring significant time, appears favorable compared to other purely character-level models like Luong and Manning (2016), demonstrating the practical viability of the proposed architecture. The fact that the model achieves strong results after just one epoch (Table 1) further supports the claim that learning morphology can speed up convergence.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity on "automatic subword learning" is weak.** The paper contrasts its method with BPE by claiming it "learns the subword unit automatically." This is potentially misleading. The model learns an *implicit* weighting scheme over character prefixes, not an *explicit* segmentation into subword units like BPE does. The analysis in Figure 5, which shows "peak energy" to detect subword boundaries, feels like a post-hoc interpretation rather than a function of the model itself. It is unclear how these peaks are defined or if this method would produce a consistent segmentation. The claim should be softened to reflect that the model learns a *distributed representation of morphology* rather than discrete subword units.

2.  **Limited ablation studies to isolate contributions.** The model has several novel components, but their individual impact is not fully dissected. The main comparison is between DCNMT (with the proposed word encoder) and CNMT (with a standard RNN encoder), which does show a significant improvement and supports the value of the encoder. However, further ablations would have strengthened the paper. For instance:
    *   What is the impact of using `tanh` versus a standard `softmax` for the attention weights in the word encoder? The authors state `tanh` "performs best in experiments," but no data is shown.
    *   How does the hierarchical decoder compare against a flat character-level decoder within the same framework, assuming one could be trained? Isolating the benefit of the hierarchical structure would be valuable.

3.  **Potential oversimplification of decoder efficiency.** The "unfolding matrix" is an effective trick for making the hierarchical decoder work in a batched training setting. However, it relies on knowing the target word boundaries in the training data beforehand. While this is standard practice, it means the model is still trained with explicit word-level supervision on the target side. At inference, the model generates delimiters dynamically, but the training regimen is not purely character-to-character in the strictest sense. This nuance is slightly under-discussed.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the mechanism for identifying "peak energy" in Figure 5? Is this a simple thresholding of the learned weights `w_t`, and how robust is this method for identifying morpheme boundaries across a wider vocabulary?

2.  The target-side word encoder is described as an "ordinary RNN," while the source-side encoder is the novel dual-RNN architecture. Was there a reason for this asymmetry? Did you experiment with using the proposed morphology-aware encoder for the target-side feedback as well?

3.  Regarding the HGRU update in Eq. (2), the new state `g_{t-1}` is determined by a hard switch based on whether the previous character was a delimiter (`a_{t-1}`). Did you consider a "softer" update mechanism, perhaps using a learned gating variable, to blend the word-level and character-level states?
