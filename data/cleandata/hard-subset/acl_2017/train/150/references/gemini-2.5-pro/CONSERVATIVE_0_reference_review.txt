### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model. The architecture is composed of four main layers: (1) a novel character-level word encoder that uses two RNNs to compute a weighted representation of a word, purportedly to learn its morphology; (2) a standard bidirectional RNN sentence encoder; (3) a word-level RNN decoder; and (4) a character-level RNN decoder that generates the final translation character by character, conditioned on the output of the word-level decoder. The primary contribution is this complex, multi-level architecture designed to operate purely at the character level, thereby eliminating the need for a fixed vocabulary and addressing the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem. A secondary contribution is the qualitative analysis suggesting that the proposed word encoder can learn representations that reflect morphological structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a significant problem in NMT:** The paper tackles the well-known OOV problem, which is a fundamental limitation of traditional word-level NMT models. Proposing a purely character-level solution is a valid and principled approach to creating an open-vocabulary system. The qualitative examples in Table 2, showing the model's ability to handle misspelled words and morphological neologisms ("convenienter"), effectively demonstrate a key advantage of character-level modeling over word-based systems.

2.  **Achieves competitive performance:** The proposed model achieves respectable BLEU scores on three translation tasks, including for a morphologically rich language (Czech). The results are shown to be competitive with or superior to several other character-level and subword-level models reported in the literature. This demonstrates that despite its complexity, the proposed architecture is capable of learning the translation task to a high standard.

3.  **Provides insightful qualitative analysis:** The authors provide visualizations (Figures 3, 4, 5) that attempt to probe the internal workings of their proposed word encoder. While not conclusive, these analyses offer some intuition that the model is learning meaningful sub-word representations and segmentation points that align with human notions of morphology. This kind of analysis is valuable for understanding what these complex models are learning.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient justification for architectural complexity:** The proposed model is exceptionally complex, involving a hierarchy of six distinct RNNs. This complexity is not rigorously justified against simpler, well-established methods that solve the same problem. The byte pair encoding (BPE) method of Sennrich et al. (2016) is a powerful and far simpler baseline for open-vocabulary NMT that has become a standard approach. The paper's performance gains over BPE are marginal (e.g., +0.86 BLEU on En-Fr) and may not warrant the substantial increase in architectural and implementation complexity. The authors need to provide a more compelling argument for why such an intricate design is necessary or superior to simpler data-driven subword segmentation techniques.

2.  **Lack of controlled baseline comparisons:** The experimental results in Table 1 are compiled from several different papers. This is not a substitute for a controlled comparison. Different papers may use subtle variations in data preprocessing, optimization, or model hyperparameters that can significantly affect final BLEU scores. For the claims of this paper to be convincing, the proposed model should be directly compared against strong baselines, particularly a BPE-based model, trained by the authors under identical conditions (data, hardware, training time). Without this, it is difficult to ascertain whether the reported performance differences are due to the novel architecture or confounding factors.

3.  **Claims of "learning morphology" are overstated and lack quantitative evidence:** The paper makes strong claims about its word encoder learning morphemes and compositional rules. However, the evidence presented is purely qualitative and anecdotal. The visualizations are interesting but open to interpretation and do not constitute rigorous proof. To substantiate these claims, a more systematic and quantitative evaluation is required. For instance, the learned word representations could be evaluated on extrinsic word similarity or analogy tasks, or the induced morphological segmentations could be compared against a gold-standard linguistic resource. As it stands, the claims of morphological learning are speculative.

4.  **Unclear efficiency trade-offs:** The authors claim their model is "efficient to train," but this is only supported by comparing training times against one other character-level model that reportedly took three months to train. The reported training times of ~2-3 weeks are still substantial. A direct comparison of training and inference speed (e.g., words per second) against a standard BPE-based transformer or RNNsearch model is missing. Such a comparison is critical for practitioners to evaluate the practical viability of this complex approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core novelty lies in the word encoder and hierarchical decoder. Could you provide an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of these two components? For example, what is the performance of a system with your word encoder but a standard character-level decoder, or a standard character encoder (e.g., simple BiRNN) with your hierarchical decoder?

2.  Your comparison to BPE relies on results from another paper. Could you clarify the training time and computational cost of a standard BPE-based NMT model within your experimental framework? This would provide a much clearer picture of the trade-off between the complexity of your model and the performance gains it achieves.

3.  The HGRU's state update mechanism in Eq. (2) appears to be a hard reset based on the word-level decoder's output. What is the intuition behind this design choice versus a softer, gated update? Does this hard boundary potentially limit the model's ability to capture long-range character-level dependencies that span across words (e.g., in agglutinative languages)?
