### Summary
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model built on a hierarchical architecture. The model processes input from characters to words to sentences, and decodes from sentences to words to characters. The architecture comprises two main novel components: (1) a source-side word encoder that uses two recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to compose a word representation from its characters, designed to capture morphological structure, and (2) a hierarchical decoder that combines a word-level RNN with a character-level RNN. The character-level decoder uses a modified Gated Recurrent Unit (HGRU) that can be reset by the word-level decoder's state, enabling efficient, end-to-end training. The authors evaluate their model on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, demonstrating competitive performance with other character-level and subword-level models while claiming greater training efficiency and an explicit ability to learn morphology.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT.** The primary contribution is the end-to-end `character -> word -> sentence -> word -> character` pipeline. This architecture features a unique source word encoder that uses a BiRNN to compute weights for the hidden states of another RNN that reads the characters, aiming to explicitly model morphemes. The system is completed by a hierarchical decoder that efficiently generates characters conditioned on word-level representations.
2.  **An efficient method for training a deep character-level NMT model.** The paper introduces a modified GRU (HGRU) and an "unfolding" mechanism in the decoder that allows for batch training of a hierarchical character-level model without requiring multiple forward passes, addressing a key efficiency challenge in similar models. This results in faster training times compared to contemporary character-level systems.
3.  **Empirical analysis of the model's ability to learn morphology.** The authors provide quantitative and qualitative evidence suggesting their proposed word encoder learns meaningful subword representations. This is demonstrated through visualizations of word embeddings, analysis of internal model weights corresponding to morphemes, and the model's ability to correctly translate misspelled and nonce words.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel and Technically Sound Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical architecture is well-conceived and addresses key challenges in character-level NMT. The source word encoder, with its dual-RNN structure for weighted composition of character states, is a specific and novel method for learning word representations. The hierarchical decoder's HGRU mechanism is a sound engineering solution to the practical problem of efficiently coupling word-level and character-level generation within a single computational graph, which is a notable technical contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Efficiency Claims:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art subword and character-level models on multiple language pairs, including the morphologically rich Czech language (Table 1). A key strength is the demonstrated training efficiency; the paper reports significantly faster training times (e.g., 15-22 days for En-Cs/Cs-En) compared to other purely character-level models which reportedly took months to train. The model also achieves strong performance after just one epoch, supporting the claim that learning morphology speeds up convergence.
3.  **Thorough Analysis and Insightful Visualizations:** The paper goes beyond reporting aggregate metrics and provides compelling analysis to support its central claims. The PCA plots in Figure 3 offer clear visual evidence for the superiority of the proposed word encoder over a standard RNN encoder in organizing words by semantic and morphological relationships. The "energy" plots in Figure 4 provide an intuitive visualization of how the model identifies morpheme-like boundaries (e.g., in "any-way" and "every-way"), offering valuable insight into the model's internal workings.
4.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** A major motivation for character-level models is their ability to handle an open vocabulary. The paper effectively demonstrates this strength with qualitative examples in Table 2. The model's capacity to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and morphologically constructed nonce words ("convenienter") provides strong, practical evidence of its advantages over word- or subword-based systems that would map such terms to `<unk>`.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Justification for Word Encoder Design Choices:** The formulation of the source word encoder lacks a clear theoretical grounding. The weights `w_t` are calculated as `exp(aff(h_t))` without a subsequent normalization step (like a softmax), and the final representation is computed as `tanh(Σ w_t * r_t)`. The paper's only justification is that this "performs best in experiments," which is insufficient. Without normalization, the values of `w_t` can grow exponentially, and their interpretation as contribution "weights" or "energy" is not straightforward. This design choice appears arbitrary and requires a more detailed explanation or ablation study to be convincing.
2.  **Limited Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** The paper claims its RNN-based word encoder generates more "meaningful" representations than CNN-based encoders, but this claim is not experimentally validated. At the time of this work, character-level CNNs were a prominent and powerful alternative for composing word representations. The experimental comparison in Figure 3 is only against a standard RNN, not a state-of-the-art CNN encoder. Including a CNN-based word encoder as a baseline within their NMT framework would have provided a much stronger test of the proposed encoder's effectiveness.
3.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Technical and Experimental Details:** Certain aspects of the model and experiments are underspecified, hindering reproducibility. The description of the HGRU's unfolding matrix `R` is abstract and would benefit from a more concrete example of how it operates on a batch of sentences with variable-length words. Furthermore, the claims of training efficiency are based on "days," which is a relative measure dependent on hardware and implementation. Providing absolute metrics like tokens/sec or sequences/sec would allow for a more objective comparison. The exact configuration of the six RNNs (e.g., which components are two-layered) could also be specified more clearly.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the design of the word encoder? Specifically, why was the `exp()` function for weights chosen without a normalization step, and what is the intuition behind applying `tanh` to the final weighted sum? How does this formulation compare empirically to a more standard attention mechanism using a softmax for normalization?
2.  The paper highlights the model's training efficiency. Could you provide more details on the implementation of the HGRU and the "unfolding matrix" for batch processing? How does the required character-level padding affect overall computational throughput compared to word-level or BPE-based models? It would be helpful if you could report training speed in an absolute metric, such as target characters or words processed per second.
3.  The analysis in Figure 3 compellingly shows the proposed word encoder's advantage over a vanilla RNN encoder. To better situate this contribution, could you comment on how you expect your encoder to compare against a strong character-level CNN encoder (e.g., from Kim et al., 2016)? Have you performed any experiments to support the claim that your RNN-based approach yields more "meaningful" representations than a CNN?
