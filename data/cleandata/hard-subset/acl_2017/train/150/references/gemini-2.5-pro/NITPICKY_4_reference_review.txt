**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper proposes a deep hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model, named DCNMT. The goal is to address the out-of-vocabulary problem of word-level models while mitigating the training difficulty of conventional character-level models. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel word encoder architecture designed to learn morphological structure. It uses two parallel RNNs: one to generate representations of character prefixes and another (a BiRNN) to compute a weight for each prefix representation. The final word embedding is a weighted sum of these prefix vectors.
2.  A hierarchical decoder that operates at two levels: a word-level RNN generates a sequence of state vectors, and a character-level RNN (a custom "HGRU" unit) consumes these states to generate character sequences for each word. The authors introduce an efficient matrix-based unfolding technique to enable effective batch training for this hierarchical process.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Word Encoder:** The proposed word encoder is a creative and well-motivated method for capturing sub-word information. The idea of learning explicit weights for character prefixes to compose a word representation is elegant. The qualitative analyses in Section 5.2 (Figures 3 and 4) provide compelling visual evidence that this architecture successfully learns representations that reflect morphological similarities and identifies implicit morpheme boundaries, which is a significant step beyond simply using the final state of an RNN.
2.  **Effective Handling of Rare and Novel Words:** The paper provides strong evidence for a key advantage of character-level modeling. The examples in Table 2, showing correct translations for misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words ("convenienter"), are a powerful demonstration of the model's ability to generalize beyond its training vocabulary by leveraging morphological rules. This is a clear practical benefit over standard word- or subword-based systems.
3.  **Training Efficiency:** Despite its apparent complexity (six RNNs), the model is shown to be remarkably efficient to train. Table 1 indicates that DCNMT achieves strong performance after just one epoch and has a faster overall training time compared to other character-level models. The matrix-unfolding trick described in Section 3.2 is a clever engineering solution that likely contributes significantly to this efficiency.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Critical Lack of Clarity in the Core Method (HGRU):** The description of the Hierarchical GRU (HGRU), a central component of the proposed decoder, is critically flawed. Equation (2), `gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit`, is mathematically ill-defined as `gt-1` is defined in terms of itself. This makes it impossible to understand the core mechanism of how the word-level state (`s_it`) is integrated into the character-level generation process at word boundaries. The surrounding text does not resolve this ambiguity. This severe lack of clarity in a key methodological contribution is a major flaw.
2.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Formatting Errors:** The paper suffers from a significant number of typos, grammatical errors, and formatting inconsistencies that detract from its scientific quality. The References section is particularly egregious, containing numerous BibTeX compilation errors (e.g., `Bahdanau et al\.,?`, `\Q2015\E`). Such errors are unacceptable in a final submission and suggest a lack of care and proofreading. Other examples include "effect" instead of "affect" (Sec. 1), "start-of-the-art" (Table 1 caption), and cropped axis labels in Figure 3. While individually minor, their sheer number undermines the paper's credibility.
3.  **Missing Experimental Details and Vague Baselines:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details required for reproducibility and fair comparison.
    *   The "tokenization" process is mentioned but not defined, which is a critical omission for a character-level model. It is unclear how punctuation and spacing are handled.
    *   The "ordinary RNN word encoder" used as a baseline for the PCA analysis in Figure 3 is not described. Its architecture is essential for interpreting the comparison.
    *   The "LISA" system used for comparison in Table 2 is neither cited nor explained, leaving the reader to guess its origin and relevance.
    *   The claim that the RNN encoder is superior to CNN encoders for generating meaningful representations (Sec. 1, 5.2) is asserted without any direct comparison or supporting evidence.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Please provide a corrected and unambiguous mathematical formulation for the HGRU state update mechanism. Specifically, how is the state `g` of the character-level decoder reset or influenced by the state `s` from the word-level decoder at word boundaries? A corrected version of Equation (2) is essential.
2.  Could you please explicitly define the architecture of the "ordinary RNN word encoder" used as a baseline in Figure 3?
3.  What is the "LISA" system referenced in Table 2? Please provide a proper citation or description.
4.  What was the "usual tokenization" applied prior to character-level modeling? For instance, was punctuation separated from words (e.g., "words." -> "words .")? This detail is crucial for understanding the input given to your model.
