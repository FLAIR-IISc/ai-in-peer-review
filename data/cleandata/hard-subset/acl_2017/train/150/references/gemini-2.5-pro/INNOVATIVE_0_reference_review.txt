### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) that attempts to bridge the gap between the efficiency of word-level models and the open-vocabulary flexibility of character-level models. The core idea is to impose a hierarchical structure on both the encoder and decoder to explicitly model morphology.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel word encoder for learning morphology:** The primary contribution is a new method for composing word representations from characters. It uses two coupled RNNs where one reads the character sequence and another, a BiRNN, generates attention-like weights for each character's hidden state. This is a bold and creative attempt to have the network automatically discover and weigh sub-word units, akin to morphemes, without explicit supervision.
2.  **An efficient hierarchical character-level decoder:** The paper proposes a two-level decoder. A word-level decoder produces a sequence of hidden states, and a character-level decoder, using a novel "HGRU" cell, uses these states to initialize generation for each target word. This is a clever architecture designed to make end-to-end character-level training and inference more computationally tractable.
3.  **Qualitative evidence of morphological learning and its benefits:** The authors provide compelling visualizations and examples suggesting their model learns meaningful sub-word structures. This learned structure appears to grant the model capabilities beyond standard NMT, such as robustly translating misspelled or nonce words (e.g., "convenienter").

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and an Ambitious Approach:** This work's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty. Rather than making an incremental improvement to existing attention mechanisms or network depths, the authors propose a fundamentally new architecture inspired by linguistic principles. The idea of learning an intra-word compositional structure via a learned weighting mechanism is elegant and ambitious. This kind of bold, principled exploration is exactly what the field needs to move beyond local optima in architectural design. The hierarchical structure (character→subword→word→sentence→word→character) is a significant and compelling new direction for sequence-to-sequence modeling.

2.  **Strong Evidence for a Powerful Inductive Bias:** The qualitative results are highly persuasive. The PCA plots in Figure 3 clearly show the proposed encoder produces more structured representations than a standard RNN encoder. The weight visualizations in Figure 4, showing high "energy" at plausible morpheme boundaries (e.g., after "any" in "anyone"), provide strong intuitive support for the claim of learning morphology. Most impressively, the ability to correctly translate nonce words by composing known morphemes (Table 2) is a powerful demonstration of generalization that typical word- or BPE-level models could never achieve. This suggests the model has acquired a genuinely useful inductive bias.

3.  **Competitiveness of a New Paradigm:** It is remarkable that a completely new and complex architecture (with six RNNs) can achieve performance competitive with highly mature and optimized baselines like BPE-based NMT. The fact that the model isn't just a theoretical curiosity but a viable, high-performing system makes its conceptual contributions much more significant. Furthermore, the claim that it can outperform baselines after just one epoch suggests that the morphological inductive bias not only improves generalization but also accelerates learning, which is a very valuable property.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper's direction, there are areas where the work could be strengthened. I see these not as reasons for rejection, but as important points for the authors to address and for the community to build upon.

1.  **Justification of the Word Encoder's Formulation:** The specific mechanism for weighting character representations in the word encoder (`tanh(sum(exp(aff(h_t)) * r_t))`) is unconventional. Using an exponent without a subsequent normalization (like a softmax) means the `w_t` are not weights in the traditional sense, and their scale is unbounded. Why was this formulation chosen over a more standard, stabilized attention mechanism? A brief justification or an ablation study comparing this to a softmax-based intra-word attention would make the contribution clearer and more grounded.

2.  **Analysis of Morphological Learning is Preliminary:** The claims of learning morphology are supported by well-chosen, compelling examples. However, the analysis is anecdotal. This is acceptable for a paper introducing such a novel idea, but it remains a limitation. A more systematic analysis, even on a small scale, would be beneficial. For instance, one could correlate the learned segmentation points with a gold-standard morphological parser's output to quantify the alignment. This is not strictly necessary for acceptance, but it would substantially bolster the paper's claims.

3.  **Lack of Direct Efficiency Comparison for the Decoder:** The paper claims the hierarchical decoder is efficient, but this is not directly compared against alternatives. For instance, how does the training and inference speed of the proposed HGRU-based decoder compare to a standard flat character-level decoder, given the same encoder? While the architectural motivation is clear, an empirical measurement of the speed-up would make the engineering contribution more concrete.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold, creative, and highly novel approach to character-level neural machine translation. The core idea of learning morphology through a hierarchical, composition-based architecture is a significant conceptual contribution. The authors provide compelling, albeit preliminary, evidence that their model learns meaningful linguistic structure and that this structure leads to improved generalization and faster learning. While the methodology could be tightened in a few places and the analysis expanded, these are minor points in the context of the work's overall ambition and novelty. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that pushes the boundaries of the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
