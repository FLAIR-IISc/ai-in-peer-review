### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper introduces a deep, hierarchical architecture for purely character-level neural machine translation (NMT). The goal is to overcome the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem inherent in word-based models and to better model morphology, while maintaining greater training efficiency than previous character-level approaches. The proposed model, DCNMT, features two key components: 1) a novel word encoder that learns a representation for each word by calculating a weighted sum of its character-prefix representations, with the weights intended to capture morpheme boundaries automatically, and 2) a hierarchical two-level decoder where a word-level decoder generates a sequence of context vectors, and a character-level decoder uses each of these vectors to generate the corresponding target word character by character. The authors demonstrate that this model achieves competitive results on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, end-to-end hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT:** The main contribution is the specific system architecture that models translation through a full character → word → sentence → word → character hierarchy. This design is a comprehensive and well-motivated approach to bridging the gap between character-level flexibility and word-level abstraction.
2.  **An efficient training mechanism for hierarchical decoding:** The paper proposes a modified GRU (HGRU) and an "unfolding matrix" to facilitate efficient batch training of the two-level decoder. This is a significant practical contribution, as it addresses a key bottleneck that has made similar hierarchical models difficult and slow to train.
3.  **Strong qualitative and quantitative analysis of morphological learning:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed word encoder learns meaningful sub-word representations. This is supported by competitive BLEU scores on a morphologically rich language pair (En-Cs), visualizations showing interpretable word representations, and qualitative examples of the model's ability to handle misspelled and nonce words.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The model's design is conceptually elegant. The word encoder, which learns to compose word meaning by weighting character prefixes, is an intuitive method for capturing morphology without explicit segmentation. The symmetric two-level decoder is a logical counterpart that allows the model to operate entirely from character to character in an end-to-end fashion. This represents a thoughtful architectural contribution beyond simply making existing models deeper.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with, or superior to, other character-level and subword-level baselines on three different language pairs. Crucially, the authors show that DCNMT trains significantly faster than other prominent character-level models (e.g., training for En-Fr takes ~19 days vs. ~27 for C2W and ~90 for the model in Luong and Manning, 2016). The rapid convergence, with strong results reported after just one epoch, is a particularly impressive and important practical advantage.
3.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis:** The paper excels in its analysis, going beyond a simple comparison of BLEU scores. The PCA projection in Figure 3 effectively illustrates that the proposed encoder produces more semantically structured representations than a standard RNN encoder. The "energy" visualizations in Figure 4 provide strong intuitive evidence that the learned weights align with morpheme boundaries. Finally, the examples in Table 2, which show the model correctly translating misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words ("convenienter"), powerfully demonstrate the model's robustness and generalization capabilities—a key promise of character-level modeling that is convincingly realized here.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Ablation and Justification of Components:** The contribution of the novel word encoder is not fully isolated and justified.
    *   The paper introduces CNMT (using a standard RNN encoder) as a baseline against DCNMT (using the proposed weighted encoder), which is a good ablation. However, this comparison is only provided for the En-Fr task. The most compelling case for the proposed encoder would be on the morphologically rich En-Cs and Cs-En tasks, but results for these are missing.
    *   The specific formulation of the word representation, `tanh(sum(w_t * r_t))`, is not well justified. The paper simply states that `tanh` "performs best in experiments," but no alternatives or results are presented. For instance, normalizing the weights `w_t` with a softmax seems like an obvious alternative that would yield a convex combination of prefix vectors. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the importance of this specific design choice.
2.  **Limited Scope of Comparison Models:** While the paper compares against relevant BPE and character-level models from its time, the comparison could be stronger. The primary subword baseline is BPE, but a discussion of or comparison to other data-driven segmentation methods could have contextualized the claim of "learning the subword unit automatically." More importantly, the entire model is based on RNNs, which are known to be slow at inference compared to non-recurrent architectures like the Transformer, which was emerging around the same time. A discussion of inference speed and potential limitations related to the recurrent nature of the model would have been valuable.
3.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Technical Details:** The description of the hierarchical decoder could be improved for clarity and reproducibility.
    *   The update mechanism for the HGRU state in Equation (2), `gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit`, is confusing as `g_t-1` appears on both sides. This appears to be a typo and likely represents a conditional reset of the state, but the exact formulation is ambiguous.
    *   The "unfolding matrix" `R` is presented as a key trick for training efficiency, but its construction and application within a mini-batch (where target sentences have varying numbers of words and characters) are not described in sufficient detail. Pseudo-code or a more detailed algorithmic description would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and make the method easier to replicate.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the comparison between DCNMT and the CNMT baseline for the English-Czech and Czech-English translation tasks? This data seems critical to substantiating the claim that your proposed word encoder is particularly effective for morphologically rich languages.
2.  Could you elaborate on the design of the word encoder's aggregation function? Specifically, have you experimented with alternatives to `tanh(sum(w_t * r_t))`, such as using a softmax to normalize the weights `w_t`, and could you share those results?
3.  Please clarify the HGRU state update in Equation (2). Is the use of `g_t-1` on both sides of the equation a typo? If so, what is the correct formulation for conditionally setting the character-level decoder's state from the word-level decoder's output?
