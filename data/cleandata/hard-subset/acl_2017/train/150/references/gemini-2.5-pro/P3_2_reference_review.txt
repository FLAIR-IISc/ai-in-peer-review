### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, purely character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphological information. The core of the model is a hierarchical architecture that operates at multiple granularities: character, subword, word, and sentence. The main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Word Encoder:** The primary contribution is a new method for composing word representations from characters. It uses a dual-RNN setup where one RNN generates character-level hidden states, and a second, bidirectional RNN computes attention-like weights for these states. The final word vector is a weighted sum of the character states. The authors claim this architecture allows the model to learn representations of morphemes and their composition rules automatically.
2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Decoder:** To generate translations character-by-character, the paper introduces a two-level decoder. A word-level RNN decoder generates a sequence of states, where each state represents a target word. A second-level character RNN (dubbed HGRU) is initialized with the word-level state and generates the corresponding character sequence until a delimiter is produced. A clever "unfolding matrix" is proposed to make this hierarchical process efficient for batch training.
3.  **In-depth Qualitative Analysis:** Beyond reporting BLEU scores, the authors provide a significant amount of analysis to support their claim of learning morphology. This includes visualizing word embeddings (PCA), analyzing the internal weights of the word encoder to identify morpheme-like units, and demonstrating the model's robustness to misspellings and unseen morphological variants (nonce words).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed word encoder is conceptually elegant. The idea of using an attention-like mechanism over characters within a word to compose its meaning is highly intuitive and directly addresses the goal of modeling morphology. It provides a more flexible alternative to fixed segmentation methods like BPE or the compositional but less-interpretable approach of CNNs over characters. The overall hierarchical structure (character -> word -> sentence -> word -> character) is a complete and end-to-end solution to the character-level NMT problem.

2.  **Strong Qualitative Evidence for Morphological Learning:** The paper's greatest strength is its analysis in Section 5. The visualizations provided are compelling. Figure 3 suggests the proposed encoder produces a more semantically structured embedding space than a standard RNN encoder. Figure 4 and 5 provide plausible evidence that the encoder's internal weights (energies) correspond to morpheme boundaries, effectively learning to segment words in a soft, data-driven manner. The examples in Table 2, where the model correctly translates misspelled words and nonce words like "convenienter," are powerful demonstrations of the practical benefits of this approach over standard word/subword models. This analysis goes beyond typical NMT papers and adds significant weight to the authors' claims.

3.  **Competitive Performance and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with, and in some cases better than, strong contemporary baselines, including BPE-based and other character-level models. Crucially, the authors highlight a significant advantage in training efficiency (Table 1) compared to other purely character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016), which were notoriously slow to train. The proposed hierarchical decoder and its efficient batching implementation seem to successfully mitigate the computational burden of processing very long character sequences.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Justification for Key Design Choices:** The word encoder computes weights `wt = exp(aff(ht))` but then does not normalize them with a softmax, instead applying a `tanh` activation to the final weighted sum. The only justification given is that it "performs best in experiments." This is insufficient. A softmax would allow the weights to be interpreted as a probability distribution (attention), which would align better with the authors' analysis of "energy." The current formulation makes the interpretation of `wt` less clear and the design feel somewhat ad-hoc. More ablation or analysis is needed here.

2.  **Claims of Novelty are Slightly Overstated:** While the specific implementation is novel, the high-level concepts are not entirely new. Hierarchical decoders have been explored before (e.g., Ling et al., 2015b; Serban et al., 2015). The concept of composing word embeddings from smaller units (characters/morphemes) was also an active area of research (e.g., Kim et al., 2016). The paper could do a better job of positioning its contributions within this existing landscape. Similarly, the claim that it "learn[s] the subword unit automatically" is also true of BPE, which learns a vocabulary of subwords from data statistics. The key difference is that this model performs composition dynamically via a neural network rather than relying on a static, pre-computed vocabulary, a distinction that should be made more clearly.

3.  **The "Learning Morphology" Claim Needs More Rigorous Validation:** The qualitative analysis is excellent but remains anecdotal. The term "learning morphology" is a very strong claim. The model is learning to weight character n-grams in a useful way, which may correlate strongly with morphology but is not necessarily the same as learning a system of grammatical morphemes. The analysis would be strengthened by a more quantitative evaluation, for instance by comparing the model's induced segmentations (from the energy peaks in Figure 5) against a gold-standard morphological analyzer (e.g., Morfessor) and reporting precision/recall.

4.  **Comparison to Baselines:** The model does not consistently or overwhelmingly outperform the simpler and widely adopted BPE-based subword models on BLEU scores. Given the significant increase in architectural complexity (six RNNs), the performance gains need to be substantial to justify this complexity for practitioners focused purely on translation quality. The main advantage seems to be robustness to OOVs/noise, which is a valid but distinct selling point from raw BLEU score.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more insight into the decision not to use a softmax to normalize the weights `w_t` in the word encoder? Does the use of `exp()` without normalization cause any training instability? How should we interpret these "energy" values if they are not part of a distribution that sums to one?

2.  The HGRU state update (Eq. 2) relies on an auxiliary binary sequence `a_t` to signal word boundaries. This seems straightforward during training where the target is known. How is this sequence `a_t` determined during inference/generation? Is it simply based on whether the previously generated character was a delimiter? A clarification of the generation process in this regard would be helpful.

3.  The analysis in Figure 3 suggests your word encoder produces "more reasonable" representations than a standard RNN encoder. Have you considered evaluating these learned word representations intrinsically on word similarity or analogy tasks (e.g., WordSim-353, Google analogy dataset) to provide a quantitative measure of their quality compared to other composition methods (CNN, standard RNN) and pre-trained embeddings?
