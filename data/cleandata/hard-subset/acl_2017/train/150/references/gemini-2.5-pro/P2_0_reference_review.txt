### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a deep, hierarchical character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model designed to overcome the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem while explicitly learning word morphology. The core of the proposed architecture, DCNMT, consists of a novel word encoder and a hierarchical decoder.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Morphologically-Aware Word Encoder:** The primary contribution is a new method for composing word representations from characters. It uses two parallel RNNs: one generates representations for character prefixes, while a second bidirectional RNN computes attention-like weights for these representations. The final word vector is a weighted sum of the prefix vectors. This architecture is designed to explicitly learn morphemes and their compositional rules.

2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Character-Level Decoder:** The paper proposes a two-level decoder. A word-level RNN generates a sequence of states, where each state represents a target word. These states are then used to initialize a second-level character-based RNN (a custom "HGRU"), which generates the character sequence for that word. This hierarchical structure avoids the long dependencies of pure character-to-character models while enabling efficient batch training through a clever unfolding matrix.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance compared to contemporary subword and character-level models on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks. Crucially, they provide compelling qualitative analysis, including visualizations and examples, to support their central claim that the model successfully learns morphological structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The paper's core idea is strong. It directly addresses the known trade-off between word-level models (computationally efficient but suffer from OOVs) and character-level models (handle OOVs but struggle with long dependencies and training difficulty). The proposed hierarchical structure is an intelligent compromise. The source-side word encoder is particularly novel; using one RNN to build context and another to weigh its contributions is a creative mechanism for learning sub-word composition that is more interpretable than a standard CNN or single RNN encoder.

2.  **Compelling Qualitative Evidence for Morphological Learning:** A major strength of this paper is the effort dedicated to validating its central claim. The analysis in Section 5.2 is excellent. The PCA plots (Figure 3) clearly show that the proposed encoder produces more semantically coherent embeddings than a standard RNN encoder. The energy visualizations (Figure 4) provide intuitive evidence that the model identifies morpheme boundaries by assigning high weights to them. Finally, the ability to correctly translate nonce words like "convenienter" (Table 2) is a powerful demonstration of the model's generalization capabilities, directly stemming from its morphological understanding. This level of analysis is often missing in NMT papers and significantly boosts the credibility of the authors' claims.

3.  **Strong Quantitative Performance and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves impressive BLEU scores, putting it on par with or ahead of strong baselines like BPE-based models and other character-level approaches (Table 1). What is particularly noteworthy is the reported training efficiency. The model reaches competitive performance in a fraction of the time reported for other pure character-level models (e.g., 15-22 days vs. 30-90 days). The one-epoch results, which already surpass some fully trained baselines, further support the claim that learning morphology accelerates convergence. This is a significant practical advantage.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Ablation Studies for Key Design Choices:** While the overall architecture is well-motivated, several specific design choices are presented without empirical justification or ablation.
    *   In the word encoder, the weights `wt` are computed using `exp(aff(ht))` but are not normalized (i.e., no softmax). The authors also use `tanh` to combine the weighted representations. The paper states `tanh` "performs best in experiments" but does not present these results. It is unclear why this unnormalized weighting scheme is preferable to a standard attention mechanism.
    *   The hierarchical decoder uses a custom "HGRU" unit. It's not clear how this compares to a simpler approach, such as resetting the hidden state of a standard GRU with the word-level vector `s_it`. An ablation study comparing HGRU to a standard GRU with state resetting would strengthen the motivation for this new component.

2.  **Ambiguity in Efficiency Claims and Comparisons:** The paper emphasizes training efficiency, but the comparison in Table 1 could be clearer. Training times are highly dependent on hardware, implementation details, and batch size. Reporting "days" without these details makes direct comparison difficult. For instance, the Luong and Manning (2016) model that took "3 months" may have been trained on different hardware or with a different optimization scheme. More importantly, the paper focuses exclusively on training speed and does not discuss inference speed. Character-by-character decoding is auto-regressively slow, and the proposed model still generates one character at a time. A comparison of decoding speed (e.g., sentences per second) against word or BPE-based models would provide a more complete picture of the model's practical efficiency.

3.  **Clarity of the Word Encoder's Operation:** The description of the word encoder could be more precise. The text states `rt` "contains information about the preceding characters," which suggests a standard left-to-right RNN. However, Figure 1 seems to imply that the weights `wt` are computed by a BiRNN that sees the whole word. If the weighting RNN is bidirectional, it has access to future characters, which complicates the interpretation of `wt` as simply the "energy of preceding characters." A clearer explanation of the information flow and dependencies within the word encoder would be beneficial.
