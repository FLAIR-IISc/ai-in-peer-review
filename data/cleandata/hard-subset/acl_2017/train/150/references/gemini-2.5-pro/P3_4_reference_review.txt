### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT) designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The model eschews fixed vocabularies and subword tokenization methods like BPE in favor of dynamically composing word representations from characters.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Morphologically-Aware Word Encoder:** The authors introduce a word encoder composed of two RNNs. One RNN computes contextual character representations, while a second bidirectional RNN computes attention-like weights over these representations. This allows the model to form a weighted composition of character sequences, which the authors argue corresponds to learning morphemes and their combination rules.
2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Decoder:** To generate translations at the character level, the paper proposes a two-level decoder. A word-level decoder generates a sequence of state vectors, and a character-level decoder (using a custom "HGRU" cell) takes each state vector in turn to generate the characters for one word until a delimiter is produced. The paper introduces an efficient batching mechanism for this process.
3.  **Strong Empirical and Qualitative Analysis:** The paper demonstrates that its model achieves competitive BLEU scores with state-of-the-art character- and subword-level models on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks. Crucially, it provides compelling qualitative evidence (via visualizations and analysis of learned representations) that the word encoder is indeed capturing morphological structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The core idea of composing word representations from characters in a way that models morphology is highly intuitive. The proposed word encoder, with its dual-RNN structure acting as a form of intra-word attention, is a principled approach to this problem. It is more expressive than using a simple CNN or RNN encoder that just takes the final state. The hierarchical design (character → subword → word → sentence → target word → target character) provides a coherent framework for end-to-end character-level translation.

2.  **Compelling Qualitative Evidence for Morphological Learning:** A major strength of the paper is its analysis in Section 5.2. The PCA projections (Figure 3) effectively show that the proposed encoder produces a more semantically structured representation space (e.g., separating "notable" from "notability") than a standard RNN encoder. Furthermore, the visualization of the internal weights (Figure 4) provides strong evidence that the model learns to place high importance on characters at morpheme boundaries, supporting the central claim of learning morphology. This analysis goes beyond simply reporting BLEU scores and offers valuable insight into the model's inner workings.

3.  **Strong Performance on Morphologically Rich Languages:** The model demonstrates particularly strong results on the En-Cs and Cs-En tasks, outperforming the BPE-based baseline and achieving results competitive with other state-of-the-art character-level models. This supports the hypothesis that explicitly modeling morphology is especially beneficial when translating to or from morphologically rich languages, where OOV rates due to inflection and derivation are high.

4.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Nonce Words:** The examples in Table 2 are excellent demonstrations of the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The ability to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and morphologically-formed nonce words ("convenienter") is a significant advantage over models reliant on fixed vocabularies (word-level or BPE-based) and highlights the model's generalization capabilities.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important details in the model's architecture are presented without sufficient justification.
    *   In the word encoder, the weights `wt` are not normalized (e.g., with a softmax), and the final representation is passed through a `tanh`. The paper states this "performs best in experiments," which is an unsatisfying explanation. A standard attention mechanism would use a softmax. An analysis of why the unnormalized, tanh-squashed version works better would strengthen the paper. Does this formulation allow multiple morphemes to be given high "energy" simultaneously?
    *   The "HGRU" in the decoder uses a hard-gating mechanism (Eq. 2) to reset its state at word boundaries. This seems somewhat ad-hoc. It would be valuable to discuss why this hard reset is necessary or if softer update mechanisms were explored.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Computational Complexity and Inference Speed:** The model is complex, involving six RNNs. The word encoder, in particular, replaces a simple, fast embedding lookup with running two RNNs over the characters of every word in the source sentence. While the authors claim the model is "efficient to train" (Table 1), this is measured in days, which is hard to compare directly without knowing the exact hardware and software setup. More importantly, the paper completely omits any discussion of inference speed (e.g., words/second). This is a critical metric, as the proposed encoder is likely to be significantly slower at decoding time than a BPE-based model, potentially limiting its practical applicability.

3.  **The "Automatic" Subword Learning Argument Needs Nuance:** The paper contrasts its method with BPE by stating "we learn the subword unit automatically." However, BPE is also an automatic, data-driven algorithm for finding subword units. The key difference is that DCNMT's segmentation is dynamic and contextual (via RNNs), whereas BPE's is static (a fixed vocabulary of subwords). The paper would be stronger if it framed its contribution this way and provided a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between these two philosophies of subword modeling, rather than implying BPE is not "automatic."

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the word encoder? Specifically, what is the intuition behind not normalizing the `wt` weights with a softmax, and what effect did you observe when you tried it? Does the `tanh` activation play a role in preventing exploding values?

2.  Regarding inference efficiency, could you provide a comparison of decoding speed (e.g., sentences or words per second) between your DCNMT model and the BPE-based baseline? This would help clarify the practical trade-offs of your model's increased representational power.

3.  The HGRU resets its state from the word-level decoder's output `s_it` at the beginning of each word. Did you experiment with conditioning the character-level decoder on `s_it` at every character step (e.g., by concatenating it to the character embedding), instead of only using it for initialization? If so, how did that affect performance?
