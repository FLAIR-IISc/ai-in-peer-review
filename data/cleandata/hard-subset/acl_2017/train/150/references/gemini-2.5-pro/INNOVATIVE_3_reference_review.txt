**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical, character-to-character neural machine translation model designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The core of the submission lies in two novel architectural components:

1.  **A Morphologically-Inspired Word Encoder:** This is the primary contribution. Instead of using a standard embedding lookup, CNN, or simple RNN, the authors construct word representations using a clever dual-RNN setup. One RNN processes the characters of a word, while a second, bidirectional RNN learns an "attention" or "energy" weight for each character's hidden state. The final word vector is a weighted sum of these character-level states. This is a bold and creative attempt to have the model learn compositional sub-word structures that approximate morphemes.

2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Decoder:** The second contribution is a multi-level decoder that operates at a word level conceptually but generates text at a character level. A first-level RNN produces a sequence of word-level state vectors. Each of these vectors is then used to initialize a second-level character-decoder RNN (termed HGRU), which generates characters until it produces a delimiter. This is an elegant way to maintain the structural benefits of word-level sequencing while achieving the open-vocabulary advantages of a character-level output.

The authors demonstrate that this model can be trained end-to-end, achieves competitive results with other character- and subword-level systems, and provides interesting qualitative benefits, such as the ability to translate misspelled or nonce words.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Architectural Novelty:** The proposed word encoder is a genuinely novel and exciting idea. It moves beyond treating word composition as a black box and instead attempts to build in an inductive bias for learning meaningful sub-word units. The idea of using a second RNN to explicitly weight the contributions of character prefixes is highly creative and linguistically motivated. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward research that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Insightful Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the novel word encoder is learning what it was designed to learn. The visualizations in Figure 3 and Figure 4 are excellent, showing that the model produces more structured word representations than a standard RNN and that the learned weights often peak at plausible morpheme boundaries (e.g., after "any" in "anyone"). This analysis, which attempts to peer inside the model and understand its mechanisms, is a major strength and greatly increases the impact of the work.

3.  **Demonstrated Practical Advantages of the Approach:** The qualitative examples in Table 2 are powerful. The model's ability to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and, even more impressively, morphologically-constructed nonce words ("convenienter") is a clear and direct demonstration of the value of its character-level compositional approach. This goes beyond simply closing the vocabulary and shows a genuine capability for generalization that is lacking in standard word- or subword-based models.

4.  **Elegant End-to-End System:** The entire character -> subword -> word -> sentence -> target word -> target character hierarchy is integrated into a single, end-to-end trainable system. The proposed HGRU and the unfolding matrix for efficient batching are clever engineering solutions that make this complex, deep architecture tractable.

**Weaknesses**

While this is a strong paper with a core novel idea, there are a few points that could be improved or clarified. I consider these to be minor issues that do not detract from the overall contribution.

1.  **Clarity of the Word Encoder Formulation:** The description of the word encoder's weighting mechanism is slightly ambiguous. The text states that `tanh` is used "instead of normalizing" the weights, but the corresponding equation applies `tanh` to the final weighted sum of representations, not to the weights themselves. The weights, `w_t = exp(aff(h_t))`, appear to be unnormalized, which could potentially lead to very large values and training instability. A more precise description of the final, working formulation would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Strength of Baselines:** The main comparison is against results reported in other papers, which may use different data processing, training schedules, or hyperparameter tuning. While the results are clearly competitive, the claims of outperforming subword-level models would be more robust if benchmarked against a strong, in-house BPE baseline (e.g., Sennrich et al., 2016) trained under identical conditions. However, given the primary focus on the novel architecture, this is not a critical flaw.

3.  **Strength of "Learning Morphology" Claim:** The analysis provides strong suggestive evidence that the model is learning morphologically-relevant features. However, claiming it explicitly "learns morphology" is a very strong statement based on visualizations. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this as a compelling interpretation rather than a proven fact, or by including a more quantitative analysis comparing the model's implicit segmentations to a gold-standard linguistic morphological analyzer.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact mathematical formulation for the word encoder? Specifically, how are the weights `w_t` combined and/or normalized before being used in the weighted sum? Was the `tanh` function part of the weighting scheme itself, or was it only applied to the final vector as shown in the equation?

2.  The state reset mechanism in the HGRU (Eq. 2) seems to replace the previous character-level state `g_{t-1}` with the word-level state `s_{it}` at a boundary. Could you confirm if this is the correct interpretation? It seems this would discard the information from the final character of the previous word. Was this intentional, and did you explore alternative ways of integrating the word-level state `s` into the character-level decoder's state `g`?

3.  The morphological segmentation analysis in Figure 5 is fascinating. Have you considered performing a more quantitative evaluation of this capability by comparing the high-energy peaks against a gold-standard morphological segmentation of a corpus? This could provide even stronger evidence for your claims.
