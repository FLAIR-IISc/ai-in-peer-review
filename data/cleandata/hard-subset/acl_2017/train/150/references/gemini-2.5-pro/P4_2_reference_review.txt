### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical neural machine translation (NMT) model that operates entirely at the character level. The model aims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem of word-level models and the inefficiency of traditional character-level models. The architecture is structured in a hierarchical fashion: characters are composed into words, words into a sentence representation, which is then decoded first into word-level representations and finally into a target character sequence. The two primary technical innovations are: (1) a novel source word encoder, composed of two RNNs, designed to explicitly learn morphological representations by computing a weighted sum of character-prefix hidden states, and (2) a hierarchical decoder that uses a word-level RNN to guide a character-level RNN. The character-level decoder employs a custom Gated Recurrent Unit (HGRU) that allows its state to be reset by the word-level decoder at word boundaries, enabling efficient batch training. The authors evaluate their model (DCNMT) on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, showing it achieves competitive BLEU scores compared to state-of-the-art character-level models and outperforms subword-based models, particularly for morphologically rich languages.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel hierarchical architecture for character-level NMT.** The model effectively bridges the gap between word-level and character-level modeling by using a two-level decoder where a word-level RNN generates abstract word representations that subsequently condition a character-level RNN. The proposed HGRU mechanism and the "unfolding matrix" provide a technically sound and efficient method for implementing this hierarchy in a batch-training setting.
2.  **A character-based word encoder that learns weighted sub-word representations.** Instead of using a standard RNN or CNN to compose character embeddings, the paper introduces a dual-RNN encoder that computes an attention-like weighted sum over character prefix representations. This design is motivated by the goal of learning morphology, and the authors provide compelling qualitative analysis suggesting the model indeed learns to identify morpheme-like units within words.
3.  **Strong empirical results demonstrating competitive translation quality and training efficiency.** The paper shows that the proposed model achieves BLEU scores that are on par with or exceed other character-level and subword-level systems on multiple language pairs. Crucially, it demonstrates significantly improved training efficiency compared to other pure character-level models, which are often prohibitively slow to train.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel and well-motivated architecture.** The model's design is intuitive and addresses a clear challenge: leveraging the benefits of character-level modeling (no OOV issues, morphological awareness) while retaining the structural advantages of word-level processing (shorter sequence lengths at the sentence level). The separation of concerns between the word-level decoder (handling sentence semantics and structure) and the character-level decoder (handling word formation) is an elegant solution. The specific mechanisms, like the HGRU state reset, are technically sound and well-explained.
2.  **Compelling qualitative analysis to support the central claims.** Beyond reporting aggregate metrics like BLEU, the authors provide strong supporting evidence for their claim of "learning morphology." The PCA visualization of word embeddings (Figure 3) effectively shows that their encoder produces more semantically coherent representations for morphological variants compared to a standard RNN encoder. Furthermore, the analysis of the internal weights of the word encoder (Figure 4) and the inferred subword segmentations (Figure 5) offer concrete evidence that the model is learning meaningful sub-word structures.
3.  **Demonstrated efficiency and effectiveness.** The experimental results in Table 1 are strong. The model not only achieves competitive BLEU scores but does so with notable training efficiency. For instance, the claim that the model achieves strong performance after just one epoch on En-Fr and En-Cs tasks highlights its practical advantage. This efficiency is a critical contribution, as the computational cost has been a major barrier to the adoption of pure character-level NMT.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of clarity and justification for some design choices.** The formulation of the word encoder in Section 3.1 could be clearer. Specifically, the use of `tanh` on the weighted sum of character representations, rather than a standard normalization like softmax over the weights, is justified only by the claim that it "performs best in experiments." This lacks technical motivation. A more thorough explanation or ablation study is needed to understand why this non-standard choice is effective. Similarly, the claim in the Table 1 caption that the model is "the simplest" is questionable, given its complex six-RNN structure. While it may have fewer parameters, its architectural complexity is high, and this claim is potentially misleading without further qualification.
2.  **Insufficient comparison with prominent alternative methods.** The paper positions its word encoder against a simple RNN encoder but omits a direct empirical comparison to Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) based encoders (e.g., Kim et al., 2016). CNNs are a widely used and powerful method for composing word representations from characters and would serve as a much stronger and more relevant baseline for the proposed dual-RNN encoder. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the novelty and performance benefits of the proposed encoder relative to other established techniques. The paper's assertion that its RNN encoder is superior to CNNs in generating "meaningful" representations is unsubstantiated.
3.  **Potential for overstating the model's explicit learning of morphology.** While the qualitative analysis is compelling, the claim that the word encoder's decoupled RNNs learn morpheme representations (`rt`) and combination rules (`wt`) respectively is an interpretation of the model's emergent behavior. The architecture does not explicitly enforce this functional separation. The model learns a useful mechanism for composing character sequences, which appears to align with morphological principles, but the paper's language at times suggests a more explicit and guaranteed discovery of linguistic morphemes than the model structure warrants.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide further justification or an ablation study for using `tanh` to aggregate the weighted prefix representations in the word encoder, as opposed to a more standard approach like a simple weighted sum with softmax-normalized weights? What is the intuition behind this choice?
2.  The caption for Table 1 describes your model as "the simplest." Could you clarify this statement? While it has fewer parameters than some competitors, its six-RNN hierarchical architecture seems more complex. Does "simplest" refer to parameter count, or is there another dimension of simplicity (e.g., computational FLOPs per step) that you are referring to?
3.  To better isolate the contribution of your novel word encoder, how does it compare empirically against a strong baseline like a CNN-based character composition layer when substituted into the same overall DCNMT architecture?
