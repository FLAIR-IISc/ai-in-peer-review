### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, purely character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model with a novel hierarchical architecture. The model aims to combine the open-vocabulary advantages of character-level models with the efficiency of word-level models. On the encoder side, it first composes word representations from their constituent characters using a novel dual-RNN encoder, which is claimed to learn morphological structure. These word representations are then fed into a sentence-level BiRNN encoder. On the decoder side, a similar hierarchy is employed: a first-level, word-based decoder generates a sequence of hidden states, each of which initializes and guides a second-level, character-based decoder to produce the characters of a single target word. The model is trained end-to-end and evaluated on English-French and English-Czech translation tasks.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel hierarchical architecture for end-to-end character-level NMT.** The proposed model introduces a complete, multi-layered framework that operates at different granularities (character, word, sentence) on both the encoder and decoder sides. This structured approach is a distinct method for tackling the long-dependency problem inherent in flat character-level models.
2.  **An efficient mechanism for hierarchical character-level decoding.** The paper introduces a specialized GRU variant (HGRU) and an "unfolding matrix" technique. This allows the two-level decoder to be trained efficiently in batches, a non-trivial technical solution that makes the proposed complex architecture practical.
3.  **Strong empirical validation of the character-level approach.** The submission demonstrates that its purely character-based model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, contemporary subword-based and other character-based NMT systems, particularly for the morphologically rich Czech language. The analysis also provides qualitative evidence of the model's ability to handle out-of-vocabulary phenomena like misspellings and nonce words.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and coherent model architecture.** The paper's core idea of a hierarchical structure to bridge word- and character-level modeling is well-conceived. It directly addresses known challenges: the OOV problem in word-level models and the computational and learning difficulties (e.g., long dependencies) in flat character-level models. The symmetric design, with a character-to-word composition on the encoder and a word-to-character decomposition on the decoder, is elegant and principled.
2.  **Technically sound and novel decoder design.** The hierarchical decoder is a key strength. Using a higher-level RNN to generate abstract word representations (`s_t`) that guide a lower-level character-generating RNN is an effective way to manage sequence length. The introduction of the HGRU and the unfolding matrix to enable efficient batch training demonstrates technical depth and provides a practical solution to a significant implementation hurdle.
3.  **Comprehensive experimental results and analysis.** The authors evaluate their model on three different translation tasks, including a pair with a morphologically rich language (Czech), which is an appropriate test case for a morphology-aware model. The results in Table 1 show that the proposed DCNMT is highly competitive with state-of-the-art baselines. Furthermore, the qualitative analyses in Section 5.2 and 5.3 (PCA plots in Figure 3, weight visualizations in Figure 4, and translation examples in Table 2) provide compelling, albeit not quantitative, evidence that the model is learning useful sub-word structures and can generalize to unseen word forms.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of clarity and rigorous justification for the word encoder design.** The proposed word encoder uses a weighting scheme (`wt = exp(aff(ht))`) that is not normalized, followed by a `tanh` activation over the weighted sum of character states. The paper states this choice "performs best in experiments" without further justification or ablation studies. This feels ad-hoc compared to a more standard and interpretable attention mechanism (i.e., using a softmax to create a probability distribution over character positions). The central claim that this mechanism "learns morphology" is supported only by visualizations. A more quantitative analysis, such as comparing the induced segmentations to gold-standard morphological segmentations, would be necessary to substantiate this claim more formally.
2.  **Insufficient comparison with closely related methods.** The paper compares against several systems but does not include a direct, controlled comparison against the most obvious alternative for the word encoder: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). CNNs are a widely used and effective method for composing word representations from characters (Kim et al., 2016). The paper dismisses CNNs with the unsubstantiated claim that "the RNN word encoder in our model is able to generate a meaningful representation of the word," but provides no direct evidence. An experiment replacing the proposed word encoder with a CNN within the same DCNMT framework would be a crucial ablation study to demonstrate the specific benefits of the proposed dual-RNN design.
3.  **Incomplete analysis of computational efficiency.** While the model is presented as "efficient to train," and training times are reported in Table 1, the overall computational profile is not fully discussed. The model is deep, comprising six separate RNNs. A significant potential weakness is the inference speed, especially as beam search is performed at the character level, which can be much slower than word- or subword-level decoding. The paper provides no information on decoding speed (e.g., words or sentences per second) compared to the baselines, which is a critical metric for the practical applicability of any NMT system.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more details on the design of the word encoder's weighting mechanism? Specifically, did you experiment with a standard attention mechanism using a softmax over the character positions to compute the weights `w_t`? If so, how did its performance compare to the non-normalized exponential function you ultimately used?
2.  The claim that the model learns morphology is a key part of the paper's narrative. Beyond the insightful visualizations, have you considered a more quantitative evaluation of this claim? For example, one could use the learned weights to produce segmentations and compare them against a linguistic gold-standard morphological parser.
3.  Regarding computational efficiency, could you provide data on the model's inference speed (e.g., sentences per second during decoding with beam search)? How does the decoding speed of DCNMT compare to that of a strong subword-level baseline like the BPE model from your experiments?
