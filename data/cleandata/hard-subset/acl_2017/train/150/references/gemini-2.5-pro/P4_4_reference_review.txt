### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model. The architecture is hierarchical, operating at character, subword, and word levels. The main components are a novel word encoder designed to learn morphological representations and a hierarchical decoder for efficient character-level generation. The word encoder composes a word's vector representation from its characters using two RNNs: one generates representations for character prefixes, while a second bidirectional RNN computes weights for these representations, effectively creating a self-attention mechanism over morpheme-like units. The decoder is also two-leveled: a word-level RNN generates a sequence of states, and each state initializes a second, character-level RNN which generates the characters for one word until a delimiter is produced. The model is trained end-to-end and evaluated on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel character-based word encoder** that uses a weighted combination of character-prefix RNN states to form word representations. The weights are dynamically computed by a separate BiRNN, providing an architecture that can learn representations of subword units without requiring explicit segmentation.
2.  **A hierarchical decoder architecture** that couples a word-level RNN with a character-level RNN generator. This design, combined with a matrix-based unfolding technique for efficient batch training, effectively bridges the semantic gap between sentence-level context and character-level output generation.
3.  **An empirical demonstration** of the model's effectiveness, showing competitive or superior BLEU scores against subword-based and other character-level models, particularly on morphologically rich languages. The analysis provides qualitative evidence supporting the claim that the model learns morphological patterns and is robust to out-of-vocabulary words, including misspellings and nonce words.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Architecture:** The proposed architecture is well-motivated and presents a novel approach to character-level NMT. The word encoder's design—using one RNN to build compositional representations and another to weight them—is an elegant method for learning sub-word structure. The hierarchical decoder, which separates the high-level task of predicting the next word's semantics (via the first-level decoder state `s_t`) from the low-level task of spelling it out (via the second-level HGRU), is a principled way to manage the long dependencies inherent in character-level models.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Efficiency:** The model achieves impressive results, demonstrating performance comparable to or better than state-of-the-art character-level models on several language pairs. The claim of improved training efficiency is well-supported by Table 1, where the model reaches a strong baseline after only one epoch and completes training faster than several competing character-level models. This suggests the architecture provides a better inductive bias for learning, which is a significant advantage.

3.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis:** The paper goes beyond reporting BLEU scores and provides insightful analysis to support its claims. The PCA visualizations in Figure 3 effectively contrast the representations learned by the proposed encoder with a standard RNN encoder, showing a more structured embedding space that aligns with morphological similarities. Furthermore, the visualization of the internal weights in Figure 4 provides plausible evidence that the encoder identifies morpheme-like boundaries. The translation examples in Table 2, which demonstrate the ability to handle misspelled words and nonce words ("convenienter"), offer strong, practical evidence of the model's robustness and its capacity to generalize beyond its training vocabulary.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several important architectural details lack clear justification. In the word encoder, an unnormalized attention mechanism is used (an `exp` function without a softmax), and the result is passed through a `tanh`. The paper states this "performs best in experiments" but provides no further analysis or intuition as to why this would be preferable to a standard, normalized softmax attention. A more detailed ablation or analysis would be needed to understand the properties of this choice. Similarly, the formulation of the first-level decoder state `s_t` as a simple weighted sum (Eq. 8) is presented without justification over other possible aggregation methods.

2.  **Unsubstantiated Claims and Missing Comparisons:** The paper claims its RNN-based word encoder "is able to generate a meaningful representation of the word" in comparison to CNN-based encoders, but this is not directly supported by evidence. The claim is vague and would be much stronger if backed by a direct comparative analysis (e.g., side-by-side PCA plots or performance on a word similarity task) against a character-CNN encoder baseline. Without this, the asserted advantage over CNNs remains speculative.

3.  **Architectural Complexity and Potential Limitations:** The model is complex, comprising six separate RNNs. While presented as "deep," this complexity might raise concerns about scalability and parameter efficiency. More critically, the hierarchical decoder's training efficiency relies on an "unfolding matrix" `R` that is constructed using pre-existing delimiters (e.g., spaces) in the target text. This dependency on explicit word boundaries seems to contradict the goal of a "purely" character-level model and may limit its applicability to languages without clear segmentation markers. The paper does not adequately discuss this limitation or how the model would handle such languages.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the unnormalized attention mechanism in the word encoder? What is the intuition behind forgoing a standard softmax normalization, and did you observe any specific behaviors (e.g., attending to multiple sub-units simultaneously) that this enables?
2.  The efficiency of the hierarchical decoder during training depends on an unfolding matrix `R`, which is built using delimiters in the target sequence. How is this matrix constructed for unsegmented languages like Chinese or Japanese? Does this reliance on pre-defined word boundaries fundamentally limit the model's claim of being a general character-level solution?
3.  Regarding the generation procedure, the first-level decoder uses a feedback representation (`r_{Y_{t-1}}`) of the previously generated word. Does this mean that at inference time, the model must fully generate all characters of a word, encode that word, and only then compute the state for the next word? If so, how does this sequential, word-by-word generation process interact with character-level beam search, and does it create a bottleneck for decoding speed?
