**Review of "Deep Character-Level Neural Machine Translation By Learning Morphology"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes DCNMT, a deep, hierarchical neural machine translation model that operates entirely at the character level. The goal is to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem of word-based models while improving upon the efficiency and performance limitations of previous character-level approaches. The architecture features a novel word encoder that composes a word representation from its characters using a two-RNN structure with a weighted-sum mechanism intended to capture morphology. The decoder is also hierarchical, with a word-level attention-based RNN providing high-level guidance to a character-level RNN (HGRU) that generates the final translation. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive BLEU scores on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, while being more efficient to train than some prior character-level models and showing a qualitative ability to learn morphology and handle misspelled words.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Character-to-Character NMT Architecture:** The primary contribution is the specific end-to-end architecture that bridges character-level processing with word-level structural guidance. The design of the encoder, which first composes word vectors via an attention-like mechanism over characters and then encodes them at the sentence level, is elegant. This is complemented by a novel hierarchical decoder where a word-level RNN effectively controls a character-level generator, a design made efficient for batch training via a custom GRU variant (HGRU).
2.  **An Empirical Demonstration of a Competitive and Efficient Purely Character-Level Model:** The paper provides strong evidence that a purely character-level model can achieve performance comparable to, and in some cases better than, strong subword-based (BPE) baselines, particularly for a morphologically rich language like Czech. Furthermore, it demonstrates that this can be achieved with reasonable training efficiency, a significant challenge for previous character-level NMT systems.
3.  **Qualitative Analysis Suggesting Learned Morphological Awareness:** A key contribution is the set of analyses that provide insight into the model's inner workings. The visualizations of the word encoder's internal weights and the resulting word representations (Figures 3, 4, 5) offer compelling, albeit indirect, evidence that the model learns to identify and use subword units akin to morphemes, without any explicit morphological supervision.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Model Design:** The hierarchical structure is a very sensible approach to character-level NMT. By operating on word-level representations in the upper layers of the encoder and decoder, the model can more easily capture long-range dependencies, a known weakness of flat character-level RNNs. At the same time, the character-level components at the lowest layer provide an open vocabulary and the flexibility to model subword structure. The proposed solution is a compelling way to combine the strengths of both word-level and character-level modeling.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The model is evaluated on three language pairs, including one with a morphologically rich target/source (Czech), which is the right context to test a morphology-aware model. The BLEU scores are competitive with or superior to the reported baselines from the time, including the widely used BPE method. The training time comparison in Table 1, showing DCNMT is significantly faster to train than another purely character-level model, is a strong argument for the architectural choices made.
3.  **Convincing Qualitative Evidence for Morphological Learning:** The analysis in Section 5.2 is excellent and provides strong support for the paper's central claim. The PCA plots effectively demonstrate that the proposed word encoder produces more semantically structured representations than a vanilla RNN encoder. The visualizations of the character weights (Figure 4) and the induced subword segmentations (Figure 5) are highly persuasive. Finally, the ability of the model to correctly translate misspelled and nonce words (Table 2) is a powerful and practical demonstration of the benefits of this approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison with Key Baselines:** The paper relies on citing results for its main baselines (BPE, C2W, etc.) from other papers. While understandable, this weakens the empirical claims, as it is difficult to ensure that differences in performance are not due to confounding factors like minor differences in data preprocessing, model size, or hyperparameter tuning. A more rigorous evaluation would have involved re-implementing at least the main BPE baseline within the same experimental framework for a controlled, head-to-head comparison of performance and training speed.
2.  **Unclear Justification for Key Design Choices in the Word Encoder:** The formulation of the word encoder's weighting mechanism is unconventional. The weights `w_t` are calculated as `exp(aff(h_t))` and are used directly in a weighted sum, which is then passed through a `tanh`. This differs significantly from a standard attention mechanism, which would normalize the weights using a softmax. The authors state this "performs best in experiments," but this is insufficient justification. This lack of theoretical or intuitive motivation for a core component of the proposed model makes the contribution harder to interpret and build upon.
3.  **Missing Ablation Studies:** The model combines several novel components: the two-RNN word encoder, the hierarchical decoder, and a target-side encoder for feedback. The paper includes one ablation (CNMT vs. DCNMT) which is useful, but the analysis would be much stronger with more. For instance, how does the proposed word encoder compare to one using a standard softmax-based attention? What is the performance impact of the hierarchical decoder versus a flat character decoder, when both use the same proposed encoder? Such ablations would help to disentangle the contributions of the different architectural innovations.
4.  **Efficiency Claims Could Be Better Substantiated:** While the model is shown to be faster than one specific character-level model, its overall efficiency compared to a standard BPE model is not fully clear. The DCNMT model contains six RNNs and processes long character sequences in its lower layers. The claim that it "almost keeps the same encoding length" is only true for the second layer of the encoder. A more detailed analysis, such as a comparison of wall-clock training time per step or total FLOPs against a BPE model with a similar parameter count, would provide a more complete picture of the computational trade-offs.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation behind the word encoder's weighting mechanism (`exp` without softmax, followed by `tanh` on the sum)? How does this formulation compare empirically to using a standard, softmax-normalized attention mechanism over the character states?
2.  Regarding the HGRU update (Eq. 2), the text says the state is "set" or "initialized" by the word-level decoder's output at word boundaries. However, the equation `g_{t-1} = (1- a_{t-1})g_{t-1} + a_{t-1}s_it` looks more like a gated update. Could you clarify if the previous character-level state is completely discarded at a boundary (`g_{t-1} = s_it`) or if there is some carry-over information?
3.  The performance of your model after just one epoch is remarkably strong, sometimes outperforming the final BPE baseline. This suggests very rapid learning. Do you have an intuition for why your architecture facilitates this, and does this trend hold if you were to use a much larger training corpus?
