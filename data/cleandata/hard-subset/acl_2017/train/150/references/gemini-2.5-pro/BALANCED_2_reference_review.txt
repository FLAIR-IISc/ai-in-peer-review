### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT, designed to overcome the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem of word-level models while maintaining computational efficiency compared to flat character-level models. The architecture is composed of four hierarchical layers. At the encoder, a novel word encoder first computes representations for source words from their constituent characters using a weighted combination of character-level RNN states. These word representations are then fed into a standard bidirectional sentence-level RNN. The decoder is also hierarchical: a word-level decoder RNN generates a hidden state for each target word, which in turn initializes the state of a second-level character-level RNN (HGRU) that generates the characters for that word until a delimiter is produced. The model is evaluated on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, showing competitive performance against subword and other character-level baselines, particularly for the morphologically rich Czech language.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel, fully character-level hierarchical NMT architecture.** The core contribution is the specific design that decomposes the character-to-character translation problem into a more structured, multi-level process (character→word→sentence→target word→target character). This allows the model to operate without a fixed vocabulary while mitigating the long-dependency challenges inherent in flat character-level models.
2.  **An insightful qualitative analysis of the model's ability to learn morphology.** The paper provides compelling visualizations (Figures 3, 4, and 5) and examples (Table 2) that offer a window into the model's inner workings. This analysis demonstrates that the character-based word encoder learns meaningful sub-word structures and can generalize to handle misspelled and nonce words, which adds significant scientific value beyond just reporting performance metrics.
3.  **Strong empirical results on morphologically rich languages.** The model achieves competitive BLEU scores on the En-Cs and Cs-En language pairs, validating the architecture's effectiveness for languages where handling morphology is critical for translation quality.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical structure is conceptually appealing. By introducing a word-level abstraction within a purely character-level framework, the model combines the benefits of both worlds: the open-vocabulary nature of character models and the more manageable sequence lengths of word models. This design choice directly addresses the primary difficulty of training char-to-char models on long sentences.
2.  **Effective Handling of OOV and Morphological Variants:** The paper provides strong qualitative evidence for the model's robustness to words not seen during training. As shown in Table 2, the ability to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words with productive morphology ("convenienter") is a significant practical advantage over standard word-level or BPE-based models, which would typically map these to an `<unk>` token or fail to interpret them correctly.
3.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis and Interpretability:** A major strength of this work is the analysis in Section 5.2. The PCA projection showing semantically coherent word representations and the visualization of the internal "energy" weights suggesting morpheme detection provide valuable insights into *how* the model works. This level of analysis helps build confidence that the model is not just a black box but is indeed learning linguistic structure as hypothesized.
4.  **Competitive Performance and Training Efficiency:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are comparable or superior to the reported baselines, especially on the morphologically complex En-Cs/Cs-En tasks. The claim that the model trains efficiently is supported by the strong performance after just one epoch (Table 1), suggesting that the architecture's morphological learning capability leads to faster convergence. This makes the proposed method a practical and interesting direction for character-level NMT.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Novelty of Architectural Components:**
    *   **Word Encoder:** The word encoder computes a weighted sum of character-level RNN states. This is functionally very similar to an attention mechanism over the characters of a word. The paper should more clearly position this component relative to prior work on character-aware word embeddings that use attention. The motivation for using `tanh` instead of a standard `softmax` for the weights is not explained or ablated, making it seem like an arbitrary design choice.
    *   **Hierarchical Decoder (HGRU):** The description of the HGRU and the training process is difficult to follow. Equation (2) appears to be a typo, as it uses `g_{t-1}` on both sides of the assignment. More critically, the "unfolding" matrix `R` used during training seems to depend on the ground-truth delimiter positions. It is unclear how this mechanism translates to inference time, when these positions are not known in advance. The generation procedure (Section 3.4) is described too briefly to fully understand how beam search is performed over this two-level generative process.
2.  **Potentially Weak Baselines:** The BPE baseline for En-Fr WMT'14 is reported at 29.70 BLEU. However, the original BPE paper (Sennrich et al., 2016) and other contemporary works report scores in the 34-35 BLEU range for this specific task. Using a significantly weaker baseline result from an unrelated paper (Firat et al., 2016) may overstate the relative performance of the proposed model. It would be more convincing to compare against stronger, more widely recognized baseline scores or to re-run the baselines within the same experimental framework.
3.  **Exaggerated Claims of Simplicity and Efficiency:** The paper claims the model is the "simplest and the smallest one". While its parameter count (54M) is competitive, an architecture with six distinct RNNs is conceptually complex, not simple. The training efficiency claim is also nuanced; while the one-epoch results are impressive, the final results require ~15-20 days of training, which is not exceptionally fast. A more detailed breakdown of training speed (e.g., sentences/sec) would be needed for a fair comparison.
4.  **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The paper compares performance against several key papers, but a deeper discussion of architectural differences is missing. For example, the model of Lee et al. (2016) is also a pure character-level model. A discussion on the pros and cons of the proposed hierarchical approach versus their deeper, flat architecture would strengthen the paper's positioning. The claim that the model generates "a meaningful representation of the word" compared to CNN encoders is asserted without evidence or direct comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the HGRU update rule, specifically Equation (2), which seems to be erroneous? Could you also elaborate on how the two-level decoding process works during inference with beam search, particularly how the model decides when to generate the next word-level state `s_{t+1}`?
2.  Could you further explain the novelty of the word encoder in relation to standard attention mechanisms applied over characters? Have you experimented with using a standard softmax for the weights, and how did it affect performance compared to the `tanh` activation?
3.  The BPE baseline for En-Fr seems significantly lower than what is commonly reported for the WMT'14 task. Could you comment on this discrepancy and how your model might fare against stronger, more contemporary baselines?
