### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical neural machine translation (NMT) model that operates at the character level, aiming to solve the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem while explicitly learning word morphology. The architecture is composed of four hierarchical layers: (1) a novel source word encoder that uses a self-attention mechanism over characters to form a word representation, (2) a standard BiRNN sentence encoder, (3) a word-level RNN decoder, and (4) a character-level RNN decoder (HGRU) whose state is periodically reset by the output of the word-level decoder. The model is trained end-to-end and demonstrates strong performance on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A Novel Morphologically-Aware Word Encoder:** The core novelty lies in the word encoder (Fig. 1), which uses one RNN to process characters and a second BiRNN to compute an unnormalized attention weight for each character's hidden state. This allows the model to compose a word representation by focusing on important morphemes. The paper provides strong qualitative evidence that this structure successfully captures morphological regularities.
2.  **An Efficient Hierarchical Decoding Architecture:** The two-level decoder, which combines a word-level RNN with a character-generating HGRU, provides a structured way to handle long character sequences. The HGRU's state-reset mechanism and the clever use of an "unfolding matrix" for batching make this complex architecture efficient to train, which is a significant advantage over other pure character-level models that are often slow.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper shows that the proposed model (DCNMT) achieves BLEU scores competitive with state-of-the-art subword and character-level models, while converging much faster. The analysis in Section 5.2, particularly the PCA plots and weight visualizations, provides compelling evidence for the claim that the model learns meaningful morphological representations.

### Strengths

1.  **Convincing Motivation and Solution for Learning Morphology:** The central claim of the paper is that it learns morphology, and it backs this up exceptionally well. The design of the word encoder is intuitive, and the analysis in Section 5.2 is a major strength. The visualizations in Figure 3 (PCA plots showing better-structured embeddings) and Figure 4 (attention weights peaking at morpheme boundaries) provide clear, interpretable evidence that the encoder is behaving as intended. This goes beyond just achieving a high BLEU score and offers insight into the model's internal workings.
2.  **Excellent Training Efficiency for a Character-Level Model:** A common drawback of character-level NMT is the difficulty and high computational cost of training due to very long sequences. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their hierarchical structure alleviates this problem. As shown in Table 1, their model achieves strong results after just one epoch and outperforms strong baselines in significantly fewer training days than competing character-level models (e.g., 15-22 days vs. 90 days for Luong and Manning, 2016 on En-Cs/Cs-En).
3.  **Well-Written and Clearly Presented:** The paper is easy to follow. The architecture, though complex with six RNNs, is broken down logically and explained well with the aid of clear diagrams (Figures 1 and 2). The experiments are well-designed, comparing the model against relevant and strong baselines on both morphologically poor (En-Fr) and rich (En-Cs) language pairs. The qualitative results on misspelled and nonce words (Table 2) effectively highlight the practical advantages of the character-level approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unclear Justification for Certain Architectural Choices:** While the overall architecture is well-motivated, some specific design details lack clear justification.
    *   In the word encoder, the attention-like weights `w_t` are unnormalized (`exp(aff(h_t))`), and the final representation is passed through a `tanh` activation. This is a departure from the standard softmax-normalized attention. The paper states `tanh` "performs best in experiments," but provides no further intuition or analysis. A brief ablation or justification for why this unnormalized, non-convex combination works better would strengthen the paper.
    *   In Section 3.3, the authors state that for the target-side feedback, "As the feedback is less important, we use an ordinary RNN to encode the target word." This justification is weak and feels arbitrary. Why is the feedback less important? An ablation study showing the impact of using the proposed morphological encoder on the target side as well would be valuable to understand this asymmetry.
2.  **Comparison to Subword Models (BPE) Could Be More Nuanced:** The paper contrasts its method with BPE by stating "we learn the subword unit automatically." However, BPE is also an automatic, data-driven algorithm for finding subword units. The key difference is that BPE is statistical and pre-computed, while DCNMT's segmentation is neural and dynamic. A more direct comparison would be beneficial. For example, does DCNMT's emergent segmentation align better with linguistic morphemes than BPE's? The analysis in Figure 5 is a good start, but a quantitative comparison or a deeper qualitative discussion against BPE segmentations would make the claimed advantages more concrete.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Inference Speed:** The paper focuses heavily on training efficiency, which is a valid and important contribution. However, for practical NMT systems, inference speed is also a critical factor. The hierarchical decoding process—generating a word-level state, then generating characters one by one until a delimiter, then repeating—seems potentially slower than a standard BPE-based model that generates larger tokens at each step. A discussion or measurement of decoding speed (e.g., words/sec) compared to the baselines would provide a more complete picture of the model's practical utility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for the word encoder's weighting mechanism? Specifically, why did you opt for an unnormalized exponential followed by a `tanh` on the sum, instead of a more standard softmax-normalized attention? Was this choice critical to performance?
2.  What was the rationale for using a simpler RNN for the target-side word representation feedback, rather than applying your proposed morphological encoder symmetrically? Did you experiment with using the morphological encoder on the target side as well, and if so, what was the impact on performance and training time?
3.  Regarding inference, how does the decoding speed of your hierarchical model compare to a strong BPE-based baseline? Does the character-by-character generation within the HGRU introduce significant latency?
