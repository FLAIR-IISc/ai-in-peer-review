### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model. The architecture is composed of four main layers: (1) a novel source word encoder that uses a weighted sum of character-level RNN states to form word representations, with weights determined by a second BiRNN; (2) a standard BiRNN sentence encoder operating on these word representations; (3) a first-level "word" decoder that generates a sequence of context vectors; and (4) a second-level character decoder (termed HGRU) that takes these context vectors as initial states to generate character sequences for each target word. The primary contributions of this work are the proposal of this complex hierarchical architecture, an empirical evaluation on three translation tasks showing performance comparable to other character-level models, and a qualitative analysis suggesting the model learns aspects of morphology.

### Strengths
1.  **Fully Character-Level Approach:** The proposed model is a pure end-to-end character-level system. This is an elegant way to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem that plagues word-level models, eliminating the need for a fixed vocabulary or fallback mechanisms.
2.  **Effective Handling of Morphological Variants and Noise:** The qualitative examples in Table 2 are compelling. The model demonstrates a capacity to correctly translate misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words with novel morphology ("convenienter"). This showcases a practical benefit of character-level modeling that is not as easily achieved with standard word-level or even BPE-based systems, which would likely map such inputs to `<unk>`.
3.  **Reasonable Training Efficiency:** Despite its architectural depth (six RNNs in total), the model appears to be relatively efficient to train. As shown in Table 1, the training time in days is competitive with, and in some cases faster than, other published character-level models, suggesting that the proposed hierarchical decoding scheme is effective from a practical standpoint.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Justification for Architectural Complexity:** The most significant weakness of this work is the introduction of a highly complex architecture to achieve performance that is only marginally better, and not consistently superior, to simpler, well-established methods. The byte pair encoding (BPE) approach (Sennrich et al., 2016) is a very strong and widely adopted baseline for handling rare words and morphology. The proposed DCNMT achieves a BLEU score of 30.56 on En-Fr, a modest +0.86 improvement over the reported BPE baseline (29.70). On Cs-En, the performance is comparable to other character-based systems. This small gain in performance does not seem to warrant the substantial increase in model complexity (six RNNs vs. a standard encoder-decoder). A much stronger empirical result would be needed to justify the adoption of this intricate design over simpler alternatives.
2.  **Unsubstantiated Claims of "Learning Morphology":** The paper's central claim is that its word encoder "learns morphology." However, the evidence provided is entirely qualitative, anecdotal, and open to interpretation.
    *   The PCA plots in Figure 3 show some separation, but this is a weak form of evidence for such a strong claim and is not compared against other strong character-composition baselines like CNNs.
    *   The "energy" plots in Figures 4 and 5 are visualizations of internal attention weights. Interpreting these peaks as morpheme boundaries is a post-hoc narrative. Without quantitative validation against a gold-standard morphological segmentation, this remains a hypothesis, not a conclusion. Simpler mechanisms also implicitly capture subword information; this paper fails to prove that its explicit mechanism is either necessary or superior in a rigorous way.
3.  **Lack of Ablation Studies and Component Analysis:** The model introduces several novel components simultaneously (the dual-RNN word encoder, the hierarchical decoder, the HGRU modification), but their individual contributions are not analyzed. An ablation study is essential to understand the source of any performance gains. For example, how does the proposed word encoder compare against a simpler character-level CNN (Kim et al., 2016) or a standard character BiRNN within the same hierarchical framework? The "CNMT" baseline is a step in this direction, but its architecture is not detailed sufficiently, and the analysis does not go far enough to justify the specific design of the DCNMT word encoder.
4.  **Inconsistent Comparison to Baselines:** The experimental results in Table 1 are aggregated from several different papers. This is problematic as training setups (e.g., data filtering, optimization, hardware) can vary significantly and affect final performance. More importantly, the baselines are run with different constraints; for instance, the BPE models are limited to a maximum sequence length of 50 words, whereas the proposed model is trained on much longer character sequences (up to 450). This is not a controlled comparison and may unfairly disadvantage the baseline models. A rigorous evaluation would require re-implementing key baselines, particularly BPE, under the exact same experimental conditions.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The primary justification for your model's complexity appears to be its translation quality. Could you provide a more direct and fair comparison to the BPE baseline by re-running it with your exact data preprocessing, training framework, and comparable maximum sequence lengths (e.g., by setting a character limit for BPE sentences that is similar to yours)?
2.  The claim of "learning morphology" is central to your paper's motivation. Can you provide a quantitative evaluation to support this? For example, by comparing the subword segmentations induced by your model's attention weights (as in Figure 5) against a gold-standard morphological analyzer to measure precision and recall?
3.  Could you perform an ablation study to isolate the impact of your novel word encoder? Specifically, how does replacing your dual-RNN word encoder with a standard character-level CNN or a simple BiRNN (while keeping the rest of the hierarchical architecture identical) affect translation performance and the qualitative "morphology learning" results?
