**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a deep, fully character-to-character neural machine translation model named DCNMT. The architecture is hierarchical on both the encoder and decoder sides to manage the long sequence lengths inherent in character-level processing. The main components are a novel source-side word encoder and a hierarchical decoder. The word encoder uses two RNNs: one to process characters sequentially and a second to compute weights for each character's hidden state, with the weighted sum forming the final word representation. The authors claim this allows the model to learn morphological features. The decoder is also two-level: a word-level decoder generates a sequence of abstract word representations, and a character-level decoder (using a custom HGRU cell) takes each of these representations to generate the corresponding character sequence until a delimiter is produced. The model is evaluated on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, achieving results competitive with state-of-the-art subword and character-level models while reporting greater training efficiency.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and complete hierarchical architecture for character-to-character NMT. The core innovation lies in structuring the model to operate on word-level representations at the sentence level while still processing characters at the input and output, effectively combining the strengths of both paradigms. The specific mechanisms for composing word vectors from characters (weighted RNN states) and decomposing them back into characters (hierarchical decoder) are the key technical contributions.
2.  An empirical demonstration that a pure character-level model can be trained efficiently and achieve strong performance competitive with BPE-based and other contemporary character-level systems. The reported training times and BLEU scores, particularly on morphologically rich languages like Czech, validate the architectural design.
3.  Qualitative and quantitative analysis suggesting the model's source word encoder learns linguistically plausible representations. The visualizations of internal weights and PCA projections of word embeddings provide evidence supporting the claim that the model captures aspects of morphology without explicit supervision.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed architecture presents an elegant solution to a known problem in character-level NMT: the computational and memory burden of applying attention over very long character sequences. By introducing a hierarchical structure that processes word-level representations at the sentence level, the model keeps the sequences for the primary encoder-decoder attention mechanism short, similar to word-based models. This is a sound and well-justified design choice that directly addresses a major bottleneck. The hierarchical decoder is a complementary and logical component for this design.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Efficiency:** The model achieves competitive BLEU scores across three translation tasks, including for a morphologically rich language (Czech). Notably, on En-Cs, it outperforms the BPE baseline and is on par with other state-of-the-art character-level models. A significant strength is the model's reported training efficiency. For instance, the paper reports training the En-Fr model in approximately 7 days for one epoch and 19 days total, a substantial improvement over the months-long training reported for some other pure character-level models cited in the paper. This makes the proposed approach practically more viable.

3.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis of OOV Handling:** The analysis in Section 5.3 provides strong evidence for the model's practical benefits. The examples of correctly translating misspelled words ("unconclusive") and unseen morphological variants ("convenienter") clearly demonstrate the model's robustness and generalization capabilities. This is a key advantage of character-level modeling that this work successfully realizes and highlights, setting it apart from subword models which would struggle with such out-of-vocabulary phenomena.

4.  **Insightful Model Analysis:** The paper provides valuable analysis of the internal workings of the proposed word encoder. The visualization of character weights in Figure 4, which suggests the model assigns high importance to morpheme boundaries (e.g., after "consum-", "er-", "-s"), and the PCA plots in Figure 3, which show better separation of morphologically related words, lend credible support to the central claim of "learning morphology." This level of analysis enhances the scientific contribution of the paper beyond just reporting performance metrics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in the Hierarchical Decoder Description:** The description of the hierarchical decoder (Section 3.2) is difficult to follow and appears to contain technical inaccuracies.
    *   Equation (2), `gt−1 = (1− at−1)gt−1 + at−1sit`, is a non-functional recursive definition. It seems intended to describe a conditional state update, where the state from the first-level decoder (`sit`) is used only at word boundaries (when `at-1=1`). The current formulation is unclear and likely a typo.
    *   The use of the "unfolding matrix" `R` for efficient batch training is described, but its relation to the generation procedure (Section 3.4) and the overall training logic is not fully explained. During training, this seems to require ground-truth word segmentations to construct the matrix `R` and to generate the feedback representations `rYt−1` for the first-level decoder. This reliance on pre-existing word boundaries seems to contradict the goal of a pure character-level model and needs significant clarification.

2.  **Insufficient Ablation and Comparison of Architectural Choices:** The paper introduces several novel components, but their contributions are not always isolated or justified against simpler alternatives.
    *   The claim that the proposed RNN-based word encoder is superior to a CNN-based one (as in Kim et al., 2016) is not substantiated with a direct experimental comparison. A quantitative result from swapping the encoder component would be necessary to validate this claim.
    *   The weighting mechanism in the word encoder uses an `exp()` function without subsequent normalization (like a softmax), followed by a `tanh` on the final sum. The paper states this "performs best in experiments" without providing any results from ablation studies. This is an unconventional choice that could lead to unstable gradients, and its superiority over a standard attention-like weighting is not established.

3.  **Potentially Overstated Claims:** Some claims in the paper are not fully supported by the evidence provided. The claim that the model "learn[s] the subword unit automatically" in contrast to BPE is imprecise. The model learns a continuous weighting scheme over characters, which is different from learning a discrete subword vocabulary like BPE. While the weights align with morpheme boundaries, the mechanism is fundamentally different, and the phrasing could be more exact.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the corrected formulation for the HGRU state update in Equation (2) and clarify how the previous character-level state is combined with the new word-level state `sit` at word boundaries?
2.  During training, how is the ground-truth target character sequence handled by the hierarchical decoder? Specifically, is the sequence pre-segmented into words to create the unfolding matrix `R` and to provide the input for the target word encoder (which generates `rYt−1`)? If so, please elaborate on this preprocessing step.
3.  The word encoder calculates weights `w_t` using an `exp` function but does not normalize them into a probability distribution. Could you provide an empirical or theoretical justification for this design choice over a standard softmax normalization?
4.  The analysis of learned morphology is compelling. Did you consider a more direct, quantitative evaluation of this capability, for instance, by assessing the model's performance on a held-out set of rare but rule-based morphological variants?
