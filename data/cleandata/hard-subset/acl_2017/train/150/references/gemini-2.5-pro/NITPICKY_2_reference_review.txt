### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a deep, hierarchical character-level neural machine translation model (DCNMT). The model is designed to learn morphological structures automatically without relying on explicit subword segmentation methods like BPE. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel source-side word encoder that uses two RNNs to compute a weighted representation of a word from its characters. One RNN reads the character sequence, while a second bidirectional RNN computes weights for each character's hidden state, aiming to identify and weigh morphemes.
2.  A hierarchical decoder composed of a word-level RNN and a character-level RNN. The word-level decoder's state is used to initialize the character-level decoder, which then generates characters until a word delimiter is produced. The paper introduces a specific gated unit (HGRU) and a matrix-based unfolding technique to facilitate efficient batch training.
3.  An empirical evaluation on En-Fr, En-Cs, and Cs-En translation tasks, showing that the proposed model achieves competitive performance against strong subword-level and other character-level baselines, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The paper also includes qualitative analyses to support the claim that the model learns morphology.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Architecture for Learning Sub-Word Structure:** The proposed word encoder is an interesting and novel method for learning representations from characters. Instead of relying on fixed segmentation (like BPE) or generic composition functions (like CNNs or simple RNNs), the use of a second RNN to dynamically weight character representations is a principled way to model the varying importance of character n-grams, potentially capturing morphemes. This is a creative architectural contribution.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves BLEU scores that are competitive with or superior to several strong baselines, including BPE-based models and other character-level systems, across multiple language pairs. The relatively efficient training time compared to some other pure character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016) is also a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides good qualitative evidence to support its central claim of learning morphology. The PCA plots in Figure 3, the attention "energy" visualizations in Figure 4, and the ability to translate nonce words like "convenienter" (Table 2) strongly suggest that the model is capturing sub-word compositional semantics beyond what a standard character-level or word-level model might.

### Weaknesses

My recommendation is primarily driven by a significant number of issues related to clarity, methodological rigor, and presentation quality, which collectively undermine the paper's contribution.

1.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Clarity:** The paper is rife with ambiguities, undefined terms, and potential typos in key equations, which make the method difficult to understand and reproduce.
    *   **Word Encoder:** In Section 3.1, the authors state that instead of a softmax for the weights `w_t`, they use `tanh` "as it performs best". This is a crucial design choice that deviates from standard attention mechanisms. It is insufficiently justified and lacks any comparative analysis. Why would a non-positive, non-normalized weight be beneficial?
    *   **Hierarchical Decoder:** Equation (2) appears to have a typo, with `g_{t-1}` on both sides of the update equation. The notation `s_{it}` is used without defining the index `i`.
    *   **Model Architecture:** In Equation (8), the term `u_{t-1}` is used without any prior definition. One can infer it is the previous state of the first-level decoder, but this should be stated explicitly.
    *   **Experimental Details:** Critical hyperparameters are missing. The beam size for beam search, a parameter with a major impact on NMT performance, is not specified. The learning rate annealing schedule is vaguely described as "annealed to 10^{-4}" without specifying the trigger condition.

2.  **Inconsistencies and Overstated Claims:**
    *   The paper claims to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issue inherent in word-level models. However, Section 4.1 states that characters not in the 120-character vocabulary are mapped to `<unk>`. This creates a character-level OOV problem, undercutting the "open vocabulary" claim. The presence of `<unk>` tokens in the analysis in Figure 5 is jarring and unexplained.
    *   The claim in Section 5.1 that "our model is the simplest" is subjective and highly debatable. An architecture with six distinct RNNs is arguably more complex than a standard encoder-decoder with BPE.
    *   The analysis of morpheme segmentation in Figure 5 is presented as a success, but the model fails to segment "greatest" into "great-est", a canonical example of English morphology. This counterexample to the paper's central claim is not discussed.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   There are numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "can effect the performance" instead of "affect", "coveres" instead of "covers").
    *   The reference section is catastrophically malformed. Entries contain BibTeX artifacts like `\.,?` and repeated author blocks (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? 2015, Bahdanau et al\.,"). This indicates a severe lack of attention to detail in the final preparation of the manuscript and is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide the corrected version of Equation (2) and explicitly define the variables `u_{t-1}` in Eq. (8) and the indexing for `s_{it}` in Section 3.2?
2.  What was the beam size used for decoding in all experiments?
3.  Could you provide more justification for using a `tanh` activation for the word encoder weights instead of a normalized softmax? For instance, what was the performance of a softmax-normalized variant in your experiments?
4.  Why did you opt for a limited character vocabulary with an `<unk>` token instead of using all characters in the training corpus to create a truly open-vocabulary model? How many character types were mapped to `<unk>`?
