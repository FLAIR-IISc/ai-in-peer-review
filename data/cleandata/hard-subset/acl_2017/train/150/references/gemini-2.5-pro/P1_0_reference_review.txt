### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a deep character-level neural machine translation (NMT) model, named DCNMT, designed to explicitly learn and leverage morphology. The architecture is hierarchical, processing information from characters to subwords, to words, and then through a sentence-level encoder-decoder. The key contributions are twofold: 1) a novel word encoder that uses two RNNs to compute a weighted representation of a word's characters, which the authors claim learns to identify morphemes, and 2) a hierarchical decoder that combines a word-level RNN with a character-level RNN (HGRU). The word-level decoder provides a high-level state for each target word, which then initializes the character-level decoder to generate the sequence of characters for that word. The authors propose an efficient batching method using an "unfolding matrix" to manage the different sequence lengths of the two decoder levels. The model is evaluated on English-French, English-Czech, and Czech-English translation tasks, showing results competitive with state-of-the-art character-level models while claiming greater efficiency and providing strong qualitative evidence of learning morphology.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Hierarchical Decoder for Efficient Character-Level Generation:** The primary contribution is the two-level decoder architecture. It elegantly combines the structural guidance of a word-level model with the flexibility of a character-level model. The proposed HGRU mechanism, which resets its state based on the word-level decoder's output, and the associated unfolding matrix for efficient batch training, represent a solid and novel engineering solution to a challenging problem.
2.  **A Morphologically-Aware Word Encoder with In-depth Analysis:** The paper introduces a word encoder based on a character-level self-attention mechanism. While the mechanism itself has precedents, its application here is coupled with a compelling and detailed analysis (PCA plots, energy visualizations, nonce-word translations) that strongly supports the claim that the model learns meaningful sub-word, morpheme-like units. This analysis is a significant contribution in its own right, as it provides interpretability and insight into the model's behavior.

### Strengths
1.  **Convincing Qualitative Analysis and Practical Benefits:** The paper's strongest aspect is its qualitative analysis. The visualizations in Figures 3, 4, and 5 provide strong, intuitive evidence for the central claim that the word encoder learns morphological structure. More compellingly, the translation examples in Table 2, especially the correct handling of misspelled words ("unconclusive") and nonce words ("convenienter"), offer a powerful demonstration of the model's practical advantages over systems reliant on a fixed vocabulary (word or subword-based). This moves beyond BLEU scores to show a tangible improvement in robustness.
2.  **Elegant and Effective Model Architecture:** The proposed hierarchical structure is well-motivated and thoughtfully designed. The separation of concerns—a word-level decoder to handle sentence structure and a character-level decoder to handle word formation—is intuitive. The HGRU mechanism is a clever way to pass information between these levels. The model's strong performance, particularly on the morphologically rich Czech language pairs, validates the effectiveness of this design.
3.  **Focus on Training Efficiency:** The authors address a major drawback of many pure character-level models: computational cost. The design of the hierarchical decoder, specifically the unfolding matrix trick, allows for efficient batch processing despite the variable-length character sequences within each word. The results reported in Table 1, showing competitive performance achieved in fewer days than competing character-level models (e.g., Luong and Manning, 2016), make a strong case for the model's practicality. The impressive performance after just one epoch further underscores this efficiency.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Discussion of the Novelty of the Word Encoder:** The word encoder, which computes a weighted sum of character representations, is functionally a self-attention mechanism over the character sequence of a word. The paper presents it as a novel architecture without positioning it within the broader context of attention mechanisms, which were already well-established. Acknowledging this connection and highlighting what makes their specific formulation (e.g., using two separate RNNs) unique or advantageous would strengthen the paper's claims of novelty.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Design Choices:** Several design choices are justified only by stating they "perform best in experiments." Most notably, in the word encoder, the weights `w_t` are not normalized via a softmax, and the final representation is passed through a `tanh`. This is a non-standard formulation for an attention-like mechanism. The paper would be significantly improved by providing some intuition or analysis as to *why* this might be better. Does it allow the model to assign high importance to multiple morphemes simultaneously, which a softmax would suppress? This lack of explanation leaves a gap in the reader's understanding of the model.
3.  **Clarity of the Hierarchical Decoder Explanation:** The explanation of the hierarchical decoder in Section 3.2 is quite dense. While the equations are provided, the intuition behind the unfolding matrix `R` and its role in enabling batch computation could be made clearer. A small, concrete diagram or a step-by-step textual walkthrough illustrating how the word-level states `[s1, s2]` are broadcast to the character positions of "g o !" would greatly improve the accessibility of this core component of the model.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to forgo a softmax normalization for the weights `w_t` in the word encoder? What was the empirical performance drop when using a standard softmax? More importantly, what is your hypothesis for why the unnormalized weights followed by a final `tanh` work better?
2.  Your model demonstrates impressive performance after only one training epoch, in some cases outperforming fully trained BPE models. Does the model's performance tend to plateau quickly after this initial large gain, or does it continue to improve at a steady rate? Understanding the learning dynamics would help clarify the "speedup" benefit you claim.
3.  Have you analyzed potential failure modes of the model? For instance, does the character-level decoder ever get "stuck" generating nonsensical or repetitive character loops, and if so, how does the word-level decoder's state reset help mitigate this?
