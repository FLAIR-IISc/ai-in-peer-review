This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent transcripts as networks and leverage topological properties for classification, aiming to overcome the challenges posed by disfluencies common in patient speech. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of this network-based approach, outperforming traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets. A secondary contribution is the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to further enhance classification accuracy. Finally, the provision of datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) for evaluating MCI detection methods is a valuable resource.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of complex network analysis, coupled with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts is a relatively novel approach. Existing work frequently relies on parsed linguistic features, which are susceptible to errors caused by disfluencies. This method offers a potentially more robust alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates that the proposed complex network features outperform traditional linguistic features across three datasets, including both English and Portuguese. The improvements are statistically significant and suggest a genuine advantage of this approach. The 80% accuracy achieved on the Arizona dataset is particularly encouraging.
3. **Dataset Diversity:** The evaluation of the method on three different datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD – increases the generalizability and robustness of the findings. The inclusion of datasets in both English and Portuguese also broadens the potential impact of the work.
4. **Effective Combination Techniques:** The use of ensemble and multi-view learning to combine different feature sets (complex networks, linguistic features, bag-of-words) yields further improvements in accuracy, showcasing the benefit of integrating diverse information sources. The 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset through ensemble learning is a notable result.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Feature Selection:** While the paper lists ten topological measurements used to characterize the networks, it lacks a detailed justification for *why* these specific metrics were chosen. A more thorough discussion of the theoretical rationale behind their relevance to MCI detection would strengthen the work.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Word Embedding Implementation:** The description of the word embedding process is somewhat brief. Providing more specifics regarding the training data, embedding dimensions, and any hyperparameter tuning would enhance reproducibility and allow for a more informed assessment of its impact. The mention of using the method from Bojanowski et al. (2016) is good, but more context on the specific settings is needed.
3. **Potential for Dataset Bias:** The demographic information provided in the tables reveals potential imbalances in the datasets (e.g., differences in age and education levels between control and MCI groups). While these are inherent characteristics of real-world data, the potential influence of these biases on the results should be acknowledged and discussed.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art ASR-Based Methods:** The paper focuses on overcoming the limitations of parsing transcripts with disfluencies. However, it would be valuable to compare the performance of this method against more recent state-of-the-art Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems, which are increasingly robust to disfluencies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific ten topological features used to characterize the complex networks? Are there any theoretical considerations or prior work that guided this selection?
2. Please provide more details regarding the word embedding process, including the training data used, the dimensionality of the embeddings, and any hyperparameter tuning performed.
3. How might the demographic imbalances observed in the datasets (e.g., age and education level differences) potentially affect the results? Have you considered any methods to mitigate the impact of these biases?
4. Have you considered comparing your method to recent ASR-based approaches for MCI detection? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?



**Additional Comments:**

* The supplementary material is helpful in providing additional details about the datasets and features used.
* The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations.
* Consider exploring the use of attention mechanisms within the network to highlight the most relevant parts of the transcripts.
* Investigating the transferability of the trained models across different datasets and languages would be a valuable direction for future research.
