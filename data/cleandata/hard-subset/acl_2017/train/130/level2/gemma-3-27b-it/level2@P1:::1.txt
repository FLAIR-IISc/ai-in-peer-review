This paper explores the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors demonstrate that this approach can outperform traditional linguistic features across three datasets – one English dataset from DementiaBank and two Portuguese datasets collected at the University of São Paulo. A key contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of complex networks in this context, especially given the challenges of noisy, disfluent speech commonly found in patient assessments. Furthermore, the authors show improvements through ensemble and multi-view learning techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks:**  Applying complex network analysis to speech transcripts for MCI detection is a relatively novel approach. The use of word adjacency networks, combined with word embeddings to capture semantic information, provides a potentially robust method for dealing with the disfluencies common in patient speech that can hinder traditional NLP methods relying on parsing. This is particularly valuable in a clinical context where manual transcript correction is impractical.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets – differing in language and collection context – strengthens the findings.  Demonstrating consistent performance gains, or at least competitive performance, across these diverse datasets increases confidence in the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of Portuguese datasets is particularly important, as NLP resources for Portuguese are less abundant than those for English.
3. **Outperformance of Linguistic Features:** The consistent outperformance of complex network features compared to traditional linguistic features is a significant finding. This supports the hypothesis that network-based representations are more resilient to the noise and agrammaticality present in the speech of individuals with MCI.
4. **Effective Combination Strategies:** The successful use of ensemble and multi-view learning to further improve performance indicates that combining different feature sets and classification algorithms is a promising direction for future research. The 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and the 4.71% improvement on the Arizona dataset are substantial.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Network Topology Metrics:** While the paper lists ten topological measurements used to characterize the networks, it lacks a detailed explanation of *why* these specific metrics are expected to be informative for differentiating between MCI patients and controls.  A more thorough discussion of the relationship between these metrics and cognitive impairment would strengthen the paper. Which metrics are most discriminative and what cognitive processes might they reflect?
2. **Embedding Details and Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions using pre-trained word embeddings but provides limited details about the specific model used (beyond mentioning fastText-like character n-gram embeddings) and its training data. The impact of different embedding models, or fine-tuning embeddings on the specific task data, is not explored. Further, the sensitivity of the results to the cosine similarity threshold used for enriching the network with semantic links isn't adequately addressed.
3. **Dataset Imbalance and Size:** The datasets are relatively small, and in some cases (Cinderella), there's a noticeable imbalance between the number of control and MCI participants.  While 5-fold cross-validation is used, small sample sizes can lead to overfitting and unstable results. A larger, more balanced dataset would be needed to confirm these findings and improve statistical power. The demographics show significant age differences between control groups in different datasets, potentially impacting results.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper compares its approach to previous work but doesn’t directly compare against very recent and relevant studies that may employ more advanced machine learning techniques (e.g., deep learning models, transformers) on similar tasks.  Positioning the results within the current state-of-the-art would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific network topology metrics used? Which of these metrics do you believe are most indicative of cognitive impairment, and why?
2. What are the specific details of the word embedding model used (e.g., training corpus, dimensionality)? How sensitive are the results to different embedding models or to fine-tuning the embeddings on the task-specific data?
3.  How was the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the network determined? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis to evaluate the impact of different thresholds on the classification accuracy?
4.  Could you discuss the potential impact of the demographic differences between the control groups in the different datasets on the results?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear, but some sentences are a bit convoluted. A careful proofread for clarity would be beneficial.
* The supplementary material provides helpful details, but including a table summarizing the key demographic characteristics of all datasets in the main paper would be helpful.
* It would be valuable to include error analysis: are there specific types of errors the model makes consistently? This might provide insights for improving the approach.
