- Core idea: represent speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI.  
- Datasets/evaluation: tested on DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery (English and Portuguese) with multiple classifiers and ensembles.  
- Main contribution: explores network-based, potentially language-independent features and embedding enrichment to better represent short/noisy texts.  
- Empirical finding: network+embedding approach improves performance, notably +10% on Cinderella and +4.71% on Arizona with multi-view learning.  
- Novelty: applying complex networks with embeddings to MCI detection is a novel alternative to traditional linguistic features.  
- Generalizability: evaluation across three datasets and comparison to linguistic baselines strengthens claims.  
- Real-world relevance: tackles scalable automated early dementia diagnosis from noisy patient speech.  
- Weakness — network construction detail: missing specifics (e.g., co-occurrence window, embedding similarity threshold) and rationale, harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness — class imbalance: datasets are imbalanced (e.g., 43 MCI vs. 326 AD) and the paper does not clearly address mitigation strategies.  
- Weakness — feature importance: lacks analysis of which network/topological features drive classification.  
- Weakness — statistical testing: reported improvements often lack statistical significance analysis.  
- Questions for authors: justify network parameter choices (window size, thresholds).  
- Questions for authors: explain how data imbalance was handled and its impact on results.  
- Questions for authors: provide feature importance analysis highlighting predictive network features.  
- Recommendation: promising work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses above.
