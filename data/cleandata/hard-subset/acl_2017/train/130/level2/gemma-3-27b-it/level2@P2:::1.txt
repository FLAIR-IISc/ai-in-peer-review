This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose modeling transcripts as networks and leveraging topological features for classification, aiming to overcome the challenges posed by disfluencies common in patient speech. The work is evaluated on three datasets (English and Portuguese) and demonstrates that the proposed approach outperforms linguistic features alone and benefits from ensemble and multi-view learning techniques.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The core contribution lies in applying complex network analysis, enhanced with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to this specific problem – dealing with potentially noisy transcripts from cognitive assessments – is a valuable exploration.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD battery) in two languages (English and Portuguese) strengthens the generalizability of the findings. This is particularly noteworthy as resources for Portuguese are comparatively limited.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement through Ensemble/Multi-View Learning:** The results convincingly show that combining different feature types (complex networks, linguistic features, BoW) using ensemble and multi-view learning yields significant improvements in classification accuracy, especially on the Cinderella and ABCD datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Practical Problem:** The paper tackles a real-world problem: the need for automated, robust MCI detection methods that are less sensitive to the noise inherent in spontaneous speech transcripts. This is directly relevant to clinical applications.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, often outperforming baseline methods. The reported accuracy improvements (up to 10% on Cinderella, 4.71% on Arizona) are substantial and suggest a practical benefit.
3. **Clear Methodology & Thorough Experiments:** The paper provides a clear description of the methodology, including network construction, feature extraction, and classification algorithms. The extensive experimentation with different feature combinations and datasets provides confidence in the robustness of the findings.
4. **Consideration of Language-Specific Challenges:** The authors acknowledge the challenges associated with linguistic analysis in Portuguese and the limitations of existing parsers, motivating their choice of a more language-independent approach (complex networks).
5. **Comparison to Relevant Literature:** The related work section appropriately positions the paper within the existing literature on MCI detection and the use of complex networks in NLP.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Network Approach Itself:** While the *application* to MCI detection is novel, the use of complex networks for text analysis is not. The core idea of representing text as a network and extracting topological features has been explored in previous work. The contribution here is more in the successful adaptation and application of this existing technique to a new, challenging domain.
2. **Lack of Detailed Word Embedding Analysis:** The paper mentions using word embeddings to enrich the networks but lacks a detailed analysis of *how* these embeddings contribute to the improved performance. It would be beneficial to explore which semantic relationships captured by the embeddings are most relevant for distinguishing between MCI and control subjects.
3. **Dataset Imbalance:** The dataset sizes are relatively small, and there's a potential for imbalance between the MCI and control groups in some datasets (although the tables show reasonably balanced splits). While 5-fold cross-validation helps mitigate this, a more detailed discussion of potential biases due to dataset imbalance would be valuable.
4. **Black Box Nature of Ensemble/Multi-View Learning:** While the ensemble/multi-view learning improves performance, the paper doesn’t provide much insight into *why* these combinations work well. Understanding which feature combinations are most effective and how they complement each other would enhance the interpretability of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific choices made in enriching the networks with word embeddings (e.g., the cosine similarity threshold)? Were different thresholds explored, and what was the impact on performance?
2. What specific linguistic features were most correlated with MCI status in the datasets, and how did these features interact with the complex network features in the multi-view learning approach?
3. Given the relatively small dataset sizes, were any techniques used to address potential overfitting, such as regularization or data augmentation?
4. Could you provide a qualitative example of how the complex network representation captures differences in speech patterns between MCI patients and healthy controls?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are a bit small and could be made larger for better readability.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key characteristics of the three datasets (e.g., number of participants, average transcript length, type of narrative).
*   The notation in Table 4-7 is a bit confusing. Perhaps clarify what "CNE-LM-BoW" means in the table caption. (Combination of Complex Networks Enriched with embeddings, Linguistic Metrics and Bag of Words).



Overall, this is a well-written and thoroughly researched paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of MCI detection. The proposed approach shows promising results and has the potential to be a useful tool for clinicians. While some aspects of the methodology could be further explored, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
