## Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enhanced with word embeddings, for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent transcripts as networks and leverage these network structures, alongside other features, for classification. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery – utilizing several classification algorithms and ensemble methods.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the exploration of complex network features as a potentially robust and language-independent method for MCI detection, particularly beneficial when dealing with the disfluencies inherent in patient speech. The enrichment of these networks with word embeddings adds a semantic layer, improving representation of short texts. The empirical evaluation on multiple datasets, including a comparative analysis against linguistic features, provides valuable insight into the effectiveness of the proposed method. Finally, the utilization of ensemble and multi-view learning strategies further refines performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** Applying complex networks, specifically enhanced with word embeddings, to MCI detection from speech transcripts is a novel contribution. This offers a potential alternative to traditional linguistic feature-based approaches that struggle with noisy data.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation across three distinct datasets (English and Portuguese) strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The comparison against baseline linguistic features provides a clear benchmark for performance.
*   **Performance Improvement:** The reported improvements, particularly the 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and 4.71% on the Arizona dataset with multi-view learning, are significant and indicate a promising approach.
*   **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** The study tackles a crucial problem in early dementia diagnosis, with a focus on a scalable and automated solution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Detail on Network Construction:** While the methodology is outlined, the specifics of the network construction (e.g., window size for co-occurrence, threshold for embedding similarity) could be more detailed to facilitate reproducibility. The justification for these parameter choices isn't discussed.
*   **Data Imbalance:** The dataset sizes (e.g. 43 MCI vs. 326 AD in Cookie Theft dataset) are imbalanced, potentially influencing the classification results.  The paper doesn’t explicitly address or mitigate this issue (e.g., through class weighting or resampling techniques).
*   **Feature Importance Analysis:** While the study demonstrates overall performance, it lacks a detailed analysis of which network features contribute most to the classification accuracy. Identifying key topological measures would provide valuable insights.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The reported improvements are not always accompanied by statistical significance testing. Establishing the statistical significance of the observed performance differences would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed rationale for the chosen parameters used in the network construction process (window size, similarity threshold)?
2.  How did you address the issue of data imbalance in the datasets? If not, what impact might this have on the reported results?
3.  Can you provide an analysis of feature importance, highlighting the network features most predictive of MCI?



Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to MCI detection. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the work and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
