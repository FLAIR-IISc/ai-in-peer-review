Summary: The paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). Using three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft in English; Cinderella and ABCD in Brazilian Portuguese), the authors extract topological network features, compare against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix variants) and Bag-of-Words, and explore ensembles and multi-view learning. Reported 5-fold CV accuracies show enriched networks often outperform linguistic features; the best results arise from multi-view combinations (up to 80% on ABCD).

Contributions (as I see them):
- Demonstration that embedding-enriched complex network features provide competitive and sometimes superior performance to standard linguistic metrics for MCI detection across English and Portuguese datasets.
- Empirical evidence that multi-view/ensemble combinations of network, linguistic, and BoW features yield additional gains over single-view models.
- A lightweight, parser-independent pipeline that tolerates disfluencies and minimizes manual preprocessing, making it attractive for scalable screening.

Strengths:
- Cross-lingual validation on three datasets (two languages, varying task types and transcript lengths) strengthens claims of generality and practical relevance.
- Clear, replicable feature design for complex networks (named topological measures; mean/SD/skewness aggregation) and a sensible embedding-based enrichment that addresses short-text sparsity.
- Consistent empirical advantage of CNE over CN and over linguistic metrics in most settings; multi-view improvements are substantial on two datasets (e.g., +10% on Cinderella).
- Focus on minimally supervised preprocessing (no lemmatization/parsing; handling disfluencies) addresses a real deployment bottleneck in clinical NLP.

Weaknesses:
- Potential subject leakage in ABCD: each participant provides two narratives; the paper does not state if folds were split by subject. If not, reported accuracies may be inflated.
- Strong demographic confounds are likely, especially in ABCD (Controls avg age 61 vs MCI 72); no covariate control, matched sampling, or stratified analysis is reported.
- Evaluation reporting is limited to accuracy; no per-class precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, calibration, or statistical significance testing. Class imbalance handling and variance estimates are missing.
- Reproducibility gaps: unspecified corpus used to train embeddings, threshold selection not nested within CV, no code or feature lists for Portuguese metrics beyond category names.
- The “language independent” claim is somewhat overstated; results depend on language-specific embeddings and Coh-Metrix variants, and BoW SVM performs competitively in places.

Questions to Authors:
- Was cross-validation stratified by subject, especially for ABCD’s two retells? If not, can you re-run with subject-wise splits?
- How were embeddings trained (corpus, size, language(s))? Were they trained on the task data, potentially causing leakage?
- Were similarity thresholds and classifier hyperparameters tuned within nested CV? Any significance tests for improvements?
- Can you report per-class metrics and control for age/education (e.g., matched subsets or regression-adjusted evaluation)?
- How robust is the approach to ASR noise compared to text transcripts?
