Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them by adding edges between semantically similar words using word embeddings. It then characterizes each transcript via topological network measures and uses standard classifiers to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). Experiments on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD) compare complex-network features (with and without embedding enrichment) to Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic metrics and Bag-of-Words (BoW), and explore ensemble and multi-view learning.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A lightweight, parser-free representation for MCI detection based on complex-network features augmented with word embeddings, applied to noisy, short neuropsychological transcripts in two languages.
2) An empirical study across three datasets showing that enriched network features are competitive with, and often outperform, linguistic metrics and BoW; and that multi-view/ensemble combinations further improve accuracy.
3) A practical insight that embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks improve the informativeness of networks built from short, disfluent narratives.

Strengths
- Language- and tool-agnostic approach: The network-based representation does not rely on parsing or heavy annotation, making it attractive for noisy clinical transcripts and for languages with limited NLP resources. The cross-language evaluation (English and Brazilian Portuguese) supports this claim.

- Embedding enrichment for short texts: Adding similarity edges addresses the sparsity/linearity of short-text networks. The empirical trend that CNE > CN in most settings is consistent and persuasive, and the approach is simple to implement.

- Multi-dataset evaluation with clinically relevant tasks: The study spans description (Cookie Theft), story retelling (ABCD), and picture-book narrative (Cinderella), which differ in length and structure. Reporting results on both raw and manually revised transcripts is valuable for understanding the dependence on clean parsing.

- Competitive results with simple baselines: On ABCD, multi-view fusion achieves 80% accuracy and linear SVM with BoW reaches 75%, showing that the task is non-trivial and that the proposed features are meaningful. The finding that CNE often outperforms Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) without manual preprocessing is practically important.

- Clear feature engineering and measures: The set of topological measurements (centralities, clustering, assortativity, path lengths) and aggregation statistics are well-motivated and standard, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and evaluation bias: In ABCD, each subject produces two narratives (immediate and delayed). The 5-fold cross-validation appears to be done at the narrative level, which risks having narratives from the same subject in both train and test folds, inflating performance. The Cookie Theft dataset may include multiple sessions per participant in DementiaBank; this risk is not addressed. Threshold tuning for similarity edges (“optimized with best values 0.7/0.4”) is described without a nested validation protocol, risking overfitting. No statistical significance tests are reported.

- Confounds and lack of control for demographics: In ABCD, groups differ markedly in age and education (e.g., control avg age 61 vs MCI 72). Without matching or controlling for covariates, classifiers may learn proxies for age/education rather than cognitive status. This is a serious concern for clinical validity.

- Limited baselines and metrics: BoW uses term frequency only; stronger text baselines such as tf-idf, word/character n-grams, or simple embedding-based document vectors (e.g., averaged word embeddings) are not evaluated. Only accuracy is reported; class-imbalance-aware metrics (F1, AUC, sensitivity/specificity) are crucial in clinical screening. Hyperparameter search and model selection procedures are not detailed.

- Ambiguity in embedding training and graph construction: It is unclear what corpora were used to train embeddings (trained on the small datasets vs large external corpora). Edges added via cosine thresholding appear unweighted; discarding similarity magnitudes may lose information. No sensitivity analysis is provided for the threshold or for the number of added edges.

- Dataset description and comparability: Cookie Theft sampling is unclear (e.g., “43 transcripts of MCI sampled from 326 probable AD; 21 possible AD”), and the control cohort size/selection is not fully specified here. Given prior work on DementiaBank, comparability would benefit from using established splits or clarifying inclusion/exclusion and session handling.

Questions to Authors
1) Data splitting: Did you enforce subject-level separation across folds for all datasets (especially ABCD, where each subject has two narratives)? If not, can you re-run with subject-level CV and report results?

2) Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold tuned (inner CV, held-out dev set)? Please report sensitivity curves and whether the same threshold was applied across folds.

3) Embeddings: What corpora were used to train fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese? Approximately how large were they? Did you try pre-trained fastText embeddings, and how did performance change?

4) Graph definition: Are semantic edges unweighted? Did you try keeping cosine similarities as weights or limiting the number of nearest neighbors per node (k-NN) instead of a global threshold?

5) Baselines and metrics: Can you add tf-idf, character n-grams, and averaged word embedding baselines, and report F1, precision/recall, AUC, and confusion matrices? Also provide statistical significance tests for key comparisons.

6) Confound control: Given age/education differences in ABCD, did you match groups or include demographics as covariates? If available, please report results on matched subsets or with regression control.

7) Reproducibility: Will you release code, exact preprocessing scripts, feature extraction parameters, and data splits (subject-level) to facilitate replication?

Constructive suggestions
- Use nested, subject-level cross-validation; report multiple metrics with confidence intervals and significance tests.
- Include stronger text baselines (tf-idf, n-grams, averaged embeddings) and ablations (CN vs CNE, with/without stopwords; weighted vs unweighted edges).
- Control for demographics or report matched analyses; consider adding demographics as a separate view in multi-view learning.
- Provide threshold and hyperparameter sensitivity analyses; explore weighted graphs or k-NN graphs for semantic edges.
- Clarify dataset composition and session handling; if possible, align with standard DementiaBank splits.
