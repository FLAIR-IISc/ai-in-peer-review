Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with semantic links derived from word embeddings (CNE) to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). Nodes are words; edges are adjacency plus similarity-above-threshold links. Topological metrics from these networks are used as features.
- The approach is evaluated against linguistic (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) features and bag-of-words (BoW) across three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD), using several classifiers, ensembles, and multi-view combinations.
- Claimed contributions (as I see them): (1) An empirical demonstration that complex network features, enriched with embeddings, can be competitive for MCI detection and sometimes outperform linguistic features, with cross-language evidence; (2) A simple multi-view/ensemble setup that improves performance in some settings; (3) A lightweight representation arguably more robust than parser-dependent features on disfluent transcripts.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual evaluation on three datasets with different elicitation protocols (picture description, story retelling, immediate/delayed recall) increases external validity. The study goes beyond DementiaBank by including two Portuguese datasets.
- The embedding-enriched network modeling addresses a known weakness of network-based features on short texts; the idea is well-motivated by prior work (Perozzi et al.) and empirically helpful (CNE > CN on most settings).
- Multi-view/ensemble combinations yield non-trivial gains in two datasets (e.g., Cinderella +10% over best individual; ABCD +4.7%), suggesting complementarity among feature families.
- The approach is relatively lightweight and language-agnostic compared to parser-heavy pipelines, aligning with the practical goal of scalable screening without manual transcript revision.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and confounds: In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate/delayed). The paper does not state that cross-validation was performed with subject-level splits, risking the same subject in training and test folds. Moreover, large demographic imbalances exist (e.g., ABCD age: control 61 vs. MCI 72; sex distribution differs), which can strongly influence language production. Without matching or controlling for age/education/sex, improvements may reflect demographic differences rather than cognitive status.
- Evaluation methodology: Only accuracy is reported; no variance across folds or statistical significance testing. Several improvements are small and may not be significant. Given class-size differences and small datasets, reporting precision/recall/F1, AUC, confidence intervals, and permutation tests would strengthen claims.
- Inconsistencies with claims: The abstract and conclusions state that complex networks outperform linguistic features “in all datasets,” but Table 6 (manually revised Cinderella) shows LM (SVM-RBF 0.725) outperforming CNE (0.675). In ABCD, BoW (SVM-linear 0.7547) beats CNE individually (0.7173). The conclusions should be tempered to reflect dataset-specific outcomes.
- Insufficient detail on embedding training and hyperparameter tuning: The corpus used to train embeddings is unspecified (only default parameters are given). If trained on the small task transcripts, embeddings may be unstable; if trained on larger corpora (e.g., Wikipedia), domain mismatch may occur. Thresholds for semantic links (0.7/0.4) are “optimized,” but no nested CV or dev set is described, risking overfitting.
- Dataset description clarity: The Cookie Theft section is confusing (e.g., “We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD.”). Control group size and demographics (Table 1) are not shown; class balance is unclear. This hinders interpretability and reproducibility.
- Limited analysis/ablation: While CNE helps, there is no ablation showing the incremental effect of similarity links vs. adjacency alone, sensitivity to the similarity threshold, or which topological metrics are most predictive. Error analyses (e.g., which narratives are misclassified) are missing.

Questions to authors
- How were cross-validation splits constructed, especially for ABCD? Were folds stratified by subject to prevent the same participant appearing in both train and test?
- What corpora were used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Did you use pre-trained fastText or train on-domain data? How sensitive are results to the embedding source and dimensionality?
- How was the similarity threshold selected? Was selection done on a separate development set or via nested CV? Please clarify to rule out overfitting.
- Can you report class counts per dataset/fold, and provide precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals? Are improvements significant (e.g., McNemar’s test)?
- Did you control for age, education, and sex, or assess whether models are leveraging demographic correlates? Have you tried adding demographics as covariates or balancing the cohorts?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Reframe claims to reflect that CNE is competitive and often superior, but not universally better than LM/BoW across all datasets and preprocessing conditions.
- Provide a subject-level CV protocol and re-run ABCD results accordingly. If demographics are imbalanced, either match groups, include demographics as features with partial effects analysis, or report subgroup analyses to ensure the model captures linguistic markers, not demographic proxies.
- Report standard deviations across folds and significance tests. Include additional metrics beyond accuracy.
- Add ablations: (a) CN vs. CNE vs. “embedding-only” graph (only similarity edges); (b) threshold sensitivity curve; (c) feature importance over network metrics.
- Strengthen the multi-view fusion: instead of majority vote, try calibrated probability averaging or stacking with a held-out validation set to learn weights.
- Clarify preprocessing choices and their impact: which disfluencies are removed/kept, effects on LM and CN features, and whether performance holds with more realistic ASR transcripts (noise, segmentation errors).
- Improve dataset descriptions (complete tables, counts, controls vs. MCI, narrative lengths) and release code and configuration details (stopword lists, graph construction, thresholds) to enhance reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a promising, relatively language-agnostic approach with encouraging cross-dataset results, but methodological clarifications and stronger controls/analyses are needed to substantiate the central claims.
