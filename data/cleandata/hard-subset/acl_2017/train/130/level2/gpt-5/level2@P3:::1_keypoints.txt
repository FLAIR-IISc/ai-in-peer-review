- Proposes a lightweight, parser-free MCI detection method using word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-similarity edges and characterized by topological features.
- Evaluates bilingually on three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) comparing enriched complex-network features (CNE) to linguistic metrics and bag-of-words, and explores ensemble/multi-view fusion.
- Reports CNE consistently outperforms linguistic metrics and that late fusion/ensembles further improve accuracy (up to 80.2% on ABCD; 10-point gain on Cinderella).

- Motivation/practicality: avoids parser brittleness on disfluent clinical transcripts and offers a language-portable pipeline needing only tokenization, stopword removal, and embeddings.
- Methodological novelty: adapts embedding-similarity edges for short/disfluent texts to address near-linear networks; CNE outperforms plain CN across datasets.
- Thorough evaluation: cross-dataset, cross-language tests with multiple classifiers (SVM/KNN/RF/NB) and baselines (LM, BoW), showing CNE often superior.
- Simple fusion helps: majority-vote ensemble/multi-view yields tangible accuracy gains over individual feature sets.
- Low resource requirements: only embeddings and basic preprocessing are needed, suiting low-resource languages and clinical settings.

- ABCD confounds: demographic differences (age, education, sex) may drive classification; no demographic-controlled analyses are reported.
- Possible subject leakage: ABCD has immediate/delayed retells and CV may not be subject-independent, risking inflated performance.
- Hyperparameter/threshold selection may leak: similarity thresholds described as “optimized” with no clarity on nested CV or tuning protocol.
- Limited metrics and uncertainty: only accuracy reported, with no sensitivity/specificity, ROC-AUC, macro-F1, confidence intervals, or statistical tests.
- Embedding reproduction issues: training corpora, coverage, and release of embeddings/models/code are unspecified despite centrality to the method.
- Length sensitivity: many network metrics scale with text length and no analysis regresses out or controls for narrative length differences.
- Language-independence claim overstated: method still requires language-specific embeddings and stopword lists; claim should be reframed as language-portable.

- Were CV folds subject-independent for all datasets, especially ABCD with immediate/delayed retells?
- How were embedding-similarity thresholds chosen and was nested CV used for threshold/hyperparameter selection?
- What corpora/tokenization were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings, and will vectors be released?
- Are CN features normalized for network size or can you report correlations with length and regression analyses controlling for length?
- How do results change when adding demographics as features or when matching groups on age/education/sex?
- Can you report sensitivity/specificity, macro-F1, confidence intervals, and significance tests for key comparisons?

- Essential revisions: ensure subject-independent CV; provide demographics-controlled analyses or baselines; use nested CV for tuning; report additional metrics with CIs and statistical tests; specify and share embedding resources and code.
- Nice-to-have: error and feature-importance analysis; robustness to ASR errors or noisy transcripts; simple length-only and demographics-only baselines; cross-dataset generalization tests to support portability.
- Minor fixes: clarify figure numbering, correct typos (“vertice,” “trainning,” “agramatical”), and standardize table captions/labels.
