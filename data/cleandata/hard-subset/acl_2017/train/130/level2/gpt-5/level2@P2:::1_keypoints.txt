- Proposal: represent transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with semantic links from embeddings (CNE) and use network topological metrics to detect MCI.
- Evaluation: compared CNE to linguistic (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and BoW across three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, Portuguese ABCD) using multiple classifiers and ensembles.
- Claimed contributions: (1) CNE can be competitive or superior for MCI detection, (2) a simple multi-view/ensemble improves performance in some settings, (3) a lightweight, parser-free representation more robust on disfluent transcripts.
- Strength — cross-lingual/external validity: evaluation on three datasets with different elicitation protocols strengthens generalizability beyond DementiaBank.
- Strength — embedding enrichment: adding semantic links addresses short-text sparsity and empirically improves CN (CNE > CN) in most settings.
- Strength — multi-view gains: ensemble combinations produce notable improvements on some datasets (e.g., +10% Cinderella, +4.7% ABCD).
- Strength — practicality: approach is relatively lightweight and language-agnostic compared to parser-dependent pipelines.
- Weakness — potential data leakage/confounds: ABCD may have subject-level leakage (two narratives per subject) and large demographic imbalances (age/sex) that could drive results.
- Weakness — evaluation reporting: only accuracy reported, no fold variance, CIs, or statistical significance tests; important metrics like precision/recall/F1 and AUC missing.
- Weakness — overstated claims: paper claims CNE outperforms linguistic features “in all datasets,” but tables show dataset-specific counterexamples (e.g., Cinderella, ABCD).
- Weakness — embedding/hyperparameter detail lacking: corpus/source for embeddings unspecified, threshold choices unexplained, and tuning procedure (nested CV vs dev set) unclear.
- Weakness — dataset description unclear: Cookie Theft and control group demographics/balances are confusing or missing, hindering interpretability.
- Weakness — limited analysis/ablation: no ablation of adjacency vs similarity links, threshold sensitivity, per-metric importance, or error analysis of misclassifications.
- Question — cross-validation: were CV splits constructed at subject level (esp. ABCD) to avoid same subject in train/test?
- Question — embeddings: which corpora or pretrained models were used for English/Portuguese embeddings and how sensitive are results to those choices?
- Question — threshold selection: how were similarity thresholds chosen and was selection done with nested CV or a separate dev set to avoid overfitting?
- Question — metrics and significance: can you report class counts, precision/recall/F1, confidence intervals, and statistical tests (e.g., McNemar, permutation)?
- Question — demographic controls: were age/education/sex controlled for or included as covariates, and have you tested whether models exploit demographic correlates?
- Suggestion — temper claims: rephrase conclusions to state CNE is competitive and often superior, but not universally better across datasets and conditions.
- Suggestion — subject-level CV and demographic handling: rerun ABCD with subject-level splits and either match/control demographics or include them as covariates/subgroup analyses.
- Suggestion — fuller evaluation: report std devs, additional metrics, and statistical significance; add ablations for CN vs CNE vs embedding-only and threshold sensitivity.
- Suggestion — better fusion and preprocessing analysis: try calibrated averaging or stacking for multi-view fusion; clarify preprocessing/disfluency handling and test robustness to ASR noise.
- Suggestion — reproducibility: improve dataset descriptions, provide full counts and narrative stats, and release code, stopword lists, graph construction details, and thresholds.
- Overall: promising, language-agnostic approach with encouraging results, but needs methodological clarifications, stronger controls, and deeper analyses to substantiate central claims.
