Summary and contributions
- Proposes a lightweight, parser-free approach for MCI detection from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges, and characterizing them via topological features.
- Provides a bilingual evaluation across three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft in English; Cinderella and ABCD in Brazilian Portuguese), comparing complex-network features against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words, and exploring ensemble and multi-view fusion.
- Reports that enriched complex-network features consistently outperform linguistic metrics across datasets and that late fusion yields further gains, reaching up to 80.2% accuracy on ABCD and a 10-point improvement on Cinderella via ensemble/multi-view.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practicality: The approach addresses known brittleness of parser-dependent features on disfluent clinical transcripts, offering a language-portable pipeline (tokenization, stopword removal, embeddings) and avoiding costly manual clean-up. This directly targets a genuine deployment barrier highlighted in prior Portuguese work.
- Methodological contribution via network enrichment: Adapts the idea of adding embedding-similarity edges to co-occurrence networks for short/disfluent texts, which is well-justified given prior observations that short texts yield near-linear networks. The empirical results show enriched CN (CNE) outperform plain CN across datasets (e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF: 0.626 vs 0.569; Cinderella SVM-RBF: 0.650 vs 0.575).
- Cross-dataset, cross-language evaluation with multiple feature families and classifiers: The study covers three datasets of varying length and elicitation protocols, reports results across SVM/KNN/RF/NB, and includes both LM and BoW baselines. CNE consistently surpasses LM as a standalone feature set (Cookie Theft: 0.626 vs 0.581; Cinderella: 0.650 vs 0.475; ABCD: 0.717 vs 0.682 with SVM-Linear/RBF where applicable).
- Fusion helps and is simple: Majority-vote multi-view/ensemble improves accuracy (e.g., Cinderella ensemble CNE-LM: 0.75 vs best individual 0.65; ABCD CNE-LM-BoW SVM-Linear: 0.802 vs best individual 0.755 BoW). Even a simple late fusion yields tangible gains, increasing practical utility.
- Minimal resource requirements: The pipeline needs only embeddings and basic preprocessing, making it attractive for languages with limited robust parsers and for clinics without annotation capacity.

Weaknesses
- Potential confounds in ABCD are not controlled: The ABCD demographics differ substantially between classes (Control age 61 vs MCI 72; education 16 vs 13.3; sex ratio differs). Without controlling or reporting results conditioned on demographics, classification may capture age/education proxies rather than cognitive status. A baseline using demographics alone, matched subsampling, or covariate control would strengthen claims.
- Possible subject leakage in cross-validation: ABCD contains two narratives per MCI subject (immediate/delayed). The paper states 5-fold CV at narrative level but does not state whether folds are subject-independent. If narratives from the same subject appear in train and test folds, performance can be inflated. This is critical to clarify and, if needed, to redo with grouped CV.
- Model/threshold selection protocol may leak information: The similarity threshold (0.7 Cookie Theft; 0.4 Cinderella/ABCD) is described as “optimized” but it is unclear whether this was done within a nested CV or on the full dataset. Similarly, no details on other hyperparameters and selection criteria are given. Without nested tuning, reported accuracies risk optimistic bias.
- Limited evaluation metrics and uncertainty estimates: Only accuracy is reported. Clinical detection tasks benefit from sensitivity/specificity, macro-F1, ROC-AUC, and confidence intervals. No per-fold variance or statistical significance testing (e.g., McNemar, bootstrap) is provided, making it difficult to assess robustness, especially given small sample sizes (e.g., Cinderella 20/20).
- Embedding training data and reproducibility: The work states training fastText-style embeddings with default parameters but does not specify the training corpora for English/Portuguese, vocabulary coverage, or whether external corpora were used. Given that embeddings are central to the method and claimed language portability, these details (and ideally released models/code) are essential for replication and fair comparison.
- Length sensitivity and normalization: Many network metrics (degree, diameter, path length, clustering) vary with network size. Narrative lengths differ across groups and datasets; LM includes basic counts but CN features may still be driven by size. No analysis regressing out length or including number of nodes/edges as covariates is provided. This risks attributing gains to length effects rather than topology/semantics.
- Claims of language independence vs practice: The approach reduces language-specific dependencies but still requires language-specific embeddings and stopword lists. The claim would be better framed as language-portable and supported by cross-task or cross-lingual transfer experiments.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Were cross-validation folds strictly subject-independent in all datasets, especially ABCD with immediate/delayed retells? If not, can results be recomputed with grouped CV?
- How were the embedding-similarity thresholds chosen? Was nested CV used? Were any other hyperparameters tuned similarly?
- What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese (size, domain, tokenization)? Will the vectors be released?
- Are CN features normalized for network size (e.g., controlling for number of nodes/edges), or can you report correlations with length and an analysis regressing out length?
- How do results change when adding demographics (age, education, sex) as features, or when matching groups on these variables?
- Can sensitivity/specificity, macro-F1, and confidence intervals be reported, and significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., CNE vs LM, fusion vs best single)?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Essential revisions: (a) Ensure subject-independent CV; (b) provide demographics-controlled analyses or baselines; (c) use nested CV for threshold/hyperparameters; (d) report additional metrics with CIs and significance; (e) specify and share embedding resources and code.
- Nice-to-have: Error and feature-importance analysis (e.g., which CN metrics matter?); robustness to ASR errors by testing on actual ASR outputs or injecting noise; a simple length-only and demographics-only baseline; a cross-dataset generalization test to support portability claims.
- Minor: Clarify figure numbering (Figure 1 vs 2), fix typos (“vertice,” “trainning,” “agramatical”), and standardize table captions/labels.
