**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. They evaluate their model on three datasets, including one from the DementiaBank and two in Portuguese, and show that complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features in all datasets. The authors also explore the use of ensemble and multi-view learning to combine multiple classifiers and feature spaces, achieving improved accuracy.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Novel approach to MCI detection**: The paper proposes a new approach to detect MCI from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings, which is a departure from traditional linguistic feature-based methods.
2. **Improved accuracy**: The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms traditional linguistic features in all three datasets, with significant improvements in accuracy.
3. **Multi-view learning**: The paper explores the use of multi-view learning to combine multiple classifiers and feature spaces, which is a novel application in the context of MCI detection.

**Strengths**

1. **Innovative approach**: The paper proposes a novel approach to MCI detection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, including one from the DementiaBank and two in Portuguese, which provides a comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.
3. **Improved accuracy**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements in accuracy over traditional linguistic feature-based methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited datasets**: While the authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, the number of datasets is still limited, and more extensive evaluation is needed to fully understand the method's performance.
2. **Dependence on word embeddings**: The approach relies heavily on word embeddings, which may not always capture the nuances of language, particularly in the context of MCI.
3. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods in MCI detection, which makes it difficult to understand the method's performance in the broader context.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words, which is a common problem in word embedding-based approaches?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the optimization process for the threshold parameter in the ensemble and multi-view learning approaches?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other languages, given the limited availability of word embeddings for non-English languages?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a novel approach to MCI detection, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, more extensive evaluation is needed to fully understand the method's performance, and the authors should consider addressing the limitations mentioned above.
