## Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose modeling transcripts as networks and leveraging these network representations with machine learning classifiers. They evaluate their approach on three datasets – one English and two Portuguese – comparing performance against linguistic features and feature combinations.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that complex network features, particularly when enhanced with word embeddings, are a viable and competitive approach for MCI detection from speech, often outperforming traditional linguistic features. A secondary contribution lies in the successful application of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to further improve classification accuracy, achieving up to a 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and 4.71% on the Arizona dataset. Finally, the work provides a valuable comparative analysis of different feature sets and classifiers in the context of this specific task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application:** Applying complex network analysis to MCI detection from speech transcripts is a relatively novel approach, offering a potentially robust alternative to parsing-dependent linguistic features.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show that network-based features perform well, often exceeding the performance of linguistic features, particularly when combined with ensemble methods. The reported improvements on the datasets are significant.
*   **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the method on three datasets, including both English and Portuguese, strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Clear Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the methodology, including network construction, feature extraction, and classification techniques.
*   **Acknowledgement of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the challenges associated with transcript quality and the need for robust ASR systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Network Approach:** While the application to MCI is novel, the core idea of using complex networks for text analysis is not new. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this work builds upon existing network-based NLP techniques.
*   **Word Embedding Details:** The description of the word embedding process is somewhat brief. Providing more details about the specific embedding model used and its parameters would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Dataset Imbalance:** The datasets appear to have imbalanced class distributions (MCI vs. control). While not explicitly addressed, this could influence the results and should be acknowledged and potentially mitigated (e.g., through resampling techniques).
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** While accuracy improvements are reported, the statistical significance of these improvements is not consistently provided. Including p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing the specific word embedding model used and how its parameters were tuned?
2.  How did you address potential issues related to class imbalance in the datasets?
3.  Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for the reported accuracy improvements?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with constructing and analyzing the complex networks, particularly for larger datasets?



Overall, this is a promising paper with a solid methodology and encouraging results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and increase the impact of this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
