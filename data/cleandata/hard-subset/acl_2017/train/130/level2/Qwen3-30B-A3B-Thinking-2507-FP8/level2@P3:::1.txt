

Summary and Contributions: The paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The main contributions are: (1) using complex network features as a language-independent representation for MCI detection that avoids manual transcript correction; (2) demonstrating that network features outperform linguistic features across three datasets; and (3) showing that ensemble and multi-view learning approaches improve classification accuracy.

Strengths: The work addresses an important clinical problem with practical implications for early MCI detection. The approach to model transcripts as complex networks is well-motivated by the challenges of disfluencies in speech transcripts, which undermine traditional linguistic feature extraction. The evaluation across three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) provides evidence of the method's robustness across different languages (English and Portuguese) and narrative lengths. The multi-view learning approach combining complex networks with linguistic features and bag-of-words representations demonstrates consistent improvements (up to 10% in Cinderella dataset), suggesting a promising direction for feature integration. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the limitations of linguistic features when applied to disfluent speech, which is a significant practical barrier in real-world clinical applications.

Weaknesses: The paper's claim that complex networks outperform linguistic features lacks sufficient evidence. While the results show better performance for network features on the raw transcripts, the linguistic features performed better on the manually revised Cinderella dataset (Table 6), which contradicts the paper's assertion that linguistic features "depend on grammatical texts to present good results." This undermines the paper's main claim about the superiority of network features. The paper fails to provide a thorough analysis of why complex networks work better, particularly given the small size of the datasets (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft). The paper also does not adequately address the computational complexity of the network construction and embedding enrichment steps, which could be a significant barrier to clinical adoption. Finally, the comparison with prior work is limited, as the paper does not directly compare with other recent NLP approaches for MCI detection, making it difficult to assess the novelty and significance of the proposed method.

Questions to Authors: How does the paper address the potential issue of network construction being sensitive to the choice of embedding model and similarity threshold? The paper uses Bojanowski's method for word embeddings but does not discuss the sensitivity of results to this choice. What evidence supports the claim that complex networks are "language independent" when the approach relies on word embeddings trained on specific languages? The paper's evaluation is limited to English and Portuguese, and the method's applicability to other languages is not demonstrated. How does the approach scale to larger datasets, particularly considering the computational cost of network construction and enrichment with embeddings? The paper does not provide any analysis of time or resource requirements for the proposed method.

Additional Comments: The paper's claim that linguistic features require grammatical texts is weakened by the results in Table 6, where linguistic features performed better on the manually revised dataset. The paper should clarify this apparent contradiction. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the practical implementation of the method in clinical settings. The authors should also consider including a comparison with more recent deep learning approaches for MCI detection, such as those using attention mechanisms or transformer models, to better position their work within current NLP trends. The paper's method for handling disfluencies (removing only filled pauses but keeping other disfluencies) is not well justified and should be explained more thoroughly.
