

Summary: This paper proposes using complex network features enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, claiming this approach avoids the need for manual correction of disfluencies in transcripts. The main contributions are: (1) a novel method for modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings for MCI detection, (2) demonstration that complex network features outperform linguistic features across multiple datasets, and (3) successful application of ensemble and multi-view learning to improve performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant problem in healthcare: early detection of MCI, which is crucial for timely intervention. The authors correctly identify the limitation of traditional linguistic approaches (disfluencies impacting parsing results) and propose a promising alternative.
2. The approach is innovative in applying complex networks to this specific problem domain, and the evaluation across three datasets (English and Portuguese) demonstrates consistent performance improvements over linguistic features in most cases.
3. The paper provides a clear explanation of how the network enrichment with word embeddings addresses the short text problem identified in prior work, making the methodology well-motivated.

Weaknesses:
1. The claim that "complex networks outperform linguistic features in all datasets" is not fully supported. For instance, in the Cookie Theft dataset (Table 4), linguistic features (LM) outperform complex networks (CN) in the GaussianNB classifier (0.5694 vs 0.5125), contradicting the main claim.
2. The paper's claim of "avoiding manual preprocessing" is misleading. While the authors state they avoid manual correction of agrammatical sentences, their pipeline still involves rule-based disfluency removal (e.g., "we automatically removed filled pauses such as uh, um, er, and ah"), which is a form of manual rule-based processing.
3. The paper states "the ensemble of algorithms and multi-view method achieved 10% of improvement in Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier," but the actual improvement is 15.38% (0.7500 vs 0.6500), not 10% as claimed. This discrepancy undermines the credibility of the results.
4. The claim of "language independence" is questionable given the reliance on language-specific word embeddings, and the evaluation is limited to English and Portuguese without demonstrating cross-lingual capability.
5. The paper lacks statistical significance testing to support the observed performance improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are meaningful.

Questions to authors:
1. How do you explain the cases where linguistic features outperform complex networks (e.g., Cookie Theft dataset with GaussianNB), and how does this affect your main claim that complex networks outperform linguistic features?
2. Can you clarify the basis for claiming the method is "language independent" given the reliance on language-specific word embeddings and limited evaluation to only two languages?
