

Summary: The paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network representations enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as co-occurrence networks and enhance them with semantic information through word embeddings, then use topological features of these networks for classification. They evaluate their approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) and compare against linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. The main contributions are: (1) a language-independent method for MCI detection that avoids manual preprocessing of disfluent speech; (2) demonstration that enriched complex networks outperform linguistic features across all datasets; (3) evidence that multi-view learning combining network features with linguistic features further improves accuracy.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a clinically significant problem with practical implications for early MCI detection, which could enable large-scale screening without requiring specialized clinicians to analyze speech transcripts. The method's potential to avoid manual transcription correction is particularly valuable for real-world applications.
2. The approach of modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings is novel for this domain. The authors provide a clear technical description of how they enrich networks with semantic information through cosine similarity thresholds, which addresses a key limitation of traditional linguistic approaches when dealing with disfluent speech.
3. The evaluation across multiple datasets (including two Portuguese datasets) demonstrates the method's robustness and potential for cross-lingual application. The consistent outperformance of network features over linguistic features across all datasets is a strong empirical contribution.
4. The multi-view learning approach that combines network features with linguistic features shows meaningful improvements (up to 10% in Cinderella dataset), demonstrating the complementary nature of these feature spaces and providing a practical method for enhancing detection systems.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for why complex network features are particularly suitable for MCI detection. The connection between specific topological measures (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness) and cognitive impairment markers is not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand which features are most informative.
2. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is limited. The paper compares only against linguistic features and BoW, but there are several recent NLP approaches for MCI detection (e.g., deep learning models, transformer-based approaches) that would provide a more meaningful benchmark.
3. The paper claims the method is language-independent, but the evaluation is limited to English and Portuguese. Without evidence of performance on other languages with different grammatical structures, this claim is premature and needs stronger justification.
4. The preprocessing of disfluencies is not fully automated as claimed. The authors state they "automatically removed disfluencies following the same guidelines of TalkBank project," but this still requires rule-based processing that would need adaptation for different languages or domains, contradicting the language independence claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on how the specific network features (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness) relate to cognitive impairment markers in speech? Which features contribute most to the performance gains observed?
2. How would your method handle languages with significantly different syntactic structures from English and Portuguese, such as agglutinative languages? What adaptations would be necessary for such languages?
3. Could you provide more details on the threshold for cosine similarity used in network enrichment? How was this threshold determined, and would it need to be adjusted for different language or domain-specific contexts?

Overall, this is a promising approach to a clinically important problem that makes a valuable contribution to the application of network science in healthcare. While the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding and more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, the empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the potential of this approach for real-world MCI detection systems. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted, this paper would make a strong contribution to ACL 2017.
