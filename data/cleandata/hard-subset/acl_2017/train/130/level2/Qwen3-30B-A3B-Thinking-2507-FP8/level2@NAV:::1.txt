

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network representations enriched with word embeddings. The main contributions are: (1) applying complex network modeling to MCI detection from speech transcripts, avoiding manual transcript correction; (2) demonstrating that network features enriched with embeddings outperform traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets; and (3) showing that multi-view learning combining network features with other representations improves classification accuracy.

The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of their approach. MCI diagnosis is challenging, and language impairment is frequently undervalued in cognitive assessments. The authors successfully address a key limitation of prior work—manual transcript correction—by developing an automated pipeline that handles disfluencies. Their method demonstrates consistent improvements across three datasets, with multi-view learning achieving 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset and 4.71% improvement in the ABCD dataset compared to best individual classifiers.

The technical execution is sound, with appropriate use of complex network metrics and thorough evaluation across multiple classifiers. The paper provides sufficient methodological detail for reproducibility, and the results are statistically significant. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of linguistic features in handling disfluencies, which justifies their network-based approach.

However, the paper could be strengthened by deeper analysis of why their approach works better than linguistic features. The paper doesn't identify which specific network metrics are most informative for MCI detection, nor does it thoroughly discuss the limitations of their method for very short narratives (a common issue in clinical assessments). The discussion of limitations is minimal, only noting the need for publicly available datasets.

The paper shows promise for clinical applications, as it offers a lightweight, language-independent method suitable for large-scale assessments. While the novelty is somewhat incremental (applying existing network techniques to a new domain), the practical benefits for MCI detection are significant. The authors should strengthen their analysis of feature importance and expand their discussion of limitations to better position this work within the field.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the intersection of natural language processing and clinical assessment with clear practical value. With minor improvements in analysis and discussion, it would make a strong conference paper.
