### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and then "enrich" this graph by adding edges between semantically similar words, identified using word embeddings. The authors extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) of varying text lengths and elicitation tasks.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The application of complex network features derived from word co-occurrence graphs for MCI detection. The proposed enrichment of these networks with semantic information from word embeddings is a well-motivated strategy to overcome data sparsity, especially in the short texts common in clinical assessments. This provides a compelling alternative to traditional linguistic features that often fail on the disfluent and agrammatical speech of MCI patients.
2.  A thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed network-based features against standard baselines, including linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The experiments across three different datasets, including two languages, provide evidence for the method's robustness. The exploration of multi-view learning to combine heterogeneous feature sets is also a valuable contribution, showing significant performance gains.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper's core idea is both novel for the task and strongly motivated. The authors correctly identify a key challenge in analyzing clinical speech: the unreliability of standard NLP tools like parsers due to disfluencies. By shifting to a graph-based representation, the method sidesteps this issue, offering a more robust, lightweight, and language-agnostic approach. The use of word embeddings to enrich these graphs is a clever way to handle the short, sparse nature of the transcripts.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is a significant strength. By testing on three datasets that differ in language (English, Portuguese), task (picture description, story retelling), and average length, the authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach. The inclusion of two distinct baselines (linguistic features and BoW) provides necessary context for the results. Furthermore, the experiments with both automatically processed and manually corrected versions of the Cinderella dataset (Table 5 vs. Table 6) effectively support the claim that linguistic features suffer from agrammatical text while network features are more resilient.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The results convincingly show that the proposed enriched complex network (CNE) features are competitive and frequently outperform the standard linguistic metrics baseline. The gains achieved through multi-view learning and ensembles are impressive, with a 10% accuracy improvement on the Cinderella dataset and a 4.7% improvement on the ABCD dataset. This highlights the complementary nature of the different feature types and validates the multi-view approach for this task. The paper clearly presents and discusses these results, effectively substantiating its claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Interpretability and Feature Analysis:** The paper proposes using a large set of topological network metrics (average, standard deviation, and skewness for 10 base measures), but provides no analysis of which features are most discriminative. This is a missed opportunity. An analysis of feature importance could provide valuable insights into *how* the structure of language breaks down in MCI. For instance, are changes in node centrality, clustering coefficient, or path length most indicative of impairment? Such an analysis would elevate the paper from a purely engineering contribution to one with potential clinical and linguistic significance.

2.  **Under-examined Strength of the BoW Baseline:** The Bag-of-Words baseline performs surprisingly well, achieving the best single-feature-set accuracy on the ABCD dataset (75.47% vs. 71.73% for CNE with SVM-Linear). The paper notes this result is "somewhat surprising" but does not discuss its implications. This strong performance from a simple baseline partially weakens the argument for the necessity of a more complex graph-based representation. A discussion is needed on why simple word frequencies might be so predictive for that specific task (e.g., lexical poverty, repetitions of specific words) and how this relates to the cognitive deficits in MCI.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on Key Methodological Components:** Several critical details are missing, which hinders reproducibility.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** It is not specified whether the word embeddings were pre-trained on a large corpus or trained only on the small, in-domain datasets. Training on such small corpora would likely yield poor-quality embeddings. This is a crucial detail for the "enrichment" step.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper reports using cosine similarity thresholds of 0.7 and 0.4 to add edges to the networks, but does not explain how these values were chosen. Were they tuned on a development set? The performance of the method could be sensitive to this threshold, and the lack of a principled selection process is a weakness.

4.  **Positioning with Respect to State-of-the-Art:** The paper claims its results are "comparable" to the state-of-the-art (60%-85%) but avoids a direct comparison, citing different datasets. While understandable, the performance on the Cookie Theft dataset (max 65% accuracy) appears lower than results reported in other works using the same DementiaBank resource (e.g., Fraser et al., 2015, who report over 80% accuracy using linguistic features). A more nuanced discussion of how the results compare to prior work on this specific, well-known dataset is necessary to properly contextualize the contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training protocol for the word embeddings? Were they pre-trained on a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or trained solely on the transcripts from your datasets?
2.  How was the cosine similarity threshold for adding semantic edges to the networks determined for each dataset? Was this parameter tuned using a separate development set, and if so, could you describe the procedure?
3.  Given the strong performance of the BoW model on the ABCD dataset, do you have any hypotheses about the linguistic phenomena it might be capturing that are particularly relevant for that short-retelling task?
4.  Have you performed any feature importance analysis to identify which network topological metrics are most predictive of MCI? This could provide valuable insight into the specific aspects of language network structure that are affected by cognitive impairment.
