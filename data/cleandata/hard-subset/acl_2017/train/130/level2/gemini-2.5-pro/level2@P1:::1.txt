### **Reviewer Report**

**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The authors model each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and, crucially, enrich this network with additional edges based on the semantic similarity of words, calculated using word embeddings. This enrichment step is designed to make the network representation more informative, especially for the short texts often produced in clinical assessments. The authors then extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks. These features are used to train various machine learning classifiers to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls.

The work is evaluated on three distinct datasets: the English Cookie Theft corpus from DementiaBank, and two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella story retelling and ABCD battery). The proposed complex network features are compared against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and a Bag-of-Words baseline. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks for Clinical NLP:** The paper introduces a well-motivated application of complex networks to the important clinical problem of MCI detection. The specific method of enriching a co-occurrence graph with semantic links from word embeddings to better model short and disfluent texts is a key contribution.
2.  **A Robust Feature Set for Agrammatical Speech:** The proposed graph-based features offer a promising alternative to traditional NLP features that often rely on syntactic parsing, which can be unreliable on the agrammatical and disfluent speech common in MCI patients. This represents a valuable practical contribution to the field.
3.  **Comprehensive Multi-lingual and Multi-task Evaluation:** The authors validate their method across three datasets spanning two languages (English and Portuguese) and different narrative tasks. This thorough evaluation strengthens the paper's claims about the general applicability of the approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Sound Methodology:** The rationale for using a graph-based representation is very clearly articulated. The authors provide a convincing argument for why methods robust to grammatical errors are needed for this task. The idea of enriching a sparse co-occurrence network with semantic information from word embeddings is an intelligent way to address the known limitations of applying network analysis to short texts.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experiments:** The experimental design is a significant strength. The authors compare their proposed features against two relevant and widely-used baselines (linguistic features and BoW). They test a variety of standard classification algorithms and also investigate the utility of combining feature sets via multi-view learning and ensembling classifiers, which ultimately yields the best results.
3.  **Strong and Promising Results:** The proposed Complex Network Enriched (CNE) features are shown to be highly competitive, often outperforming the linguistic feature baseline on its own. The improvements gained from multi-view learning are substantial (e.g., a 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset), and the final accuracy of 80% on the ABCD dataset is impressive for this challenging task. The positive results across multiple datasets suggest the method is robust.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Detail on Key Methodological Components:** The success of the network enrichment process hinges on two critical components that are not described in sufficient detail:
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The paper states that embeddings were trained using the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016) with default parameters, but does not specify the training corpus. Were the embeddings trained on a large, general-domain corpus (like Wikipedia), or on a smaller, in-domain corpus of clinical transcripts? This choice has a major impact on the quality and relevance of the semantic similarities used to enrich the networks.
    *   **Threshold Optimization:** A cosine similarity threshold was "optimized" to decide when to add a semantic edge. The paper needs to clarify how this optimization was performed. If the threshold was tuned using the same data used for reporting final cross-validation scores (e.g., by testing several values and reporting the best result), this could lead to an overestimation of the model's performance. A description of the validation process (e.g., using a separate development set or a nested cross-validation loop) is needed to ensure the results are sound.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Interpretation:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the method works, it provides little insight into *why* it works. An analysis of the features themselves would greatly strengthen the paper. For example, which of the topological metrics are most discriminative between MCI patients and controls? Do the network structures systematically differ (e.g., do MCI networks show lower connectivity or longer path lengths)? Providing such an analysis or even visualizing example networks from each class could offer valuable insights into the linguistic markers of MCI and elevate the paper from a successful application of a technique to a more impactful scientific contribution.
3.  **Clarity of Comparison with Baselines:** The comparison between the proposed method and the linguistic features (LM) baseline could be more direct. In Table 6 (manually cleaned Cinderella data), LMs achieve a higher accuracy (72.5%) than CNE (67.5%). The authors use this to argue that LMs require clean text, which is a fair point. However, it also highlights that on cleaner text, LMs might be a stronger feature set. A more direct discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial. Furthermore, on the ABCD dataset, the BoW baseline is very strong, outperforming the CNE features with a linear SVM. While the combined multi-view model is best, the strength of the BoW baseline deserves a brief discussion, as it suggests simple lexical choice may be a very powerful signal in that specific task.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the word embeddings? Specifically, what training corpus was used, and was it different for the English and Portuguese models?
2.  Could you please elaborate on the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for adding semantic edges to the networks? Was a separate development set used, or was this parameter tuned within the final evaluation folds?
3.  Have you performed any feature importance analysis to identify which network metrics are most predictive? Such an analysis could provide interesting linguistic or cognitive insights into the nature of language changes in MCI.
