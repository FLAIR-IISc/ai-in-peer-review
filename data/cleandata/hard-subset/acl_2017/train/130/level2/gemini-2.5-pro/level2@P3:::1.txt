### Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for automatically detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and then enrich this network with additional edges based on semantic similarity between words, calculated using word embeddings. This enrichment step is designed to make the network representation more informative, especially for the short and often agrammatical texts produced during neuropsychological assessments. The topological features of these enriched networks are then used to train machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) of varying lengths and styles. The results demonstrate that the proposed complex network features are competitive with, and often outperform, traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words baselines. Furthermore, the work shows that combining different feature types using multi-view learning can yield significant performance improvements.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a novel text representation method that enriches sparse word co-occurrence networks with semantic information from word embeddings. This approach directly addresses the challenge of applying graph-based methods to short texts, resulting in denser, more informative network representations that are robust to the disfluencies common in clinical speech.
2.  A second significant contribution is the comprehensive empirical validation of this method on the clinical task of MCI detection. The evaluation across three different datasets, two languages, and against strong baselines provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness and potential for practical application.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The method of using word embeddings to add semantically-motivated edges to a co-occurrence graph is a clever and well-motivated solution to the problem of data sparsity in graph-based text analysis. It elegantly combines structural (syntagmatic) and semantic (paradigmatic) information into a single representation. This addresses a known limitation of using complex networks for short texts, which often result in trivial, linear graphs.
2.  **Robustness to Agrammaticality:** The proposed approach is inherently less dependent on correct syntax than methods relying on parsing and deep linguistic analysis. This makes it particularly suitable for analyzing the speech of individuals with cognitive impairments, which frequently contains repetitions, false starts, and other disfluencies that break standard parsers. The comparison between the automatically processed and manually revised versions of the Cinderella dataset (Tables 5 and 6) supports the claim that linguistic features benefit more from clean, grammatical text, highlighting the value of the proposed network-based method.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The work is validated across three distinct datasets, demonstrating its applicability to different languages (English, Portuguese) and elicitation tasks (picture description, story narration, story recall). The inclusion of multiple strong baselines (linguistic features from Coh-Metrix, Bag-of-Words) and the exploration of various classification algorithms and combination techniques (ensembles, multi-view learning) make the empirical evaluation robust and convincing.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The research addresses an important real-world problem with significant clinical implications. By developing a more automated method for analyzing speech transcripts that circumvents the need for extensive manual correction or highly robust NLP tools (which are scarce for many languages), the work presents a promising direction for scalable, low-cost screening tools for MCI.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Selection:** The method introduces a crucial hyperparameter: the cosine similarity threshold for adding semantic edges to the network. The paper reports the optimal values found (0.7 and 0.4) but provides no details on how this threshold was selected or an analysis of how sensitive the model's performance is to this choice. A discussion of the tuning process (e.g., nested cross-validation on a development set) and a sensitivity analysis would significantly strengthen the claim of robustness. This is an essential revision for reproducibility and credibility.
2.  **Lack of Detail on Word Embeddings:** The paper states that it uses the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016) but does not specify the training corpora for the English and Portuguese embeddings. The source, domain, and size of the corpus used to train embeddings can have a substantial impact on their quality and, consequently, on the performance of the downstream task. Clarifying these details is essential for reproducibility and for a complete understanding of the experimental conditions.
3.  **Absence of Feature Analysis:** The work extracts ten different types of topological network measures (and their moments), resulting in a sizable feature set. However, there is no analysis of which of these network features are most discriminative for detecting MCI. A feature importance analysis could provide valuable insights into the specific structural properties of language that are affected by MCI, moving the paper beyond a pure engineering result towards one with greater clinical and scientific interpretability. While not essential for acceptance, this would greatly increase the paper's impact.
4.  **Statistical Significance:** The paper reports improvements in accuracy percentages, but it is not clear if these gains are statistically significant. Given the relatively small size of the datasets, especially the Cinderella dataset (n=40), even seemingly large percentage gains might not be statistically robust. Conducting significance tests would provide stronger evidence for the claims made about the superiority of the proposed models.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could the paper clarify the methodology for selecting the cosine similarity threshold? Was this parameter tuned using a separate validation set within each fold of the cross-validation to avoid data leakage?
2.  Could details be provided on the corpora used to train the English and Portuguese word embeddings? The domain of the training data seems particularly relevant for a clinical task.
3.  The results on the ABCD dataset (Table 7) show that a linear SVM on Bag-of-Words features performs very well (75.47% accuracy), not far behind the best multi-view model (80.18%). Could the paper elaborate on the trade-offs between the proposed network model and this strong, simpler baseline? This could help delineate the scenarios where the additional complexity of the network approach is most warranted.
