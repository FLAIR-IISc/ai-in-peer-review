**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The authors address the challenge of short, agrammatical texts common in clinical data by enriching word co-occurrence networks with semantic edges derived from word embeddings. Topological features are extracted from these enriched networks (CNE) and used for classification. The method is evaluated on three datasets in English and Portuguese, demonstrating that CNE features, especially when combined in a multi-view learning setup, effectively distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls, outperforming traditional linguistic features.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a robust feature extraction method for clinical text analysis. By using complex networks enriched with word embeddings, the approach captures linguistic patterns relevant to MCI without relying on syntactic parsers that are fragile to the disfluencies present in patient speech.
2.  A secondary contribution is a comprehensive empirical study across multiple datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, ABCD). This evaluation convincingly demonstrates the method's effectiveness across different languages and text lengths and highlights the benefits of a multi-view learning approach for this task.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper's method is well-motivated, directly addressing a key limitation of prior work: the difficulty of applying standard NLP tools to noisy clinical transcripts. The proposed CNE model is an elegant solution that bypasses this parsing dependency.
2.  The experimental validation is strong. The authors test their approach on three distinct datasets and compare it against relevant baselines. The consistent performance improvements, particularly from the multi-view models which achieve up to 80% accuracy, provide compelling evidence for the method’s utility.
3.  The approach has significant practical potential as a scalable, automated, and language-agnostic tool for MCI screening, which could lower costs and improve access to early diagnosis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The paper lacks an analysis of the extracted network features. There is no discussion on which topological properties are most discriminative for MCI detection. A feature importance analysis would offer valuable insight into *why* the network representation is effective.
2.  The strength of the Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline on the ABCD dataset (75.5% accuracy) is not adequately discussed. Its performance, higher than the standalone CNE model, slightly undermines the central claim of the network model's superiority. A deeper analysis of this result is needed.
3.  Reproducibility is a concern regarding the word embeddings. The paper does not specify the corpus used for training them, yet the quality of these embeddings is critical to the network enrichment process. Using publicly available, pre-trained models would strengthen the results.
