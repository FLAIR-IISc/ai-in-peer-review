**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a graph-based method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The approach leverages word co-occurrence to encode features and uses a variety of graph-derived features, including lexical and syntactic characteristics. The model is evaluated on three datasets using 5-fold cross-validation and multiple classifiers, with varying performance across datasets.

**Contributions**

1. **Graph-based approach for MCI detection**: The paper proposes a novel approach for detecting MCI from speech transcripts using graph-based features, which is a departure from traditional linguistic feature-based methods.
2. **Use of word embeddings for graph enrichment**: The authors use word embeddings to enrich the graph features, which allows for the capture of semantic relationships between words.
3. **Evaluation on multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates the proposed approach on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery datasets, which demonstrates its applicability to different datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The graph-based approach proposed in the paper is a novel contribution to the field of MCI detection from speech transcripts.
2. **Use of word embeddings**: The use of word embeddings for graph enrichment is a strength of the paper, as it allows for the capture of semantic relationships between words.
3. **Evaluation on multiple datasets**: The evaluation of the proposed approach on multiple datasets demonstrates its applicability to different datasets and increases the confidence in the results.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Poor writing quality**: The writing quality of the paper is poor, with numerous grammatical and spelling errors, which makes it difficult to understand the content.
2. **Unclear problem definition**: The ML problem and instance definition are unclear, which makes it difficult to understand the proposed approach and its evaluation.
3. **Lack of domain-specific NLP tools**: The paper does not leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools, which could have improved the performance of the proposed approach.
4. **Unclear class distribution**: The class distribution is unclear, which makes accuracy a questionable metric, and F1 (and class balance) should be reported instead.
5. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The results are reported with unjustified precision on very small datasets and lack statistical significance testing, which reduces the confidence in the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify the ML problem and instance definition, and provide more details on the dataset and tables?
2. How did you choose the word embedding method, and what are the advantages of using this method over simpler word-node similarity approaches?
3. Can you provide more details on the class distribution and the evaluation metrics used, and consider reporting F1 (and class balance) instead of accuracy?
4. How did you address the issue of overfitting, given the small size of the datasets?
5. Can you provide more details on the statistical significance testing performed, and consider adding more robust statistical analysis to the results?
