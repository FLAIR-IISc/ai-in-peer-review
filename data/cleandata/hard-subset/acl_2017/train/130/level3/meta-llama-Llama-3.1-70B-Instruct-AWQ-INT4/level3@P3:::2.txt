This paper proposes a graph-based method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. They evaluate their model on three datasets and report competitive results with traditional methods.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The application of complex network features to detect MCI in speech transcripts, which is a novel approach in this field.
2. The use of word embeddings to enrich the complex networks, which allows for the capture of semantic information.
3. The evaluation of the proposed method on three datasets, including the DementiaBank and two Portuguese datasets.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The authors address a well-defined clinical problem and provide a clear motivation for their approach.
2. They use appropriate datasets and provide a thorough evaluation of their method.
3. The use of complex network features and word embeddings is a novel approach in this field, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology.

However, there are also some weaknesses:

1. The writing quality is poor, with numerous grammatical and spelling errors throughout the paper.
2. The machine learning problem and instance definition are unclear, and the dataset/tables describe studies rather than transcript-level data.
3. The reported counts are inconsistent, and the class distribution is unclear, making accuracy a questionable metric.
4. The results are reported with unjustified precision on very small datasets, and lack statistical significance testing.
5. The authors do not leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools, which could potentially improve their results.

Some questions to the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the preprocessing steps and how you handled disfluencies in the transcripts?
2. How did you select the threshold parameter for the cosine similarity, and what is the justification for the chosen value?
3. Can you provide more information on the class distribution of the datasets and how you handled imbalanced classes?
4. How do you plan to address the limitations of your approach, such as the small size of the datasets and the lack of statistical significance testing?

Additional comments:

* The authors should consider using more robust evaluation metrics, such as F1-score, to account for class imbalance.
* The use of domain-specific biomedical NLP tools could potentially improve the results and provide more insights into the linguistic features of MCI.
* The authors should provide more details on the computational resources and time required to train and evaluate their model.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work and how the proposed approach differs from existing methods.
