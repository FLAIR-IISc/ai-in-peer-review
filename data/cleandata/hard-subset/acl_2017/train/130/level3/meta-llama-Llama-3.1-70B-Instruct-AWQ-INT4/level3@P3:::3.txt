**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. They evaluate their model on three datasets and compare the performance of complex network features with linguistic features and traditional statistical features. The results show that complex networks are suitable for detecting MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to detect MCI from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings.
2. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets and compare the performance of complex network features with linguistic features and traditional statistical features.
3. The results show that complex networks are suitable for detecting MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to detect MCI from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets, which provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
3. The results show that complex networks are suitable for detecting MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets, which is a promising finding.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks methodological details, such as the exact scheme used for multi-view learning and the threshold parameter used for embedding cosine distance.
2. The set of "linguistic features" is insufficiently described, and Section 4.2.2 needs to be expanded.
3. The paper does not provide statistical significance tests to support experimental conclusions.
4. The related work coverage is superficial, and core prior methods and direct comparisons need more depth and precise methodological descriptions.
5. The abstract is too long and needs to be shortened.
6. The paper requires considerable editing to improve readability and clarity.
7. The removal of disfluencies is unjustified and needs to be explained or reconsidered.
8. The use of term-frequency rather than tf-idf weighting in the Bag-of-Words classification needs to be justified.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the exact scheme used for multi-view learning and the threshold parameter used for embedding cosine distance?
2. Can you expand on the set of "linguistic features" used in the paper and provide more details on how they were extracted?
3. Can you provide statistical significance tests to support experimental conclusions?
4. Can you provide more depth and precise methodological descriptions of core prior methods and direct comparisons?
5. Can you justify the removal of disfluencies and explain why term-frequency rather than tf-idf weighting was used in the Bag-of-Words classification?
6. Can you provide more details on the datasets used in the paper, including the specific datasets used to train the word embeddings?
7. Can you clarify the text preprocessing steps and order, including lemmatization and other normalization steps?
