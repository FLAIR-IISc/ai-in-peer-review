Paper proposes a graph-based method using word co-occurrence and graph-derived lexical/syntactic features to detect MCI from speech transcripts, evaluated on three datasets with 5-fold CV and multiple classifiers.

Contrib: Novel graph-based approach for MCI detection from speech transcripts as an alternative to traditional linguistic features.

Contrib: Word embeddings are used to enrich graphs to capture semantic relationships between words.

Contrib: Evaluation performed on three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, Arizona-Battery) demonstrating cross-dataset applicability.

Strength: The graph-based method is a novel contribution to MCI detection from transcripts.

Strength: Use of word embeddings to enrich graph features captures semantic relationships effectively.

Strength: Evaluation on multiple datasets increases confidence in generalizability.

Weakness: Paper suffers from poor writing quality with many grammatical and spelling errors.

Weakness: ML problem and instance definition are unclear, hindering understanding of the approach and evaluation.

Weakness: Does not leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools that could improve performance.

Weakness: Class distribution is unclear, making accuracy a questionable metric; F1 and class balance should be reported.

Weakness: Results lack statistical significance testing and are reported with unjustified precision on small datasets.

Question: Please clarify the ML problem and instance definition and provide more dataset details and tables.

Question: Explain how and why the word embedding method was chosen versus simpler word-node similarity approaches.

Question: Provide class distribution details and use appropriate metrics (e.g., F1) rather than accuracy.

Question: Describe how overfitting was addressed given the small dataset sizes.

Question: Provide details on statistical significance testing and consider more robust statistical analysis of results.
