## Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts across three datasets. The core idea of modeling transcripts as graphs and leveraging network properties is interesting, particularly given the challenges of parsing disfluent speech common in MCI patients. The authors evaluate their approach with several classifiers and demonstrate varying performance across datasets, suggesting the method's sensitivity to data characteristics. The use of multiple datasets, including both English and Portuguese, is a positive aspect.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the *application* of complex network analysis to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech, and the exploration of word embedding enrichment within this framework. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to this clinical task, and the combination with embeddings, represents a worthwhile investigation. A secondary contribution is the evaluation across diverse datasets, highlighting the potential and limitations of the approach in different linguistic contexts.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The writing quality is poor, riddled with grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, hindering comprehension. More critically, the definition of the “ML problem” and the “instance” is unclear. The description of the datasets focuses on the *studies* from which the transcripts originate, rather than providing clear details about the transcript-level data itself (e.g., length, topic, elicitation protocol). The reported counts and statistics appear inconsistent, raising concerns about data handling. The motivation for using graph enrichment over simpler word-node similarity approaches is not adequately justified. Furthermore, the authors fail to leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools that could enhance feature extraction and potentially improve performance. 

The evaluation methodology also raises concerns. The class distribution within each dataset is not reported, making accuracy a potentially misleading metric. Reporting F1-score and considering class balance is crucial. Results are presented with unjustified precision on relatively small datasets, and a lack of statistical significance testing prevents a robust assessment of the findings. The comparison to linguistic features is also not thoroughly explored, and the impact of manual vs. automatic transcript segmentation isn’t clearly delineated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Clinical Problem:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important clinical problem – early detection of MCI.
*   **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The use of three datasets (English and Portuguese) provides a broader assessment of the method’s generalizability.
*   **Novel Application:** Applying complex network analysis to MCI detection from speech is a novel approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Poor Writing Quality:** Significant grammatical errors and unclear phrasing.
*   **Unclear Problem Definition:** Lack of clarity regarding the ML problem and instance definition.
*   **Insufficient Justification:** Weak motivation for graph enrichment over simpler methods.
*   **Lack of Domain Expertise:** Failure to leverage biomedical NLP tools.
*   **Inadequate Evaluation:** Missing F1-score, lack of statistical significance testing, and unjustified precision.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed description of the transcript-level data for each dataset (e.g., average length, topic, elicitation protocol)?
2.  What is the class distribution within each dataset?
3.  Why was complex network analysis chosen over simpler word-node similarity approaches?
4.  Have you considered incorporating domain-specific biomedical NLP tools to enhance feature extraction?
5.  Can you provide statistical significance testing to support your reported results?
