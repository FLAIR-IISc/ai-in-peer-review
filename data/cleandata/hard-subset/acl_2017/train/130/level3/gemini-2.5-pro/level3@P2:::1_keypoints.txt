- Proposes representing transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs enriched with semantic edges from pre-trained embeddings to detect MCI robustly despite short/agrammatical patient speech.  
- Evaluated on three datasets (Cookie Theft—English; Cinderella and ABCD—Portuguese), comparing CNE features vs linguistic metrics and BoW; CNE (especially in ensembles/multi-view) outperforms baselines, reaching 80% on ABCD.  
- Novel application of complex networks enriched with embeddings for clinical text analysis, avoiding reliance on brittle syntactic parsing.  
- Extends MCI detection research to Brazilian Portuguese by collecting and evaluating two Portuguese datasets.  
- Provides a systematic comparative analysis showing multi-view learning combining different feature spaces yields improved performance.  
- Methodology is well-motivated and creative, addressing non-canonical patient speech with a graph-based approach.  
- Tackles a high-impact clinical problem (early MCI detection) with potential for scalable, non-invasive screening tools.  
- Evaluation across multiple datasets and languages (including Portuguese) strengthens the paper’s applicability and contribution.  
- Lacks key methodological details harming reproducibility (unclear embedding training source and subword handling).  
- Portuguese datasets are very small, raising concerns about high variance, limited generalizability, and unclear statistical significance.  
- Manuscript clarity and organization need improvement: contains irrelevant citations, omits transcription-norm explanations, and has typographical errors.  
- Question: Were embeddings trained only on the experimental transcripts or on larger external corpora for English and Portuguese?  
- Question: How are OOV words defined in the experiments, and how does the subword model interact with the word-level graph?  
- Question: Were statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar’s test or bootstrapping) performed to confirm the reported accuracy improvements?
