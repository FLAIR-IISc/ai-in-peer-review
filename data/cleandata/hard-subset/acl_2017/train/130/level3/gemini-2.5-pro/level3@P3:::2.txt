### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To address data sparsity in short transcripts, these graphs are "enriched" by adding edges between words with high semantic similarity, as measured by word embedding cosine similarity. A variety of topological features are extracted from these graphs and used to train several machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) and compared against standard linguistic features and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline. The authors report that their graph-based features outperform the baselines and that performance can be further improved using multi-view learning.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **Application and evaluation of a graph-based feature representation for MCI detection across multiple datasets.** The paper demonstrates the viability of using complex network features for this clinical task on three different datasets, covering two languages. This provides evidence for the method's potential generalizability.
2.  **An empirical comparison showing graph-based features can be competitive with or superior to traditional linguistic features.** The work provides a direct comparison to feature sets derived from tools like Coh-Metrix, arguing that the graph-based approach is more robust to the agrammatical and disfluent nature of patient speech, which can impair traditional NLP parsers.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a significant and well-motivated clinical problem.** The automatic detection of MCI from speech is a clinically important task, as it can enable early, non-invasive screening for neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's. The paper clearly frames the problem and the potential impact of an automated solution.
2.  **The core method is well-suited for the problem's data characteristics.** The central motivation for using a graph-based representation—that it is more robust to the disfluencies and agrammaticality common in patient speech than parsing-dependent linguistic features—is sound. This positions the work as a practical approach to a known challenge in clinical NLP.
3.  **Evaluation across multiple and diverse datasets.** The authors validate their approach on the English DementiaBank corpus as well as two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella and ABCD). Using multiple corpora, including one that is publicly available, strengthens the study's conclusions and suggests the method is not overtuned to a single data distribution or language.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant lack of clarity in the experimental setup and problem definition.** The manuscript's description of the machine learning task is ambiguous and inconsistent.
    *   **Unclear Instance Definition:** It is unclear what constitutes a single data point for classification. For the ABCD dataset, the text states there are 23 MCI and 20 Control subjects, but that they produced 46 MCI and 39 Control narratives, respectively. Are the instances the subjects or the narratives? This fundamental ambiguity makes it impossible to fully understand the experimental design and interpret the results.
    *   **Inconsistent Data Counts:** The participant counts in the demographic tables (e.g., Table 1) do not clearly map to the number of transcripts used. For the Cookie Theft dataset, the text mentions using 43 MCI transcripts, but the table lists demographics for 43 MCI *and* 43 Control participants. The exact number of instances per class is never explicitly stated for any dataset.
    *   **Poor Writing Quality:** The manuscript is in urgent need of proofreading. There are numerous grammatical errors, typos ("syntatic," "outperfom"), and awkward phrasings throughout the paper that impede readability and comprehension.

2.  **Flawed evaluation metrics and reporting.** The evaluation methodology suffers from several issues that call the validity of the conclusions into question.
    *   **Inappropriate Metric:** Given the small dataset sizes and unclear (but likely imbalanced) class distributions, reporting only classification accuracy is insufficient and potentially misleading. A high accuracy score could be achieved by a model that simply predicts the majority class. Class-specific precision, recall, and F1-score are necessary for a meaningful evaluation.
    *   **Unjustified Precision and Lack of Statistical Testing:** Results are reported with up to four decimal places of precision (e.g., 0.7173 in Table 7). On small test sets (e.g., 5-fold CV on 40 subjects in the Cinderella dataset results in test folds of 8 instances), this level of precision is statistically meaningless. More importantly, no statistical significance tests are performed to determine whether the reported performance differences between models are genuine improvements or simply due to random variation in the data splits.

3.  **Weak motivation for the proposed graph enrichment technique.** The authors propose enriching word co-occurrence graphs with edges derived from word embedding similarity to handle short texts. However, the motivation for this specific approach over simpler, more established alternatives is not provided. It is unclear why creating a denser graph is superior to using embeddings more directly (e.g., by averaging them to form a document vector). Furthermore, the similarity threshold is a critical hyperparameter, but its optimization process is not described, hindering reproducibility.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the datasets, could you please clarify the precise definition of a single instance used for classification? Is it one transcript per patient, or can a patient contribute multiple transcripts (as suggested for the ABCD dataset)? Please explicitly state the number of instances for the Control class and the MCI class for each of the three datasets.
2.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for the proposed graph enrichment method? Why is adding edges based on embedding similarity preferable to other common techniques for leveraging word embeddings in short text classification, such as averaging word vectors to create document representations?
3.  Given the small and potentially imbalanced nature of the datasets, accuracy is not an ideal evaluation metric. Could you provide the class-wise precision, recall, and F1-scores for your experiments? Additionally, have you performed any statistical significance tests to confirm that the reported improvements of your method over the baselines are statistically meaningful?
