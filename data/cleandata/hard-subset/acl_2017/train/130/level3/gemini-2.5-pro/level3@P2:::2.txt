### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using features derived from complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and then "enrich" this graph by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on pre-trained word embeddings. The authors extract a set of topological features from these enriched graphs and use them to train various machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three different datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against baselines using traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words representations.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel graph-based feature representation for MCI detection.** The proposed method of creating word co-occurrence networks and enriching them with semantic information from word embeddings is a creative approach to representing speech transcripts. This representation has the potential to be more robust to the disfluencies and agrammatical structures common in MCI speech compared to methods that rely heavily on syntactic parsing.
2.  **An empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and languages.** The authors demonstrate the applicability of their method on three distinct datasets with different characteristics (e.g., elicitation task, language, text length). The results show that the complex network features are competitive with, and in some cases outperform, standard linguistic features, providing evidence for the method's potential.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses an Important Clinical Problem:** The automatic detection of MCI is a clinically significant and challenging task. Early diagnosis can lead to better patient outcomes, and developing automated, language-based screening tools is a valuable research direction. This work is well-motivated and tackles a problem of high interest to the computational linguistics and clinical NLP communities.

2.  **Novelty of the Proposed Method:** The use of complex network theory for this task is a departure from more common feature engineering approaches that rely on parsers or acoustic features. The idea to enrich a structural (co-occurrence) network with semantic (embedding similarity) information to better handle short and noisy texts is particularly interesting and provides a new way to think about feature extraction from clinical transcripts.

3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Setup:** The authors should be commended for evaluating their model on three different datasets, including data in both English and Portuguese. This demonstrates a commitment to assessing the generalizability of their approach beyond a single corpus or language. Furthermore, comparing against both linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and a standard text classification baseline (BoW) provides a solid context for interpreting the performance of the proposed method.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Setup and Reporting:** The manuscript suffers from significant clarity issues that make it difficult to fully understand the experiments and results.
    *   The definition of a classification "instance" is ambiguous. The dataset tables describe the number of subjects, but the results seem to be based on classifying individual narratives. For example, the ABCD dataset has 23 MCI and 20 control subjects, but they produced 46 and 39 narratives, respectively. It is unclear how these multiple narratives from the same subject are handled in the 5-fold cross-validation to avoid data leakage.
    *   The number of participants and samples seems very low for drawing strong conclusions. For instance, the Cinderella dataset has only 40 subjects total.
    *   The writing quality requires substantial revision. There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings throughout the paper that impede readability and reduce its overall professional quality.

2.  **Insufficient Evaluation Metrics and Statistical Analysis:**
    *   The paper relies exclusively on accuracy as its evaluation metric. Given the small dataset sizes and the potential for class imbalance (the distribution is never stated), accuracy can be a misleading metric. Reporting precision, recall, and F1-score for each class would provide a much clearer picture of the model's performance.
    *   The results are reported with up to four decimal places of precision, which is unjustifiable for experiments on such small datasets and gives a false sense of significance.
    *   Most importantly, the paper lacks any form of statistical significance testing. With small performance differences between methods (e.g., a 2.3% improvement on the Cookie Theft dataset), it is impossible to determine whether the proposed model is genuinely superior to the baselines or if the improvements are simply due to random variation in the data splits.

3.  **Weak Methodological Justification:** While the idea of enriching networks is interesting, the motivation is not clearly articulated. The paper does not explain why this specific hybrid approach (co-occurrence + similarity) is superior to simpler alternatives. For example, why not build a graph based *only* on word embedding similarity (e.g., a k-NN graph)? A more detailed ablation study or justification is needed to support the specific design choices of the proposed network construction method.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact number of classification instances per class (MCI vs. Control) for each dataset? Furthermore, how did you ensure that all narratives from a single participant were kept within the same fold during cross-validation to prevent train-test contamination?

2.  Given the potential for class imbalance in clinical datasets, could you please provide the F1-scores (or precision/recall) for your key results in addition to accuracy?

3.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-test or McNemar's test) to confirm that the performance gains of your proposed CNE method over the baselines (LM, BoW) are statistically significant?

4.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for combining co-occurrence edges with word similarity edges? Have you experimented with a network constructed purely from word similarity, and how does it compare to your proposed hybrid CNE model?
