- Summary: Proposes enriched word co-occurrence networks (augmented with embedding-based semantic edges) to detect MCI from speech transcripts, extracting topological features to train classifiers and evaluating on three datasets (1 EN, 2 PT) against linguistic and BoW baselines; reports competitive performance and gains from multi-view learning.

- Contribution 1: Applies enriched complex network features to MCI detection, combining co-occurrence graphs with embedding links to handle disfluent/short texts.

- Contribution 2: Provides comparative evaluation on three datasets (including two Portuguese), offering cross-lingual insights and robustness analysis versus linguistic and BoW features.

- Strength 1: Tackles an important clinical problem with a well-motivated goal to avoid dependence on brittle parsing of disfluent patient speech.

- Strength 2: Uses a sound, established graph-based methodology and a reasonable strategy to enrich sparse networks with semantic edges; experimental setup compares multiple baselines and classifiers.

- Strength 3: Empirical results show enriched networks (CNE) outperform basic networks (CN) and are competitive with linguistic features; multi-view fusion yields additional gains.

- Weakness 1: Critical methodological details missing—multi-view learning description is vague (which classifiers per view and voting scheme unspecified).

- Weakness 2: Linguistic-features baseline is under-specified—no clear list of which of the Coh-Metrix features (106 EN / 58 PT) were used or a supplement link.

- Weakness 3: Word-embeddings provenance is unspecified—no description of corpus used to train embeddings, impacting result interpretation.

- Weakness 4: Disfluency removal is unjustified—filled pauses, false starts and repetitions (known MCI markers) were removed without strong rationale, conflicting with paper motivation.

- Weakness 5: BoW baseline uses raw term frequency (tf) instead of tf-idf without justification, potentially weakening the baseline.

- Weakness 6: No statistical significance testing is reported, making it unclear whether observed accuracy differences are meaningful given small datasets.

- Weakness 7: Related work section is superficial and lacks methodological discussion, limiting understanding of novelty relative to prior studies.

- Weakness 8: Manuscript clarity issues—overlong abstract, convoluted sentences, undefined acronyms (e.g., WML, PLN), and missing citations/transcription norms.

- Question 1: Which corpus was used to train the word embeddings?

- Question 2: Why were disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions, false starts) removed despite their known diagnostic value for cognitive decline?

- Question 3: What is the rationale for using tf instead of tf-idf for the BoW baseline?

- Question 4: Please specify the exact multi-view implementation: which classifier trained per view and how majority voting was performed (e.g., tie-breaking, weighting).

- Question 5: Will you include statistical significance tests in the final version to substantiate claimed performance improvements?
