### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a method for automatically detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs and enrich these graphs with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. This approach is designed to be robust to the agrammatical and disfluent nature of speech from individuals with cognitive impairments, which can challenge traditional NLP pipelines that rely on parsing. The method is evaluated on three datasets: the English DementiaBank "Cookie Theft" dataset, and two Brazilian Portuguese datasets ("Cinderella" and "Arizona Battery"). The authors report that their complex network features, particularly when enriched with embeddings, are competitive with and often outperform standard linguistic features and Bag-of-Words representations. They also show that multi-view learning and classifier ensembles can further improve performance, achieving accuracies up to 80% on the Arizona dataset.

### Contributions
1.  The primary contribution of this work is the application and evaluation of a complex network-based approach for MCI detection on Brazilian Portuguese speech transcripts. This is significant as it extends research in this critical health domain to a lower-resource language, providing valuable baselines and insights. The creation and use of two new Portuguese datasets for this task is a key part of this contribution.
2.  A secondary contribution is the demonstration that enriching co-occurrence networks with word embeddings is an effective strategy for this task, particularly for handling the short and noisy texts common in neuropsychological assessments. The paper effectively argues that this graph-based method is more resilient to the disfluencies present in MCI speech compared to methods reliant on syntactic parsing, offering a practical advantage by reducing the need for manual transcript correction.

### Strengths
1.  **Important Application to a Lower-Resource Language:** The paper tackles a clinically relevant problem—the early detection of cognitive decline—and applies NLP methods to Brazilian Portuguese. Much of the existing work in this area focuses on English. By evaluating their methods on Portuguese data, the authors make a valuable contribution to the field, highlighting the challenges and potential of NLP for healthcare applications in different linguistic contexts.
2.  **Robust Method for Noisy Clinical Data:** The core motivation for using complex networks—to avoid the brittleness of traditional parsers on agrammatical text—is well-founded and compelling. The speech of individuals with MCI is often filled with repetitions, false starts, and syntactic errors. The proposed method, which relies on word adjacency and semantic similarity, is inherently more robust to such noise. This is a significant practical strength, as it moves towards a more automated and scalable analysis pipeline that does not depend on costly manual clean-up of transcripts.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Comparison:** The authors compare their proposed features (complex networks with and without embeddings) against two strong baselines: a large set of linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and a standard Bag-of-Words model. They test these feature sets across multiple classifiers and also explore combinations using ensemble and multi-view learning. This comprehensive evaluation on three different datasets strengthens their conclusion that the network-based representation is a viable and powerful approach for this classification task.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Generalizability Due to Small Datasets:** The two Portuguese datasets are very small (N=40 for Cinderella, N=43 subjects for Arizona Battery). While the collection of clinical data is challenging, conclusions drawn from such small sample sizes are preliminary and may not generalize well. The reported performance gains, while promising, may not be statistically significant, and the models could be overfitting to the specific characteristics of the participants in this study. This severely limits the broader impact and reliability of the findings.
2.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility in Methods:** Several key methodological details are missing, making the work difficult to reproduce.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** It is unclear how the word embeddings for Portuguese were trained. The paper states it uses the method from Bojanowski et al. (2016), but does not specify the training corpus. Was it trained only on the very small in-domain datasets, or on a larger external Portuguese corpus? This decision has a massive impact on the quality of the embeddings and the validity of the results.
    *   **Handling of OOV Words:** The paper mentions using character n-grams to handle "out-of-vocabulary words" but never defines what the "vocabulary" is in this context. Is it the vocabulary of the training set for a given cross-validation fold? Or the vocabulary of the pre-trained embedding model? This ambiguity makes a crucial part of the method unclear.
3.  **Unfocused Writing and Literature Review:** The manuscript's writing could be significantly tightened. Sections 1-3 contain extensive background that could be condensed to better highlight the paper's core contributions. The literature review includes citations that are overly general (e.g., Salton, 1989 for Bag-of-Words) or thematically distant (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection), which distracts from the main topic. Furthermore, there is a contradictory tone between the optimistic introduction and the more measured conclusion, which acknowledges that distinguishing healthy subjects from those with MCI "is not easy." A more consistent narrative would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the Portuguese word embeddings? Specifically, what corpus was used for training, and was it an external, large-scale corpus or limited to the data from the described experiments?
2.  Could you please define what constitutes an "out-of-vocabulary" word in your pipeline and elaborate on how the character-level information from Bojanowski et al.'s model is integrated with the word-level co-occurrence network?
3.  The transcription norms cited (NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2) will be unfamiliar to most readers. Could you briefly explain any key aspects of these norms and how they might influence the resulting text and the network structures? Providing an English-glossed example from a Portuguese transcript would be extremely helpful for understanding the data's characteristics.
