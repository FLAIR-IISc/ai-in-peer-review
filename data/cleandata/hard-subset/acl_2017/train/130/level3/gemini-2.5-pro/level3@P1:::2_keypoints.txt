- Summary: Proposes detecting MCI from speech transcripts by building co-occurrence graphs enriched with embedding-based semantic edges, extracting topological features, and classifying on three datasets (English and Portuguese); multi-view combinations performed best.

- Contribution 1: Empirical study applying complex network features for MCI detection across three datasets, two languages, and different elicitation tasks.

- Contribution 2: A specific method to enrich word-adjacency graphs with embedding-similarity edges to make graph representations more informative for short texts.

- Strength 1: Tackles an important clinical problem and aims to avoid reliance on fragile syntactic parsing for disfluent patient speech.

- Strength 2: Novel application of complex networks to MCI detection, capturing relational (syntactic and semantic) information without parsing.

- Strength 3: Evaluation across multiple datasets, languages, and tasks increases the potential generality of findings.

- Strength 4: Comprehensive experiments exploring multiple classifiers, ensembles, and feature combinations including multi-view learning.

- Weakness 1: Experimental setup unclear—ambiguous unit of analysis (subjects vs. transcripts) and missing instance counts per class.

- Weakness 2: Lacks a strong baseline using embeddings directly (e.g., averaged document vectors) to justify the enrichment approach.

- Weakness 3: Results reported with spurious precision and no statistical significance testing given very small sample sizes.

- Weakness 4: Sole reliance on accuracy is inappropriate if class imbalance exists; missing class distributions and metrics like precision/recall/F1.

- Weakness 5: Manuscript quality suffers from grammatical errors and poor phrasing, hindering readability.

- Question 1: Clarify number of transcripts used and class counts (Control vs. MCI) for each dataset.

- Question 2: Provide precision, recall, and F1 (and class distributions) in addition to accuracy.

- Question 3: Provide results for a baseline that uses word embeddings directly (e.g., averaged vectors) to isolate the network contribution.

- Question 4: Report statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar’s test or paired t-test) to support claims of improvement.
