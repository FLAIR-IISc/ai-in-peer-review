### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes using complex networks (CN), enriched with word embeddings, to automatically classify individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) versus healthy controls based on speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs and add edges based on the cosine similarity of word embeddings to better represent short texts. They evaluate this approach on three datasets: the English DementiaBank "Cookie Theft" task, and two Portuguese datasets ("Cinderella" story retell and "ABCD" battery). The method is compared against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words models. The reported classification accuracies range from approximately 60% to 85% across different feature sets, classifiers, and datasets, with multi-view learning combinations often yielding the best results.

### **Contributions**

1.  **Primary Contribution:** The application of NLP methods for MCI detection to Brazilian Portuguese, a relatively lower-resource language in this domain. Most existing work focuses on English, so this represents a valuable effort to broaden the scope of computational cognitive assessment.
2.  **Secondary Contribution:** A demonstration that complex network features, particularly when enriched with semantic information from word embeddings, can serve as a viable alternative to traditional linguistic features that rely on parsers. This approach is potentially more robust for handling the agrammatical and disfluent speech common in clinical populations.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Important Application to a Lower-Resource Language:** The paper addresses the clinically significant problem of early MCI detection. By focusing on Portuguese, it makes a valuable contribution to a language community that is underrepresented in computational linguistics research for healthcare. This is a clear and important strength.
2.  **Novel Approach for Noisy Clinical Data:** The motivation to use complex networks as a representation that is less sensitive to the disfluencies and grammatical errors present in patient speech is well-founded. Traditional NLP pipelines relying on syntactic parsing often fail on such data, and the authors' approach provides a practical way to circumvent this dependency. The results, showing that the network-based features are competitive with or outperform linguistic features, support this argument.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets and Methods:** The authors evaluate their model on three distinct datasets with different tasks and languages. They systematically compare complex networks (CN), enriched complex networks (CNE), linguistic metrics (LM), and Bag-of-Words (BoW) using several standard classifiers. Furthermore, they explore ensemble and multi-view learning techniques, providing a thorough analysis of how different feature sets perform both individually and in combination.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Clarity and Reproducibility Issues:** The paper suffers from a lack of technical detail and transparency, making the work difficult to reproduce or fully understand.
    *   **Dataset Limitations:** The novel Portuguese datasets are extremely small (20 MCI vs. 20 controls for Cinderella; 23 MCI vs. 20 controls for ABCD) and are not publicly available. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings and prevents other researchers from building upon this work.
    *   **Ambiguous Methodology:** Key details about the methodology are either missing or unclear. It is never specified what corpus was used to train the word embeddings. Training them solely on the tiny in-domain datasets (e.g., 40 total transcripts) would be highly ineffective and lead to poor quality vectors. If an external corpus was used, it must be cited. Similarly, the paper is ambiguous about how the word-level network and the subword (n-gram) representations for "out-of-vocabulary" (OOV) words are integrated, and the definition of OOV in this context is not provided.
2.  **Poorly Focused Writing and Literature Review:** The manuscript's structure and focus could be significantly improved.
    *   **Lengthy and Unfocused Sections:** Sections 1–3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 are overly long and filled with general background information on MCI, NLP, and complex networks that could be heavily condensed. This detracts from the paper's core contributions.
    *   **Irrelevant Citations:** The literature review contains several citations that are either too general (e.g., Salton 1989 for TF-IDF) or irrelevant to the core topic (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection). These references feel like padding and should be replaced with more domain-specific citations.
3.  **Inconsistent Tone and Overstated Claims:** The paper's narrative is contradictory. The introduction presents the results optimistically, but the conclusion states that "it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments," which downplays the method's success and feels inconsistent with the earlier tone.
4.  **Numerous Technical and Typographical Errors:** The manuscript contains multiple errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading. These include grammatical mistakes ("as it a lightweight", Line 29), incorrect acronyms ("PLN" instead of NLP, Line 188), duplicated words ("certain of the the dishes", Line 264), inconsistent decimal formatting in Table 3, and a broken reference link with a duplicate DOI (Lines ~992–1002). These errors undermine the professionalism and credibility of the work.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  **Word Embedding Training:** Could you please clarify precisely what corpus was used to train the Portuguese word embeddings? Was it only the small in-domain Cinderella and ABCD datasets, or was a larger, external corpus of Portuguese used? This detail is critical for interpreting the validity of your results.
2.  **Network Construction:** How are the word-level co-occurrence network and the subword-based representations (for OOV words) formally integrated? Does this combination offer a measurable improvement in accuracy over using just one representation?
3.  **Out-of-Vocabulary Words:** Given the small size of your corpora, how did you define "out-of-vocabulary" words? Was this determined per fold during cross-validation, or against an external, pre-defined vocabulary?
4.  **Data Transparency:** To improve the paper's impact and aid reader comprehension, would it be possible to include an English-translated example of a transcript from one of the Portuguese datasets? Likewise, a figure illustrating a sample network or the vector space of key words could make your method much more concrete.
