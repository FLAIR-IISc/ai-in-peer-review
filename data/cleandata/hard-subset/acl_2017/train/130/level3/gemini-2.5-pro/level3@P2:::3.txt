### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs, which are then enriched with additional edges based on word embedding similarity to better handle short texts. Features derived from these graphs are used to train various classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against traditional linguistic features and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline. The results suggest that the complex network features, particularly when combined using multi-view learning, are effective for this classification task.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel application of complex network features for MCI detection.** This work explores a promising alternative to traditional linguistic feature engineering, which often struggles with the disfluent and agrammatical nature of speech from individuals with cognitive impairment.
2.  **An empirical evaluation across multiple languages and datasets.** The study provides results on the English DementiaBank dataset as well as two distinct Portuguese datasets (Cinderella and ABCD), lending credibility to the robustness of the proposed method.
3.  **An effective demonstration of multi-view learning for this task.** The paper shows that combining the proposed network features with linguistic and BoW features via a multi-view approach can yield significant performance improvements over using any single feature set.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and interesting application.** The use of complex networks to model speech transcripts for MCI detection is a creative and well-motivated approach. It directly addresses a known limitation of standard NLP pipelines (e.g., parsers) that are brittle when processing atypical language common in clinical populations. The method's relative independence from deep linguistic analysis makes it an attractive and scalable alternative.
2.  **Evaluation on diverse datasets.** The authors should be commended for evaluating their method on three different datasets, spanning two languages and different elicitation tasks (picture description, story retelling). This strengthens the paper's conclusions and suggests the approach may generalize well across different conditions.
3.  **Strong results from feature combination.** The paper effectively demonstrates that multi-view learning and classifier ensembles can substantially boost classification accuracy. The 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and 4.7% on the ABCD dataset are significant and highlight the complementary nature of the different feature representations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical methodological details are missing.** The paper is difficult to evaluate and reproduce due to several omissions. The multi-view learning approach is not described in sufficient detail; the exact combination scheme (e.g., hard voting, soft voting, a learned meta-classifier) must be specified. It is also unclear which corpus was used to train the word embeddings; this is a crucial detail for assessing potential domain mismatch or data leakage. Finally, the "linguistic features" are only listed in an appendix without sufficient description of how they are computed or why specific ones were chosen.
2.  **Questionable preprocessing decisions.** The decision to remove disfluencies such as filled pauses ("uh", "um"), false starts, and repetitions is a major concern. These phenomena are often key indicators of cognitive load and processing difficulty, and are widely considered to be diagnostically relevant for MCI and dementia. Removing this potentially crucial information without a strong justification is counter-intuitive and may artificially limit the performance of all models.
3.  **Lack of statistical rigor.** The paper makes several claims about its method "outperforming" others, but provides no statistical significance tests to support these conclusions. Given the relatively small datasets and modest differences in accuracy in some cases, it is essential to demonstrate that the observed improvements are statistically significant and not merely due to random variation in the data splits.
4.  **Poor clarity and presentation.** The manuscript is in need of significant editing. The abstract is too long, and many sentences are convoluted and ungrammatical, impeding comprehension (e.g., lines 29–30, 70, 147–149). Acronyms are often used without being defined (e.g., WLM, ABCD, PLN), and some citations and dataset links are missing. These issues collectively make the paper difficult to read and diminish its impact.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed description of your multi-view learning implementation? Specifically, how are the predictions from the classifiers trained on different feature sets combined?
2.  On what corpus were the word embeddings trained? If they were trained on the small, in-domain datasets, how did you mitigate the risk of poor quality or overfitting? If an external corpus was used, please specify it.
3.  Could you please justify the decision to remove disfluencies? Given their established clinical value in diagnosing cognitive impairment, have you run experiments where they are retained to see how they impact performance?
4.  Why was term frequency (TF) used for the Bag-of-Words baseline instead of the more standard TF-IDF weighting? TF-IDF is often a stronger baseline, and the surprisingly high performance of the TF-based SVM on the ABCD dataset warrants further analysis.
5.  Could you perform statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-test, bootstrap, or permutation test) on your main results to validate the claims that your proposed methods outperform the baselines?
