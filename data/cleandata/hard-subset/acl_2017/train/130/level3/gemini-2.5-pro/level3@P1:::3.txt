### Summary

This paper presents a method for identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using features derived from complex networks. The primary motivation is that traditional linguistic features, which often rely on parsers, are unreliable when applied to the disfluent and agrammatical speech common in MCI patients. The proposed approach models each transcript as a word co-occurrence network. To address the issue of sparse networks from short texts, the authors "enrich" the networks by adding edges between words with high cosine similarity in a word embedding space. The authors evaluate this approach using three datasets (one in English from DementiaBank, and two in Portuguese). They compare the performance of their Complex Network Enriched (CNE) features against traditional linguistic features and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline, using several classifiers. The results show that the CNE features are competitive, and a multi-view learning approach that combines different feature sets achieves the highest accuracy, improving over the best single classifier by up to 10%.

### Contributions

1.  **An empirical study on the utility of complex network features for MCI detection.** The primary contribution is the novel application and evaluation of complex network representations for the clinical task of identifying MCI from speech. The paper provides evidence that this graph-based approach, especially when enhanced with word embeddings, is a viable alternative to parser-dependent linguistic features, particularly for noisy, real-world transcripts.
2.  **A comparative analysis across multiple datasets and feature types.** The evaluation on three different datasets, including two in Portuguese, strengthens the paper's claims. By systematically comparing network-based features against both linguistic and BoW features, the study offers a valuable benchmark and highlights the relative strengths of each representation for this specific domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and novel application.** The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem: the early detection of cognitive decline. The rationale for using complex networks—to avoid the brittleness of syntactic parsers on disfluent speech—is compelling and well-argued. While the methods themselves (complex networks for text, word embeddings) are not new, their application to this clinical domain is a creative and promising direction.
2.  **Comprehensive experimental setup.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct datasets, varying in language (English, Portuguese) and task (picture description, story retelling). The comparison against multiple strong baselines (linguistic features via Coh-Metrix, BoW) and several standard classifiers provides a robust assessment of the proposed method's performance. The inclusion of ensemble and multi-view learning experiments further explores how different feature types can be effectively combined.
3.  **Promising results demonstrating the value of feature combination.** The experiments show that the enriched network features (CNE) are competitive and often outperform linguistic features in individual classifier settings. More importantly, the significant performance gains achieved through multi-view learning underscore a key takeaway: that different feature sets (network, linguistic, lexical) capture complementary information, and combining them is a powerful strategy for this task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of methodological clarity and reproducibility.** The paper omits several crucial details that are necessary to fully understand and reproduce the experiments. The multi-view learning approach is mentioned but never described; the exact mechanism for combining classifiers trained on different feature spaces (e.g., voting, stacking, etc.) is not specified. It is unclear what corpus was used to train the word embeddings; if it was only the small in-domain datasets, the embeddings are likely to be of poor quality. Furthermore, the set of 58 "linguistic features" for Portuguese is not adequately described, with Section 4.2.2 referring to a supplementary section that is not clearly delineated as such.
2.  **Unjustified preprocessing and experimental design choices.** Some key decisions in the experimental pipeline are not well-justified. The authors state they automatically removed disfluencies like filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions. However, these speech phenomena are often considered important clinical markers of cognitive impairment, and their removal could be discarding valuable predictive information. The decision to remove them should be strongly motivated or reconsidered. Similarly, the BoW baseline uses raw term frequency rather than the standard and typically more effective tf-idf weighting, without any justification for this choice.
3.  **Insufficient analysis and poor presentation.** The experimental results are presented without any statistical significance tests, making it difficult to determine whether the reported improvements (e.g., the 4.71% gain in Table 7) are statistically meaningful or simply due to chance. The related work section is superficial and lacks a deep engagement with prior methods. Finally, the manuscript is in need of significant editing. The abstract is too long, and numerous sentences throughout the paper are awkwardly phrased, contain grammatical errors, and impede readability. This overall lack of polish detracts from the scientific contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a precise description of your multi-view learning implementation? What specific combination scheme was used to integrate the predictions from classifiers trained on different feature views (e.g., majority vote on class predictions, averaging probabilities, a meta-classifier)?
2.  What corpus was used to train the word embeddings mentioned in Section 4.2.1? Was it trained only on the three small experimental datasets, or was a larger external corpus (like Wikipedia or a news corpus) used?
3.  The removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions) seems counter-intuitive, as these are often informative signals for diagnosing cognitive impairment. Can you provide a justification for this preprocessing step? Did you experiment with keeping them?
4.  Why was term frequency (tf) chosen for the Bag-of-Words representation instead of the more standard tf-idf weighting?
5.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate that the performance improvements from CNE over baselines, and from multi-view learning over individual models, are statistically significant?
6.  Could you provide a more detailed list or description of the 58 Coh-Metrix-Dementia features used for the Portuguese datasets?
