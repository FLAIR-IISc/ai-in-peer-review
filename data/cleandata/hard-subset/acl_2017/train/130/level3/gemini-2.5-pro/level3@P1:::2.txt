### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and then "enrich" this graph by adding new edges between semantically similar words, identified using pre-trained word embeddings. The authors argue that this approach is more robust to the agrammatical and disfluent nature of patient speech, which poses challenges for traditional NLP tools that rely on syntactic parsing. A set of topological features (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering coefficient) are extracted from these enriched graphs and used to train various machine learning classifiers (SVM, KNN, Random Forest). The method is evaluated on three datasets—one in English (Cookie Theft) and two in Portuguese (Cinderella, Arizona Battery)—and compared against baselines using linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The results suggest that the complex network features are competitive and, in some cases, outperform the baselines, with multi-view learning (combining feature sets) yielding the best performance.

### Contributions
1.  **An Empirical Study of Graph-Based Text Representation for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application and evaluation of complex network features for the clinical task of identifying MCI from speech transcripts. The study is conducted across three distinct datasets, covering two languages and different elicitation tasks, which provides valuable evidence for the potential of this feature representation in a domain where text is often noisy and short.

2.  **A Method for Enriching Co-occurrence Networks for Short Texts:** The paper proposes a specific technique to augment standard word adjacency graphs with edges based on word embedding similarity. This is motivated as a way to make graph-based representations more informative for short texts, which are common in clinical assessments and might otherwise produce sparse, linear graphs with limited descriptive power.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses an Important and Challenging Clinical Problem:** The automatic detection of MCI is a significant clinical goal, as early diagnosis can improve patient outcomes. The paper tackles a known challenge in this area: the difficulty of applying standard NLP pipelines to the disfluent and often ungrammatical speech produced by patients. The motivation to find representations that are less dependent on high-quality parsing is well-founded and compelling.

2.  **Novel Application of Complex Networks:** While complex networks have been used in other NLP tasks, their application to MCI detection from speech transcripts appears novel. The method provides an alternative feature engineering paradigm that captures relational information between words (both syntactic adjacency and semantic similarity) without relying on fragile parsing tools. This is a well-suited approach for the target domain.

3.  **Evaluation across Multiple Datasets and Languages:** The authors validate their approach on three different datasets, which strengthens the paper's conclusions. The inclusion of both English and Portuguese data, as well as transcripts of varying lengths and from different tasks (picture description, story recall), suggests that the findings may have some level of generality.

4.  **Thorough Exploration of Classifiers and Feature Combinations:** The experimental setup is reasonably comprehensive. The authors test four different classification algorithms and explore both ensemble methods and multi-view learning to combine their proposed features with more traditional ones. This provides a more complete picture of the proposed features' utility, both in isolation and in combination with other information sources.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Clarity in the Experimental Setup and Unit of Analysis:** A major weakness is the ambiguity regarding the machine learning problem definition. The paper discusses numbers of *subjects* but classification is presumably performed on *transcripts*. The tables do not clearly state the number of instances (transcripts) per class for each experiment. For example, the ABCD dataset is described as having 23 MCI subjects producing 46 narratives and 20 controls producing 39. Are these 85 narratives treated as independent instances for classification? This lack of clarity makes it difficult to interpret the results and assess the severity of the class imbalance.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for the Proposed Enrichment Method:** The motivation for enriching co-occurrence graphs is to handle short texts. However, the paper lacks a strong baseline to demonstrate the value of this specific enrichment strategy. A crucial missing comparison is against a model that uses the word embeddings directly as features (e.g., by averaging them to form a document vector). Such a baseline is simple, strong, and would directly test whether the complex network formalization adds value beyond the semantic information already present in the embeddings themselves.

3.  **Flawed and Potentially Misleading Reporting of Results:** The results are reported with a degree of precision (e.g., 0.7547 accuracy) that is not meaningful given the very small size of the datasets (e.g., N=40 for the Cinderella dataset). Furthermore, no statistical significance testing is performed. With small sample sizes, the observed differences in performance between models could easily be due to random chance. Without statistical tests, claims that one method "outperforms" another are unsubstantiated.

4.  **Inappropriate Evaluation Metric:** The paper relies exclusively on accuracy as the evaluation metric. The number of subjects listed in the dataset descriptions suggests a potential class imbalance. Accuracy is a poor metric for imbalanced classification problems, as a classifier can achieve high accuracy by simply predicting the majority class. The authors should report the class distribution for each dataset and provide more informative metrics such as F1-score, precision, and recall.

5.  **Poor Manuscript Quality:** The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors, spelling mistakes, and awkward phrasing that significantly hinder readability and comprehension. This lack of polish makes it difficult to follow the technical details and detracts from the overall professionalism of the submission, suggesting it was not carefully proofread before submission.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Please clarify the exact number of instances (transcripts) used for classification in each of the three datasets, as well as the number of instances per class (Control vs. MCI).
2.  Given the potential for class imbalance, could you please report precision, recall, and F1-score for your experiments in addition to accuracy?
3.  To better isolate the contribution of the complex network structure, could you provide results for a baseline that uses the word embeddings directly as features (e.g., by averaging word vectors to get a single feature vector per transcript)?
4.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar's test or a paired t-test on cross-validation folds) to support the claims of improvement between different feature sets and models? This seems critical given the small dataset sizes.
