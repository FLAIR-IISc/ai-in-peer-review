**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using features derived from complex networks. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs and enrich them by adding edges between semantically similar words, identified via word embeddings. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) against linguistic and Bag-of-Words features, using several classifiers.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical evaluation of a graph-based feature representation for the clinical task of MCI detection across multiple, linguistically diverse datasets.
2.  A demonstration that this graph-based approach, particularly when enriched with semantic information from word embeddings, can achieve competitive performance compared to standard NLP feature sets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clinically Relevant Problem and Datasets:** The paper addresses the important and challenging task of early MCI detection. The use of established clinical datasets like DementiaBank, alongside newly presented Portuguese data, grounds the research in a practical and relevant context.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation by comparing their proposed graph-based features against two strong baseline feature sets (linguistic and Bag-of-Words). The assessment across multiple datasets and various standard classification algorithms provides a solid comparative analysis of the method's utility.
3.  **Alternative Feature Representation:** The proposed graph-based method offers an interesting alternative to traditional NLP pipelines that often rely on syntactic parsers. This is particularly valuable for analyzing clinical speech, which is frequently disfluent and agrammatical, making parser-dependent feature extraction unreliable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Reporting:** The manuscript's description of the experimental setup is ambiguous and inconsistent. For example, the number of subjects and the number of narratives are confounded in the dataset descriptions (e.g., Section 4.1.3), making it unclear what the unit of classification is (a subject or a narrative). This fundamental ambiguity severely hinders the interpretability and reproducibility of the results.
2.  **Insufficient Justification and Analysis:** The motivation for enriching the co-occurrence graph with similarity-based edges is not well-justified against simpler alternatives, such as directly averaging word embeddings. Furthermore, the results are reported with a misleadingly high level of precision (e.g., 0.8018 accuracy) on very small datasets without any statistical significance testing. It is impossible to determine whether the reported performance gains are meaningful or simply due to random variation.
3.  **Inappropriate Evaluation Metric and Poor Readability:** The authors do not report the class distribution for their datasets, making accuracy a potentially misleading metric; F1-score should be reported. Finally, the manuscript's quality is low, with numerous grammatical errors that impede comprehension and detract from the paper's scientific credibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact number of instances (and their class distribution) for each dataset? What is the fundamental unit of classification—a subject or a transcript?
2.  Can you provide F1-scores for your experiments to account for any potential class imbalance?
3.  Have you performed statistical tests (e.g., t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) to confirm that the observed improvements from your proposed method are statistically significant?
