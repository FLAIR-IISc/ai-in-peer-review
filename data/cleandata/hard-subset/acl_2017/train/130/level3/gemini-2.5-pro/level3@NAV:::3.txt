### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for the task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model texts as word co-occurrence networks, add semantic edges based on embedding similarity, and use the network's topological properties as features for classification. The primary contribution is an empirical study applying this representation to the MCI detection problem on three datasets, providing an informative comparison against traditional linguistic and Bag-of-Words features. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that enriching these networks with semantic information from embeddings can improve performance on the short texts typical of clinical assessments.

### Strengths

1.  **Clinically Relevant Problem and Well-Described Domain:** The paper addresses the important challenge of early MCI detection. The problem, its clinical context, and the datasets used are well-motivated and described, making the study interesting and accessible. The application of complex network analysis to this specific domain is a novel contribution.

2.  **Sound Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is conducted across three different datasets, including texts in both English and Portuguese, which strengthens the generalizability of the findings. Comparing the proposed method against established baselines like linguistic features and Bag-of-Words provides a solid foundation for assessing its performance.

3.  **Promising Preliminary Results:** The reported results suggest that complex network features are a competitive alternative to linguistic features, particularly when enriched with word embeddings. The exploration of multi-view learning, which combines different feature sets, shows potential, achieving the highest accuracy on two of the three datasets.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Methodological Omissions:** The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Key components are inadequately explained, including the specific multi-view learning scheme used, the full set of "linguistic features," and the corpus used to train the word embeddings (the text implies it was the small experimental datasets, which would be a major limitation). These details are essential for validating the work.

2.  **Unjustified Preprocessing and Baseline Choices:** The decision to remove disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, repetitions) is highly questionable, as these are often important clinical markers for MCI. This choice must be rigorously justified or reconsidered. Similarly, the use of term-frequency over the standard tf-idf for the Bag-of-Words baseline is not explained and may weaken the baseline comparison.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis and Poor Presentation:** The conclusions lack statistical rigor, as no significance tests are reported to validate performance differences between models. The related work section is superficial, missing direct comparisons to prior state-of-the-art methods. The manuscript itself requires considerable editing for clarity and readability; the abstract is overly long, numerous sentences are confusing, and many terms, acronyms, and dataset citations are missing. These presentation issues severely hinder the paper's impact.
