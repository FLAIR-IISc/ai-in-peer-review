- Proposes detecting MCI from speech transcripts using word co-occurrence graphs enriched with embedding-similarity edges, extracting graph features for classification across three datasets.  
- Shows graph-based features can be competitive with or superior to traditional linguistic features and BoW, claiming improved robustness to disfluent patient speech.

- Addresses an important clinical problem (automatic MCI screening from speech) with clear potential impact.  
- Graph-based representation is well-motivated for noisy, agrammatical patient speech.  
- Evaluation on three diverse datasets (English DementiaBank and two Portuguese corpora) strengthens claims of generalizability.

- Experimental setup and problem definition are unclear: ambiguous instance definition (subjects vs. narratives) and inconsistent data counts across tables and text.  
- Manuscript has poor writing quality with many typos and grammatical errors that impede comprehension.

- Evaluation metrics and reporting are flawed: reporting only accuracy is insufficient for small/imbalanced datasets.  
- Reported precision (many decimal places) is unjustified given small test folds, and no statistical significance tests were performed.

- Weak motivation for the graph enrichment: unclear why adding embedding-based edges is preferable to simpler embedding-based document representations (e.g., averaged vectors).  
- Critical hyperparameter (embedding similarity threshold) and its optimization process are not described, harming reproducibility.

- Question: Clarify what constitutes a single classification instance for each dataset and provide exact instance counts per class.  
- Question: Explain the motivation for graph enrichment versus direct embedding-based document representations.  
- Question: Provide class-wise precision/recall/F1 and report statistical significance tests for claimed performance differences.
