- Proposes a novel graph-based representation for MCI detection using word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-based semantic edges.  
- Evaluated on three datasets (English + two Portuguese) and shows complex-network features are competitive with or sometimes better than standard linguistic and BoW baselines.  
- Addresses an important clinical problem: automated MCI screening from speech transcripts.  
- Method shows novelty by combining structural co-occurrence networks with semantic embedding similarity to handle short/noisy clinical texts.  
- Evaluation across multiple datasets and languages strengthens claims of generalizability and includes comparisons to Coh-Metrix and BoW baselines.  
- Experimental setup and reporting lack clarity: ambiguous instance definition and unclear handling of multiple narratives per subject in cross-validation.  
- Dataset sizes are small (e.g., 40 subjects in Cinderella), limiting the strength of conclusions.  
- Writing quality and presentation need substantial revision due to grammatical errors and awkward phrasing.  
- Evaluation relies solely on accuracy; precision, recall, and F1 for each class are not reported.  
- Results are reported with unjustified numeric precision and no statistical significance tests to validate differences.  
- Methodological justification is weak: insufficient explanation for hybrid co-occurrence+similarity design and no ablation vs. alternatives (e.g., similarity-only k-NN graphs).  
- Question: clarify exact number of classification instances per class and how narratives from the same participant were kept in the same CV fold.  
- Question: provide class-wise precision/recall/F1 in addition to accuracy.  
- Question: report statistical significance tests comparing CNE to baselines (e.g., paired t-test, McNemar).  
- Question: justify combining co-occurrence with similarity edges and report experiments comparing the hybrid model to a pure similarity-based graph.
