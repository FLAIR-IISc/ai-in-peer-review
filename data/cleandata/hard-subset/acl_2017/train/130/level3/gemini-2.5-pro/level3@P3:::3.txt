### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes using complex network representations of speech transcripts to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors model each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and enrich it with additional edges based on semantic similarity from pre-trained word embeddings. From these graphs, they extract a set of topological features (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering coefficient) and use them to train various machine learning classifiers (SVM, k-NN, etc.). The proposed method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against baselines using linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) representation. The results suggest that the enriched complex network features are competitive with, and sometimes outperform, the linguistic features. The authors also show that combining feature sets using multi-view learning can further improve classification accuracy.

### Contributions
1.  **Application of Enriched Network Features to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex network features, enhanced with word embedding-based semantic links, to the clinical task of MCI detection from speech. While the constituent methods are not new, their combination and application in this domain to circumvent issues with parsing disfluent speech is a valuable exploration.
2.  **Comparative Analysis on Multiple Datasets:** The paper presents a useful comparative study on three distinct datasets, including two in Portuguese, which is underrepresented in this area of research. This evaluation provides insights into the robustness of the network-based approach compared to more standard linguistic and BoW features across different languages and narrative tasks.

### Strengths
1.  **Interesting and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper addresses the important clinical challenge of early MCI detection using automated language analysis. The motivation to use representations that are robust to the agrammatical and disfluent nature of patient speech is compelling and highly relevant. The study provides an informative exploration of an alternative to traditional parsing-based feature extraction.
2.  **Sound Core Methodology:** The central idea of modeling text as a graph is well-established, and the strategy of enriching a sparse co-occurrence network with semantic edges from word embeddings to better handle short texts is a reasonable and sound approach. The experimental setup, which compares the proposed features against standard baselines across multiple datasets and classifiers, provides a solid framework for evaluation.
3.  **Promising Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed complex network features are a viable method for this task, achieving performance comparable or superior to a large set of linguistic features. The finding that enriched networks (CNE) consistently outperform basic networks (CN) validates the core technical contribution. Furthermore, the demonstrated gains from multi-view learning highlight a promising direction for integrating different sources of information.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Critical Methodological Details are Missing:** The paper's reproducibility and the validity of its conclusions are undermined by a lack of crucial details.
    *   **Multi-view Learning:** The multi-view learning approach is inadequately described. The paper mentions combining classifiers via "majority vote" in the results section, but the methodology section (4.3) is generic. It is unclear which classifier was trained on each feature view and how exactly the voting was implemented. This must be specified.
    *   **Linguistic Features:** The "Linguistic Features" baseline is poorly defined. Section 4.2.2 and Appendix A.2 list categories of features from Coh-Metrix but do not specify which of the 106 (English) or 58 (Portuguese) features were actually used. A clear description or a link to a supplementary file with the full feature list is necessary for this baseline to be meaningful.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** It is not stated which corpus was used to train the word embeddings. Section 4.2.1 implies they were trained for this work, but the data source is omitted. Training on the small target datasets would be problematic, while using a large external corpus would be standard. This is a fundamental detail that significantly impacts the interpretation of the results.

2.  **Unjustified Preprocessing and Baseline Choices:** Several methodological decisions are questionable and lack justification.
    *   **Disfluency Removal:** The authors remove filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions. This is a significant concern, as a large body of research has identified these exact phenomena as powerful indicators of cognitive impairment. While this may help traditional parsers, the motivation for the network approach was to *avoid* reliance on clean, grammatical text. This decision seems counter-intuitive and must be rigorously justified or reconsidered.
    *   **Bag-of-Words Baseline:** The BoW baseline uses term frequency (tf) rather than the standard and typically more effective tf-idf weighting. This potentially weakens the BoW baseline, making the proposed method appear stronger in comparison. A justification for this choice is required.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis and Poor Presentation:**
    *   **No Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports percentage improvements in accuracy without any statistical tests of significance. Given the small dataset sizes, it is impossible to determine whether the observed differences between methods are meaningful or simply due to chance. This is a major omission in an empirical paper.
    *   **Superficial Related Work:** The related work section lists previous studies but fails to discuss their methodologies in sufficient detail. This makes it difficult to understand the precise novelty of the current work in the context of prior art.
    *   **Clarity and Readability:** The manuscript requires substantial editing. The abstract is too long and dense. Many sentences are convoluted and unclear (e.g., lines 34–38, 147–149, 323–332). Acronyms (e.g., WML, PLN) are used without definition, and crucial citations for transcription norms and specific dataset versions are missing.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the exact corpus used to train the word embeddings?
2.  Can you provide a strong justification for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions, etc.), given their established importance as markers for cognitive decline?
3.  Could you explain the rationale for using term frequency (tf) instead of the more standard tf-idf weighting for the Bag-of-Words baseline?
4.  Please specify the exact implementation of your multi-view learning model. For a given multi-view experiment (e.g., CNE-LM), which classifiers were trained on each view, and how was the majority vote performed?
5.  Do you plan to add statistical significance tests to the final version to support your claims of performance improvements?
