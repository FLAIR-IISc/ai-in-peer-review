**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for automatically detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and enrich this structure with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from pre-trained word embeddings. This approach is designed to be robust to the short and often agrammatical nature of patient speech, which poses a challenge for traditional NLP methods relying on syntactic parsing. The authors evaluate their method on three datasets—one in English (Cookie Theft) and two in Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella and ABCD)—using several machine learning classifiers. They compare the performance of features derived from these enriched complex networks (CNE) against standard linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The results indicate that the CNE features, especially when combined with other feature sets in ensemble or multi-view learning settings, outperform the baseline methods, achieving a top accuracy of 80% on the ABCD dataset.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Graph-Based Representation for Clinical Text Analysis:** The primary contribution is the application and evaluation of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for MCI detection. This method offers a compelling alternative to syntax-dependent linguistic features, as it is better suited to handle the disfluencies and grammatical errors common in the speech of individuals with cognitive impairment. The enrichment with embeddings helps to mitigate data sparsity issues inherent in the short texts produced during clinical assessments.
2.  **Application and Evaluation on a Less-Resourced Language:** The study makes a valuable contribution by applying modern NLP techniques for MCI detection to Brazilian Portuguese. By collecting and analyzing two Portuguese datasets, the authors expand the scope of this research beyond English and provide a benchmark for a language with fewer available resources for clinical NLP.
3.  **Comparative Analysis of Feature Sets:** The paper provides a systematic comparison between the proposed network-based features, traditional linguistic metrics (from Coh-Metrix), and BoW. The finding that multi-view learning, which combines classifiers trained on different feature spaces, yields significant performance improvements is an important practical result for the field.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Creative Methodology:** The proposal to use complex networks enriched with semantic information from word embeddings is a novel and well-motivated approach for this task. The authors correctly identify that traditional linguistic features derived from parsers are brittle when applied to the non-canonical language found in MCI patient transcripts. A graph-based model that does not rely on strict syntactic correctness is a clever way to circumvent this limitation.
2.  **Focus on a High-Impact Clinical Application:** The automatic detection of MCI is a clinically significant problem, as early diagnosis can lead to better patient outcomes. Applying NLP to develop non-invasive, scalable screening tools is a research direction with high potential for societal impact, and this paper makes a solid contribution to this effort.
3.  **Evaluation across Multiple Datasets and Languages:** The evaluation on three distinct datasets, including two in Portuguese, strengthens the paper's findings. This demonstrates that the approach is not tailored to a single corpus or language and highlights its potential for broader applicability. The inclusion of Portuguese is particularly commendable, as it helps to address the linguistic imbalance in computational health research.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility:** Several crucial details about the experimental setup are missing, which hinders the reproducibility of the work. It is unclear how the word embeddings were trained—specifically, whether they were trained only on the small in-domain clinical datasets or on a larger external corpus. This is a critical detail, as the quality and source of embeddings would heavily influence the results, especially for Portuguese. The paper also fails to clearly explain how the subword information (character n-grams) is used to handle "out-of-vocabulary" words and how it interacts with the word-level graph representation.
2.  **Small Dataset Sizes and Limited Generalizability:** The Portuguese datasets are very small (e.g., the Cinderella dataset contains only 20 MCI patients and 20 controls). While acknowledging the difficulty of obtaining clinical data, results from such small samples are susceptible to high variance and may not generalize well. The reported performance gains, while promising, should be interpreted with caution, as it is unclear if they are statistically significant. A discussion of this limitation and its implications would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Clarity and Organization of the Manuscript:** The paper's presentation could be significantly improved. The introduction and related work sections are verbose and contain several citations to general NLP topics (e.g., pedestrian detection, sentiment classification, Salton 1989) that are not directly relevant and could be removed to sharpen the focus. In contrast, important details, such as an explanation of the Portuguese transcription norms (NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2), are omitted. The text also contains several typographical errors (e.g., "as it a lightweight", "PLN tools") that detract from the overall quality.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the training protocol for the word embeddings? Were they trained using only the transcripts from the experimental datasets, or was a larger external corpus (e.g., a web crawl or Wikipedia dump) used for both English and Portuguese?
2.  The paper mentions using a subword model to generate embeddings for out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. Could you please define what constitutes an OOV word in your experiments? For instance, are these words not present in the pre-trained embedding model, or words not seen during training on the clinical data?
3.  Given the small sample sizes of the datasets, have you performed any statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar's test or bootstrapping) to validate that the observed accuracy improvements of your proposed CNE and multi-view models over the baselines are statistically meaningful?
