

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes using complex network features enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, with a focus on Portuguese language. The authors model transcripts as complex networks and enrich them with semantic information to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. The main contributions claimed are: 1) modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings, 2) demonstrating that this approach outperforms linguistic features for MCI detection, and 3) showing that ensemble/multi-view learning can improve accuracy.

The strongest contribution is the study of NLP for detecting cognitive impairment in Portuguese, a lower-resource language. This addresses an important gap in the literature, as most previous work has focused on English.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application to a lower-resource language**: The paper's focus on Portuguese is significant, as most MCI detection work has been conducted in English. This expands NLP applications to under-resourced languages and has potential clinical relevance for Portuguese-speaking populations.

2. **Creative technical approach**: The idea of using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to handle speech disfluencies is theoretically interesting. The authors correctly identify that traditional linguistic features suffer from parsing errors in disfluent speech, and their network approach may provide a more robust alternative.

3. **Practical motivation**: The paper addresses a genuine clinical need for automated MCI detection systems that don't require manual transcription correction, which is time-consuming and expensive in clinical settings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Extremely limited dataset**: The study uses only 20 MCI patients and 20 controls (total 40 subjects), which is far too small for meaningful machine learning evaluation. This severely limits the statistical significance of the results and the ability to generalize the findings. The claimed accuracy range (60-85%) is too broad to draw meaningful conclusions from such a small sample.

2. **Critical technical ambiguities**: The paper lacks essential details about how word embeddings were trained (whether using only the study's datasets or external corpora), how word-level and subword networks interact, and the precise definition of "out-of-vocabulary" words. These omissions make the method difficult to reproduce and assess.

3. **Contradictory tone and overclaiming**: The introduction is overly optimistic ("complex networks are suitable to detect MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets"), while the conclusion downplays the results ("it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments"). This contradiction undermines the paper's credibility.

4. **Poorly structured and written**: Sections 1-3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 are unnecessarily long, with important details buried in excessive text. The literature review contains irrelevant citations (e.g., Salton 1989, pedestrian detection), and the paper contains multiple technical/typographical errors that suggest poor proofreading.

5. **Lack of methodological transparency**: The paper fails to provide an English-translated transcript example, sample network/embedding outputs, or clear explanation of Portuguese transcription norms (NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2), limiting reproducibility and understanding.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify how the word embeddings were trained? Specifically, did you use only the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or did you leverage external corpora to train the embeddings?

2. Given the small dataset size (20 MCI + 20 controls), how did you address concerns about statistical significance and potential overfitting?

3. Could you provide an example of a Portuguese transcript with both the original text and the network representation to illustrate your method?

4. In Table 5, linguistic features (LM) outperform CNE for some classifiers in the Cinderella dataset. How do you reconcile this with your claim that "complex networks are suitable to detect MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets"?

5. Could you clarify the definition and source of "out-of-vocabulary" words mentioned in Section 4.2.1?

This paper has potential value in applying NLP to MCI detection in lower-resource languages, but the extremely small dataset, technical ambiguities, contradictory tone, and lack of methodological transparency significantly undermine the contribution. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
