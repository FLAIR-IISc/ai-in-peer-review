

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network representations enriched with word embeddings. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information derived from word embeddings before extracting topological features for classification. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. Application of complex network modeling to MCI detection from speech transcripts, which is a novel approach in this specific clinical domain.
2. Demonstration that topological features of enriched networks outperform traditional linguistic features for MCI detection across three datasets.
3. Exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning approaches to combine different feature spaces for improved classification performance.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a clinically relevant problem with clear societal impact. Early detection of MCI is crucial for timely intervention, and the proposed automated approach could potentially scale to large populations, addressing a significant limitation in current clinical practice.

2. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across three distinct datasets (English and Portuguese) from different clinical settings, which enhances the generalizability of the findings. The results show consistent improvements over traditional linguistic features in all datasets, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.

3. The authors make a reasonable effort to address the challenge of disfluencies in speech transcripts by avoiding manual preprocessing steps, which is a practical consideration for large-scale implementation. The use of complex networks as a language-independent representation is particularly valuable for non-English languages where NLP tools are less developed.

## Weaknesses

1. The manuscript suffers from poor writing quality with numerous grammar and spelling errors throughout, significantly hindering readability and comprehension. This is a serious issue for a publication venue like ACL, where clear communication is paramount.

2. The problem definition and dataset descriptions are confusing and inconsistent. The paper reports participant counts at the study level rather than transcript level, and the tables present inconsistent counts (e.g., Table 1 shows 43 MCI and 21 possible AD, but the text states 43 MCI from 326 AD patients). This lack of clarity makes it difficult to assess the experimental setup and results.

3. The motivation for the specific graph enrichment approach is unclear. The paper doesn't adequately justify why the proposed enrichment method (adding edges based on cosine similarity) is superior to simpler alternatives like directly using word embeddings or other semantic similarity measures. This weakens the novelty claim.

4. The evaluation metrics are problematic. The paper reports accuracy without addressing class imbalance (which is present in all datasets), making the reported performance potentially misleading. F1 scores or balanced accuracy should have been reported, especially given the small dataset sizes. The precision of reported results (e.g., 80.18% accuracy) on small datasets (ABCD has 43 MCI and 40 control narratives) is unjustified and lacks statistical significance testing.

5. The paper fails to leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools that could have enhanced the approach. For example, it doesn't incorporate specialized tools for clinical language analysis or leverage existing knowledge about language impairments in MCI patients, which would have strengthened the clinical relevance of the work.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact relationship between participant counts and transcript counts in each dataset? The paper reports participant numbers but doesn't specify how many transcripts were generated per participant (e.g., in the ABCD dataset, 23 MCI subjects produced 46 narratives - does this mean each subject provided two narratives?).

2. Why was the linear SVM on BoW features (75.47% accuracy) reported as "somewhat surprising" when the paper states that BoW features are generally weaker than network features? This seems contradictory to the main claims of the paper.

3. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the reported performance improvements (e.g., the 10% improvement in Cinderella dataset), especially given the small sample sizes?

4. How does your approach compare to simpler methods that directly use word embeddings (without the network structure) for the same task? The paper claims network features are superior but doesn't provide this comparison.

5. Given the small dataset sizes, how confident are you that the observed improvements (e.g., 4.71% in ABCD) aren't due to chance? Could you report confidence intervals for the accuracy results?
