

**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network features enriched with word embeddings, applied to speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments. The primary contribution is the demonstration that complex network representations, particularly when enriched with semantic information through word embeddings, provide a robust, language-independent method for MCI detection that handles disfluencies better than traditional linguistic features. The second contribution is the successful application of this approach to Portuguese speech transcripts, where traditional NLP tools would struggle due to the lack of robust parsers for the language. The third contribution is the demonstration that combining network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features through multi-view learning yields significant improvements in classification accuracy (up to 10% in the Cinderella dataset).

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in healthcare diagnostics where language impairment is an important marker for MCI but is frequently undervalued due to challenges in processing disfluent patient speech. The approach of modeling transcripts as complex networks that can handle disfluencies without manual correction is a significant practical contribution to the field of computational linguistics applied to healthcare.

2. The results demonstrate that the network-based approach outperforms traditional linguistic features across all three datasets (including Portuguese datasets where linguistic features would be particularly challenging to extract), with the enriched network approach achieving up to 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset. The multi-view learning approach that combines network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features shows particularly promising results, with up to 10% improvement over the best individual classifier.

3. The paper makes a valuable contribution to low-resource language processing by successfully applying NLP techniques to Portuguese speech transcripts without requiring manual transcription correction. This is significant because Portuguese is a lower-resource language with limited robust NLP tools, making traditional approaches impractical for large-scale assessments.

**Weaknesses**

1. The datasets used are extremely small (e.g., 20 MCI + 20 controls for the Cinderella dataset), which severely limits the statistical power of the results and makes generalizability questionable. This small sample size is particularly problematic for a medical diagnostic application where robustness is critical.

2. The paper lacks sufficient technical details about the word embedding training process. It's unclear whether embeddings were trained on external corpora or just the study's datasets, which is crucial for reproducibility. The paper also fails to clarify the definition of "out-of-vocabulary" words and how this impacted the results.

3. The manuscript contains numerous technical and typographical errors that undermine its credibility (e.g., "as it a lightweight" on line 29, "PLN" instead of "NLP" on line 188, duplicate words, incorrect decimal formatting in Table 3, and formatting errors in references), which suggests insufficient attention to detail.

4. The literature review is unnecessarily long and contains irrelevant citations (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection, Salton 1989), which distracts from the core contributions and makes it difficult to assess the paper's positioning in the field.

5. The paper exhibits contradictory tone - it's overly optimistic in the introduction about the results but downplays their significance in the conclusion, stating that "it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments" despite reporting up to 80% accuracy.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Please clarify how the word embeddings were trained - were they trained on external corpora or just the study's datasets? If external, which ones were used, and how did you handle out-of-vocabulary words?

2. Could you provide a clear definition of "out-of-vocabulary" words in this context and explain how their handling affected the network enrichment process?

3. Please elaborate on the NURC/SP transcription norms (N. 338 EF and 331 D2) used for the Portuguese datasets and how they compare to standard transcription practices.
