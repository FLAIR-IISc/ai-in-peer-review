

**Review**

This paper proposes a graph-based approach using word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors evaluate their method across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), reporting improved classification performance compared to linguistic features alone, with multi-view learning achieving up to 10% improvement in one dataset.

**Main Contributions:**  
1. Application of complex network features to MCI detection from speech transcripts (novel in this context, though the graph methodology itself is not new).  
2. Multi-view learning approach combining different feature spaces (network, linguistic, BoW) for improved classification.  
3. Evaluation across three diverse datasets representing different languages (English/Portuguese) and text lengths.

**Strengths:**  
1. The clinical problem addressed is highly relevant, as early MCI detection could significantly impact patient care.  
2. The multi-view learning approach demonstrates practical improvement (up to 10%) over individual classifiers in one dataset.  
3. The paper uses multiple datasets, providing some evidence of cross-dataset applicability.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Poor writing quality** with numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology throughout the manuscript.  
2. **Unclear problem definition**: Dataset descriptions report study-level statistics (e.g., participant demographics) rather than transcript-level characteristics, with inconsistent reporting of sample sizes and class distributions.  
3. **Lack of justification** for graph enrichment over simpler word-similarity approaches; no comparison to standard word embedding techniques.  
4. **Inappropriate metrics**: Accuracy is reported without addressing class imbalance (e.g., ABCD dataset has 23 MCI vs. 20 controls), and precision/recall/F1 scores are omitted.  
5. **Overstated results**: Accuracy values are reported with two decimal places for small datasets (e.g., 20 MCI/20 controls), with no statistical significance testing for performance differences.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. Why was the graph enrichment approach necessary when standard word embedding similarity measures could have been applied directly?  
2. How do you account for the class imbalance in your datasets, and why wasn't F1-score or balanced accuracy reported?  
3. What statistical tests were performed to confirm the significance of the 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset?
