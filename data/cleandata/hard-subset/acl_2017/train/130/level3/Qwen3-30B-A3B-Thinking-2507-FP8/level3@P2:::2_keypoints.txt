- Novel approach: use complex network representations enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI from speech transcripts.  
- Model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks, enrich with embedding-based semantic edges, and extract topological features for classification.  
- Demonstrate enriched network features outperform traditional linguistic features and explore ensemble/multi-view learning across three datasets.  
- Clinical relevance: addresses early MCI detection with potential for scalable, population-level screening.  
- Evaluation across three datasets (English and Portuguese) strengthens generalizability and shows consistent improvements.  
- Practical handling of disfluencies and language-independent representation useful for low-resource/non-English settings.  
- Poor writing quality with many grammar and spelling errors that hinder readability and suitability for ACL.  
- Confusing/inconsistent problem and dataset descriptions, with unclear participant vs. transcript counts and inconsistent table numbers.  
- Unclear motivation for the specific graph enrichment method; no strong justification vs. simpler alternatives like direct embeddings or other similarity measures.  
- Problematic evaluation: reports accuracy despite class imbalance, lacks F1/balanced accuracy and statistical significance testing; over-precise numbers unjustified on small datasets.  
- Fails to leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools or incorporate clinical knowledge of MCI-related language impairments.  
- Question: clarify participant-to-transcript mapping for each dataset (e.g., narratives per subject).  
- Question: explain why linear SVM on BoW was described as "somewhat surprising" given claims that BoW is weaker.  
- Question: provide statistical significance tests for reported improvements, especially large gains on small datasets.  
- Question: compare the proposed network+embedding approach to simpler methods using word embeddings without network structure.  
- Question: report confidence intervals and quantify how likely observed improvements are due to chance given small sample sizes.
