

# Review for ACL 2017 Paper: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a graph-based method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim this approach outperforms linguistic features in three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery). As a reviewer, I identify three contributions:

1. The application of complex network features to MCI detection from speech transcripts represents the primary contribution, as it introduces a novel approach to a clinically relevant problem.
2. The evaluation across three diverse datasets (English and Portuguese) demonstrates the method's potential generalizability.
3. The use of graph-derived features (lexical, syntactic, etc.) provides an alternative to traditional linguistic features for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. **Clinically Relevant Problem**: The paper addresses an important clinical problem (MCI detection for early dementia diagnosis), which is well-motivated by the literature. Early detection of MCI can lead to timely intervention and better outcomes, making the research impactful.

2. **Multiple Dataset Evaluation**: The authors evaluate their method on three distinct datasets from different languages (English and Portuguese), demonstrating the approach's potential to generalize across different linguistic contexts. This multi-dataset evaluation is a significant strength that adds credibility to the approach.

3. **Novel Feature Representation**: The use of complex network features as a representation for speech transcripts is a novel approach in the MCI detection domain, offering an alternative to traditional linguistic features that are sensitive to disfluencies in patient speech.

## Weaknesses

1. **Unclear Methodology and Motivation**: The paper fails to convincingly explain why graph-based methods are superior to simpler word-node similarity approaches. The motivation for graph enrichment versus direct word embedding similarity is unclear, and the paper does not provide a clear comparison with simpler baselines that would establish the necessity of their complex network approach.

2. **Inadequate Evaluation Metrics and Reporting**: The paper reports accuracy without addressing class imbalance, which is critical for medical diagnosis tasks. The class distribution across datasets is not clearly presented (e.g., Table 1 shows 43 MCI and 21+29 AD for Cookie Theft, but not clear control vs. MCI split), making accuracy a misleading metric. The paper should report F1 scores and class balance metrics to properly evaluate performance on imbalanced medical datasets.

3. **Poor Writing Quality and Methodological Ambiguity**: The manuscript suffers from poor grammar and spelling, which undermines the clarity of the technical contributions. More critically, the description of datasets (e.g., "transcribed samples, manually segmented of the DementiaBank") is inconsistent with the reported data (Table 1 shows "43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD" - this is not a standard control vs. MCI split). The reported precision (e.g., "80.18% accuracy") on small datasets (e.g., ABCD has 43 MCI and 40 controls) is unjustified and lacks statistical significance testing.

4. **Lack of Domain-Specific NLP Consideration**: The paper does not leverage existing biomedical NLP tools or resources, which are critical for clinical language analysis. For example, the authors mention that Portuguese lacks robust parsers but don't use any domain-specific resources that could address this limitation.

5. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison**: The paper claims their method "outperforms linguistic features in all datasets" but fails to provide a clear comparison with strong baselines that would establish the novelty of their approach. The comparison with simpler word embedding approaches is missing.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the class distribution (control vs. MCI) in each dataset, particularly for the Cookie Theft dataset where the description states "43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD" but the evaluation appears to compare MCI to controls?

2. Why did the authors choose to use accuracy as the primary metric without addressing class imbalance, and how would the results change if F1-score or other balanced metrics were reported?

3. Could you provide a comparison with simpler approaches (e.g., using word embeddings directly without graph enrichment) to establish the necessity of the graph-based method?

4. The paper mentions "the results show that complex networks are suitable to detect MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these claims. How were the statistical significance of the improvements established?

This paper addresses an important clinical problem with a novel approach, but the methodology lacks sufficient justification, the evaluation is flawed, and the writing quality is poor. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these concerns before it would be suitable for publication.
