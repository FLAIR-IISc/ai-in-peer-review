

This paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, addressing the challenge of disfluencies that hinder traditional linguistic feature extraction. The main contributions are: (1) a novel framework using complex network features enriched with semantic information to represent short speech transcripts; (2) empirical demonstration that this approach outperforms traditional linguistic features across three distinct datasets; and (3) a multi-view learning strategy that achieves significant improvements (up to 10%) over individual classifiers.

Strengths: The paper addresses a clinically important problem with clear motivation. The complex network approach with word embeddings provides a language-independent representation that avoids the parsing errors caused by disfluencies in MCI speech. The evaluation across three diverse datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and ABCD) demonstrates consistent performance improvements over linguistic features, with the enriched network features (CNE) achieving the highest accuracy in most cases. The multi-view learning approach (combining CNE, linguistic features, and BoW) delivered particularly strong results, with 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier and 4.71% improvement in the ABCD dataset. The authors also provide a compelling rationale for using network features over linguistic features, noting that linguistic features require grammatical texts to perform well.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient detail on key methodological aspects. The linguistic features (Section 4.2.2) are inadequately described, making it difficult to assess their completeness or compare them to the network features. The word embedding training corpus is unclear—does the paper use the three small datasets combined or a larger external corpus? The removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions, etc.) is unjustified, as these may be important MCI indicators, and the paper fails to explain why this preprocessing step is appropriate. The Bag-of-Words implementation uses term frequency without justification when tf-idf is standard practice, and the paper lacks statistical significance tests to validate the reported improvements. The multi-view learning approach (Section 4.3) is not described in sufficient detail—what is the exact scheme for combining the different feature spaces?

The manuscript requires substantial editing for clarity. The abstract is too long (200+ words), and numerous sentences need rewriting for better flow (e.g., lines 22-25, 29-30, 34-38). Key terms like WML subtest, ABCD, and PLN should be defined upon first use. Dataset descriptions (Section 4.1) are confusing—why were some datasets manually segmented while others used automatic methods? The preprocessing steps (lines 233-235) need clarification regarding the order and purpose of lemmatization and other normalization steps. Table 1 should include more than just demographic statistics, and the paper lacks citations for key resources like DementiaBank and transcription norms.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify the exact corpus used to train the word embeddings (the paper implies the three small datasets were used together).
2. Justify the removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions), as these may contain valuable MCI indicators.
3. Explain why term frequency was used instead of tf-idf for Bag-of-Words features.
4. Provide a detailed description of the multi-view learning scheme, including how the different feature spaces were combined and how weights were assigned.
5. Include statistical significance tests to support the reported performance improvements.
