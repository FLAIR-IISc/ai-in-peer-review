

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes using complex network representations (enriched with word embeddings) to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcriptions as complex networks, enrich them with semantic information via word embeddings, and use topological features for classification. They evaluate on three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) and report that their approach outperforms linguistic features in most cases, with multi-view learning providing additional improvements (10% in Cinderella, 4.71% in ABCD).

## Main Contributions
1. Application of complex network features to MCI detection in speech, offering a language-independent representation less sensitive to disfluencies than traditional linguistic approaches.
2. Demonstration that network features can outperform linguistic features for MCI detection, particularly in cases where disfluencies are present.
3. Development of a multi-view learning approach combining network, linguistic, and BoW features for improved classification.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important clinical problem with practical implications for early MCI detection. The focus on speech transcripts as a low-cost, non-invasive assessment method is well-motivated, and the authors clearly demonstrate the limitations of current linguistic approaches due to disfluencies in patient speech.

2. The datasets used (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) are well-described, representing different linguistic contexts (English vs. Portuguese) and assessment protocols. The paper provides clear demographic information for each dataset, which is crucial for reproducibility.

3. The core idea of using complex networks to model short texts is well-motivated and potentially valuable for clinical applications where disfluencies are common. The authors effectively demonstrate that network features can work with unprocessed transcripts, avoiding the need for expensive manual correction.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient methodological detail:** The multi-view learning approach is not described in sufficient detail. The exact scheme (how classifiers are combined, how weights are assigned to different feature spaces) must be specified, particularly since the authors claim it's "an elegant solution" that "allows different weights to be given for each type of feature."

2. **Inadequate description of linguistic features:** Section 4.2.2 is insufficiently detailed. The authors should expand on the specific linguistic features used (beyond listing categories), particularly for Portuguese, and explain why certain metrics were excluded due to parsing issues.

3. **Unjustified preprocessing choices:** The removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is unjustified. The authors state they removed them "like Fraser et al. (2015)," but fail to explain why these are unimportant for MCI detection. Disfluencies may actually be important indicators of cognitive impairment, and their removal should be justified or reconsidered.

4. **Missing statistical validation:** The paper lacks statistical significance tests to support the experimental conclusions. The claimed improvements (e.g., 10% in Cinderella) need to be validated with appropriate statistical tests to demonstrate they're not due to chance.

5. **Insufficient dataset and embedding details:** It's unclear which datasets were used to train the word embeddings (the paper implies the three small datasets, but this is not explicitly stated). Additionally, the paper fails to clarify which corpus produced the word embeddings (lines 290-299, Section 4.2.1), which is critical for reproducibility.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please specify exactly how the multi-view learning approach combines classifiers (e.g., weights, optimization method) and provide the implementation details.
2. Justify the removal of disfluencies - why are they unimportant for MCI detection, and what evidence supports this claim?
3. Explain why term frequency (not tf-idf) was used for bag-of-words representation without justification.
4. Clarify which dataset(s) were used to train the word embeddings and provide the exact corpus details.

## Additional Comments
The manuscript requires considerable editing for clarity and readability. Numerous sentences need rewording (lines 22-25, 29-30, 34-38, 70, 73, 115-117, 147-149, etc.), and the abstract is too long. The paper also needs to define acronyms (WML, ABCD, PLN), clarify preprocessing steps (lines 233-235), and fix minor errors (lines 322, 620, 735). The related work section is superficial and lacks precise methodological descriptions for direct comparisons. The paper should cite transcription norms (lines 340-440) and provide links for datasets/tools referenced (DementiaBank, Picnic picture/Western Aphasia Battery). Table 1 should include additional dataset statistics beyond demographics.
