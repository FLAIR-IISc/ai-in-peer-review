

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network features enriched with word embeddings from speech transcripts, addressing the challenge of disfluencies in clinical assessments. The primary contribution is the application of complex network modeling to represent speech transcripts without requiring manual correction of disfluencies, which is a significant practical advantage over existing linguistic feature approaches. The secondary contribution is the demonstration of language-independence through evaluation on both English and Portuguese datasets, with a focus on Portuguese—a lower-resource language where parsing tools are limited. The tertiary contribution is the multi-view learning framework that combines complex network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features, achieving notable improvements in classification accuracy.

Strengths: The most compelling strength is the practical solution to the disfluency problem in clinical speech transcripts. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their complex network approach with word embeddings outperforms traditional linguistic features across all datasets (65-80% accuracy), eliminating the need for time-consuming manual transcript correction. The multi-view learning approach is particularly strong, with a 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier. The evaluation on three distinct datasets (including two Portuguese) provides valuable cross-lingual validation, demonstrating the method's robustness beyond English. The paper also makes a meaningful contribution to the underexplored area of MCI detection in Portuguese, where linguistic tools are limited.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is the extremely small dataset sizes, particularly for the Portuguese datasets (20 MCI + 20 controls), which severely limits the statistical significance of results and generalizability. The paper's contradictory tone—optimistic in the introduction but cautious in the conclusion about the difficulty of distinguishing MCI from controls—undermines credibility. The technical details about embedding training are insufficient; it's unclear whether embeddings were trained solely on the study's Portuguese datasets or on external corpora, and the paper doesn't clarify how "out-of-vocabulary" words were handled. The literature review contains irrelevant citations (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) and could be significantly condensed to focus on relevant NLP for clinical applications. The manuscript also contains multiple technical errors (e.g., "as it a lightweight" on line 29, duplicated words, incorrect reference formatting) that require correction.

Questions to Authors: 1) Please clarify how word embeddings were trained for Portuguese—was it using only the study's datasets or external corpora? 2) What specific Portuguese transcription norms (NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2) were followed, and how did these affect the embedding process? 3) Can you provide a sample English-translated transcript from the Portuguese datasets to illustrate the disfluencies handled by your approach? 4) Why does the paper claim to use "language independent representation" when the embedding training likely requires language-specific resources?

Additional Comments: The paper would benefit from a more focused literature review, removing irrelevant citations and concentrating on NLP for clinical assessment. The manuscript requires significant compression in sections 1-3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 to highlight core contributions. The technical explanation of how word embeddings enriched the network should be clarified, particularly regarding the relationship between word-level and subword (n-gram) networks. The small dataset sizes should be acknowledged as a major limitation in the conclusion, rather than downplayed. The paper's potential impact is significant for clinical applications where manual transcript correction is impractical, but this potential is currently limited by the methodological and dataset constraints.
