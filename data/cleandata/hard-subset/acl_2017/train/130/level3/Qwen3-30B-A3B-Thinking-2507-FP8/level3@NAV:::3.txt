

This paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, addressing limitations in linguistic feature-based approaches that require manual preprocessing. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating complex network features as a lightweight, language-independent representation for MCI detection; (2) showing that network features outperform linguistic features across three datasets; and (3) exploring ensemble and multi-view learning to improve classification accuracy.

Strengths include well-described datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) and a clear problem statement. The approach is interesting as it avoids manual transcription correction, which is particularly valuable for large-scale assessments. The results show promising performance, with multi-view learning achieving up to 10% improvement in accuracy over individual classifiers.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. Methodological details are insufficient: the multi-view learning approach lacks precise description, and linguistic features are poorly explained (Section 4.2.2 needs expansion). Crucially, the removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions) is unjustified despite evidence suggesting these may be important MCI indicators. The Bag-of-Words implementation uses term frequency without tf-idf justification, and no statistical significance tests are reported to validate results. The related work is superficial, lacking detailed comparisons with prior methods. The manuscript requires substantial editing for clarity, with numerous sentences needing rewording (e.g., lines 22-25, 34-38, 70, 73, 115-117). Critical details are missing: word embeddings were likely trained on the three small datasets (not clarified), dataset segmentation needs explanation, and Table 1 lacks essential statistics. Acronyms (WML, ABCD, PLN) are undefined, and transcription norms are referenced without citations.

The paper's core contribution—applying complex networks to MCI detection—is promising but undermined by methodological gaps and insufficient justification for key choices. While the problem and datasets are well-described, the lack of technical detail and statistical rigor significantly weakens the claims. The authors must address these issues before the work can be considered for publication.
