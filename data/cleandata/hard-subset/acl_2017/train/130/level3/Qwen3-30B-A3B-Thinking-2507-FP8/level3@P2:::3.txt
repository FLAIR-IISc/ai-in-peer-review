

# Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper investigates the use of complex network features enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech transcripts as complex networks, enrich them with semantic information via word embeddings, and evaluate these features against linguistic features and bag-of-words representations. The main contributions I identify are:

1. The application of complex network features (with word embedding enrichment) to MCI detection from speech transcripts, demonstrating that these features can outperform linguistic features in certain datasets without requiring manual parsing corrections.
2. The demonstration that multi-view learning combining different feature types (network features, linguistic features, and bag-of-words) can improve classification accuracy, with a 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier.
3. The provision of a detailed comparative evaluation across three distinct datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD), highlighting the potential of network-based approaches for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses an important clinical problem (early MCI detection) with a potentially valuable approach that avoids the need for manual transcript correction. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the authors demonstrate the practical relevance of their work through evaluation across multiple datasets.

2. The comparative evaluation across three distinct datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) provides valuable evidence of the approach's generalizability. The paper shows that complex network features can outperform linguistic features in all three datasets, which is a significant finding given the challenges of parsing disfluent speech.

3. The multi-view learning approach demonstrates meaningful improvements in classification accuracy (up to 10% in one dataset), suggesting that combining different feature types can yield better results than using any single feature type alone. This provides a useful direction for future work in the domain.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper lacks critical methodological details. The exact implementation of multi-view learning is not sufficiently described, and it's unclear how the combination of features and classifiers is performed. The paper also fails to specify which dataset(s) were used to train the word embeddings, which is crucial for reproducibility.

2. The preprocessing choice to remove disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is unjustified and potentially problematic. Since disfluencies are known to be important indicators of cognitive impairment in MCI patients, removing them without justification could artificially improve results and undermine the paper's claims about handling disfluent speech.

3. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the reported improvements. Without significance tests, it's impossible to determine whether the observed improvements (e.g., 4.71% in ABCD dataset) are meaningful or could be due to random chance.

4. The paper suffers from significant issues with clarity and presentation. The description of linguistic features (Section 4.2.2) is insufficient, and the manuscript requires extensive editing to improve readability. Many sentences are unclear or poorly constructed, and key terms (e.g., WML subtest, ABCD) are not properly defined.

## Questions to Authors

1. Please clarify which dataset(s) were used to train the word embeddings. The paper implies the three small datasets were used, but this needs explicit confirmation.

2. Please provide justification for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) from the transcripts. Given that these may be important MCI indicators, the authors should either justify their removal or provide results with disfluencies retained.

3. Why was term frequency (not tf-idf) used for the bag-of-words representation? Please provide justification for this choice.

4. Please clarify the exact implementation of the multi-view learning approach. How are the different feature spaces combined, and what is the specific scheme used for the majority voting?
