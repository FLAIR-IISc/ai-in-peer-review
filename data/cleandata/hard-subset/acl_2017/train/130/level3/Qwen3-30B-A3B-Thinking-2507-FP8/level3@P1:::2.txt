

Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features enriched with word embeddings. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that complex network features provide a language-independent representation suitable for MCI detection that outperforms traditional linguistic features, (2) developing a method to enrich co-occurrence networks with semantic information via word embeddings to better represent short clinical transcripts, and (3) showing that ensemble and multi-view learning approaches can further improve classification performance across multiple datasets.

Strengths: The work addresses an important clinical problem with significant real-world impact. MCI detection is critical for early intervention in dementia progression, and the paper successfully demonstrates that complex network features can effectively capture linguistic markers of cognitive impairment without requiring grammatically correct text. The use of multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) across different clinical contexts provides strong evidence for the approach's generalizability. The paper's technical contribution of enriching networks with word embeddings to better represent short clinical transcripts is novel and well-motivated by the challenges of disfluencies in clinical speech.

Weaknesses: The manuscript suffers from poor writing quality with numerous grammar and spelling errors that undermine readability and professionalism. The problem definition is unclear - it's not specified whether the classification task is at the participant level or transcript level, and reported counts in tables (e.g., 21 with possible AD vs. 43 with MCI in Cookie Theft dataset) contradict the stated sample sizes. The motivation for using complex network enrichment with word embeddings over simpler approaches (like using word embeddings directly for classification) is not well justified, and the paper doesn't compare with more straightforward semantic similarity approaches. The evaluation lacks critical elements: class imbalance is not addressed (the paper reports only accuracy, not F1 or balanced metrics), statistical significance testing is missing, and results are reported with unjustified precision (e.g., 80.18% accuracy) on very small datasets (e.g., 23 MCI and 20 control subjects in ABCD dataset). The paper also fails to leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools that could potentially improve performance.

The paper's claims about "outperforming linguistic features in all datasets" are problematic because the linguistic feature results in Table 5 are inconsistent with the claims - in the Cinderella dataset, linguistic features (LM) achieve 0.5250 accuracy while complex networks (CN) achieve 0.5250, and CNE achieves 0.6000, but the paper states "CNE is better than using only complex networks" without showing consistent superiority across all datasets. The improvement claims (e.g., "10% improvement in Cinderella dataset") lack statistical significance testing.

The authors also fail to properly contextualize their results against prior work. The paper states their approach achieves "comparable accuracy to the values reported by other authors (60-85%)" without providing a meaningful comparison, as the datasets used in prior work are different.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Can you clarify whether classification is performed at the transcript level or participant level, and how participant-level results are aggregated?
2. Why was the complex network enrichment approach chosen over simpler semantic similarity methods (e.g., using word embeddings directly as features)?
3. How do you address the class imbalance in your datasets, and why is accuracy reported as the primary metric when it's misleading in imbalanced settings? 

The paper has a strong clinical motivation and promising technical approach, but the methodological weaknesses, unclear presentation, and inadequate evaluation significantly undermine its contribution. With substantial revisions addressing the evaluation issues and clarifying the methodology, this work could make a meaningful contribution to the field.
