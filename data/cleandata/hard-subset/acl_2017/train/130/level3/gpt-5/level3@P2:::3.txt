Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them with semantic links derived from word embeddings. It extracts topological features from these enriched graphs (and from plain co-occurrence graphs), and compares them against parser-dependent linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW) features to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). Experiments cover three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese). Multi-view (feature-space) combinations and ensemble classifiers are also explored.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features are competitive with, and often outperform, parser-based linguistic features for MCI detection across two languages and three tasks, with reasonable performance using lightweight, parser-independent representations.
2) Cross-dataset evaluation and multi-view ensembling: demonstrating that combining feature spaces (CNE, LM, BoW) can yield further gains on Cinderella and ABCD.
3) Practical modeling tweak for short, noisy clinical transcripts: augmenting sparse co-occurrence graphs with embedding-similarity edges to mitigate short-text linearity and improve topological signal.

Strengths
- Relevance and scope: Addresses an important, clinically meaningful problem (early detection of MCI) using language biomarkers, with experiments spanning English and Portuguese and multiple elicitation tasks. This cross-language, cross-task perspective increases the potential impact and generality.
- Lightweight, language-agnostic features: The graph-based approach avoids heavy syntactic preprocessing that is fragile on disfluent, agrammatical transcripts, aligning with the goal of scalable, automated screening.
- Sensible modeling intuition and empirical support: Enriching co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based edges is a principled way to densify short-text networks. The reported gains of CNE over CN (and over LM in several settings) support the intuition.
- Multi-view combination shows practical value: Majority-vote fusion across feature spaces improves accuracy on Cinderella (+10% absolute over best individual classifier) and ABCD (up to 80% accuracy), suggesting complementary information across views.
- Transparent baseline classifiers and open-source toolkit: Using multiple standard learners (SVM, KNN, RF, NB) with scikit-learn, and reporting per-feature-space results, helps readers gauge where the signal comes from.

Weaknesses
- Missing methodological detail limits reproducibility and interpretation:
  - Multi-view learning: The paper motivates learning weights for views but seems to use simple majority voting; the exact fusion scheme, per-view classifier choices, and any calibration/stacking are not specified.
  - Word embeddings: It is unclear which corpora were used to train embeddings (the three small datasets, external large corpora, or both), for both English and Portuguese. Training data size, preprocessing, and whether vectors are shared or per-dataset are not described.
  - Thresholding and graph construction: The cosine-similarity threshold selection procedure (tuning protocol, per-dataset vs global) and whether similarity edges are weighted or binarized need clarification. Definitions of some topological measures (e.g., average shortest path length per node; directed vs undirected; handling of disconnected components) are underspecified.
  - Cross-validation protocol: With multiple narratives per ABCD subject (immediate and delayed recall), folds must be split by subject to prevent leakage. The paper does not state whether CV was stratified and subject-disjoint.
- Questionable preprocessing choice: Removal of filled pauses, short false starts, and repetitions is not justified. These disfluencies are plausible MCI indicators; removing them may discard predictive signal and undermines the claim to handle disfluent transcripts. An ablation is needed to justify this choice.
- Baselines and reporting: BoW uses raw term frequency without tf-idf or character n-grams; this choice is unmotivated and likely weakens the baseline. No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals are reported, making it difficult to assess whether observed gains (often a few percentage points) are reliable given small datasets.
- Related work needs deeper coverage and positioning: The discussion of prior MCI/AD detection from language (and acoustic) is superficial, lacks precise methodological contrasts, and misses direct comparisons on shared datasets (e.g., DementiaBank). Some cited items are incomplete or inconsistent.
- Presentation and clarity: The manuscript requires substantial editing for clarity and compliance:
  - Abstract is too long; multiple sentences throughout are unclear or ungrammatical; several acronyms are undefined at first mention (e.g., ABCD, WLM/WMS, PLN).
  - Dataset descriptions need explicit naming, links/citations (DementiaBank, WAB “Picnic” picture, NURC/SP norms), segmentation rationale, and additional statistics (n narratives, tokens per narrative, class balance).
  - Section 4.2.2 “Linguistic Features” is too terse; the exact set used (English and Portuguese), tool versions, and any preprocessing must be specified.

Questions to authors
- Multi-view fusion: Precisely what scheme did you use (per-view classifier types and hyperparameters, probability calibration, weighting vs majority vote)? Did you try stacking with a held-out development set?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings (size, domain, token counts)? Did you train on the small task datasets only, or leverage larger external corpora? Will you release vectors?
- Graph construction: Are similarity edges weighted by cosine or binarized at the threshold? Is the network directed or undirected? How do you handle disconnected components when computing path-based metrics?
- Threshold tuning: How was the cosine threshold selected (nested CV vs outer folds)? Provide a sensitivity analysis across thresholds.
- Cross-validation splits: In ABCD, were folds split by subject so the two recalls from a subject never appear in different folds?
- Preprocessing/disfluencies: Why remove filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions? Please provide an ablation comparing with/without removal. How does this interact with the goal of being robust to disfluency?
- Baselines: Did you try tf-idf weighting, character/word n-grams, or regularized logistic regression? If so, how do they compare? If not, please justify.

Additional comments
- Expand Section 4.2.2 with the exact list of Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia features used (you allude to 58 of 73), tool versions, and any language-specific preprocessing.
- Clarify preprocessing order (tokenization, case-folding, stopword removal, lemmatization omitted, handling of numerals/diacritics) for all datasets.
- Provide dataset/tool links and proper citations (DementiaBank, WAB “Cookie Theft”/Picnic picture, NURC/SP norms, DeepBond), and ensure ACL citation style compliance. Fix missing/incomplete references and typos.
- Augment tables with dataset statistics: number of subjects/narratives per class, average tokens/sentences, standard deviations; include subject-level counts for ABCD.
- Report statistical testing (e.g., paired t-test/Wilcoxon across folds) or confidence intervals; include per-fold results in supplementary material if page limits constrain.
- Shorten the abstract to focus on the core idea, key numbers, and main takeaways; avoid redundancy.
- Consider an error analysis to illustrate what the network features capture vs LM/BoW, and include feature importance or ablations (CN vs CNE, each topological measure).

Overall: Interesting and promising empirical application with cross-language scope, but acceptance hinges on addressing missing methodological details, strengthening baselines and statistical analysis, and improving clarity and presentation.
