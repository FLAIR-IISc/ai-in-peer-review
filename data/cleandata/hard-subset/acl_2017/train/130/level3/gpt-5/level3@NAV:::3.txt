Summary
The paper studies automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings, alongside linguistic and BoW features. Experiments on three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft in English; Cinderella and ABCD in Brazilian Portuguese) show enriched network features are competitive, and multi-view/ensemble combinations yield further gains.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A cross-dataset, cross-language empirical evaluation demonstrating that complex network features enriched with embeddings are viable for MCI detection and often outperform traditional linguistic metrics.
- Evidence that multi-view combinations of feature spaces can improve accuracy on some datasets (e.g., +10% on Cinderella; up to 80% accuracy on ABCD).

Strengths
- Addresses an important clinical problem with a lightweight, language-agnostic representation less dependent on fragile parsing; datasets and tasks are generally well described.
- Solid empirical coverage across three datasets and two languages; reports multiple classifiers and combinations, with concrete improvements (e.g., Tables 4–7).
- Clear motivation for enriching co-occurrence networks to mitigate short-text topology issues; the approach is reasonable and informative.

Weaknesses
- Key methodological details are missing: the multi-view learning scheme is underspecified; feature-space training/combination and voting weights are unclear. The embedding-enrichment thresholding and its tuning protocol also lack detail.
- The linguistic feature set is insufficiently specified (Section 4.2.2); selection criteria and final feature lists need expansion. Acronyms/terms (e.g., WLM subtest, ABCD, PLN) are not consistently defined.
- Word embeddings’ training corpus is unclear; the text implies training on the small task datasets, which risks leakage and poor coverage.
- Preprocessing choices are under-justified: removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is questionable given their diagnostic value; BoW uses tf rather than tf-idf without justification.
- No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals; conclusions are not supported by inferential analysis.
- Writing and presentation require substantial editing: abstract is too long; many sentences need rewording (e.g., lines 22–25, 29–30, 34–38, 70, 73, 115–117, 147–149); minor typos and grammar errors persist; related work is superficial and lacks precise methodological comparisons.
- Dataset descriptions/segmentations are unclear (lines 323–332); Table 1 lacks dataset-level statistics beyond demographics; missing citations/links for datasets/tools and transcription norms; citation style compliance issues.

Questions to Authors
- Precisely specify the multi-view learning setup: per-view classifiers, decision fusion (majority vote vs. weighted), how weights were set, and whether validation avoided leakage.
- What corpus was used to train embeddings (size, language(s), domain)? If trained on task data, please reconsider and/or justify.
- Justify removal of disfluencies; report results with and without removal. Provide full preprocessing pipeline and order (tokenization, stopwords, lemmatization decisions).
- Why choose tf over tf-idf for BoW? Provide ablations.
- Report statistical significance (e.g., permutation tests or bootstrap CIs) for key comparisons.
- Define feature computation details (e.g., “shortest path” measure, exact cosine threshold values and tuning). Provide explicit dataset names and segmentation protocol; expand Table 1 with narrative lengths, token counts, and sentence stats.
- Add missing citations/links (DementiaBank, WAB/Picnic, NURC norms) and ensure conference citation style compliance.
