Summary
This paper targets automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts in English and Brazilian Portuguese. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and enrich it by adding edges between semantically similar words using cosine similarity of word embeddings. A set of standard topological metrics (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering, assortativity) is computed and aggregated (mean, stdev, skew) as features. These “complex network enriched” features are compared against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix variants) and bag-of-words (BoW), using several classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, GNB) and combined via ensemble and multi-view learning. Evaluations on three datasets—DementiaBank Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella narratives (Portuguese), and ABCD retellings (Portuguese)—report best accuracies of roughly 65% (Cookie Theft), 75% (Cinderella), and 80% (ABCD) with multi-view combinations; enriched networks generally outperform linguistic features on noisy, unedited transcripts.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding: embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features are competitive across two languages and multiple tasks, often outperforming parser-dependent linguistic metrics on disfluent transcripts.
- Practical insight: multi-view/ensemble combinations of network, linguistic, and BoW features can improve accuracy on small datasets.
- Robustness claim: network-based features provide a lightweight, parser-free representation that tolerates disfluencies better than traditional linguistic features (particularly on unedited narratives).

Strengths
- Relevance and scope: The clinical problem is important; the paper evaluates across English and Portuguese, including two under-resourced Portuguese datasets, which increases the potential impact and generalizability.
- Lightweight, language-agnostic approach: The proposal avoids reliance on brittle parsing and hand-crafted resources, addressing realistic ASR/transcript noise.
- Systematic comparisons: The work contrasts three feature families (network, linguistic, BoW) and reports that enriched networks consistently beat linguistic metrics on noisy data; the Cinderella “cleaned” vs “uncleaned” comparison is insightful.
- Multi-view learning: The use of multi-view/ensembles is reasonable for heterogeneous features and shows consistent gains over single-view models on two datasets.
- Use of standard toolkits: The implementation is based on well-known libraries (fastText-like embeddings, scikit-learn), improving potential reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and inconsistent figure/table references, which hinder comprehension. Several statements are imprecise or confusing (e.g., data counts, method descriptions).
- Problem setup and data leakage risk: It is unclear whether cross-validation was performed at the subject level. For ABCD (two narratives per subject), narrative-level CV risks train/test leakage. Class counts and distributions are not consistently reported; some numbers conflict across sections and tables.
- Evaluation methodology: Accuracy alone is insufficient given likely class imbalance; macro F1, precision/recall, ROC-AUC, and confusion matrices are needed. No confidence intervals or statistical significance tests are reported; decimal precision seems unjustified on small samples.
- Motivation and ablations: The paper lacks ablations to justify the specific graph design. It is unclear whether co-occurrence+embedding enrichment is superior to (i) a pure embedding similarity graph (e.g., kNN in embedding space), or (ii) simple document embeddings (averaged fastText/SIF, doc2vec) with linear classifiers. Threshold selection lacks a principled, nested tuning procedure or sensitivity analysis.
- Baseline and feature coverage: Stronger baselines are missing (e.g., TF-IDF with linear models, averaged embeddings, doc2vec). The work does not incorporate acoustic/prosodic cues that are known to be informative for MCI/AD, nor domain-specific clinical features; the claim of language independence would be stronger with such comparisons or a rationale for excluding them.
- Dataset reporting: For Cookie Theft, the control sample size is unclear; counts and demographics are inconsistently described. The preprocessing (disfluency removal) is only partially specified, and it is unclear how this interacts with the features and evaluation splits.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds constructed at the subject level for all datasets (especially ABCD with two narratives per subject) to prevent leakage? Please clarify folds, stratification, and whether speaker identity was confined to a single fold.
- Class balance: What are the exact class counts per dataset and per fold? Please report macro/micro F1, precision/recall, ROC-AUC, and confusion matrices.
- Hyperparameters: How were the embedding thresholds (0.7/0.4) selected? Was tuning nested within CV to avoid test contamination? Provide sensitivity analyses over thresholds, embedding dimensionality, window size, and number of neighbors when constructing similarity edges.
- Ablations/baselines: How does a pure embedding similarity graph (without co-occurrence edges) perform? How do simple document embedding baselines (averaged fastText, SIF, doc2vec) and TF-IDF+linear SVM compare? Any per-feature ablations for the network metrics?
- Multi-view details: Are votes equally weighted, or learned? Did you try stacking or calibrated probability averaging? Provide per-view contributions and error overlap analyses.
- Confounds: Given the ABCD demographic differences (e.g., age), did you control for or include demographics in models? Could results be driven by confounds?
- Reproducibility: Will code, preprocessing scripts, and trained embeddings be released? Please specify the exact stopword lists, tokenizers, and disfluency removal rules.

Additional Comments
- Thorough language editing is needed; ensure tables/figures and references are correct and complete.
- Add statistical testing (e.g., McNemar or bootstrap tests) and report confidence intervals from repeated CV.
- Temper claims: enriched networks do not uniformly beat all baselines; on cleaned Cinderella, linguistic features are stronger. Reflect this nuance.
- Clarify ethical approvals and consent for the Brazilian datasets.
- Discuss limitations (small sample sizes, potential overfitting) and plans for external validation.
