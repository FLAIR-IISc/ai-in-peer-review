Summary
This paper targets automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts across three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD). It models transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs and enriches them with word-embedding-based similarity edges, extracting diverse graph-derived topological features alongside linguistic and BoW features. Multiple classifiers and multi-view/ensemble combinations are evaluated via 5-fold cross-validation; performance varies by dataset but suggests the enriched graph features are competitive.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Cross-dataset empirical study of MCI detection from transcripts (English and Portuguese) comparing graph-based, linguistic, and BoW features with multi-view/ensemble schemes.
2) Application-focused adaptation of graph-of-words enriched with embeddings to short, disfluent clinical transcripts, with a broad set of graph metrics.
3) Evidence that enriched graph features can match or exceed traditional linguistic metrics without heavy parsing.

Strengths
- Clinically relevant problem, with appropriate datasets spanning different elicitation protocols and languages; the focus on robustness to disfluencies is timely.
- The feature-space breadth (graph topology, Coh-Metrix(-Dementia), BoW) and multi-view/ensemble exploration provide a useful comparative perspective; enriched graphs often yield the strongest accuracies.
- Method is lightweight and language-independent in principle, reducing reliance on error-prone parsing for disfluent speech.

Weaknesses
- Writing quality is poor (grammar, spelling, organization), impeding clarity and reproducibility.
- Problem setup is unclear: unit of analysis (subject vs. transcript), repeated measures (ABCD immediate/delayed recall), and 5-fold protocol raise concerns about subject-level leakage; dataset counts are inconsistent and tables emphasize study demographics rather than transcript-level statistics; class distributions are not reported.
- Evaluation relies primarily on accuracy; with unknown balance, this is inadequate. No macro-F1/weighted-F1, AUROC/PR-AUC, or confidence intervals/significance tests; results are reported with unjustified precision on very small samples. Hyperparameter/threshold tuning appears non-nested.
- Methodological motivation is underdeveloped: the benefit of enrichment over simpler word-similarity graphs or content-based baselines is not convincingly shown; limited ablations/sensitivity analyses.
- Baselines omit strong modern alternatives (e.g., pre-trained transformer models, clinical/biomedical NLP resources); no error analysis or interpretability.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- What is the exact instance definition and cross-validation protocol? Were folds stratified by subject to prevent leakage, especially in ABCD with two narratives per subject?
- Provide per-dataset class distributions, transcripts per class, and ensure that performance is reported as macro-F1 (plus AUROC/PR-AUC). Include CIs and statistical tests across folds.
- How were thresholds and hyperparameters selected? Was nested CV used?
- Add ablations: co-occurrence vs. enrichment-only graphs, individual graph metrics, and threshold sensitivity.
- Which embedding models and training corpora were used for English/Portuguese? Any in-domain clinical data?
