Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts in English and Brazilian Portuguese. It models each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and enriches this graph with edges between semantically similar words using word embeddings. Topological features of these enriched graphs, along with conventional linguistic metrics and bag-of-words (BoW), are used with multiple classifiers and combined via ensemble and multi-view learning. Experiments on three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella narratives, and ABCD recall tasks) suggest that enriched complex-network (CN) features are competitive with or stronger than parser-dependent linguistic features; multi-view and ensemble methods yield additional gains on some datasets.

Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength)
1) Application and evaluation of embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features for MCI detection across three datasets and two languages, showing that such lightweight, language-agnostic features can be effective on noisy transcripts.
2) Empirical finding that enriched CN features are competitive with, and sometimes outperform, parser-dependent linguistic metrics, especially when transcripts are disfluent or unedited.
3) Demonstration that multi-view/ensemble combinations of CN, linguistic, and BoW features can improve accuracy over single-view classifiers on some tasks.

Strengths
- Addresses a clinically meaningful, underexplored space with cross-lingual evidence: The focus on language-based MCI detection and evaluation on English and Portuguese data increases potential impact and generality. The approach avoids reliance on high-quality parsers, aligning with the realities of disfluent clinical speech.
- Lightweight, language-independent representation: Modeling transcripts as word-adjacency graphs enriched with embeddings is simple to compute, robust to ASR/transcription noise, and less dependent on annotated resources. This is a practical advantage over syntactic parsers, especially for Portuguese where robust parsing is limited.
- Clear empirical signal that enrichment helps small/noisy texts: For short, near-linear networks, topological signals can be weak; adding embedding-based edges plausibly densifies the graph and improves discriminative topological measures. The reported gains of CNE over CN across datasets support this.
- Multi-view/ensemble gains in specific settings: The reported improvements (e.g., 10% accuracy gain on Cinderella via ensemble/multi-view) indicate that the feature spaces capture complementary information, which is encouraging for practical systems.
- Breadth of evaluation with multiple classifiers and a consistent 5-fold protocol: Using SVM, kNN, RF, GNB and comparing views (CN/CNE/LM/BoW) helps establish baseline competitiveness and supports the claim that the method is generally applicable.

Weaknesses
- Dataset clarity, class distribution, and potential leakage: The paper does not clearly specify per-dataset transcript counts by class, nor whether cross-validation is stratified at the subject level. In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate and delayed recall); if folds are not split by subject, the same speaker could appear in train and test folds, inflating accuracy. The Cookie Theft description mixes MCI extraction with AD counts and lacks control counts. These issues undermine the validity of reported results.
- Confounding demographics and lack of controls: In ABCD, controls average 61 years vs. 72 for MCI (and education differs). Without matching or controlling for age/education, models may learn demographic proxies rather than MCI. No analyses controlling for these variables are presented.
- Evaluation metrics and statistical rigor: Accuracy alone is insufficient with unknown class balance. The paper should report per-class precision/recall/F1, macro-F1, balanced accuracy, AUC, and confusion matrices. Improvements (e.g., +10%) lack statistical significance testing (e.g., McNemar’s test, bootstrap, or paired tests across folds). Thresholds (0.7/0.4) appear tuned without nested CV, risking overfitting.
- Insufficient ablations and baselines: The motivation for embedding-based enrichment versus simpler document representations (e.g., mean word embeddings, SIF-weighted averages, doc2vec) or graph-only similarity networks is not tested. No ablation on the enrichment threshold, edge weighting, or on which topological features matter most. BoW is limited to tf; tf-idf and regularized linear models are standard baselines. Without these comparisons, the novelty/benefit of the graph approach is less convincing.
- Embedding training corpus unspecified: It’s unclear on what corpora the fastText embeddings were trained (English and Portuguese). Training on the small task data risks instability and leakage; external corpora and vocabulary coverage need reporting.
- Methodological inconsistencies across datasets: Disfluency removal differs by dataset; stopword removal is standard, but function words often carry signal in cognitive impairment. The rationale for discarding them should be tested. Manual “revised” Cinderella transcripts are not comparable to unedited versions, and the paper mixes both without a clear protocol.
- Writing quality and presentation: The manuscript contains grammatical issues, inconsistent terminology, table/numbering inconsistencies, and over-precise reporting (e.g., 0.6500) not justified by dataset sizes, which hinders readability and confidence.

Questions to authors
- Provide exact counts per dataset: number of subjects, number of narratives per class, and total tokens/unique words. For Cookie Theft, specify control counts (and confirm MCI definitions relative to AD/possible AD).
- How were folds constructed? Confirm that cross-validation is stratified by subject (not transcript), especially for ABCD with immediate/delayed recalls. If not, please re-run with subject-level splits.
- What corpora were used to train embeddings in English and Portuguese? Were pre-trained fastText embeddings used? If trained in-domain, what is the total token size, and how do you mitigate overfitting?
- Report class distributions and add F1 (macro/micro), balanced accuracy, AUC, and confusion matrices. Are gains significant under paired tests across folds? Please include confidence intervals or variance.
- Ablations: (i) CNE vs. CN vs. mean embeddings/doc2vec; (ii) with/without stopwords; (iii) enrichment threshold sensitivity; (iv) edge weighting by cosine similarity instead of binary edges; (v) feature importance for topological metrics.
- Controls for demographics: Can you match groups on age/education or include them as covariates/features and re-evaluate? Do results hold after controlling?
- Preventing tuning bias: Were similarity thresholds and classifier hyperparameters tuned with nested CV? If not, can you re-run with nested CV?

Additional comments
- Consider tf-idf with linear SVM/logistic regression as a stronger BoW baseline, and character n-grams which are robust to disfluencies.
- Release code, feature extraction scripts, and fold assignments to support reproducibility.
- Clarify disfluency preprocessing and ensure consistent pipelines across datasets.
- Improve language, formatting, and reference consistency; reduce unwarranted numerical precision.
