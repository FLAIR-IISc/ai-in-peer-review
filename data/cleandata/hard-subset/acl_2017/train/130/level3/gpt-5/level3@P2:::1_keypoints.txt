- Summary: Proposes detecting MCI from transcripts using word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-based semantic links; evaluated on three datasets and compared to linguistic metrics and BoW, showing CNE is often competitive or superior.

- Contribution 1: Enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding links mitigates sparsity in short, disfluent transcripts and yields competitive detection performance.

- Contribution 2: Empirical evaluation on two Portuguese datasets indicates parser-independent features can work well in a lower-resource language and with noisy, automatically segmented transcripts.

- Contribution 3: Combining CNE, linguistic metrics, and BoW via multi-view/ensemble methods provides additional accuracy gains on some datasets (e.g., ABCD, Cinderella).

- Strength: Tackles an important clinical task and under-represented language (Portuguese); avoids brittle parsing dependencies.

- Strength: Methodologically sensible choice to add embedding-derived semantic edges and to use subword-aware embeddings for OOV/disfluency robustness.

- Strength: Cross-dataset evaluation shows consistent gains from CNE; multi-view/ensemble yield notable improvements on specific datasets.

- Strength: Practical, minimally supervised preprocessing pipeline (automated sentence segmentation, selective disfluency removal) suits noisy clinical transcripts.

- Strength: CNE appears robust when parsing is unreliable; linguistic metrics improve on manually corrected data, supporting CNE’s practical utility.

- Weakness: Key reproducibility details missing—embedding training corpora, hyperparameters, OOV definition, edge creation rules, stopword/tokenization/disfluency heuristics.

- Weakness: Similarity threshold selection (0.7/0.4) and tuning protocol not described, risking optimistic performance estimates.

- Weakness: Experimental design issues—small datasets, unclear grouped CV for multi-sample subjects (ABCD), reliance on accuracy only, and lack of confidence intervals or significance testing.

- Weakness: Baselines are weak/incomplete—BoW uses raw term frequency but no tf-idf, and no doc2vec/sentence-embedding baselines.

- Weakness: Limited ablation and sensitivity analyses (impact of enrichment vs plain networks, threshold sensitivity, subword vs standard embeddings, disfluency removal effects, per-language breakdown).

- Weakness: Writing/organization problems—overlong background, tangential citations, contradictory tone, and multiple typographical/formatting errors.

- Weakness: Generalization claims (lightweight, language independent) overreach given small, language-specific evaluations and lack of cross-language transfer tests.

- Questions to authors: Report embedding corpora/languages/sizes/hyperparameters and vocabulary coverage.

- Questions to authors: Explain how cosine similarity thresholds were chosen/tuned, whether edges are weighted or binary, and any caps on added edges.

- Questions to authors: Confirm cross-validation protocol and whether folds were grouped by subject to prevent data leakage.

- Questions to authors: Provide stronger baselines (tf-idf, doc2vec/sentence embeddings) and metrics (precision/recall/F1/AUC) with CIs and significance tests.

- Questions to authors: Detail automatic disfluency removal rules and analyze their impact on performance.

- Questions to authors: Clarify OOV definition with subword models and provide comparisons to standard skip-gram embeddings.

- Additional suggestions: Include translated transcript examples and visual before/after network graphs, compress background, clarify transcription norms and DeepBond quality, fix typos/formatting, and consider releasing code and embeddings for reproducibility.

- Overall: Interesting and promising idea for Portuguese clinical speech, but needs clearer methodology, stronger experimental rigor, ablations, and tighter writing to meet conference standards.
