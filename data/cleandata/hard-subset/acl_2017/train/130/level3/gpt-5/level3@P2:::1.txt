Summary
The paper investigates detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word co-occurrence complex networks enriched with semantic links derived from word embeddings. The authors evaluate the approach on three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD), compare against linguistic metrics and BoW features, and explore ensemble and multi-view learning. They report that enriched complex network features (CNE) are competitive and often superior to linguistic features, with additional gains from multi-view and ensemble methods.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A pragmatic modeling contribution: enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based semantic edges to mitigate sparsity in short, disfluent transcripts, yielding competitive or superior detection performance to traditional linguistic features across datasets (notably Portuguese).
2) An empirical contribution on clinical NLP for a lower-resource language: evaluation on two Portuguese datasets (including automatically segmented transcripts) demonstrating that parser-independent features can be effective without costly manual cleanup.
3) A practical finding: combining views (CNE, LM, BoW) via multi-view and ensemble learning provides additional accuracy gains on some datasets (e.g., ABCD up to ~80%).

Strengths
- Addresses an important clinical task and language setting. Early detection of MCI via discourse is valuable, and Portuguese is under-represented in clinical NLP. Avoiding dependency on robust parsing is well-motivated given disfluencies and tool limitations.
- Methodological idea is reasonable and domain-adapted. Augmenting co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity links is a natural way to add semantic connectivity to sparse networks from short transcripts, and the choice of subword-aware embeddings is sensible given OOV/disfluency issues.
- Cross-dataset evaluation and consistent gains from CNE. Across Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD, CNE is often the best single feature set. Reported improvements include multi-view gains on ABCD (80.18% with SVM-linear, +4.71% over best individual classifier) and Cinderella (+10% with ensemble/multi-view over best single).
- Practical pipeline choices align with the application. Minimal, fully automated preprocessing (sentence segmentation via DeepBond; selective disfluency removal; no lemmatization) supports the claim of a lightweight approach less brittle to agrammaticalities.
- Results suggest robustness when parsing is unreliable. On the manually revised Cinderella set, linguistic metrics improve, underscoring that CNE may be preferable in realistic, noisy transcripts.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and missing methodological details. It is unclear how and on what corpora the embeddings were trained (Portuguese vs English; in-domain transcripts vs external text; size; domain). The paper mentions subword embeddings for OOV but does not define how “OOV” is determined nor clarify the interaction between word-level vs subword representations. The similarity threshold selection (0.7 vs 0.4 across datasets) and its tuning protocol (inside each CV fold vs on the full data) are not described, risking optimistic estimates. Key implementation details (edge creation order, weighting, stopword lists, tokenization rules, disfluency heuristics) are insufficient for replication.
- Experimental design leaves validity questions. Datasets are small (e.g., 20 MCI + 20 controls in Cinderella), and ABCD includes multiple narratives per subject; it is not stated whether cross-validation was grouped by subject to avoid leakage across folds. Evaluation relies on accuracy only; there are no class distributions, confidence intervals, significance tests, or secondary metrics (precision/recall/AUC), making it hard to assess stability. The BoW baseline uses raw term frequency only; tf-idf (a standard, stronger baseline) is missing.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses. The paper would benefit from: (i) a clear ablation showing the delta from adding embedding-based edges vs plain co-occurrence; (ii) sensitivity to the similarity threshold; (iii) effects of subword modeling vs standard skip-gram for embeddings; (iv) impact of disfluency removal; and (v) per-language analysis to support the “language independent” claim.
- Writing and organization issues. Sections 1–3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 are overly long with general background; the literature review includes tangential citations (e.g., Salton 1989, pedestrian detection, sentiment classification) that could be pruned. There is a contradictory tone: early optimism vs a conclusion that emphasizes the difficulty of the task without reconciling with the reported gains. Numerous typographical/formatting errors (e.g., “as it a lightweight,” PLN→NLP, duplicated words, inconsistent decimal separators in Table 3, duplicate DOIs/broken links) distract from the content.
- Generalization claims outpace evidence. The approach is described as lightweight and language independent, but the evaluation is on small datasets, with language-specific preprocessing and embeddings. Stronger cross-corpus and cross-language transfer tests (e.g., training on Portuguese, testing on English, or vice versa) are not provided.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Embeddings: What corpora, languages, and sizes were used to train the embeddings for English vs Portuguese? Were they trained solely on the target transcripts or on large external corpora? Please report training hyperparameters and vocabulary coverage.
- Network enrichment: How was the cosine similarity threshold chosen (0.7/0.4)? Was tuning nested within CV folds? Are edges weighted by similarity or binary? Do you cap added edges per node?
- Cross-validation protocol: For ABCD (two narratives per subject) and any multiple samples per subject elsewhere, did you ensure subject-disjoint folds? If not, please re-run with grouped CV.
- Baselines and metrics: Can you include tf-idf BoW, doc2vec or sentence embeddings, and report precision/recall/F1/AUC with confidence intervals and significance tests?
- Disfluency handling: What exact rules were used for automatic removal? Any analysis of its impact on performance?
- OOV and subword: How do you define OOV in this setting, given subword models? Did you compare subword vs standard skip-gram embeddings?

Additional Comments
- Provide an English-translated transcript example for Portuguese datasets and a visual example of a co-occurrence network before/after enrichment to aid intuition.
- Compress background, focus the related work on clinical speech/MCI and complex networks in NLP; remove tangential citations.
- Clarify and briefly explain the NURC/SP transcription norms and sentence segmentation quality (DeepBond accuracy).
- Fix typographical/formatting issues: “as it a lightweight,” PLN→NLP, duplicated words, consistent decimal separators (e.g., 72.0), reference DOIs/links.
- Consider releasing code, trained embeddings, and (if permissible) feature matrices to support reproducibility.

Overall, the idea of enriching co-occurrence networks with embeddings for clinical speech is interesting and promising, especially for Portuguese. However, the paper needs clearer methodological detail, stronger experimental rigor, and tighter writing to meet ACL standards.
