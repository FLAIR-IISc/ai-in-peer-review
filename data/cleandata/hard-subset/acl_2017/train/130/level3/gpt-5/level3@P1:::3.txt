Summary
The paper investigates automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word adjacency networks and enriching them with word-embedding-based similarity edges. It evaluates network-derived topological features against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag-of-Words (BoW), and explores ensemble and multi-view combinations across three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, Portuguese ABCD). Results suggest enriched complex networks are competitive and sometimes superior to linguistic features, with further gains from multi-view learning.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: Demonstration that complex network features enriched with word embeddings are viable, lightweight, and often strong predictors for MCI detection across English and Portuguese speech transcripts, including short and noisy narratives.
- Secondary: Empirical comparison of feature families (network, linguistic, BoW) and their combinations via ensemble/multi-view learning on three datasets, with reported improvements over individual models.
- Tertiary: Practical claim of reduced dependence on high-precision parsing and manual transcript correction by adopting language-independent network representations.

Strengths
- Timely, clinically relevant task with multilingual scope: The paper tackles an important problem and evaluates on three datasets spanning two languages and different elicitation paradigms, increasing ecological validity.
- Lightweight, language-independent representation: The network approach reduces reliance on parsers and handcrafted rules that struggle with disfluent transcripts, aligning with the goal of scalable, low-resource clinical screening.
- Embedding-enriched networks for short texts: Augmenting co-occurrence graphs with semantic similarity edges addresses sparsity in short/disfluent narratives and yields consistent gains over plain networks in multiple settings.
- Empirical gains from combining views: Multi-view combinations (e.g., CNE-LM-BoW) provide notable improvements (e.g., +10% on Cinderella, 80% on ABCD), indicating complementary information across feature families.
- Transparent baseline inclusion: The finding that linear SVM on BoW is strong in some settings (e.g., 75% on ABCD) is informative and sets a realistic baseline for future work.

Weaknesses
- Missing methodological details limit reproducibility and interpretability: The multi-view learning scheme is underspecified (how base learners are selected, how predictions are combined, how weights are learned, whether decisions are calibrated). The threshold tuning for embedding edges (0.7/0.4) lacks a clear, leakage-free protocol (nested CV or held-out dev set).
- Insufficient description of “linguistic features”: Section 4.2.2 does not enumerate the actual metrics used per dataset nor justify exclusions; readers cannot reproduce the feature set or assess which subgroups drive performance.
- Unclear word embedding training data: It is not specified which corpora (and in which languages) were used to train the embeddings, whether in-domain or large external corpora were employed, and whether English and Portuguese models were trained separately or shared. Training on the small task datasets would be inadequate.
- Preprocessing decisions are questionable and under-justified: Filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions are removed in Cookie Theft and Cinderella, despite being known markers of cognitive impairment; this choice requires justification, ablation, or reversal. Sentence segmentation protocols (manual vs automatic) also need rationale and consistency.
- Evaluation lacks statistical validation and may risk leakage: No significance tests or confidence intervals are reported to support claims of superiority. It is unclear whether cross-validation was subject-independent (especially in ABCD with two narratives per subject), which is critical to avoid optimistic estimates. Hyperparameter tuning appears to be done on the same folds used for testing.
- Related work treatment is superficial: Prior MCI/AD detection from speech (features, modeling choices, evaluation protocols) needs deeper coverage and more precise contrasts to situate novelty, especially vis-à-vis recent embedding-based and ASR-inclusive approaches.
- Clarity and presentation issues: The manuscript needs substantial editing for readability; acronyms are sometimes undefined; datasets/tools lack proper links/citations; several sentences are unclear; and the abstract is overlong.

Questions to Authors
- Multi-view learning: What exact scheme was used (feature spaces per view, base learners per view, combination rule, any learned weights or stacking, calibration)? Was combination done per-fold using only training data?
- Embeddings: What corpora and sizes were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were fastText models pretrained on large external corpora? Were embeddings fixed or updated? Same hyperparameters for both languages?
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds split by subject (so that a subject’s multiple narratives never appear in both train and test)? How was the embedding-edge threshold selected without test leakage (nested CV/dev set)?
- Preprocessing: Why were filled pauses/false starts/repetitions removed? Do results change if they are retained? Can you report an ablation?
- BoW representation: Why term-frequency instead of tf-idf? Does tf-idf improve or hurt performance across datasets?
- Graph features: How are shortest-path-based metrics computed for disconnected graphs? How many nodes/edges per transcript before/after enrichment (per dataset)?

Additional comments
- Expand Section 4.2.2 to list the exact Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) variables used per dataset, with brief definitions and selection rationale; consider moving full lists to supplementary if needed.
- Clarify preprocessing order and steps (tokenization, stopword removal, lemmatization decision, sentence segmentation method per dataset, disfluency handling) and provide links/citations for transcription norms and datasets (DementiaBank, Western Aphasia Battery/Picnic picture, NURC/SP norms).
- Explicitly name the three datasets in Section 4.1 and define “segmented”; explain why some were manually segmented and some automatically, and provide per-dataset statistics beyond demographics (number of subjects/narratives, sentences/tokens per narrative, vocabulary size).
- Report statistical significance (e.g., stratified randomization tests) or confidence intervals; consider nested CV; control for multiple comparisons.
- Define or fix acronyms/terms on first use (e.g., WLM vs WMS/WLM, ABCD, PLN), and correct typos/grammar and unclear sentences noted in the manuscript (lines cited in the notes).
- Provide the cosine-similarity threshold search range/step and sensitivity analysis; consider reporting average added edges per document and the effect on graph density.
- Ensure ACL citation style compliance and verify missing or malformed citations; shorten the abstract to the essentials.
- If page limits constrain details, provide a reproducibility appendix or supplementary with code, feature lists, and precise hyperparameters.
