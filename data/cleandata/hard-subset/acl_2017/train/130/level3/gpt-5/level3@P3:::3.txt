Summary
The paper applies complex networks (CN) built from word co-occurrence and enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, comparing CN features with linguistic metrics and BoW across three datasets (English Cookie Theft and two Brazilian Portuguese datasets: Cinderella and ABCD). Ensemble and multi-view combinations are explored. Results indicate enriched CN features are competitive and sometimes superior to linguistic metrics and BoW, with best accuracies around 65–80% depending on dataset.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding that complex-network features enriched with embeddings yield competitive performance for MCI detection across three datasets/languages, sometimes outperforming parser-dependent linguistic features.
2) A practical evaluation framework that avoids heavy manual preprocessing and parsing, including experiments with ensemble and multi-view combinations.
3) A cross-dataset analysis spanning English and Brazilian Portuguese narrative/description tasks with both manual and automatic segmentations.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and clinical relevance: The domain, tasks, and challenges (short texts, disfluencies, parsing fragility) are well motivated; the study is interesting and informative for clinical NLP.
- Lightweight, language-agnostic representation: Modeling transcripts as enriched CNs is a reasonable, tool-lean alternative to parser-heavy pipelines; this is especially appealing for Portuguese, where robust parsers are scarce.
- Multi-dataset evaluation: Results across three datasets (including two in Portuguese) and two segmentation regimes increase external validity and interest to the community.
- Sensible baselines: The inclusion of linguistic metrics and BoW baselines provides useful comparative context; some combinations (e.g., multi-view CNE-LM-BoW for ABCD) show gains.
- Transparent limitations of parsing-dependent features: The contrast with a manually revised Cinderella set helps substantiate the claim that parser-heavy features are brittle on noisy transcripts.

Weaknesses
- Missing methodological specifics: The multi-view learning approach is not described beyond “majority vote.” The exact scheme (views, base learners per view, calibration, weighting/stacking vs. voting, training protocol) must be fully specified. Similarly, hyperparameters for SVM/C/γ, K in KNN, RF trees, feature scaling, and selection are not reported.
- Embeddings training corpus unclear: It appears embeddings were trained on the small task datasets, which is likely insufficient. Specify the corpora per language, vocab size, OOV rates, and whether larger external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia/news) were used; otherwise justify and report ablations.
- Preprocessing choices under-justified: Removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) and removing stopwords are both questionable for MCI detection, where they may be strong markers. Provide justification and ablation (with/without). Clarify the exact preprocessing order (tokenization, casing, stopword removal, disfluency handling, sentence segmentation, normalization/lemmatization) and ensure consistency across sections.
- Evaluation rigor: No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals are reported. Accuracy alone is insufficient; add stratified, subject-level 5-fold CV with variance, CI, and significance tests (e.g., McNemar/permutation/paired bootstrap), plus precision/recall/F1 and ROC-AUC. Ensure subject-level splits for ABCD (two narratives per subject) to avoid leakage.
- Incomplete feature descriptions: Section 4.2.2 is too terse; list/define all linguistic features used per language and which were discarded. For CN features, define “shortest path” conventions (giant component, treatment of disconnected graphs), clarify local vs. global metrics, and detail how cosine-similarity edges are added (directed/undirected, weighted by similarity or binarized, threshold tuning protocol).
- BoW weighting choice: Using raw term frequency instead of tf-idf is not justified. Provide rationale and/or include tf-idf results.
- Related work is superficial: Expand coverage and provide precise methodological descriptions for core prior work in MCI/AD detection (features, datasets, evaluation protocols) and situate your approach with direct comparisons where possible.
- Readability and clarity: The manuscript needs substantial editing. The abstract is too long; several sentences are unclear; acronyms (e.g., WLM, ABCD, PLN) and terms should be defined at first use; some dataset descriptions are confusing or contradictory (e.g., Cookie Theft sampling, “segmentation” meaning).

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Multi-view: Precisely describe the multi-view pipeline. Are classifiers trained per feature space then combined by unweighted majority vote? Any learned weights/stacking? How are ties resolved? Are base learners identical across views?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used per language? Were embeddings trained solely on the task datasets or on large external corpora? Report vocabulary size, OOV rates, and whether subword embeddings were trained jointly or separately per language. Did you tune embedding dim/window/epochs? Any domain adaptation?
- Preprocessing: Exact order and rules for removing disfluencies/stopwords and sentence segmentation (manual vs. DeepBond vs. ASR simulation). Did you test retaining disfluencies and function words? If not, can you provide a quick ablation?
- CV protocol: Are folds stratified by subject (especially in ABCD with two retells)? Were thresholds and hyperparameters tuned within inner CV to avoid test leakage?
- CN construction: Are added similarity edges weighted by cosine or binarized at threshold τ? Is the network undirected? How do you handle disconnected graphs when computing average shortest path/diameter? On which component are these computed?
- BoW: Why tf instead of tf-idf? Any normalization (l2) and feature selection? Can you report tf-idf results?
- Metrics: Please add significance tests and report CIs; also include F1/ROC-AUC.

Additional Comments
- Expand Section 4.2.2 with a complete list of “linguistic features” used (English Coh-Metrix and Coh-Metrix-Dementia), and cite tool versions/URLs.
- Clarify which corpus produced the embeddings (lines 290–299, Sec. 4.2.1). Report threshold tuning for cosine similarity (search grid, selection criterion).
- Dataset clarity: Explicitly name the three datasets early; define “segmented”; explain why some narratives were manually segmented; ensure Cookie Theft class composition is correct (MCI vs controls) and not AD. Provide links/citations for DementiaBank, Cookie Theft/BDAE, Western Aphasia Battery (Picnic picture), NURC/SP transcription norms.
- Table 1 (and others): Include counts per class, number of narratives, tokens per narrative, vocabulary size, disfluency rates, duration, and segmentation method.
- Justify removing disfluencies (lines 375–388), with references; cite transcription norms (lines 340–440).
- Feature definitions: Specify “shortest path” in feature 6; define the exact threshold τ; and state whether assortativity/diameter are global or summarized over components.
- Report compliance with ACL citation style (line 185) and fix missing citations (line 172). Shorten the abstract.
- Language/clarity: Reword unclear or ungrammatical sentences (e.g., lines 22–25, 29–30, 34–38, 70, 73, 115–117, 147–149, 323–332, 641–645). Fix minor errors/typos (e.g., lines 322, 620, 735).
- If expanding feature descriptions beyond page limits, check ACL guidelines for supplementary material.
- Consider reporting weighted networks (co-occurrence frequency and cosine weight) and an ablation on stopword retention, which may carry diagnostic signal in MCI.
