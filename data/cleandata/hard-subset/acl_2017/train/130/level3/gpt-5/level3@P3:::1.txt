Summary
The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). It evaluates English Cookie Theft descriptions and two Brazilian Portuguese datasets (Cinderella narratives and ABCD immediate/delayed recalls), comparing complex-network (CN) features (with and without embeddings), linguistic metrics, and bag-of-words using several classifiers, plus simple ensemble and multi-view combinations. Reported accuracies range roughly from the mid-60s to 80%, with the best results on the ABCD dataset (≈80% with multi-view).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Application and evaluation of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, to classify MCI vs. control from speech transcripts, including lower-resource Portuguese. Primary.
2) Multi-view combination of CN, linguistic, and BoW features showing modest gains in some settings. Secondary.
3) Empirical evidence that CN+embeddings can be competitive with and sometimes outperform hand-crafted linguistic metrics in this domain. Secondary.

Strengths
- Novel use in Portuguese clinical narratives: The study targets a clinically relevant, lower-resource setting (Brazilian Portuguese) where robust parsers are scarce. This increases the work’s potential impact beyond English-only studies.
- Lightweight, parser-free features: CN features with embedding enrichment avoid fragile parsing pipelines for disfluent narratives, aligning with the goal of scalable, minimally supervised assessment.
- Clear, interpretable baseline comparisons: The paper contrasts CN (with/without embeddings) against linguistic metrics and BoW using several standard classifiers, making the empirical comparison reasonably transparent.
- Reported performance aligns with prior literature: Accuracies between ≈60–85% match prior work ranges, supporting the claim that language-based detection is promising yet challenging.
- Practical contribution: Demonstrates that embedding-enriched networks mitigate short-text limitations in network topology, a known issue in CN-based text modeling.

Weaknesses
- Small, heterogeneous datasets and potential evaluation leakage: The Portuguese datasets are very small (Cinderella: 20 MCI + 20 controls; ABCD: 23 MCI + 20 controls). For ABCD, subjects contribute multiple narratives; it is unclear whether cross-validation is subject-disjoint. If folds are at the narrative level, the same subject could appear in train and test, inflating accuracy. This must be clarified and, if needed, corrected with subject-level CV.
- Reproducibility and clarity of embedding training: It is unclear what corpora were used to train embeddings (only task transcripts vs. external corpora like Portuguese Wikipedia/large web corpora). Without this, results are hard to reproduce and the role of external data is ambiguous, especially for Portuguese.
- Unclear integration and benefit of word-level vs. subword modeling: The paper mentions fastText-style subword embeddings for OOV handling but does not precisely describe how word-level and subword information are used in network construction and whether combining them yields gains. The “out-of-vocabulary” definition and its source are not specified.
- Overlong, unfocused exposition and literature review: Sections 1–3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 contain excessive background and general citations (e.g., Salton 1989; sentiment classification; pedestrian detection) that distract from the core contribution. The related work should more tightly focus on cognitive impairment, discourse analysis, and network/embedding methods directly relevant to this task.
- Inconsistent tone and claims: The introduction reads optimistic about accuracy improvements, while the conclusion emphasizes that distinguishing healthy vs impaired is not easy. The paper should reconcile these statements and more carefully calibrate claims versus the small datasets and limited generalization.
- Missing analyses and reporting details: No statistical significance testing, confidence intervals, or per-fold variance. Threshold tuning is mentioned but it is unclear if done within training folds (nested CV). No ablations for the similarity threshold or network construction steps. No subject demographics balancing checks in folds (age/education can confound results).

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: For ABCD and any dataset with multiple narratives per subject, did you ensure subject-disjoint folds? If not, can you re-run with subject-level CV and report results?
- Embedding training: Precisely which corpora (size, domain, language) did you use to train the fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were embeddings trained exclusively on task transcripts (ABCD/Cinderella) or augmented with external data? Please provide training settings and vocabulary coverage.
- OOV definition: How do you define OOV in your pipeline? Relative to which vocabulary (embedding vocabulary, BoW vocabulary)? What proportion of tokens were OOV before subword composition?
- Word- vs subword-based networks: Are edges computed purely from word-level vectors composed via subword models, or are character n-grams/nodes used directly? Did you test any hybrid networks or show accuracy changes when subword composition is disabled?
- Data splits and confounds: Were folds stratified by class and controlled for age/education imbalances? Can you report per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices?
- Threshold selection: Was the cosine-similarity threshold tuned within the training folds (nested CV)? Please report sensitivity to this threshold and the number of edges added per transcript.
- Impact of stopword removal: Given discourse/cohesion signals, did you assess performance without removing stopwords or with POS-based filtering?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Improve reproducibility: Release code, configuration files, and (where permissible) derived features; include one Portuguese transcript with an English translation; provide sample CN visualizations before/after embedding enrichment and illustrative nearest neighbors for key tokens.
- Clarify Portuguese resources: Explain how embeddings are computed for Portuguese, including tokenization, diacritics handling, casing, and how NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms affect preprocessing and modeling.
- Tighten writing and structure: Compress Sections 1–3 and streamline 4.1/4.2.3 to focus on your method, network enrichment, and evaluation protocol. Move general CN/NLP background to a concise related work.
- Focus the literature review: Remove or de-emphasize tangential citations (Salton 1989 in BoW primer; sentiment classification; pedestrian detection) and concentrate on dementia/MCI, discourse, CN/graph text models, and embeddings.
- Reporting: Add confidence intervals or standard deviations over CV folds; include significance tests for multi-view/ensemble gains; report macro/micro F1.
- Minor technical/typographical fixes:
  - “as it a lightweight” → “as it is a lightweight” (Abstract, line 29).
  - PLN → NLP (around line 188).
  - Duplicated words (around line 264).
  - Decimal formatting consistency in Table 3 (use periods consistently: e.g., 72.0 not 72,0).
  - Duplicate DOI and incorrect reference link(s) around lines ~992–1002; fix URLs and missing DOI suffixes across several Amancio entries.
  - Ensure consistent naming of datasets and classifier labels across Tables 4–7; verify Cinderella accuracies are not duplicated/mislabeled between Tables 5 and 6.
- Interpretation: Temper claims to reflect dataset size and language/domain specificity; explicitly acknowledge limits on generalization and the need for larger, public, subject-disjoint benchmarks.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The idea of embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks for MCI detection in Portuguese is interesting and practically motivated, but the paper needs clearer methodology (especially embeddings and CV), tighter writing, and stronger reproducibility to meet ACL standards. Addressing the evaluation and clarity issues could substantially strengthen the submission.
