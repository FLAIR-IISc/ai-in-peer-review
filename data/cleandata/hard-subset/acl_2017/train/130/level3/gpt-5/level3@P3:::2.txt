Summary and Contributions
- The paper targets automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts across three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft in English; Cinderella narratives and ABCD recall in Brazilian Portuguese).
- It proposes a graph-based representation of transcripts using word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges, from which diverse graph-theoretic features (centrality, clustering, path-based, assortativity, etc.) are extracted. These are compared and combined with linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag-of-Words.
- Evaluation uses 5-fold cross-validation with several classifiers and ensemble/multi-view combinations; performance varies notably by dataset.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength):
1) Cross-lingual evaluation of an embedding-enriched co-occurrence network feature set for MCI detection on three clinical datasets, including Portuguese resources that are less commonly studied.
2) Empirical finding that graph-derived features are competitive with or better than standard linguistic metrics on noisy transcripts, and that multi-view/ensemble combinations can boost accuracy on some datasets.
3) A lightweight, parsing-agnostic pipeline intended to be robust to disfluencies and limited NLP tooling, relevant for clinical settings.

Strengths
- Clinically relevant, well-motivated task: Language-based screening for MCI is important and under-resourced, and the paper focuses on realistic narrative/description tasks used in clinics (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD). This increases potential impact.
- Multilingual, multi-dataset evaluation: Testing on English and Brazilian Portuguese with different elicitation paradigms (picture description, story narration, story recall) provides some evidence of generality and sheds light on where methods work best or fail.
- Lightweight, parser-independent features: The graph-based approach avoids dependence on high-precision parsing and costly manual transcript cleaning. This is attractive for transcripts with disfluencies and for languages with weaker tools.
- Comparative evaluation with multiple classifiers and multi-view combinations: The authors compare CNE against CN, BoW, and Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) and explore ensembles/multi-view, offering a broader perspective than a single-model comparison.
- Sensible baseline inclusion: The paper includes BoW and Coh-Metrix baselines, which are commonly used in dementia detection work, enabling some contextualization of the results.

Weaknesses
- Data clarity and possible leakage/confounds: The instance definition and counts are unclear and inconsistent (e.g., Cookie Theft selection of “43 transcripts of patients with MCI” sampled from AD cohorts; control counts for that dataset are not clearly specified). In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives; unless folds are subject-independent, cross-validation risks leakage. Additionally, Table 3 suggests strong demographic confounds (e.g., mean age 61 vs. 72 in ABCD control vs. MCI). Without subject-level splits and demographic control/covariates, reported gains may reflect confounding rather than true signal.
- Evaluation metrics and reporting: Class distributions are not reported per dataset; accuracy alone is inadequate if classes are imbalanced. The paper should report per-class precision/recall/F1 and macro-F1 (and ideally ROC-AUC), along with confusion matrices and class priors. Reported accuracies have excessive precision for small samples, and there is no statistical significance testing or confidence intervals, making it hard to assess reliability.
- Methodological motivation and ablation: The motivation for enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges is not sufficiently contrasted against simpler alternatives, such as (i) networks defined purely by embedding similarity, (ii) document-level pooled embeddings (e.g., averaged word vectors, doc2vec), (iii) lexical cohesion measures derived directly from embeddings, or (iv) simpler graph kernels. An ablation isolating the contribution of similarity edges, threshold choice, and embedding source/domain would strengthen the case.
- ML setup under-specified: It is unclear how the similarity threshold and other hyperparameters were tuned (whether strictly within training folds); feature scaling/normalization is not described; and there is no feature selection/regularization discussion given high feature dimensionality vs. small n. No variance across folds is reported, and multi-view voting/weighting lacks principled learning or validation.
- Writing quality and presentation: The manuscript has many grammatical/spelling issues and organization problems (e.g., tables and narrative sometimes describe study-level rather than transcript-level data; mislabeled figures; inconsistent counts). These hinder interpretability and reproducibility.
- Missed domain-specific baselines/tools: The approach does not leverage domain-specific resources (e.g., clinical concept normalization, UMLS mapping, entity linking, disfluency detection) that have shown value in clinical NLP. While the “tool-light” motivation is fair, at least a discussion or limited comparison would contextualize trade-offs.

Questions to Authors
- Data splits and leakage: Are folds strictly subject-independent across all datasets, especially for ABCD with two narratives per subject? If not, please re-run with subject-level CV and report updated results.
- Class balance: What are the exact number of MCI vs. control transcripts in each dataset and in each fold? Please report class priors and per-class metrics (precision/recall/F1), macro-F1, and ROC-AUC.
- Demographic confounds: Given large age/education differences (e.g., ABCD), did you control for demographics (matching, regression, stratification, or including them as features)? If not, can you evaluate after controlling for these variables?
- Hyperparameter tuning: How was the embedding-similarity threshold selected (0.7; 0.4)? Was tuning nested within CV? Were any other graph construction parameters tuned? How sensitive are results to the threshold?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings? Are they in-domain (clinical/speech) or general? Are the two languages comparable in training size/quality?
- Ablations: Can you report (i) co-occurrence-only vs. similarity-only networks, (ii) pooled document embeddings or doc2vec, (iii) CNE with/without specific topological measures, and (iv) CNE vs. simple lexical cohesion metrics based on embeddings?
- Statistical testing: Please provide confidence intervals over CV folds and statistical significance testing for key comparisons (e.g., CNE vs. LM, multi-view vs. best single model).
- Evaluation granularity: For ABCD (immediate vs. delayed recall), do you evaluate them separately as conditions? If combined, can you analyze performance per condition?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, feature extraction scripts, and exact train/test splits to ensure results can be verified?

Overall, this paper addresses a meaningful clinical problem with a parser-light, multilingual approach and provides promising results. However, substantial clarifications and methodological fixes (especially around data splits, confounds, and evaluation metrics) are required before the empirical claims can be trusted. The manuscript also needs careful language editing.
