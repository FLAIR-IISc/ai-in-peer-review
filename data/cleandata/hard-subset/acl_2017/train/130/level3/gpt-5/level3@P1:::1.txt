Summary
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word-embedding-based similarity edges to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). Topological features from these enriched networks are compared against standard linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag-of-Words (BoW), across three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese). Classifiers include SVM (linear/RBF), k-NN, Random Forest, and GaussianNB, as well as simple ensembles and multi-view combinations. The method generally outperforms purely linguistic metrics and, in multi-view settings, yields accuracy improvements (e.g., +10% absolute on Cinderella and up to 80% on ABCD).

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An empirical demonstration that complex-network topology enriched with word embeddings is a robust alternative to parser-dependent linguistic features for detecting MCI from noisy, disfluent transcripts, including in a lower-resource language (Portuguese).
- Secondary: Evidence that simple multi-view and ensemble combinations of network features with BoW and linguistic metrics bring consistent gains over single-feature-space models across datasets.
- Tertiary: A lightweight, language-agnostic pipeline that avoids lemmatization and dependency parsing, with practical potential for fully automated screening where ASR outputs are imperfect.

Strengths
- Practical, language-agnostic modeling for disfluent transcripts: By avoiding lemmatization and syntactic parsing, the approach is less brittle for clinical speech with agrammaticality and disfluencies. The enriched network (CNE) consistently improves over plain co-occurrence networks (CN) and often outperforms linguistic metrics across datasets (e.g., Cookie Theft: SVM-RBF 0.626 CNE vs 0.581 LM; Cinderella: 0.650 CNE vs 0.475 LM; ABCD: 0.717 CNE vs 0.590 LM with SVM-linear).
- Cross-lingual evaluation including lower-resource Portuguese: The inclusion of Cinderella and ABCD (both Portuguese) addresses a critical gap. Results on automatically segmented ABCD transcripts (CNE-LM-BoW 0.802) underscore robustness to more realistic preprocessing.
- Sensible enhancement for short texts via embedding-based links: The motivation to densify near-linear networks in short/fragmented narratives is sound, and the consistent CNE > CN deltas support the design choice.
- Multi-view/ensemble gains are clearly quantified: The paper shows tangible improvements from combining feature spaces (e.g., Cinderella ensemble of algorithms with CNE-LM: 0.750; ABCD multi-view CNE-LM-BoW: 0.802), indicating complementary information across representations.
- Clear feature engineering on the network side: A well-defined set of topological metrics (centralities, clustering, assortativity, path length statistics with mean/SD/skew) provides transparency and interpretability opportunities.

Weaknesses
- Limited datasets and evaluation rigor: Sample sizes are small (e.g., Cinderella 20 MCI/20 control; ABCD 23 MCI/20 control with two narratives per MCI subject), making generalization uncertain. Only accuracy is reported; there is no per-class breakdown, confidence intervals, or statistical significance testing. It is unclear if cross-validation folds are subject-disjoint (critical for ABCD where the same subject produces two narratives), raising risk of speaker leakage.
- Insufficient detail on embeddings and OOV handling: While fastText-style subword embeddings are mentioned (Bojanowski et al.), the training corpora, vocabulary sizes, language coverage (separate English/Portuguese models?), and whether external data were used are not specified. The notion of “out-of-vocabulary” is invoked but not operationalized (relative to which vocabulary? transcripts-only vs external corpora?). Threshold selection for similarity edges (0.7/0.4) lacks a principled or data-driven justification beyond brief tuning.
- Ambiguities in graph construction and feature computation: It is unclear whether edges are added across sentence boundaries; whether networks are per narrative or aggregated; and how punctuation/ASR artifacts influence tokenization. The interplay between word-level nodes and subword-informed embeddings could be explained more precisely (the text mentions subword embeddings but networks over words; clarifying this would avoid confusion about “subword networks”).
- Overlong and somewhat unfocused exposition: Sections 1–3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 include general background and citations (e.g., Salton, sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) that add little to the core contribution. The paper would benefit from a tighter related work focused on dementia/MCI language analytics and on graph-based NLP specifically.
- Writing quality and presentation issues: There are multiple typos and formatting inconsistencies (e.g., “as it a lightweight”; “PLN” should be “NLP”; duplicated words; decimal commas in Table 3; duplicate DOIs/links), and a somewhat contradictory tone (early optimism vs concluding difficulty of the task). Baselines are limited (BoW with tf only; no tf-idf n-grams, doc2vec, or simple neural baselines), making it hard to situate gains relative to strong text classification baselines.

Questions to Authors
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were pre-trained fastText models used, or were models trained only on the small datasets? What were vocabulary sizes and OOV rates per dataset?
- Graph construction: Are edges allowed across sentence boundaries? Are networks built per narrative (one graph per sample)? How sensitive are results to the similarity threshold; was it selected via nested CV?
- Evaluation protocol: For ABCD, are immediate and delayed recalls from the same subject placed in the same fold to avoid leakage? Are folds subject-disjoint across all datasets?
- Feature processing: Were features standardized per fold? Any dimensionality reduction or feature selection applied?
- Baselines: How does BoW with tf-idf n-grams (e.g., SVM-linear) perform? Have you compared to doc2vec or logistic regression with regularization?
- Reproducibility: Will code, trained models, and (anonymized) features be released? Can you share an English translation of a Portuguese transcript and example network visualizations before/after enrichment?

Additional comments
- Tighten the literature review; focus on dementia/MCI language work and graph-based NLP; prune unrelated citations.
- Compress Sections 1–3 and clarify methods with a precise description of embedding training, OOV definition, tokenization, sentence boundary handling, and threshold tuning strategy.
- Report precision/recall/F1 per class, confidence intervals, and statistical tests; ensure subject-disjoint CV.
- Add stronger text baselines (tf-idf character/word n-grams with linear SVM), and consider a simple neural baseline.
- Fix typographical/formatting issues: “as it a lightweight”; “PLN”→“NLP”; duplicated words; Table 3 decimal formatting; duplicate DOI/incorrect links. Standardize references. Provide examples of NURC/SP transcription conventions and a sample Portuguese transcript (with English translation). Include visualizations of networks pre/post enrichment.
