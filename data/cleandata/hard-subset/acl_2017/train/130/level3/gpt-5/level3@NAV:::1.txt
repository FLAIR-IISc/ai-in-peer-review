Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word-adjacency complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), evaluating English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD). Reported accuracies range roughly 60–80%, with multi-view/ensemble combinations improving over single feature families.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that complex-network features enriched by embeddings are competitive for MCI detection, including on Portuguese, a lower-resource language, and can outperform traditional linguistic metrics under noisy transcripts.
2) Demonstration that multi-view and ensemble learning yields non-trivial gains (e.g., +10% on Cinderella; ~80% on ABCD with CNE-LM-BoW).
3) A practical, parser-light pipeline that is more tolerant to disfluencies than parser-dependent features.

Strengths
- Addresses low-resource language: Applying NLP to Brazilian Portuguese MCI assessments is valuable and underexplored, with clear clinical relevance.
- Competitive performance across diverse feature sets: Enriched complex networks (CNE) consistently match or exceed linguistic metrics; multi-view combinations achieve the best results (e.g., 0.80 on ABCD; 0.75 on Cinderella).
- Analysis on both raw and manually revised transcripts: This comparison supports the claim that CN features are less sensitive to parsing-quality than traditional metrics.
- Clear framing of a language-independent approach: The complex-network representation is lightweight and avoids heavy manual normalization.

Weaknesses
- Limited data and potential leakage: Portuguese datasets are very small (20 MCI/20 controls for Cinderella; ABCD with repeated narratives per subject). Clarify whether cross-validation is subject-wise to avoid leakage between immediate/delayed retells.
- Insufficient technical detail on embeddings: It is unclear how Portuguese embeddings were trained (only ABCD+Cinderella vs. external corpora), how OOV is defined/handled, and how subword n-grams interact with word-level networks.
- Ambiguity on network variants: The relationship between word-level and subword-augmented networks and whether their combination improves accuracy is not specified.
- Reproducibility and accessibility: Portuguese transcripts are not public; provide at least an English-translated example, code, and trained embeddings/models.
- Presentation issues: Overly long Sections 1–3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3; literature review includes tangential citations (e.g., Salton 1989, sentiment, pedestrian detection). Several technical/typographical errors (e.g., “as it a lightweight,” PLN→NLP, duplicated words, decimal formatting in Table 3, duplicate DOI/incorrect link).

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Were cross-validation folds stratified by subject (especially for ABCD’s immediate/delayed retells)? If not, please re-run subject-wise CV.
- What corpora were used to train Portuguese embeddings? Provide training size, preprocessing, and vocabulary statistics.
- How exactly are OOV tokens defined with subword embeddings, and how are their vectors used to add similarity edges?
- Do you have evidence that combining word- and subword-based networks improves accuracy over either alone?
- Please add a Portuguese transcript snippet with an English translation, and include a sample network visualization and embedding-similarity thresholds.
