- Paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI; evaluated on English Cookie Theft and two Brazilian Portuguese datasets (Cinderella, ABCD), reporting ≈60–80% accuracy (best ≈80% on ABCD).

- Main contribution: application and evaluation of embedding-enriched complex network (CN) features for MCI detection, including lower-resource Portuguese.

- Secondary contributions: multi-view combination of CN, linguistic, and BoW features; empirical evidence that CN+embeddings can match or outperform hand-crafted linguistic metrics.

- Strength: novel, clinically relevant application in Portuguese where robust parsers are scarce.

- Strength: lightweight, parser-free CN features suitable for disfluent clinical narratives.

- Strength: clear, interpretable baseline comparisons across CN (±embeddings), linguistic metrics, and BoW with multiple classifiers.

- Strength: reported performance aligns with prior literature, supporting feasibility of language-based MCI detection.

- Strength: demonstrates embedding enrichment mitigates short-text limitations in CN modeling.

- Weakness: very small, heterogeneous datasets (Cinderella: 40 subjects; ABCD: 43 subjects) and potential evaluation leakage if cross-validation is not subject-disjoint.

- Weakness: unclear reproducibility — unspecified corpora and settings used to train fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese.

- Weakness: unclear OOV definition and proportions, and unclear how word-level vs. subword information is integrated into network construction.

- Weakness: overlong, unfocused background and literature review containing tangential citations that distract from core contributions.

- Weakness: inconsistent tone between optimistic introduction and cautious conclusion around generalization and difficulty of detection.

- Weakness: missing statistical analyses — no significance testing, confidence intervals, per-fold variance, or nested CV for threshold tuning.

- Weakness: no ablation studies for similarity threshold, network construction choices, or stopword handling; potential confounds (age/education) not addressed in splits.

- Question: Did you ensure subject-disjoint cross-validation for datasets with multiple narratives per subject (e.g., ABCD)?

- Question: Precisely which corpora, sizes, domains, and training settings were used for the English and Portuguese embeddings?

- Question: How is OOV defined (relative to which vocabulary), and what proportion of tokens were OOV before subword composition?

- Question: Are edges computed from word-level vectors composed via subword models or from character n-grams/nodes directly, and were hybrids tested?

- Question: Were folds stratified/controlled for age and education, and can you report per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices?

- Question: Was the cosine-similarity threshold tuned within training folds (nested CV), and how sensitive are results to the threshold and number of edges added?

- Question: Did you assess performance without stopword removal or with POS-based filtering, given discourse/cohesion signals?

- Suggestion: Improve reproducibility by releasing code, configs, sample transcripts (with translations if needed), CN visualizations, and embedding vocab details.

- Suggestion: Clarify Portuguese preprocessing (tokenization, diacritics, casing) and transcription norms used.

- Suggestion: Tighten writing and related work to focus on dementia/MCI, discourse, CN/graph text models, and embeddings; remove tangential citations.

- Suggestion: Report confidence intervals/standard deviations, macro/micro F1, significance tests, and per-fold results; fix minor typos, reference issues, and table labeling.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline — promising and practically motivated but needs clearer methodology (embeddings, CV), stronger evaluation, and improved reproducibility to be acceptable.
