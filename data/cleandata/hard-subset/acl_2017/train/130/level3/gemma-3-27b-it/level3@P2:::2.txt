This paper investigates the use of complex network features for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, evaluating the approach across three datasets. The core idea is to model transcripts as graphs, enriching word co-occurrence networks with word embeddings, and leveraging topological properties of these graphs as features for classification.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of complex network representations as a viable feature set for MCI detection from speech, offering a potentially lightweight and language-independent approach. While graph-based methods have been applied in NLP, applying them specifically to the problem of MCI detection using speech transcripts, and demonstrating competitive performance compared to traditional linguistic features, represents a novel application. A secondary contribution is the evaluation of this approach across multiple datasets – English and Portuguese – demonstrating some degree of generalizability. Finally, the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning methods to combine network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features offers a practical enhancement to the core method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Clinical Problem:** The paper addresses a clinically important problem – the early detection of MCI – and leverages a potentially valuable data source: speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments. Early diagnosis is crucial for intervention, making this a worthwhile research direction.
2. **Novel Application of Complex Networks:** The application of complex network analysis to speech transcript data for MCI detection is relatively novel. The authors explore a different representation of text compared to traditional feature engineering approaches, which could potentially capture subtle linguistic differences indicative of cognitive impairment.
3. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the proposed method across three datasets (including both English and Portuguese) strengthens the findings and suggests a degree of robustness. The inclusion of datasets with varying characteristics (short descriptions vs. longer narratives) is particularly valuable.
4. **Feature Combination:**  The investigation of ensemble and multi-view learning demonstrates an effort to improve performance by combining the strengths of different feature sets (network-based, linguistic, and bag-of-words). The reported improvements, especially on the Cinderella dataset, are encouraging.
5. **Clear Methodological Description:** The paper provides a relatively clear description of the complex network construction process, including the enrichment with word embeddings, and the topological measures used for feature extraction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Poor Writing Quality:** The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and inconsistencies in terminology. This significantly hinders readability and impacts the overall clarity of the presentation. A thorough revision by a native English speaker is essential.
2. **Unclear Problem Definition & Data Description:** The description of the datasets is confusing. The paper refers to "datasets" but often describes the *studies* from which the transcripts were obtained. Details about the individual transcript characteristics (length, number of speakers, etc.) are lacking. Reported participant counts and demographic data are inconsistent across tables.  The notion of an "instance" for classification isn't well-defined: is it a sentence, a narrative, or something else?
3. **Lack of Justification for Graph Enrichment:** The motivation for enriching the co-occurrence network with word embeddings is not fully convincing. The authors mention Perozzi et al. (2014), but don't adequately explain why this specific approach is superior to simpler methods of measuring word similarity or incorporating semantic information.
4. **Limited Use of Domain-Specific NLP Tools:** The paper largely ignores the wealth of existing biomedical NLP tools and resources that could be leveraged for feature extraction. Considering features derived from tools designed for analyzing clinical text could significantly improve performance.
5. **Inappropriate Evaluation Metric:** Reporting only accuracy is problematic given potential class imbalance in the datasets.  The number of MCI vs. control subjects may not be equal, making accuracy a misleading metric. F1-score, precision, recall, and AUC should be reported to provide a more comprehensive evaluation. 
6. **Statistical Significance Testing:** The reported results lack statistical significance testing. Given the small dataset sizes, it’s crucial to assess whether the observed performance differences are statistically significant. p-values should be provided for all comparisons. The reported accuracy values are presented with unjustified precision (e.g., 0.8018) given the limited sample sizes.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you clarify the precise definition of an "instance" in your classification setup? (e.g., is it a single narrative, a segment of a narrative, etc.).
2. What is the class distribution (number of MCI vs. control subjects) for each of the datasets?
3. Why were specific features from Coh-Metrix-Dementia discarded? Were these decisions based on preliminary experiments or theoretical considerations?
4. What is the rationale for using a cosine similarity threshold of X for adding edges based on word embeddings? Was this threshold optimized, and if so, how?
5. Have you considered using other evaluation metrics besides accuracy, such as F1-score, precision, recall, or AUC? If not, why?



**Additional Comments:**

- The citation style is inconsistent.
- The supplementary material contains a lot of irrelevant information (e.g., the full transcript example).
- Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential areas for future research.
