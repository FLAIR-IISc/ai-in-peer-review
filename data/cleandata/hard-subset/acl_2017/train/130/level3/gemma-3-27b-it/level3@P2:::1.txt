## Review for ACL 2017

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts in Portuguese. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, demonstrating that network-based features can outperform traditional linguistic features for this task, and that combining these approaches through ensemble and multi-view learning provides further improvements.

**Summary of Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in the application of complex network analysis, specifically word co-occurrence networks enhanced with word embeddings, to the challenging problem of MCI detection from spontaneous speech in Portuguese. While complex networks have been previously used in NLP, their application to the analysis of cognitively impaired speech, particularly in a lower-resource language like Portuguese, is novel. A secondary contribution is the comparative evaluation of network features against traditional linguistic features, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Finally, the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to further improve classification accuracy represents a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application to a Challenging Problem:** The application of complex network analysis to the detection of MCI from speech transcripts is a promising approach. This is especially relevant given the difficulties associated with parsing the often agrammatical speech of patients with cognitive impairment. The method’s ability to bypass reliance on robust parsing is a significant advantage.
2. **Focus on a Lower-Resource Language:** The work addresses an important gap in the research landscape by focusing on Portuguese, a language with fewer available NLP resources compared to English. This contribution increases the generalizability of NLP techniques for cognitive assessment beyond widely studied languages.
3. **Demonstrated Performance Improvements:** The results presented show that the proposed complex network features, and particularly those enriched with word embeddings, achieve competitive and, in some cases, superior performance compared to traditional linguistic features across all three datasets. The observed improvements with ensemble and multi-view learning are also encouraging.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three distinct datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD – provides a reasonable level of robustness and demonstrates the adaptability of the approach to varying speech elicitation paradigms (description vs. narrative).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Sizes:** The primary limitation of this study is the relatively small size of the datasets used. The Cinderella and ABCD datasets, in particular, contain only 20 controls and 20/23 MCI patients, respectively. These sample sizes raise concerns about the statistical power and generalizability of the findings.
2. **Overly Detailed and Redundant Presentation:** Sections 1-3, and portions of Section 4.1 and 4.2.3, contain a significant amount of background information and descriptive details that could be substantially condensed. The paper would benefit from a more focused presentation of the core methodological contributions.
3. **Lack of Clarity Regarding Embeddings:** The description of how word embeddings were generated is insufficient. It is unclear whether embeddings were trained solely on the provided datasets (ABCD, Cinderella) or if external corpora were used. The choice of pre-trained vs. task-specific embeddings significantly impacts interpretability and performance. Furthermore, the interaction between word-level and subword (n-gram) network modeling is not clearly explained.
4. **Irrelevant or Weakly Justified Citations:** The literature review includes citations that are tangential to the core contributions (e.g., Salton 1989, sentiment classification, pedestrian detection). Pruning the literature review to focus on relevant work in computational linguistics, cognitive assessment, and complex network analysis would strengthen the paper.
5. **Technical and Typographical Errors:** The manuscript contains multiple technical and typographical errors (e.g., "as it a lightweight", incorrect references, decimal formatting). Careful proofreading is essential.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Were the word embeddings pre-trained on a larger corpus, or were they trained specifically on the provided datasets? If pre-trained, please specify the corpus used.
2. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the process used to enrich the co-occurrence networks with semantic information? Specifically, what threshold for cosine similarity was used, and how was this value determined?
3. Given the small dataset sizes, have you performed any power analysis to estimate the statistical significance of the observed improvements?
4. Could you provide an example of a transcribed segment, the corresponding complex network visualization (before and after embedding enrichment), and a brief explanation of how the network features capture relevant linguistic information? This would help readers better understand the practical application of your method.



**Additional Comments:**

Adding an English translation of a representative transcript example would significantly enhance the accessibility of the paper. Consider including ablation studies to assess the individual contribution of each network feature. The definition of “out-of-vocabulary” words (Line 264) needs clarification.
