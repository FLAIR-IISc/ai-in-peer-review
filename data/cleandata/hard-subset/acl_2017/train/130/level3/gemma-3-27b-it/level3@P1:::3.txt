This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts as networks, leveraging network topology to differentiate between individuals with and without MCI, and demonstrate improvements over traditional linguistic features and baseline classifiers across three datasets. The core contributions are the application of complex network analysis to this clinical problem, the use of word embeddings to enrich network representation for short texts, and the demonstration of improved performance through ensemble and multi-view learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Network Science:** Applying complex network analysis to the problem of MCI detection from speech is a valuable contribution. While network analysis has been used in NLP, its application to clinical speech assessment, particularly for a condition like MCI where subtle linguistic changes are critical, is relatively unexplored. This offers a potentially robust and language-independent approach.
2. **Addressing the Challenge of Disfluencies:** Recognizing the impact of disfluencies on traditional parsing-based linguistic feature extraction, the authors propose a method that is less reliant on perfect transcript quality. This is a significant practical advantage, as manual transcript correction is often infeasible for large-scale assessments. The attempt to bypass the need for meticulously cleaned transcripts is commendable.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona Battery – strengthens the findings.  The datasets vary in language (English and Portuguese) and narrative structure (short descriptions vs. longer stories), providing a more generalizable assessment of the proposed method. The comparison with established linguistic features adds further weight to the results.
4. **Effective Combination of Methods:**  The use of ensemble and multi-view learning demonstrates a thoughtful approach to maximizing performance. Combining network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features resulted in notable improvements, highlighting the complementary nature of these approaches. The 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and 4.71% on the Arizona dataset are substantial.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Methodological Detail in Multi-view Learning:** The description of the multi-view learning approach is insufficient. While mentioned as a key contributor to performance gains, the exact combination scheme (e.g., weighting methods, specific classifiers used for each view) is not clearly defined.  This makes it difficult to assess the robustness and replicability of the results.
2. **Insufficient Description of Linguistic Features:** Section 4.2.2 provides a limited description of the linguistic features used. While referencing the Coh-Metrix tools, a more detailed explanation of *which* specific features were employed and their relevance to MCI would be beneficial.  A table listing the selected features would significantly improve clarity.
3. **Justification for Preprocessing Choices:** The removal of disfluencies is questionable. Disfluencies *themselves* can be indicative of cognitive impairment. The authors need to strongly justify this preprocessing step, potentially by demonstrating its impact on performance with and without disfluency removal, or by acknowledging its potential drawback.
4. **Statistical Significance Testing Missing:** The results are presented as accuracy scores without any statistical significance testing.  It is essential to report p-values or confidence intervals to determine if the observed performance differences are statistically significant, rather than due to chance.
5. **Limited Related Work Discussion:** The related work section lacks depth, particularly regarding existing research on applying machine learning to MCI detection using linguistic features. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods would contextualize the contribution of this work more effectively.
6. **Word Embedding Corpus Unclear:** It's unclear whether the word embeddings were trained on the three datasets used for classification, or a larger external corpus. If the former, the small dataset size could limit the quality of the embeddings. This needs clarification.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Please provide a detailed description of the multi-view learning scheme, including the classifiers used for each feature space and the method for combining their predictions.
2. Can you elaborate on the specific linguistic features selected from Coh-Metrix and their rationale for inclusion? A table listing the features would be helpful.
3. What justification is provided for the removal of disfluencies? Have you considered analyzing the impact of disfluencies on performance?
4.  Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to support the reported performance improvements.
5. Which corpus was used to train the word embeddings? If it was the datasets used for classification, can you comment on the potential impact of dataset size on embedding quality?

**Additional Comments:**

- The abstract is too lengthy; please condense it to meet conference guidelines.
- Numerous sentences require rewriting for improved clarity and grammatical correctness.
- Provide explicit citations/links for referenced datasets and tools.
- Define acronyms and specialized terms (e.g., WML subtest, ABCD).
- Thoroughly review and address all minor errors and typos throughout the manuscript.



Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to MCI detection using complex network analysis. Addressing the identified weaknesses and responding to the questions raised would significantly strengthen the manuscript and enhance its impact. While the novelty is incremental, the practical application and robust evaluation make it a worthwhile contribution.
