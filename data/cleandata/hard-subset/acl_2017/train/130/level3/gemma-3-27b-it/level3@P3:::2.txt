## Review

This paper investigates the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using a graph-based approach that leverages word co-occurrence and word embeddings. The authors evaluate their method on three datasets and explore combinations with traditional linguistic features and classifiers. While the clinical problem addressed is important and the datasets utilized are appropriate, the paper suffers from significant methodological flaws and presentation issues that substantially limit its contribution.

**Summary:** The paper proposes a method to represent speech transcripts as complex networks, enriching these networks with word embeddings to capture semantic information. The resulting network features are then used in conjunction with machine learning classifiers to distinguish between individuals with MCI and healthy controls. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve competitive performance with traditional linguistic features, and that combining different feature sets through ensemble and multi-view learning can further improve accuracy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The exploration of complex networks as a feature representation for MCI detection from speech transcripts is a reasonable direction. This provides an alternative to reliance on parsing-dependent linguistic features, potentially mitigating issues with disfluencies.
2. **Dataset Compilation & Evaluation:**  The use of three datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese) – provides a valuable basis for evaluating the generalizability of the proposed method.  Including datasets in different languages is a positive aspect.
3. **Exploration of Feature Combination:** Investigating the combination of network-based features with traditional linguistic features and the use of ensemble/multi-view learning offers insights into potential improvements in classification performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Clinical Problem:** MCI diagnosis is an important area of research, and automated methods for early detection are highly desirable.
2. **Appropriate Datasets:** The datasets used represent a reasonable attempt to gather data relevant to the task, including both English and Portuguese language samples. The inclusion of narrative speech is also fitting for studying language-based indicators of cognitive impairment.
3. **Methodological Diversity:** The authors explore multiple classification algorithms and feature combinations, demonstrating a willingness to evaluate the robustness of their approach.
4. **Potential for Robustness:** The graph-based approach, by design, is less sensitive to parsing errors arising from disfluencies, which is a significant advantage in the context of spontaneous speech from individuals with cognitive impairment.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Poor Writing Quality:** The manuscript is marred by numerous grammatical errors and stylistic issues, hindering readability and comprehension.  A thorough revision is essential.
2. **Unclear Problem & Instance Definition:** The description of the datasets and the “instances” being classified is vague. The paper refers to datasets as *studies* rather than collections of individual transcript-level data.  Reported counts are inconsistent and do not clearly align with the described groups.  It’s unclear *what* is being classified – patients, narratives, sentences?
3. **Lack of Motivation for Graph Enrichment:** The rationale for using graph enrichment with word embeddings, specifically *over* simpler word-node similarity approaches, is poorly articulated. The authors do not sufficiently justify the added complexity.
4. **Neglect of Biomedical NLP Resources:** The authors do not leverage existing, specialized biomedical NLP tools that could potentially improve feature extraction and robustness.
5. **Inappropriate Evaluation Metric:** The primary evaluation metric is accuracy, but the class distribution within the datasets is not reported. In cases of imbalanced classes, accuracy can be misleading. F1-score (and a clear statement of class balance) is crucial for a fair assessment.
6. **Insufficient Statistical Rigor:** Results are reported with unjustified precision on very small datasets. Critically, there is *no* statistical significance testing reported, making it impossible to determine whether observed differences are meaningful or due to chance.  P-values or confidence intervals are essential.
7. **Feature Representation Details Lacking:** The details regarding the construction and enrichment of the complex networks are insufficient for reproducibility. Parameters used for word embedding and similarity thresholds are mentioned only briefly.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Instance Definition:** Please clearly define what constitutes a single "instance" in your experiments (e.g., a full transcript, a sentence, a paragraph).
2. **Class Balance:** What is the precise class distribution (number of controls vs. MCI patients) for each dataset?
3. **Statistical Significance:**  Please provide statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to support your claims of improved performance.
4. **Parameter Justification:** Justify the choice of parameters used for word embedding, cosine similarity thresholds, and network construction. How were these parameters determined?
5. **Reproducibility:** Provide sufficient details regarding the pre-processing steps, feature extraction, and experimental setup to allow for reproducibility of your results.
6. **Comparison to Baseline:** How does the performance of your graph-based method compare to a simple baseline approach using TF-IDF and a standard classifier?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references are not formatted consistently.
* The supplementary material is helpful but could be better organized.
* Consider reporting results separately for the manually revised Cinderella dataset and the automatically segmented ABCD dataset to assess the impact of data quality on performance.



Overall, while the research direction is potentially promising, the paper requires significant revisions to address the methodological flaws, improve clarity, and provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. As it stands, the manuscript is not suitable for acceptance.
