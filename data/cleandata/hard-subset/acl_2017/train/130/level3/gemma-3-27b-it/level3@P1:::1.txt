This paper investigates the use of complex networks and word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts in Portuguese. The authors model transcripts as networks, enrich them with word embeddings to better capture semantic information, and then apply machine learning classifiers to distinguish between individuals with and without MCI. The work is evaluated on three datasets: one English dataset from DementiaBank and two Portuguese datasets collected at the University of São Paulo Medical School. Results demonstrate that complex network features can effectively detect MCI and outperform traditional linguistic features. Combining classifiers through ensemble and multi-view learning further improves performance, achieving up to 80% accuracy on the Arizona dataset. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in applying complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to the challenging problem of MCI detection from speech in a lower-resource language (Portuguese). This approach offers a potentially robust method for automatic analysis, reducing reliance on potentially error-prone manual transcript correction and computationally expensive parsing techniques. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the creation and use of these Portuguese speech datasets for MCI research, providing resources for future work in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks:**  The use of complex network features, specifically word adjacency networks enriched with word embeddings, for MCI detection is a novel approach.  This method offers a potentially language-independent representation of text, which is especially important when dealing with languages like Portuguese that lack robust NLP resources. The reported improvements over solely relying on linguistic features suggest the method effectively captures relevant information from the transcripts, even in the presence of disfluencies.

2. **Addressing a Real-World Problem with Limited Resources:** The study tackles a significant healthcare challenge – the early detection of MCI – while acknowledging the limitations of available resources for Portuguese language processing.  By minimizing reliance on complex linguistic parsing, the proposed method provides a viable path for automated assessment in clinical settings where manual transcript correction is impractical.

3. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets, including both English and Portuguese data, strengthens the generalizability of the findings. This demonstrates the potential of the method to be adapted and applied to different populations and data collection protocols. The inclusion of the publicly available DementiaBank dataset also facilitates comparison with existing work.

4. **Effective Combination of Techniques:** The improvement achieved through ensemble and multi-view learning highlights the benefit of combining different feature sets and classifiers. This suggests that integrating topological network measures with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations yields a more comprehensive and accurate assessment.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Size:** The relatively small size of the Portuguese datasets (20 controls and 20 MCI patients for Cinderella, 23 and 20 for ABCD) limits the statistical power and generalizability of the results.  It’s difficult to confidently conclude the robustness of the method with such limited sample sizes.  Further validation with larger and more diverse datasets is crucial.

2. **Inconsistent Tone & Overstated Claims:** The introduction presents an overly optimistic view of the potential impact, while the conclusion acknowledges the difficulty in distinguishing between healthy and impaired subjects. This discrepancy creates a confusing impression. The claims of significant improvement should be tempered, given the small dataset size and the observed accuracy levels (ranging from 60-85%).

3. **Lack of Technical Detail & Reproducibility:** Several aspects of the methodology lack sufficient detail to ensure reproducibility. The method for training the word embeddings isn't fully described – was it trained only on the provided datasets or using larger, external corpora?  Providing an example of a transcript and the corresponding network/embedding representation would greatly enhance understanding. Including an English translation of a sample transcript would also improve accessibility.

4. **Irrelevant Literature Review & Excessive Length:**  The literature review contains citations that are tangentially related to the core focus of the paper (e.g., Salton 1989, sentiment classification, pedestrian detection).  A more focused review, concentrating on relevant work in MCI detection and complex network analysis of text, would be more impactful.  Furthermore, sections 1-3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 are overly verbose and could be significantly condensed.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Word Embedding Training:** Please provide a detailed description of how the word embeddings were trained. Was this done solely on the provided datasets, or were pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) used and fine-tuned? If so, specify which embeddings and the training parameters.
2. **"Out-of-Vocabulary" Words:** Can you clarify the definition of "out-of-vocabulary" words in your method? How are these words handled when constructing the network and calculating embeddings?
3. **Subword Networks:** The paper mentions combining word-level and subword networks.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how this combination works and whether it consistently improves accuracy compared to using only word-level networks?
4. **Transcription Norms:** Please detail the specific implications of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms for the complex network analysis and embedding process. Are there features of these norms that uniquely impact the results?




**Additional Comments:**

- Please carefully proofread the manuscript for technical and typographical errors. Examples include “as it a lightweight” (Line 29), PLN→NLP (Line 188), duplicated words (Line 264), incorrect decimal formatting in Table 3, and a duplicate DOI and incorrect reference link (Lines ~992–1002).
- Consider providing statistical significance tests for the observed performance improvements.
- While the work is a valuable step, the authors should acknowledge the limitations of the small dataset size and the need for future work with larger and more diverse data.
