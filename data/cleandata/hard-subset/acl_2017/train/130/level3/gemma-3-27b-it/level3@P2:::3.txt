## Review for ACL 2017

This paper explores the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors investigate a lightweight, language-independent approach to a challenging problem, leveraging network analysis as an alternative to traditional linguistic feature engineering. The work is well-motivated, addressing the difficulties of parsing agrammatical speech common in MCI patients. The datasets used, including the DementiaBank and datasets collected at the University of São Paulo, are a valuable contribution, particularly the inclusion of Portuguese language data.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in the application and evaluation of complex network features, augmented with word embeddings, as a viable method for MCI detection. While complex networks have been used in NLP previously, their application to this specific domain – detecting MCI from potentially noisy speech transcripts – is novel. A second, significant contribution is the creation and utilization of these datasets, particularly the inclusion of Brazilian Portuguese speech data, which expands the scope of research in this area. Finally, the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to combine different feature sets (network, linguistic, and BoW) demonstrates a thoughtful approach to model optimization and yields improvements in performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Network Analysis:** The core idea of representing speech transcripts as complex networks and leveraging topological features for MCI detection is innovative. This approach offers a potential solution to the challenges posed by disfluencies and agrammatical speech, bypassing the reliance on accurate parsing. The reported performance, particularly with the enriched networks, supports the feasibility of this approach.
2. **Valuable Datasets:** The inclusion of three datasets, in both English and Portuguese, strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The diversity in data collection methods (narratives, descriptions, immediate and delayed recall) provides a more robust evaluation of the proposed methodology. The detailed description of the datasets is helpful for reproducibility.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluated multiple classification algorithms (GaussianNB, KNN, SVM, RF) and explored both ensemble and multi-view learning techniques. This thorough evaluation provides a strong basis for comparison and demonstrates the potential benefits of combining different approaches. The inclusion of a Bag-of-Words baseline provides a useful point of reference.
4. **Outperformance of Linguistic Features:** The reported results showing that complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features in all datasets is a noteworthy finding. This suggests that the network-based approach is less sensitive to the noise and irregularities present in the speech of MCI patients.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** The description of the multi-view learning approach is lacking. The exact scheme used for combining the different views (complex networks, linguistic features, BoW) is not clearly explained. How were the weights determined for each view? Further elaboration is needed.
2. **Limited Description of Linguistic Features:** Section 4.2.2 provides a brief overview of the linguistic features used, but lacks detail. A more thorough description of the specific metrics included in Coh-Metrix and Coh-Metrix-Dementia is necessary for understanding their contribution to the overall performance.
3. **Word Embedding Corpus Clarification:** The paper states that word embeddings were used to enrich the networks, but it is unclear which corpus was used to train these embeddings. Was it trained on all three datasets combined, or a separate corpus? This is a crucial detail that needs clarification, as the quality of the embeddings can significantly impact the performance of the network-based approach.
4. **Disfluency Removal Justification:** The decision to remove certain types of disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is questionable. Disfluencies can be informative indicators of cognitive impairment, and their removal may inadvertently remove valuable signals. The authors should justify this preprocessing step or reconsider its inclusion.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The results are presented as accuracy values without any statistical significance testing. It is unclear whether the observed differences in performance between different methods and feature sets are statistically significant. Adding p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the conclusions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Please provide a detailed explanation of the multi-view learning scheme used, including how the weights for each view were determined.
2. Can you elaborate on the specific linguistic features included in the Coh-Metrix and Coh-Metrix-Dementia toolsets?
3. Which corpus was used to train the word embeddings employed in this study?
4.  Please justify the removal of disfluencies from the speech transcripts. If possible, could you perform an ablation study to assess the impact of disfluency removal on performance?
5. Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) to support the reported performance differences?

**Additional Comments:**

- The abstract is too long and should be condensed.
- The manuscript requires significant editing for clarity and readability. Numerous sentences and phrases are awkwardly worded and need to be revised.
- Provide citations/links for datasets and tools referenced (DementiaBank, Picnic picture/Western Aphasia Battery, transcription norms).
- Explain acronyms and terms (e.g., WML subtest, ABCD, PLN).
- Review for adherence to the ACL conference citation style.
