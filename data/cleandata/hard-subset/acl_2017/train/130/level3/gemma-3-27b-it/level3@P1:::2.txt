This paper investigates the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features. The authors propose modeling transcripts as word co-occurrence networks, enriched with word embeddings, and applying various classification algorithms to distinguish between individuals with and without MCI across three datasets: DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona Battery (all in English or Portuguese). The key contribution lies in exploring complex network representations as a potentially robust and language-independent approach for MCI detection, particularly in the face of disfluencies common in patient speech. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of performance gains through ensemble and multi-view learning approaches, combining network features with traditional linguistic features. Finally, the evaluation on multiple datasets, including both English and Portuguese, represents a valuable resource for the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks:** The application of complex network analysis to the problem of MCI detection from speech is a promising direction. Traditional methods relying on parsing and linguistic features are vulnerable to errors introduced by disfluencies.  The authors correctly identify this limitation and propose a potentially more resilient approach based on network topology. This represents the most significant contribution of the paper.

2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the proposed method on three different datasets, encompassing both English and Portuguese, increases the generalizability and robustness of the findings. This is particularly important given the linguistic variations and potential dataset-specific biases. The datasets themselves, sourced from DementiaBank and the University of São Paulo Medical School, are valuable resources for the research community.

3. **Exploration of Ensemble and Multi-View Learning:** The investigation of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques demonstrates an effort to maximize the predictive power of the features.  Achieving a 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and a 4.71% improvement on the Arizona dataset with multi-view learning highlights the benefit of integrating different feature representations. This contributes to a more comprehensive approach to the problem. 

4. **Addressing a Clinically Relevant Problem:**  Early detection of MCI is crucial for timely intervention and management of potential progression to Alzheimer’s disease.  The paper tackles a clinically important problem with a potential for real-world impact.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Data Representation and ML Setup:** The description of the datasets and the machine learning problem is unclear. The paper refers to datasets containing "transcripts," but the description of those datasets focus on studies performed with subjects, rather than the transcripts themselves. Reported counts of sentences and words seem inconsistent, and the nature of the data instances (e.g., individual sentences, entire narratives) is not well defined.  This makes it difficult to assess the validity of the results.

2. **Unclear Motivation for Network Enrichment:** While the authors enrich the co-occurrence network with word embeddings, the rationale for this specific approach over simpler methods like direct word similarity calculations (e.g., cosine similarity between word vectors) is not clearly articulated. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the justification for the chosen method.

3. **Limited Leverage of Existing Biomedical NLP Resources:** The authors do not appear to leverage established biomedical NLP tools or resources. The use of Coh-Metrix is mentioned, but there's no indication of exploring other specialized tools for dementia/cognitive impairment detection. Incorporating such tools could enhance feature representation and potentially improve performance. 

4. **Inappropriate Evaluation Metrics and Statistical Rigor:**  Reporting only accuracy, particularly given potential class imbalances within the datasets, is insufficient. The class distribution is not explicitly stated. Metrics like F1-score, precision, and recall, alongside a discussion of class balance, are necessary for a comprehensive evaluation.  Furthermore, the reported accuracy improvements (e.g., 2.3%, 4.71%) lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to determine if the observed improvements are genuinely meaningful or due to chance.  The precision of the reported results (e.g. 0.6500) is also questionable given the small dataset sizes.

5. **Poor Writing Quality:** The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and inconsistencies, hindering readability and comprehension. A thorough revision by a native English speaker is strongly recommended.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the data instances used for classification (e.g., are you classifying individual sentences, entire narratives, or something else)?  Please clarify the data preprocessing steps applied to create these instances.

2.  What is the class distribution (number of MCI vs. control subjects) within each dataset? 

3.  Why was the cosine similarity threshold chosen for enriching the networks with word embeddings?  Did you explore different threshold values, and if so, what were the results?

4.  Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) for the reported accuracy improvements to demonstrate their statistical significance.

5.  Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of parsing-based approaches in the context of these datasets and how complex networks specifically overcome these limitations?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references section is extensive but could be formatted more consistently.
*   The supplementary material is helpful, but could be better integrated into the main text with clearer cross-referencing.
*   Consider exploring feature selection techniques to identify the most informative network-based features.
