This paper explores the use of complex networks and machine learning for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, enriching network representations with word embeddings. The core contribution lies in applying these network-based methods to the MCI detection task, demonstrating their competitiveness with traditional linguistic feature-based approaches, and highlighting the benefit of combining features through ensemble and multi-view learning. A secondary contribution is the exploration of these techniques on Portuguese language data, offering a valuable extension beyond the predominantly English-language literature in this area. The datasets used, including DementiaBank and newly collected Portuguese data, are a further valuable aspect of this work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Timely Problem:** MCI detection is a critical healthcare challenge, and the exploration of automated, scalable methods using speech analysis is highly relevant. The focus on leveraging linguistic information without relying on computationally expensive and error-prone manual transcript correction is a strong motivation.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The paper employs multiple datasets – English and Portuguese – providing a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods than a single dataset study. The use of 5-fold cross-validation is appropriate.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that complex networks, particularly when enriched with word embeddings, achieve competitive performance compared to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches. The improvements achieved through ensemble and multi-view learning are promising.
4. **Exploration of Portuguese Data:** Applying these techniques to Portuguese language data expands the scope of research and addresses a need for resources in languages other than English. This is particularly important given the diversity of populations affected by MCI.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** The description of the multi-view learning approach is inadequate. The exact scheme used for combining the classifiers (e.g., weighting scheme, aggregation method) needs to be clearly specified. This hinders reproducibility and understanding of the method’s effectiveness.
2. **Underspecified Linguistic Features:** Section 4.2.2 provides a superficial description of the linguistic features. While mentioning Coh-Metrix, there’s a lack of detail regarding *which* specific metrics were used and why. Expanding this section is crucial. It is unclear why some metrics were discarded for the Portuguese data.
3. **Ambiguous Word Embedding Training Data:** The paper states the word embeddings were trained on the three datasets but doesn't clarify *how* – were they trained jointly, or separately on each dataset? Specifying the dataset(s) used and the training parameters is essential.
4. **Questionable Disfluency Removal:** Removing disfluencies is a questionable decision. Disfluencies *may* be indicative of cognitive impairment. The paper provides insufficient justification for this removal and doesn’t discuss potential biases introduced. The reference to transcription norms is not directly linked to justifying the disfluency removal.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The paper presents accuracy scores without any statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA). This makes it difficult to determine whether the observed differences in performance are statistically meaningful or due to chance.
6. **Superficial Related Work:** The related work section lacks depth. Core prior methods and a precise methodological description of comparisons with other approaches are missing. For example, simply stating that others used linguistic features is insufficient; detail the specific features and classification algorithms they employed.
7. **Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The abstract is excessively long and needs to be concise. Numerous sentences throughout the manuscript suffer from awkward phrasing and lack clarity, hindering readability. Specific line numbers are noted in the original prompt.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Please provide a detailed description of the multi-view learning scheme used, including the specific aggregation method and any weighting parameters.
2. Can you provide a complete list of the linguistic features used from Coh-Metrix and justify the selection of these features?
3. On which dataset(s) were the word embeddings trained, and what were the training parameters (e.g., embedding dimension, window size, number of epochs)?
4. What is the justification for removing disfluencies from the transcripts? Have you considered the potential impact of this removal on the results? Could you present results with and without disfluency removal?
5. Please provide statistical significance tests to support the claims of improved performance.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Provide citations/links for DementiaBank, Picnic picture/Western Aphasia Battery, transcription norms, and the Coh-Metrix tool.
*   Define acronyms like WML, ABCD, and PLN upon their first use.
*   Clarify the order of text preprocessing steps (tokenization, lemmatization, etc.).
*   Correct minor errors and typos throughout the manuscript.
*   Confirm adherence to the conference citation style.
*   Investigate the possibility of supplementary material for a more detailed description of the linguistic features, if page limits prevent full inclusion.
