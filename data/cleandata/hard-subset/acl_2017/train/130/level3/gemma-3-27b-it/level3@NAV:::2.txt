## Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

This paper investigates the use of graph-based features derived from speech transcripts to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors propose modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings, applying this approach to three datasets – two in Portuguese and one in English – and evaluating performance with multiple classifiers. While the clinical problem addressed is well-defined and important, and the use of multiple datasets is a positive aspect, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its methodology, presentation, and analysis. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the application of complex network analysis to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech, and the attempt to leverage word embeddings for improved representation. However, the novelty is limited; the use of graph-based methods and word embeddings are established techniques in NLP. The exploration of their combination within this clinical context is a reasonable investigation, but presented without sufficient justification.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Clinical Problem:** MCI detection is an important and challenging task, and the exploration of automated methods using speech data is valuable.
*   **Multiple Datasets:** The use of three datasets (albeit with differing characteristics, as noted below) allows for a broader evaluation of the proposed method.
*   **Diverse Feature Exploration:** The authors investigate a range of features, including topological network measures, linguistic features, and bag-of-words representations, offering a comparatively holistic approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Poor Writing Quality:** The manuscript is plagued by grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, hindering comprehension.
*   **Unclear Problem Definition:** The description of the datasets and the data used is inconsistent. The paper refers to “studies” rather than transcript-level data, and reported counts lack clarity. This makes it difficult to assess the validity of the results.
*   **Lack of Justification:** The motivation for using graph enrichment over simpler word-node similarity approaches is not adequately explained. The added complexity needs clear justification.
*   **Missed Opportunity for Biomedical NLP:** The authors do not leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools which could potentially improve feature extraction and accuracy.
*   **Inappropriate Evaluation Metrics:** The reliance on accuracy alone, given the likely class imbalance in the datasets, is problematic. F1-score and a clear reporting of class distribution are essential.
*   **Insufficient Statistical Rigor:** Results are reported with unjustified precision (e.g., multiple decimal places on small datasets) and lack statistical significance testing. The observed improvements may be due to chance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the datasets, specifically outlining the number of participants in each diagnostic group for each dataset?
2.  Why were domain-specific biomedical NLP tools not utilized in this study?
3.  Why was accuracy chosen as the primary evaluation metric, and will you provide results using F1-score and other metrics more appropriate for potentially imbalanced datasets?
4.  What statistical tests were used (if any) to determine the significance of the observed performance differences?
5.  Please clarify the motivation behind using complex networks and word embeddings over more traditional approaches like TF-IDF weighted word co-occurrence.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful but also contains inconsistencies.
*   Consider providing example transcripts and illustrating the network construction process visually.



Overall, while the research addresses a relevant problem, the paper requires substantial revision to address the methodological flaws, improve clarity, and provide stronger evidence to support the claims. As it stands, it is not suitable for acceptance.
