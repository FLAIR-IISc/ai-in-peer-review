- Paper models speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI in Portuguese and English, using machine learning classifiers on three datasets.  
- Primary contribution: applying embedding-enriched complex network analysis for MCI detection in a lower-resource language and providing new Portuguese datasets.  

Strengths:
- Novel use of word-adjacency complex networks with embeddings, offering a potentially language-independent text representation resilient to disfluencies.  
- Practical focus on MCI detection with reduced reliance on costly/fragile linguistic parsing, useful for clinical settings with limited NLP resources.  
- Evaluation on three datasets (including DementiaBank) improves generalizability and allows comparison with prior work.  
- Ensemble and multi-view learning combining network, linguistic, and BoW features yield improved classification performance.  

Weaknesses:
- Portuguese datasets are very small, limiting statistical power and generalizability of findings.  
- Tone and claims are inconsistent—introduction is optimistic while conclusions are more cautious, leading to overstated impact given dataset limits.  
- Methodological details are incomplete (e.g., how embeddings were trained, handling of OOV words) reducing reproducibility.  
- Literature review and several sections are overly long or include tangential citations and verbose text that should be condensed.  

Questions for authors:
- How were the word embeddings trained—only on these datasets or using external pre-trained embeddings, and what were the training parameters?  
- How are "out-of-vocabulary" words defined and handled during network construction and embedding computation?  
- How are word-level and subword networks combined in practice, and does this consistently outperform word-only networks?  
- What are the specific implications of the cited NURC/SP transcription norms for network construction and embeddings?  

Additional comments:
- Manuscript needs careful proofreading and correction of technical/typographical errors and reference issues.  
- Provide statistical significance tests for reported performance improvements.  
- Authors should explicitly acknowledge dataset size limitations and the need for larger, diverse validations.
