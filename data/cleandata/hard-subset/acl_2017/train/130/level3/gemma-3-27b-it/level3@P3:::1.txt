## Review

This paper investigates the application of complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts in Portuguese and English. The authors aim to provide a lightweight, language-independent approach to MCI detection, addressing challenges posed by disfluencies in patient speech. While the study presents a promising avenue for research – particularly concerning the application of NLP techniques to a lower-resource language like Portuguese – the work suffers from several limitations regarding dataset size, methodological clarity, and presentation.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors model speech transcripts as complex networks, augmenting node connections with semantic similarity based on word embeddings. They then extract topological features from these networks and use them, alongside linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, to classify individuals as having MCI or being healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets: the Cookie Theft picture description dataset (English), the Cinderella story retelling dataset (Portuguese), and the Arizona Battery for Communication Disorders of Dementia (ABCD) dataset (Portuguese). Results indicate that complex networks can effectively distinguish between groups, and combining features through ensemble and multi-view learning can further improve accuracy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Exploration of Complex Network Analysis for MCI Detection:** The application of complex network features to the task of MCI detection from speech transcripts is a valuable contribution, providing a novel approach distinct from traditional linguistic feature engineering.
2. **Addressing a Lower-Resource Language:** Adapting and applying these methods to Portuguese speech data is significant, as resources for NLP in Portuguese are comparatively limited, and this work contributes to bridging that gap.
3. **Initial Investigation of Feature Combination:** The exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to combine network, linguistic, and bag-of-words features provides initial insights into the potential benefits of integrative approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Approach:** Utilizing complex networks offers an alternative perspective compared to traditional NLP methods focused on parsing and syntactic analysis, potentially mitigating issues arising from speech disfluencies.
2. **Multilingual Evaluation:** The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets provides a broader context for evaluating the generalizability of the proposed method.
3. **Demonstrated Accuracy:** The reported classification accuracies (ranging from ~60% to 85%) are competitive with existing literature in the field, suggesting the approach has merit.
4. **Addressing Disfluencies:** The attempt to avoid manual correction of transcripts acknowledges a practical challenge in analyzing real-world speech data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Size:** The datasets used (20 patients + 20 controls for Cinderella and ABCD, 43 MCI + 21 control for Cookie Theft) are extremely small. This severely limits the statistical power of the study and hinders the generalizability of the findings. The results may be heavily influenced by the specific characteristics of these small cohorts.
2. **Contradictory Tone:** The introduction and results section initially present an optimistic view of the method’s performance. However, the conclusion downplays the ease of distinguishing between healthy and impaired subjects, creating a confusing and contradictory narrative.
3. **Lack of Methodological Clarity:** Several aspects of the methodology are unclear. Specifically, the training of word embeddings is not fully explained (were external corpora used in addition to the provided datasets?), and the interaction between word-level and subword (n-gram) networks is ambiguous. It's unclear if combining these improves accuracy and, if so, how.
4. **Ambiguous OOV Definition:** The definition and source of "out-of-vocabulary" words are unclear. It's crucial to specify how these words were handled and whether this impacted the results.
5. **Literature Review Issues:** The literature review includes citations that are largely irrelevant or overly general (e.g., Salton 1989, sentiment classification, pedestrian detection). This weakens the positioning of the work within the relevant research landscape. The review should be focused on studies directly related to MCI detection, speech analysis, and complex network approaches.
6. **Overly Detailed Sections:** Sections 1-3 and parts of 4.1/4.2.3 are excessively long and contain information that doesn't directly contribute to the core novelty of the work. These sections should be significantly condensed to focus on the key contributions.
7. **Technical Errors:** The manuscript contains multiple technical and typographical errors (e.g., “as it a lightweight”, PLN→NLP, duplicated words, decimal formatting in Table 3, duplicate DOI and incorrect reference link).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the training of the word embeddings? Specifically, were any external corpora used in addition to the provided datasets?
2.  Please clarify the interaction between the word-level and subword (n-gram) networks. Does combining them improve accuracy, and if so, how is this achieved?
3.  Could you define precisely what constitutes an “out-of-vocabulary” word in your experiments and explain how these words were handled?
4.  Can you provide an example of an English-translated transcript (even a short excerpt) and illustrate how it is represented as a complex network, including an example of the network visualization and the resulting node/edge features?
5.  Could you elaborate on the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms and explain any specific challenges these norms presented during the data preprocessing stage?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Providing example outputs of the network/embedding process (e.g., a visualization of the network for a sample transcript) would significantly improve understanding.
*   A more rigorous statistical analysis, considering the small sample sizes, would strengthen the conclusions.
*   The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the limitations of the study and future research directions.
