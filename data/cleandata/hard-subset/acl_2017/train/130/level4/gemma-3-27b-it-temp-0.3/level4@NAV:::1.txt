## Review

This paper investigates the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) to speech transcripts – specifically narratives and descriptions – to identify individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 F06.7). The authors report an ability to differentiate between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141-144). However, the conclusion (lines 781-785) presents a more nuanced view, stating that accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% indicates challenges in reliably distinguishing between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments. Despite this tempered conclusion, the work is promising and motivates further investigation into the methods employed.

The study utilizes a corpus comprising only 20 healthy controls and 20 MCI patients (total n=40). A significant limitation is the corpus’s language – Portuguese – which restricts accessibility for researchers unfamiliar with the language. Enhancing the paper with greater technical detail would be beneficial, including at least one example of a short transcript translated into English, and potentially a partial visualization of the network architecture with embeddings for a sample transcript.

The introduction and related work section is comprehensive. However, some cited references appear tangential to the core focus of the paper; for example, citations related to general NLP background (e.g., Salton 1989, section 4.2.3) or unrelated tasks like sentiment classification and pedestrian detection (lines 652-654) could be omitted.  Section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) could be significantly condensed.  The suggestion is to streamline the initial five pages, concentrating the literature review directly on the present work and dedicating more space to a detailed discussion of the technical innovations. This should include examples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.

The relatively short length of the narratives, particularly within the ABCD dataset, raises questions regarding the effectiveness of identifying word similarities for embedding construction. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but the source of these initial continuous representations is unclear. Were they learned solely from the ABCD+Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora utilized? Similarly, the training source for the sub-word level (n-gram) networks used to generate embeddings (lines 513-525) requires clarification. It is unclear whether the combined use of these two network types demonstrably improves accuracy, and the origin of the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516 should be specified.

Overall, this research addresses an important question – how NLP can contribute to the early detection of cognitive impairments – and is therefore a valuable contribution. The focus on Portuguese NLP is also noteworthy, and the paper would benefit from a discussion of the challenges and strategies involved in computing embeddings for a language with limited resources compared to English. 

The manuscript requires revision, specifically through shortening sections 1-3, compressing section 4.1, and expanding the explanation of the experimental setup. Further clarification regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
