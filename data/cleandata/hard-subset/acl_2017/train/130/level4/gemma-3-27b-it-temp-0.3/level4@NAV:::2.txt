## Review:

This paper investigates the identification of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based approach that utilizes word co-occurrence information to construct a network, with features encoded based on lexical, syntactic, and other graph properties. Performance is evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation with a variety of classifiers, revealing dataset-specific variations in model effectiveness. The work addresses a clearly defined problem and employs suitable datasets for investigation.

However, the paper has several limitations:

1.  Clarity is significantly hindered by issues with English language usage. A thorough review for grammatical errors and spelling would substantially improve readability.
2.  The core machine learning problem is not clearly articulated. The classification task – seemingly a binary assessment of each transcript as either MCI or No MCI – requires more precise definition. Dataset descriptions should focus on transcript-level statistics rather than the originating studies. Details such as patient age are not relevant to the classification task and contribute to excessive length (approximately two pages) dedicated to dataset description. Furthermore, inconsistencies in the presented data (e.g., discrepancies between reported participant numbers in the text and Table 1) raise concerns.
3.  The rationale for graph enrichment is unclear. An alternative approach of representing words as nodes connected by vector similarity, regardless of co-occurrence, warrants consideration and justification.
4.  Despite utilizing datasets from the biomedical domain, the work does not leverage any domain-specific tools or resources.
5.  Due to the lack of clarity regarding dataset class distributions, the suitability of accuracy as an evaluation metric is questionable. For a binary classification problem, the F1-score would be a more informative metric.
6.  Reporting results to four decimal places on small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without accompanying statistical significance testing makes it difficult to determine the practical significance of observed gains.
