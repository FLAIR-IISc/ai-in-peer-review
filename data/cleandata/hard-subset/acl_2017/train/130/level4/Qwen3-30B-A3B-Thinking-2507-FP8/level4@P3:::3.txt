

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a novel application of complex network features enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model narratives as complex networks (with co-occurrence edges) and enhance them using semantic word embeddings to better represent short, disfluent texts. The main contributions are: (1) a language-independent framework that avoids manual transcript correction and parsing dependencies, (2) empirical validation showing complex networks outperform linguistic features across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), and (3) demonstration that multi-view learning combining network features with linguistic/BoW features achieves significant accuracy gains (up to 10% improvement). These contributions are distinct from prior work, which heavily relies on parsing-dependent linguistic features.

**Strengths**  
1. **Problem Relevance and Novelty**: MCI detection is critical for early Alzheimer's intervention, and the paper effectively addresses a key gap: disfluencies in speech transcripts undermine parsing-based linguistic features. The proposed network approach circumvents this by being language-independent and avoiding manual preprocessing, which is particularly valuable for real-world deployment. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method outperforms linguistic features (e.g., 75% accuracy vs. 50–65% for linguistic features on the Cinderella dataset), showing it is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different paradigm.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The study evaluates three distinct datasets (including two Portuguese datasets not previously used in MCI detection), providing strong evidence of generalizability. The multi-view learning approach (combining network, linguistic, and BoW features) achieves notable gains (e.g., 80% accuracy on ABCD vs. 75.47% for BoW), with statistically significant improvements (10% on Cinderella, 4.71% on ABCD). This demonstrates practical utility beyond isolated case studies.

3. **Technical Clarity in Methodology**: The network modeling with word embedding enrichment (via cosine similarity) is well-motivated and clearly explained. The decision to omit lemmatization (due to parsing noise in disfluent texts) is justified, and the preprocessing pipeline (e.g., removing filled pauses but retaining disfluencies) is transparently described. The use of standard topological metrics (PageRank, betweenness, etc.) provides a robust foundation for feature extraction.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Ambiguous Methodology Details**: Critical implementation details are missing. The paper states word embeddings were trained but does not specify whether they were trained on the MCI datasets or external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia). For small datasets like ABCD (43 samples total), training embeddings on such limited data risks overfitting or poor generalization. Similarly, the "threshold" for edge creation in enriched networks is not defined (e.g., cosine similarity cutoff), making replication impossible. The authors should clarify this in the revision.

2. **Inconsistent Disfluency Handling**: The paper removes "filled pauses" (e.g., "uh," "um") but retains other disfluencies (revisions, paraphasias). This is contradictory: disfluencies are known to be diagnostic for MCI (e.g., Tóth et al., 2015), so removing *some* while keeping others undermines the study's premise. The authors must justify this selective filtering and clarify whether disfluency features were tested as standalone predictors.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The results claim "improvements" (e.g., 10% on Cinderella), but no statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests, McNemar’s test) are provided to confirm these differences are significant. Without this, the claimed improvements could be due to chance, especially given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 20 MCI/20 control in Cinderella). The authors should add significance testing for all comparative results.

4. **Superficial Related Work**: The related work section cites prior MCI detection studies but fails to contrast them with the current approach. For example, Aluísio et al. (2016) manually revised transcripts to improve parsing—this is the *problem* the current paper solves, yet the paper does not explicitly position itself against this limitation. A clearer discussion of how this work advances beyond parsing-dependent prior methods would strengthen the contribution.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Were the word embeddings trained on the MCI datasets or external corpora? If the former, how was overfitting mitigated given the small dataset sizes?  
2. Why were filled pauses removed but other disfluencies (e.g., repetitions, revisions) retained? Could disfluency features (e.g., frequency of false starts) themselves be predictive?  
3. What statistical tests were performed to confirm the significance of the reported accuracy improvements (e.g., 10% on Cinderella, 4.71% on ABCD)?  
4. In Table 5, the manual revision of Cinderella (Table 6) shows linguistic features outperform networks (50% vs. 52.5% for LM vs. CN). How does this align with the claim that networks "outperform linguistic features"?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The abstract should be shortened to 150–200 words, emphasizing the core contribution (language-independent network features) and key results (e.g., "network features outperform linguistic features, with multi-view learning improving accuracy by 10%").  
- Clarify "multi-view learning" in Section 4.3 (e.g., "feature space fusion via majority voting" vs. "weighted combination").  
- Fix grammatical errors (e.g., "impacting in parsing results" → "impacting parsing results"; "as it a lightweight" → "as a lightweight").  
- Cite DementiaBank and Western Aphasia Battery references (e.g., "DementiaBank (Becker et al., 1994)").
