

Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network representations enriched with word embeddings. The main contributions are: (1) modeling speech transcripts as complex networks to represent short texts without requiring grammatical correctness, (2) demonstrating that network features outperform traditional linguistic features for MCI detection across multiple datasets, and (3) showing that ensemble and multi-view learning approaches further improve classification accuracy.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful clinical problem (early MCI detection) with practical implications for healthcare. The authors successfully demonstrate that complex network representations provide a language-independent, lightweight solution that avoids the need for manual transcript correction, which is particularly valuable for large-scale assessments.
2. The methodology is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in existing approaches (disfluencies in speech transcripts making traditional linguistic analysis unreliable). The results consistently show that the network-based approach outperforms linguistic features across all three datasets, with significant improvements (up to 10% accuracy gain) when combining methods.
3. The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across three distinct datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD), demonstrating the robustness of the approach across different languages and assessment protocols. The results are comparable to state-of-the-art methods in the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail about key components, making the methodology difficult to reproduce. The definition of "linguistic features" is unclear, and the paper does not specify whether word embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets or external corpora (which would be problematic given the small dataset sizes). The multi-view learning implementation is described only superficially without technical details.
2. The rationale for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is unclear and potentially problematic. Given that disfluencies are a known marker of cognitive impairment, their removal might discard important diagnostic features. The paper does not provide justification for this preprocessing step or evaluate the impact of retaining disfluencies.
3. The paper omits important methodological choices without explanation, such as why tf-idf weighting was not used for Bag-of-Words features (a standard practice in text classification) and why statistical significance testing was not performed to validate the reported accuracy improvements.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify whether word embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets or external corpora. If the former, given the small size of the datasets (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft), how were the embeddings trained to avoid overfitting?
2. The paper removes disfluencies, but disfluencies are known to be relevant to cognitive impairment. Please explain why disfluencies were removed, and whether the authors considered analyzing disfluencies as features rather than eliminating them.
3. Why was tf-idf not used for Bag-of-Words features when it is the standard weighting scheme in text classification? How do the authors explain the strong performance of simple term frequency?
4. The paper claims improvements of up to 10% accuracy but does not provide statistical significance testing for these differences. Please provide p-values or confidence intervals for the reported improvements.
5. The multi-view learning approach is mentioned but not properly defined. Please clarify the exact implementation details of the multi-view learning scheme used in the experiments.
