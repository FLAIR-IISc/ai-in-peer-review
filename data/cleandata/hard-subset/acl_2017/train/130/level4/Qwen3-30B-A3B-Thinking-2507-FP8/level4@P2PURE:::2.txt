

- Strengths:
This paper addresses the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. It proposes a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words in transcripts. Features are derived from multiple graph characteristics, including lexical and syntactic properties. Results are presented using 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers, with varying performance observed across the three datasets. The work targets a well-defined problem and utilizes appropriate datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper exhibits several significant shortcomings:
1.  Poor English usage impedes readability. The manuscript would greatly benefit from thorough grammar and spelling review.
2.  The core machine learning problem is inadequately described. It remains unclear whether each transcript was classified as MCI or non-MCI. If so, dataset descriptions should report statistics at the transcript level, not the study level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should present transcript-level data, not details of the studies generating transcripts. Patient age is irrelevant to the classification task. Approximately two pages are devoted to describing datasets with details that do not impact the classification, which is excessive. Additionally, inconsistencies exist: Section 4.1.1 states 326 participants were involved, yet the sum of males (52) and females (42) in Table 1 is 94, not 326.
3.  The motivation for enriching the graph structure is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node and connect nodes based on vector similarity, independent of co-occurrence?
4.  The datasets originate from a biomedical domain, yet no domain-specific tools or linguistic resources were leveraged.
5.  The class distribution across datasets is unspecified, making accuracy an inappropriate evaluation metric for binary classification. F1 score would have been a more suitable metric.
6.  Results are reported to four decimal places for very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without statistical significance testing for performance differences. Thus, the significance of reported gains is unclear.
