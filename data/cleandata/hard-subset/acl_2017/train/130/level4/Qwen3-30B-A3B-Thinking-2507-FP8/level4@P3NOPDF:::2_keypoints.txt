- Clinically important task: detecting MCI from speech transcripts using three datasets.  
- Proposes a novel graph-based method that encodes lexical and syntactic features via word co-occurrence.  
- Evaluated across multiple classifiers with 5-fold cross-validation; dataset diversity increases relevance.  
- Manuscript has serious readability issues (grammar, spelling, awkward phrasing) and needs professional editing.  
- Core task description is ambiguous; dataset sections provide study-level metadata instead of transcript-level statistics.  
- Inconsistent and unexplained participant counts across sections undermine credibility.  
- Graph construction rationale is unclear; no justification for using co-occurrence over semantic similarity (e.g., embedding cosine).  
- Paper fails to leverage domain-specific biomedical NLP tools or embeddings, reducing domain applicability.  
- Evaluation relies on accuracy without reporting class distributions, making results misleading for potentially imbalanced data.  
- Statistical reporting is weak: overprecise metrics for small datasets and no significance testing or confidence intervals.  
- Recommendations: present transcript-level dataset stats and remove irrelevant patient metadata.  
- Recommendations: explicitly justify and compare graph design choices (co-occurrence vs semantic similarity).  
- Recommendations: report class distributions, use F1/AUC, apply appropriate statistical tests, and avoid excessive decimal precision.  
- Recommendations: incorporate biomedical NLP resources (domain embeddings, UMLS) to improve feature relevance.  
- Overall: promising approach on a meaningful problem, but methodological, evaluation, and domain-adaptation flaws make the paper currently unsuitable for publication.
