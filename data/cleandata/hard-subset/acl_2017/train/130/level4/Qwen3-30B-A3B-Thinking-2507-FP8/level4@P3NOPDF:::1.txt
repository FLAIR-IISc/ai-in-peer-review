

**Review of ACL 2017 Submission**  

This paper explores the application of NLP to Portuguese speech transcripts (narratives/descriptions) for identifying mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 F06.7). While the topic holds promise for cross-lingual cognitive assessment, significant methodological and presentation issues undermine its contribution.  

**Strengths**  
The core motivation—leveraging NLP for early MCI detection—is timely and clinically relevant. The paper’s initial claim of distinguishing MCI patients from healthy controls (lines 141–144) is intriguing, though later tempered by a conclusion stating "accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%... means it is not easy to distinguish" (lines 781–785). This contradiction creates confusion; the work merits attention only if the accuracy range is contextualized (e.g., compared to baselines or clinical thresholds). The focus on Portuguese NLP is valuable, given the scarcity of resources for low-resource languages, and the corpus construction (ABCD/Cinderella datasets) offers a useful starting point.  

**Major Weaknesses**  
1. **Inconsistent Accuracy Claims & Dataset Limitations**: The study uses a minuscule dataset (20 MCI patients + 20 controls), severely limiting statistical validity. More critically, the Portuguese-only corpus renders the work inaccessible to non-Portuguese readers, violating ACL’s international standards. Including even one English-translated transcript example (e.g., from ABCD/Cinderella) would greatly enhance reproducibility and clarity.  

2. **Overly Detailed & Irrelevant Literature Review**: Sections 4.2.3 and 1–3 are excessively verbose, cluttered with tangential references (e.g., Salton 1989 for NLP background, sentiment classification, pedestrian detection lines 652–654). These should be condensed to 2–3 sentences per key work, focusing *only* on NLP approaches for clinical diagnostics. The current 5-page introduction could be streamlined to 3 pages, freeing space to detail technical innovations (e.g., embedding methodologies).  

3. **Unclear Methodology for Word Embeddings**: The paper claims word-level networks were built from "continuous word representations" (lines 272–289), but omits critical details:  
   - Source of continuous representations (ABCD/Cinderella only? External corpora?)  
   - Justification for combining n-gram and word-level networks (lines 513–525), especially since "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) are mentioned without explanation.  
   - How short narratives (e.g., ABCD) enabled meaningful similarity calculations.  
   Without this, the embedding approach lacks credibility. The claim that "two kinds of networks together provide better accuracy" (line 520) remains unsubstantiated.  

**Minor Issues**  
- **Terminology**: "PLN" (line 188) should be "NLP" (standard ACL terminology).  
- **Typos**: "as it a lightweight" (line 29) → "as a lightweight"; "out of cookie out of the cookie" (line 264) → corrected.  
- **Table 3**: "72,0" (row 2, column 3) → "72.0" (standard decimal format).  
- **References**: Duplicate DOI (lines 995–996 vs. 1001–1002) and broken link (lines 992–993) need fixing.  
- **Clarity**: Briefly explain NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms (line 974) to aid non-Portuguese readers.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper addresses an important problem but fails to deliver a rigorous or replicable contribution. The small dataset, language barrier, unresolved methodological gaps (especially regarding embeddings), and contradictory accuracy claims prevent it from meeting ACL’s standards. While the clinical motivation is strong, the technical execution is underdeveloped. The authors should:  
1. **Expand the dataset** (min. 100 samples per group) and provide **English examples** of transcripts.  
2. **Revise the literature review** to focus exclusively on NLP for clinical diagnostics.  
3. **Clarify embedding training** (data sources, OOV handling, network fusion rationale).  
4. **Reconcile accuracy claims** with empirical results (e.g., report baselines, confidence intervals).  

Without these changes, the work lacks the novelty and technical depth expected for ACL. It is currently **not acceptable** in its current form. However, with significant revisions addressing the methodological ambiguities and dataset limitations, it could become a valuable contribution to low-resource NLP for healthcare.  

**Recommendation**: *Reject*. *Major Revision* would require substantial rework beyond typical revisions.
