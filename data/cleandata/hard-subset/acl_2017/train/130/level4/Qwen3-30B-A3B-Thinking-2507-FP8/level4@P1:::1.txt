

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The primary contribution is the application of complex network analysis to MCI detection, which overcomes parsing challenges in disfluent patient speech. The secondary contribution is the demonstration that enriching networks with word embeddings significantly improves performance, particularly for short narratives. The tertiary contribution is the successful integration of ensemble and multi-view learning to further enhance classification accuracy across three datasets.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel approach to a meaningful clinical problem**: The paper addresses a critical gap in MCI diagnosis by proposing a method that avoids parsing errors common in traditional linguistic approaches. The authors demonstrate that complex network features consistently outperform linguistic features across all three datasets, which is a significant technical contribution. This is particularly valuable for clinical applications where disfluencies in patient speech undermine standard NLP tools.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The study evaluates the approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft in English, Cinderella and ABCD in Portuguese), providing robust evidence of the method's generalizability. The use of multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, GaussianNB) and the comparison of individual classifiers with ensemble and multi-view learning approaches offer a thorough assessment of the method's performance.

3. **Advancement for low-resource languages**: By demonstrating effective MCI detection in Portuguese with limited linguistic resources (no robust dependency parser), the paper makes an important contribution to NLP for languages with fewer resources. This is particularly relevant for clinical applications in non-English speaking populations, where linguistic tools are often lacking.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Excessive background in introduction and related work**: The introduction and related work sections contain unnecessary details about general NLP concepts (e.g., Salton 1989, sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) that do not directly relate to MCI detection. This makes it difficult to focus on the paper's core innovation. The authors should compress these sections to focus strictly on relevant NLP methods for clinical language analysis.

2. **Insufficient detail on word embedding generation**: The paper lacks clarity on how word embeddings were generated, which is critical for reproducibility. The authors mention "sub-word level (n-grams) networks" (lines 513-525) but do not clarify whether embeddings were trained on ABCD/Cinderella datasets alone or on external corpora. The explanation of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is insufficient, and the paper should specify how these were handled in Portuguese processing.

3. **Extremely limited dataset sizes**: The Cinderella and ABCD datasets each contain only 20 healthy and 20 MCI participants (40 total), which is too small for reliable machine learning evaluation. The paper does not address the risk of overfitting or discuss how this limited sample size affects generalizability. Additionally, the Portuguese datasets are inaccessible to non-Portuguese speakers, limiting reproducibility. The authors should provide at least one English-translated example of a transcript and a network visualization.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper states that "accuracy ranges from 60% to 85%" (lines 781-785) but claims in the abstract that their method outperforms linguistic features. Can you clarify how your best results (80% on ABCD) compare to the state-of-the-art results reported in the literature (which also range from 60-85%)?

2. Please clarify the source of word embeddings used to enrich the networks. Were these trained on ABCD and Cinderella datasets alone, or on external corpora? How were out-of-vocabulary words handled in the embedding process?

3. Given the extremely small dataset sizes (20 healthy + 20 MCI per dataset), how did you address the risk of overfitting? What statistical tests were performed to validate the significance of your results?
