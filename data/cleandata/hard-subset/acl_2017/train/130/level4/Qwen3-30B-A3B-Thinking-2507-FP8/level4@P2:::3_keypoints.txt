- Summary: Proposes detecting MCI from speech transcripts using complex network representations enriched with word embeddings; key contributions are network modeling for short texts, superior network features, and ensemble/multi-view gains.

- Strength 1: Addresses an important clinical problem and offers a language-independent, lightweight approach that avoids manual transcript correction for scalable assessment.

- Strength 2: Well-motivated method tackling disfluency issues; network-based features consistently outperform linguistic features with up to ~10% accuracy improvement when combined.

- Strength 3: Thorough evaluation on three datasets (English and Portuguese) showing robustness across languages and comparable performance to state-of-the-art.

- Weakness 1: Insufficient methodological details hinder reproducibility—unclear definition of "linguistic features" and whether embeddings were trained on small MCI datasets or external corpora.

- Weakness 2: Removing disfluencies is questionable because disfluencies can be diagnostic for MCI; no justification or analysis of their impact is provided.

- Weakness 3: Omits explanation for key choices (e.g., not using tf-idf for BoW) and fails to report statistical significance for reported accuracy improvements.

- Question 1: Were word embeddings trained on the MCI datasets or on external corpora, and how was overfitting avoided with small datasets?

- Question 2: Why were disfluencies removed instead of analyzed as potential diagnostic features?

- Question 3: Why was tf-idf not used for Bag-of-Words, and how is the strong performance of raw term frequency explained?

- Question 4: Can the authors provide statistical significance testing (p-values or confidence intervals) for the reported accuracy improvements?

- Question 5: Please clarify the exact implementation details of the multi-view learning scheme used in the experiments.
