

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from neuropsychological assessments. The primary contribution is a language-independent approach that avoids manual transcript correction (a major limitation of prior work), using network topology combined with semantic word embeddings to represent short texts. The second contribution is the evaluation across three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD), demonstrating that network features outperform linguistic features in all cases. The third contribution is the demonstration that ensemble and multi-view learning methods improve accuracy by up to 10% on specific datasets.

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical innovation**: The paper addresses a critical limitation in prior MCI detection work—manual correction of disfluencies in speech transcripts—which hinders scalability. By modeling transcripts as complex networks with semantic enrichment, the approach eliminates this bottleneck, making it suitable for large-scale clinical applications.  
2. **Robust multi-dataset evaluation**: The authors validate their method across three distinct datasets (English and Portuguese), showing consistent performance improvements over linguistic features. The ABCD dataset results (80% accuracy) are particularly compelling, outperforming prior work in the field.  
3. **Clear technical explanation**: The methodology for enriching networks with word embeddings is well-described, with a helpful example (Fig. 2) illustrating how semantic connections enhance network representation compared to standard co-occurrence networks.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient dataset size and accessibility**: The Cinderella and ABCD datasets are extremely small (20–23 MCI cases each), limiting statistical reliability. Crucially, the Portuguese-only datasets (Cinderella, ABCD) lack English examples or translations, making it impossible for non-Portuguese speakers to verify the approach. The paper should include at least one English-translated transcript and network visualization to improve accessibility.  
2. **Unclear word embedding methodology**: The paper states that word embeddings are "trained on external corpora" (lines 513-525) but fails to specify the source. Was it trained on the datasets themselves, or external Portuguese corpora? The discussion of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is also vague—without knowing the training data, it's unclear how robust the embeddings are.  
3. **Inconsistent claims about performance**: The introduction claims "high accuracy" (lines 141–144), but the conclusion notes that "accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%... means it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments" (lines 781–785). This inconsistency undermines confidence in the results. The authors should clarify whether 80% accuracy (ABCD) is indeed high for this task or merely "better than prior work."  
4. **Overly technical background**: Sections 1–3 contain excessive NLP background (e.g., references to sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) that distract from the core contribution. The authors should trim these sections to focus on MCI-specific challenges and their solution.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Please clarify the source of the word embeddings (e.g., training corpus size, language resources used). Did the authors use external Portuguese corpora or solely the study datasets?  
2. Provide one English-translated excerpt from the ABCD or Cinderella datasets, along with a corresponding network visualization (e.g., Fig. 2) to illustrate the embedding process.  
3. Address the performance inconsistency: Is 80% accuracy (ABCD) considered "high" for MCI detection, or is it merely incremental improvement over prior work? Please contextualize this in the discussion.
