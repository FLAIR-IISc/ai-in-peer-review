

**Summary and Main Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network modeling of speech transcripts, enriched with word embeddings. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enhance them with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short, disfluent texts common in neuropsychological assessments. The method is evaluated across three datasets: English transcripts from DementiaBank's Cookie Theft task, Portuguese narratives from the Cinderella story task, and the Arizona Battery for Communication Disorders of Dementia (ABCD) dataset. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a language-independent framework for MCI detection that avoids manual correction of disfluencies, (2) empirical demonstration that complex network features outperform traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets, and (3) a systematic analysis of ensemble and multi-view learning approaches for improving classification accuracy.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel application of complex networks to MCI detection**: The paper successfully applies complex network theory to a critical clinical problem, offering a language-independent approach that circumvents parsing errors caused by speech disfluencies. This addresses a significant practical limitation in prior work that relied on manual transcript correction. The method's robustness across English and Portuguese datasets demonstrates its generalizability.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The comprehensive evaluation across three distinct datasets (including two in Portuguese) provides compelling evidence that network-based features outperform linguistic features. The paper reports consistent improvements (up to 10% in the Cinderella dataset) when using ensemble and multi-view learning approaches, suggesting the method's practical utility.

3. **Practical significance**: The approach eliminates the need for manual transcript correction—a costly, time-intensive process that hinders large-scale deployment. This is particularly valuable for clinical settings where resources are limited and automated processing is essential.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Poor writing quality and unclear methodology**: The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology (e.g., "complex networks" vs. "CN" without clear definition). The problem formulation is unclear: it's not specified whether each transcript is a single classification instance (as implied) or if multiple utterances per transcript were used. The dataset descriptions (Tables 1-3) focus on irrelevant demographic details rather than transcript-level statistics, making it difficult to assess the data's suitability for classification. For instance, Table 1 lists total participants (43 MCI + 21 AD = 64) but claims 326 people were "sampled from," creating confusion.

2. **Lack of justification for network enrichment**: The motivation for enriching co-occurrence networks with word embeddings is unclear. The authors state that "short texts have almost linear networks" but don't explain why semantic enrichment specifically addresses this issue. A more direct explanation of how word embeddings resolve the linearity problem would strengthen the methodological contribution.

3. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The paper reports accuracy with four decimal places on small datasets (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft), which is statistically unsound. No statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) are provided to validate the reported improvements (e.g., "10% improvement"). Given the small sample sizes, accuracy is an inappropriate metric without addressing class imbalance (e.g., MCI vs. control ratios are not reported in Tables 1-3).

4. **Neglected domain-specific considerations**: The paper treats the biomedical domain as generic text classification, ignoring opportunities to leverage domain-specific linguistic patterns or clinical knowledge. For example, MCI-specific disfluency patterns (e.g., semantic paraphasias) could have been incorporated to improve feature relevance.

5. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: While the paper compares its method to linguistic features, it doesn't benchmark against recent neural network approaches (e.g., transformers) that have shown strong performance in clinical text analysis. This limits the perceived novelty of the approach.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper states that "short texts have almost linear networks" but doesn't quantify this. Please provide evidence (e.g., network metrics) showing how semantic enrichment specifically mitigates this issue in the datasets used.  
2. The classification instances are ambiguous: are transcripts treated as single instances, or are utterances within transcripts used? Please clarify the exact problem formulation and how the 43 MCI transcripts in the Cookie Theft dataset relate to the classification task.  
3. Given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts), why was accuracy chosen as the primary metric instead of F1 or AUC? Please report class distributions and justify the evaluation methodology.  
4. The paper mentions that manual transcript correction was avoided but doesn't quantify the time/cost savings. Could you provide a rough estimate of how much this approach reduces preprocessing effort compared to prior work?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper's core idea is promising but undermined by poor writing and methodological gaps. A thorough rewrite to clarify the problem, methodology, and evaluation would significantly strengthen the contribution. The authors should also explicitly address how their approach differs from similar network-based text analysis methods (e.g., Amancio et al. 2012) in the context of clinical text.
