

Strengths:  
This paper explores applying NLP to Portuguese speech transcripts (narratives/descriptions) for identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 F06.7). The authors initially claim successful distinction between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141–144), yet the conclusion cites accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% (lines 781–785), stating "it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments." This discrepancy between the optimistic introduction and cautious conclusion creates tension but ultimately generates interest in the methodology. The work is significant for advancing NLP in healthcare and for Portuguese, a lower-resource language. However, the dataset (20 MCI patients + 20 controls) is small, entirely in Portuguese (hindering accessibility for non-speakers), and lacks English examples. Including a translated transcript snippet, network embeddings visualization, or technical details about the pipeline would substantially strengthen reproducibility and clarity.

Weaknesses:  
The introduction overemphasizes general NLP background, including tangential references (e.g., Salton 1989 in 4.2.3; sentiment classification, pedestrian detection in lines 652–654). Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) should be condensed to focus strictly on MCI-related NLP work. The paper would benefit from compressing Sections 1–3 (5 pages) to prioritize technical innovation and include English examples of ABCD/Cinderella narratives.  

Critical technical gaps require clarification:  
1. **Word embedding sources**: The authors generate word-level networks from continuous representations (lines 272–289) but do not specify training data sources. Were embeddings trained solely on ABCD/Cinderella datasets, or did they leverage external corpora? Line 513–525 mentions sub-word (n-gram) networks for embeddings, yet the training data remains unclear.  
2. **Embedding methodology**: How were similarities between words computed for short Portuguese narratives (ABCD dataset)? The claim that "two kinds of networks together provide better accuracy" (lines 513–525) lacks evidence; the paper does not justify this combination or address whether it outperforms single-network approaches.  
3. **Out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words**: Line 516 references OOV words, but their origin is unexplained. Given the small dataset, OOVs likely stem from limited vocabulary coverage—this needs elaboration.  

General Discussion:  
The research direction is valuable: leveraging NLP for cognitive impairment detection, especially for under-resourced languages like Portuguese. The paper should explicitly address how embeddings were computed for Portuguese (e.g., using multilingual models or transfer learning) and clarify transcription norms (NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2) to aid reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a promising interdisciplinary approach but requires significant revision to address methodological transparency, dataset limitations, and accessibility. The core idea merits attention, but current execution lacks sufficient detail to validate claims or enable replication. Strengthening the technical exposition, clarifying embedding training, and providing English examples would transform this into a compelling contribution. The 60–85% accuracy range suggests meaningful potential, but the paper must reconcile its initial optimism with the conclusion’s caution through clearer methodology and data reporting.
