

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex networks enriched with word embeddings, applied to speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments. The primary contributions are: (1) a framework for modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with semantic information via word embeddings, which avoids manual correction of disfluencies; (2) empirical demonstration that this network-based representation outperforms linguistic features across three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD); and (3) the successful application of multi-view learning to combine network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features, achieving up to 10% accuracy improvement.  

**Strengths**  
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem: current approaches to MCI detection rely on linguistic features that require manually corrected transcripts, which is impractical for large-scale assessment. By modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings, the authors avoid this bottleneck while capturing semantic relationships. The method is language-agnostic and lightweight, making it suitable for automated clinical screening.  
2. The evaluation is comprehensive, using three distinct datasets (English and Portuguese) with different narrative lengths. The results consistently show that the network-based approach outperforms linguistic features across all datasets (e.g., 75% accuracy on Cinderella vs. 65% for linguistic features), and multi-view learning achieves significant gains (10% improvement on Cinderella).  
3. The authors provide a clear technical justification for their preprocessing choices, particularly the exclusion of lemmatization (which can introduce errors in disfluent speech) and the enrichment of networks with word embeddings to address the limitations of short texts. The ablation studies (Tables 4–7) convincingly demonstrate the value of network enrichment.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Disfluency handling lacks justification**: The paper removes disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, repetitions) during preprocessing but fails to explain why this is appropriate. Given that disfluencies are a known marker of MCI (e.g., in Tóth et al., 2015), removing them may discard critical diagnostic features. The authors should clarify whether disfluencies were intentionally excluded to avoid parsing errors, or whether they were retained in the final model.  
2. **Insufficient detail on word embeddings**: The paper states that word embeddings were trained using "default parameters" but does not specify whether the embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets themselves or on external corpora. Given the small size of the datasets (e.g., 20 MCI and 20 control participants in Cinderella), training embeddings on such limited data could lead to poor generalization. The authors must clarify this and justify their choice.  
3. **Lack of statistical significance**: The paper reports accuracy improvements (e.g., "10% improvement on Cinderella") but provides no statistical tests to confirm these differences are meaningful. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it is unclear whether the improvements are robust or merely due to chance.  
4. **Inadequate explanation of multi-view learning**: The paper claims multi-view learning "achieved the highest accuracy" but omits details about the implementation (e.g., how feature spaces were combined, whether weights were learned). This makes it difficult to reproduce or evaluate the approach.  
5. **Missing comparison to tf-idf**: The paper uses term frequency for BoW features but does not justify why tf-idf (the standard weighting scheme in NLP) was not employed. The authors should address this omission and explain why their choice was preferable.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why were disfluencies (e.g., "uh," repetitions) removed during preprocessing, given that they may be indicative of MCI? Could the authors provide evidence that these features were not useful for classification?  
2. Which datasets were used to train the word embeddings? If the embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets themselves, how did the authors mitigate overfitting given the small sample sizes?  
3. Why was tf-idf not used for BoW features, especially since it is the standard in text classification?  
4. How was the threshold for edge creation in the enriched networks (based on cosine similarity) determined? Was it optimized, or fixed to a default value?  
5. Were statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) performed to confirm that the accuracy improvements from multi-view learning are significant?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper’s abstract and early sections would benefit from tightening. For instance, "impacting in parsing results" should be "impacting parsing results," and "as it a lightweight" should be "as it is a lightweight." The related work could be strengthened by explicitly comparing the authors' approach to prior work on disfluency handling (e.g., Fraser et al., 2015; Vincze et al., 2016) and clarifying how their method differs. The claim that complex networks are "language independent" is overstated, as word embeddings are language-specific; the authors should qualify this statement. Finally, the paper should cite DementiaBank (https://talkbank.org/DementiaBank/) and the Western Aphasia Battery's Picnic picture when first mentioned.
