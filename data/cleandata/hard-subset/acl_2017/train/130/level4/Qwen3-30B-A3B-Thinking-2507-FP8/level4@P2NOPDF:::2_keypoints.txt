1. Addresses a clinically significant problem: identifying MCI from speech transcripts across three datasets.  
2. Proposes a graph-based method leveraging word co-occurrence to derive lexical, syntactic, and linguistic features.  
3. Evaluates models using 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers with dataset-dependent performance.  
4. Problem is well-motivated and datasets are appropriate for the task.  
5. Manuscript has significant grammatical and spelling errors that hinder readability and require thorough editing.  
6. Problem definition is ambiguous—transcript-level vs participant-level classification is unclear, and dataset tables report study-level rather than transcript-level statistics.  
7. Dataset descriptions overemphasize irrelevant details (e.g., patient age) and consume excessive space.  
8. Critical data inconsistency: reported participant totals (Section 4.1.1) conflict with Table 1’s male/female sums, undermining confidence in the data.  
9. Unclear justification for the chosen graph construction (co-occurrence) versus alternative graph representations like vector-similarity links.  
10. Method lacks domain-specific adaptation—no use of clinical ontologies or medical NLP tools despite biomedical datasets.  
11. Class distributions are not reported, making accuracy an inappropriate primary metric for potentially imbalanced binary classification; F1-score is recommended.  
12. Statistical reporting is weak: overly precise results for small datasets and no significance testing to assess whether differences are meaningful.  
13. Overall, substantial revisions are needed to clarify methodology, fix data inconsistencies, justify design choices, improve statistical rigor, and correct language issues.
