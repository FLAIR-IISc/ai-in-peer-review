

Strengths:  
This paper investigates applying NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) for identifying mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors initially claim successful distinction between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141–144), yet the conclusion states accuracy ranges from 60% to 85% (lines 781–785), noting this "is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments." While the opening is more optimistic than the conclusion, the overall message remains encouraging, prompting interest in the methodology.  

The dataset comprises only 20 healthy participants and 20 controls (20+20), and is incomprehensible to non-Portuguese speakers. To enhance accessibility, we recommend including more technical details, an English-translated transcript example, and a sample network visualization with embeddings for a representative transcript.  

Weaknesses:  
The introduction provides a thorough literature review but includes tangential references. For instance, Salton (1989) (section 4.2.3) offers broad NLP background and could be omitted. Similarly, references to "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652–654) are unrelated and should be removed. Sections 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) and the first five pages can be significantly condensed to focus strictly on the paper’s core topic, with greater emphasis on technical innovation and English-translated examples from the ABCD or Cindarela datasets.  

The ABCD dataset’s short Portuguese narratives raise questions about word similarity computation for embedding generation. Section 3.1 (lines 272–289) states word-level networks derive from continuous word representations, but the source of these representations is unclear: were they trained solely on ABCD+Cinderella, or supplemented with external corpora? Similarly, section 4.3 (lines 513–525) mentions sub-word (n-gram) networks for embeddings, yet the training source remains unspecified. The paper also fails to justify whether combining these network types genuinely improves accuracy, nor does it clarify the origin of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516).  

General Discussion:  
The work is timely, as NLP-driven cognitive impairment detection holds significant potential. Its focus on Portuguese—a low-resource language—adds value, but the paper should better explain how embeddings are computed for such languages. The text requires revision: shorten sections 1–3, compress section 4.1, and expand experimental explanations. Clarification of NURC/SP Norm 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription standards would also be beneficial.
