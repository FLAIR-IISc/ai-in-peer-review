- Addresses a clinically significant problem: automated detection of MCI from speech transcripts.  
- Proposes a novel graph-based method modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word-embedding semantics.  
- Method avoids manual transcript correction required by traditional linguistic analyses.  
- Evaluated on three real-world datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, ABCD).  
- Reports consistent improvement of embedding-enriched complex networks over linguistic features, with ensemble/multi-view yielding further gains (e.g., +10% Cinderella, +4.71% ABCD).

- Manuscript has many grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, requiring thorough language editing.  
- Task definition is unclear: transcript-level vs. participant-level classification and transcript-level class counts are not provided; tables show irrelevant demographic details and inconsistent counts.  
- Methodological motivation is weak: unclear why enrich co-occurrence networks with embeddings instead of directly connecting words by vector similarity.  
- Lacks use of domain-specific biomedical tools or clinical language insights that could strengthen the approach.  
- Evaluation uses accuracy without addressing class imbalance; appropriate metrics like F1 and class distributions are not reported.  
- Statistical rigor is insufficient: tiny sample sizes, no significance testing, and over-precise reporting (four decimals) undermine result reliability.

- Clarify whether the classification unit is each transcript or each participant.  
- Explain how the cosine-similarity threshold for adding edges was chosen and justified.  
- Justify reporting results to four decimal places given small datasets and provide statistical validation.  
- Explain why embeddings are used to enrich co-occurrence networks instead of building graphs solely from semantic similarity.
