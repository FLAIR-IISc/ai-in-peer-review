

Summary: This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), claiming this approach outperforms traditional linguistic features. The authors demonstrate this methodology across three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery), showing that complex network features achieve competitive classification accuracy with improvements from ensemble and multi-view learning approaches.

Main Contributions: 
1. The novel application of enriched complex networks to model speech transcripts for MCI detection, avoiding the need for manual transcript correction required by linguistic approaches.
2. Demonstrated superiority of network-based features over linguistic features across all datasets, with multi-view learning achieving up to 10% improvement in classification accuracy.
3. Validation of the approach across multiple languages (English and Portuguese) and datasets, providing a language-independent solution for MCI detection.

Strengths:
1. The work addresses an important clinical challenge (early MCI detection) with a practical solution that avoids labor-intensive manual transcript correction, which is critical for large-scale assessments.
2. The complex network approach with word embeddings demonstrates robust performance across multiple datasets, with the multi-view method achieving the highest accuracy (80%) on the ABCD dataset, a 4.71% improvement over the best individual classifier.
3. The paper provides valuable insights into the potential of NLP for cognitive assessment, particularly for under-resourced languages like Portuguese, where traditional linguistic approaches face significant challenges due to lack of robust parsing tools.

Weaknesses:
1. The datasets used are relatively small (20 MCI and 20 controls for the Cinderella dataset), and the Portuguese-language focus limits accessibility and reproducibility for non-Portuguese speaking researchers without translation support.
2. The paper contains excessive background material and irrelevant references (e.g., sentiment classification and pedestrian detection examples) that could be condensed to focus on the core technical contribution.
3. Critical technical details about word embedding training are missing, including the source of training data (ABCD and Cinderella only or external corpora) and how out-of-vocabulary words were handled, which undermines the reproducibility of the approach.
4. The manuscript contains several technical errors (e.g., "as it a lightweight" on line 29, "PLN" instead of "NLP" on line 188), which need correction to maintain academic rigor.

Questions to Authors:
1. What specific corpora were used to train the word embeddings, and how did this compare to training on the ABCD and Cinderella datasets alone?
2. Please clarify how out-of-vocabulary words were handled during the embedding process, as this is critical for understanding the model's robustness.
3. Could you provide a sample English-translated transcript from the ABCD or Cinderella datasets to demonstrate the approach for non-Portuguese speakers?
