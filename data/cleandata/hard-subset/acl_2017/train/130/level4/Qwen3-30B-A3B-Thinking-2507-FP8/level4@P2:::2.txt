

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network representations of speech transcripts, enriched with word embeddings. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enhance them with semantic information through word embeddings, avoiding manual preprocessing required by traditional linguistic approaches. The main contributions are:  
1. A language-agnostic method for MCI detection using complex network features that handles disfluencies without manual transcript correction.  
2. Demonstration that network-based features outperform linguistic features across three diverse datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, ABCD), with multi-view learning achieving up to 10% accuracy improvement.  
3. A comprehensive evaluation showing the robustness of network-based features across different text lengths and linguistic contexts, with applications to both English and Portuguese datasets.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel methodology addressing a critical gap**: The paper tackles a significant challenge in MCI diagnosis—disfluencies in speech transcripts that undermine traditional linguistic analysis. By modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings, the authors provide a robust, language-independent solution that avoids the need for manual transcript correction, which is prohibitively expensive for large-scale assessment.  
2. **Strong empirical validation**: The approach is rigorously evaluated across three distinct datasets with varying linguistic and structural properties. The results consistently show that enriched complex networks (CNE) outperform linguistic features (LM) and bag-of-words (BoW) across all datasets, with multi-view learning achieving the highest accuracy (80% on ABCD, 75% on Cinderella). This robustness across datasets strengthens the paper's contribution.  
3. **Practical relevance**: The work directly addresses a real-world clinical need for early, scalable MCI detection. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves accuracy comparable to existing state-of-the-art approaches (60-85%), while offering a fully automated pipeline that avoids dependency on language-specific parsers or manual annotation.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Poorly structured presentation and language issues**: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that significantly hinder comprehension. For example, the description of the Cookie Theft dataset (Section 4.1.1) states "326 people diagnosed with probable AD" but Table 1 shows only 64 participants (43 MCI + 21 AD). The age and education details in Tables 1-3 are irrelevant to the classification task and consume excessive space. A thorough language edit is essential before publication.  
2. **Unclear problem formulation**: The paper fails to clearly define the classification task. It is unclear whether each transcript (not patient) constitutes a single instance, or if multiple transcripts per patient were used. The dataset descriptions should focus on transcript-level statistics (e.g., "43 MCI transcripts, 21 AD transcripts") rather than study-level demographics. The inconsistent data counts (e.g., 326 people vs. 64 in Table 1) undermine confidence in the results.  
3. **Lack of statistical validation**: The results report accuracy to four decimal places (e.g., 0.7173) on small datasets (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft), yet no statistical tests are provided to confirm whether observed improvements (e.g., 10% in Cinderella) are significant. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it is impossible to assess whether these gains are meaningful or due to random variation.  
4. **Insufficient justification for methodology**: The paper does not adequately explain why enriching co-occurrence networks with semantic similarity (via cosine threshold) is superior to alternative approaches, such as directly connecting nodes based on word embedding similarity. This omission weakens the methodological contribution.  
5. **Inappropriate evaluation metric**: The paper relies solely on accuracy for binary classification, despite unclear class distributions (e.g., 43 MCI vs. 21 AD transcripts in Cookie Theft). Given potential class imbalance, F1-score would be a more appropriate metric, as accuracy can be misleading in imbalanced settings.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Please clarify the classification instance: Is each transcript treated as an independent sample (with multiple transcripts per patient), or are patients the instances? Provide transcript counts per class (not patient counts) in Tables 1-3.  
2. How were the cosine similarity thresholds for network enrichment selected? Was this optimized on a validation set, or was a fixed value used?  
3. Given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts), how were statistical significance tests performed for the reported improvements (e.g., 10% in Cinderella)?  
4. Why were linguistic features discarded for Portuguese in the original dataset (Section 4.2.2), and how does this affect the comparability of results across languages?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper's core contribution—using enriched complex networks for MCI detection—has significant potential. However, the current presentation obscures this contribution due to language issues, unclear problem formulation, and lack of statistical validation. Addressing these weaknesses would substantially strengthen the paper.
