

Summary: The paper presents a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network features derived from speech transcripts, enriched with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are: (1) modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to automatically detect MCI without manual transcript correction; (2) demonstrating that complex network features outperform linguistic features across three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD); and (3) showing that ensemble and multi-view learning approaches improve classification accuracy, with the multi-view method achieving 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier.

Strengths: The study thoroughly describes the problem domain, datasets, and methodology for MCI detection using speech transcripts. The paper's approach to modeling transcripts as complex networks with semantic enrichment addresses a significant limitation in current methods that require manual correction of disfluencies in patient speech. The experimental evaluation across three distinct datasets provides strong evidence that the proposed method is effective, with clear quantitative results showing consistent performance improvements over linguistic features and baseline classifiers.

Weaknesses: The paper requires substantial revision to improve clarity and completeness. The explanation of multi-view learning is insufficient, making it difficult to understand the implementation details. The description of linguistic features lacks specificity, and it is unclear which exact features were used. The paper does not clarify what datasets were used to generate the word embeddings, which is problematic given the small size of the provided datasets. The rationale for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is not adequately justified, despite their potential relevance to MCI detection. The paper fails to explain why tf-idf weighting was not used for Bag-of-Words features, which is the standard approach in text classification. Statistical significance tests are missing to validate the claimed performance improvements. The related work section is superficial and lacks detailed comparisons with prior approaches.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify: (1) Which datasets were used to train the word embeddings, and why were these specific datasets chosen over larger external corpora? (2) What is the precise rationale for removing disfluencies, given their potential diagnostic value in MCI detection? (3) Why was tf-idf not used for Bag-of-Words features, and how would this change the results? (4) Please provide statistical significance tests for the reported accuracy improvements across datasets. (5) Please elaborate on the multi-view learning implementation details, including how feature spaces were combined and how weights were assigned.
