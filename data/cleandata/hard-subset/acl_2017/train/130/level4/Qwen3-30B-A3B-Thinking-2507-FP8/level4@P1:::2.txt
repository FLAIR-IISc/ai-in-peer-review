

Strengths:
This paper addresses a clinically significant problem—detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through automated analysis of speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments. The authors propose a novel graph-based approach that models transcripts as complex networks, leveraging word co-occurrence relationships while enriching these networks with semantic information from word embeddings. This represents a meaningful methodological contribution, as it avoids the need for manual transcript correction required by traditional linguistic analysis approaches. The work is well-motivated, targets a clinically relevant problem, and uses appropriate datasets reflecting real-world assessment scenarios. The authors evaluate their approach across three distinct datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD), demonstrating consistent performance improvements over traditional linguistic features. Their results show that complex networks enriched with word embeddings outperform linguistic features across all datasets, with ensemble and multi-view learning providing additional gains (10% improvement in Cinderella and 4.71% in ABCD).

Weaknesses:
The paper requires substantial revisions to address critical issues:

1. Language Quality: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that significantly impede readability. A thorough language edit is essential before this work can be considered for publication.

2. Problem Definition: The classification task is poorly defined. The paper should explicitly state that each transcript is classified as MCI or Control (not as individuals), and provide transcript-level statistics. Tables 1-3 should present transcript counts (MCI vs. Control) rather than demographic details of participants, which are irrelevant to the classification task. The description of the Cookie Theft dataset (section 4.1.1) states 326 people were involved, but Table 1 shows fewer than 100 participants.

3. Methodological Justification: The motivation for enriching co-occurrence networks with semantic information is unclear. Why not directly construct a graph where words are connected based on vector similarity without relying on co-occurrence? This fundamental methodological choice requires stronger justification, particularly since the authors acknowledge that disfluencies make traditional linguistic features problematic.

4. Domain-Specific Considerations: The authors work in a biomedical domain but do not leverage any domain-specific tools or knowledge that could enhance their approach. While the paper mentions the clinical significance of language impairment in MCI, it fails to incorporate domain-specific insights that could improve the analysis.

5. Evaluation Metrics: The paper reports accuracy without addressing class imbalance, which is critical for a binary classification task. F1 score would have been a more appropriate metric. Additionally, the class distribution is not clearly presented, making it difficult to assess whether accuracy is a meaningful measure.

6. Statistical Rigor: Results are reported to four decimal places for datasets with very few samples (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft). The paper lacks statistical tests to determine whether the reported improvements (e.g., 10% in Cinderella) are statistically significant. The small dataset sizes and lack of statistical validation undermine the reliability of the reported performance gains.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you clarify the exact classification task? Is each transcript classified as MCI or Control, or are the classifications based on individual participants?
2. What motivated the specific threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity? How was this threshold determined?
3. Given the small dataset sizes, how do you justify reporting results to four decimal places without statistical validation of the differences?
4. Why did you choose to enrich networks with word embeddings rather than directly constructing graphs based on semantic similarity without co-occurrence?
