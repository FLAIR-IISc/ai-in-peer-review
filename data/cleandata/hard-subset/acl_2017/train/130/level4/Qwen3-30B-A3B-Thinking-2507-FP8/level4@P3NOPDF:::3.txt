

The paper presents a study on detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech narratives, leveraging existing representations and features. The problem statement, datasets, and domain context are well-motivated and clearly described, making the work relevant to the ACL community. The proposed methodology and experimental design are generally sound, though several critical gaps undermine the contribution's novelty and reproducibility. I recommend major revisions to address the following points before acceptance.

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Methodological Ambiguity**:  
   - The core multi-view learning approach lacks essential details. The paper states "multi-view learning" is used but omits the specific architecture, fusion strategy, or rationale for its selection. Without this, the technical contribution remains unclear.  
   - The linguistic feature set is inadequately defined. Terms like "syntactic complexity" or "lexical diversity" require concrete operationalization (e.g., specific metrics or formulas). Similarly, the rationale for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions) is absent. Given that disfluencies are clinically relevant markers of MCI, this preprocessing choice needs strong justification—e.g., evidence that they confound the task or were irrelevant in prior work.  
   - Word embeddings are trained on "the 3 datasets" (Cinderella, DementiaBank, ABCD), but their combined size (~1,000–2,000 transcribed narratives) is likely insufficient for robust embeddings. The paper must clarify whether external data (e.g., general-domain corpora) was used and cite sources for embedding training.  

2. **Experimental Rigor**:  
   - The absence of statistical significance tests for performance differences (e.g., between classifiers or feature sets) weakens the conclusions. Without p-values or confidence intervals, claims about "superior performance" of certain methods are unsubstantiated.  
   - The choice of TF-IDF weighting is unexplained. As the standard for Bag-of-Words (BoW) in NLP, its omission requires justification (e.g., "TF-IDF was tested but yielded worse results due to X"). The paper’s reliance on simple term frequency alone is questionable.  
   - The rationale for excluding disfluencies from the BoW model (Lines 115–117) is unclear. If disfluencies are removed from the *input* for BoW, why not also for other features (e.g., linguistic features)? This inconsistency needs addressing.  

3. **Presentation and Clarity**:  
   - The related work (Section 3) is superficial. It lists prior studies but fails to contrast their methods, datasets, or results with this work’s approach. For instance, it omits how the current multi-view framework differs from prior MCI studies using linguistic features (e.g., Aluisio et al., 2016). A comparative table would clarify the novelty.  
   - Key terms lack definitions: "PLN" (Line 188), "WML subtest" (Line 170), and "ABCD" (Line 323) appear without citation or explanation. DementiaBank (Line 157) and the "Picnic picture" (Line 162) require authoritative references.  
   - Repetitive phrasing and grammatical errors persist (e.g., "impacting in parsing results" → "impacting parsing results"; "consisted of" → "consist of"). The abstract is overly verbose and should be condensed to 150 words.  

**Specific Revisions Needed**  
- **Section 4.2.2 (Linguistic Features)**: Expand to define *all* features (e.g., "lexical diversity = number of unique words / total words") and justify their relevance to MCI.  
- **Section 4.2.1 (Word Embeddings)**: Explicitly state the training corpus (e.g., "embeddings trained on DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD using Word2Vec with 100 dimensions") and acknowledge dataset limitations.  
- **Section 4.3 (Classification)**: Detail the multi-view learning scheme (e.g., "late fusion via weighted averaging of SVM and LSTM outputs") and add statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests).  
- **Figures/Tables**: Table 1 needs demographic statistics (e.g., mean age, gender distribution) and dataset sizes. The caption should clarify "demographic information" includes these.  
- **Citations**: Ensure all references (e.g., DementiaBank, Western Aphasia Battery) follow ACL guidelines. Add citations for "transcription norms" (Lines 340–440).  

**Recommendation**  
This work addresses a meaningful clinical problem with a technically feasible approach. However, the current manuscript lacks the methodological precision and experimental rigor expected at ACL. The ambiguities around feature engineering, preprocessing choices, and statistical validation prevent a clear assessment of the proposed method’s novelty. I strongly recommend *major revision* to resolve these issues. If addressed, the paper could make a valuable contribution to clinical NLP. Without these revisions, the claims about the "best scenario" (Line 147) and multi-view learning remain unsubstantiated.
