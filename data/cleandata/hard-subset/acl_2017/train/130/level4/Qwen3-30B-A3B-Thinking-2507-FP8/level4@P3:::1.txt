

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network features enriched with word embeddings, applied to speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks, enhanced with semantic similarity from word embeddings, to create a language-independent representation that avoids manual preprocessing of disfluent speech. The primary contributions are: (1) a robust framework for MCI detection that bypasses manual transcript correction, (2) empirical demonstration that network-based features outperform linguistic features across three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD), and (3) a multi-view learning strategy that achieves up to 10% accuracy gains over individual classifiers.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical Impact on Clinical Assessment**: The work addresses a critical gap in MCI diagnosis—language impairment is frequently undervalued in cognitive assessments, yet disfluencies in patient speech invalidate traditional linguistic feature extraction. By modeling transcripts as complex networks, the authors eliminate the need for manual correction of disfluencies (e.g., repetitions, revisions), enabling scalable, automated screening. This is particularly valuable for resource-constrained clinical settings.  

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The approach outperforms linguistic features in all three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, ABCD), with the enriched network (CNE) achieving the highest accuracy. For instance, in the Cinderella dataset, the multi-view method (CNE-LM-BoW) improved accuracy by 10% over the best individual classifier (75% vs. 65%). The results are comparable to state-of-the-art methods (60–85% accuracy), demonstrating clinical viability without relying on language-specific resources.  

3. **Innovative Integration of Network and Embedding Techniques**: The enrichment of co-occurrence networks with semantic word embeddings (via cosine similarity) effectively captures short-text semantics, addressing a key limitation of prior work (Amancio, 2015c). The method’s adaptability to small texts (e.g., ABCD narratives with ~5 sentences) is particularly noteworthy, as prior network-based NLP approaches struggled with such brevity.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited Dataset Size and Diversity**: The Cinderella and ABCD datasets each contain only 20 MCI and 20 control participants (40 total), with ABCD having just 23 MCI and 20 controls. This small sample size limits statistical power and generalizability. The authors acknowledge this limitation (lines 781–785), yet the paper does not discuss how they addressed overfitting or whether results hold with larger cohorts. A stronger case would include cross-dataset validation or ablation studies on dataset size.  

2. **Ambiguity in Word Embedding Training**: The paper states that word embeddings are generated from "continuous word representations" (lines 272–289) but fails to clarify the training source. Were embeddings trained on ABCD/Cinderella only, or on external corpora (e.g., Portuguese Wikipedia)? The mention of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) implies the training corpus was limited, yet this is not explained. Without this detail, it is unclear whether the embeddings generalize beyond the specific datasets.  

3. **Overuse of Irrelevant Background References**: Sections 4.1–4.2 include tangential citations (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) that distract from the core contribution. For example, lines 608–621 redundantly describe ensemble learning without linking it to the MCI problem. Streamlining these sections would enhance focus on the paper’s technical novelty.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How were word embeddings trained? Were they derived from external Portuguese corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, news), or solely from the ABCD/Cinderella datasets? If the latter, how were out-of-vocabulary words handled during inference?  
2. The Cinderella dataset (Table 2) includes two versions: manually revised (Table 6) and automatically processed (Table 5). Why does the revised version (which removes disfluencies) show *lower* accuracy for linguistic features (LM) but *higher* for networks (CNE)? Does this imply networks are more robust to disfluencies?  
3. The ABCD dataset has very short narratives (5.23 sentences on average). How does the network enrichment method avoid overfitting with such limited text?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper’s title and abstract overstate the novelty ("enriching complex networks with word embeddings"). While the *application* to MCI is novel, the network-enrichment technique (Perozzi et al., 2014) is not. Clarify the contribution as "application to MCI diagnosis" rather than methodological innovation.  
- Include a brief English translation of one Portuguese transcript (e.g., from ABCD) and a visual example of the enriched network (Fig. 2) to improve accessibility.  
- Correct minor errors: "as it a lightweight" (line 29), "PLN" (line 188 → "NLP"), and "72,0" (Table 3 → "72.0").  

**Overall Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution to clinical NLP by demonstrating a scalable, language-independent method for MCI detection. With clarifications on dataset size, embedding training, and a sharper focus on the MCI-specific innovation, it would be a strong fit for ACL. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the questions above.
