

- Strengths:  
This paper addresses the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. It proposes a graph-based approach that leverages co-occurrence information between words in transcripts, encoding features using lexical, syntactic, and other graph properties. The authors report results via 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers, demonstrating varying performance across datasets. The work targets a well-defined clinical problem and utilizes appropriate datasets.  

- Weaknesses:  
The paper suffers from several critical issues:  
1.  **Readability**: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and spelling mistakes, significantly hindering comprehension. A thorough language review is essential before publication.  
2.  **Problem Definition and Dataset Description**: The core classification task is inadequately described. If each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI (rather than patient-level), dataset tables (1–3) should report transcript-level statistics, not study details. Irrelevant information (e.g., patient age) is overemphasized, consuming approximately two pages of text without impact on the classification task. Additionally, inconsistencies exist: Section 4.1.1 states 326 participants were involved, yet Table 1’s reported male/female counts sum to <100.  
3.  **Graph Construction Justification**: The motivation for enriching the graph (beyond co-occurrence) is unclear. Why not construct a word-node graph with edges based on vector similarity (e.g., cosine similarity), rather than solely relying on co-occurrence?  
4.  **Domain-Specific Approach**: The datasets originate from a biomedical domain, yet no domain-specific tools or linguistic resources are leveraged.  
5.  **Evaluation Metrics**: The class distribution across datasets is ambiguous, making accuracy an inappropriate evaluation metric for an imbalanced binary classification task. F1-score would have been a more suitable measure.  
6.  **Statistical Rigor**: Results are reported to four decimal places for extremely small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts), with no statistical significance testing for performance improvements. This renders claims about model gains unsubstantiated.
