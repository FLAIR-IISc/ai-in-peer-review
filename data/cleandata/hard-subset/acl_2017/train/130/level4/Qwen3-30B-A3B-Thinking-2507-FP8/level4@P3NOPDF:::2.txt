

**Strengths:**  
This paper addresses a clinically significant problem—identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) via speech transcript analysis—using three distinct datasets. The authors propose a novel graph-based approach that leverages word co-occurrence to encode lexical and syntactic features, evaluated across multiple classifiers with 5-fold cross-validation. The methodology targets a well-defined problem, and the use of diverse datasets strengthens the study’s relevance.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Readability and Clarity:** The manuscript suffers from inconsistent grammar, spelling errors, and awkward phrasing (e.g., "is problem," "a number of classifiers"). These issues significantly hinder comprehension and require thorough professional editing.  

2. **Problem Definition and Dataset Description:**  
   - The core classification task is ambiguously described. It appears each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI, yet the dataset sections (Tables 1–3) focus on study-level details (e.g., patient demographics, age) irrelevant to the task. The description of patient counts (e.g., 326 participants in Section 4.1.1 vs. <100 total in Table 1) is inconsistent and unexplained, undermining credibility.  
   - The manuscript dedicates ~2 pages to non-essential dataset metadata. Tables should report *transcript-level statistics* (e.g., number of transcripts per class, speech duration), not study characteristics. Age, for instance, is immaterial to the classification task and should be omitted.  

3. **Graph Construction Justification:** The motivation for enriching the graph with co-occurrence-based features is unclear. Why not construct the graph using word vector similarity (e.g., cosine similarity between embeddings) instead of co-occurrence? This alternative would better capture semantic relationships without relying on contextual word adjacency. The authors should explicitly justify their design choice.  

4. **Domain-Specific Adaptation:** The biomedical nature of the datasets (e.g., clinical speech) is overlooked. Standard NLP tools for biomedical text (e.g., UMLS, domain-specific embeddings) could enhance feature relevance. Their absence weakens the approach’s applicability to the target domain.  

5. **Evaluation Metrics and Class Imbalance:** The paper reports accuracy without addressing class distribution (e.g., MCI vs. non-MCI ratio in each dataset). For binary classification, accuracy is misleading if classes are imbalanced; F1-score or AUC would be more appropriate. The absence of this discussion limits the validity of results.  

6. **Statistical Rigor and Reporting:**  
   - Results for small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts in one dataset) are reported to four decimal places without statistical validation (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals). This overstates precision.  
   - The lack of statistical tests for performance differences between models (e.g., "model X improved by 0.0012 over model Y") makes it impossible to assess significance. For such low-resource settings, bootstrapping or paired t-tests are essential.  

**Recommendations:**  
- **Revise for Clarity:** Restructure the dataset section to present *transcript-level statistics* (e.g., "Dataset A contains 120 MCI transcripts and 150 non-MCI transcripts"). Remove all non-essential details (e.g., patient age).  
- **Justify Graph Design:** Explicitly compare co-occurrence vs. semantic similarity approaches and explain why the former is superior for this task.  
- **Improve Evaluation:** Report class distributions, switch to F1-score (or similar), and apply statistical tests for model comparisons. For small datasets, avoid excessive decimal precision.  
- **Leverage Domain Tools:** Integrate biomedical NLP resources to enrich features (e.g., using MedLine for word embeddings).  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The problem is meaningful, and the graph-based approach shows potential. However, critical flaws in methodology description, evaluation rigor, and domain adaptation prevent the results from being convincing. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the contribution. The paper is currently unsuitable for publication in its present form.
