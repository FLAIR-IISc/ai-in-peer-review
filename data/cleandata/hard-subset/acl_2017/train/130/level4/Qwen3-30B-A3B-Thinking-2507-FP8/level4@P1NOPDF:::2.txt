

Strengths:  
This paper addresses the well-defined problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) patients through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence patterns within transcripts, extracting features from lexical, syntactic, and other graph properties. Results are presented via 5-fold cross-validation using multiple classifiers, with performance varying across the three datasets. The choice of problem and datasets is appropriate and relevant.  

Weaknesses:  
The paper contains several significant issues that require substantial revision:  

1.  **Poor English Quality**: The manuscript contains frequent grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, making it difficult to read. A professional language edit is essential before reconsideration.  

2.  **Unclear Problem Formulation & Dataset Description**:  
    - The core classification task is ambiguously described. It appears each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI. If so, dataset descriptions must report numbers at the *transcript level* (e.g., number of transcripts per class), not the participant level.  
    - Tables 1–3 describe the *studies* that generated the transcripts, not the *transcripts themselves*—this is misleading. Crucially, the patients' ages are irrelevant to the classification task and should be omitted.  
    - The paper unnecessarily dedicates ~2 pages to irrelevant dataset details. A critical inconsistency exists: Section 4.1.1 states 326 participants were involved, yet Table 1’s sum of males and females is under 100. This discrepancy must be resolved.  

3.  **Lack of Justification for Graph Construction**: The motivation for enriching the graph (beyond simple co-occurrence) is unclear. Why not construct a graph where each word is a node connected by semantic similarity (e.g., vector cosine similarity), rather than relying solely on co-occurrence? The authors must justify their graph construction choice.  

4.  **Absence of Domain-Specific Techniques**: The datasets originate from a biomedical domain, yet the authors do not leverage any domain-specific linguistic or clinical tools (e.g., medical ontologies, specialized NLP pipelines), which would be expected for such a task.  

5.  **Inappropriate Evaluation Metrics**: The class distribution across datasets is not clearly stated, making it unclear whether accuracy is a valid metric. For a binary classification task, the F1-score (or similar) would be more appropriate than accuracy, especially if class imbalance exists.  

6.  **Overstated Results & Lack of Statistical Validation**: Results are reported to four decimal places for very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts), without statistical significance testing for performance differences. This makes it impossible to assess whether observed gains are meaningful or due to chance.  

These issues significantly undermine the paper’s contribution and clarity. Addressing them—particularly the problem formulation, dataset description, graph justification, and statistical rigor—is essential for publication.
