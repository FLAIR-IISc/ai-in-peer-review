

Strengths:  
This paper addresses the clinically significant problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence patterns of words in transcripts to derive features capturing lexical, syntactic, and other linguistic properties. Results are evaluated via 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers, with performance varying across datasets. The problem definition is well-motivated, and the chosen datasets are appropriate for the task.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Language Quality**: The manuscript contains significant grammatical and spelling errors, substantially hindering readability. A thorough language edit is essential before publication.  

2. **Problem Definition and Dataset Presentation**:  
   - The core classification task is ambiguously described. If each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI (which appears to be the case), dataset tables (1–3) should present *transcript-level* statistics (e.g., number of transcripts per class), not study-level details (e.g., number of participants).  
   - Irrelevant details (e.g., patient age) are overemphasized in the dataset description, consuming excessive space (2 pages) without contributing to the classification task.  
   - A critical discrepancy exists: Section 4.1.1 states 326 participants were involved, but Table 1’s male/female totals sum to <100. This inconsistency undermines confidence in the data.  

3. **Graph Construction Justification**:  
   The rationale for enriching the graph via co-occurrence (instead of a more direct approach, e.g., representing words as nodes connected by vector similarity) is unclear. The authors should explicitly justify why co-occurrence-based graphs are superior to alternative graph constructions for this task.  

4. **Lack of Domain-Specific Adaptation**:  
   The datasets originate from a biomedical domain, yet the method does not leverage domain-specific resources (e.g., clinical ontologies, medical NLP tools). This omission limits the approach’s relevance to the target domain.  

5. **Evaluation Metrics and Class Imbalance**:  
   The class distribution across datasets is not reported, making it impossible to assess whether accuracy is a suitable metric (e.g., for imbalanced data). For a binary classification task, F1-score would be a more appropriate primary metric than accuracy.  

6. **Statistical Rigor**:  
   Results are reported to four decimal places for small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts), without statistical significance testing for performance differences. This over-precision obscures whether observed gains are meaningful or due to chance.  

Overall, while the problem is well-motivated, the paper requires substantial revisions to clarify its methodology, address critical data inconsistencies, justify key design choices, and improve statistical reporting. The current presentation risks misleading readers due to language issues, ambiguous task definition, and insufficient methodological justification.
