

Summary: This paper addresses the problem of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts using complex network representations. The main contributions are: (1) modeling speech transcripts as enriched co-occurrence networks to capture linguistic features without linguistic preprocessing; (2) demonstrating that these network features outperform traditional linguistic features across three distinct datasets; (3) achieving significant improvements through ensemble and multi-view learning approaches.  

Strengths:  
1. The paper tackles a clinically significant problem (early MCI detection) using a novel graph-based approach that avoids dependency on manual linguistic preprocessing, which is critical given the disfluencies in patient speech transcripts.  
2. The multi-dataset validation across English (DementiaBank) and Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD) datasets demonstrates robustness and language independence, with the enriched network features (CNE) achieving the highest accuracy in all cases.  
3. The ensemble and multi-view learning approaches yield substantial improvements (e.g., 10% on Cinderella, 4.71% on ABCD), showing practical value for clinical applications where accuracy gains matter.  

Weaknesses:  
1. The manuscript contains significant language errors and grammatical issues, which undermine readability and professionalism; a thorough language edit is essential before publication.  
2. Dataset descriptions are excessively detailed with irrelevant information (e.g., patient age, education, and study procedures), while omitting critical classification-relevant details (e.g., transcript counts per class). Table 1’s inconsistent numbers (e.g., 326 total participants but <100 in demographics) create confusion.  
3. The rationale for enriching co-occurrence networks with semantic edges (via cosine similarity) is unclear; the authors do not justify why this approach is superior to directly building similarity-based networks without co-occurrence.  
4. The evaluation relies solely on accuracy for imbalanced binary classification without statistical validation of improvements (e.g., p-values for 4.71% gains on ABCD), and F1-score would be more appropriate given the clinical context.  
5. The method does not leverage domain-specific biomedical tools or features (e.g., clinical markers), limiting its applicability to real-world MCI screening pipelines.
