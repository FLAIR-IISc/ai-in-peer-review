

- Strengths:
This paper proposes applying NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors claim to distinguish between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141–144). However, the conclusion (lines 781–785) states that "accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%... means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments," presenting a less optimistic view than the introduction. Despite this discrepancy, the overall message is encouraging, prompting interest in the specific methods employed.  
The submitted dataset comprises only 20 healthy participants and 20 controls (20+20), and is inaccessible to non-Portuguese speakers. Including more technical details, an English-translated example transcript, and a sample network with embeddings for a transcript would significantly strengthen the paper.

- Weaknesses:
The introduction and related work section (Section 4.2.3) contains superficially relevant references (e.g., Salton 1989) and unrelated work (e.g., "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images," lines 652–654). Sections 1–3 and 4.2.3 should be condensed to focus strictly on the paper’s topic, prioritizing technical innovation and including English translations of ABCD/Cinderella narratives.  
The short Portuguese narratives (especially in ABCD) raise questions about how word similarities were derived for embeddings. Section 2.3 (lines 272–289) states that word-level networks are generated from continuous word representations, but the source for these representations is unclear: are they derived solely from ABCD+Cinderella, or do external corpora contribute? Similarly, Section 4.3 (lines 513–525) mentions sub-word (n-gram) networks for embeddings but omits the training source. The claim that combining both network types improves accuracy requires justification, and the origin of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is unspecified.

- General Discussion:
The study’s focus on NLP for detecting cognitive impairments is valuable. Its contribution to Portuguese NLP—particularly addressing embedding computation for low-resource languages—deserves explicit explanation.  
The text requires revision: shorten Sections 1–3, compress Section 4.1, and elaborate on experimental details. Clarification on the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms is also needed.
