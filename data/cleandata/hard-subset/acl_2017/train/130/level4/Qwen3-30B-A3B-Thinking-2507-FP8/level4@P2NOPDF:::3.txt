

The paper investigates the application of established linguistic representations and methods for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through speech analysis. The problem statement, datasets, and domain context are comprehensively described. While the proposed approach and experimental design are generally sound, the paper requires substantial revisions to enhance clarity, justification, and methodological rigor. The study is promising but falls short in several critical areas.

**Major Weaknesses:**  
The paper overstates novelty by presenting largely existing techniques as novel. Crucially, key methodological choices lack justification:  
1.  **Disfluency Removal:** The rationale for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is absent. Given their potential diagnostic relevance in MCI, this omission is significant.  
2.  **Word Embeddings:** It is unclear whether embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets (which appear small) or external corpora. The paper states "the 3 datasets" but fails to specify them clearly (e.g., "Cinderella and [other dataset]"), creating confusion.  
3.  **Feature Selection:** The "linguistic features" set is undefined. The justification for excluding common approaches like tf-idf (the standard weighting scheme for BoW) is missing.  
4.  **Multi-View Learning:** The specific multi-view learning framework used is entirely omitted, preventing evaluation of this core component.  
5.  **Statistical Significance:** No tests are provided to validate the reported performance differences, undermining the conclusions.  
6.  **Related Work:** This section is superficial. It lacks comparative details on *how* prior methods achieved results (e.g., specific algorithms used in dementia speech analysis) and fails to position the current work clearly against them.

**Overall Recommendation:**  
The paper addresses an important clinical problem using standard NLP techniques. However, the lack of justification for key methodological choices (disfluency removal, tf-idf omission, embedding source), the superficial related work, and the absence of statistical validation significantly weaken the contribution. The paper must be substantially revised to address these points before acceptance. The current version does not demonstrate sufficient novelty or methodological rigor for ACL. While the core idea is promising, the execution requires major improvements to meet the conference's standards.
