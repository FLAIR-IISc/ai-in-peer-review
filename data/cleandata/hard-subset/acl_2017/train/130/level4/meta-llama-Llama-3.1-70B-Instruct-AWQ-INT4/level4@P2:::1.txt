**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features enriched with word embeddings. The authors model transcripts into complex networks and apply machine learning algorithms to distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI. The paper presents a comprehensive review of relevant work, including linguistic features, acoustic features, and machine learning methods used in previous studies.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a new approach to detecting MCI using complex network features enriched with word embeddings, which outperforms traditional linguistic features in all datasets.
2. They evaluate their model on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery in Portuguese, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. The authors combine classifiers through ensemble and multi-view learning, achieving improvements in accuracy compared to individual classifiers.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to detecting MCI using complex network features enriched with word embeddings, which is encouraging and warrants further exploration.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive review of relevant work, including linguistic features, acoustic features, and machine learning methods used in previous studies.
3. The paper presents a clear and well-structured evaluation of the proposed approach on three datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper starts with a detailed introduction and review of relevant work, which can be compressed to focus strictly on the paper topic.
2. Some references are not directly related to the topic, such as sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images, and can be omitted.
3. The paper lacks technological details, such as examples of short transcripts translated to English and sample networks with embeddings.
4. The source for learning continuous word representations and sub-word level (n-grams) networks is not clearly stated.
5. The paper could benefit from more explanations about the experiments and clarification on the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the source for learning continuous word representations and sub-word level (n-grams) networks?
2. How did you handle out-of-vocabulary words in the datasets?
3. Can you provide examples of short transcripts translated to English and sample networks with embeddings to illustrate the proposed approach?
4. How did you select the parameters for the machine learning algorithms, and what was the impact of parameter tuning on the results?
