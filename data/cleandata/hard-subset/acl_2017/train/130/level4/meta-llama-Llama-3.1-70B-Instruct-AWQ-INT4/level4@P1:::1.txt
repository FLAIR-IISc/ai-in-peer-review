**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim that their method can distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI, achieving competitive results with traditional linguistic metrics. The main contributions of this work are:

1. The application of complex network features to speech transcripts for MCI detection.
2. The use of word embeddings to enrich complex networks, improving their representation of short texts.
3. The evaluation of the proposed method on three datasets, including two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella and Arizona-Battery) and one English dataset (DementiaBank).

**Strengths**

This paper proposes an innovative approach to MCI detection using NLP techniques. The authors' claim that their method can distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI is encouraging, and the reader becomes curious to see more details about the approach. The paper's focus on Portuguese datasets is also a strength, as it contributes to the development of NLP resources for languages with relatively fewer resources.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper's introduction and review of relevant work are overly detailed and include some references that are not directly related to the topic. Compressing the first 5 pages and focusing on the paper's topic would improve the paper's clarity.
2. The paper lacks technological details, such as examples of short transcripts translated to English and sample networks with embeddings. Including these details would help readers understand the approach better.
3. The paper does not clearly explain the source of the continuous word representations used to generate word embeddings. Are these representations learned from the ABCD and Cinderella datasets or external corpora? Clarifying this point is essential to understanding the approach.
4. The paper mentions the use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings but does not explain the source of the training data. Are these networks trained on the same data as the word-level networks, and do they provide better accuracy?
5. The paper contains some minor errors, such as repeated words and formatting issues (e.g., Table 3, row 2, column 3).

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the source of the continuous word representations used to generate word embeddings?
2. How do you ensure that the sub-word level (n-grams) networks provide better accuracy, and what is the source of the training data for these networks?
3. Can you include examples of short transcripts translated to English and sample networks with embeddings to help readers understand the approach better?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words, and what is the source of these words?

**Additional Comments**

The paper needs revision to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The authors should compress the introduction and review of relevant work, add more technological details, and clarify the source of the continuous word representations and sub-word level (n-grams) networks. Additionally, the authors should proofread the paper carefully to eliminate minor errors.
