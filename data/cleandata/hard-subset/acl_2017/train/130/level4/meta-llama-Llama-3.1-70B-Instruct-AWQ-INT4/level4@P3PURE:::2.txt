**Review**

This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words found in transcripts. Features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers, exhibiting different performance across the three datasets. The work targets a well-defined problem and utilizes appropriate datasets.

However, the paper suffers from several drawbacks. Firstly, the manuscript would benefit significantly from a thorough review of grammar and spellings, as the current text is challenging to read due to incorrect English usage.

Secondly, the main machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described. It is unclear what constitutes a single instance of classification. It appears that each transcript was classified as MCI or No MCI, but this should be explicitly stated. If this is the case, the dataset descriptions should provide numbers at the transcript level, rather than the study level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on describing the data, rather than the studies that produced the transcripts. Additionally, the age of the patients is irrelevant to the classification task, and a significant amount of text (approximately two pages) is devoted to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the end classification task. Furthermore, there are inconsistencies in the numbers reported; for example, in Section 4.1.1, the text mentions 326 people, but the total number of males and females in Table 1 is less than 100.

Thirdly, the motivation behind enriching the graph is unclear. A more straightforward approach would be to represent each word as a node in the graph and connect them based on the similarity between their vectors, irrespective of co-occurrence.

Fourthly, despite the datasets being from a biomedical domain, no domain-specific tools have been leveraged.

Fifthly, since the dataset class distribution is unclear, it is uncertain whether accuracy is a suitable measure for evaluation. In any case, given that this is a binary classification task, reporting F1 score would have been desirable.

Lastly, the results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (43 transcripts) without statistical tests to determine the significance of the increments. Therefore, it is unclear whether the gains are statistically significant.
