This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. A graph-based method leveraging co-occurrence information between words found in transcripts is described. Features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical, syntactic properties, and many others. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with a number of classifiers. Different models exhibit different performance across the three datasets. This work targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings. The main contributions of this work are:

1. The application of complex network features to the problem of MCI detection, which provides a lightweight and language-independent representation of speech transcripts.
2. The use of word embeddings to enrich the complex networks, allowing for the capture of semantic information and improving the representation of short texts.
3. The evaluation of the proposed approach on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery datasets.

**Strengths**

1. The paper targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.
2. The proposed approach is novel and provides a new perspective on the problem of MCI detection.
3. The use of complex network features and word embeddings allows for the capture of both syntactic and semantic information in speech transcripts.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper suffers from poor writing and incorrect usage of English, making it difficult to read and understand.
2. The main machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described, and the dataset descriptions are unclear.
3. The motivation behind enriching the graph with word embeddings is not clearly explained.
4. The paper does not leverage domain-specific tools or expertise from the biomedical domain.
5. The evaluation metric used (accuracy) may not be suitable for the problem, and the results are reported without statistical tests or significance analysis.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clearer description of the machine learning problem being addressed and the dataset descriptions at the transcript level?
2. What is the motivation behind enriching the graph with word embeddings, and why not represent each word by a node in the graph and connect them by similarity between their vectors?
3. Can you provide more details on the domain-specific tools or expertise used in the biomedical domain to support the proposed approach?
4. Can you report additional evaluation metrics, such as F1 score, and provide statistical tests or significance analysis to support the results?
