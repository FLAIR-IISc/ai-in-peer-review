- Strengths:
This paper proposes an innovative application of NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors claim to have successfully distinguished between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144). However, the conclusion (lines 781-785) tempers this optimism, stating that "accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% ... means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments." Despite this discrepancy, the paper's message is encouraging, and the reader is left eager to learn more about the methodology.

The corpus submitted in the dataset consists of 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, but its non-English content (Portuguese) may limit accessibility. To enhance the article, the authors could provide more technological details, including at least one example of a short transcript translated into English, and possibly a sample network with embeddings for this transcript.

- Weaknesses:
The paper begins with a detailed introduction and review of relevant work. However, some cited references, such as (Salton 1989) in section 4.2.3, are more general NLP background and could be omitted. Others, like "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654), are not directly related to the topic and could also be removed. The first five pages, particularly section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621), could be condensed to focus more on the paper's topic and technological innovation. Including samples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives would be beneficial.

The relatively short narratives in Portuguese, especially in the ABCD dataset, raise questions about how the authors identified similarities between words to construct word embeddings. The authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but it is unclear what the source is for learning these continuous word representations. Are they based solely on the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora used? Similarly, the use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings (lines 513-525) raises questions about the training source. It is also unclear whether combining the two types of networks improves accuracy and what "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) are, and where they originate from.

- General Discussion:
This paper is interesting because it explores how NLP can aid in discovering cognitive impairments. Another notable aspect is its focus on NLP for Portuguese, a language with relatively fewer resources compared to English. It is essential to explain how embeddings are computed for such languages.

The text requires revision, including shortening sections 1-3, compressing section 4.1, and adding more explanations about the experiments. Clarification on the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
