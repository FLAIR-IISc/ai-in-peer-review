**Review**

This paper explores the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) to speech transcripts to identify patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors claim to have successfully distinguished between healthy control participants and patients with MCI, which is an encouraging finding. However, the conclusion notes that the accuracy of this distinction is not straightforward, ranging from 60% to 85%. Despite this discrepancy, the paper's message is intriguing, and the reader is motivated to learn more about the methodology and results.

The dataset used in this study consists of 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, with transcripts in Portuguese. To improve the paper's accessibility, it would be beneficial to include more technical details, provide at least one example of a short transcript translated into English, and possibly a sample network with embeddings for this transcript.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's introduction and review of relevant work are thorough, but some cited references can be omitted as they are not directly related to the topic or are outdated (e.g., Salton, 1989). The discussion of sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images (lines 652-654) can also be removed, as they are not relevant to the paper's topic. The first five pages of the paper can be condensed, focusing on the topic at hand and providing more details on the technological innovations, including samples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.

The use of relatively short narratives in Portuguese, especially in the ABCD dataset, raises questions about how the similarities between words were found to construct word embeddings. The authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but it is unclear what the source is for learning these continuous word representations. Are they based solely on the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora used? Similarly, the use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings (lines 513-525) raises questions about the source of the training data and the benefits of combining these two types of networks.

**General Discussion**

The study of how NLP can help discover cognitive impairments is an important area of research, and this paper contributes to this effort. The use of NLP for Portuguese is also noteworthy, as it is a language with relatively fewer resources compared to English. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how embeddings are computed for such a language.

**Revision Suggestions**

To improve the paper, the authors should:

* Shorten sections 1-3 and compress section 4.1
* Provide more explanations about the experiments and the sources used for learning continuous word representations
* Clarify the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms
* Include more technical details, such as examples of short transcripts translated into English and sample networks with embeddings
