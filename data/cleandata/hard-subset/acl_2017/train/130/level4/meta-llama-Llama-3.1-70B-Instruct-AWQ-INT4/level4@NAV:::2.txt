- Strengths:
This paper tackles the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through speech transcript analysis from three distinct datasets. It proposes a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words in the transcripts. Various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties, are utilized for feature encoding. The results are presented using 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers, with varying performance observed across the datasets. The work addresses a well-defined problem and employs suitable datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper has several limitations:

1. The manuscript's readability is hindered by grammatical and spelling errors, which would significantly benefit from a thorough review of English usage.

2. The primary machine learning problem being addressed lacks clear description. Specifically, it is unclear what constitutes a single instance of classification. Assuming each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI, the dataset descriptions should provide transcript-level information. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on data description rather than the studies that produced the transcripts. The age of the patients appears irrelevant to the classification task. Excessive text (2 pages) is devoted to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the classification task. Furthermore, discrepancies in the numbers, such as the inconsistency between the number of participants mentioned in section 4.1.1 and the total number of males and females in Table 1, are unclear.

3. The motivation behind enriching the graph is not explicitly stated. An alternative approach could involve representing each word as a node in the graph and connecting them based on the similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence.

4. Despite the biomedical domain of the datasets, no domain-specific tools have been utilized.

5. The unclear class distribution of the datasets makes it challenging to determine the suitability of accuracy as an evaluation metric. Given the binary classification task, F1 score would be a more desirable metric.

6. The results are reported to four decimal places for small datasets (43 transcripts) without statistical tests to assess the significance of the increments. Consequently, it is uncertain whether the observed gains are statistically significant.
