- Strengths:
This paper tackles the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. It proposes a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words in the transcripts. The features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers, showing varying performance across the datasets. This work addresses a well-defined problem and utilizes suitable datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper has several drawbacks:

1. The manuscript is difficult to read due to incorrect English usage. A thorough review of grammar and spellings would significantly improve its clarity.

2. The main machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described. Specifically, it is unclear what constitutes a single instance of classification. It appears that each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI. If so, the dataset descriptions should provide information at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on describing the data rather than the studies that produced the transcripts. The age of the patients seems irrelevant to the classification task, and excessive text (approximately 2 pages) is devoted to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the classification task. Furthermore, there are inconsistencies in the numbers presented (e.g., the total number of males and females in Table 1 is less than 100, despite the text stating that 326 people were involved in Section 4.1.1).

3. The motivation behind enriching the graph is unclear. A simpler approach could be to represent each word as a node in the graph and connect them based on the similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence.

4. Despite the datasets being from a biomedical domain, no domain-specific tools have been utilized.

5. Due to the unclear class distribution of the datasets, it is uncertain whether accuracy is a suitable evaluation metric. Given that this is a binary classification task, reporting the F1 score would be desirable.

6. The results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (43 transcripts) without statistical tests to determine the significance of the increments. Consequently, it is unclear whether the observed gains are statistically significant.
