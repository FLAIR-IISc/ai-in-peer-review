This paper proposes to apply NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) in order to identify patients with MCI (mild cognitive impairment, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors claim that they were able to distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI. The paper's beginning is more optimistic than the conclusion, but the message is encouraging, and the reader becomes curious to see more details about what has been actually done.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to detecting mild cognitive impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features and word embeddings. The authors model transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. They evaluate their model on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery in Portuguese. The results show that complex networks are suitable for detecting MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Complex Network Features for MCI Detection**: The authors propose a novel approach to detecting MCI using complex network features and word embeddings.
2. **Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings**: The authors model transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments.
3. **Evaluation on Multiple Datasets**: The authors evaluate their model on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery in Portuguese.

**Strengths**

1. **Encouraging Results**: The authors report encouraging results, with complex networks outperforming linguistic features in all datasets.
2. **Novel Approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to detecting MCI using complex network features and word embeddings.
3. **Evaluation on Multiple Datasets**: The authors evaluate their model on multiple datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery in Portuguese.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Corpus Size**: The corpus submitted in the dataset is constructed for 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants only (20+20), which is a relatively small sample size.
2. **Language Barrier**: The corpus is in Portuguese, which may limit its accessibility to readers who do not speak the language.
3. **Lack of Technological Details**: The paper could benefit from more technological details, including examples of short transcripts translated to English and sample networks with embeddings.
4. **Redundant References**: Some references, such as those related to sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images, are not directly related to the topic and could be omitted.
5. **Unclear Word Embedding Source**: The paper does not clearly state the source for learning the continuous word representations used to generate word embeddings.

**Questions to Authors**

1. What is the source for learning the continuous word representations used to generate word embeddings?
2. Are the word embeddings generated using only the datasets provided (ABCD+Cinderella) or external corpora?
3. How do the authors ensure that the two kinds of networks (word-level and sub-word level) provide better accuracy?
4. What are the "out-of-vocabulary words" mentioned in line 516, and where do they come from?

**Additional Comments**

The text needs revision, including shortening sections 1-3, compressing 4.1, and adding more explanations about the experiments. Some clarification about the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms could also be provided.
