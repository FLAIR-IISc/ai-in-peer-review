**Review**

**Strengths:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through the analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The proposed method leverages a graph-based approach, utilizing co-occurrence information between words in the transcripts. The features are encoded based on various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers, exhibiting varying performance across the three datasets. The work targets a well-defined problem and utilizes suitable datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper suffers from several drawbacks:

1. The manuscript's readability is hindered by incorrect English usage. A thorough review of grammar and spellings would significantly improve the paper's clarity.

2. The main machine learning problem is poorly described. It is unclear what constitutes a single instance of classification. It appears that each transcript is classified as either MCI or non-MCI. If this is the case, the dataset descriptions should provide information at the transcript level, and Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on the data rather than the studies that produced the transcripts. The age of the patients is irrelevant to the classification task, and the detailed descriptions of the datasets (spanning two pages) could be condensed to exclude non-essential information. Furthermore, there are discrepancies in the numbers reported, such as the total number of males and females in Table 1 being less than 100, despite the text stating that 326 people were involved.

3. The motivation behind enriching the graph is unclear. A simpler approach would be to represent each word as a node in the graph and connect them based on the similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence.

4. The datasets belong to the biomedical domain, but the authors have not leveraged any domain-specific tools.

5. Due to the unclear class distribution of the datasets, it is uncertain whether accuracy is a suitable evaluation metric. For binary classification tasks, the F1 score would be a more desirable metric.

6. The results are reported to four decimal places for small datasets (43 transcripts) without statistical tests to determine the significance of the increments. Therefore, it is unclear whether the gains are statistically significant.
