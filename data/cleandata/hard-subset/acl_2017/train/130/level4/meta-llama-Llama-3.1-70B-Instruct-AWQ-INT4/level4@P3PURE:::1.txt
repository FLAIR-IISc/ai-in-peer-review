**Strengths:**

This paper proposes an innovative application of NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors claim to have successfully distinguished between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144). Although the conclusion (lines 781-785) tempers this optimism, stating that "accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%... means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments," the initial message is encouraging and piques the reader's interest in learning more about the methodology.

The dataset, consisting of 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, is a valuable contribution, despite being limited to Portuguese-speaking individuals. To enhance the article's clarity and accessibility, it would be beneficial to include more technical details, such as an example of a short transcript translated into English, and possibly a sample network with embeddings for this transcript.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper begins with a detailed introduction and review of relevant work, but some cited references, such as (Salton, 1989) in section 4.2.3, are not directly related to NLP and could be omitted. Other references, like those on "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654), are also unrelated to the topic and could be removed. The suggestion is to condense the first five pages, focusing the review strictly on the paper's topic, and providing more detailed explanations of the technological innovations, including samples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.

The relatively short narratives in Portuguese, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raise questions about how similarities between words were identified to construct word embeddings. The authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but the source for learning these continuous word representations is unclear. Are they based solely on the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora used? Similarly, the use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings (lines 513-525) raises questions about the training source. It is also unclear whether combining the two types of networks improves accuracy and what the "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) refer to.

**General Discussion:**

This paper is interesting in its exploration of how NLP can aid in the discovery of cognitive impairments. Another valuable aspect is its focus on NLP for Portuguese, a language with relatively fewer resources compared to English. The authors should provide more explanations about computing embeddings for such languages.
