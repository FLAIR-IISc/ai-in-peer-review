- Strengths:
This paper tackles the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through the analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words in the transcripts. Features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties, among others. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers, yielding varying performance across the three datasets. This work addresses a well-defined problem and utilizes suitable datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper suffers from several drawbacks:

1. The manuscript is challenging to read due to incorrect English usage, grammatical errors, and spelling mistakes. A thorough review of grammar and spellings would greatly improve its clarity.

2. The main machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described. Specifically, the definition of a single instance of classification is unclear. It appears that each transcript was classified as either MCI or non-MCI. If this is the case, the dataset descriptions should provide numbers at the transcript level, rather than the study level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on describing the data, rather than the studies that produced the transcripts. The age of the patients is irrelevant to the classification task and could be omitted. A significant amount of text (approximately two pages) is devoted to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the classification task. Furthermore, there are inconsistencies in the numbers presented; for example, in Section 4.1.1, the text states that 326 people were involved, but the total number of males and females in Table 1 is less than 100.

3. The motivation behind enriching the graph is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node in the graph and connect them based on the similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence?

4. Although the datasets are from the biomedical domain, no domain-specific tools have been utilized.

5. Given the unclear class distribution of the datasets, it is uncertain whether accuracy is a suitable measure for evaluation. In any case, since this is a binary classification task, the F1 score would have been a desirable metric.

6. The results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (43 transcripts) without statistical tests to assess the significance of the improvements. Therefore, it is unclear whether the gains are statistically significant.
