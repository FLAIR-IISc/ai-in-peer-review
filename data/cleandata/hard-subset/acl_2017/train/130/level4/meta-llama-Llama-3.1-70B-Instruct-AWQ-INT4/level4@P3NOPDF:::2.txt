**Review for ACL 2017 Submission**

This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words found in the transcripts. Features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers, exhibiting varying performance across the three datasets. This work targets a well-defined problem and utilizes suitable datasets.

However, the paper suffers from several drawbacks. Firstly, the manuscript is difficult to read due to incorrect usage of English, and a thorough review of grammar and spellings would greatly improve its clarity. 

A major weakness of the paper is the poor description of the main machine learning problem being addressed. Specifically, it is unclear what constitutes a single instance of classification. It appears that each transcript is classified as either MCI or non-MCI, but this should be explicitly stated. Furthermore, the dataset descriptions should provide information at the transcript level, rather than the study level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on describing the data rather than the studies that produced the transcripts. Additionally, the age of the patients is irrelevant to the classification task and could be omitted. A significant amount of text (approximately 2 pages) is devoted to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the end classification task. The reviewer also noted discrepancies in the numbers reported, such as the inconsistency between the total number of participants mentioned in Section 4.1.1 (326 people) and the sum of males and females listed in Table 1 (less than 100).

Another concern is the motivation behind enriching the graph. The authors could provide more insight into why they chose to represent the data in this manner, rather than using a simpler approach such as representing each word as a node in the graph and connecting them based on the similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence.

The datasets used in this study are from the biomedical domain, yet the authors do not leverage any domain-specific tools. This oversight may limit the effectiveness of their approach.

The class distribution of the datasets is unclear, making it challenging to determine whether accuracy is a suitable evaluation metric. Given that this is a binary classification task, reporting the F1 score would have been a more desirable metric. 

Lastly, the results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (43 transcripts), without statistical tests to assess the significance of the increments. As a result, it is unclear whether the observed gains are statistically significant.

**Recommendations for Improvement**

To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

1. Revise the manuscript to improve clarity and address grammatical errors.
2. Provide a clear description of the machine learning problem being addressed, including what constitutes a single instance of classification.
3. Refine the dataset descriptions to focus on relevant information and omit irrelevant details.
4. Justify the choice of graph enrichment and explore alternative approaches.
5. Leverage domain-specific tools to improve the effectiveness of their approach.
6. Report additional evaluation metrics, such as the F1 score, and conduct statistical tests to assess the significance of the results.

By addressing these concerns, the authors can improve the overall quality and impact of their work.
