**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words in transcripts and encodes features using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties.

**Main Contributions**
1. **Application of complex networks to MCI detection**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of complex networks in detecting MCI from speech transcripts, outperforming traditional linguistic features in some cases.
2. **Enriching complex networks with word embeddings**: The authors propose a method to enrich complex networks with word embeddings, which improves the representation of short texts and enhances the detection of MCI.
3. **Multiview learning and ensemble methods**: The authors explore the use of multiview learning and ensemble methods to combine different feature spaces and classifiers, achieving improved results in some cases.

**Strengths**
1. **Well-defined problem and datasets**: The paper targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets to evaluate the proposed approach.
2. **Novel application of complex networks**: The authors demonstrate the potential of complex networks in detecting MCI from speech transcripts, a novel application of this technique.
3. **Improved results with multiview learning and ensemble methods**: The authors show that combining different feature spaces and classifiers can lead to improved results in some cases.

**Weaknesses**
1. **Poorly written manuscript**: The paper suffers from incorrect usage of English, grammar, and spellings, making it difficult to read and understand.
2. **Unclear machine learning problem description**: The paper does not clearly describe the machine learning problem being addressed, including the definition of a single instance of classification.
3. **Insufficient dataset description**: The dataset descriptions do not provide relevant information for the classification task, such as the number of transcripts and class distribution.
4. **Lack of domain-specific tools**: The paper does not leverage domain-specific tools or expertise from the biomedical domain.
5. **Inadequate evaluation metrics**: The paper uses accuracy as the primary evaluation metric, which may not be suitable for this binary classification task, and does not report statistical tests or confidence intervals.
6. **Unclear motivation for enriching the graph**: The paper does not provide a clear motivation for enriching the graph with word embeddings, and alternative approaches are not considered.

**Questions to Authors**
1. Can you provide a clearer description of the machine learning problem being addressed, including the definition of a single instance of classification?
2. How do you justify the use of accuracy as the primary evaluation metric, and can you report additional metrics such as F1 score and confidence intervals?
3. Can you provide more details on the motivation behind enriching the graph with word embeddings, and consider alternative approaches?
4. How do you plan to address the limitations of the current manuscript, including the poorly written text and insufficient dataset description?
