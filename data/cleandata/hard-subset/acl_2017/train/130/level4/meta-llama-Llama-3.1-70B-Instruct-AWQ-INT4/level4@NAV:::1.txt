**Review**

**Strengths:**

This paper proposes an innovative application of NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) for identifying patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors' claim to distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144) is promising, despite the conclusion (lines 781-785) noting that the accuracy of 60% to 85% indicates the difficulty in distinguishing between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments. This discrepancy piques the reader's interest in understanding the methodology.

The corpus, comprising 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, is a valuable contribution, although its limited size and Portuguese language may restrict accessibility. Incorporating more technological details, such as examples of short transcripts translated to English and sample networks with embeddings, would enhance the article's clarity.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's introduction and review of relevant work are thorough, but some cited references, such as (Salton 1989) in section 4.2.3, are not directly relevant to the topic and could be omitted. Other references, like "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654), are also unrelated and could be removed. Condensing the first five pages to focus on the paper's topic and providing more technological details, including English translations of the ABCD and/or Cindarela narratives, would improve the article's coherence.

The short narratives in Portuguese, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raise questions about how word similarities were determined to construct word embeddings. The authors' explanation of generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289) lacks clarity on the source of these representations. Similarly, the use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings (lines 513-525) requires further explanation on the training source and the benefits of combining both network types. The origin of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is also unclear.

**General Discussion:**

This paper's exploration of NLP in discovering cognitive impairments is valuable, and its focus on Portuguese is a welcome contribution to the field. The authors' approach to computing embeddings for a language with relatively fewer resources (compared to English) is also noteworthy.

**Revision Suggestions:**

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Shorten sections 1-3 and compress section 4.1.
2. Provide more explanations about the experiments.
3. Clarify the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms.
