**Strengths:**

This paper explores the application of NLP to speech transcripts, specifically narratives and descriptions, to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI). The authors claim to have successfully distinguished between healthy control participants and patients with MCI. Although the conclusion presents a more nuanced view, stating that accuracy ranged from 60% to 85%, making it challenging to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments, the initial message is encouraging and sparks curiosity about the methodology employed.

The dataset consists of 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, totaling 40 subjects. However, the corpus is limited to Portuguese, making it inaccessible to non-speakers. To enhance the article, it would be beneficial to include more technological details, such as examples of short transcripts translated to English, and possibly a sample network with embeddings for a given transcript.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper begins with a detailed introduction and review of relevant work. Some cited references, such as (Salton 1989) in section 4.2.3, are more general NLP background and could be omitted. Other references, like those on sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images (lines 652-654), are not directly related to the topic and could also be removed. It is suggested that the first five pages be condensed, focusing the review on the paper's topic and providing more details on technological innovations, including English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.

The short narratives in Portuguese, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raise questions about how similarities between words were found to construct word embeddings. The authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289). However, it is unclear what the source is for learning these continuous word representations. Are they based solely on the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora used? Similarly, in lines 513-525, the authors mention using sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings. What is the source for the training, and are we certain that combining the two types of networks yields better accuracy? Additionally, the concept of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is unclear, and their origin is not specified.

**General Discussion:**

This paper is interesting in its exploration of how NLP can aid in discovering cognitive impairments. Another notable aspect is its focus on NLP for Portuguese, a language with relatively fewer resources compared to English. It is essential to explain how embeddings are computed for such languages.
