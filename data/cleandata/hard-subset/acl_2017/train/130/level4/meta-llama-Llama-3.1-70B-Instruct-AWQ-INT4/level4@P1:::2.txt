**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features. The authors model transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts. They evaluate their model on three datasets and report competitive results with traditional methods.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Application of complex network features to MCI detection**: The authors successfully apply complex network features to detect MCI from speech transcripts, demonstrating the potential of this approach in neuropsychological assessments.
2. **Enrichment of complex networks with word embeddings**: The authors propose a method to enrich complex networks with word embeddings, which improves the representation of short texts and leads to better classification results.
3. **Evaluation on multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery datasets, demonstrating the generalizability of their method.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-defined problem**: The paper targets a well-defined problem of identifying patients with MCI from speech transcripts.
2. **Appropriate datasets**: The authors use relevant datasets to evaluate their approach, including datasets from biomedical domains.
3. **Competitive results**: The authors report competitive results with traditional methods, demonstrating the potential of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Poor writing quality**: The paper suffers from incorrect usage of English, making it difficult to read and understand.
2. **Unclear problem description**: The main machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described, and it is unclear what constitutes a single instance of classification.
3. **Irrelevant dataset details**: The paper includes irrelevant details about the datasets, such as the age of patients, which do not affect the end classification task.
4. **Lack of domain-specific tools**: The authors do not leverage domain-specific tools from the biomedical domain, which may have improved their results.
5. **Insufficient evaluation metrics**: The authors only report accuracy as an evaluation metric, which may not be sufficient for a binary classification task. F1 score would have been a desirable additional metric.
6. **Lack of statistical tests**: The authors do not report statistical tests to determine the significance of their results, which makes it unclear if the gains are significant.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify what constitutes a single instance of classification in your problem?
2. How did you determine the optimal threshold for enriching the complex networks with word embeddings?
3. Can you provide more details on why you chose to use complex network features over other approaches?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of class imbalance in your datasets?
5. Can you provide additional evaluation metrics, such as F1 score, to complement your accuracy results?
