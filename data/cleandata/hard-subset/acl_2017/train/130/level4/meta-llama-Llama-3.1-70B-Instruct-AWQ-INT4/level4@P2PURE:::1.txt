- Strengths:
This paper explores the application of NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) for identifying patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors claim to have successfully distinguished between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144). However, in the conclusion (lines 781-785), they note that the accuracy of 60% to 85% indicates that differentiating between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments is challenging. Despite the discrepancy in tone between the introduction and conclusion, the message is encouraging and piques the reader's interest in the methodology.

The dataset consists of 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, but it is limited to Portuguese-speaking individuals. To enhance the article, it would be beneficial to include more technical details, provide at least one example of a short transcript translated into English, and possibly a sample network with embeddings for this transcript.

- Weaknesses:
The paper begins with a detailed introduction and review of relevant work. However, some cited references, such as (Salton 1989) in section 4.2.3, are more general NLP background and could be omitted. Other references, like "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654), are not directly related to the topic and could also be removed. It is suggested that the first five pages be condensed, focusing the review strictly on the paper topic, and providing more details on technological innovations, including samples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cindarela narratives.

The relatively short narratives in Portuguese, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raise questions about how similarities between words were found to construct word embeddings. The authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289). However, the source for learning these continuous word representations is unclear; it is not specified whether the datasets ABCD+Cinderella alone were used or if external corpora were employed. Furthermore, in lines 513-525, it is mentioned that sub-word level (n-grams) networks were used to generate word embeddings. The source for training these networks is also unclear, and it is uncertain whether combining the two types of networks yields better accuracy. Additionally, the origin of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is not specified.

- General Discussion:
This paper is interesting from the perspective of studying how NLP can aid in discovering cognitive impairments. Another notable aspect is its focus on NLP for Portuguese, a language with relatively fewer resources compared to English. It is essential to explain how embeddings are computed for such languages.

The text requires revision, including shortening sections 1-3, compressing section 4.1, and providing more explanations about the experiments. Clarification on the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be beneficial.
