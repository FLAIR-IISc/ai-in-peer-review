### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for automatically detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts. The core of the method involves representing each transcript as a complex network where words are nodes and co-occurrence defines the initial edges. To better handle the short and often disfluent nature of clinical texts, this co-occurrence network is "enriched" by adding new edges between non-adjacent words if the cosine similarity of their word embedding vectors exceeds a certain threshold. A set of topological features (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering coefficient) is extracted from these enriched graphs. These features are then used to train various machine learning classifiers to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese), comparing the performance of the proposed network features against traditional linguistic features and a Bag-of-Words representation. The authors also explore combining feature sets using multi-view learning and classifier outputs using ensembles, showing further performance gains.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Engineering Approach for a Clinical Task:** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to represent clinical speech transcripts for MCI detection. This is a well-motivated approach that aims to be more robust to the agrammatical and disfluent language often found in this domain, which can be challenging for traditional NLP tools that rely on syntactic parsing.

2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposed method against strong baselines (linguistic features and BoW) on three distinct datasets, including data in both English and Portuguese. This cross-dataset and cross-lingual validation strengthens the claim that the network-based approach is a viable and potentially generalizable method for this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The paper tackles a clinically significant problem: the early detection of MCI. The authors correctly identify a key challenge in this area—the difficulty of applying standard NLP tools to noisy, disfluent speech transcripts. Their proposal to use complex network analysis is a compelling alternative, as graph topology is potentially less sensitive to grammatical errors than features derived from syntactic parsing. Enriching the graph with semantic information from word embeddings to handle short texts is also a sensible and innovative step.

2.  **Thorough Comparative Analysis:** The experimental design is a significant strength. The authors do not evaluate their method in isolation. They systematically compare its performance against two widely-used feature sets: sophisticated linguistic metrics (from Coh-Metrix) and a standard Bag-of-Words baseline. This comparison is conducted across multiple classification algorithms (SVM, KNN, Random Forest, etc.) and on three different datasets, providing a robust and convincing assessment of the proposed features' effectiveness.

3.  **Exploration of Advanced Classification Techniques:** The paper goes beyond a simple feature-by-feature comparison by investigating the utility of ensemble and multi-view learning. The results, such as the 10% accuracy improvement on the Cinderella dataset from a multi-view approach, demonstrate that combining the proposed network features with other feature types is a promising direction. This analysis adds depth to the study and provides valuable insights for future work in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in the Experimental Setup and Evaluation:** The paper's description of the machine learning setup is ambiguous, which makes the results difficult to fully interpret.
    *   **Unit of Classification:** It is not explicitly stated what constitutes a single instance for classification. Is it a single transcript? If so, the paper should clarify the total number of transcripts per class (MCI vs. Control) for each dataset. The numbers in Tables 1, 2, and 3 describe the demographics of participants in the studies that *generated* the data, not the final dataset counts used for the classification experiments. For example, Section 4.1.1 mentions 43 transcripts from MCI patients, but Table 1 lists 29 males and 14 females in the MCI group, which adds up to 43 *people*. It's crucial to know the exact number of data points per class.
    *   **Choice of Metric:** Without knowing the class distribution, it is unclear if accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. For clinical diagnosis tasks, which can have imbalanced classes, metrics such as F1-score, precision, and recall are often more informative.

2.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The authors report performance improvements, sometimes very small, and present accuracy values to four decimal places. However, given the small size of the datasets (e.g., N=40 for the Cinderella dataset), it is essential to determine if these improvements are statistically significant or simply due to chance. Without statistical tests (e.g., a paired t-test or bootstrapping), claims about the superiority of one method over another (e.g., CNE achieving 0.6264 vs. LM achieving 0.5806 in Table 4) are unsubstantiated.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for the Network Construction Method:** The core methodological contribution is the enrichment of a co-occurrence network with similarity-based edges. However, the rationale for this specific hybrid approach is not fully justified. Why is it better to start with a co-occurrence graph and add semantic links, rather than building a graph purely from word embedding similarities? An ablation study analyzing the separate contributions of the co-occurrence structure and the semantic enrichment would strengthen the paper's claims. Furthermore, the selection of the similarity threshold is a critical hyperparameter, but its impact on network structure and final performance is not discussed.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, which impede readability and detract from the overall quality of the manuscript. While the technical ideas are generally comprehensible, the presentation needs significant improvement. A thorough proofreading and editing pass, preferably by a native English speaker, would greatly enhance the clarity and impact of this work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact number of data instances (transcripts) used for classification for each class (MCI and Control) in all three datasets? In light of this, could you also report the F1-score, precision, and recall for your main results to provide a more complete picture of performance, especially if the classes are imbalanced?

2.  Have you performed any statistical tests to confirm that the reported performance gains of your proposed complex network features over the baseline methods are statistically significant?

3.  Could you provide more intuition or an empirical analysis (e.g., an ablation study) justifying the decision to enrich a co-occurrence graph rather than using a graph based solely on word vector similarity? How sensitive is your model's performance to the choice of the cosine similarity threshold?
