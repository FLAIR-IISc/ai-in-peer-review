### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by applying Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to speech transcripts of narratives and descriptions in Portuguese. The core idea of using linguistic features, modeled through network representations of word embeddings, to detect cognitive decline is timely and important. While the paper tackles a valuable problem and presents encouraging preliminary results, it suffers from a significant lack of clarity in its methodology and an imbalanced structure that prioritizes background information over crucial experimental details.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Important Application and Novel Task:** The paper addresses a significant real-world problem: the early detection of cognitive decline. Applying NLP to speech transcripts for this purpose is a promising research direction, and the work is a valuable contribution to the field of clinical NLP.

2.  **Encouraging Results:** The paper's motivation is compelling, suggesting the ability to distinguish between healthy controls and patients with MCI. While the conclusion rightly tempers initial optimism by noting the difficulty of the task (with accuracies from 60-85% indicating it is "not easy to distinguish" between the two groups), the overall message is encouraging and effectively piques the reader's interest in the methodology.

3.  **New Corpus for a Lower-Resourced Language:** A key contribution is the construction of a new Portuguese corpus for this task. Research on languages other than English is crucial for the field, and this dataset is a welcome addition.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the lack of clarity regarding the experimental methodology, particularly the generation of word embeddings.
    *   The paper describes using both word-level and sub-word-level networks to generate representations. However, it is unclear what corpus was used to train these representations. Given the small size of the task-specific dataset (20 patients and 20 controls), it seems highly unlikely that it alone would be sufficient to learn meaningful embeddings. The authors must clarify whether external, larger Portuguese corpora were used for pre-training. This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and for evaluating the validity of the results.
    *   The mention of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is confusing without specifying the vocabulary's source.
    *   The paper would be significantly strengthened by an analysis justifying the combination of word-level and sub-word-level networks. An ablation study demonstrating that both components contribute positively to the final performance would be highly beneficial.

2.  **Imbalanced Paper Structure:** The paper dedicates excessive space to general background information at the expense of technical depth.
    *   The introduction and related work sections are overly long and could be significantly condensed. Several citations appear tangential to the core topic (e.g., general NLP background like Salton 1989, or unrelated applications such as "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection" in lines 652-654).
    *   Trimming these initial sections (pages 1-5) would create necessary space for a more detailed and much-needed explanation of the novel technical contributions and experimental setup.

3.  **Limited Accessibility of a Key Contribution:** While the new corpus is a strength, its utility for the broader ACL community is limited as it is only available in Portuguese. To enhance the paper's impact and comprehensibility, the authors should consider including a short transcript sample with an English translation. A visualization of the corresponding network embeddings for such a sample would also greatly improve the reader's understanding of the proposed method.

### **General Discussion**

Overall, this paper addresses a valuable and challenging problem. The application of network-based NLP methods to Portuguese clinical data is a welcome contribution. However, to be considered for publication, the paper requires significant revision. The authors should prioritize clarity and reproducibility by:

1.  Drastically condensing the introductory and background sections to focus on the work most relevant to this specific task.
2.  Greatly expanding the methodology section with precise details on the training corpus for the embeddings, the model architecture, and the justification for their technical choices.
3.  Providing more context for the dataset, such as clarifying the NURC/SP transcription norms and including an English-translated example.

With these changes, this paper has the potential to be an interesting and valuable contribution to the field.
