### Summary

This paper proposes a method for automatically detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core of the approach is to model each transcript as a complex network where words are nodes and co-occurrences are edges. To handle the short and often disfluent nature of clinical transcripts, these networks are enriched with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors extract topological features from these enriched graphs and use them in several machine learning classifiers to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets—one in English and two in Portuguese—and the results indicate that the complex network features, especially when combined with other feature sets in a multi-view learning setup, can outperform traditional linguistic and bag-of-words baselines.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application and evaluation of a complex network model enriched with word embeddings for the clinical task of MCI detection from speech. This approach is well-motivated as a way to circumvent the fragility of traditional syntactic parsers when processing the agrammatical and disfluent language often produced by individuals with cognitive impairment.
2.  **Cross-Lingual Evaluation on Clinical Datasets:** The paper provides a valuable evaluation across three distinct datasets, including two newly presented corpora for Brazilian Portuguese. This demonstrates the potential of the method beyond English and highlights its applicability to languages with fewer robust NLP resources, a significant challenge in computational health.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper tackles an important clinical problem with a promising and relatively novel NLP technique. The motivation to use complex networks as a representation that is robust to the disfluencies and agrammaticality common in MCI speech is a key strength. Enriching these networks with word embeddings to add semantic information, especially for short texts, is a sensible and effective extension.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation. They test their method on three different datasets that vary in language (English, Portuguese) and elicitation task (picture description, story retelling). They compare their proposed features against two strong baselines (linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and bag-of-words) and experiment with multiple classifiers as well as ensemble and multi-view learning techniques. This comprehensive setup provides solid evidence for their claims.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves strong performance, particularly on the Portuguese datasets. The multi-view learning approach, which combines different feature types, yields state-of-the-art results on the Cinderella and ABCD datasets, with the latter reaching 80% accuracy. This demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed features, especially in combination with other information sources.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Crucial Lack of Detail on Word Embedding Training:** The word embeddings are central to the paper's method of enriching the complex networks, yet their training process is critically underspecified. The authors state they use the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016) with default parameters, but they never specify what text corpus was used for training. For the results to be meaningful and reproducible, it is essential to know if the embeddings were trained on (a) only the very small in-domain clinical datasets (which would likely yield poor quality embeddings), (b) a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia), or (c) pre-trained models. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the contribution and novelty of the results.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Interpretation of Results:** The paper presents a large number of results but offers little insight into *why* the method works. Which of the ten topological network features are most discriminative for detecting MCI? What kinds of semantic relationships (added by the embeddings) are most helpful in strengthening the network signal? A more in-depth feature analysis or error analysis would elevate the paper from a report of an engineering success to a more scientific contribution that could inform future work in this area.
3.  **Clarity and Organization of the Paper:** The paper's presentation could be significantly improved. The initial sections on background and related work are overly long and could be condensed to focus more sharply on the paper's core contributions. For instance, general citations to foundational work like Salton (1989) or tangential work on "pedestrian detection in images" could be removed. The space saved could be used to provide the missing details on the methodology (Weakness 1) and a more thorough analysis of the results (Weakness 2).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact training procedure for the word embeddings for both Portuguese and English? Specifically, what corpora were used for training? If only the experimental datasets were used, please justify how meaningful embeddings could be learned from such small data.
2.  The results show that enriching the networks with embeddings (CNE) generally improves performance over standard co-occurrence networks (CN). Could you provide some analysis or examples of the types of semantic links that are added and how they might contribute to distinguishing MCI from control subjects?
3.  For the Cinderella dataset, the manually revised version (Table 6) shows linguistic features (LM) performing better than CNE, while the opposite is true for the automatically processed version (Table 5). This seems to strongly support your motivation. Could you please elaborate on this finding? Does it imply that the primary advantage of the complex network approach is its robustness to noisy, ungrammatical text, rather than it being an inherently superior representation?
