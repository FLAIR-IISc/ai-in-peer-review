### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. These networks, built on word co-occurrence, are enriched with semantic information from word embeddings to better handle the short and often agrammatical texts produced during neuropsychological assessments. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compare features derived from these networks against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. The paper also explores combining these feature sets using ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to improve classification accuracy.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks, enhanced with word embeddings, to the clinical task of identifying MCI from speech. This approach is compelling because it is less dependent on traditional NLP pipelines (e.g., parsers) that often fail on the disfluent, agrammatical language characteristic of patients with cognitive decline.
2.  **A Comparative Analysis Across Multiple Datasets and Languages:** The study provides a valuable empirical evaluation of the proposed method against standard baselines on three distinct datasets, spanning two languages (English and Portuguese) and different elicitation tasks. This cross-dataset, cross-lingual evaluation strengthens the paper's findings.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Clinically Relevant and Challenging Problem:** The automatic detection of MCI is a significant healthcare challenge, and leveraging linguistic markers from speech is a promising, non-invasive diagnostic avenue. The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem and highlighting the limitations of existing NLP tools on this type of data, thereby positioning its proposed method as a well-justified alternative.
2.  **Well-Motivated Methodological Approach:** The core idea of using complex networks to bypass the brittleness of syntactic parsers on disfluent speech is strong. Furthermore, the strategy of enriching these networks with word embeddings to add semantic density and overcome data sparsity in short transcripts is a thoughtful and logical enhancement.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The authors validate their approach on three separate datasets, including the publicly available DementiaBank corpus and two Portuguese corpora. Testing the method on different languages, narrative types (picture description vs. story recall), and text lengths demonstrates a commitment to assessing the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Reproducibility:** Several key details of the methodology are underspecified, making the work difficult to reproduce and fully assess.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The process for generating word embeddings is critically underspecified. It is unclear whether they were trained on the small in-domain corpora (which would be insufficient) or a larger, external corpus. This detail is essential for understanding the source of the semantic information used to enrich the networks.
    *   **Multi-view Learning:** The paper states that multi-view learning was used to combine feature sets but provides no details on the specific algorithm or implementation. It is unclear if this was a simple majority vote over classifiers trained on different feature views or a more sophisticated technique like co-training.
    *   **Linguistic Features:** The set of "linguistic features" is not clearly defined in the main paper. While a list is provided in the supplementary material, the main text should summarize which of the 106 English and 58 Portuguese features were most informative or how they were selected, providing more insight than just a long list.
2.  **Questionable Data Processing and Feature Engineering Choices:** The rationale for certain experimental decisions is not well-justified and may have negatively impacted the results or their interpretation.
    *   **Removal of Disfluencies:** The authors state that filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions were automatically removed. These speech phenomena are often considered important clinical markers for cognitive impairment. Removing them may discard valuable predictive information, and the decision to do so without justification or an ablation study is a significant weakness.
    *   **Bag-of-Words Baseline:** For the BoW baseline, the authors use term frequency (TF). This is a reasonable choice, but the paper does not justify why the more standard and often more effective TF-IDF weighting scheme was not also considered or used.
3.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The experimental results are presented without the necessary statistical backing to fully support the conclusions.
    *   **Lack of Significance Testing:** The paper reports improvements in accuracy but does not include statistical significance tests. Without them, it is difficult to determine whether the observed differences between models (e.g., CNE vs. LM, or multi-view vs. single-view) are statistically meaningful or simply due to random variation in the cross-validation splits.
    *   **Superficial Related Work:** The related work section provides a useful overview but could be strengthened by a more in-depth comparison. For instance, discussing *how* the features and methods used in prior work differ from the complex network approach and *why* the proposed method might be advantageous would provide better context for the paper's contributions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the source and training details for the word embeddings? Specifically, what corpus were they trained on, and what was its size?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for removing disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) from the transcripts? Given their potential clinical relevance, have you considered experiments where these phenomena are retained as features?
3.  Could you elaborate on the specific multi-view learning approach used? Was it a simple majority vote over classifiers trained independently on different feature spaces, or a more integrated method?
4.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate that the performance gains of your proposed CNE model and the multi-view combinations are statistically significant compared to the baselines?
