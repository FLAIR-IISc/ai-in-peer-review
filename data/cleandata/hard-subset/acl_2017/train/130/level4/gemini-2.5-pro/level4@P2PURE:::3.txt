This paper presents a novel application—detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives—using a combination of largely existing representations, feature sets, and methods. The authors provide a thorough description of the problem domain and the datasets used. Although some key details are missing, the proposed methodology and experimental design appear sound. Overall, the study is interesting and informative.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed. Primarily, it requires significant editing to improve clarity and readability. Several key methodological details are either missing or insufficiently explained. For example:
*   The specific multi-view learning approach is not detailed.
*   The set of "linguistic features" is not clearly defined.
*   It is unclear which datasets were used to train the word embeddings; the paper's own datasets seem too small for this purpose.
*   The rationale for removing disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, false starts) is not provided, despite their potential significance as indicators of MCI.
*   The authors do not justify the decision to use term frequency over the more standard tf-idf weighting for the Bag-of-Words (BoW) models.
*   The experimental conclusions are not substantiated with statistical significance tests.
*   The related work section is superficial and lacks depth.

Detailed comments are provided below.

**General Readability and Clarity**
*   **Abstract:** The abstract is overly long and should be condensed.
*   The sentence "However, MCI disfluencies produce agrammatical speech impacting in parsing results" is grammatically incorrect. It should be revised (e.g., "...impacting the parsing results").
*   It is unclear what is meant by manually correcting grammatical errors in transcripts. Since such errors can be indicators of MCI, the motivation for removing them should be explained. This point only becomes clearer later in the paper; consider adding examples of the specific disfluencies being addressed early on.
*   The phrase "as it a lightweight and language independent representation" needs rephrasing for grammatical correctness.
*   The three datasets mentioned are not clearly identified. Consider rephrasing to be more explicit, for example: "...the other two datasets: Cinderella and..."
*   The phrase "15% a year" is ambiguous. Please clarify its meaning in this context.
*   The sentence on line 73 is unclear and should be rephrased.
*   The sentence on lines 641-645 needs to be rewritten for clarity.
*   A period is missing at the end of line 735.

**Related Work**
*   The discussion of related work, particularly in lines 166-182, is too superficial. It would be more impactful to detail the specific methods used in prior studies and explicitly compare them to the approach proposed in this paper.
*   Line 172 appears to be missing necessary citations.
*   Please check the conference citation guidelines. For example, the citation on line 185 should likely be in the format: "Aluisio et al. (2016) used..."
*   The acronym "PLN" (line 188) is used without being defined.

**Datasets and Pre-processing**
*   Please provide a citation or link to the Dementia Bank public corpora (line 157).
*   A citation or link for the "Picnic picture of the Western Aphasia Battery" would be helpful (line 162).
*   An explanation of the WML subtest is needed (line 170).
*   The description of the datasets (lines 323-332) is confusing.
    *   What does "manually segmented" mean in this context (e.g., segmented into sentences)? Why was this not done automatically for all datasets?
    *   The acronym "ABCD" is not defined.
    *   The text itemizes two datasets but then refers to three, which is inconsistent. It would be clearer to refer to the datasets by name rather than as "first," "second," and "third."
*   **Table 1 Caption:** The caption for Table 1 promises "statistics of the dataset," but only demographic information is provided.
*   The description of pre-processing (lines 233-235) could be more accurate. Lemmatization is not always the final step, and other common normalization steps appear to have been omitted.
*   **Lines 375-388:** The rationale for removing disfluencies like filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions is unclear. These are potentially strong features for detecting MCI and their removal requires justification.
*   A citation or link describing the transcription norms used would be beneficial (lines 340-440).
*   The phrase "multidisciplinary team with psychiatrists" (line 399) should be rephrased, e.g., "...consisting of psychiatrists..."

**Methodology and Experiments**
*   The claim that Bag-of-Words models have problems with disfluencies (lines 115-117) is not obvious and requires an explanation.
*   Please clarify what is meant by "the best scenario" (lines 147-149).
*   **Section 4.2.1 & Lines 290-299:** It is not specified whether the word embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets or on larger, external corpora. The MCI datasets described seem too small to generate robust embeddings.
*   **Section 4.2.2:** This section needs to be significantly expanded. The "linguistic features" are a core component of the methodology but are not described in sufficient detail for replication. Please check conference guidelines regarding the use of supplementary material for such details.
*   **Line 560:** Does "The shortest path" refer to the path defined in feature 6? Please clarify.
*   **Line 620:** The paper should justify the use of term frequency over the more common and often more effective tf-idf weighting scheme. Also, a comma is needed after "In this work".
*   **Line 662:** It is unclear what "the threshold parameter" refers to. Is it the threshold for the word embedding cosine distance?
*   **Section 4.3:** The paper completely omits details about the multi-view learning scheme that was used. This is a critical omission that prevents a full understanding and replication of the work.
*   The paper does not report statistical significance tests for the experimental results, making it difficult to validate the conclusions drawn from performance differences between models.
