### Summary

This paper proposes a graph-based method using NLP techniques to classify individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) versus healthy controls, based on speech transcripts of narratives in Portuguese. The authors construct graph representations from word and sub-word level embeddings derived from these transcripts and use network metrics as features for a classification task. The work addresses an important clinical application and demonstrates the potential of computational methods for cognitive assessment.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this paper is its focus on a significant and valuable problem: the automated detection of early-stage cognitive decline from linguistic data. Applying graph-based text representation methods to this clinical task is an interesting and promising direction. The authors report classification accuracies that, while not yet sufficient for clinical deployment, are encouraging and suggest the approach has merit. The paper's introductory claim of being able to distinguish between MCI patients and controls rightly piques the reader's interest. Furthermore, the focus on Portuguese is a welcome contribution to the NLP community, as research on languages other than English is crucial.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising topic, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in clarity and methodological detail, which hinder the reader's ability to assess the validity and reproducibility of the work.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity:** The most critical weakness is the ambiguity surrounding the creation of the word embeddings. The paper is based on a small dataset of transcripts from only 40 participants (20 MCI, 20 controls). It is unclear whether the word embeddings were trained from scratch on this small, domain-specific corpus, or if they were derived from a larger, pre-trained Portuguese language model. If trained only on the provided dataset, the embeddings would likely suffer from severe data sparsity, casting doubt on their quality and the subsequent results. This is a crucial detail that must be clarified.

2.  **Unclear Technical Rationale:** The authors state they use both word-level and sub-word level networks to generate features. However, the motivation for combining these two approaches is not well-explained, and no ablation study is presented to demonstrate that the combination provides a tangible benefit over either method alone. The source for training the sub-word models is also not specified. This is further confused by the mention of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516), as it is impossible to understand their origin without knowing the primary vocabulary source.

3.  **Paper Structure and Focus:** The paper dedicates excessive space to general background information on NLP and literature review. For instance, fundamental concepts requiring citations like (Salton 1989) are unnecessary for an ACL audience. Similarly, references to unrelated tasks such as "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654) are distracting and should be omitted. The introductory sections (1-3) and the background on graph-based representations (4.1, 4.2.3) could be significantly condensed to focus strictly on the paper's core technological innovation.

4.  **Accessibility of Data:** While work on Portuguese is valuable, the complete inaccessibility of the data to non-speakers makes it difficult for a broad audience to appreciate the nuances of the task. The paper would be substantially improved by including a short, translated excerpt of a transcript.

### Suggestions for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend the following revisions:

1.  **Clarify the Embedding Process:** Explicitly state the corpus used to train the word and sub-word embeddings. If an external corpus was used, please name it and describe its size and domain. If only the study's 40-participant corpus was used, the authors must include a thorough discussion on the limitations of this approach and how they mitigated data sparsity issues.

2.  **Refocus the Narrative:** Significantly condense the first five pages. Remove general NLP background and focus the literature review on work directly related to computational linguistics for cognitive and neurological assessment. The saved space should be reallocated to a more detailed explanation of the proposed methodology, the dataset, and the analysis of the results.

3.  **Provide Illustrative Examples:** Include an English translation of a sample narrative from the ABCD or Cinderella dataset. This would provide valuable context for international readers. A visualization of the graph representation generated for this sample would be an excellent addition to make the proposed method more concrete.

4.  **Explain Domain-Specific Details:** Provide a brief explanation of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms for readers who are not familiar with them.
