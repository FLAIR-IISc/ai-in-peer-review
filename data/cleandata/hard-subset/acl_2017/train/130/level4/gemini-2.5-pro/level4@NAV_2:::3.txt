### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and augment it with semantic edges based on word vector similarity to better handle short texts. This representation is then used to extract topological features for classification. The method is evaluated on three distinct datasets (one in English, two in Brazilian Portuguese) and compared against baselines using linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The study also explores combining these feature sets through ensemble and multi-view learning.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive empirical study on MCI detection:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation of complex network features against more traditional NLP features (linguistic and BoW) across three datasets, including data from a less-resourced language (Portuguese). This comparative analysis on varied data is a significant contribution.
2.  **Application of enriched network models to clinical data:** The work successfully demonstrates that enriching word adjacency networks with semantic information from word embeddings is a viable strategy for modeling the short and often disfluent speech transcripts characteristic of neuropsychological assessments.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses an important clinical problem:** The paper tackles the challenging and clinically relevant task of early MCI detection through automated, non-invasive speech analysis. This represents a valuable application of NLP with the potential for significant societal impact.
2.  **Thorough description of datasets and domain:** The authors provide a detailed background on MCI and a clear description of the three datasets used for evaluation, including participant demographics and data elicitation methods. This level of detail is commendable and aids in understanding the experimental context.
3.  **Systematic comparison of feature sets and methods:** The study methodically compares the proposed complex network approach with standard linguistic and BoW features. Furthermore, the exploration of multi-view and ensemble learning to combine these diverse feature types is a logical extension that demonstrates performance improvements, strengthening the paper's findings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of methodological clarity and key details:** Several crucial details of the methodology are either omitted or insufficiently explained. For example, the specific corpus used to train the word embeddings is not mentioned; if trained only on the small in-domain datasets, the embeddings would likely be of poor quality. The implementation of the multi-view learning is also unclear, with only a brief mention of majority voting. A complete list and description of the "linguistic features" are relegated to supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the baseline in the main paper.
2.  **Questionable rationale for data preprocessing:** The justification for some data preprocessing steps is not well-established. Most notably, the decision to remove speech disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is counterintuitive, as these phenomena are often considered important clinical markers for cognitive decline. Similarly, the choice of term frequency (TF) for the BoW baseline over the more standard TF-IDF weighting scheme is not explained.
3.  **Absence of statistical significance testing:** The paper reports performance improvements for various methods, but no statistical tests are provided to validate these results. Without significance testing, it is impossible to determine if the observed differences in accuracy are meaningful or simply due to chance, which undermines the strength of the paper's conclusions.
4.  **Superficial discussion of related work:** While the paper lists several relevant prior studies, the related work section lacks a deep comparative analysis. The discussion would be much stronger if it detailed *how* the methods in prior work differ and more directly contextualized the current paper's approach and results against previous findings.

### Questions to Authors

1.  What corpus was used to train the word embeddings? Given that the experimental datasets are small, using them for training seems insufficient to generate robust embeddings. Please clarify the source and size of the training data.
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for removing speech disfluencies from the transcripts? These are often considered highly informative features for detecting cognitive impairment. Did you conduct any experiments that included them?
3.  Could you provide more specific details on the multi-view learning implementation? For instance, how were the outputs from classifiers trained on different feature spaces combined to produce a final prediction?
4.  Why was term frequency (TF) chosen for the Bag-of-Words representation instead of the more common TF-IDF weighting scheme?
5.  Have you performed statistical significance tests to confirm that the reported performance gains of your proposed methods over the baselines are statistically significant?
