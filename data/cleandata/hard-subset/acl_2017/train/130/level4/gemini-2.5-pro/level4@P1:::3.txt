### **Summary**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling the texts as complex networks. The primary contribution is the application of this graph-based representation, enriched with word embeddings to handle short texts, to the clinical task of MCI detection. The authors extract topological features from these networks and use them in several machine learning classifiers, evaluating their approach on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese). The results suggest that these network features are competitive with, and in some cases outperform, traditional linguistic and Bag-of-Words (BoW) features. The study is interesting, well-motivated, and explores a promising direction for automated clinical assessment.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Application of an Existing Technique:** The application of complex networks to MCI detection from speech is a novel and compelling idea. Traditional NLP pipelines relying on parsers can be brittle when processing the agrammatical and disfluent speech common in clinical populations. A graph-based representation that is less dependent on strict grammatical structure presents a promising and robust alternative, and the paper makes a good case for its utility.

2.  **Thorough Problem Motivation and Dataset Description:** The paper provides a clear and detailed motivation for the importance of early MCI detection. Furthermore, the descriptions of the three distinct datasets used for evaluation are thorough, giving the reader a solid understanding of the data's source, demographics, and elicitation tasks. This context is valuable for interpreting the results and understanding the challenges of the domain.

3.  **Multi-lingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted on three separate datasets, covering two different languages (English and Portuguese) and various narrative tasks (picture description, story retelling). This strengthens the paper's claims about the robustness and language-independent nature of the proposed features.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Critical Omissions in Methodology:** The paper omits several crucial details about the experimental setup, which hinders reproducibility and a full assessment of the work.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The source corpus for training the word embeddings is not specified. The three clinical datasets described in the paper appear far too small to train meaningful embeddings. If a larger, external corpus was used, it must be identified and described. This is a critical detail for understanding the source of the semantic information being used.
    *   **Multi-view Learning:** The multi-view learning approach is mentioned as a key technique for achieving the best results, yet it is not described. The reader is left to guess how the classifiers trained on different feature spaces were combined beyond a simple "majority vote."
    *   **Linguistic Features:** The specific set of linguistic features used is not clearly defined in the main paper, making it difficult to perform a direct comparison with the proposed network features.

2.  **Questionable Preprocessing of Clinical Data:** The decision to remove all disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) from the transcripts is not well-justified and seems counter-intuitive. Such speech patterns are often considered important clinical markers of cognitive impairment. By removing them, the models may be losing access to a powerful source of predictive information. The paper should provide a strong rationale for this choice or, preferably, investigate the impact of including these phenomena.

3.  **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The results are presented without any tests for statistical significance. The paper makes several claims of improvement (e.g., "a 10% of improvement," "a 4.71% of improvement"), but without statistical validation, it is unclear whether these performance differences are meaningful or simply due to random variation in the data splits. This lack of rigor weakens the conclusions drawn from the experiments.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper would benefit significantly from editing for clarity and fluency. The abstract is overly long, and several sentences throughout the text are difficult to parse. Additionally, the related work section is somewhat superficial, listing prior studies without sufficiently detailing their methods or clearly articulating how the current work differentiates itself and builds upon them.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify what corpus was used to train the word embeddings? If it was an external corpus, please provide a citation.

2.  Could you provide more detail on your multi-view learning implementation? Specifically, how were the predictions from classifiers trained on different feature spaces combined to produce a single result?

3.  What was the reasoning behind removing disfluencies from the transcripts? Given that these are often clinically relevant markers, have you considered their potential as predictive features?

4.  In your Bag-of-Words experiments, you used term frequency (TF). Could you explain why this was chosen over the more standard TF-IDF weighting scheme?

5.  Do you plan to conduct statistical significance tests to validate your results and support the claims of improvement? This would greatly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
