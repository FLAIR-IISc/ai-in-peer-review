### **Summary**

This paper addresses the important problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose a graph-based method that represents transcripts based on word co-occurrence and extracts lexical and syntactic features from the graph. While the problem is well-defined and the use of multiple datasets is commendable, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, experimental design, and reporting that undermine its contributions. The core ideas may have merit, but the current manuscript is not ready for publication.

### **Strengths**

*   **Problem:** The paper targets a well-defined and clinically relevant problem: the automatic detection of MCI, which is a critical area of research in computational health.
*   **Methodology:** The proposed approach of using a graph-based representation to capture relationships between words in a transcript is an interesting direction.
*   **Data:** The authors use three different datasets to evaluate their models, which is a good practice for testing the generalizability of the proposed method.

### **Weaknesses**

The paper has several major weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**
The manuscript is very difficult to read due to numerous grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and unconventional sentence structures. The clarity of the paper is severely compromised, making it challenging to understand the authors' methodology and claims. The paper would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading and revision by a native English speaker.

**2. Problem Formulation and Data Description:**
The description of the core machine learning task is unclear. The paper needs to explicitly define what constitutes a single instance for classification. It appears that each transcript is treated as a single data point to be classified as "MCI" or "Control," but this is not stated clearly.

Furthermore, the description of the datasets is confusing and contains irrelevant information while omitting crucial details.
*   **Irrelevant Details:** Nearly two pages are dedicated to describing the studies from which the data were collected, including details like patient age, which are not used as features in the classification task. This space could be used more effectively to describe the proposed method and results.
*   **Missing Details:** The descriptions in Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on the statistics of the data used for the task (e.g., number of transcripts, average length, class distribution), not the demographics of the original study participants.
*   **Inconsistencies:** There are apparent contradictions in the numbers provided. For instance, Section 4.1.1 states that 326 people were involved in a study, yet the corresponding Table 1 lists a combined total of fewer than 100 males and females. Such discrepancies must be resolved.

**3. Methodological Justification:**
The motivation behind the specific graph construction and feature enrichment is not well-justified. The authors should explain why a co-occurrence-based graph is the optimal choice. For example, why was this approach chosen over an alternative where each word is a node, and edges are weighted by the semantic similarity of their word embeddings, irrespective of co-occurrence? Without such a discussion or comparison, the choice of methodology feels arbitrary.

**4. Experimental Setup and Evaluation:**
The evaluation of the proposed method is insufficient and lacks rigor.
*   **Missing Metrics:** The class distribution (i.e., the number of MCI vs. Control samples) for each dataset is not reported. In clinical applications, class imbalance is common, which can make accuracy a misleading metric. The authors should report Precision, Recall, and F1-score to provide a more complete picture of model performance.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** Results are reported to four decimal places, which suggests a high degree of precision. However, these results are obtained on very small datasets (e.g., one dataset has only 43 transcripts). Without performing statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests or bootstrap tests), it is impossible to determine whether the reported performance improvements of one model over another are statistically meaningful or merely due to chance. This is a critical omission.
*   **Leveraging Domain Knowledge:** The data originates from the biomedical domain, yet the approach does not leverage any domain-specific resources or tools. The work could be strengthened by incorporating features from medical ontologies (e.g., UMLS) or using clinical NLP tools to extract more domain-relevant information from the transcripts.
