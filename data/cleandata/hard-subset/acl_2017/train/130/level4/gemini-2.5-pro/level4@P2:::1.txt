### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing the texts as complex networks. The core idea is to create word co-occurrence networks and enrich them with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. This approach is designed to be more robust to the agrammatical and disfluent nature of patient speech, which poses challenges for traditional NLP methods that rely on syntactic parsing. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Brazilian Portuguese) and compared against linguistic features and Bag-of-Words models.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks:** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks, enhanced with word embedding-based semantic links, to the clinical task of MCI detection. This is a well-motivated approach to capture textual properties from short, noisy transcripts without relying on fragile syntactic parsers.

2.  **Cross-Lingual Evaluation:** The authors validate their method on both English and Brazilian Portuguese datasets. This demonstrates the language-independent potential of the network-based representation and provides valuable results for Portuguese, a language with fewer resources for this specific clinical application.

3.  **Exploration of Feature Combination:** The paper effectively shows that combining the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and Bag-of-Words features through multi-view learning can yield significant performance improvements, achieving up to 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Methodology:** The paper's core premise is strong. It correctly identifies a significant limitation of prior work: the reliance on linguistic features derived from parsers that perform poorly on the disfluent, often agrammatical speech of patients. The proposed complex network approach cleverly bypasses this dependency on syntactic correctness. The further step of enriching these networks with word embeddings to add semantic density to sparse, short texts is a logical and thoughtful enhancement.

2.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is conducted across three distinct datasets with different languages (English, Portuguese) and elicitation tasks (picture description, story recall). The authors compare their proposed features against two strong and relevant baselines: a comprehensive set of linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and a standard Bag-of-Words model. This rigorous comparison provides convincing evidence for the utility of their method.

3.  **Promising Results:** The results support the paper's claims. The enriched complex network (CNE) features consistently outperform traditional linguistic features in most individual classifier settings (Tables 4, 5, 7). Furthermore, the substantial accuracy gains achieved through multi-view learning (e.g., a 10% absolute improvement on the Cinderella dataset and 4.71% on the ABCD dataset over the best individual classifier) highlight the complementary nature and diagnostic value of the proposed network features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Word Embedding Methodology:** The paper's most significant weakness is the insufficient detail regarding the generation of word embeddings. The authors state they use the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016), but critically omit what corpus was used for training. For the results to be meaningful and reproducible, it is essential to know if the embeddings were trained on: (a) only the small, in-domain clinical datasets, which would likely result in poor-quality representations; (b) a large, external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia); or (c) a combination of both. This missing detail makes it difficult to assess the contribution of the embeddings and the overall methodology.

2.  **Presentation and Focus:** The paper dedicates considerable space to explaining well-known concepts (e.g., Bag-of-Words, definitions of various classifiers) and general background, while the novel aspects of the work are described relatively briefly. The paper would be stronger if it condensed Sections 1-3 and parts of Section 4 to create space for a more in-depth explanation of the network enrichment process and a deeper analysis of the results. For instance, an analysis of which network metrics are most discriminative for MCI detection would add significant value.

3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The datasets used, particularly for the Portuguese experiments, are quite small (e.g., 20 control vs. 20 MCI participants). While the reported accuracy improvements are promising, the lack of statistical significance testing makes it difficult to determine if these gains are robust or potentially due to chance. Given the small sample sizes, reporting confidence intervals or results from significance tests (e.g., a paired t-test) would be necessary to substantiate the claims of superiority of one method over another.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the word embeddings used for both English and Portuguese? Specifically, what text corpus or corpora were they trained on? Was this an external, large-scale corpus, or was it limited to the data from the described experiments?

2.  Regarding the "out-of-vocabulary words" mentioned on line 516, could you elaborate on their source? If you used pre-trained embeddings from a large external corpus, do these words represent domain-specific terms from the transcripts that were not present in the pre-training data?

3.  Given the small sample sizes of the datasets, have you performed any statistical significance tests to confirm that the performance improvements of the CNE-based models over the linguistic feature baselines are statistically significant?
