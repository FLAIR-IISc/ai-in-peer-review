**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from spoken language transcripts. The central premise is that traditional Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques, which rely on parsers and linguistic feature extractors, are brittle and perform poorly on the agrammatical and disfluent speech characteristic of individuals with MCI. To overcome this, the authors propose a method based on complex networks. They model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and enrich this graph with additional edges based on the semantic similarity of words, derived from word embeddings. This enrichment is intended to improve the representation of short texts. Topological features are then extracted from these graphs and used to train various machine learning classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three different datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against baselines using linguistic features and Bag-of-Words. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The novel application of complex network analysis, specifically word co-occurrence graphs enriched with word embedding-based semantic links, to the clinical problem of MCI detection from speech. This method offers a potentially more robust, language-independent alternative to standard NLP pipelines that struggle with disfluent text.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation of the proposed network-based features against traditional linguistic and Bag-of-Words features across three distinct datasets. The results demonstrate that the network-based features are competitive and, in some cases, superior to the baselines, particularly when combined in a multi-view learning framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated approach to a significant problem:** The automatic detection of MCI is a clinically important area of research. The paper's motivation is strong, as it correctly identifies a critical challenge in the field: the difficulty of applying standard NLP tools to the non-standard language often produced by patients. Proposing a method that is inherently more robust to grammatical errors and disfluencies is a valuable direction of inquiry.
2.  **Novelty in feature representation:** While complex networks have been used in other NLP tasks, their application to MCI detection from speech transcripts, particularly the strategy of enriching co-occurrence graphs with semantic information from word embeddings to handle short texts, is a novel contribution. This approach provides a lightweight and principled way to capture structural and semantic properties of language without relying on fragile parsers.
3.  **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets:** The authors validate their method on three different datasets that vary in language (English and Portuguese), task (picture description vs. narrative recall), and average text length. This multifaceted evaluation strengthens the paper's conclusions, suggesting that the proposed method has a degree of generalizability and is not tailored to a single, specific type of data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of clarity in the experimental setup and data reporting:** The description of the machine learning task is ambiguous, which hinders the reproducibility and interpretation of the results.
    *   It is not explicitly stated what constitutes a single classification instance. Is it one transcript, or are all transcripts from a single patient aggregated? This is especially crucial for the ABCD dataset, where each subject provides two narratives.
    *   Tables 1, 2, and 3 present demographic information about the participants in the original clinical studies but fail to provide the essential statistics for the classification task itself, such as the total number of instances (transcripts) per class for each dataset. This makes it impossible to assess potential class imbalance.
    *   There are confusing discrepancies in the numbers provided. For instance, Section 4.1.1 mentions 326 people in the broader study, but the totals in Table 1 are much smaller, which could be clarified by better distinguishing the source study from the experimental dataset.
2.  **Insufficient methodological justification and analysis:** The paper's core proposal is to *enrich* a co-occurrence network with semantic links. However, the rationale for this specific design choice over simpler alternatives is not provided. A key ablation study is missing: comparing the proposed enriched network to a graph constructed purely from word embedding similarity (i.e., connecting words if their cosine similarity is above a threshold, irrespective of co-occurrence). Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the co-occurrence backbone is a necessary or beneficial component of the model.
3.  **Inadequate evaluation metrics and lack of statistical validation:** The evaluation relies solely on classification accuracy, which is a poor metric for clinical tasks where class imbalance is common and the cost of misclassification can be high. Reporting metrics such as precision, recall, and F1-score for the MCI class would provide a much more complete and meaningful assessment of performance. Furthermore, the performance gains reported are modest (e.g., 2.3% and 4.71%) on very small datasets. Without statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar's test or bootstrapping), it is impossible to conclude whether these improvements are statistically significant or merely due to chance. Reporting results to four decimal places gives a false sense of precision.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Please clarify the exact unit of classification for your experiments. Is each transcript an independent instance? For the ABCD dataset, where patients provide two narratives, how are these handled in the cross-validation splits to avoid having data from the same patient in both the training and testing sets?
2.  Could you provide the precise number of instances for the Control and MCI classes for each of the three datasets used in your classification experiments? This information is critical for interpreting the accuracy scores.
3.  What is the rationale for your specific graph construction method (enriching a co-occurrence graph) compared to an alternative approach where the graph is built entirely from semantic similarity between word vectors?
4.  Given the small dataset sizes, have you performed any statistical tests to confirm that the reported improvements from your proposed model (e.g., CNE over LM, or multi-view over individual classifiers) are statistically significant?
