- Task: identify MCI from spoken transcripts using word co-occurrence graphs enriched with word-embedding semantic links; extract topological features for classification.
- Contribution 1: novel use of complex networks (co-occurrence + embedding-based edges) as a parser-robust, language-independent feature for MCI detection.
- Contribution 2: extensive empirical evaluation across three datasets (English, Portuguese) showing network features competitive with or better than linguistic and BoW baselines, especially in multi-view setups.
- Strength 1: well-motivated clinical problem and clear rationale for needing parser-robust methods for disfluent speech.
- Strength 2: novel feature representation—enriching co-occurrence graphs with semantic links to better represent short texts without fragile NLP pipelines.
- Strength 3: evaluation on multiple datasets varying in language, task, and text length supports generalizability.
- Weakness 1: unclear experimental setup—ambiguous unit of classification (transcript vs. patient) and handling of multiple transcripts per subject.
- Weakness 2: inadequate data reporting—missing per-class instance counts and potential class imbalance; demographic tables are confusing relative to experimental datasets.
- Weakness 3: missing methodological justification—no ablation comparing enriched co-occurrence graphs to graphs built solely from embedding similarity.
- Weakness 4: insufficient evaluation metrics and validation—only accuracy reported, no precision/recall/F1 for MCI, no statistical significance testing; reported gains are small and datasets are small.
- Question 1: clarify the classification unit and how multiple narratives per subject (ABCD) are partitioned in cross-validation to prevent leakage.
- Question 2: provide exact numbers of instances per class (Control vs. MCI) for each dataset used in experiments.
- Question 3: justify the choice to enrich a co-occurrence backbone versus building graphs purely from semantic similarity.
- Question 4: report whether statistical tests were performed (e.g., McNemar, bootstrapping) to assess significance of the reported improvements.
