### **Summary**

This paper addresses the task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose a method based on modeling transcripts as complex networks, where words are nodes and co-occurrence defines the initial edges. To better handle the short and often sparse nature of these texts, they enrich these networks by adding new edges between words with high cosine similarity, based on pre-trained word embeddings. Features derived from the topological properties of these enriched networks are then used to train various machine learning classifiers (SVM, k-NN, etc.). The approach is evaluated on three datasets—one in English (Cookie Theft) and two in Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD)—and compared against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and bag-of-words representations. The results suggest that the complex network features, particularly when enriched, are competitive and often outperform the baseline feature sets. The authors also show that combining feature sets using multi-view learning can further improve classification accuracy.

### **Contributions**

1.  **Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks, specifically enriched with semantic information from word embeddings, to the clinical task of MCI detection from speech. This approach is well-motivated as a lightweight, language-independent representation that is potentially more robust to the agrammatical and disfluent speech common in patient transcripts, which often poses a challenge for traditional NLP tools like parsers.
2.  **Comparative Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets and Languages:** A significant contribution is the empirical evaluation of the proposed method across three different datasets, spanning two languages (English and Portuguese) and different elicitation tasks. This provides a more comprehensive picture of the method's effectiveness compared to evaluating on a single corpus. The comparison against established feature sets like linguistic metrics and bag-of-words provides a valuable benchmark.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Approach:** The core idea of using complex network topology to capture linguistic patterns for MCI detection is innovative. The authors provide a strong rationale for this choice: traditional linguistic feature extractors (e.g., parsers) are often brittle and perform poorly on the disfluent, fragmented, and sometimes agrammatical speech of individuals with cognitive impairment. The proposed graph-based method largely bypasses the need for deep linguistic processing, which is a significant advantage.
2.  **Targeting a Clinically Significant Problem:** The paper tackles an important and challenging problem. The early detection of MCI is crucial for patient care and potential intervention. Developing automated, scalable, and non-invasive methods based on language analysis is a highly valuable research direction with clear societal impact.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors test their method thoroughly by (a) using three distinct datasets, which demonstrates generalizability across languages and tasks; (b) comparing against multiple strong baselines (linguistic features and bag-of-words); and (c) employing several different classification algorithms as well as more advanced ensemble and multi-view learning techniques. This rigorous setup strengthens the credibility of their findings.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Setup:** The description of the machine learning task is insufficiently detailed, hindering reproducibility and interpretation.
    *   It is not explicitly stated what constitutes a single classification instance. The text implies that each transcript is a single data point, but this should be confirmed.
    *   The dataset statistics in Tables 1, 2, and 3 appear to describe the demographics of the participants in the original data collection studies, not the final datasets used for classification. The number of instances per class (Control vs. MCI) is never stated for any of the three datasets. This is a critical omission. For example, in Section 4.1.1, the text mentions 326 people diagnosed with probable AD, but Table 1 lists demographic information for fewer than 100 subjects in total. This discrepancy is confusing.
    *   Without knowing the class distribution, it is impossible to determine if accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. Class imbalance is common in clinical datasets, and in such cases, accuracy can be highly misleading.
2.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The datasets are relatively small (e.g., the Cinderella dataset has only 40 participants). Results are reported with high precision (four decimal places), and small improvements are highlighted as significant (e.g., a "2.3% of improvement"). However, no statistical significance tests are performed. On small datasets, such minor differences in accuracy could easily be due to random chance or the specific cross-validation split. Without statistical validation, it is difficult to conclude that the proposed method is genuinely superior to the baselines.
3.  **Insufficient Justification for Network Construction Method:** While the motivation for enriching the network to handle short texts is clear, the rationale for the specific enrichment strategy—adding similarity-based edges to an existing co-occurrence network—is not well-defended against alternatives. For instance, why not construct the network entirely based on word similarity, ignoring co-occurrence? Or why is this hybrid approach superior? A more detailed ablation study or theoretical justification would strengthen the paper's methodological contribution.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing that impede readability. While the core ideas are understandable, the quality of the writing detracts from the paper's impact and makes it difficult to follow the technical details in several sections. The manuscript would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading by a native English speaker.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact number of instances (transcripts) per class (Control vs. MCI) for each of the three datasets? Given the potential for class imbalance in these clinical datasets, could you also report metrics more robust to imbalance, such as precision, recall, and F1-score?
2.  Given the small dataset sizes and the small margins of improvement reported, have you performed any statistical significance tests (e.g., t-test, bootstrap sampling) to confirm that the performance gains from your proposed method (CNE) and the multi-view models are statistically significant?
3.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for your specific network enrichment strategy (i.e., augmenting a co-occurrence graph with similarity edges)? Have you experimented with alternative graph construction methods, such as building a network purely from word vector similarity, and how do they compare?
