### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing the text as complex networks. The core idea is to model word co-occurrence as a network and then enrich this structure with semantic information derived from word embeddings. This approach is designed to be more robust to the disfluencies and agrammatical sentences common in patient speech, which often challenge traditional NLP methods reliant on syntactic parsing. The authors evaluate their method on three datasets—one in English and two in Brazilian Portuguese—and compare its performance against linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations, also exploring ensemble and multi-view learning techniques.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The application of word embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI detection from speech.** This serves as a lightweight and potentially more robust alternative to traditional parsing-dependent linguistic features. The method's strength lies in its ability to model textual properties without relying on grammatically well-formed input, which is a significant advantage when analyzing patient transcripts.
2.  **An empirical evaluation of the proposed method on multiple datasets, including for a less-resourced language (Brazilian Portuguese).** The study provides a comparative analysis showing that the network-based features are competitive and, in some cases, outperform standard linguistic features, particularly when manual transcript correction is not performed.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Approach to an Important Clinical Problem.** The paper addresses the significant challenge of early MCI detection by applying a novel NLP methodology. Using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to model disfluent speech is a creative approach that circumvents the brittleness of traditional syntactic parsers. This direction is promising for developing automated systems that can operate on raw, uncorrected speech transcripts.
2.  **Focus on a Less-Resourced Language.** A notable strength of this work is its evaluation on two Brazilian Portuguese datasets. Much of the research in this area focuses on English, and by providing data and analysis for Portuguese, the authors make a valuable contribution to the field and demonstrate the language-agnostic potential of their method.
3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Agrammatical Text.** The central hypothesis that complex network features are more resilient to the disfluencies in patient speech than standard linguistic metrics is well-supported by the results. The network-based method's superior performance over linguistic features on the automatically processed Cinderella dataset (Table 5 vs. Table 6) provides compelling evidence for this claim, suggesting its practical utility in real-world clinical applications where manual data cleaning is not scalable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Crucial Methodological Details Regarding Word Embeddings are Missing.** The paper's primary technical contribution is the enrichment of networks with word embeddings, yet the process for generating them is critically under-specified. For the two small Portuguese datasets, it is unclear whether the embeddings were trained solely on the provided transcripts (which would likely yield poor-quality vectors) or on a larger, external corpus. This ambiguity undermines the reproducibility and credibility of the results. The source of "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) is also unexplained. Without these details, it is impossible to properly assess the validity of the proposed method.
2.  **Overstated Claims and Inconsistent Framing of Results.** The paper's claims of success seem exaggerated when compared to the reported performance. The absolute accuracy scores, while showing improvements, are modest. Furthermore, there is a contradiction between the optimistic tone at the beginning of the paper (e.g., lines 141-144, claiming the ability to distinguish between groups) and the more realistic concession in the conclusion that this is a difficult classification task (lines 781-785). In the ABCD dataset results (Table 7), the simple BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves a strong accuracy of 75.47%, not far behind the best multi-view result of 80.18%, which questions the significance of the complex network features' contribution in that instance.
3.  **Poor Paper Organization and Presentation.** The paper would benefit significantly from restructuring. The introduction and related work sections are overly long and contain citations to topics not directly relevant to the work (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection). In contrast, the methodology section lacks the necessary depth, especially concerning the word embedding process. This imbalance detracts from the paper's clarity and focus. The inclusion of an English-translated transcript example from the Portuguese datasets would have greatly improved accessibility. The reference list also contains errors (e.g., the DOI for Amancio, 2015b is duplicated for Amancio, 2015a), suggesting a need for more careful proofreading.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the word embeddings used for the Portuguese datasets (Cinderella and ABCD): Were these trained exclusively on the transcripts within these datasets, or was a pre-trained model or a larger external Portuguese corpus used? If an external corpus was used, please specify it. Clarifying this is essential for understanding the methodology and reproducibility.
2.  In the results for the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW feature set with a linear SVM performs very strongly (75.47% accuracy) on its own. Could you elaborate on what specific linguistic phenomena or patterns related to MCI your Complex Network Enriched (CNE) features are capturing that the simpler BoW model misses, which justifies their inclusion in the best-performing multi-view model?
3.  Could you provide a brief explanation of the "NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms"? A concise description of these standards would help readers better understand the nature of the source data.
