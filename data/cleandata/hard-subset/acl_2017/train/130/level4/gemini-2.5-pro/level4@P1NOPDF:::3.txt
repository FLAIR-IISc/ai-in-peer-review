This paper presents a novel application of existing methods and feature sets to the important task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives. The authors provide a thorough description of the problem domain and the datasets used. The overall study is interesting, informative, and the proposed solution appears reasonable.

However, the paper has several significant drawbacks that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication. First, the manuscript requires considerable editing to improve clarity and readability. Second, several key methodological details are missing or unclear. For instance, the multi-view learning approach is not described, the set of "linguistic features" is not well-defined, and the source data for training the word embeddings is not specified (the described datasets seem too small for this purpose). Third, some experimental design choices are not well-justified. The removal of disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, false starts) is questionable, as these are likely important indicators of MCI. Similarly, the reason for using term frequency instead of the more standard tf-idf weighting for the Bag-of-Words models is not explained. Finally, the experimental conclusions are not substantiated with statistical significance tests, and the related work section is somewhat superficial.
