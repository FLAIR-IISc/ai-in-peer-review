- Paper tackles the clinically relevant task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts but is not ready for publication due to major issues.
- Strength: Targets an important computational health problem with clear clinical relevance.
- Strength: Proposes an interesting graph-based representation to model word relationships in transcripts.
- Strength: Evaluates models across three datasets, which is good for testing generalizability.
- Clarity/presentation: Manuscript is hard to read with numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing; needs thorough proofreading.
- Problem formulation: Unclear definition of a classification instance (e.g., whether each transcript is a single labeled example).
- Data description: Overly detailed, irrelevant demographic descriptions; lacks essential dataset statistics (number of transcripts, average length, class distribution).
- Data inconsistencies: Contradictory numbers between text and tables (e.g., participant totals vs. table counts) that must be resolved.
- Method justification: Choice of co-occurrence-based graph construction and feature enrichment is not motivated or compared to reasonable alternatives (e.g., embedding-based edges).
- Evaluation metrics: Class distributions are not reported and accuracy alone is insufficient; Precision, Recall, and F1 should be reported.
- Statistical rigor: No significance testing reported despite small datasets (one dataset has only 43 transcripts); reported numeric precision is misleading.
- Domain knowledge: The approach fails to leverage biomedical resources or clinical NLP tools (e.g., UMLS), missing an opportunity to incorporate domain-relevant features.
