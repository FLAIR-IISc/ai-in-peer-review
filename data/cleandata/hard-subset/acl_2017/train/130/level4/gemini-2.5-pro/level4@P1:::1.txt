### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents an approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using features derived from complex networks. The authors model transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs and enrich these graphs with additional edges based on word embedding similarity. This method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against baselines using linguistic features and Bag-of-Words. The results show that the complex network features, particularly when enriched, are competitive and can outperform traditional linguistic features, with multi-view learning further improving classification accuracy.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  The novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to the clinical task of MCI detection from speech transcripts. This approach offers a promising alternative to traditional NLP methods that rely on syntactic parsers, which often perform poorly on the disfluent and agrammatical speech common in this domain.
2.  An empirical evaluation across three different datasets, including two for Brazilian Portuguese. The work on Portuguese is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates the method's utility for a language with comparatively fewer resources than English.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Application and Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the significant challenge of early MCI detection, an area where automated, language-based tools could have a substantial clinical impact. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of existing work: the reliance on parsers and linguistic feature extractors that are brittle when processing atypical, disfluent speech. Their proposal to use complex networks as a more robust representation is well-motivated.
2.  **Novelty of the Method for the Task:** While complex networks have been used in other NLP tasks, their application to modeling MCI-related speech deficits is novel. The strategy of enriching the standard co-occurrence network with semantic information from word embeddings to better handle short texts is a clever and effective enhancement.
3.  **Encouraging Results:** The proposed method achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to strong baselines based on established linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words. The gains observed from using multi-view learning and ensembles to combine different feature types further strengthen the paper's findings, showing that the network-based features provide complementary information.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper's primary weakness is the insufficient detail regarding the training of the word embeddings, particularly for the Portuguese datasets. The transcripts, especially in the ABCD dataset, are very short. This raises a critical question: what corpus was used to train the embeddings? If they were trained solely on the small, in-domain datasets, the quality of the embeddings would be questionable. If an external corpus was used, details about this corpus are essential for reproducibility and for understanding the results. This ambiguity undermines a core component of the proposed method.
2.  **Paper Organization and Focus:** The initial sections of the paper are overly detailed with general background knowledge (e.g., introductions to BoW, SVMs) and cite references that are not directly relevant to the core topic (e.g., pedestrian detection, sentiment classification). This space could have been better used to elaborate on the novel technical aspects of the work, as mentioned above. The paper would be stronger if the background sections were condensed to focus more on the key contributions.
3.  **Data Accessibility and Presentation:** The Portuguese datasets are inaccessible to readers who do not speak the language, making it difficult to gain an intuitive understanding of the data's characteristics. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it included at least one short sample transcript translated into English, along with a corresponding visualization of its enriched network. This would make the authors' method and the nature of the data much more concrete for a broader audience.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the experimental details for training the Portuguese word embeddings? Specifically, what corpus was used? Was it only the Cinderella and ABCD datasets, or did you use a larger, external Portuguese corpus? This is crucial for interpreting the validity of the results obtained on these short narratives.
2.  You mention using the method by Bojanowski et al. (2016) to handle "out-of-vocabulary words." Could you specify what constitutes an OOV word in your setup? Is it a word not present in a pre-trained model's vocabulary, or a word not seen during training on the task-specific corpus?
3.  Could you provide a brief explanation of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms used for the Portuguese datasets? This would help readers better understand the preprocessing steps and the nature of the transcript data.
