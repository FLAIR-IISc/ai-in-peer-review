### Strengths

This paper applies Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to speech transcripts to identify patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors initially claim a notable ability to distinguish between healthy controls and patients with MCI (lines 141-144). However, this optimism is tempered in the conclusion (lines 781-785), where they state that accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% "means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments." This apparent discrepancy between the introduction and conclusion is intriguing and encourages a closer look at the methodology.

The primary dataset is small, consisting of transcripts from 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants, and is inaccessible to non-Portuguese speakers. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including more technical details. Specifically, providing a short transcript excerpt translated into English, along with a sample visualization of the corresponding network and word embeddings, would greatly improve the work's clarity and impact.

### Weaknesses

The paper dedicates excessive space to its introduction and a review of related work, some of which could be omitted for conciseness. For instance, the citation to Salton (1989) in section 4.2.3 refers to a foundational concept that may not require explicit citation. Similarly, references to unrelated topics like "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654) should be removed. Section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) could also be shortened. The authors should condense the first five pages, focusing the background review strictly on the paper's topic, to create space for a more detailed discussion of the technological innovations and experimental setup.

The methodology for generating word embeddings lacks crucial details, particularly given the small size of the Portuguese narratives in the ABCD dataset. The authors state that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289) and also use sub-word level (n-gram) networks to generate embeddings (lines 513-525). This raises several key questions:
*   What was the source corpus used to learn the initial continuous word representations? Were only the ABCD and Cinderella datasets used, or was a larger, external Portuguese corpus leveraged?
*   Similarly, what was the training source for the sub-word n-gram networks?
*   What is the source of the "out-of-vocabulary words" mentioned in line 516?
*   Is there empirical evidence to support the claim that combining both word-level and sub-word-level networks provides better accuracy than using either one alone?

### General Discussion

The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of using NLP to help detect cognitive impairments. Its focus on Portuguese is also a valuable contribution, as it explores methods for a language with fewer NLP resources compared to English.

However, the manuscript requires significant revision. The authors should shorten Sections 1-3, condense Section 4.1, and expand the experimental section with more detailed explanations of the embedding generation process. Additionally, a brief clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with them.
