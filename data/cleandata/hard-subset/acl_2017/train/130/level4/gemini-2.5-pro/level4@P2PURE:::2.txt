### Strengths:

This paper addresses the problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence information within the transcripts. Features for classification are then derived from various characteristics of this graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The method is evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation with several standard classifiers. The work targets a well-defined and relevant problem and employs appropriate datasets for the investigation.

### Weaknesses:

The paper suffers from several significant drawbacks:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is difficult to read due to numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The manuscript would benefit greatly from thorough proofreading to improve clarity and correct spelling and grammar.

2.  **Problem and Data Description**: The machine learning task is not clearly defined. It is not explicitly stated what constitutes a single instance for classification, though one assumes it is a single transcript. If this is the case, the dataset descriptions should provide statistics at the transcript level. The current tables (1, 2, and 3) describe the patient cohorts of the original studies rather than the specifics of the dataset used for the task. Much of the two pages dedicated to dataset description contains details, such as patient age, that are irrelevant to the proposed classification method. Furthermore, there are confusing inconsistencies in the reported numbers. For instance, Section 4.1.1 states that 326 people were involved in a study, yet the sum of males and females in Table 1 is less than 100.

3.  **Methodological Motivation**: The motivation for the specific graph enrichment strategy is unclear. The authors should justify their choice of a co-occurrence-based graph over alternative constructions, such as one where nodes (words) are connected based on the similarity of their vector representations, irrespective of co-occurrence.

4.  **Use of Domain Knowledge**: Given that the datasets are from the biomedical domain, it is surprising that no domain-specific tools or resources (e.g., medical ontologies, clinical NLP toolkits) were leveraged to potentially improve feature engineering.

5.  **Evaluation Metrics**: The paper relies on accuracy as the primary evaluation metric, but the class distribution of the datasets is never reported. Without this information, it is impossible to determine if accuracy is a meaningful measure, as it can be misleading on imbalanced datasets. For a binary classification task, reporting the F1-score would have been a more informative and appropriate choice.

6.  **Statistical Significance**: Results are reported with up to four decimal places of precision, which is inappropriate for very small datasets (e.g., one contains only 43 transcripts). The paper presents no statistical significance tests, making it impossible to determine if the reported performance improvements between different models are meaningful.
