This paper addresses the important clinical problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing linguistic patterns in speech transcripts. The authors propose a graph-based method that models word co-occurrence to extract a variety of features, including lexical, syntactic, and graph-based properties. This approach is evaluated on three different datasets using 5-fold cross-validation with several standard classifiers.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is its focus on a well-defined and clinically relevant problem. The use of multiple datasets to evaluate the proposed method is commendable, as it provides a more robust assessment of the approach's potential for generalizability. The proposal to use a graph-based representation to capture linguistic information for this task is an interesting direction.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its promising direction, the paper suffers from several major drawbacks that limit its contribution in its current form.

1.  **Clarity of the Experimental Setup and Problem Formulation:** The paper's core weakness is a lack of clarity regarding the machine learning task and experimental design. It is not explicitly stated what constitutes a single instance for classification; one assumes it is a single transcript, but this should be clearly defined. Consequently, the dataset descriptions in Tables 1, 2, and 3 are confusing. They describe the demographics of the parent studies from which the data was sourced rather than the statistics of the actual dataset used for the machine learning task (e.g., the number of transcripts per class, class distribution, average transcript length, vocabulary size, etc.). Much of the two pages dedicated to dataset description contains information, such as patient age, that is irrelevant to the classification task as presented. Furthermore, there appear to be inconsistencies in the reported numbers. For instance, Section 4.1.1 mentions 326 participants, while Table 1 accounts for fewer than 100 individuals, which makes it difficult for the reader to understand the data.

2.  **Inadequate Evaluation and Metrics:** The lack of clarity about the dataset, particularly the class distribution, makes it difficult to assess the choice of evaluation metric. The authors rely solely on accuracy, which can be a misleading measure, especially if the classes are imbalanced. For a binary classification task of this nature, it is standard and essential to report other metrics such as F1-score, precision, and recall to provide a more complete and informative picture of model performance.

3.  **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The presentation of results is not statistically sound. Performance is reported to four decimal places, which implies a level of precision that is not justified by the small size of the datasets (e.g., one dataset has only 43 transcripts). More importantly, the paper does not include any statistical significance testing. Without such tests, it is impossible to determine whether the reported performance differences between various models or feature sets are statistically meaningful or simply due to chance. As a result, any claims about the superiority of one method over another are unsubstantiated.

4.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:** The motivation for several key design choices in the proposed method is not sufficiently explained. The paper proposes constructing a graph based on word co-occurrence, but the rationale for this specific approach over other potential graph representations is not discussed. For example, why was co-occurrence chosen as the basis for edges instead of a method based on semantic similarity, such as connecting nodes based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings? A more thorough justification and comparison to alternatives would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

5.  **Missed Opportunity for Domain-Specific Information:** Given that the data is from a specialized biomedical domain, the work could have been enhanced by leveraging domain-specific resources. For example, medical ontologies or clinical NLP toolkits could have been used to enrich the feature set or inform the graph structure, potentially leading to more powerful representations.

6.  **Readability and Presentation:** The manuscript's clarity is a significant concern. It contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that make it difficult to follow the technical descriptions and argumentation. The paper would benefit greatly from a thorough round of proofreading and editing to improve its overall quality and impact.
