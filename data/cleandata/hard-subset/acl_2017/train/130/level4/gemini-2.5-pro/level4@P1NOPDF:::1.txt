**Strengths**

This paper proposes an NLP-based approach to identify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7) by analyzing speech transcripts of narratives and descriptions. The paper presents a somewhat contradictory message regarding its success. While the introduction claims the ability to distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144), the conclusion offers a more measured assessment, stating that the "accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%... means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments" (lines 781-785). Nevertheless, this frank assessment is encouraging and piques the reader's interest in the methodology.

A significant limitation is the small, Portuguese-only dataset, comprising 20 patients and 20 control participants. This makes the work difficult to evaluate for non-speakers and limits the generalizability of the findings. The paper would be greatly strengthened by incorporating more technical details, including at least one English-translated transcript example and perhaps a visualization of a sample network with its corresponding embeddings.

**Weaknesses**

The paper's primary weakness lies in its structure and focus. The initial sections, including the introduction and review of related work, are overly detailed and could be significantly condensed. Several citations could be omitted to improve conciseness, such as general NLP background references (e.g., Salton 1989 in section 4.2.3) and those unrelated to the core task, like "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images" (lines 652-654). Section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) could also be shortened. I recommend compressing the first five pages to focus more sharply on the paper's specific contributions. The freed space should be used to provide a more detailed account of the technological innovations, including the suggested English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.

A key point requiring clarification is the generation of word embeddings, especially given the short length of the narratives in the Portuguese datasets. The authors state that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but the source corpus for learning these initial representations is not specified. Were they trained solely on the small ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or was a larger, external Portuguese corpus used? This question also applies to the sub-word level (n-gram) networks mentioned in lines 513-525. Furthermore, the paper should provide evidence that combining these two network types improves accuracy. Clarity is also needed regarding the "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516)—their origin is unclear without knowing the vocabulary source.

**General Discussion**

From a broader perspective, the paper addresses an important and interesting application of NLP: the potential for discovering cognitive impairments. The focus on Portuguese is also commendable, as it highlights the challenges and importance of developing methods for languages with fewer resources than English. To this end, a more detailed explanation of how embeddings were computed for Portuguese would be a valuable contribution.

Overall, the paper would benefit significantly from revision. My primary suggestions are to shorten Sections 1-3, condense Section 4.1, and expand the experimental section with more technical explanations. Additionally, a brief clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would be helpful for context and reproducibility.
