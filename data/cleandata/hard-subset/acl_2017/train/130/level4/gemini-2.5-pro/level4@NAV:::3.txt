This paper presents a novel application of largely established representations, feature sets, and methodologies to the task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from spoken narratives. The authors provide a thorough description of the problem context, domain, and datasets used. The proposed methodology and experimental setup are generally sound, although they lack certain key details. Overall, this is an interesting and informative study.

Regarding weaknesses, the manuscript would benefit from significant editing to enhance its clarity and readability. Several key aspects of the methodology are either missing or insufficiently explained. For instance, the specifics of the multi-view learning approach are omitted, and the composition of the "linguistic features" set is not clearly defined. It is ambiguous whether the word embeddings were trained on the three small, in-domain datasets—which would be insufficient for this purpose—or on a larger external corpus. The rationale for removing disfluencies (e.g., filled pauses, repetitions) is not provided; these are often considered important predictive features for MCI. Furthermore, the paper does not justify the choice to forgo the standard tf-idf weighting scheme for its Bag-of-Words (BoW) models. The experimental conclusions are not substantiated with statistical significance tests, and the discussion of related work is somewhat superficial.
