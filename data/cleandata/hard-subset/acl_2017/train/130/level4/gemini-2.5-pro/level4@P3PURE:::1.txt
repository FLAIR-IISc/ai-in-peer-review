### Summary

This paper proposes an interesting application of NLP to speech transcripts for the purpose of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The approach, based on constructing network representations from text, is promising. However, the paper suffers from a lack of critical technical details regarding the methodology, particularly the generation of word embeddings. Furthermore, the extensive background and related work sections could be significantly condensed to allow for a more detailed explanation of the core technical contributions.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Application:** The paper addresses a valuable and socially relevant problem: using NLP techniques on patient narratives to help detect cognitive impairments. The goal of distinguishing between healthy controls and patients with MCI is a well-motivated and impactful research direction.

2.  **Focus on a Less-Resourced Language:** The work on Portuguese is a welcome contribution. It is important to explore how NLP methods, particularly those involving embeddings, can be effectively applied to languages with fewer readily available resources compared to English.

3.  **Engaging Premise:** The authors claim in the introduction that they can distinguish between healthy participants and MCI patients (lines 141-144), but later note in the conclusion that the task is "not easy," with accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% (lines 781-785). While this reflects an inconsistency between the initial optimism and the final results, the overall message is encouraging and makes the reader curious about the technical details of the approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity:** The paper's primary weakness is the omission of crucial details about how the word and sub-word embeddings were generated.
    *   The narratives, especially in the ABCD dataset, are relatively short. This raises the question of how meaningful similarities between words were learned to construct embeddings. The authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289) and also use sub-word level (n-gram) networks (lines 513-525). However, the source corpus for training these representations is never specified. Were they trained only on the small (20+20 participant) ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or was a larger external corpus utilized? This is a critical missing detail needed to assess the validity of the approach.
    *   The paper mentions "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516), but it is unclear what vocabulary this is in reference to without knowing the training source.
    *   The authors propose using two kinds of networks but do not provide evidence (e.g., an ablation study) to demonstrate that their combined use provides better accuracy than either one individually.

2.  **Verbose and Unfocused Background Sections:** The first five pages of the paper, covering the introduction and related work, are unnecessarily long and could be significantly compressed.
    *   Much of the literature review could be more tightly focused on the paper's specific topic. Some citations are for general NLP background (e.g., Salton 1989) and could be omitted. Other references are not directly relevant to the task (e.g., "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images," lines 652-654) and should be removed.
    *   Sections like 4.2.3 (e.g., lines 608-621) could also be shortened. The suggestion is to condense Sections 1-4.1 and use the space to elaborate on the technical innovations and experimental setup.

3.  **Dataset Accessibility and Illustration:** The dataset consists of transcripts in Portuguese, which makes it difficult for non-speakers to understand the data and the method's application. To improve the paper's clarity and impact, it would be highly beneficial to include at least one short transcript example translated into English. Furthermore, a visualization of a sample network with its corresponding embeddings for that transcript would greatly improve the reader's understanding.
