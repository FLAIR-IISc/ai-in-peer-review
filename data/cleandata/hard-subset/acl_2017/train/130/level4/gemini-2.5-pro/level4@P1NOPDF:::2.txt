**Strengths**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence information from the transcripts. Features for the classification task are then derived from various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The authors report results from 5-fold cross-validation using several classifiers, noting that model performance varies across the three datasets. The work targets a well-defined and important problem and utilizes appropriate datasets for the task.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, in its current form, has several significant drawbacks:

1.  **Clarity and Writing:** The paper is difficult to read due to numerous grammatical errors and unconventional phrasing. The manuscript would benefit significantly from thorough proofreading and editing by a native English speaker to improve its clarity and impact.

2.  **Problem Formulation:** The core machine learning task is not clearly defined. It is unclear what constitutes a single instance for classification, though it appears that each transcript is treated as a single data point to be classified as either MCI or non-MCI. If this assumption is correct, the dataset statistics should be reported at the transcript level. However, Tables 1, 2, and 3 focus on the demographics of the originating studies rather than the characteristics of the final dataset used for classification (e.g., number of transcripts per class, transcript length statistics). Much of the two-page dataset description is dedicated to details, such as patient age, that seem irrelevant to the text-based classification task as described. Furthermore, there are apparent inconsistencies in the data description. For example, Section 4.1.1 states that 326 people were involved in a study, yet the corresponding Table 1 lists fewer than 100 total male and female participants, making the numbers difficult to reconcile.

3.  **Methodological Motivation:** The motivation for the proposed graph construction and enrichment method is not well-justified. The paper does not explain why this specific co-occurrence-based approach was chosen over alternative graph construction methods, such as connecting word nodes based on the similarity of their vector representations.

4.  **Use of Domain-Specific Resources:** Given that the datasets are from the biomedical domain, it is surprising that no domain-specific resources or tools (e.g., medical ontologies, specialized parsers, or biomedical word embeddings) were leveraged to potentially enhance feature representation and improve performance.

5.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The class distribution (i.e., the proportion of MCI vs. non-MCI cases) for the datasets is not provided. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric, especially if the classes are imbalanced. For a binary classification task like this one, reporting the F1-score, precision, and recall in addition to accuracy would provide a more complete and reliable picture of the models' performance.

6.  **Statistical Significance:** The results are reported with up to four decimal places of precision, which implies a level of certainty that is not warranted for experiments on very small datasets (e.g., one with only 43 transcripts). More importantly, the paper does not include statistical significance tests. Without them, it is impossible to determine whether the reported performance improvements between different models are statistically significant or simply due to random chance.
