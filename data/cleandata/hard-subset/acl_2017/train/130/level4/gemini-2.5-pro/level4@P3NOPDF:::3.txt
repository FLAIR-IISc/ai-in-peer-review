This paper presents a novel application of existing NLP techniques for the important clinical task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives. The authors provide a thorough description of the problem domain and the datasets used. The overall approach is interesting, and the study is an informative contribution to an important area.

However, the paper in its current form has several significant drawbacks. The primary issues are a lack of clarity and the omission of key methodological details, which hinder reproducibility and weaken the paper's conclusions. Furthermore, some preprocessing choices are not well-justified, and the experimental results lack statistical validation. The related work section could also be expanded to better situate the study within the existing literature.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Methodological Clarity and Omitted Details:** Several core components of the proposed method are either undefined or insufficiently described.
    *   **Multi-View Learning:** The paper states that multi-view learning is used but provides no details on the specific scheme. Which algorithm was used (e.g., co-training, multi-kernel learning)? How were the views defined and integrated? This is a critical omission.
    *   **"Linguistic Features":** Section 4.2.2, which should describe these features, is extremely sparse. The paper must explicitly define what these features are for the work to be understandable and reproducible. The authors should consider using supplementary material if space is a concern, as permitted by conference guidelines.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** It is not clear what corpus was used to train the word embeddings. If they were trained on the three MCI datasets described, this corpus is likely too small to produce robust embeddings. If an external corpus was used, it must be cited. This detail is crucial for interpreting the performance of embedding-based features.

2.  **Justification for Methodological Choices:** The rationale for several key decisions is unclear and, in some cases, counterintuitive for the task.
    *   **Disfluency Removal:** The authors state that disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) were removed from the transcripts. This is a perplexing choice, as a large body of literature suggests that these are powerful indicators of cognitive decline and MCI. The paper should provide a strong justification for removing what could be a vital feature set. The argument in the abstract (L22-23) that disfluencies produce "agrammatical speech impacting in parsing results" is not a sufficient explanation and needs to be substantiated.
    *   **BoW Weighting Scheme:** The paper uses term frequency (TF) for its Bag-of-Words (BoW) model. The authors should explain why they chose this over the more standard and often more effective TF-IDF weighting scheme.
    *   **Manual Transcript Correction:** The abstract suggests manual correction of transcripts (L24-25), which is confusing. The presence of grammatical errors could itself be a signal for MCI. This needs to be clarified.

3.  **Experimental Rigor and Presentation:**
    *   **Significance Testing:** The paper compares the performance of several models but does not report any tests for statistical significance. Without these tests, it is impossible to determine whether the observed differences in accuracy are meaningful or simply due to random variation. This is essential for validating the paper's conclusions.
    *   **Dataset Description:** The description of the datasets is confusing. In lines 323-332, the text itemizes two dataset sources (i and ii) but then refers to three datasets. It would be much clearer to explicitly name each dataset throughout the paper. Acronyms like "ABCD" are used without being defined.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The manuscript requires significant editing to improve readability. The abstract is too long, and many sentences are awkwardly phrased or grammatically incorrect, which detracts from the paper's content.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Abstract:** The abstract should be shortened and rewritten for clarity. For example:
    *   L22-23: "impacting in parsing results" should be "impacting the parsing results."
    *   L29-30: Rephrase "as it a lightweight and language independent representation."
    *   L34-38: The description of the datasets is confusing. Consider rephrasing to clearly name them, e.g., "...using a new dataset, along with two others from DementiaBank: the Cinderella story and the Picnic picture description."

*   **Introduction & Related Work:**
    *   L70: "15% a year" is ambiguous. Please clarify the statistic.
    *   L115-117: Please explain why BoW models would have problems with disfluencies.
    *   L166-182: The related work section is superficial. It would be much stronger if it detailed the specific methods used in prior studies and more clearly contrasted them with the approach in this paper.
    *   L172: Citations are missing.
    *   L185: Please adhere to ACL citation guidelines (e.g., "Aluisio et al. (2016) used...").
    *   L188: The acronym "PLN" is not defined.

*   **Methodology:**
    *   L233-235: Lemmatization is not always the final step in preprocessing. This sentence could be rephrased. The paper also omits discussion of other common steps like stopword removal.
    *   L322: "consisted of" should be "consist of."
    *   L340-440: A citation or link to the transcription norms would be helpful.
    *   Table 1 Caption: The caption claims the table contains "statistics of the dataset," but it only shows demographic information. Please add statistics (e.g., document lengths, vocabulary size) or revise the caption.
    *   L620: Add a comma: "In this work, term frequency was..."
    *   L641-645: This sentence needs to be rewritten for clarity.

*   **Missing Citations:**
    *   L157: Please provide a citation or link for DementiaBank.
    *   L162: Please provide a citation for the "Picnic picture of the Western Aphasia Battery."
    *   L170: Please briefly explain the WML subtest.

In summary, this paper addresses an interesting and important problem. However, it is not yet ready for publication. The authors need to perform a major revision to clarify their methodology, justify their design choices, add statistical rigor to their experiments, and significantly improve the overall writing and presentation.
