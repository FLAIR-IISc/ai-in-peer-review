- Strengths:
This paper addresses the challenge of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by leveraging linguistic data from speech transcripts sourced from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based methodology that utilizes co-occurrence statistics between words within the transcripts to construct a network representation. A diverse set of features, encompassing lexical, syntactic, and other graph-based properties, are extracted for the classification task. The method is evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation across several standard classifiers, with results varying by dataset. Overall, the work tackles a well-defined and important clinical problem using relevant and established datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The manuscript has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed:

1.  The manuscript's clarity is significantly hampered by numerous grammatical errors and unconventional phrasing. A thorough round of proofreading and copy-editing is essential to improve readability.

2.  The machine learning task is not clearly formulated. It is unclear what constitutes a single instance for classification—is it an entire transcript per patient? If so, the dataset statistics should be presented at the transcript level. The tables currently provide details about the original studies but lack critical information about the final dataset used for this work (e.g., number of transcripts per class). Furthermore, much of the lengthy dataset description includes details, such as patient age, that are not used in the classification pipeline. Finally, there are inconsistencies in the reported numbers; for instance, section 4.1.1 mentions 326 participants, yet Table 1 totals to fewer than 100 individuals.

3.  The paper lacks a clear motivation for its specific graph construction and enrichment strategy. The authors do not justify why a co-occurrence-based graph is superior to an alternative approach, such as constructing a graph where nodes (words) are connected based on the semantic similarity of their vector embeddings.

4.  Despite using datasets from the biomedical domain, the study does not leverage any domain-specific knowledge or tools (e.g., medical ontologies, clinical term extractors) that could potentially enhance the feature representation.

5.  The class distribution (i.e., the number of MCI vs. control transcripts) is not reported. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric, as it can be misleading on imbalanced datasets. Reporting metrics more robust to class imbalance, such as the F1-score, is strongly recommended for this binary classification task.

6.  The results are reported with overly high precision (four decimal places), which is not justified given the small size of the datasets (e.g., as few as 43 transcripts). Critically, the paper fails to include statistical significance testing, making it impossible to determine if the reported performance differences between models are meaningful or simply due to random variation.
