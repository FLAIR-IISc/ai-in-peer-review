### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the use of complex network representations of speech transcripts for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and extract topological features for classification. To address the challenge of short texts, which produce sparse, linear networks, the authors enrich these co-occurrence graphs with additional edges based on the semantic similarity of words, calculated using word embeddings. The proposed method is evaluated on three datasets: the English "Cookie Theft" picture description task from DementiaBank, and two Portuguese datasets ("Cinderella" narrative retelling and the Arizona Battery for Communication Disorders of Dementia). The performance of the complex network features is compared against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. The authors also explore combining these different feature sets using ensemble and multi-view learning approaches to improve classification accuracy.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the novel application of word-embedding-enriched complex networks for representing speech transcripts in this clinical domain. This approach is well-motivated as a method to create a robust representation that is less susceptible to the agrammaticality and disfluencies common in patient speech, which often hinder traditional parsing-based methods.

2.  **Cross-Lingual Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation on three distinct datasets, covering both English and Portuguese. This cross-lingual validation strengthens the claim of the method's potential for language-independence and provides valuable results for a less-resourced language in this domain.

3.  **Demonstration of Feature Combination Efficacy:** The authors show that their proposed network-based features can outperform standard linguistic features and, crucially, that combining different feature types (network, linguistic, and BoW) through multi-view learning yields significant performance gains over any single feature set.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Methodology:** The proposed method of modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings is a novel and well-reasoned approach for this task. It directly addresses a key challenge: the short and often agrammatical nature of patient speech transcripts. Standard NLP pipelines that rely on robust parsing can fail on such data, whereas the proposed graph-based representation offers a promising, more resilient alternative.

2.  **Tackles a Significant Practical Problem:** The work addresses the important real-world problem of performance degradation of standard NLP tools on clinical data. By proposing a lightweight representation that does not require deep linguistic processing (like parsing or even lemmatization), the authors present a potentially more robust and scalable solution for real-world clinical applications.

3.  **Extensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough. The authors validate their approach across three datasets with different characteristics (language, task, length), compare it against multiple strong baselines (linguistic features, BoW), and explore various classifiers as well as advanced classification schemes like multi-view and ensemble learning. This extensive evaluation provides a convincing case for the method's utility and highlights the benefits of feature combination, with results showing up to a 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset (Table 5).

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Critical Experimental Details:** The paper omits several crucial details regarding the experimental setup, which compromises clarity and reproducibility. Specifically:
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** It is unclear what corpus was used to train the word embeddings. The three datasets described in the paper are likely too small to train meaningful embeddings from scratch. If pre-trained vectors were used, their source and specifications should be detailed.
    *   **Multi-View Learning Implementation:** The mechanism for multi-view learning is not sufficiently described. The paper mentions it is an "elegant solution" and that weights can be learned by a regression method, but the implementation for the experiments simply uses majority vote. This needs clarification.
    *   **BoW Representation:** The authors use term frequency (TF) for the BoW model but do not provide a rationale for not using the more standard and often more effective TF-IDF weighting scheme.

2.  **Questionable Preprocessing of Disfluencies:** A key decision in the preprocessing pipeline—the removal of disfluencies such as filled pauses ("uh", "um"), false starts, and repetitions—is not adequately justified. These linguistic phenomena are often considered important clinical markers for cognitive impairment, as they can signal word-finding difficulties or disorganized thought. Their removal may discard valuable predictive information, and the paper would be stronger if it provided a clear rationale for this choice or an ablation study showing its impact.

3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports improvements in accuracy percentages (e.g., "a 4.71% of improvement" on the ABCD dataset) but fails to include any statistical significance testing. Without such tests, it is difficult to ascertain whether the observed differences between methods are statistically meaningful or simply due to random variation in the cross-validation splits. This is a critical omission for an empirical paper making claims of superior performance.

4.  **Superficial Analysis of Results:** The analysis of the results is somewhat superficial. While the tables present a wealth of data, the text offers limited insight into *why* certain methods perform better on specific datasets. For instance, the surprisingly strong performance of a simple linear SVM on BoW features for the ABCD dataset (75.47%, Table 7) warrants more discussion. A deeper analysis could provide valuable insights into the nature of the datasets and the strengths of each feature representation.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the corpus used to train the word embeddings? Section 4.2.1 mentions using the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016), but it is unclear whether you used pre-trained vectors or trained them on the (relatively small) datasets from this study.

2.  What is the rationale for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) from the transcripts? Given that these can be strong indicators of cognitive decline, could you comment on whether you experimented with retaining them and what the impact was on performance?

3.  Could you provide more detail on the implementation of the multi-view learning? The paper states that majority voting was used for both ensemble and multi-view learning; could you confirm this was the sole method used for the multi-view results reported in the tables?
