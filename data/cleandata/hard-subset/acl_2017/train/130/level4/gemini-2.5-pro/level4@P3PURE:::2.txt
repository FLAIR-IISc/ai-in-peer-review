### Strengths

This paper addresses the important problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence information within the transcripts. A variety of features are extracted from the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties. The authors report results using 5-fold cross-validation with several standard classifiers, noting that model performance varies across the three datasets. The work targets a well-defined and relevant clinical problem and utilizes appropriate datasets for the task.

### Weaknesses

Despite its interesting premise, the paper has several significant weaknesses that should be addressed.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation**: The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, which makes it difficult to follow the authors' arguments. The paper would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading for grammar and style.

2.  **Problem and Data Description**: The machine learning task is not clearly defined. It is unclear what constitutes a single instance for classification—is it an entire transcript? If so, the dataset statistics should be presented at the transcript level, rather than at the study participant level. Currently, Tables 1, 2, and 3 describe the original studies from which the data were sourced, including details like patient age, which appear irrelevant to the classification task as formulated. These descriptions consume nearly two pages, yet they obscure the essential characteristics of the dataset used for training and evaluation (e.g., number of transcripts per class, transcript length statistics). Furthermore, there are confusing discrepancies in the numbers presented. For instance, Section 4.1.1 mentions 326 participants, while Table 1 accounts for fewer than 100. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to properly assess the experimental setup.

3.  **Methodological Motivation**: The motivation for the specific graph construction method is not well-justified. It is not explained why a co-occurrence graph was chosen over other potential representations. For example, why not construct a graph where every word is a node, and edges are weighted by the semantic similarity of their vector representations, independent of direct co-occurrence?

4.  **Use of Domain-Specific Tools**: Given that the data comes from a biomedical domain, the work could have been strengthened by leveraging domain-specific resources. The current approach does not incorporate any medical ontologies or specialized NLP tools for clinical text that might capture nuances relevant to cognitive impairment.

5.  **Evaluation Metrics**: The choice of evaluation metric is questionable. The paper does not report the class distribution (MCI vs. Control) for the datasets. If the classes are imbalanced, accuracy can be a misleading metric. For a binary classification task like this, reporting precision, recall, and F1-score is standard and would provide a much more complete and robust picture of model performance.

6.  **Statistical Rigor**: The results analysis lacks statistical rigor. Performance improvements are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., one with only 43 transcripts). Without statistical significance tests (such as t-tests or bootstrap resampling), it is impossible to determine whether the reported gains between different models are statistically significant or simply due to random variation in the data splits.
