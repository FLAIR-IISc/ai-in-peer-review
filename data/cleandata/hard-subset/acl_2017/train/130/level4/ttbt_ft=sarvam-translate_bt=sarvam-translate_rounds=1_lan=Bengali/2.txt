**- Energy:**

This paper analyzes speech transcripts from three different datasets to investigate the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). A graph-based method is described using the co-occurrence information within the words found in the transcripts. The features are encoded using various characteristics of the graphs, such as lexical and syntactic features, and many other attributes. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation and various classifiers. Different models across the three datasets demonstrate different performances. This work targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.

**- Weakness:**

This paper has several shortcomings:

1.  It is difficult to read due to the incorrect use of English. A grammar and spelling check of the current manuscript would be very helpful.
2.  The main machine learning problem discussed here is poorly described. What was a single example of classification? It seems that each transcript is classified as MCI or No MCI. If this is the case, the number of transcript levels should be described in the dataset descriptions. Data should be described in Tables 1, 2, and 3, not the research that created the transcripts. The age of the patients is irrelevant to the classification task. Many of the writings (2 pages) are only used to describe the datasets, which does not affect the final classification task. Also, I was not sure why the numbers were not being added. For example: Section 4.1.1 states that 326 people were involved. But the total number of men and women in Table 1 is less than 100?
3.  What is the purpose behind enriching the graph? Why shouldn't each word be represented by a node in the graph and connected through the similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence?
4.  The datasets are taken from a biomedical domain. No domain-specific tools were used.
5.  Since the class distribution of the datasets is unclear, it is difficult to determine whether accuracy is a good measure for evaluation. In both cases, since it is a binary classification task, F1 could have been a desirable metric.
6.  Results up to 4 decimal places have been reported in a very small dataset (43 transcripts), where statistical tests on the increment have not been performed. Therefore, it is not clear whether these improvements are significant.
