**Strengths:**

This paper proposes applying NLP to speech transcripts (stories and descriptions) to identify patients with MCI (Mild Cognitive Impairment, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors claim to have been able to distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144). However, in the conclusion, lines 781-785, they state that "...an accuracy of 60% to 85%... means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and people with cognitive impairment". Therefore, the beginning of the paper is more optimistic than the conclusion, but the message is still encouraging and the reader becomes eager to see what has actually been done.

The corpus presented in the dataset is only created for 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants (20+20), and it is incomprehensible to those who do not speak Portuguese. It would be good to include more technical details in the article and possibly include at least one small transcript example that has been translated into English, and ultimately a (partial) sample network with embedding for this transcript.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper begins with a detailed introduction and review of the relevant work. Some of the cited references are more or less NLP background, so they can be omitted, for example (Salton 1989) in section 4.2.3. Other references are not directly related to the topic, for example "sentiment classification" and "pedestrian detection in images", lines 652-654, and they can also be omitted. In general, lines 608-621, section 4.2.3 can also be shortened, etc. etc. It is suggested that the first 5 pages be compressed, the review be strictly focused on the paper's topic, and more detail be considered on the technical innovation, including examples of English translations of ABCD and/or Cinderella stories.

The relatively short stories in Portuguese, especially in the ABCD dataset, raise the question of how similarities between words have been found, in order to construct word embedding. In lines 272-289, the authors explain that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representation. What is the source of continuous word representation learning; are these only datasets ABCD+Cinderella, or were external corpora used? Lines 513-525 state that sub-word level (N-gram) networks were used to generate word embedding. Then, what is the source of training? Are we sure that two types of networks provide better accuracy together? And what are the "words outside the vocabulary" (line 516), where do they come from?

**General Discussion:**

It is important to study how NLP can help in the search for cognitive impairments; the paper is interesting from this perspective. Another interesting aspect is that it is related to NLP for Portuguese, and it is important to explain how it calculates embedding for a language with relatively fewer resources (than English).

The text needs revision: shortening sections 1-3, compressing 4.1, and adding more explanations about the experiments. Some explanations can be given about the NUTC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription criteria.
