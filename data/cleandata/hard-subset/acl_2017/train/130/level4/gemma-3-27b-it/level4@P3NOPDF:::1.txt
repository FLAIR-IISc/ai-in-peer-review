This paper explores the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to speech transcripts – specifically narratives and descriptions – with the goal of identifying individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors demonstrate a capacity to differentiate between healthy controls and MCI patients, reporting accuracy rates ranging from 60% to 85%. While the initial framing of the results is optimistic, the conclusion acknowledges the challenges inherent in distinguishing between these groups, a nuance that appropriately tempers expectations. Overall, the work addresses an important problem and presents a potentially valuable direction for research.

However, the paper exhibits several areas requiring attention. The corpus, consisting of transcripts from 20 healthy controls and 20 MCI patients, is limited in size and accessibility due to its exclusive use of Portuguese. Increasing the corpus size and providing English translations of sample transcripts – perhaps including a visualization of a sample network with word embeddings – would significantly improve the paper’s impact and facilitate understanding for a broader audience.

The introductory sections (1-3) and related work (4.2.3, lines 608-621) are overly extensive. Several cited references appear to represent general NLP background knowledge (e.g., Salton, 1989) and can be removed to streamline the presentation. Furthermore, citations relating to disparate fields like sentiment classification and pedestrian detection detract from the paper's focus and should be omitted. A more concise and targeted literature review, focusing specifically on relevant work in cognitive impairment detection and Portuguese NLP, would strengthen the paper. A tighter focus on the technological innovation is crucial.

The method for constructing word embeddings raises questions about data sources and comparative performance. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289) and utilizing sub-word level (n-gram) networks (lines 513-525). It is unclear whether these representations are learned solely from the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or if external corpora were leveraged. Clarification on this point is essential. Moreover, the paper doesn’t definitively establish whether combining both network types demonstrably improves accuracy, and the origin of the "out-of-vocabulary words" (line 516) remains undefined. A detailed ablation study and more rigorous analysis of these components would be beneficial.

Despite these weaknesses, this work is valuable due to its exploration of NLP’s potential in cognitive impairment detection and its focus on Portuguese, a relatively low-resource language. This raises a crucial point: the paper would benefit from a discussion on the challenges and strategies employed when computing embeddings for languages with limited resources compared to English. Further elaboration on the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms could also provide valuable context.

Finally, several minor technical issues require correction:

*   Line 029: Replace “as it a lightweight” with “as in a lightweight.”
*   Line 188: Change “PLN” to “NLP.”
*   Line 264: Correct the repetition in “out of cookie out of the cookie.”
*   Table 3, row 2, column 3: Change “72,0” to “72.0.”
*   Lines 995-996 & 1001-1002: The DOI numbers are duplicated.
*   Lines 992-993: The link behind the title points to the subsequent paper in the list.



In summary, while the paper presents an interesting application of NLP, significant revisions are needed to improve its clarity, conciseness, and technical rigor. Addressing the points outlined above will substantially strengthen the contribution and broaden its appeal.
