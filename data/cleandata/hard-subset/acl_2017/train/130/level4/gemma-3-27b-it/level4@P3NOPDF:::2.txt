This paper investigates the problem of identifying individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through the analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words within the transcripts. Graph features are encoded based on lexical, syntactic, and other characteristics, and evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation with several classifiers. The study demonstrates varying performance across the three datasets, addressing a well-defined problem with appropriate data sources.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. 

First, the manuscript is difficult to follow due to numerous errors in English usage. A thorough review for grammar and spelling is essential before further consideration.

Second, the core machine learning task is poorly articulated. The paper lacks clarity on the unit of classification. If each transcript represents a single instance, classified as either MCI or non-MCI, this should be explicitly stated and reflected in the dataset descriptions. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should present data *about* the datasets themselves, not the studies that generated the transcripts. The inclusion of demographic details like patient age appears largely irrelevant to the classification task and consumes substantial space (approximately two pages) with details that do not directly impact the modeling process. Furthermore, inconsistencies in the reported data raise concerns; for example, Section 4.1.1 states the involvement of 326 individuals, while the gender breakdown in Table 1 totals fewer than 100.

Third, the motivation for enriching the graph with co-occurrence information is not adequately justified. A simpler approach – representing each word as a node and connecting nodes based on vector similarity, regardless of co-occurrence – could potentially be equally effective and warrants discussion. Why is co-occurrence deemed essential for capturing relevant information?

Fourth, given the biomedical domain of the datasets, the paper lacks any evidence of leveraging domain-specific natural language processing tools or resources. Exploring established biomedical lexicons or linguistic features could potentially improve performance.

Fifth, the lack of clarity regarding the class distribution within each dataset makes it difficult to assess the suitability of accuracy as an evaluation metric. For a binary classification problem, reporting F1-score would provide a more informative measure of performance, particularly in cases of imbalanced datasets.

Finally, the results are reported to four decimal places despite the small dataset sizes (e.g., 43 transcripts in one case). Without statistical significance testing to validate these incremental gains, it remains unclear whether observed improvements are meaningful or due to chance. The authors should incorporate statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) to demonstrate the statistical significance of their findings.
