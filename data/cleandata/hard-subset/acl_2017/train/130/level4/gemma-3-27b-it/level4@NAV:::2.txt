## Review:

This paper investigates the identification of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors present a graph-based approach that utilizes word co-occurrence information to construct a network, and derive features based on lexical, syntactic, and other graph characteristics. Performance is evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers, revealing variability in model performance across the datasets. The work addresses a clearly defined problem with the use of suitable datasets.

However, the paper exhibits several weaknesses. 

1.  The manuscript’s readability is hindered by linguistic errors; a thorough review for grammar and spelling is strongly recommended.
2.  The machine learning framing of the problem is unclear. The core task appears to be transcript-level classification (MCI vs. No MCI), and if this is correct, the dataset descriptions should reflect this, providing statistics at the transcript level.  Tables 1, 2, and 3 should focus on dataset characteristics, rather than the studies that generated them.  Details such as patient age are not relevant to the classification task and contribute to excessive length (approximately two pages) without improving the clarity of the core method. Furthermore, inconsistencies in the presented data (e.g., participant numbers in Section 4.1.1 vs. Table 1) raise concerns.
3. The rationale behind the graph enrichment strategy is not adequately explained. A simpler graph representation, connecting words based on vector similarity regardless of co-occurrence, warrants consideration and justification for its exclusion.
4.  Despite utilizing datasets from a biomedical domain, the authors do not leverage any domain-specific natural language processing tools or resources.
5.  Given the lack of clarity regarding dataset class distribution, the suitability of accuracy as an evaluation metric is questionable.  For a binary classification task, reporting the F1-score would be more informative.
6.  The presentation of results to four decimal places on small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without statistical significance testing makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of reported gains.
