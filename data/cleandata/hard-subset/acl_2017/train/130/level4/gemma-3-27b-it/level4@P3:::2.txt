## Review for ACL 2017

This paper investigates the problem of identifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using a graph-based approach. The authors leverage co-occurrence information between words within transcripts to construct complex networks, extracting features from these networks and employing several classifiers for prediction. The method is evaluated on three datasets, and the results demonstrate competitive performance compared to traditional linguistic features. This work addresses a relevant and challenging problem with potentially high impact, and the use of multiple datasets strengthens the evaluation.

**Contributions:**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The core contribution lies in demonstrating the feasibility and effectiveness of representing speech transcripts as complex networks for the task of MCI detection. This provides a novel, language-independent approach that bypasses the challenges of parsing potentially disfluent speech.
2. **Integration of Word Embeddings:** Enriching the complex network representation with word embeddings to capture semantic information is a valuable addition. This addresses the limitation of solely relying on co-occurrence and enhances the network’s ability to represent meaning.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Datasets:** The evaluation across three datasets—DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD Battery—provides a more robust assessment of the method’s generalizability than would be possible with a single dataset. The inclusion of datasets in both English and Portuguese is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The use of complex networks for MCI detection from speech transcripts is a relatively unexplored area, offering a potentially valuable alternative to traditional NLP techniques that rely heavily on parsing and linguistic feature engineering.
2. **Robustness to Disfluencies:** The authors correctly highlight the challenge of disfluencies in speech transcripts and position their method as a potential solution, as it minimizes reliance on accurate parsing. The network-based representation inherently handles some level of noise and grammatical errors.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show that complex network features perform comparably to, and in some cases outperform, traditional linguistic features across all three datasets. The improvements achieved through ensemble and multi-view learning further demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.
4. **Clear Problem Definition & Relevance:** The paper addresses a clinically significant problem – the early detection of MCI – and motivates its importance effectively. The potential for automated screening and diagnosis is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity and Polish:** The paper suffers from several issues related to writing quality. Grammatical errors and awkward phrasing hinder readability and make it difficult to fully grasp the nuances of the methodology. A thorough proofread and revision are strongly recommended.
2. **Dataset Description & Relevance:** While the datasets are a strength, their description is overly detailed and focuses on irrelevant information (e.g., patient ages). The descriptions should be streamlined to focus solely on details pertinent to the classification task, such as the number of transcripts per class, average transcript length, and potential biases. Discrepancies in reported numbers (e.g., total participants vs. gender breakdown) raise concerns about data accuracy.
3. **Motivation for Embedding Integration:** The rationale for integrating word embeddings is not fully convincing. The authors should more clearly explain *why* adding embeddings is expected to improve performance and how it addresses limitations of the initial co-occurrence network.  Exploring alternatives like directly representing word similarity in the network without co-occurrence would strengthen this point.
4. **Evaluation Metrics & Statistical Significance:** Accuracy alone is insufficient for evaluating binary classification performance, especially with imbalanced datasets (if present). Reporting precision, recall, F1-score, and AUC would provide a more comprehensive picture. Crucially, statistical significance tests are missing. The small dataset sizes necessitate statistical validation to determine if observed differences are meaningful. Reporting results to four decimal places without statistical testing is misleading.
5. **Limited Domain-Specific Knowledge Integration:** Despite operating in the biomedical domain, the paper lacks integration of relevant domain knowledge or specialized tools for analyzing linguistic impairments associated with cognitive decline.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of network topology (word co-occurrence) and explain why this representation is particularly well-suited for handling disfluent speech?
2. What is the class distribution within each dataset? Reporting this information is crucial for interpreting the reported accuracy scores.
3.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate the significance of the observed performance improvements?
4. How sensitive is the performance of the method to the parameters used for constructing the complex networks (e.g., window size for co-occurrence, similarity threshold for embedding integration)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider clarifying the meaning of "instance" in the context of the classification task. Is each transcript a single instance?
*   The reference list is extensive but contains minor formatting inconsistencies.
*   The Supplementary Material (Section A) is helpful but could be further organized and integrated into the main text for better readability.
