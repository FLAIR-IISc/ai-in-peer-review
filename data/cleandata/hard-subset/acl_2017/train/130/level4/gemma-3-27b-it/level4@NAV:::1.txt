## Review

This paper investigates the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to speech transcripts – narratives and descriptions – with the goal of identifying individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors report an ability to differentiate between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141-144), although the conclusion (lines 781-785) qualifies this, noting that accuracies ranging from 60% to 85% indicate difficulty in reliably distinguishing between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairment. Despite this cautious framing, the work presents an encouraging premise and generates reader interest in the specifics of the approach.

The study utilizes a dataset comprising transcripts from 20 healthy participants and 20 controls. A limitation is the dataset’s exclusive use of Portuguese, rendering it inaccessible to those unfamiliar with the language. Enhancing the paper would involve providing greater technical detail, and incorporating at least one example of a short transcript translated into English, alongside a partial representation of a network with corresponding embeddings for that transcript. 

The paper begins with a comprehensive introduction and literature review. However, some cited works represent general NLP background (e.g., Salton 1989, section 4.2.3) and could be omitted.  Additionally, references peripherally related to the core topic – such as those concerning sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images (lines 652-654) – could be removed. In general, section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) and the initial five pages could benefit from condensation, refocusing the discussion on the central research question and providing a more in-depth exploration of the technological innovations. Including English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives would be beneficial.

The relatively brief narratives, particularly within the ABCD dataset, raise questions regarding the efficacy of identifying word similarities for constructing word embeddings. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but the source of these representations is unclear. Were they learned solely from the ABCD+Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora utilized?  Similarly, the training source for the sub-word level (n-gram) networks used to generate word embeddings (lines 513-525) requires clarification. It remains unclear whether the combination of these two network types demonstrably improves accuracy, and the origin of the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516 should be specified.

Overall, the investigation of NLP’s potential for identifying cognitive impairments is a valuable endeavor, and this paper represents a worthwhile contribution. The focus on Portuguese language NLP is particularly noteworthy, and the challenges of constructing embeddings for a language with limited resources deserve discussion. 

The manuscript requires revision, specifically through shortening sections 1-3, condensing section 4.1, and elaborating on the experimental details. Further clarification regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
