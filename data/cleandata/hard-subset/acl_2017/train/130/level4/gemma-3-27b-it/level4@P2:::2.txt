This paper investigates the problem of identifying individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The proposed method utilizes a graph-based approach, leveraging co-occurrence information between words within the transcripts to create network representations. Various graph characteristics, along with lexical and syntactic properties, are then used as features for classification. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with several classifiers, demonstrating varying performance across the datasets. The work addresses a relevant clinical problem and employs appropriate datasets for evaluation.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the demonstration of complex network analysis as a viable method for automatically detecting MCI from speech transcripts. This is particularly valuable given the challenges posed by disfluencies in patient speech, which can hinder traditional NLP techniques reliant on parsing.
2. **Integration of Word Embeddings:** Enriching the co-occurrence networks with word embeddings improves the representation of semantic relationships within the short texts characteristic of neuropsychological assessments. This enhancement appears to contribute to improved classification accuracy.
3. **Comparative Evaluation Across Diverse Datasets:**  The evaluation of the method on three datasets – one English and two Portuguese – provides a broader assessment of its generalizability and robustness compared to studies limited to a single language or data source.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Utilizing complex networks to model speech transcripts for MCI detection represents a departure from conventional NLP methods and offers a potentially more robust approach to handling the inherent noise in patient speech.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results demonstrate competitive performance compared to traditional linguistic features, particularly with the enriched network approach.  The improvements observed, especially on the Arizona dataset, suggest a promising direction for future research.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The use of 5-fold cross-validation provides a reliable estimate of the model’s performance and helps to mitigate the risk of overfitting.
4. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem of MCI detection and motivates the need for automated diagnostic tools.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity and Readability:** The manuscript suffers from noticeable issues with English grammar and clarity, hindering comprehension. A thorough revision for grammar, spelling, and sentence structure is strongly recommended.
2. **Dataset Description and Relevance:** The dataset descriptions are overly detailed, focusing on characteristics irrelevant to the classification task (e.g., patient age). Tables 1, 2, and 3 should concisely present information directly related to the dataset composition at the transcript level (e.g., number of MCI vs. control transcripts). Discrepancies within the dataset descriptions (e.g., participant numbers) raise concerns about data consistency.
3. **Motivation for Embedding Enrichment:** The rationale for enriching the graph with word embeddings could be more clearly articulated. Exploring the benefits of solely using a vector-similarity-based network, without the initial co-occurrence network, could provide valuable insight.
4. **Lack of Domain-Specific Feature Engineering:** While the approach is novel, the study doesn't leverage existing domain-specific knowledge or tools from the biomedical or neuropsychological fields that could potentially enhance feature extraction.
5. **Evaluation Metrics:** Given the potential for imbalanced classes within the datasets, relying solely on accuracy can be misleading. Reporting precision, recall, and F1-score would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model’s performance.
6. **Statistical Significance:** The presentation of results to four decimal places on small datasets without statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests) makes it difficult to ascertain the practical significance of observed improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could the authors clarify the class distribution (number of MCI vs. control transcripts) for each dataset?
2. What specific benefits did the authors observe from enriching the co-occurrence network with word embeddings, compared to a network solely based on vector similarity?
3. Did the authors consider incorporating any domain-specific linguistic features or tools relevant to cognitive impairment assessment?
4. Could the authors provide a statistical analysis of the reported results to determine the significance of the observed improvements?
