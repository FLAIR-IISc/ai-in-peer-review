This paper explores the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to speech transcripts – specifically narratives and descriptions – to identify individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors report an ability to differentiate between healthy controls and MCI patients, initially presenting optimistic results (lines 141-144). However, the conclusion (lines 781-785) qualifies this, stating that accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% indicates difficulty in consistently distinguishing between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments. Despite this nuanced conclusion, the work presents a promising direction and motivates further investigation.

A key contribution is the creation of a corpus for this task, though its current size – comprising 20 healthy controls and 20 MCI patients – is limited. A significant barrier to broader understanding is the corpus’s language: it is entirely in Portuguese. To increase accessibility and facilitate reproducibility, the authors should include more technical details within the paper, and crucially, provide at least one translated example transcript (to English). Illustrating the process with a sample network diagram displaying word embeddings for a given transcript would further clarify the methodology.

The paper begins with a comprehensive introduction and literature review. While thorough, it includes references that are overly broad or tangential to the core contribution. For instance, citations serving as general NLP background (e.g., Salton 1989, section 4.2.3) could be streamlined. Similarly, references to unrelated areas like sentiment classification and pedestrian detection (lines 652-654) should be removed. A focused revision of sections 1-3, particularly lines 608-621 and beyond, is recommended to concentrate on work directly relevant to NLP for cognitive impairment detection. This compression should be offset by a more detailed discussion of the technical innovations.

The brevity of the narratives, especially within the ABCD dataset, raises questions regarding the efficacy of identifying word similarities for constructing word embeddings. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but the source of these representations remains unclear. Were they learned solely from the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora utilized? Similarly, the training source for the sub-word level (n-gram) networks used to generate embeddings (lines 513-525) requires clarification. It is also important to justify whether the combined use of these two network types demonstrably improves accuracy. Finally, the paper should define the origin of the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516.

Despite these weaknesses, this research addresses an important problem – leveraging NLP to aid in the discovery of cognitive impairments. Furthermore, its focus on Portuguese NLP is valuable, particularly regarding the challenges of creating embeddings for languages with limited resources compared to English.

To strengthen the paper, a substantial revision is needed. This should include shortening the introductory sections, condensing section 4.1, and expanding the description of the experiments. Additional context regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
