This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. A graph-based method leveraging co-occurrence information between words in transcripts is described, with features encoded using various graph characteristics – lexical, syntactic properties, and others. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with a number of classifiers. Different models exhibit varying performance across the three datasets. This work addresses a well-defined problem and utilizes appropriate datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Application of Network Analysis:** The application of complex network analysis to speech transcripts for MCI detection is a significant contribution. This approach provides a potentially robust and language-independent method, circumventing the challenges posed by disfluencies common in patient speech.
2.  **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the model on three distinct datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) strengthens the findings and demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets expands the potential impact of the work.
3.  **Combination of Features & Classifiers:** The exploration of combining complex network features with linguistic features and the use of ensemble and multi-view learning demonstrate a thorough investigation of potential performance enhancements. The reported improvements, particularly on the Cinderella and Arizona datasets, are promising.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from issues with clarity and readability. Improvements in grammar, spelling, and overall writing quality are necessary. The presentation of the datasets is overly detailed and focuses on information irrelevant to the classification task. Specifically, Tables 1, 2, and 3 present demographic information (e.g., patient age) that does not directly contribute to the classification task and distracts from the key data points (number of transcripts per class). There are also inconsistencies in the dataset descriptions (e.g., participant numbers not summing correctly).
2.  **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation methodology lacks rigor. Reporting accuracy to four decimal places on small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without statistical significance testing is misleading.  Given the potential for imbalanced class distributions, accuracy may not be the most appropriate metric; F1-score or other measures of precision and recall would provide a more informative assessment.
3.  **Justification of Methodological Choices:** The rationale behind enriching the co-occurrence network with word embeddings is not fully explained. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the benefits of this approach compared to alternative methods, such as directly representing words as nodes and connecting them based on vector similarity.
4.  **Limited Domain-Specific Integration:** While the datasets originate from a biomedical domain, the paper does not leverage domain-specific knowledge or tools. Integrating relevant linguistic or neuropsychological insights could potentially improve performance.
5.  **Unclear Instance Definition:** The description of the machine learning problem lacks clarity. The definition of a single instance of classification (e.g., a transcript representing one data point) should be explicitly stated, along with a clear description of the data at the transcript level.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a justification for choosing accuracy as the primary evaluation metric, and discuss the potential impact of class imbalance (if any) on the results? Would reporting F1-score or other relevant metrics provide a more comprehensive evaluation?
2.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for enriching the co-occurrence network with word embeddings? How does this approach differ from, and potentially improve upon, directly constructing a network based on word vector similarity?
3.  How were the parameters for the classifiers (e.g., SVM kernel, k in k-NN) chosen? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed, and if so, what method was used?
