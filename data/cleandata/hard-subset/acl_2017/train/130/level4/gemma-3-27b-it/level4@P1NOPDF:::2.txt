This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. It describes a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words within the transcripts. Features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties, among others. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with a number of classifiers, and different models exhibit varying performance across the three datasets. This work addresses a well-defined problem and utilizes appropriate datasets.

However, the paper suffers from several drawbacks.

1. The paper is difficult to read due to issues with English usage. A thorough review for grammar and spelling would significantly improve its clarity.

2. The machine learning problem is poorly described. Is each transcript being treated as a single instance for classification – specifically, classified as either MCI or No MCI? If so, the dataset descriptions should present data at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should describe the datasets themselves, not the studies that produced the transcripts. Patient age is largely irrelevant to the classification task, and a significant portion of the text (two pages) is dedicated to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the final classification. Furthermore, the numbers presented are occasionally inconsistent – for example, section 4.1.1 states 326 people were involved, while the male and female counts in Table 1 total less than 100.

3. The motivation behind enriching the graph is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node and connect them based on the similarity of their vector representations, regardless of co-occurrence?

4. The datasets originate from a biomedical domain, yet no domain-specific tools or knowledge appear to have been leveraged.

5. Given the unclear dataset class distribution, it is difficult to determine whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. In any case, for a binary classification task, the F1-score would have been a more informative metric.

6. Results are reported to four decimal places for very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without any statistical significance testing. This makes it difficult to assess whether the observed gains are meaningful.
