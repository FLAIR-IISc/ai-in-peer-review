This paper investigates the application of complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors aim to automatically identify MCI using a language-independent representation, addressing the challenges posed by disfluencies in patient speech. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the feasibility of using complex network features, particularly when augmented with word embeddings, to distinguish between individuals with and without MCI across multiple datasets. A secondary contribution is the application of this approach to the Portuguese language, a relatively under-resourced language in NLP, offering insights into adapting techniques for languages with limited resources. Finally, the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to combine different feature sets and improve classification accuracy represents a notable, though less central, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Application of Complex Networks:** The application of complex network analysis, combined with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech is a valuable contribution. This approach offers a potentially robust and language-independent method for analyzing linguistic data, circumventing the need for extensive manual preprocessing and reliance on traditional parsing techniques susceptible to disfluencies. The results demonstrate competitive performance compared to linguistic features, highlighting the potential of this approach.

2.  **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona Battery – strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The inclusion of datasets in both English and Portuguese provides valuable insights into the cross-linguistic applicability of the proposed method. The reported improvements on the Arizona dataset, achieving 80% accuracy with the multi-view approach, are particularly encouraging.

3.  **Comprehensive Feature Exploration & Combination:** The paper systematically explores various feature types – topological network metrics, linguistic features, and bag-of-words representations – and effectively combines them using ensemble and multi-view learning. The multi-view learning approach, specifically, demonstrates a significant performance boost in several cases, indicating the benefits of leveraging complementary information from different feature spaces.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Dataset Size & Representativeness:** The datasets employed, particularly the Cinderella and ABCD datasets, are relatively small (20+20 and 23+20 participants, respectively). This limited sample size may impact the statistical power and generalizability of the results. Furthermore, the demographic information presented suggests potential imbalances (e.g., gender distribution) that could introduce bias.

2.  **Insufficient Detail on Word Embedding Training:** The description of the word embedding generation process lacks crucial details. While the authors mention using a character n-gram approach (Bojanowski et al., 2016), they do not clearly specify the corpus used for pre-training the embeddings. Were the embeddings pre-trained on a large external corpus, or were they trained solely on the limited in-house datasets? Clarification on this aspect is vital for understanding the robustness and transferability of the embeddings. Furthermore, the rationale for combining sub-word level networks with the co-occurrence networks is not fully explained.

3.  **Overly Broad Introduction & Redundancy:** The introduction and related work sections (Sections 1 & 2) contain a considerable amount of background information that is not directly relevant to the core contributions of the paper. While contextualization is important, a more focused discussion of prior work specifically related to MCI detection and network-based NLP would improve clarity and conciseness. Additionally, some references seem tangential (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection). The paper could benefit from a more streamlined presentation of the literature review.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What corpus was used to train the word embeddings employed in the complex network enrichment process? Please provide details on the size, source, and characteristics of the training data.
2.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind combining the co-occurrence network with the network built from word embeddings based on character n-grams? What specific benefits are expected from this integration?
3.  Given the relatively small dataset sizes, have you performed any power analysis to determine the statistical significance of the observed improvements?
4.  Regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms, could you provide a brief explanation of their key principles, particularly as they relate to handling disfluencies and other speech variations?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider providing example translations of short transcripts from the Portuguese datasets to enhance accessibility for a broader audience.
*   A visual representation of a sample complex network, with highlighted embeddings, would greatly aid understanding.
*   Address the minor technical errors noted in the original review (e.g., “as it” vs. “as in”, PLN vs. NLP, repeated words).
