This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three different datasets. A graph-based method leveraging co-occurrence information between words in the transcripts is described. Features are encoded using various graph characteristics, including lexical and syntactic properties, among others. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with a number of classifiers, demonstrating varying performance across the three datasets. This work addresses a well-defined problem and utilizes appropriate datasets.

However, the paper suffers from several drawbacks.

1. The paper is difficult to read due to numerous errors in English usage. A thorough review for grammar and spelling would significantly improve clarity.

2. The machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described. Is each transcript being treated as a single instance of classification? It appears each transcript is classified as either MCI or No MCI; if so, the dataset descriptions should reflect this at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should describe the data itself, not the studies that generated the transcripts. Patient age is irrelevant for the classification task, and a substantial amount of text (approximately two pages) is dedicated to describing the datasets with details that do not impact the final classification performance. Furthermore, inconsistencies in the data presentation are present (e.g., section 4.1.1 states 326 people were involved, while the male and female counts in Table 1 sum to less than 100).

3. The motivation behind enriching the graph is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node and connect them based on the similarity of their vector representations, regardless of co-occurrence?

4. The datasets originate from a biomedical domain, yet the authors do not leverage any domain-specific tools or knowledge.

5. Given the unclear dataset class distribution, it is difficult to assess whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. Regardless, for a binary classification task, F1-score would be a more informative metric.

6. Results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without any statistical significance testing. Consequently, it is unclear if the observed gains are statistically significant.
