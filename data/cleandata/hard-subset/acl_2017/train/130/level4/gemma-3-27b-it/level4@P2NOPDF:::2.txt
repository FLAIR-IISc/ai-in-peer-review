This paper investigates the problem of identifying individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors propose a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence information to create a network representation of the transcripts. Graph features, incorporating lexical, syntactic, and other properties, are then extracted and used for classification. The approach was evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation with several classifiers, revealing varying performance across the datasets. The work addresses a clearly defined problem and utilizes appropriate datasets for investigation.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that significantly impact its clarity and the interpretability of its findings.

First, the manuscript is difficult to follow due to numerous grammatical errors and stylistic issues. A thorough review and editing for grammar and spelling would substantially improve readability. 

Second, the machine learning task itself is not clearly articulated. It appears the goal is to classify each transcript as representing either an individual with MCI or without MCI. If this is the case, the dataset descriptions should focus on characteristics *at the transcript level*, rather than details of the studies from which the transcripts originated. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should present data relevant to the classification task itself, rather than study demographics. The inclusion of patient age, for instance, seems largely irrelevant to the classification problem addressed.  Furthermore, a substantial portion of the paper (approximately two pages) is dedicated to detailed dataset descriptions that do not directly inform the classification task. Additionally, inconsistencies in the reported data raise concerns – for example, the text in section 4.1.1 states 326 participants were involved, while the gender breakdown in Table 1 accounts for fewer than 100.

Third, the motivation for the specific graph enrichment strategy is unclear. The authors should justify why they chose to build the graph based solely on word co-occurrence, rather than a more general approach where each word is represented as a node and connected by a measure of semantic or vector similarity, irrespective of direct co-occurrence.

Fourth, despite utilizing datasets from the biomedical domain, the paper does not appear to leverage any domain-specific natural language processing tools or resources that could enhance performance.

Fifth, the class distribution within each dataset is not clearly stated. Without this information, it's difficult to assess whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. For a binary classification task such as this, reporting the F1-score would provide a more informative measure of performance. 

Finally, the results are presented with an excessive level of precision (to four decimal places) given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 43 transcripts in one case).  Critically, no statistical significance tests were performed to demonstrate that the observed differences in performance are meaningful. Without such tests, it is impossible to determine if the reported gains are statistically significant or due to random chance.
