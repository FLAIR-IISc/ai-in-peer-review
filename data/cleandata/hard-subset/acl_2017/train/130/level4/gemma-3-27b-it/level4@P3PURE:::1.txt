This paper investigates the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) to speech transcripts – specifically narratives and descriptions – to identify patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors report the ability to distinguish between healthy control participants and those with MCI (lines 141-144). However, the conclusion (lines 781-785) presents a more cautious assessment, stating that accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% indicates difficulty in consistently differentiating between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments. This discrepancy creates an initial impression of optimism that is later tempered, but overall the work is encouraging and motivates further investigation. 

A key limitation is the relatively small corpus, comprising only 20 healthy controls and 20 MCI patients (20+20). Furthermore, the corpus is exclusively in Portuguese, restricting accessibility for those unfamiliar with the language. To enhance the paper’s impact, I recommend including more detailed technical explanations, and providing at least one example of a short transcript translated into English. Illustrating the process with a partial network visualization demonstrating word embeddings for a sample transcript would also be beneficial.

The paper begins with a comprehensive introduction and review of relevant work. However, some cited references appear to provide general NLP background (e.g., Salton 1989 in section 4.2.3) and could be omitted. Similarly, references to areas outside the core focus, such as sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images (lines 652-654), should be reconsidered. In general, section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) could be significantly condensed. I suggest streamlining the first five pages by focusing more tightly on the paper’s specific contributions and dedicating more space to detailed technological explanations. This should include examples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cindarela narratives.

The brevity of the Portuguese narratives, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raises questions regarding the effectiveness of identifying word similarities for constructing word embeddings. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289). However, the source of these continuous word representations is unclear. Were they learned solely from the ABCD+Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora utilized? Similarly, lines 513-525 detail the use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks for generating word embeddings. Clarification is needed regarding the training data for this approach. Is there evidence that combining these two types of networks demonstrably improves accuracy? Finally, the nature of the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516 should be specified, including their origin.

Overall, this work is valuable as it explores the potential of NLP in identifying cognitive impairments, and specifically addresses the challenges of applying NLP to a relatively low-resource language like Portuguese. Explaining how embeddings are computed for languages with limited resources compared to English is an important contribution. 

The text would benefit from revision through shortening sections 1-3, compressing 4.1, and adding further explanations regarding the experiments. Additional clarification regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
