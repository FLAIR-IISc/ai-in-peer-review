The paper describes a novel application of largely existing representations, feature sets, and methods: namely, detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) in speech narratives. The nature of the problem, datasets, and domain are thoroughly described. While lacking some detail, the proposed solution and experiments are reasonable. Overall, I found the study interesting and informative.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) the demonstration that complex network features, particularly when enriched with word embeddings, can effectively discriminate between individuals with and without MCI; (2) the successful application of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to improve classification accuracy, achieving notable gains on the Cinderella and Arizona datasets; and (3) the provision of a comparative analysis demonstrating that complex network features outperform traditional linguistic features across the evaluated datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Application of Complex Networks:** The application of complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts is a valuable contribution. This approach offers a language-independent representation, potentially circumventing the challenges posed by disfluencies and grammatical errors common in the speech of individuals with MCI.
2.  **Effective Use of Ensemble and Multi-View Learning:** The integration of ensemble and multi-view learning demonstrates a sophisticated approach to combining different feature spaces (complex networks, linguistic features, and bag-of-words). The observed improvements in classification accuracy, particularly the 10% gain on the Cinderella dataset and the 4.71% improvement on the Arizona dataset, highlight the benefits of this technique.
3.  **Comparative Performance:** The consistent outperformance of complex network features compared to traditional linguistic features across all datasets is a significant finding. This suggests that the network-based approach captures relevant information about cognitive impairment that may be missed by more conventional linguistic analysis methods.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three distinct datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona Battery – strengthens the generalizability of the findings and provides a robust assessment of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Detail in Multi-View Learning:** The description of the multi-view learning process is insufficient. Critical details, such as the specific combination methods and weighting schemes employed, are missing, hindering reproducibility and a full understanding of the results.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Feature Engineering Choices:** The rationale behind certain feature engineering decisions is unclear. For example, the removal of disfluencies is not adequately justified, given that these may be informative markers of cognitive impairment. Similarly, the choice of term frequency over the more commonly used tf-idf weighting scheme for the bag-of-words representation requires further explanation.
3. **Limited Discussion of Word Embedding Generation:** The methodology for generating word embeddings requires more clarity. It is not explicitly stated whether these were pre-trained on the datasets used in the study or generated from external corpora. The impact of the embedding source on the results should be discussed.
4.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The lack of statistical significance testing to support the reported accuracy improvements weakens the conclusions drawn from the experiments. Without measures of statistical significance, it is difficult to determine whether the observed differences are genuine or due to chance.
5.  **Superficial Related Work:** While the related work section touches upon relevant research, it lacks depth. A more detailed discussion of existing methods, including their strengths and limitations, would provide valuable context for the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide a detailed description of the multi-view learning scheme employed, including the methods used to combine the different feature spaces and any weighting schemes applied?
2.  What was the rationale behind removing disfluencies from the transcripts? Have you considered exploring the potential of disfluencies as features for MCI detection?
3.  What dataset(s) were used to generate the word embeddings? If pre-trained embeddings were used, please provide details about their source and characteristics.
4.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) to support the reported accuracy improvements?
5.  Please elaborate on the challenges and limitations of using linguistic features for MCI detection, especially in the context of transcriptions containing disfluencies.
