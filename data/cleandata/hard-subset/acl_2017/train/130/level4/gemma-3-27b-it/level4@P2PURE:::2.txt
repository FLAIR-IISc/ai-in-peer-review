- Strengths:
This paper investigates the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) through analysis of speech transcripts from three distinct datasets. The authors describe a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words within the transcripts. Features are encoded based on various graph characteristics, encompassing lexical, syntactic, and other properties. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with several classifiers, demonstrating varying performance across the datasets. The work addresses a well-defined problem and employs appropriate datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper suffers from several drawbacks that hinder its clarity and impact.

1. The manuscript is difficult to follow due to numerous errors in English usage. A thorough review for grammar and spelling would significantly improve readability.
2. The core machine learning problem is not clearly articulated. Specifically, it is unclear what constitutes a single instance for classification; it appears each transcript is classified as either MCI or No MCI. If this is the case, the dataset descriptions should present data at the transcript level, rather than focusing on the studies that generated the transcripts. Information about patient age is irrelevant to the classification task and consumes significant space (two pages) describing dataset details that do not impact the classification process. Furthermore, inconsistencies in the reported numbers raise concerns – for example, section 4.1.1 states 326 participants were involved, while Table 1 shows fewer than 100 participants based on the reported gender distribution.
3. The motivation for enriching the graph in the described manner is unclear. A simpler approach – representing each word as a node connected by a measure of vector similarity, irrespective of co-occurrence – warrants consideration.
4. Despite utilizing datasets from the biomedical domain, the paper does not leverage any domain-specific tools or resources.
5. The unclear dataset class distribution makes it difficult to assess the suitability of accuracy as an evaluation metric. In any case, for this binary classification task, the F1-score would be a more informative metric.
6. Results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without any statistical significance testing to support the reported increments. Consequently, the practical significance of the observed gains remains unclear.
