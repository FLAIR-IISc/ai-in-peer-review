This paper explores the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives using complex network features enriched with word embeddings. The problem is well-motivated, and the datasets are described thoroughly. The study investigates a promising approach – leveraging network representations of text to overcome the challenges posed by disfluencies in speech from individuals with MCI. Overall, the work is interesting and demonstrates the potential of complex networks for this task.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex network analysis, coupled with word embeddings, to the problem of detecting MCI from speech transcripts. This provides a potentially robust approach, less reliant on traditional NLP pipelines sensitive to disfluencies.
2.  **Comparative Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive comparative evaluation of complex network features against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations across three datasets. This benchmarking highlights the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
3.  **Multi-view Learning Integration:** The exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques to combine different feature sets demonstrates a thoughtful approach to improving classification accuracy. The reported improvements, particularly on the Cinderella and ABCD datasets, are noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** Using complex networks as a core feature representation for MCI detection is a novel and potentially impactful idea. It offers a potentially more robust method compared to methods heavily reliant on parsing and syntactic analysis, which are sensitive to the grammatical errors often found in speech from individuals with MCI.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing three different datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD) and employing a range of classification algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes). The use of 5-fold cross-validation strengthens the validity of the results.
3.  **Clear Presentation of Datasets:** The datasets are described in detail, including participant demographics and the elicitation methods used. This transparency facilitates reproducibility and allows for comparison with other studies.
4.  **Demonstrated Performance Gains:** The results demonstrate that complex network features can achieve competitive and, in some cases, superior performance compared to traditional linguistic features, particularly when combined using multi-view learning. The 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset and the 4.71% improvement on the ABCD dataset are significant.
5.  **Practical Relevance:** The potential to automate MCI detection from speech transcripts is clinically relevant, as early diagnosis is crucial for effective intervention and management of cognitive decline.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** A major weakness is the absence of statistical significance testing. While accuracy scores are presented, there's no information about whether the observed differences between methods are statistically significant. This makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the superiority of any particular approach.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on Multi-view Learning:** The description of the multi-view learning approach is insufficient. Specifically, the weighting scheme or method used to combine the different feature spaces needs to be clearly explained.  How the "majority vote" was implemented requires clarification.
3.  **Ambiguity Regarding Word Embeddings:** The source of the word embeddings is unclear. Were they pre-trained on a large corpus or trained specifically on the three datasets used in this study? If the latter, the limited size of the datasets may raise concerns about the quality of the embeddings and their generalization ability.
4.  **Justification for Disfluency Removal:** The rationale for removing disfluencies from the transcripts is not fully convincing. While it acknowledges the potential impact of disfluencies on parsing, it doesn't address the possibility that these disfluencies themselves could be informative features of MCI.
5.  **Limited Exploration of Feature Engineering:** While the paper explores a variety of features, there's limited discussion of feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques. Investigating the most informative features could further improve performance.
6.  **Superficial Related Work Discussion:** The related work section, while covering relevant studies, lacks depth. A more detailed discussion of the specific methods used in previous work and a critical comparison with the proposed approach would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or ANOVA) to support your claims about the performance differences between the different methods?
2.  Please provide a detailed description of the multi-view learning approach, including the weighting scheme or method used to combine the feature spaces.
3.  Where were the word embeddings trained? If trained on the datasets used in this study, please discuss the potential limitations of this approach given the relatively small dataset sizes.
4.  What was the rationale behind removing disfluencies, and did you consider including them as features in your model?
5.  Did you explore any feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques? If so, what were the results?
