This paper proposes applying NLP techniques to speech transcripts – narratives and descriptions – to identify patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors report their ability to distinguish between healthy control participants and those with MCI (lines 141-144). However, the conclusion (lines 781-785) presents a more nuanced view, stating that “...accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%…. means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments.” Thus, the paper begins with a more optimistic tone than its conclusion, but overall, the message is encouraging and piques the reader’s curiosity about the methods employed.

The dataset comprises transcripts from 20 healthy participants and 20 control participants (totaling 40), and is currently limited to Portuguese. To improve accessibility, it would be beneficial to incorporate more technical detail into the paper, and to include at least one example of a short transcript translated into English. A partial network visualization with embeddings for this translated transcript would also be helpful for understanding the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper begins with a detailed introduction and review of relevant work. While comprehensive, some of the cited references are more broadly NLP background material and could be omitted – for example, (Salton 1989) in section 4.2.3. Other references appear tangentially related to the core topic, such as those concerning “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection in images” (lines 652-654), and could also be removed. Generally, section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) could be condensed. I suggest compressing the first five pages, focusing the review more tightly on the paper’s specific contributions, and expanding the discussion of technical innovation, including samples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cindarela narratives.

The relatively short narratives, especially within the ABCD dataset, raise questions about how word similarities were identified for constructing word embeddings. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289). However, the source of these continuous word representations is unclear – are they learned solely from the ABCD+Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora utilized? Similarly, lines 513-525 describe using sub-word level (n-grams) networks to generate word embeddings. Again, clarification is needed regarding the training data for these networks. Is there evidence that combining these two types of networks yields improved accuracy? Finally, what constitutes the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516, and from what source do they originate?

**General Discussion:**

Investigating the application of NLP to the discovery of cognitive impairments is a valuable endeavor, and this paper makes a relevant contribution. The focus on NLP for Portuguese is also noteworthy, as it’s important to address the challenges of computing embeddings for languages with fewer available resources compared to English. 

The text would benefit from revision through shortening sections 1-3, condensing section 4.1, and providing more detailed explanations of the experimental setup. Further clarification regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be beneficial.
