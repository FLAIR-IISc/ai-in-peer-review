This paper investigates the application of complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of automatically identifying MCI using this approach, achieving accuracies ranging from 60-85% across three datasets. While acknowledging the inherent difficulty in distinguishing between healthy and impaired individuals, the work presents a promising avenue for automated cognitive assessment, particularly valuable for languages with limited NLP resources like Portuguese. The primary contribution lies in successfully adapting complex network methods – typically used in text analysis – to the challenging domain of analyzing disfluent speech transcripts for cognitive decline detection. A secondary contribution is the application and evaluation of this technique to Portuguese language data, expanding the scope of similar research predominantly focused on English. Finally, the combination of network features with traditional linguistic features via ensemble and multi-view learning shows some performance improvement, indicating a potentially fruitful direction for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks:** The core strength of this work is the innovative application of complex network analysis to the problem of MCI detection from speech. This offers a potentially robust, language-independent approach that circumvents the limitations of traditional parsing-based methods susceptible to errors from disfluencies. The results demonstrate the viability of this approach, providing a compelling alternative or complement to existing techniques.
2. **Focus on a Resource-Limited Language:** The inclusion of Portuguese datasets is significant. Developing NLP tools for languages with fewer resources is a crucial challenge, and this work demonstrates a practical application of such tools for a clinically relevant task. This broader applicability enhances the impact of the research.
3. **Integration of Multiple Approaches:** The exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning, combining network features with linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, showcases a thoughtful attempt to leverage complementary information sources. The observed improvements in performance, particularly in the Cinderella dataset (10% improvement), suggest a worthwhile direction for further exploration.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overly Detailed and Discursive Introduction:** The initial sections (introduction and related work) are excessively lengthy and include references tangential to the core contribution. While providing context is valuable, the extensive background information obscures the novelty of the proposed method. A more focused introduction, concentrating specifically on the challenges of MCI detection and the limitations of existing linguistic approaches, would strengthen the paper.
2. **Lack of Clarity Regarding Embedding Training:** The description of word embedding creation is insufficient. The authors mention using both datasets and sub-word information (n-grams) for training, but the details are unclear. Specifying the source of the pre-trained embeddings (if any), the training parameters, and a justification for the chosen approach would significantly improve transparency and reproducibility. Specifically, clarifying whether the embeddings were trained *only* on the provided datasets or augmented with external corpora is critical.
3. **Limited Discussion of Network Properties and Their Relevance:** While the paper presents a range of topological network metrics, it lacks a deep discussion of *why* these specific metrics are expected to be informative for MCI detection. A theoretical justification linking these network characteristics to cognitive impairment would strengthen the interpretation of the results.
4. **Small Dataset Size:** The size of the datasets, particularly the Portuguese datasets (20 MCI and 20 control participants each), is relatively small. While the authors acknowledge this implicitly by highlighting the difficulty of achieving high accuracy, it limits the generalizability of the findings. Larger, more diverse datasets would be needed to confirm the robustness of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the training procedure for the word embeddings? Specifically, what external corpora, if any, were used? What were the hyperparameters used during training?
2. Can you elaborate on the rationale for selecting the specific network metrics used to characterize the complex networks? What theoretical connection do these metrics have to the underlying cognitive processes affected by MCI?
3. Given the relatively small dataset size, have you considered using data augmentation techniques or transfer learning approaches to improve the generalizability of the model?
