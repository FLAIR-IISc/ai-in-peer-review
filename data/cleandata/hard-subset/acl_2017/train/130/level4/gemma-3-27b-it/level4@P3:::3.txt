## Review for ACL 2017 – Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

This paper explores the application of complex network features, combined with machine learning, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The study is well-motivated, addressing an important problem in early dementia diagnosis, and utilizes a relatively novel approach of enriching complex networks with word embeddings. The datasets are described thoroughly, and the experimental setup appears reasonable. Overall, I found the study interesting and potentially valuable.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that complex network representations of speech transcripts, coupled with machine learning classifiers, can effectively distinguish between individuals with and without MCI. This provides a promising alternative to traditional linguistic feature-based approaches, particularly given the challenges posed by disfluencies in patient speech.
2. **Enrichment of Networks with Word Embeddings:** The authors enhance the discriminatory power of the network representation by incorporating semantic information via word embeddings. This allows the model to capture relationships between words beyond simple co-occurrence, potentially improving performance on short, often fragmented, patient narratives.
3. **Multi-View and Ensemble Learning:** The authors explore combining different feature sets (complex networks, linguistic features, bag-of-words) using multi-view learning and ensemble methods, achieving improved accuracy compared to individual feature sets or classifiers. This suggests a synergistic benefit from integrating different types of information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Leveraging complex network analysis for MCI detection is a relatively unexplored area, providing a fresh perspective compared to established methods based on hand-crafted linguistic features. The combination with word embeddings further enhances the novelty.
2. **Robustness to Disfluencies:** The authors explicitly address the issue of disfluencies, a common problem in patient speech, by employing a method that is less reliant on perfect grammatical structure than traditional parsing-based approaches. This is a significant advantage in a real-world clinical setting.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on three different datasets – one English and two Portuguese – increasing the generalizability of the findings. The use of multiple classification algorithms and comparison against baseline linguistic features provides a strong evaluation framework.
4. **Performance Gains through Combination:** The results demonstrate that combining complex networks with other feature types, through multi-view learning and ensemble methods, leads to measurable improvements in classification accuracy. This highlights the value of integrating diverse sources of information.
5. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of MCI diagnosis and the potential role of automated speech analysis in improving early detection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper presents accuracy results without providing any statistical tests to determine the significance of the observed differences. Without such tests, it is difficult to confidently assess whether the improvements reported are genuine or due to chance.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Multi-View Learning:** The description of the multi-view learning approach is too brief. More details on the specific combination strategies used and the rationale behind them would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Justification for Feature Choices:** While the authors mention the challenges with linguistic features in the presence of disfluencies, a more detailed justification for the specific linguistic features chosen, and why they were retained despite these challenges, would be helpful.
4. **Word Embedding Source Ambiguity:** It remains unclear whether the word embeddings were pre-trained on the datasets used in the study, or on external corpora. This is a critical detail as the quality and relevance of the embeddings can significantly impact performance.
5. **Missing Discussion of Disfluency Handling:** The decision to remove disfluencies is not fully explained. While acknowledging they impact parsing, the potential diagnostic value of these very features (repetitions, hesitations) in relation to MCI is not discussed.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:** Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) to support the claims of performance improvement?
2. **Multi-View Learning Details:** Please elaborate on the specific methods used for multi-view learning. How were the different classifiers combined, and what criteria were used for weighting their contributions?
3. **Word Embedding Training Data:** On what corpus were the word embeddings trained? If using pre-trained embeddings, please specify the source.
4. **Disfluency Rationale:** What was the rationale for removing disfluencies from the transcripts? Have you considered exploring disfluency-based features as potential indicators of MCI?
5. **Parameter Tuning:** Could you elaborate on the parameter tuning process used for the classifiers and the word embedding model?
