- Strengths:
This paper explores the application of NLP techniques to speech transcripts – specifically narratives and descriptions – to identify patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors report the ability to differentiate between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141-144). However, the conclusion (lines 781-785) tempers this claim, stating that accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% indicates difficulty in reliably distinguishing between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments. While the initial presentation is more optimistic, the overall message is encouraging and motivates further investigation into the methods employed. 
The dataset comprises transcripts from 20 healthy controls and 20 MCI patients (total of 40 participants), and is currently limited to Portuguese speakers. Enhancing the paper with greater technical detail and including at least one translated example transcript (to English) would be beneficial. A visual illustration, such as a partial network diagram displaying word embeddings for a sample transcript, would also aid understanding.

- Weaknesses:
The paper begins with a comprehensive introduction and literature review. However, some cited works present general NLP background information (e.g., Salton 1989, section 4.2.3) and could be omitted to streamline the paper. Furthermore, references to unrelated areas like sentiment classification and pedestrian detection (lines 652-654) should be removed. In general, section 4.2.3 (lines 608-621) could be significantly condensed. I recommend compressing the first five pages to focus more tightly on the core contribution and providing a more detailed exploration of the technical innovations, including English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.
The brevity of the narratives, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raises concerns about the reliability of identifying word similarities for constructing meaningful word embeddings. The authors describe generating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289), but the source of these continuous representations is unclear. Were they learned solely from the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora utilized? Similarly, lines 513-525 describe the use of sub-word level (n-gram) networks for generating word embeddings; the training data for these networks also requires clarification. It is unclear whether the combination of these two network types demonstrably improves accuracy, and the origin of the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516 should be specified. 

- General Discussion:
Investigating the potential of NLP to aid in the detection of cognitive impairments is a valuable pursuit, and this paper represents a worthwhile contribution in that regard. The focus on Portuguese NLP is also noteworthy, given the relatively limited resources available for the language compared to English. It is important to detail how embeddings are computed for lower-resource languages. 
The text would benefit from revision through shortening sections 1-3, condensing 4.1, and providing more comprehensive explanations of the experimental setup. Additional clarification regarding the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
