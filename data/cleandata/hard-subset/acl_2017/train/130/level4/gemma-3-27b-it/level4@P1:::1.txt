This paper proposes to apply NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) in order to identify patients with MCI (mild cognitive impairment, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors demonstrate the ability to distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141-144). While the initial presentation is optimistic, the conclusion (lines 781-785) acknowledges the inherent difficulty of this task (“… accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% …. means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments”), making the overall message encouraging and stimulating further investigation.

The datasets used consist of transcriptions from 20 healthy controls and 20 MCI patients, primarily in Portuguese. While this presents a challenge for non-Portuguese speakers, the work’s contribution to NLP for a resource-scarce language is valuable. To enhance understanding, the paper could benefit from more detailed technical explanations and the inclusion of at least one example of a short transcript translated into English, alongside a sample network visualization with embeddings for that transcript.

**Strengths:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to the challenging task of MCI detection from speech transcripts. This approach offers a lightweight and language-independent representation, sidestepping the complexities of parsing disfluent speech often present in patient data.

A significant strength is the evaluation across three datasets – DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery – providing robustness and demonstrating applicability to varying data characteristics. The outperformance of complex network features compared to traditional linguistic features across these datasets further supports the method’s efficacy. 

The combination of classifiers through ensemble and multi-view learning is also a notable strength, demonstrating an improvement of up to 10% on the Cinderella dataset and 4.71% on the Arizona dataset compared to the best individual classifier. This suggests a synergistic effect when integrating different modeling perspectives.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper’s introductory sections (1-3) and related work review are somewhat lengthy and could be streamlined. Some cited references, while relevant to NLP generally (e.g., Salton 1989), don’t directly contribute to the core argument. Similarly, references to areas like “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection” (lines 652-654) feel tangential and could be omitted. A more focused review of directly relevant literature would improve clarity and conciseness. Specifically, sections 4.1 and 4.2.3 could be condensed.

The relatively short narratives, particularly in the ABCD dataset, raise questions about the effectiveness of word embedding generation. The methodology described for creating word-level networks from continuous word representations (lines 272-289) needs further clarification. The source of these continuous word representations is unclear – are they learned solely from the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or are external corpora utilized? The use of sub-word level (n-grams) networks for embedding generation (lines 513-525) also warrants more explanation. Is the combination of these two network types demonstrably superior, and what constitutes the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned (line 516)?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What external corpora, if any, were used to train the word embeddings?
2.  Can the authors provide a justification for the use of both full-word and sub-word level networks in generating word embeddings? Is there empirical evidence supporting the benefit of this combination?
3.  Could the authors provide more detail regarding the specific characteristics of the “out-of-vocabulary words” encountered and how they were handled?

**General Discussion:**

This work addresses an important problem – leveraging NLP to aid in the early detection of cognitive impairments. The application of these techniques to Portuguese language data is particularly noteworthy, given the relative scarcity of resources compared to English. The paper demonstrates a promising approach, though further refinement of the methodology and more in-depth analysis of the embedding generation process are warranted.

The text requires revision to enhance clarity and conciseness. Specifically, sections 1-3 should be streamlined, sections 4.1 compressed, and more detailed explanations of the experiments provided. A brief explanation of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would be beneficial.
