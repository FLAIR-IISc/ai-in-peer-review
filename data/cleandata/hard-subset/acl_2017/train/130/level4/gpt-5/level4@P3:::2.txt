Summary and contributions
This paper investigates automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network (CN) representations enriched with word embeddings. Transcripts are modeled as word co-occurrence graphs; additional edges are added when word vectors exceed a cosine similarity threshold, aiming to mitigate sparsity in short, disfluent texts. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD), comparing CN/CNE features to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW) using several classifiers, and experimenting with ensemble and multi-view combinations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that embedding-enriched word adjacency networks can distinguish MCI from controls across English and Portuguese datasets, sometimes outperforming traditional linguistic and BoW features.
2) A practical modeling choice to enrich co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based edges to better represent short, noisy transcripts without heavy reliance on parsers.
3) A comparison of single-view vs multi-view and ensemble strategies across three feature spaces, with reported gains on two datasets.

Strengths
- Relevance and scope. The paper targets an important, clinically relevant problem and evaluates on multiple datasets spanning two languages and differing elicitation protocols. The focus on representations less dependent on syntactic parsing is well motivated by disfluency in neuropsychological speech.
- Language-independent, lightweight representation. Modeling transcripts as co-occurrence graphs enriched with embeddings is attractive for low-resource languages and noisy transcripts, where reliable parsing is difficult. Using subword embeddings to address OOV is also a practical choice.
- Empirical coverage. The study compares several classifiers and feature sets, and considers ensemble and multi-view combinations. The reported gains on Cinderella (up to 10% absolute improvement with ensemble/multi-view) and the best accuracy on ABCD (80% with multi-view) suggest the approach is competitive with established baselines.
- Movement toward automation. The use of automatic sentence segmentation (DeepBond) and removal of filled pauses indicates progress toward a fully automated pipeline, in contrast to prior work that relied on extensive manual transcript revision.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation. The paper is difficult to read due to numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology. Several sections (especially dataset descriptions) are lengthy yet omit key details needed to understand the machine learning setup. A thorough language and organization pass is needed.
- Task framing and dataset statistics. The unit of classification appears to be a single transcript labeled as MCI vs control, but this is not stated clearly. Per-dataset transcript counts, class balance, and fold stratification are missing. The demographics tables mix study-level facts (e.g., “326 people diagnosed with probable AD”) with this paper’s subset, causing confusion (e.g., male/female counts do not match text). Age and education are not used as features, yet occupy space that could report crucial statistics such as transcripts per class, tokens per transcript after preprocessing, and OOV rates.
- Methodological motivation and ablations. The rationale for combining co-occurrence edges with embedding-based similarity edges is only partially justified. Why not a pure similarity graph? What about weighted edges instead of binary thresholds? There is no ablation comparing (i) co-occurrence only, (ii) similarity-only, and (iii) hybrid with different thresholds, or sensitivity analyses for the threshold choice. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the enrichment step.
- Evaluation methodology. Accuracy alone is insufficient, especially if classes are imbalanced (class distributions are not reported). Precision, recall, F1 (macro and per-class), ROC-AUC, and confusion matrices are needed. Reporting to four decimal places on small datasets is misleading; no statistical significance testing (e.g., paired tests across folds) is provided despite multiple comparisons. Hyperparameters (including the similarity threshold) appear to be tuned to maximize accuracy without nested cross-validation, risking optimistic estimates.
- Data leakage and reproducibility risks. The training corpora for word embeddings are not specified, nor whether embeddings were trained separately per language and whether the transcripts were included (which would induce leakage). Multi-view and ensemble procedures are under-specified (e.g., probability calibration, voting scheme, base learner tuning). Feature dimensionality is high relative to sample sizes, yet there is no feature selection, regularization, or variance control; this raises overfitting concerns. Preprocessing differs across datasets (e.g., a manually revised Cinderella variant versus an automatic version), challenging comparability.

Questions to authors
- Task and data:
  - What is the exact unit of classification (one transcript per sample)? Please provide, for each dataset, the number of transcripts per class, total tokens per transcript after preprocessing, and class balance within each cross-validation fold. Were folds stratified by class?
  - The Cookie Theft section mentions 326 probable AD and 21 possible AD participants but states you used 43 MCI transcripts. Please reconcile these numbers and the male/female counts in Table 1 with the subset actually used here.
- Embeddings and graphs:
  - On what corpora did you train the subword embeddings, and separately for English vs Portuguese? Did the training corpora include any of the evaluation transcripts? What were OOV rates per dataset?
  - Why choose a hybrid co-occurrence-plus-similarity graph rather than a pure similarity graph? Can you report an ablation comparing co-occurrence only, similarity only, and hybrid (with weighted edges), and a sensitivity analysis for the similarity threshold?
- Evaluation:
  - Please report precision, recall, F1 (macro and by class), ROC-AUC, and confusion matrices for all settings, and provide statistical significance tests for claimed improvements. Use nested cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning (including threshold choice) to avoid optimistic bias.
  - Given the small sample sizes, can you limit decimal precision and provide confidence intervals over repeated runs with different random seeds?
- Modeling details and reproducibility:
  - How were stopword lists defined per language? Were graphs built per transcript or aggregated? Were edge weights used anywhere? How many features did each view have after preprocessing? Was any feature scaling or selection applied?
  - For multi-view learning, how were classifiers combined (hard vs soft voting; probability calibration)? Were base learner hyperparameters tuned per view? Please release code and detailed configuration to enable replication.
- Analysis:
  - Can you provide an error analysis highlighting typical false positives/negatives? Which CN metrics contributed most to discrimination? Are there qualitative differences between MCI and control graphs that you can visualize?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an impactful problem with an appealing, language-agnostic representation and encouraging preliminary results across multiple datasets. However, the current presentation leaves key methodological and evaluation details unclear, and the empirical evidence lacks stronger baselines, ablations, and statistically grounded analysis. With substantial revisions to improve clarity, add missing details, strengthen the evaluation, and justify design choices, the work could make a useful empirical contribution.
