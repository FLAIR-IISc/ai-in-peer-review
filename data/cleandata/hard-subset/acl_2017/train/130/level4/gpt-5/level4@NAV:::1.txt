Review
- Strengths:
This paper explores applying NLP techniques to speech transcripts (both narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI; ICD-10 F06.7). The authors state that they can differentiate healthy controls from individuals with MCI (lines 141–144). Yet in the conclusion (lines 781–785) they report “accuracy ranging from 60% to 85%,” acknowledging that distinguishing healthy subjects from those with cognitive deficits is challenging. While the opening is more upbeat than the closing, the overall take-home is encouraging and motivates the reader to seek more methodological detail.
The released corpus covers only 20 healthy patients and 20 control participants (20+20) and is inaccessible to readers who do not speak Portuguese. The paper would benefit from additional technical detail, including at least one short transcript translated into English and, ideally, a (partial) example network with embeddings derived from that transcript.

- Weaknesses:
The manuscript begins with an extensive introduction and literature review. Some citations are general NLP background that could be omitted (e.g., Salton 1989 in section 4.2.3). Other references are tangential (e.g., “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection in images,” lines 652–654) and could also be removed. In general, lines 608–621 (section 4.2.3) can be streamlined. I suggest compressing the first five pages to tightly focus the review on the paper’s topic, and allocating more space to the technical contributions, including English translations of ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.
Given the relatively short Portuguese narratives—particularly in the ABCD dataset—it is unclear how word similarities were established to construct word embeddings. Lines 272–289 indicate that word-level networks are built from continuous word representations. What data were used to learn these continuous representations: only ABCD + Cinderella, or external corpora as well? Lines 513–525 say subword (n-gram) networks were used to produce word embeddings. Again, what training sources were employed? Is there evidence that combining these two types of networks improves accuracy? Also, what are the “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516), and where do they originate?

- General Discussion:
Investigating how NLP can assist in detecting cognitive impairments is valuable, and from this standpoint the paper is compelling. The focus on Portuguese NLP is another strength; it is important to clarify how embeddings are obtained for a language with comparatively fewer resources than English.
The text needs revision: shorten sections 1–3, condense 4.1, and provide more detail on the experimental setup and results. Some explanation of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription guidelines would also help.
