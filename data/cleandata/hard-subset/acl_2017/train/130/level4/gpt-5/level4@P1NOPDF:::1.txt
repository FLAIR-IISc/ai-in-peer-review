- Strengths:
This paper applies NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 F06.7). The authors report that they can distinguish healthy controls from patients with MCI (lines 141–144). However, in the conclusion (lines 781–785), they state that “accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% … means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments.” The opening is therefore more optimistic than the conclusion, but the overall message is encouraging and motivates the reader to look for more methodological detail. The released dataset includes only 20 patients and 20 controls (20+20) and is in Portuguese, which limits accessibility for non-Portuguese readers. It would help to add more technical details and include at least one short transcript with an English translation, and perhaps a (partial) sample network with embeddings for that transcript.

- Weaknesses:
The paper begins with a lengthy introduction and literature review. Some citations are general NLP background and can be omitted (e.g., Salton 1989 in Section 4.2.3). Other references (e.g., “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection in images,” lines 652–654) are not directly relevant and could also be removed. Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) can be shortened. Overall, I suggest compressing the first five pages, focusing the review strictly on the paper’s topic, and expanding the technological contributions, including English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.

Given the relatively short Portuguese narratives—especially in the ABCD dataset—the paper should explain how word similarities were learned to construct embeddings. In lines 272–289, the authors state that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations. What is the training source for these representations: only ABCD+Cinderella, or were external corpora used? Lines 513–525 indicate that subword (n-gram) networks were used to generate word embeddings. Again, what is the training source? Is there evidence that combining the two types of networks improves accuracy? Also, please clarify what “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516) refers to and where they come from.

- General Discussion:
It is important to investigate how NLP can help detect cognitive impairments; from this perspective, the paper is interesting. The focus on Portuguese is also valuable; please explain how embeddings are computed for a language with fewer resources than English. The text would benefit from revision: shorten Sections 1–3, compress 4.1, and add more detail about the experiments. Some clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
