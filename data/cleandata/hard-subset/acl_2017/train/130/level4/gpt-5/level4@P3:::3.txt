Summary and contributions
This paper investigates automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding-based similarity edges. The authors compute topological features from these enriched networks and compare them against linguistic features and bag-of-words (BoW) baselines across three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD recall). They also explore ensembles and multi-view learning (combining classifiers trained on different feature sets).

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that complex-network features enriched with word embeddings (CNE) can be competitive with, and sometimes outperform, traditional linguistic and BoW features for MCI detection in noisy, short, and cross-lingual transcripts.
2) A cross-dataset, cross-language evaluation on three clinically relevant corpora, including Portuguese datasets that are less represented in prior work, and an analysis of ensemble/multi-view combinations.
3) A practical, parser-light pipeline intended to be more robust to disfluencies and resource-poor settings.

Novelty is moderate (the components are mostly known), but the application and comparative evaluation in this clinical context are valuable.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and clinical relevance: The paper articulates why early MCI detection matters and why language-based methods are attractive, particularly in low-resource or noisy-transcript settings where dependency on robust parsing is problematic.
- Cross-language, multi-dataset evaluation: Using English and Brazilian Portuguese datasets with differing task types and transcript lengths strengthens the claim of generality and language independence. The inclusion of Portuguese clinical data is a notable contribution.
- Lightweight representation: Modeling transcripts as co-occurrence networks enriched via embeddings provides an alternative to parser-derived features and appears robust to short texts when embeddings are used.
- Consistent comparative baselines: The study reports results across several standard classifiers and multiple feature spaces (CNE, linguistic metrics, BoW), and explores ensemble and multi-view strategies. Gains reported for multi-view/ensemble combinations on Cinderella and ABCD are encouraging.
- Practical insight: The finding that simple linear SVM with BoW performs strongly in at least one setting underscores the importance of comparing to well-tuned, strong baselines.

Weaknesses
- Missing methodological details: Key pieces are under-specified. The multi-view learning scheme is described conceptually but not operationally (e.g., exact training/combination protocol, whether probabilities or votes are used, and how per-view weights—if any—are learned). The paper implies both majority voting and possible regression-based weighting but does not clarify what was actually used. Similarly, the definition and selection of the “threshold parameter” for embedding similarity edges require detail (search range, selection procedure, whether tuned within folds).
- Word embeddings training data: It is unclear on which corpora the embeddings were trained (only on the small task datasets or on larger external corpora), which model and tokenization were used for each language, and how OOV handling via subword models was integrated. Given dataset sizes, in-domain training seems risky; external corpora would introduce domain mismatch and potential leakage if not carefully controlled. These choices strongly affect performance and reproducibility.
- Treatment of disfluencies: The rationale for removing filled pauses, repetitions, and short false starts is not convincing in this clinical setting, where such phenomena are often diagnostic. At minimum, an ablation comparing “with vs. without disfluencies” is important. The current preprocessing may remove valuable signal and limits the conclusions about language-based markers of MCI.
- Evaluation and statistical analysis: Only accuracy is reported. Given potential class imbalance and clinical relevance, metrics such as precision/recall, F1, sensitivity/specificity, and AUC should be included. Confidence intervals or statistical significance tests are needed to support claims of improvement (especially the reported 2–10% gains). Without significance testing, it is difficult to assess robustness across folds. Confusion matrices would help interpret error patterns.
- Clarity and organization: Several sections need editing for grammar and clarity. Dataset descriptions are sometimes inconsistent or confusing (e.g., segmentation methods, counts, and the “first/second/third” references). The Related Work section summarizes datasets and tasks but often omits methodological detail and comparative positioning. Claims such as “CN outperform linguistic features in all datasets” are contradicted by Table 6 (where linguistic features with RBF SVM reach 0.725 vs. 0.675 for CNE in one condition), and should be tempered.

Questions to authors
1) Embeddings: What corpora (size, domain, language) were used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were pre-trained vectors (e.g., FastText) used? Please report training parameters beyond defaults, tokenization, and handling of multiword items.
2) Multi-view learning: Precisely describe the combination scheme. Were per-view probabilities averaged or were votes tallied? Were view weights learned (e.g., via logistic regression stacking), or fixed? How was stacking cross-validated to avoid leakage?
3) Threshold selection: How were cosine similarity thresholds (0.7 for Cookie Theft; 0.4 for Cinderella/ABCD) chosen? Was the selection nested within cross-validation folds?
4) Disfluencies: Why were filled pauses, repetitions, and short false starts removed? Can you provide results with and without these disfluencies to quantify their impact?
5) BoW weighting: Why use term frequency rather than tf-idf? Could you report tf-idf results, especially since linear SVM+BoW already performs strongly?
6) Metrics and significance: Please report additional metrics (F1, sensitivity, specificity, AUC) and conduct appropriate statistical tests (e.g., McNemar’s test, paired bootstrap) to assess significance of improvements.
7) Dataset details: For each dataset, please provide exact class counts, mean/median transcript lengths, and segmentation method. For Cookie Theft, how many controls vs. MCI? Are demographics balanced across groups? Were any demographic covariates controlled for?
8) Reproducibility: Will code, feature extraction scripts, and trained models be released? This would help verify the network construction (e.g., window size, directed vs. undirected edges) and topological feature computation.

Additional comments (minor/editorial)
- Improve grammar and clarity throughout; several sentences require rephrasing (e.g., “impacting in parsing results,” “as it a lightweight,” inconsistent use of “dataset(s) consisted of,” etc.).
- Clearly name the three datasets early and avoid “first/second/third.” Define ABCD on first mention and provide links/citations for DementiaBank, Western Aphasia Battery “Picnic” picture, and transcription norms.
- Clarify whether networks are directed, weighted, and the co-occurrence window size used. Ensure feature definitions are precise (e.g., “shortest path length” vs. “diameter”).
- Align claims with tables; avoid overgeneralizations such as “outperform in all datasets.”
- Consider adding a short error analysis and an ablation (CNE vs. CN; with vs. without embeddings; with vs. without disfluencies).

Overall evaluation
An interesting application with promising results and practical appeal. With clearer methodological specification, stronger evaluation (including significance), and a more rigorous discussion of design choices (embeddings and disfluency handling), this work could make a solid contribution.
