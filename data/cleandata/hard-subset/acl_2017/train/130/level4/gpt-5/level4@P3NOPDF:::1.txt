Summary
This paper investigates whether NLP features derived from Portuguese speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) can help identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (ICD-10 F06.7). The authors build word-level and subword-level networks to obtain embeddings and report classification performance distinguishing MCI from healthy controls, with accuracy reported between 60% and 85%. The topic is timely and clinically relevant, and focusing on Portuguese is valuable.

Strengths
- Important problem: using language to detect cognitive impairment is impactful and clinically motivated.
- Linguistic focus on Portuguese: addressing a relatively low-resource language increases the paper’s relevance for multilingual NLP.
- Encouraging preliminary results: the reported accuracies suggest signal in the data, warranting further exploration.
- The manuscript raises meaningful methodological questions about embeddings in small, domain-specific corpora.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Inconsistent messaging: The abstract/early sections (lines 141–144) emphasize successful discrimination, while the conclusion (lines 781–785) downplays separability (“accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% … means that it is not easy to distinguish…”). Please reconcile these statements and calibrate claims accordingly.
- Limited dataset and accessibility: The dataset appears to include only 20+20 participants, and all transcripts are in Portuguese. The small size raises concerns about generalizability; the lack of English examples limits accessibility for a broad readership.
- Insufficient methodological detail on embeddings: It is unclear what corpora were used to train continuous word representations (lines 272–289). Similarly, for subword-level networks (lines 513–525), the training source and procedure are not specified. The definition and origin of “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516) are also unclear.
- Overlong background and off-topic citations: Some references are background-only (e.g., Salton 1989 in Section 4.2.3) or not directly related to the task (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection; lines 652–654). Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) and earlier sections could be more concise and focused on the contributions.
- Experimental clarity: The paper would benefit from clearer descriptions of experimental setup, including data splits, evaluation protocol, and ablations demonstrating the value of word vs. subword networks and their combination.

Requests for clarification
- Embedding training data: Were word/subword embeddings trained solely on the ABCD and Cinderella transcripts, or were external Portuguese corpora used? If external data were used, please specify sources, sizes, preprocessing, and domain relevance.
- Subword modeling: How exactly were subword (n-gram) networks constructed and used to generate embeddings (lines 513–525)? What hyperparameters and tokenization settings were used?
- OOV handling: What constitutes “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516)? OOV relative to which vocabulary (transcript-only, external corpus, or pretrained model)? How were OOVs embedded?
- Comparative performance: Do combined word+subword networks outperform word-only or subword-only networks? Please provide ablation results and statistical significance where feasible.
- Data details: Please confirm participant counts (20+20), label distribution, and whether any demographic variables (e.g., age, education) were controlled or balanced, given their known effects on linguistic performance.

Suggestions for revision
- Streamline the introduction and related work: Compress Sections 1–3 and Section 4.1, removing broad NLP background and tangential references (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) to focus on cognitive impairment detection, clinical speech analysis, and embeddings in low-resource settings. Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) can be shortened.
- Provide concrete, accessible examples: Include at least one short transcript excerpt with an English translation and, if possible, a schematic or partial visualization of the corresponding network/embeddings. This will help readers understand the proposed features and their interpretability.
- Detail the embedding pipeline: Clearly describe training data sources, preprocessing, tokenization, hyperparameters, and dimensionality. If using external Portuguese corpora, justify their selection and discuss domain mismatch. Address how limited in-domain data affects embedding quality and what mitigation strategies were used (e.g., subword models, transfer learning).
- Strengthen experimental transparency: Specify data splits, cross-validation or hold-out protocols, performance metrics beyond accuracy (e.g., precision/recall/F1), and any regularization or calibration steps. Include ablations that isolate the contributions of each component.
- Align claims with results: Ensure that the abstract, main text, and conclusion present a consistent, measured interpretation of the reported accuracies and limitations.

Overall assessment
This is a promising study addressing an important clinical application in a low-resource language. The empirical results are encouraging, but the paper currently needs clearer methodological detail, streamlined related work, accessible examples, and consistent framing of findings. With these revisions—especially clarifying embedding sources, OOV handling, and providing ablations—the work would be substantially strengthened and more informative for both NLP and clinical audiences.
