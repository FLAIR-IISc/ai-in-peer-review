Summary and contributions
This paper applies mostly existing representations and methods—complex networks (CNs), word embeddings, BoW, and standard classifiers—to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The problem, datasets, and clinical context are well motivated, and the experiments are generally reasonable.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) Demonstration that complex network features enriched with word embeddings (CNE) can effectively distinguish MCI from controls in English and Brazilian Portuguese transcripts, and in several cases outperform traditional linguistic features.
2) Empirical evidence that combining feature spaces via multi-view learning and classifier ensembling can further improve performance, yielding notable gains on the Cinderella and ABCD datasets.
3) A cross-lingual evaluation on three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Cinderella narratives, and ABCD), including automated sentence segmentation for a more realistic, scalable pipeline.

Strengths
- Language- and resource-lean representation: Modeling transcripts as CNs and enriching with embeddings offers a lightweight, language-independent approach that avoids dependence on robust parsers, which is especially useful for noisy, disfluent speech and under-resourced languages.
- Consistent empirical gains for CNE: Across datasets, CNE generally yields higher accuracy than plain CNs and often outperforms linguistic features. This supports the claim that network enrichment remedies sparsity/linearity issues in short texts.
- Multi-view and ensemble improvements: Combining classifiers and feature views yields measurable improvements (e.g., +10% on Cinderella and 4.7% on ABCD over the best single classifier), indicating complementary information across representations.
- Cross-dataset, cross-language study: Evaluating on English and Portuguese datasets (including both picture descriptions and narrative recall) strengthens external validity and showcases adaptability to different task formats and preprocessing assumptions.

Weaknesses
- Missing methodological details: The paper omits crucial specifics of the multi-view learning scheme (how views are combined, weighting/learning of view contributions, training protocol) and does not fully specify the “linguistic features” used in each language. The source and size of the data used to train word embeddings are also unclear; the described datasets seem too small to learn reliable embeddings without external corpora.
- Questionable removal of disfluencies: Filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions are often informative for MCI detection. The rationale for removing them (while citing disfluency as a challenge for parsing) needs justification, given that CN/CNE and BoW do not inherently require grammatical text.
- Bag-of-Words choices: The decision to use term frequency rather than tf–idf is not justified, despite tf–idf being a standard and often stronger baseline for text classification.
- Lack of statistical significance testing: No significance tests are reported to support claims of superiority. Given small sample sizes and modest absolute gains, statistical validation is important.
- Superficial related work: The related work section summarizes datasets and outcomes but does not sufficiently detail methods or compare them to the current approach (feature types, classifiers, preprocessing, strengths/limitations), making it hard to position the contribution.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in the rebuttal)
- Multi-view learning: What exact scheme was used? How were views combined (e.g., majority voting, stacking, weighted voting)? If weights were learned, by which regression method and with what validation protocol?
- Word embeddings: On what data were embeddings trained (the study datasets only or external corpora)? Please specify language resources, corpus sizes, and training parameters beyond defaults. How were OOV tokens handled in practice?
- Disfluency removal: Why were filled pauses, false starts, repetitions, etc. removed? Did you evaluate models with and without these phenomena? Given their known diagnostic value, please justify or provide ablations.
- BoW weighting: Why use tf rather than tf–idf? Did you compare tf–idf and, if so, with what results?
- Significance: Are the reported improvements statistically significant? Please provide appropriate tests (e.g., approximate randomization, bootstrap, McNemar) for key comparisons.
- Threshold parameter: Please define the “threshold parameter” precisely (cosine similarity threshold for adding embedding-based edges) and describe how it was tuned per dataset.

Additional comments and editorial suggestions
- Abstract: Shorten; clarify phrasing (e.g., “impacting the parsing results”), and fix “as it a lightweight and language independent representation.”
- Clarify datasets early: Explicitly name the three datasets (Cookie Theft/DementiaBank, Cinderella, ABCD), their languages, and segmentation procedures; avoid “first/second/third” references and inconsistencies (e.g., “manually segmented of the DementiaBank and Cinderella”).
- Clarify clinical rates: “15% a year” should be stated unambiguously (e.g., annual conversion rate to AD of approximately 15% for amnestic MCI, vs. 1–2% in the general population).
- BoW and disfluencies: Explain why BoW is expected to suffer from disfluencies; a brief rationale would help.
- “Best scenario”: Clarify what “the best scenario for application of network features” means (e.g., dataset characteristics, preprocessing).
- Citations/links: Provide references or links for DementiaBank; the Western Aphasia Battery’s Picnic picture; the Wechsler Logical Memory subtest; and the transcription norms (NURC/SP). Ensure missing citations are added (e.g., line 172 in the draft).
- Terminology and acronyms: Define “PLN” on first use. Use consistent citation style per ACL guidelines (e.g., “Aluísio et al. (2016) used…”).
- Preprocessing description: Reframe the normalization pipeline; lemmatization is not necessarily “the last step,” and other common steps (e.g., lowercasing) could be acknowledged. Clarify that lemmatization was omitted due to POS tagging challenges with disfluent transcripts.
- Network features: Confirm whether “shortest path” in feature descriptions (e.g., diameter; average shortest path length) is consistent, and clarify any ambiguity (e.g., feature numbering in the draft).
- Tables/captions: Ensure dataset statistics match captions (demographics vs. additional stats promised). Correct minor grammar (e.g., “consisted of” → “consist of”).
- Style/grammar: Improve phrasing such as “multidisciplinary team with psychiatrists” → “a multidisciplinary team consisting of psychiatrists…”, and add missing commas (e.g., “In this work, term frequency was…”). Add missing period (line 735 in the draft).
- Supplementary material: The “Linguistic Features” section should be expanded for clarity in the main paper; if relying on supplementary material, ensure ACL page-limit compliance and include a concise summary in the main text.

Overall evaluation
Interesting and informative study with a resource-lean, cross-lingual approach and solid empirical signals for CNE and multi-view learning. However, acceptance should be contingent on clarifying the methodology (especially multi-view and embeddings), justifying preprocessing choices, strengthening baselines (tf–idf), and reporting statistical significance.
