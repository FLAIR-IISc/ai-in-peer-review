Summary
This paper investigates automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings, and classifying topological network features. The approach is motivated as a lightweight, parser-independent alternative that is more robust to disfluencies common in neuropsychological narratives. The authors evaluate on three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), and Cinderella and ABCD (Brazilian Portuguese), and compare complex networks (with and without embedding enrichment) against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words, using multiple classifiers as well as ensemble and multi-view combinations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Application and empirical validation of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for MCI detection from speech transcripts, showing competitive or superior accuracy to traditional linguistic features on English and Portuguese datasets, without relying on heavy parsing or manual transcript cleanup.
2) Cross-lingual evaluation across three datasets with different narrative types and preprocessing regimes, including scenarios with automatic sentence segmentation; demonstration that multi-view/ensemble combinations can improve over single feature spaces.
3) Analysis indicating that embedding-enriched networks mitigate the sparsity/linearity problems of short transcripts, yielding meaningful topological signals for classification.

Strengths
- Relevance and practicality: The focus on parser-light, language-agnostic features addresses real constraints in clinical transcripts (disfluencies, agrammaticality, resource-poor languages). The cross-lingual evaluation (English and Portuguese) strengthens the case for broader applicability.
- Empirical evidence that network enrichment helps: Across datasets, enriched networks (CNE) generally outperform plain co-occurrence networks and compare favorably to linguistic features (e.g., Cookie Theft: CNE-RBF 62.6% vs LM 58.1%; Cinderella: CNE-RBF 65.0% vs LM up to 52.5%; ABCD: CNE-LM-BoW multi-view 80.2%, improving over best single feature space). This supports the claim that embeddings inject useful semantic information into sparse network structures.
- Sensible multi-view design: Treating heterogeneous feature families with separate classifiers and combining them avoids naive concatenation and appears beneficial (e.g., Cinderella: ensemble/multi-view reaches 75%; ABCD: multi-view 80.2%).
- Comparative setups and variants: The manuscript contrasts raw vs manually revised transcripts (Cinderella) and includes an ASR-like segmentation setting (ABCD), which is informative for deployment considerations and highlights the dependence of linguistic features on clean parsing.

Weaknesses
- Missing critical methodological details about embeddings: The paper does not clearly specify the corpora used to train word/subword embeddings for English and Portuguese, whether pretraining used external data, the size/domain of those corpora, and whether any data leakage could occur. The handling and prevalence of out-of-vocabulary items are also unclear. These details are essential to assess reproducibility and the claimed language-independence.
- Limited data and potential evaluation leakage: The datasets are small (e.g., 20 MCI/20 control for Cinderella; ABCD includes two retellings per subject). The paper should confirm that cross-validation splits are by subject (not by narrative) to avoid having the same speaker in both train and test folds, especially in ABCD with immediate and delayed retellings. Without subject-level splits, reported accuracies may be optimistic.
- Lack of statistical testing and error analysis: Improvements (e.g., +2–10%) are reported without significance testing or confidence intervals. There is no analysis of which transcripts/phenomena are correctly vs incorrectly classified, limiting insight into what the network features capture relative to linguistic/BoW baselines.
- Presentation and focus: The first half contains substantial general NLP background and peripheral citations (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection; classic IR references) that can be trimmed in favor of method details, ablations, and interpretability (e.g., illustrative enriched networks, effects of similarity thresholds). The narrative sometimes overstates early and hedges later (“able to distinguish” vs “not easy to distinguish”), which could be harmonized.
- Minor quality issues: Typos, formatting inconsistencies (e.g., decimal commas), duplicated DOIs/links, and small transcription artifacts detract from polish and ease of reading.

Questions to the authors
- Embedding training: What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were they trained only on the task datasets or on large external corpora (e.g., fastText Common Crawl, Wikipedia)? Please report vocabulary sizes, token counts, and domain match, and clarify whether training and test partitions share pretraining data in a way that could bias results.
- OOV and subword: How frequent are OOV tokens per dataset, and how much does subword modeling reduce OOV rates? Please quantify and relate to performance.
- Splitting protocol: For ABCD (two retellings per participant), were folds created by subject? Likewise for other datasets, please confirm subject-disjoint folds. If not, can you re-run with leave-subject-out CV?
- Hyperparameter tuning: How were thresholds for similarity edges chosen (0.7 for Cookie Theft, 0.4 for the Portuguese datasets)? Were they tuned on training folds only? How sensitive are results to these thresholds and to embedding dimensionality/context window?
- Baselines and significance: Can you provide statistical tests (e.g., paired t-test, bootstrap) comparing CNE to LM and BoW? Also, how does multi-view compare to simple feature concatenation with a strong classifier?
- Interpretability: Can you add a small example (with English translation for Portuguese narratives) showing the plain co-occurrence network and the enriched network for the same transcript, illustrating added semantic edges and corresponding topological metric changes?

Additional comments (minor)
- Consolidate background and remove tangential citations (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection; classic IR references) unless directly supporting design choices. Compress Sections 1–3 and 4.2.3; expand method specifics and ablations.
- Provide brief clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms for readers unfamiliar with these conventions.
- Copyediting:
  - Abstract: “as it a lightweight” → “as it is a lightweight” or “as a lightweight”.
  - “PLN” → “NLP”.
  - Fix duplicated words in examples (e.g., “out of cookie out of the cookie”).
  - Use consistent decimal points (e.g., Table 3: 72.0 instead of 72,0).
  - Fix duplicated DOI and incorrect hyperlinking noted at the end of the references.

Overall recommendation
Promising direction with solid preliminary evidence that embedding-enriched complex networks can provide robust, language-flexible signals for MCI detection. With clearer methodology (especially embeddings and splits), statistical support, and tighter presentation with illustrative examples, the paper would make a stronger contribution.
