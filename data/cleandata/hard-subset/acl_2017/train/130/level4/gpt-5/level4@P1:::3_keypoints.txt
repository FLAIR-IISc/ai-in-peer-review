- Contribution: Shows complex network features enriched with word embeddings (CNE) can distinguish MCI from controls and sometimes outperform traditional linguistic features in English and Portuguese.
- Contribution: Demonstrates that multi-view learning and classifier ensembling further improve performance, with notable gains on Cinderella and ABCD datasets.
- Contribution: Provides a cross-lingual evaluation on three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) and uses automated sentence segmentation for a scalable pipeline.

- Strength: Uses a language- and resource-lean representation (CNs + embeddings) that avoids heavy parsing and suits noisy/disfluent or under-resourced-language transcripts.
- Strength: CNE yields consistent empirical gains over plain CNs and often beats linguistic features, addressing sparsity in short texts.
- Strength: Multi-view and ensemble methods provide complementary information and measurable performance improvements.
- Strength: Cross-dataset and cross-language experiments (English and Portuguese; picture descriptions and narratives) strengthen external validity.

- Weakness: Missing methodological details about the multi-view learning scheme, view combination, and training protocol.
- Weakness: Unclear source, size, and training details of word embeddings; datasets seem too small to train reliable embeddings without external corpora.
- Weakness: Removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) is questionable and not justified, despite their diagnostic value.
- Weakness: BoW baseline uses term frequency without justification for not using tf–idf, which is a stronger standard baseline.
- Weakness: No statistical significance testing reported to support claims of superiority given small samples and modest gains.
- Weakness: Related work section is superficial and does not sufficiently compare methods, features, or preprocessing choices.

- Question: Precisely describe the multi-view learning scheme and how views were combined (voting, stacking, learned weights) and validated.
- Question: Specify on what data embeddings were trained, corpus sizes, training parameters, and handling of OOV tokens.
- Question: Justify removal of disfluencies and report ablation results with/without these phenomena.
- Question: Explain why tf was used for BoW instead of tf–idf and report any comparisons.
- Question: Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., approximate randomization, bootstrap, McNemar) for key comparisons.
- Question: Define the cosine similarity threshold parameter for adding embedding-based edges and describe tuning per dataset.

- Editorial: Shorten and clarify the abstract and fix phrasing/typos.
- Editorial: Name and clarify the three datasets, languages, and segmentation procedures early and consistently.
- Editorial: State clinical rates unambiguously (e.g., annual conversion rates) and clarify “best scenario” for network features.
- Editorial: Add citations/links for mentioned datasets/tests/transcription norms and fix missing references.
- Editorial: Define acronyms (e.g., PLN) on first use and use consistent citation style per ACL.
- Editorial: Reframe preprocessing description (e.g., lemmatization not necessarily last), explain omission of lemmatization, and clarify network feature definitions.
- Editorial: Ensure tables/captions match content, expand the linguistic-features description in the main paper or concise summary if in supplementary, and fix minor grammar/style issues.

- Overall: Interesting, resource-lean cross-lingual study with promising signals for CNE and multi-view learning, but acceptance should require clarifying methodology (multi-view, embeddings), justifying preprocessing, strengthening baselines, and reporting statistical significance.
