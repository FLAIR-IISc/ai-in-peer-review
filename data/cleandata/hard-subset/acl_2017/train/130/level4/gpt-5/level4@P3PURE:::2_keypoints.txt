- Summary: Paper proposes a graph-based method using word co-occurrence and graph-derived lexical/syntactic features to detect MCI from speech transcripts across three datasets with 5-fold CV and multiple classifiers.
- Strength: Clear and important application—automatic MCI detection from speech transcripts.
- Strength: Uses three datasets and a graph representation exploiting word co-occurrence.
- Strength: Feature set includes multiple graph-derived properties (lexical, syntactic, etc.).
- Strength: Evaluation uses 5-fold cross-validation and multiple classifiers, showing dataset-dependent classifier behavior.
- Weakness: Manuscript readability suffers from grammatical and spelling errors; needs thorough language editing.
- Weakness: Problem formulation is unclear—uncertain what an instance is and transcript-level counts/class distributions are not reported.
- Weakness: Tables 1–3 focus on source studies rather than data used for classification (e.g., number of transcripts, class labels, lengths).
- Weakness: Dataset description includes irrelevant details (e.g., patient age) and contains numerical inconsistencies that must be reconciled.
- Weakness: Graph construction and the motivation for “enrichment” are unclear; alternatives like vector-similarity edges are not discussed or justified.
- Weakness: Method does not leverage or justify omission of domain-specific biomedical/NLP tools and resources.
- Weakness: Class distribution is not reported, making accuracy an insufficient metric; F1/precision/recall per class are needed, especially under imbalance.
- Weakness: Results are reported with excessive precision on small datasets and lack statistical significance testing or confidence intervals.
- Suggestion: Revise manuscript for grammar and clarity.
- Suggestion: Define instance/label space clearly and restructure dataset section and tables to report transcript-level counts and class distributions.
- Suggestion: Reconcile numerical inconsistencies and report relevant dataset statistics (e.g., average transcript length).
- Suggestion: Provide clear rationale for graph construction, compare to distributional-similarity alternatives, and justify enrichment steps.
- Suggestion: Discuss or incorporate domain-specific tools/resources from biomedical/NLP literature.
- Suggestion: Report class distributions and use F1/precision/recall alongside or instead of accuracy.
- Suggestion: Perform statistical tests or report confidence intervals and avoid over-precise reporting for small samples.
- Overall assessment: Promising and well-scoped approach but hampered by clarity, dataset reporting, metric choice, and significance-testing issues that must be addressed to strengthen the paper.
