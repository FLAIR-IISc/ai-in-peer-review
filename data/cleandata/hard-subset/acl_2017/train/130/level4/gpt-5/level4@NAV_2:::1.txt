Strengths:
This paper explores the use of NLP on speech transcripts (both narratives and descriptions) to identify individuals with mild cognitive impairment (MCI, ICD-10 F06.7). The authors assert they can discriminate between controls and MCI participants (lines 141–144). Yet the conclusion (lines 781–785) states that accuracies “ranging from 60% to 85% … mean that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments,” which is less emphatic than the opening. Despite this tonal shift, the overall take-away is encouraging and motivates interest in the technical details. The dataset described for Portuguese comprises 20 MCI and 20 controls (20+20), and the narratives are inaccessible to readers who do not speak Portuguese. Including more methodological specifics and at least one short transcript example translated into English—possibly with a corresponding (partial) embedding-enriched network—would improve clarity and accessibility.

Weaknesses:
The paper opens with an extensive introduction and literature review. Several citations are general NLP background that could be omitted (e.g., Salton 1989 in Section 4.2.3), and some are tangential (e.g., “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection in images,” lines 652–654). Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) can be streamlined, and more broadly, the first five pages could be tightened to focus on material directly relevant to the paper’s contribution, with space reallocated to the novel technical aspects and concrete examples (including English translations for ABCD and/or Cinderella subsets). The relatively short Portuguese narratives—especially in ABCD—raise questions about how word similarities were obtained to construct embeddings. Lines 272–289 indicate that word-level networks are derived from continuous word representations; however, the training source for these representations is unclear. Were embeddings trained solely on ABCD+Cinderella, or on external corpora? Lines 513–525 note subword (character n-gram) information was used to generate word embeddings; again, what training data were used? Is there evidence that combining co-occurrence and subword-enhanced networks improves accuracy, and by how much? Finally, please clarify what “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516) refers to in this context and their provenance.

General Discussion:
The topic—leveraging NLP to detect cognitive impairment—is important, and the study is therefore of interest. The focus on Portuguese is also valuable; in that context, it would help to explain how embeddings were constructed for a comparatively lower-resource language. The manuscript would benefit from revisions that: (i) shorten Sections 1–3; (ii) condense Section 4.1; and (iii) expand the experimental description (training data for embeddings, thresholds, classifier settings, ablations). A brief clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also aid readers.
