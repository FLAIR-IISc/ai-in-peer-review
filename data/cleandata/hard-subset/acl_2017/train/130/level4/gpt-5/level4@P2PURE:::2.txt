- Strengths:
This paper addresses the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three datasets. It proposes a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence in the transcripts. Features are derived from various graph characteristics, including lexical and syntactic properties, among others. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers. Different models show varying performance across the three datasets. The work targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper has several shortcomings:
1. The manuscript is difficult to read due to incorrect English usage. It would benefit greatly from a thorough review of grammar and spelling.
2. The core machine learning setup is poorly described. What constituted a single classification instance? It appears that each transcript was classified as MCI or non-MCI. If so, the dataset descriptions should report counts at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should describe the data used for classification, not the studies that produced the transcripts. Patient age is irrelevant to the classification task as presented. A substantial amount of space (about two pages) is spent describing dataset details that do not affect the final classification. In addition, some numbers do not add up. For example, in Section 4.1.1 the text states that 326 people were involved, yet the total number of males and females in Table 1 is under 100.
3. The motivation for enriching the graph is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node and connect nodes based on similarity between their vector representations, irrespective of co-occurrence?
4. The datasets are from a biomedical domain, but no domain-specific tools are used.
5. Because the class distribution is not reported, it is impossible to determine whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. In any case, for a binary classification task, F1 would be a desirable metric.
6. Results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without statistical tests over the observed improvements. Consequently, it is unclear whether the gains are significant.
