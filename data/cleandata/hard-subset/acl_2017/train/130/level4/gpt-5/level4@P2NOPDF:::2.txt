Strengths:
This paper tackles the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts drawn from three datasets. It proposes a graph-based approach that leverages word co-occurrence, with features derived from lexical, syntactic, and other graph characteristics. The study evaluates multiple classifiers using 5-fold cross-validation, and reports that performance varies across datasets. The problem is well defined, and the chosen datasets are appropriate.

Weaknesses:
1. Writing quality. The manuscript is difficult to follow due to frequent grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. It would benefit substantially from thorough editing for grammar and spelling.

2. Task formulation and dataset description. The core machine learning task is not clearly specified. What constitutes a single instance—an entire transcript classified as MCI vs. non-MCI? If so, the dataset descriptions should report counts and properties at the transcript level. Tables 1–3 currently describe the source studies rather than the actual data used for classification. Details such as patient age appear irrelevant to the stated task and occupy excessive space (about two pages). There are also inconsistencies in the reported numbers; for example, Section 4.1.1 cites 326 participants, yet Table 1 lists fewer than 100 males and females combined.

3. Graph design rationale. The motivation for “enriching” the graph is not clearly justified. Why not construct a graph where each word is a node and edges reflect similarity in vector space, independent of co-occurrence? The paper should explain and justify the chosen construction relative to such alternatives.

4. Domain adaptation. Although the datasets come from a biomedical context, no domain-specific tools or resources appear to be used.

5. Evaluation metrics. Because the class distribution is not reported, it is hard to judge whether accuracy is an appropriate metric. For a binary classification task, F1 (and related measures) would be more informative.

6. Statistical validity and reporting. Results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts), with no statistical tests to assess differences. It is therefore unclear whether the observed gains are meaningful.
