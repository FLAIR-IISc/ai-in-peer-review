This paper presents a novel application of largely existing representations, feature sets, and methods to the task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives. The problem setting, datasets, and domain are thoroughly described. While some details are missing, the proposed approach and experiments are generally reasonable. Overall, I found the study interesting and informative.

Main drawbacks:
- The paper requires substantial editing to improve readability.
- Several key methodological details are missing or unclear:
  - The multi-view learning approach is not described.
  - The set of “linguistic features” needs clearer definition.
  - It is not clear which datasets were used to train the word embeddings (the three datasets described seem too small for that purpose).
- The removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions, etc.) is not well justified; such phenomena may be important for MCI detection.
- The choice to omit tf-idf for BoW features is not motivated.
- No statistical significance testing is reported to support the conclusions.
- The related work section is described somewhat superficially and would benefit from more detail and direct comparisons.

Detailed comments:
- Abstract:
  - The abstract should be shortened; see notes below.
  - Lines 22–23: Rephrase. “However, MCI disfluencies produce agrammatical speech impacting in parsing results” → “…impacting the parsing results?”
  - Lines 24–25: Do you mean manually correcting grammatical errors in transcripts? It is not clear why this should be done—don’t grammatical errors themselves indicate MCI? Only after reading the Introduction and Related Work does your intent become clear. Consider adding brief examples of disfluencies.
  - Lines 29–30: Rephrase: “as it a lightweight and language independent representation.”
  - Lines 34–38: Clarify exactly which are the three datasets. For example: “the other two are Cinderella and …”

- Introduction and Background:
  - Line 70: “15% a year” — clarify what “per year” means here.
  - Line 73: Rephrase for clarity.
  - Lines 115–117: It is not obvious why BoW would also struggle with disfluencies; provide an explanation.
  - Lines 147–149: Clarify what is meant by “the best scenario.”

- Data and Resources:
  - Line 157: Provide a link or citation for DementiaBank.
  - Line 162: Provide a link or citation for the “Picnic picture of the Western Aphasia Battery.”
  - Line 170: Briefly explain what the WML subtest is.
  - Line 172: Add missing citations.
  - Lines 166–182: This appears to be the core of the related work but is described somewhat superficially. Please specify the precise methods used in those studies and how they compare to the present work.
  - Line 185: Please follow the conference citation guidelines (e.g., “Aluisio et al. (2016) used …”).
  - Line 188: Define “PLN.”
  - Lines 233–235: Rephrase. Lemmatization is not necessarily the last step in text preprocessing/normalization, and other common steps appear to be omitted.

- Word Embeddings and Features:
  - Lines 290–299: Were the word embeddings trained on the MCI datasets or on external corpora?
  - Section 4.2.1: Clarify which dataset(s) were used to generate the word embeddings.
  - Line 560: Does “shortest path” refer to the definition given in feature 6? Please clarify.
  - Section 4.2.2 (Linguistic Features): This section needs significant expansion and clarification. Also check the conference guidelines for what may be moved to “Supplementary Material.”
  - Line 620: “In this work term frequency was …” → “In this work, term frequency was …” Also, why not use tf-idf, which is a common weighting scheme?
  - Lines 641–645: Rewrite this sentence for clarity.
  - Line 662: What is “the threshold parameter”? Is it the threshold for cosine distance in word embeddings?

- Data Processing and Annotation:
  - Line 322: “consisted of” → “consist of.”
  - Lines 323–332: Rewrite for clarity. What does “manually segmented of the DementiaBank and Cinderella” mean? Segmented into sentences? Why weren’t all datasets automatically segmented? Define “ABCD.” You list datasets in i) and ii) but then refer to three datasets—explicitly naming each dataset would reduce confusion.
  - Table 1 caption: Demographic information is provided, but the “additional statistics of the dataset” described in the caption are not present.
  - Lines 375–388: Justify the removal of filled pauses, false starts, repetitions, etc. These may be important features for MCI detection.
  - Line 399: “… multidisciplinary team with psychiatrists …” → “…multidisciplinary team consisting of psychiatrists …”
  - Lines 340–440: Provide a link or citation describing the transcription norms used.

- Classification and Evaluation:
  - Line 735: Add a period.
  - Section 4.3 (Classification Algorithms): Details of the multi-view learning scheme are entirely omitted. Please describe the exact approach used. Also report statistical significance tests to substantiate the experimental conclusions.

- General:
  - The paper would benefit from overall editing to improve flow and readability.
  - Please provide significance testing for key comparisons and clarify methodological choices (e.g., the decision to exclude disfluencies and to forgo tf-idf).

In summary, this is an interesting and informative study applying known techniques to MCI detection from speech narratives, with a solid problem description and reasonable experimental setup. However, important methodological details are missing, the rationale for several design choices is unclear, significance testing is absent, and the related work needs deeper treatment. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper.
