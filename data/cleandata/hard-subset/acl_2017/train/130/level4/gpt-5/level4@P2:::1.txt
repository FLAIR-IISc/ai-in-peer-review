Summary and contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts (picture descriptions and narratives) as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings, and uses network topology features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. control.
- Across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD), enriched network features generally outperform traditional linguistic metrics and BoW. Ensemble and multi-view approaches further improve accuracy.
- The work emphasizes a lightweight, language-agnostic pipeline that avoids heavy manual preprocessing, aiming at scalable screening.

Strengths
- Lightweight, language-independent representation: Modeling transcripts as enriched co-occurrence networks is attractive for noisy, agrammatical speech and for languages with limited NLP tools. This is appropriate for clinical transcripts where parsing is brittle.
- Cross-lingual evaluation including Portuguese: Applying the approach to Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD) is valuable, given fewer available resources and limited prior work, and highlights the practicality of parser-free features.
- Empirical gains from enriched networks and multi-view/ensembles: Enriching networks with embeddings consistently improves over plain CN and often over linguistic metrics. Multi-view/ensemble setups yield further gains (e.g., +10% on Cinderella, +4.71% on ABCD).
- Minimal manual intervention: The pipeline removes only some disfluencies and avoids lemmatization and full parsing, aligning with the goal of scalable, automated screening. This design choice is well motivated by the clinical setting.
- Results are broadly in line with prior ranges: Reported accuracies fall within published ranges for related tasks, supporting the plausibility of the approach.

Weaknesses
- Unclear embedding training data and OOV handling: The paper does not specify the corpora used to train embeddings (English vs. Portuguese; in-domain vs. external), how subword fastText was trained, or vocabulary coverage/OOV rates. Given the short, noisy transcripts (especially ABCD), this is critical for reproducibility and for interpreting the gains from enrichment.
- Background and citations are overly broad: Several references are tangential (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection) and general background (e.g., Salton 1989) can be trimmed. Sections 1–3 and 4.2.3 could be substantially condensed to make room for methodological detail and analysis.
- Limited transparency and interpretability: No illustrative example of a transcript and its resulting network (with/without embedding edges) is provided in the main text, and no English translation is shown for Portuguese examples, which hinders comprehension for a broader ACL audience.
- Messaging inconsistency: Early claims (e.g., lines 141–144) suggest clear class separation, while the conclusion acknowledges that 60–85% accuracy indicates the task remains difficult. The paper would benefit from a more consistent framing of effect sizes and limitations.
- Dataset size and confounds: Datasets are small (e.g., 20 MCI/20 controls for Cinderella; short ABCD narratives). Demographic imbalance (e.g., ABCD age/education differences) may confound results; there is no analysis controlling for these factors or reporting stratified performance.

Questions to authors (for author response)
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? In- or out-of-domain? Pretrained fastText or trained from scratch? Provide training sizes, preprocessing, and hyperparameters beyond the defaults.
- Subword modeling: You used fastText subword embeddings to mitigate OOV. What were the observed OOV rates per dataset before subword composition? Do subword embeddings alone (without co-occurrence enrichment) explain the gains?
- Ablations: Can you report ablations isolating (i) plain CN, (ii) embeddings-only similarity networks, and (iii) co-occurrence + embeddings, to validate the added value of each component? Also, how sensitive are results to the similarity threshold (0.4/0.7)?
- Data balance and confounds: Given ABCD’s demographic differences (e.g., age, education, sex distribution), did you control for or match these variables, or include them as features? Any stratified analyses?
- Robustness in short texts: For ABCD’s very short narratives, how stable are network measures (e.g., diameter, clustering) across folds? Any variance analysis across runs?
- Preprocessing choices: You removed some disfluencies but kept revisions/paraphasias. How do results change if you vary disfluency removal? Any sensitivity to stopword removal or the decision not to lemmatize?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models/embeddings, and exact feature lists, along with English translations for sample Portuguese transcripts and an example network visualization?

Suggestions for revision
- Streamline Sections 1–3 and 4.2.3; remove tangential citations (e.g., sentiment classification, pedestrian detection; Salton 1989) to free space for:
  - Precise description of embedding training data, thresholds, and ablations.
  - At least one short transcript example with an English translation and a corresponding network (plain vs. enriched).
  - Clarification of NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 norms and their implications for segmentation/transcription.
  - Discussion of demographic confounds and controls.
- Harmonize the framing of results across abstract and conclusion to avoid mixed messages about task difficulty.
