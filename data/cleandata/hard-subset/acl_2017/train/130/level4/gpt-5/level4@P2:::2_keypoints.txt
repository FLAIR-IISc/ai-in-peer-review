- Tackles automatic detection of MCI from speech transcripts across three datasets (English and Brazilian Portuguese).
- Models transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings to reduce sparsity in short, disfluent texts, then extracts topological features for classification.
- Compares complex-network features to linguistic metrics and BoW across multiple classifiers, exploring ensemble and multi-view learning with reported gains (e.g., +10% on Cinderella).

- Strong practical relevance: early MCI detection from language with cross-language evaluation increasing generalizability.
- Lightweight, parser-free representation that aims to be robust to disfluency; embedding-based enrichment addresses short-text sparsity.
- Thorough empirical setup using multiple datasets, several classifiers, and ensemble/multi-view exploration; some improvements suggest complementary feature value.
- Provides comparative baselines showing cases where enriched network features outperform other individual feature sets.

- Paper clarity and writing are poor, with pervasive grammatical errors and awkward phrasing needing substantive editing.
- Task formulation and dataset reporting are underspecified: unit of classification unclear, per-class transcript counts and per-fold distributions missing, and numerical inconsistencies between text and tables.
- Model design rationale is under-justified; no explanation or ablation comparing co-occurrence+embedding edges versus pure similarity graphs.
- Lacks use/discussion of domain-specific resources (clinical lexicons, clinical embeddings, ASR-tailored preprocessing) for the biomedical task.
- Evaluation reporting is insufficient: class distributions unclear, reliance on accuracy may be misleading for imbalanced data, and detailed metrics (precision/recall, F1, ROC–AUC, confusion matrices) are absent.
- Statistical rigor missing: accuracies reported to many decimals on small samples without confidence intervals or significance testing; embedding threshold tuning procedure is underspecified and risks overfitting.

- What exactly is the classification unit (one transcript per narrative), and are multiple narratives per subject treated independently while preventing subject-level leakage across folds?
- For each dataset, provide number of transcripts per class, mean/SD tokens per transcript, and per-fold class distributions; clarify participant-count discrepancies.
- Please report macro/micro F1, per-class precision/recall, ROC–AUC, confusion matrices, confidence intervals, and statistical significance tests; justify using accuracy if classes are imbalanced.
- Why combine co-occurrence and embedding-similarity edges instead of a pure similarity graph? Provide ablations for (i) co-occurrence only, (ii) similarity only, (iii) combined, across thresholds.
- How were similarity thresholds selected (search range, validation protocol) and were they tuned per dataset without leaking test data?
- Were embeddings trained separately for English and Portuguese, on which corpora and sizes; how were OOV tokens and disfluencies handled; were domain-specific embeddings or lexicons tested?
- What preprocessing was applied to disfluencies and stopwords per dataset and language, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Did you attempt cross-dataset or cross-language evaluation to assess generalization and domain transferability?
