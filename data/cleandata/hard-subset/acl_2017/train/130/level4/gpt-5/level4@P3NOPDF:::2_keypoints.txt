- Addresses clinically relevant task: detecting MCI from speech transcripts across three datasets.
- Uses multiple datasets to assess generalizability.
- Proposes graph-based features built from word co-occurrence plus lexical, syntactic, and graph-derived features.
- Evaluates models via 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers; performance varies by dataset.

- Manuscript needs substantial language editing for grammar, clarity, and definition of technical terms.
- Task formulation is unclear: specify classification unit (transcript vs. patient) and exact labels (MCI vs. non-MCI).
- Dataset reporting is insufficient: provide instance-level statistics (number of transcripts per class, average lengths) and reconcile inconsistencies in counts and tables.
- Revise tables to reflect data used for classification rather than study-level properties; report demographics only if used as features.
- Graph design is under-motivated: justify enrichment choices, edge weighting, and inclusion of syntactic/dependency edges.
- Consider and/or compare alternative graph constructions (e.g., embedding-based similarity) and include ablation studies.
- Lack of domain-specific features/tools: either incorporate clinical/discourse-aware resources or justify their omission and analyze their impact.
- Evaluation metrics are incomplete given possible class imbalance: report class balances, use precision/recall/F1 (macro), and consider ROC-AUC/PR-AUC.
- Clarify CV procedure: whether folds are stratified and whether patient-level grouping prevented leakage.
- Statistical reporting is weak: avoid excessive decimal precision on small samples, add significance testing or confidence intervals, and consider repeated CV or bootstrapping for stability.
- Overall: promising idea but requires clearer task/dataset description, stronger justification of graph choices, domain-aware features, robust evaluation, and improved writing.
