- Strengths:
This paper applies NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with MCI (mild cognitive impairment, ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors report that they can distinguish between healthy controls and MCI patients (lines 141–144). However, in the conclusion (lines 781–785) they state that accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% implies that distinguishing healthy subjects from those with cognitive impairments is not straightforward. Thus, the opening is more optimistic than the conclusion, but the overall message is still encouraging and motivates the reader to seek more methodological detail.
The released corpus includes only 40 subjects (20 patients and 20 controls), and it is not accessible to readers who do not speak Portuguese. The paper would benefit from more technical detail and at least one short transcript translated into English, as well as an illustration (even partial) of a sample network with embeddings derived from that transcript.

- Weaknesses:
The paper begins with an extensive introduction and literature review. Some citations are general NLP background and could be omitted, e.g., Salton (1989) in section 4.2.3. Other references are tangential to the topic, e.g., “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection in images” (lines 652–654), and can also be removed. More broadly, lines 608–621 in section 4.2.3 could be shortened. The overall suggestion is to compress the first five pages, keep the review tightly focused on the paper’s topic, and expand the technological contribution, including sample English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.
Given the relatively short Portuguese narratives, especially in the ABCD dataset, it is unclear how word similarities were obtained to construct embeddings. In lines 272–289, the authors state that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations. What data were used to learn these representations—only ABCD + Cinderella, or external corpora as well? In lines 513–525, they note that subword (n-gram) networks were used to generate word embeddings. Again, what was the training source? Is there evidence that combining both types of networks yields better accuracy? Also, what exactly are the “out-of-vocabulary words” mentioned in line 516, and where do they come from?

- General Discussion:
Studying how NLP can aid in detecting cognitive impairments is important, and from that standpoint the paper is interesting. Another valuable aspect is the focus on Portuguese; it is important to explain how embeddings are computed for a comparatively lower-resource language (relative to English).
The text needs revision: shorten sections 1–3, compress section 4.1, and provide more detail about the experimental setup and results. Some clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also help.
