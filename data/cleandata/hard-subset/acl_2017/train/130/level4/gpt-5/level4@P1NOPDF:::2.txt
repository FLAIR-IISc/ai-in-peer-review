- Strengths:
This paper addresses the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three datasets. It proposes a graph-based method that leverages word co-occurrence, with features derived from various graph characteristics, including lexical and syntactic properties. Results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers, and the models show varying performance across the three datasets. The paper targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper has several drawbacks:

1. The manuscript is difficult to read due to incorrect English usage. It would benefit significantly from thorough editing for grammar, spelling, and style.

2. The core machine learning task is poorly described. What constituted a single classification instance? It appears that each transcript was classified as MCI or non-MCI. If so, the dataset descriptions should report counts at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should describe the data used in this study, not just the studies that produced the transcripts. The patients’ ages seem irrelevant for the classification task as presented. Too much space (about two pages) is devoted to describing the datasets with details that do not affect the downstream classification. In addition, some numbers do not add up. For example, Section 4.1.1 states that 326 people were involved, but the total number of males and females in Table 1 sums to fewer than 100.

3. The motivation for enriching the graph is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node and connect nodes based on similarity between their vector representations, regardless of co-occurrence?

4. The datasets are from a biomedical domain, yet no domain-specific tools or resources appear to be leveraged.

5. Because the class distribution is not reported, it is unclear whether accuracy is an appropriate evaluation metric. For a binary classification task, F1 would be a more informative measure, especially under class imbalance.

6. Results are reported to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts), without statistical tests comparing improvements. It is therefore unclear whether the observed gains are statistically significant.
