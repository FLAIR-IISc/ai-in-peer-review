- Strengths:
This paper investigates the problem of identifying patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts from three datasets. It introduces a graph-based approach that leverages word co-occurrence within transcripts. Features are constructed from various graph characteristics, encompassing lexical, syntactic, and other properties. Results are presented using 5-fold cross-validation across multiple classifiers, with different models showing varying performance across the datasets. The work addresses a clearly defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.

- Weaknesses:
The paper has several shortcomings:
1. The manuscript is difficult to read due to incorrect English usage; a thorough grammar and spelling edit is needed.
2. The primary machine learning task is not clearly specified. What constitutes a single classification instance? It appears each transcript is classified as MCI or non-MCI. If so, dataset descriptions should report counts at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should describe the data rather than the originating studies. Patient age is not relevant to the classification objective. Too much text (about two pages) is devoted to dataset descriptions that do not affect the final task. Additionally, some numbers do not reconcile; for example, Section 4.1.1 mentions 326 participants, but the total number of males and females in Table 1 is under 100.
3. The motivation for enriching the graph is unclear. Why not represent each word as a node and connect nodes by similarity between their vectors, independent of co-occurrence?
4. Although the datasets are biomedical, no domain-specific tools are employed.
5. With class distribution unspecified, it is unclear whether accuracy is an appropriate metric. For a binary task, F1 would be preferable.
6. Reporting results to four decimal places on very small datasets (e.g., 43 transcripts) without statistical significance testing makes it unclear whether the gains are meaningful.
