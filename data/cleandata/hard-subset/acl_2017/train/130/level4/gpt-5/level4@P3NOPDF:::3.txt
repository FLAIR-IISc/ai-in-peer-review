This paper applies largely established representations and methods to the novel task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives. The problem setting, datasets, and clinical motivation are well motivated, and the experimental design is generally reasonable. I found the study interesting and informative, and the application is timely and valuable.

However, the paper requires significant editing for clarity and completeness. Several key methodological details are missing, some choices are insufficiently justified, and related work is treated superficially. Below I summarize major concerns and then provide line-specific comments and suggested rephrasings.

Main strengths
- Clear motivation and clinically relevant task; datasets and problem context are generally well described.
- Sensible use of multiple feature families and representations; the overall experimental framework is sound.
- The study is likely to be useful to both NLP and clinical audiences once clarified.

Main weaknesses and questions
- Clarity and presentation: many passages are hard to follow; important terms are undefined.
- Missing methodological details: the specific multi-view learning scheme is not described; the exact “linguistic features” are under-specified; the source and size of data used to train word embeddings are unclear.
- Questionable preprocessing: removal of disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions, false starts) is not justified, although these are potentially informative for MCI.
- Baselines and weighting: BoW features do not use tf-idf; rationale is not provided.
- No statistical significance testing to support claims about performance differences.
- Related work is summarized superficially and does not clearly position this work against prior methods.

Detailed comments and suggestions

Abstract
- Please shorten and sharpen the abstract to focus on problem, approach (feature groups and classifiers), datasets, and main results.
- Lines 22–23: Rephrase to “However, disfluencies in MCI speech produce agrammatical output that impacts parsing results.”
- Lines 24–25: Clarify whether transcripts were manually corrected for grammatical errors and why. If corrections are for parser robustness, explain the trade-off, since grammatical errors may themselves be diagnostic. Including a brief example of typical disfluencies would help.
- Lines 29–30: Rephrase to “as it is a lightweight and language-independent representation.”
- Lines 34–38: Clearly enumerate the three datasets by name the first time they are introduced, e.g., “We use three datasets: (i) DementiaBank ABCD…, (ii) Cinderella…, and (iii) …”

Introduction and Background
- Line 70: “15% per year” is ambiguous. Do you mean annual incidence or progression rate? Provide a citation and clarify the metric.
- Line 73: Needs rephrasing for clarity.

Related Work
- Lines 166–182: Expand this core section. Specify for each cited study: dataset(s), features (e.g., acoustic, lexical, syntactic, discourse), representations (e.g., tf-idf, embeddings), models (e.g., SVM, LR, neural), and performance metrics. Then state how your approach differs (e.g., different feature integration, multi-view learning, narrative task).
- Line 185: Follow conference citation style, e.g., “Aluísio et al. (2016) used…”
- Line 188: Define “PLN” at first occurrence (e.g., “PLN (Portuguese NLP)” if applicable).
- Provide citations/links for DementiaBank and the Western Aphasia Battery (WAB) “Picnic” picture (Line 162), and explain the WML subtest (Line 170). Add missing citations at Line 172.

Data and Preprocessing
- Lines 157–172: Add references/links to DementiaBank and any other public corpora used.
- Lines 233–235: Rephrase to acknowledge that lemmatization is not necessarily the final normalization step. Also describe other standard steps you applied (e.g., lowercasing, punctuation handling, number normalization, stopword policy).
- Lines 323–332: Clarify dataset descriptions. Define ABCD at first mention. Specify what “manually segmented” means (e.g., into sentences, clauses, or narrative units) and why some datasets were manually segmented while others were automatically segmented; discuss potential effects on features. If there are three datasets, list all three by name consistently (avoid “first/second/third”).
- Table 1 caption: It currently promises additional dataset statistics that are not shown. Either adjust the caption or add the promised statistics (e.g., number of speakers/samples, mean/SD of narrative length, total tokens, class balance).
- Lines 375–388: Provide a strong justification for removing filled pauses, repetitions, and false starts, or reconsider this decision. These phenomena are often diagnostic in MCI/AD. If retained, explain how they are represented (e.g., tokenized labels), and if removed, consider an ablation study showing their impact.
- Lines 340–440: Provide a citation or link describing transcription and annotation conventions.

Representations and Features
- Section 4.2.1: State precisely which dataset(s) and how much text were used to train the word embeddings, and with which algorithm, dimensionality, window, min-count, epochs, and pre/postprocessing. If external corpora were used, list them; if only the MCI datasets were used, discuss size limitations. Clarify whether embeddings are domain-adapted or pre-trained and fine-tuned.
- Lines 290–299: Explicitly answer whether embeddings were trained on your datasets or external data; if external, ensure no data leakage across splits.
- Line 560: If “shortest path” refers to a syntactic dependency path feature, define it where Feature 6 is introduced and provide an example.
- Section 4.2.2 (Linguistic Features): Expand substantially. Enumerate features by category (lexical richness, POS and morphosyntactic distributions, dependency-based features, discourse measures, fluency/disfluency markers, readability, narrative structure, etc.), including precise definitions and formulas. If space is tight, include a concise main-text summary and move full definitions to the appendix or supplementary material (per ACL guidelines).
- Line 620: “In this work, term frequency was …” Also justify not using tf-idf for BoW; tf-idf is standard and may improve performance. If you chose TF-only deliberately (e.g., to emphasize frequent function words), explain and consider adding tf-idf as a baseline.
- Lines 641–645: Rewrite for clarity; the current sentence is hard to parse.
- Line 662: Specify what the “threshold parameter” refers to (e.g., cosine similarity threshold for embedding-based similarity). Report its tuned value and tuning procedure.
- Line 735: Add a period.
- Line 322: “consisted of” should be “consist of.”

Classification and Learning
- Section 4.3: The description of multi-view learning is missing. Specify the scheme used (e.g., feature concatenation vs. co-training, multiple-kernel learning, late fusion/stacking, multi-view SVM). If late fusion, describe base classifiers, meta-learner, validation protocol, and how views are combined. If MKL, specify kernels and weights. Provide hyperparameters and tuning details.
- Explain why BoW is expected to suffer from disfluencies (Lines 115–117), or temper that claim; disfluency tokens can also carry signal.
- Define “best scenario” (Lines 147–149): do you mean the best-performing feature set, model, or data configuration?
- Report statistical significance for key comparisons (e.g., best model vs. baselines) using appropriate tests (paired bootstrap, McNemar’s, approximate randomization), with confidence levels.

Results and Discussion
- Clearly state the datasets, splits, and evaluation metrics for each experiment. Ensure no speaker overlap across train/dev/test. If using cross-validation, report mean and standard deviation and provide significance tests.
- Include an error analysis or feature importance discussion to support claims about which features drive performance.

Minor wording/typo fixes
- Line 399: “…multidisciplinary team with psychiatrists…” → “…a multidisciplinary team consisting of psychiatrists…”
- Throughout: ensure consistent terminology and dataset naming.

Overall, the work addresses an important problem and has promise. With clearer exposition, full methodological details (especially for multi-view learning and feature definitions), justified preprocessing choices, stronger baselines (e.g., tf-idf), and significance testing, the paper would be substantially strengthened.
