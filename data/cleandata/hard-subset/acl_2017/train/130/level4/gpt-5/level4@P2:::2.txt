Summary and key contributions
- The paper tackles automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts across three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft in English; Cinderella and ABCD in Brazilian Portuguese).
- It models transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word embeddings to mitigate sparsity in short, disfluent texts, then extracts topological features for classification.
- It compares complex-network features against linguistic and BoW features across multiple classifiers, and explores ensemble and multi-view learning, reporting gains in some settings (e.g., +10% on Cinderella via ensemble/multi-view).

Strengths
- Clear problem focus with practical relevance: early detection of MCI from language. The cross-language evaluation (English and Portuguese) increases potential impact and generalizability.
- Lightweight, parser-free representation: modeling transcripts as complex networks aims to reduce reliance on fragile parsing in disfluent speech, and the embedding-based enrichment directly addresses short-text sparsity.
- Thorough empirical setup: multiple datasets, several classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, GNB), and exploration of ensemble and multi-view combinations. The reported improvements for Cinderella and ABCD suggest complementary value across feature spaces.
- Comparative baselines: the paper contrasts complex-network features with linguistic metrics and BoW, showing cases where enriched networks perform best among individual feature sets.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and writing quality: pervasive grammatical errors and awkward phrasing make the paper difficult to read. Substantive editing is needed for grammar, terminology, and structure.
- Task formulation and data accounting are underspecified. The unit of classification appears to be the transcript (MCI vs control), but this is never crisply stated. Dataset descriptions focus on study protocols and demographics rather than the counts relevant for ML (number of transcripts per class, per fold). There are numerical inconsistencies (e.g., Section 4.1.1 mentions 326 people, but Table 1 totals are far lower). Tables 1–3 emphasize age/education, which are not used by the classifiers, while key ML-relevant statistics are missing.
- Model design rationale is under-justified. The motivation for “co-occurrence + embedding edges” versus a pure similarity graph (nodes as words, edges by cosine similarity above a threshold) is not explained. A design comparison or ablation (co-occurrence only vs similarity-only vs combined) would clarify the contribution of each component.
- Domain adaptation: despite the biomedical context, no domain-specific resources (e.g., dementia-focused lexicons, clinical embeddings, ASR-tailored preprocessing) are used or discussed, and the potential added value (or pitfalls) for this task is not assessed.
- Evaluation metrics and reporting: class distributions are unclear, making accuracy a potentially misleading metric. For a binary task with possible imbalance, per-class precision/recall, F1, ROC–AUC, and confusion matrices are needed. Reported accuracies to four decimals on small samples (e.g., 43 transcripts) without statistical testing or confidence intervals make it difficult to judge robustness; significance testing (e.g., McNemar’s test, bootstrap CIs) is warranted. Threshold selection for embedding similarity is said to be “optimized,” but the procedure (search space, validation split) is not detailed, raising concerns about overfitting in small datasets.

Questions to authors
- Task/unit of analysis: What exactly is a “sample” for classification—one transcript per narrative? Are multiple narratives per subject treated as independent instances (e.g., ABCD immediate vs delayed recall), and how is subject-level leakage prevented across folds?
- Data accounting: For each dataset, please provide the number of transcripts per class, total tokens per transcript (mean/SD), and per-fold class distributions. Clarify the discrepancies between participant counts in the text and the demographic tables.
- Metrics: Please report macro/micro F1, precision/recall per class, ROC–AUC, and confusion matrices. If classes are imbalanced, justify accuracy or use balanced accuracy. Include confidence intervals and statistical significance tests for key comparisons.
- Graph design: Why combine co-occurrence and embedding-similarity edges rather than use a pure similarity graph? Provide ablations comparing (i) co-occurrence only, (ii) similarity only, and (iii) combined, across thresholds. How were similarity thresholds chosen (search range, validation protocol), and were they tuned per dataset without test leakage?
- Embeddings: Were embeddings trained separately for English and Portuguese? On what corpora and sizes? How are OOV and disfluencies handled in practice? Did you test domain-specific embeddings or retrofitting with clinical lexicons?
- Preprocessing: Which disfluencies were removed vs retained per dataset, and were stopwords removed consistently across languages? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Generalization: Did you attempt cross-dataset evaluation (train on one, test on another) to assess robustness and language/domain transferability?
