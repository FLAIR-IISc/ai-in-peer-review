- Paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings to classify MCI vs. control.
- Evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD) with enriched networks generally outperforming traditional linguistic metrics and BoW; ensembles/multi-view improve accuracy.
- Emphasizes a lightweight, language-agnostic, parser-free pipeline suitable for noisy clinical transcripts and low-resource languages.

Strengths:
- Lightweight, language-independent representation is appropriate for noisy, agrammatical clinical speech and languages with limited NLP tools.
- Cross-lingual evaluation including Portuguese demonstrates practicality and novelty for low-resource settings.
- Enriched networks + multi-view/ensemble methods yield consistent empirical gains over plain co-occurrence and many linguistic metrics.
- Minimal manual preprocessing (limited disfluency removal, no lemmatization/parsing) aligns with scalable automated screening goals.
- Reported accuracies fall within prior published ranges, supporting result plausibility.

Weaknesses:
- Embedding training data, preprocessing, and OOV handling are unspecified, harming reproducibility and interpretation of enrichment gains.
- Background and citations are overly broad or tangential and should be trimmed to free space for methods and analysis.
- Lack of illustrative examples or visualizations (no transcript→network example; no English translations for Portuguese samples) reduces transparency and interpretability.
- Messaging is inconsistent about effect sizes: early claims imply clear separation while conclusions acknowledge task difficulty.
- Small datasets and demographic imbalances (e.g., ABCD age/education differences) risk confounding; no control or stratified analyses reported.
- No variance/robustness analysis for very short texts (e.g., ABCD) or sensitivity to preprocessing choices.

Questions for authors:
- What corpora, domains, sizes, preprocessing, and hyperparameters were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings (pretrained vs. trained from scratch)?
- What were the observed OOV rates per dataset before subword composition, and do subword embeddings alone explain the gains?
- Can you provide ablations for plain CN, embeddings-only similarity networks, and combined co-occurrence+embeddings, and sensitivity to similarity thresholds (0.4/0.7)?
- Did you control for or match demographic variables (age, education, sex) in ABCD or include them as features; any stratified analyses?
- How stable are network measures on very short narratives across folds/runs; any variance analysis?
- How do results change with different disfluency removal, stopword removal, or lemmatization choices?
- Will you release code, trained models/embeddings, feature lists, English translations for Portuguese samples, and example network visualizations for reproducibility?

Suggestions for revision:
- Condense Sections 1–3 and 4.2.3 and remove tangential citations to make room for methodological details and analyses.
- Add precise description of embedding training data, thresholds, and ablation studies.
- Include at least one short transcript with English translation and corresponding plain vs. enriched network visualization.
- Discuss NURC/SP norms’ implications for segmentation/transcription and address demographic confounds and controls.
