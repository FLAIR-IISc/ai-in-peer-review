Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). It evaluates several classifiers on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD), compares complex-network features to linguistic and bag-of-words (BoW) features, and explores ensemble and multi-view combinations.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that complex-network features, particularly when enriched with embeddings, are competitive with or superior to parser-dependent linguistic features for MCI detection on noisy, short transcripts.
2) Demonstration that multi-view and ensemble combinations can improve accuracy over single-feature, single-classifier baselines on two of the datasets.
3) A lightweight, largely language-independent pipeline that avoids heavy manual preprocessing and parsing.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual evaluation on three datasets (English and Portuguese), covering both picture description and narrative recall, increases external validity.
- The approach directly targets practical constraints in clinical transcripts (short texts, disfluencies, limited language resources), and embedding-enriched networks are a sensible way to mitigate sparsity.
- Systematic comparison across feature families (network topology, linguistic metrics, BoW) and multiple classifiers, plus experiments with ensemble and multi-view setups.
- Reported gains from embedding-enrichment and multi-view combinations are consistent across datasets and baselines.

Weaknesses
- Methodological details are underspecified. The multi-view learning scheme (how views are combined, weighting/learning strategy, training protocol) is absent; the exact list of “linguistic features” used (especially for Portuguese) is not clearly itemized; and it is unclear what corpora were used to train word embeddings (the in-domain datasets seem too small).
- Preprocessing choices need justification: removing disfluencies (filled pauses, false starts, repetitions) may discard informative signals for MCI. Similarly, using raw term frequency for BoW without trying tf–idf lacks rationale.
- No statistical significance testing is reported, making it hard to assess whether observed improvements are robust.
- Parts of the related work are treated superficially, with missing citations/links (e.g., DementiaBank, WAB Picnic picture), missing definitions (e.g., “PLN”), and limited discussion of how prior methods compare to the proposed approach.
- Writing and organization require substantial editing for clarity (ambiguous phrasing, dataset descriptions, and some metric definitions).

Questions for the authors
- Multi-view learning: What exact scheme did you use (per-view classifier types, combination rule, any learned weights, training/validation protocol)? Was the combination tuned within the cross-validation folds?
- Embeddings: Which corpora and preprocessing were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? If using only the three datasets, how did you mitigate data sparsity? If using external corpora, please specify sources and sizes.
- Preprocessing: Why were disfluencies removed? Did you experiment with keeping them and/or encoding them as features?
- BoW weighting: Did you try tf–idf? If not, why? If yes, how did results compare?
- Statistical testing: Can you report significance tests (e.g., McNemar, bootstrap) for key comparisons (CNE vs. LM; single vs. multi-view)?
- Threshold parameter: Is this the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges? How was it selected, and was selection nested within cross-validation?

Detailed comments (by location)
- Abstract: Shorten and tighten. Clarify the three datasets explicitly (Cookie Theft; Cinderella; ABCD). Fix phrasing such as “impacting in parsing results” → “affecting parsing results,” and “as it a lightweight and language independent representation.”
- Early motivation: If grammatical errors/disfluencies are diagnostic, clarify why manual correction is undesirable and why you still chose to remove some disfluencies. Provide examples of retained vs. removed phenomena.
- Data description: Clearly name all three datasets when first introduced and avoid ambiguous “first/second/third” references. Define ABCD upon first use. Explain “segmented”: sentence segmentation? Why were only some datasets automatically segmented?
- DementiaBank: Add a link/citation. For WAB “Picnic picture,” add a citation/link. Briefly explain the Wechsler Logical Memory (WLM) subtest.
- Related work (core paragraph): Expand with concrete feature sets, modeling choices, and key results in prior MCI/AD speech studies, and position your approach relative to them (advantages, trade-offs).
- Citations and terminology: Follow ACL style (e.g., “Aluísio et al. (2016) used …”). Define “PLN.”
- Preprocessing: Lemmatization need not be “the last step”; acknowledge other common normalization steps and motivate omissions (e.g., robustness to disfluencies, tool availability).
- Embeddings (Sec. 4.2.1 and earlier mention): Specify training data (in-domain vs. external), tokenization, OOV handling, and whether the fastText subword model was trained from scratch or reused. Clarify whether thresholds (0.7, 0.4) were tuned per dataset within CV.
- Network features: Ensure definitions are precise and consistent. For “shortest path” and “diameter,” confirm whether they are computed on the largest connected component; clarify the item referencing “feature 6.”
- Linguistic features (Sec. 4.2.2): List exactly which Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia metrics were used in the main paper (not only in supplemental), and which were excluded and why (e.g., parser dependence, manual annotation).
- BoW (Line ~620): “In this work, term frequency was …” Justify choosing tf over tf–idf; consider reporting tf–idf results or explaining why tf–idf underperformed.
- Classification details: Provide kernel parameters (for SVM-RBF), k for k-NN, RF configuration, and how hyperparameters were selected (nested CV). Clarify the “threshold parameter” reference and ensure punctuation (e.g., missing period around line ~735).
- Significance: Include statistical tests for key improvements (e.g., multi-view vs. best single classifier).
- Tables and captions: Ensure table captions match content (demographics vs. additional dataset statistics). Consider adding basic dataset stats (narratives, tokens per narrative).
- Transcription norms: Provide links/citations for NURC/SP norms and any sentence segmentation tool (e.g., DeepBond).

Overall assessment
The study is interesting and promising, with a practical, language-agnostic approach and solid empirical comparisons. With clearer methodological details, stronger justification of preprocessing choices, and statistical testing, the paper would be much stronger. I recommend revision focusing on clarity, methodological transparency, and significance analyses.
