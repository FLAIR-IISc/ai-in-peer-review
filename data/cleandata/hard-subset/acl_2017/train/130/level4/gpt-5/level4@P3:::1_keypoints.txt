- Summary: Paper models transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with embeddings to detect MCI, evaluated on English and Portuguese datasets against linguistic features and BoW using multiple classifiers and ensembles.

- Contribution 1: Empirical validation that embedding-enriched complex networks (CNE) are competitive or superior to traditional linguistic features for MCI detection without heavy parsing.

- Contribution 2: Cross-lingual evaluation on three datasets with varied narrative types and preprocessing, showing ensemble/multi-view benefits.

- Contribution 3: Shows embedding enrichment mitigates sparsity in short transcripts, producing useful topological signals for classification.

- Strength: Practical, parser-light approach addresses disfluencies and resource-poor languages, supported by English and Portuguese experiments.

- Strength: Quantitative evidence that CNE often outperforms plain networks and linguistic features across datasets.

- Strength: Multi-view/ensemble design sensibly combines heterogeneous feature families and improves results.

- Strength: Comparative experiments include raw vs revised transcripts and ASR-like segmentation, informative for deployment.

- Weakness: Missing critical details about embedding training corpora, sizes, domains, and possible data leakage, harming reproducibility.

- Weakness: Small datasets and unclear splitting (possible same-subject data in train/test, especially ABCD) risk optimistic results.

- Weakness: No statistical significance testing or error analysis to support reported improvements and interpret model behavior.

- Weakness: Paper contains excessive general NLP background and should focus more on method details, ablations, and interpretability.

- Weakness: Minor quality issues (typos, formatting inconsistencies, duplicated DOIs/links) reduce polish.

- Question: Which corpora were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings, vocabulary sizes, and was pretraining separate from task data?

- Question: What are OOV rates per dataset and how much do subword models reduce OOVs; how does this affect performance?

- Question: Were cross-validation folds subject-disjoint (especially for ABCD with two retellings per subject); if not, can authors re-run subject-level CV?

- Question: How were similarity thresholds chosen for building edges (e.g., 0.7 vs 0.4), were they tuned on training folds only, and how sensitive are results to these hyperparameters?

- Question: Can authors provide statistical tests (paired t-test, bootstrap) comparing CNE to LM and BoW, and compare multi-view to simple concatenation?

- Question: Can authors add an illustrative example showing plain vs enriched networks for the same transcript and resulting topological metric changes?

- Minor comment: Trim tangential citations/background, expand methods and ablations, and clarify transcription norms (NURC/SP conventions).

- Minor copyedits: Fix wording in abstract, replace “PLN” with “NLP”, remove duplicated words, use consistent decimal points, and fix duplicated DOI/hyperlink errors.

- Overall recommendation: Promising approach with solid preliminary evidence; needs clearer embedding/splitting methodology, statistical validation, and tighter presentation to strengthen the contribution.
