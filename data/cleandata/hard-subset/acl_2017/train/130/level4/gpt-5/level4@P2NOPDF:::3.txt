This paper presents a novel application of largely established representations, feature sets, and methods to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech narratives. The problem setting, datasets, and domain context are described in reasonable depth. Although some methodological details are missing, the proposed approach and experiments are generally sound. Overall, the study is interesting and informative.

That said, the paper requires substantial editing to improve clarity and precision. Several key technical details are either unclear or omitted: the specific multi-view learning framework; the definition and grouping of “linguistic features”; the corpora used to train word embeddings (the three datasets described seem too small for this); and the rationale for removing disfluencies, which are likely informative for MCI. The choice not to use tf-idf for BoW is unexplained. Statistical significance testing is absent, weakening the empirical claims. Finally, the related work section is treated somewhat superficially and would benefit from deeper comparison.

Specific comments:

- Abstract: It should be more concise.  
  Lines 22–23: Rephrase “impacting in parsing results” to “impacting the parsing results.”  
  Lines 24–25: Do you mean manually correcting grammatical errors in transcripts? If so, why is this warranted, given that such errors may themselves be diagnostic of MCI? The intent becomes clear only after reading later sections; consider adding brief examples of disfluencies to clarify.

- Lines 29–30: Rephrase “as it a lightweight and language independent representation” to a grammatical form, e.g., “as it is a lightweight, language-independent representation.”

- Lines 34–38: Clarify exactly which three datasets are used. Consider explicit phrasing such as: “the other two datasets are Cinderella and …”

- Line 70: “15% a year” is ambiguous—does it refer to annual incidence, rate of progression, or something else?

- Line 73: Needs rephrasing for grammatical correctness.

- Lines 115–117: It is not evident why BoW would be especially sensitive to disfluencies. Please explain the expected failure modes (e.g., inflated vocabulary, sparsity, noise) and whether preprocessing mitigates them.

- Lines 147–149: Define what is meant by “the best scenario” (e.g., best-performing feature set, data split, or evaluation condition).

- Line 157: Provide a citation or link to DementiaBank.

- Line 162: Provide a citation or link for the Western Aphasia Battery “Picnic” picture.

- Line 170: Briefly explain the WML subtest.

- Line 172: Add the missing citations.

- Lines 166–182: This seems to be the core of the related work but is treated briefly. Please specify the exact methods used in prior work, their features and models, datasets and sizes, and how they compare to yours (e.g., overlap/differences in tasks, evaluation, and results).

- Line 185: Follow the conference citation style, e.g., “Aluísio et al. (2016) used …”

- Line 188: Define “PLN” at first use.

- Lines 233–235: Rephrase; lemmatization is not necessarily the final step in normalization. Consider noting other common steps (e.g., tokenization, lowercasing, punctuation handling, de-duplication, sentence segmentation).

- Lines 290–299: Specify whether embeddings were trained on the MCI datasets, external corpora, or both. If trained solely on the three datasets, discuss data sufficiency and any strategies (e.g., pretraining, fine-tuning).

- Line 322: “consisted of” → “consist of.”

- Lines 323–332: This section needs rewriting.  
  • Clarify “manually segmented of the DementiaBank and Cinderella”: segmented into sentences, clauses, or utterances?  
  • Why weren’t all datasets segmented with the same (preferably automatic) procedure?  
  • Define “ABCD.”  
  • The itemization lists two datasets (i and ii), yet you later refer to three; explicitly name all datasets instead of “first/second/third.”

- Table 1 caption: You mention demographic information but promise additional dataset statistics; include those statistics or revise the caption.

- Lines 375–388: Justify removing filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions. These phenomena are often informative for cognitive impairment; if removed, please explain why and report results with and without them.

- Line 399: Rephrase to “a multidisciplinary team consisting of psychiatrists …”

- Lines 340–440: Provide a citation or link describing the transcription conventions.

- Section 4.2.1: Clarify the training data for word embeddings and the training setup (algorithm, window size, dimensions, min count, epochs). If external data are used, list sources and sizes.

- Line 560: If “shortest path” refers to feature 6, make the cross-reference explicit and define how it is computed (e.g., dependency path length).

- Section 4.2.2 (Linguistic Features): Substantially expand this section. Clearly list each feature, how it is computed, tools/resources used, parameter settings, and any normalization. If space is tight, consult the conference policy on supplementary material and consider moving detailed definitions there.

- Line 620: Add the comma: “In this work, term frequency was …” Also, why was tf-idf not used, given its ubiquity and robustness? Consider reporting both.

- Lines 641–645: The sentence is hard to parse and should be rewritten for clarity.

- Line 662: Specify what “the threshold parameter” refers to (e.g., cosine similarity threshold for word embeddings), how it was selected, and its value(s).

- Line 735: Add a period.

- Section 4.3 (Classification Algorithms): Provide details about the multi-view learning scheme (e.g., co-training, multiple kernel learning, late fusion/stacking), how views are defined, and how decisions are combined. Report statistical significance (e.g., paired tests, bootstrap, or randomization tests) to support claims about differences in performance.

In summary, the paper addresses an important application and presents promising results, but it needs clearer exposition, more precise methodological detail, stronger motivation for preprocessing choices (especially the treatment of disfluencies), and statistical validation of findings. Strengthening the related work comparison and clarifying dataset and feature construction will substantially improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
