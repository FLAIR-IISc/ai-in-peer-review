- Strengths:
This paper applies NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with MCI (ICD-10 F06.7). The authors report the ability to distinguish healthy controls from MCI patients (lines 141–144). The conclusion (lines 781–785) is more cautious, noting that accuracies between 60% and 85% imply that distinguishing healthy from impaired subjects is not easy. Despite this tonal mismatch, the overall message is encouraging and motivates interest in the approach and its details.

The publicly described Portuguese datasets include only 20 MCI and 20 controls (20+20), and are inaccessible to readers who do not speak Portuguese. It would strengthen the paper to provide more implementation detail and include at least one short transcript translated into English, along with a (partial) example of a network enriched with embeddings for that transcript.

- Weaknesses:
The introduction and related work are extensive and include background NLP citations that could be omitted (e.g., Salton 1989 in Section 4.2.3). Some cited work is not directly relevant to the task (e.g., “sentiment classification” and “pedestrian detection in images,” lines 652–654). Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) could be shortened, as could other parts of the first five pages. I recommend compressing the literature review to focus strictly on the topic and devoting more space to the technical innovation, including concrete examples (e.g., English translations of ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives).

The relatively short Portuguese narratives—especially in the ABCD dataset—raise questions about how reliable word similarities for embeddings were obtained. Lines 272–289 state that word-level networks are generated from continuous word representations. What corpus was used to train these representations? Were embeddings trained solely on ABCD+Cinderella, or were external corpora used? Lines 513–525 indicate that subword (n-gram) information was used to generate embeddings. Again, what was the training source? Is there empirical evidence that combining co-occurrence networks with embedding-based links improves accuracy over either alone, for these datasets? Also, please clarify the origin of “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516) in this setup.

- General Discussion:
The topic is important: NLP can help detect cognitive impairments. The focus on Portuguese is particularly valuable, but it also requires a clearer explanation of how embeddings were obtained for a language with fewer resources than English (training data, preprocessing, and validation). The paper would benefit from revising Sections 1–3 to be more concise, compressing 4.1, and adding more detail on the experimental setup and design choices. Please also add a brief explanation of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms for readers unfamiliar with them.

- Questions to Authors:
1) What corpora were used to train word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were embeddings trained in-domain, on external corpora, or both?
2) How were hyperparameters for embedding training chosen (e.g., vector size, window size, epochs), and were they tuned per language or dataset? Any evaluation of embedding quality?
3) What is the empirical contribution of embedding-enriched links beyond co-occurrence? Can you report ablations comparing CN vs. CNE on each dataset with significance testing?
4) What proportion and types of out-of-vocabulary words did you observe, and from where do they arise? How does the subword model mitigate OOV in practice on these transcripts?
5) For the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, can you provide a small, anonymized example transcript (with English translation) and a corresponding partial network visualization to illustrate the method?
6) Can you clarify the discrepancy in tone between the optimistic early claims and the cautious conclusion, and contextualize your accuracies with respect to dataset size, class balance, and task difficulty?
