Strengths:
- The paper applies NLP to speech transcripts (narratives and descriptions) to identify patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI; ICD-10 code F06.7). The authors report that they can distinguish between healthy control participants and patients with MCI (lines 141–144). However, in the conclusion (lines 781–785) they state that “accuracy ranging from 60% to 85% … means that it is not easy to distinguish between healthy subjects and those with cognitive impairments.” This makes the introduction more optimistic than the conclusion. Nevertheless, the overall message is encouraging and prompts interest in more methodological detail.
- The submitted dataset comprises only 40 subjects—20 healthy patients and 20 control participants (20+20)—and is not accessible to readers who do not speak Portuguese. It would be helpful to add more technological detail and include at least one short transcript translated into English, along with (part of) a sample network with embeddings for that transcript.

Weaknesses:
- The paper opens with a detailed introduction and extensive related work, but some citations are unnecessary background for this paper (e.g., Salton 1989 in Section 4.2.3), and others are tangential (e.g., sentiment classification and pedestrian detection in images, lines 652–654). Section 4.2.3 (lines 608–621) can be shortened. More broadly, the first five pages should be compressed to focus tightly on the paper’s topic, freeing space to elaborate on the technical innovations, including samples of English translations of the ABCD and/or Cinderella narratives.
- Given the relatively short Portuguese narratives—especially in the ABCD dataset—it is unclear how reliable word similarities were obtained for constructing word embeddings. In lines 272–289, the authors state that they generate word-level networks from continuous word representations. What is the source for training these continuous representations—only the ABCD and Cinderella datasets, or were external corpora used? In lines 513–525, the paper says subword-level (n-gram) networks were used to generate word embeddings. Again, what was the training source? Is there evidence that combining these two types of networks improves accuracy? Also, what precisely are the “out-of-vocabulary words” (line 516), and where do they come from?

General Discussion:
- It is important to investigate how NLP can help detect cognitive impairments; from this perspective, the paper is interesting. The focus on Portuguese is also valuable; please explain how embeddings were computed for a language with relatively fewer resources than English.
- The text would benefit from revision: shorten Sections 1–3, compress Section 4.1, and add more detailed explanations of the experiments. Some clarification of the NURC/SP N. 338 EF and 331 D2 transcription norms would also be helpful.
