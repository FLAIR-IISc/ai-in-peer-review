This paper explores the problem of identifying patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) by analyzing speech transcripts available from three different datasets. It describes a graph-based method that leverages co-occurrence information between words found in the transcripts. Features are encoded using various characteristics of the graph, including lexical and syntactic properties, and many others. The results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation with multiple classifiers. Different models perform differently across the three datasets. This work targets a well-defined problem and uses appropriate datasets.

This paper has several weaknesses:

1.  It is difficult to read due to the use of incorrect English. The current manuscript would greatly benefit from grammar and spelling checks.
2.  The main machine learning problem being addressed is poorly described. What was an example of classification? It seems that each transcript was classified as MCI or No MCI. If so, the dataset description should describe the numbers at the transcript level. Tables 1, 2, and 3 should describe the data, not the study that prepared the transcripts. The age of the patients is irrelevant to the classification task. Too much text (2 pages) is used only to describe the dataset, which does not affect the final classification task. Furthermore, I was not sure why the numbers do not add up. For example: Section 4.1.1 says that 326 people were included. But the total number of men and women in Table 1 is less than 100?
3.  What is the motivation behind enriching the graph? Why is each word not represented by a node in the graph, and why are they not connected by similarity between their vectors, regardless of co-occurrence?
4.  The datasets are from the biomedical domain. Domain-specific tools were not leveraged.
5.  Since the dataset class distribution is unclear, it is not clear whether accuracy is a good measure for evaluation. In any case, since this is a binary classification task, F1 would be a desirable metric.
6.  The results are reported to 4 decimal places on a very small dataset (43 transcripts), without statistical tests. Therefore, it is not clear whether the benefits are significant.
