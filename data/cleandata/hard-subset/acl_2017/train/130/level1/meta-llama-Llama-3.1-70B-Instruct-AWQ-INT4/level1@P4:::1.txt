**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts into complex networks and enrich them with word embeddings to better represent short texts. They evaluate their approach on three datasets and compare the performance of different machine learning algorithms and feature combinations. The results show that complex networks are suitable for detecting MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to MCI detection**: The authors propose a new approach to detect MCI from speech transcripts using complex networks and word embeddings, which is different from traditional linguistic feature-based approaches.
2. **Enriching complex networks with word embeddings**: The authors adapt the approach of modeling networks with word embeddings to enrich the networks with semantic information, which improves the performance of complex network-based features.
3. **Evaluation on multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, including the DementiaBank, Cinderella, and Arizona-Battery datasets, which demonstrates the robustness of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive performance**: The authors show that complex network-based features are competitive with traditional linguistic features in detecting MCI.
2. **Robustness to disfluencies**: The authors' approach is robust to disfluencies in speech transcripts, which is a common problem in traditional linguistic feature-based approaches.
3. **Language independence**: The authors' approach is language-independent, which makes it suitable for detecting MCI in different languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited interpretability**: The authors' approach is based on complex networks and word embeddings, which makes it difficult to interpret the results and understand why certain features are important for detecting MCI.
2. **Dependence on word embeddings**: The authors' approach relies heavily on word embeddings, which may not always capture the nuances of language and may not be suitable for all types of texts.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on three datasets, which may not be representative of all types of MCI detection tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of limited interpretability in their approach?
2. Can the authors provide more details on how they selected the hyperparameters for their approach, such as the threshold parameter for the cosine similarity?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other types of texts, such as written texts or texts from different languages?
