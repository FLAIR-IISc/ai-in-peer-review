### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The authors address the challenge of applying network analysis to short texts—common in clinical assessments—by enriching standard word co-occurrence networks with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. Topological features are extracted from these enriched networks and used in various machine learning classifiers. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated across three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) with different linguistic characteristics. The proposed network-based features are compared against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words, and are also combined using multi-view and ensemble learning techniques.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Adaptation of Complex Networks for a Clinical Task:** The primary contribution is the application of complex network analysis to MCI detection. The specific methodological adaptation—enriching a co-occurrence graph with semantic links from word embeddings to make the representation more robust for short texts—is a well-motivated and novel approach to a known limitation of graph-based text models.
2.  **A Comprehensive, Multi-lingual Evaluation:** The authors provide a strong empirical validation of their method on three distinct datasets. This evaluation covers two languages (English, Portuguese) and multiple elicitation tasks (picture description, story narrative, story recall), lending significant credibility to the generalizability of their findings.
3.  **Demonstration of a Robust Alternative to Parser-based Features:** The paper convincingly argues that its network-based representation is more robust to the agrammatical and disfluent speech characteristic of MCI patients than traditional linguistic features that often depend on syntactic parsers. This claim is supported by strong comparative results, particularly on unedited transcripts.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Methodology:** The paper addresses a significant clinical problem where standard NLP tools often fail. The motivation for using complex networks to bypass the brittleness of syntactic parsers on disfluent speech is clear and compelling. The proposed solution to enrich sparse networks for short texts using semantic information from word embeddings is an elegant and effective way to overcome a key methodological challenge.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The use of three datasets with different properties provides a robust test of the method's capabilities. The comparison against two strong and relevant baselines—computationally intensive linguistic metrics (LM) and a standard Bag-of-Words (BoW) model—allows for a clear assessment of the proposed features' value. Furthermore, the exploration of multiple classifiers and the successful application of multi-view learning demonstrates how these new features can be integrated into a state-of-the-art system.
3.  **Strong and Promising Results:** The results clearly show the promise of the proposed approach. The enriched complex network features (CNE) are shown to be competitive with, and often superior to, the linguistic feature baseline across individual classifiers (e.g., on the Cinderella dataset, CNE achieves 65% accuracy vs. 47.5% for LM with an SVM-RBF). The fact that the highest overall accuracies are achieved by combining the CNE features with other views (e.g., 75% on Cinderella, 80% on ABCD) confirms that the network representation captures a unique and valuable signal.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, and the methodology is described with sufficient detail to understand the workflow. The authors specify the network construction process, the features extracted, and the parameters used for word embeddings. The use of the public DementiaBank dataset further aids the potential for future work to build upon and compare against this method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Deeper Feature Analysis:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a vector of topological network features can improve classification accuracy. However, it stops short of providing any analysis or interpretation of *which* of these features are most discriminative. An analysis of the specific topological differences between MCI and control networks (e.g., in clustering coefficient, assortativity, or path length) would provide invaluable insight into how cognitive decline manifests in language structure. This would elevate the paper's contribution from a purely engineering achievement to one with greater clinical and linguistic significance.
2.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports several accuracy improvements, some of which are modest (e.g., a 2.3% improvement on Cookie Theft, 4.71% on ABCD). Given the relatively small size of the datasets (N=40-86), it is critical to establish whether these gains are statistically significant. Without formal significance testing (e.g., using McNemar's test or bootstrapping), it is difficult to confidently conclude that the observed differences between models are not simply due to random variation in the data splits.
3.  **Potentially Contradictory Preprocessing Choices:** For the DementiaBank data, the authors state they removed disfluencies like filled pauses, false starts, and repetitions, which are often considered important diagnostic markers of cognitive impairment. This decision seems to contradict the central claim that the complex network method is valuable precisely because it is robust to such phenomena. This raises the question of whether the model's performance is dependent on this cleaning step, which would limit its applicability to truly raw transcripts. The rationale for this preprocessing step needs to be better justified.
4.  **Positioning Relative to Baselines:** The Bag-of-Words baseline performs surprisingly well, even outperforming the proposed CNE features in the best individual classifier scenario on the ABCD dataset (Table 7: 75.5% for BoW vs. 71.7% for CNE with SVM-Linear). While the final multi-view model is superior, this result somewhat weakens the claim of the network's inherent superiority as a standalone representation and warrants more discussion.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The BoW baseline delivered a stronger result than the CNE features on the ABCD dataset (Table 7) when using the best individual classifier for each. Could you elaborate on why a simple word frequency model might be more effective than your structural network model for that specific task (short-term recall), and how this impacts the general claims about your method?
2.  Could you provide the rationale for removing disfluencies (filled pauses, repetitions) from the Cookie Theft data? Given that these are often clinically relevant, did you test your model's performance with and without this preprocessing step? This would help clarify the model's true robustness to agrammatical speech.
3.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to confirm that the performance gains of CNE over the baselines, and of the multi-view models over the best single-feature models, are statistically significant?
4.  Could you provide any feature analysis to shed light on which topological properties of the speech networks are most altered by MCI? For instance, do MCI networks show lower clustering, longer path lengths, or different centrality distributions? This would provide a valuable link between your model's features and the underlying cognitive processes.
