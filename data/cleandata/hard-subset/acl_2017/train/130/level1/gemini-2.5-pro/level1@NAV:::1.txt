**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model transcripts as word co-occurrence graphs and then "enrich" them by adding new edges between semantically similar words, identified using word embeddings. This technique is designed to create a denser, more informative representation, which is particularly useful for the short and often agrammatical texts produced by patients. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The application and evaluation of a novel feature engineering approach that uses word-embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI detection. This method is shown to be a robust representation for noisy, disfluent speech, outperforming traditional linguistic features in several experiments.
2.  A comprehensive empirical study across three distinct datasets (in English and Portuguese), which validates the proposed method against standard baselines. The study effectively demonstrates that a multi-view approach, combining the proposed network features with other feature sets, yields the best classification performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Method:** The approach directly addresses a key challenge in this domain: analyzing the short, disfluent, and agrammatical speech of MCI patients, for which traditional parsing-based linguistic features often fail. Enriching sparse co-occurrence networks with semantic information from word embeddings is an elegant and effective solution to this problem.
2.  **Rigorous and Generalizable Evaluation:** The method is thoroughly tested on three diverse datasets that vary in language, task, and text length. This demonstrates the potential generalizability of the approach. The comparison against strong baselines (linguistic features, bag-of-words) and the use of multiple classifiers and combination techniques provide a robust validation of the results.
3.  **Strong and Promising Performance:** The proposed enriched network features (CNE) are highly competitive on their own and, when combined with other features in a multi-view learning framework, achieve the highest accuracy on all datasets (e.g., 80% on the ABCD dataset). This highlights the complementary value of the network-based representation for the MCI detection task.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Detail on the Network Enrichment Process:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding semantic edges is a critical hyperparameter, but its selection (0.7 for one dataset, 0.4 for others) is stated without justification. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process or a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this threshold.
2.  **Absence of Feature Analysis:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a set of ten topological metrics is effective for classification, but it offers no insight into which specific network properties (e.g., centrality, clustering, path length) are most discriminative for MCI. A feature importance or ablation analysis would provide valuable explanatory power, clarifying *why* the network models are effective.
3.  **Limited Contextualization with State-of-the-Art:** The authors correctly note that direct comparison with prior work is difficult due to differing datasets. However, the work could be better contextualized by discussing its position relative to recent deep learning or large language model-based approaches for text classification on clinical data, providing a clearer picture of its contribution in the current research landscape.
