### **Summary**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The authors address a key limitation of prior work: the poor performance of standard linguistic feature extractors (e.g., parsers) on the agrammatical and disfluent speech common in clinical populations. To overcome this, they model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks. Recognizing that transcripts from clinical assessments are often short, resulting in sparse, uninformative networks, they enrich these networks with additional edges between semantically similar words, identified using word embeddings. Topological features are then extracted from these enriched networks and used for classification. The proposed method is evaluated on three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) of varying text lengths. The performance of the enriched complex network (CNE) features is compared against standard complex networks (CN), linguistic features (LM), and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The paper also explores combining these feature sets using multi-view learning and ensembling classifiers, demonstrating that a combination of features yields the best performance.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A novel application of word-embedding-enriched complex networks for a clinical NLP task.** The primary contribution is the adaptation and application of complex network analysis to the challenging domain of MCI detection from noisy speech transcripts. The specific technique of using word embeddings to densify networks from short texts is a key element that makes the approach viable for this task.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the robustness of network features.** The paper provides a systematic comparison of the proposed network-based features against established baselines (linguistic features, BoW) across three distinct datasets. The results show that the network-based approach, which is less dependent on grammatical correctness, is competitive with and often outperforms traditional linguistic features, particularly on less-sanitized text.
3.  **An effective demonstration of multi-view learning for this task.** The work shows that combining the proposed network features with linguistic and lexical features via a multi-view approach consistently improves classification accuracy, suggesting that the network topology captures complementary information about cognitive-linguistic impairment not fully represented by other feature types.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated approach addressing a clear problem.** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of applying standard NLP tools to disfluent clinical speech. The proposed method, which avoids reliance on syntactic parsing, is a well-justified and practical solution to this problem. The motivation is strong and the proposed technique is a direct response to the stated limitations of prior work.
2.  **Thorough experimental design and evaluation.** The study's claims are supported by experiments on three different datasets, covering two languages and varying narrative lengths. This strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The inclusion of multiple feature sets as baselines (CN, LM, BoW) and several standard classifiers provides a comprehensive picture of the proposed method's performance in different contexts.
3.  **Novelty of the approach in the target domain.** While complex networks and word embeddings are not new in NLP, their combined application to enrich representations of short, noisy clinical texts for MCI detection is novel and contributes a new perspective to computational clinical linguistics. The results suggest this is a promising direction for analyzing impaired language.
4.  **Promising results, particularly from feature combination.** The enriched network features (CNE) consistently outperform the basic network features (CN) and are competitive with or better than linguistic metrics (LM) on their own. More importantly, the multi-view learning results show significant accuracy gains (e.g., a 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset), providing strong evidence that network features capture a distinct and valuable signal for this classification task.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of detail regarding key methodological components.** Several crucial details of the methodology are underspecified, hindering reproducibility and full assessment of the work.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The paper does not state what corpus was used to train the word embeddings. This is a critical detail, as the quality and domain-specificity of embeddings heavily influence performance. If trained on the small in-domain datasets, this would be a significant methodological flaw.
    *   **Network Enrichment:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a central hyperparameter, yet its selection process is not described. The paper simply states the optimal values (0.7 and 0.4). It is unclear how these were determined (e.g., on a separate validation set) and how sensitive the model's performance is to this parameter.
    *   **Classifier Hyperparameters:** Details regarding the tuning of classifier hyperparameters (e.g., C for SVM, k for k-NN) are omitted. Given the small datasets, performance can be highly dependent on proper hyperparameter tuning within a cross-validation framework.

2.  **Clarity of multi-view learning implementation.** The paper describes its feature combination method as "multi-view learning" and states it uses "majority vote." This is ambiguous. It is unclear if separate classifiers were trained on each feature set and their votes were aggregated, or if features were concatenated into a single vector for one classifier. A more precise description of the multi-view architecture is necessary to understand how the different feature spaces were integrated.

3.  **Absence of statistical significance testing.** The datasets are relatively small (e.g., n=40 for Cinderella). Accuracy differences of a few percentage points, as reported in several tables, may not be statistically significant. Without statistical tests, it is difficult to confidently conclude that one method is superior to another. This is particularly important when comparing the main proposed method (CNE) against strong baselines like LM and BoW.

4.  **Limited interpretation of results.** The paper successfully demonstrates that network features are useful for classification but provides no analysis of *what* these features capture about MCI. For instance, which of the ten topological metrics are most discriminative? Do the networks of MCI patients exhibit different structural properties (e.g., higher diameter, lower clustering coefficient) than controls? Such an analysis would provide valuable linguistic and clinical insights beyond a purely engineering contribution.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please specify the corpus used for training the word embeddings? Were they pre-trained on a large, general-domain corpus or trained on the task-specific datasets?
2.  How was the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment selected for each dataset? Could you provide a brief analysis of how classification performance varies as this threshold is changed?
3.  For the multi-view learning experiments, could you please clarify the exact procedure? Were separate classifiers trained on each feature view (CNE, LM, BoW) with their predictions combined via majority vote? If so, which base classifier was used for each view in the final ensemble/multi-view models reported?
4.  Given the small dataset sizes, did you perform any statistical tests to assess the significance of the reported differences in accuracy between different feature sets and models?
