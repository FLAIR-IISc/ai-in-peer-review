**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using features derived from complex networks. The authors argue that traditional NLP methods relying on parsing are brittle when applied to the disfluent and often agrammatical speech of MCI patients, and that manual correction of transcripts is not scalable. To overcome this, they represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence network. Recognizing that networks from short transcripts can be sparse and topologically uninformative, their key proposal is to "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between non-adjacent words based on the semantic similarity of their word embeddings. They extract a set of standard topological metrics from these enriched networks and use them as features for classification. The approach is evaluated on three datasets—one in English (Cookie Theft) and two in Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD)—comparing the network features against standard linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The results show that the enriched network features generally outperform the linguistic features, and that combining different feature sets using multi-view learning yields the best performance.

**Contributions**

1.  **A novel text representation method for clinical text classification.** The primary contribution is the application and validation of a method that enriches sparse word co-occurrence networks with semantic information from word embeddings. This approach is well-motivated for the task of classifying short and noisy clinical transcripts, as it creates a denser, more informative graph structure without relying on brittle syntactic parsers. This is a creative way to combine the structural information of graphs with the semantic information of distributed representations.
2.  **A robust empirical study across multiple datasets and languages.** The paper provides a strong evaluation on three distinct datasets, which vary in language (English, Portuguese), task (picture description, story recall), and text length. The inclusion of a comparison between automatically processed and manually cleaned versions of the same dataset provides compelling evidence for the authors' claim that their method is particularly advantageous when dealing with noisy, uncorrected transcripts.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant methodology.** The paper's core motivation—that standard NLP tools fail on disfluent clinical speech—is well-established. The proposed solution is a direct and clever response to this problem. Instead of trying to fix the text for the tools, the authors use a representation that is inherently more robust to agrammaticality. The idea of using word embeddings to add semantic edges to a syntactic co-occurrence graph is a strong one, as it directly addresses the sparsity problem in short texts, as clearly illustrated in Figure 2.
2.  **Comprehensive and convincing experimental results.** The evaluation is thorough. The authors compare their proposed features against two strong and relevant baselines (linguistic features and BoW) across multiple datasets and classifiers. Their findings consistently show the superiority of their enriched network features (CNE) over standard linguistic metrics on unprocessed text. The 10% accuracy improvement on the Cinderella dataset from using a multi-view model (Table 5) is significant and demonstrates the complementary nature of the proposed features. Furthermore, the experiment on the manually cleaned Cinderella data (Table 6), where the performance of linguistic features improves dramatically, strongly supports the authors' central hypothesis about the robustness of their method.
3.  **High potential for clinical impact.** The work addresses a significant real-world problem: the need for scalable and automated tools for early screening of cognitive decline. By developing a method that is language-agnostic in principle and requires minimal manual text correction, this research represents a valuable step towards creating practical diagnostic aids. This aligns well with the goals of clinical NLP.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Crucial details about word embedding training are missing.** The paper states that word embeddings were generated using the method from Bojanowski et al. (2016), but it does not specify what corpus was used for training. Were pre-trained vectors for English and Portuguese used? Or were they trained on the small, in-domain clinical datasets? The latter would be highly problematic due to data sparsity and would likely not capture rich semantic relationships. This omission is a critical flaw that hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the source of the performance gains. The semantic information is the "enrichment," so its origin must be clear.
2.  **Lack of comparison to other robust representation methods.** The paper's argument for avoiding parsing-based features is strong. However, it fails to compare its complex network approach to other modern methods that are also robust to syntactic errors. For instance, a simple but strong baseline would be to simply average the word embeddings of a transcript to create a single document vector. This would test whether the complex topological structure adds value beyond the semantic information contained in the embeddings themselves. While methods like BERT were nascent in 2017, other sentence or document embedding techniques existed and would have provided a more challenging and informative point of comparison.
3.  **Insufficient information on hyperparameter tuning.** The method relies on a crucial hyperparameter: the cosine similarity threshold for adding semantic edges to the network. The authors report that optimal values of 0.7 and 0.4 were used, but they provide no details on how these values were selected. Was a separate validation set used? Was tuning performed within the 5-fold cross-validation loop? If the latter, it could lead to information leakage from the test fold into the model selection process, inflating the reported accuracy. Similarly, no details are provided about the hyperparameter tuning for the machine learning classifiers (e.g., the C and gamma parameters for SVM), which is standard practice for rigorous evaluation. This lack of detail undermines the credibility and reproducibility of the experimental results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the corpora used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were these embeddings pre-trained on large, general-domain corpora, or were they trained specifically on the datasets used in this study?
2.  The similarity threshold for enriching the networks is a key component of your method. How was this hyperparameter optimized? Please describe the validation procedure you used to select the values of 0.7 and 0.4 to ensure that the test data was not used during model selection.
3.  In the ABCD dataset results (Table 7), the BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves a very strong accuracy of 75.5%, outperforming the CNE features alone. Why do you think this simple baseline is so effective on this specific dataset, and what unique, complementary information do your network features provide that allows the multi-view model to reach over 80% accuracy?
