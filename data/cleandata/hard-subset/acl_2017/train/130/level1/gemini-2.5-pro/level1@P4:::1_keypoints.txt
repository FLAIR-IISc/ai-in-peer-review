- Summary: Proposes word-embedding-enriched complex networks (CNE) to densify short, noisy speech-transcript co-occurrence graphs for MCI detection, extracting topological features and comparing CNE vs CN, LM, and BoW across three datasets, with multi-view/ensemble combinations yielding best results.

- Contribution 1: Novel application of embedding-enriched complex networks to MCI detection from noisy clinical transcripts.

- Contribution 2: Comprehensive empirical evaluation across three datasets (two languages, varying lengths) showing robustness of network features versus linguistic and lexical baselines.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates that multi-view learning / feature combination (CNE + LM + BoW) consistently improves classification accuracy.

- Strength 1: Well-motivated approach that avoids reliance on brittle syntactic parsers for disfluent clinical speech.

- Strength 2: Thorough experimental design using multiple datasets, baselines, and classifiers, supporting generalizability.

- Strength 3: Novelty in combining word embeddings with complex networks for short, noisy clinical texts.

- Strength 4: Promising empirical gains, especially from combining features (e.g., ~10% improvement on Cinderella).

- Weakness 1: Key methodological details underspecified—training corpus for word embeddings not reported.

- Weakness 2: Network enrichment hyperparameter (cosine similarity threshold) selection and sensitivity analysis are not described.

- Weakness 3: Classifier hyperparameter tuning details (e.g., SVM C, k for k-NN) and cross-validation procedures are omitted.

- Weakness 4: Multi-view learning implementation is unclear—ambiguous whether separate classifiers with majority vote or feature concatenation was used.

- Weakness 5: No statistical significance testing reported despite small dataset sizes, so reported accuracy differences may be unreliable.

- Weakness 6: Limited interpretation of results—no analysis of which network/topological metrics are most discriminative or how MCI networks structurally differ from controls.

- Question 1: Which corpus was used to train the word embeddings—pretrained large general-domain or trained on the task-specific datasets?

- Question 2: How was the cosine similarity threshold chosen for network enrichment, and how does performance vary with this threshold?

- Question 3: Precisely how was multi-view learning implemented—were separate classifiers trained per view with majority vote, or were features concatenated; which base classifiers were used in the final ensemble?

- Question 4: Were statistical tests performed to assess significance of reported accuracy differences given small sample sizes?
