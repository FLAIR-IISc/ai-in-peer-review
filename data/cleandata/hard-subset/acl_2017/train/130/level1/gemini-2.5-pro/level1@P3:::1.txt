### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To address the challenge of short and sparse texts common in clinical assessments, these graphs are "enriched" by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on a cosine similarity threshold over their word embeddings. A set of ten topological features (e.g., PageRank, clustering coefficient, diameter) are extracted from these enriched networks. These features are then used in various machine learning classifiers (SVM, k-NN, etc.) to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets: the English DementiaBank Cookie Theft corpus, and two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella story retelling and the ABCD battery). The authors compare their network-based features against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW), and also explore performance gains from classifier ensembles and multi-view learning across different feature sets.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel application of word-embedding-enriched complex networks to a clinical NLP task.** The paper successfully adapts graph-based text representation for MCI detection, a domain where traditional syntax-based methods often fail due to the disfluent and agrammatical nature of the speech. The enrichment with word embeddings is a clever solution to the problem of network sparsity in short texts.
2.  **A comprehensive, multi-lingual empirical evaluation.** The study provides a solid comparison of the proposed method against strong baselines (linguistic features, BoW) across three distinct datasets in two languages. This demonstrates the potential versatility and robustness of the approach across different tasks and languages.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Novel Methodology:** The paper's primary strength is its core idea. The motivation to move away from syntax-dependent linguistic features, which are notoriously brittle when applied to the disfluent speech of cognitively impaired individuals, is compelling. The proposed complex network representation is a principled way to capture lexical and structural information without relying on a parser. The further step of enriching these networks with semantic information from word embeddings to handle short texts is a significant and well-reasoned methodological contribution.

2.  **Potential Robustness to Noise and Agrammaticality:** By design, the graph-based method is more resilient to the repetitions, false starts, and grammatical errors prevalent in MCI transcripts than methods requiring clean parsing. This is a crucial practical advantage, as it reduces the need for expensive and time-consuming manual correction of transcripts, moving the field closer to fully automated systems. The comparison between the automatically processed and manually revised Cinderella datasets (Tables 5 & 6) provides some evidence for this, showing competitive performance on the less clean data.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation. They test their method on three different datasets, including two in Portuguese, which is valuable for a community often focused on English. They compare against two very relevant baselines: a sophisticated set of linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and a simple but powerful BoW model. Furthermore, the exploration of multiple classifiers, ensembles, and multi-view learning provides a comprehensive picture of how these different feature representations behave individually and in combination. The finding that multi-view learning consistently improves performance is an important one.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Critical Experimental Details:** The paper omits crucial information about the experimental setup, making the results difficult to reproduce and interpret.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The source and training data for the word embeddings are not specified. Were they trained on a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or on the task data itself? If the latter, was care taken to exclude test data from the training process? The domain and size of the training corpus are critical for the quality of the embeddings, which is the cornerstone of the "enrichment" step. This is a major omission.
    *   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold for edge creation is described as "optimized," but the process is not explained. Was this done using a separate validation set within each cross-validation fold? If the threshold was tuned using the test data, it would constitute data leakage and could lead to overestimated performance.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Overstated Claims:** The paper presents many results tables but lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the method works.
    *   **Feature Importance:** No analysis is provided on which of the 10 topological features are most discriminative. Does MCI affect network diameter, assortativity, or node centrality? Answering this would provide valuable clinical insights and elevate the paper from a purely engineering contribution to one with scientific impact.
    *   **Overstated Performance:** The conclusion that "topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" is not consistently supported by the data. For instance, on the manually processed Cinderella data (Table 6), the linguistic model (LM) with SVM-RBF achieves 72.5% accuracy, significantly outperforming the enriched network (CNE) model's 67.5%. On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline is the strongest single feature set. The paper's main strength appears to be in *combining* features via multi-view learning, not in the superiority of network features alone. This should be discussed more transparently.

3.  **Statistical Power and Baselines:**
    *   **Small Datasets:** The datasets are very small (e.g., 20 MCI vs. 20 controls for Cinderella), which makes the results susceptible to noise. The paper reports raw accuracy scores without any measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation across folds) or tests of statistical significance. A 3-5% difference in accuracy on a dataset of 40 samples is unlikely to be statistically significant, making it hard to draw firm conclusions about the relative performance of the models.
    *   **Strength of BoW:** The BoW baseline performs remarkably well, achieving the single best result on the ABCD dataset (75.5%). This important finding is somewhat buried. It suggests that simple lexical choice (what words are used) is a very powerful indicator in this domain, potentially more so than the complex structural relationships the network model aims to capture. This deserves a more prominent discussion.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks? Specifically, was a separate validation set used within each cross-validation fold?
2.  Could you provide details on the corpora used to train the word embeddings for both English and Portuguese?
3.  Given the small dataset sizes, have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate whether the observed performance differences (e.g., between CNE and LM, or between the best multi-view model and the best single-feature model) are statistically meaningful?
4.  Could you provide a feature importance analysis to identify which of the topological network metrics were most discriminative for the MCI classification task? This would greatly enhance the interpretability of your results.
