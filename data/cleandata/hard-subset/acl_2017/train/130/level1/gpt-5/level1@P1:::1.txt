Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches these networks with edges based on word-embedding similarity, then uses topological features from these graphs to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. The approach is evaluated on three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese), comparing complex-network features to linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW), and exploring ensemble and multi-view (feature-space) combinations.

As I see it, the main contributions (in decreasing order of strength) are:
1) Empirical finding that embedding-enriched complex network (CNE) features are competitive and often superior to standard linguistic features for MCI detection across multiple datasets/languages, with minimal preprocessing.
2) A simple multi-view combination (majority vote across classifiers trained on different feature spaces) that yields additional gains, notably 10% on the Portuguese Cinderella dataset and 4.7% on ABCD.
3) A modeling tweak that enriches sparse co-occurrence graphs for short transcripts with semantic edges derived from word embeddings, improving the informativeness of network topology.

Strengths
1) Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation with minimal manual preprocessing.
- The paper evaluates English and Brazilian Portuguese data of varying length/elicitation, showing that the method is not language-specific and is robust to noisy, disfluent transcripts—a key practical advantage given the difficulty of accurate parsing and the cost of manual normalization.

2) Clear, lightweight feature design with demonstrated effectiveness.
- The network features are simple and do not require resource-heavy parsers. CNE outperforms linguistic features on Cookie Theft and ABCD and matches/exceeds most baselines on Cinderella (unprocessed), supporting the claim that the approach is viable for low-resource or noisy settings.

3) Sensible use of baseline methods and combinations.
- Comparing CNE to BoW and Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) is appropriate; adding multi-view/ensemble combinations is practical and yields sizable improvements in some settings (e.g., 0.75 accuracy on Cinderella via ensemble of CNE+LM). The observation that BoW with linear SVM performs best on ABCD (0.7547) is candid and informative.

4) Addressing short-text sparsity via embedding-enriched graphs.
- Adding semantic edges to co-occurrence networks is a reasonable and well-motivated fix for the near-linear networks induced by short texts, with demonstrated utility.

Weaknesses
1) Potential subject-level leakage and confounding demographics undermine validity of reported gains.
- In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate and delayed recall). The paper uses 5-fold cross-validation at the narrative level, which likely places narratives from the same subject into both train and test folds, inflating performance. A subject-independent split (or leave-one-subject-out CV) is essential. Moreover, ABCD groups are strongly unmatched (controls: younger and more educated than MCI; 61 vs. 72 years; 16 vs. 13.3 years of education). Without controlling for (or reporting) the contribution of age/education, results may reflect demographic differences rather than cognitive status.

2) Hyperparameter tuning and evaluation clarity are insufficient.
- The cosine similarity thresholds (0.7 for Cookie Theft, 0.4 for Cinderella/ABCD) are said to be “optimized,” but it is unclear whether nested CV or a development set was used. If tuned on the test folds, this biases accuracy upward. Similarly, no statistical significance tests are reported, despite small sample sizes and modest improvements; only accuracy is presented (no precision/recall/F1/AUC), limiting interpretability.

3) Ambiguity in dataset composition and preprocessing choices.
- For Cookie Theft, the text says “We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD,” which is confusing (MCI vs AD?) and lacks details about the control group and matching. Preprocessing choices (e.g., stopword removal) are described for network modeling but it is unclear whether they also apply to BoW or LM features, which affects comparability. Removing filled pauses and repetitions, known to carry clinical signal, may also remove informative cues; the rationale and consistency across datasets need clarification.

4) Limited methodological novelty and weak analysis of why it works.
- Using co-occurrence networks for text and enriching edges via embedding similarity follows Perozzi et al. (2014); the novelty is the application to MCI detection. There is little analysis of which network features are most discriminative, how they vary with transcript length, or how much the embedding-augmentation contributes (e.g., ablations over thresholds, pre-trained vs. in-domain embeddings, or against simple embedding-based document features such as averaged word vectors).

5) Inconsistent claims and missing controls for length effects.
- The paper claims CN features outperform linguistic metrics “in all datasets,” yet in the manually processed Cinderella set, LM with SVM-RBF achieves 0.725 and exceeds CNE (0.675), which the authors later acknowledge in the discussion. Also, network measures often correlate with graph size; length normalization or explicit control analyses are not reported, raising the possibility that some gains reflect length differences rather than topology per se.

Questions to authors
- ABCD splitting: Did you ensure subject-independent folds (i.e., both narratives from the same subject never appear across train/test)? If not, can you re-run with leave-one-subject-out or grouped CV?
- Demographic confounds: Can you report results after matching or controlling for age and education (e.g., regression control, stratified sampling), or include these as features and assess their individual predictive power?
- Threshold tuning: How were similarity thresholds selected? Was nested CV used? Please provide sensitivity analyses over thresholds and report stability.
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Have you tried off-the-shelf fastText embeddings? How do results vary with embedding dimensionality/context window?
- Feature importance: Which network statistics (mean/std/skew) contribute most? Are results robust to controlling for transcript length and vocabulary size?

Additional comments
- Please clarify preprocessing per feature space (CN/CNE vs. LM vs. BoW), and whether stopwords/disfluencies removal was consistent.
- Consider reporting ROC-AUC and significance tests, and adding a subject-independent evaluation on Cookie Theft if multiple sessions per subject exist.
