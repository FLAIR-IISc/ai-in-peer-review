Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing each transcript as a word co-occurrence (adjacency) network and enriching it with edges derived from word-embedding cosine similarity above a threshold. It then extracts topological network measures (e.g., PageRank, degree, clustering) and uses their distributional statistics as features for traditional classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD), and compared against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW). It also explores late fusion via majority voting across feature spaces and across classifiers.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) Demonstration that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features are competitive for MCI detection from transcripts and can surpass linguistic and BoW baselines on several datasets, particularly in settings with limited parser robustness and short texts.
2) Cross-lingual evaluation (English and Portuguese) and exploration of simple multi-view/ensemble combinations showing additional gains, notably on the Cinderella and ABCD datasets.
3) A lightweight, parsing-independent pipeline that aims to be robust to disfluencies and minimal preprocessing, with some automation of sentence segmentation and disfluency removal.

Strengths
1) Methodological robustness to noisy, short, and agrammatical transcripts. The network-based features do not require PoS tagging or parsing, which are error-prone on disfluent speech. The approach is particularly apt for short texts, and embedding-based enrichment counteracts network sparsity (supported by higher CNE vs CN accuracies across datasets and classifiers in Tables 4–7).
2) Consistent, competitive performance across multiple datasets and languages. Enriched networks (CNE) are often the best individual feature type; e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF reaches 0.626 (CNE) and improves to 0.650 with fusion; ABCD SVM-Linear achieves 0.717 (CNE) and 0.802 with triple-view fusion; Cinderella raw improves from 0.650 (CNE) to 0.750 via fusion. These results show generality beyond a single corpus and language.
3) Clear ablation among feature families and simple late-fusion improvements. The comparisons among CN, CNE, LM, and BoW isolate the contribution of embedding enrichment and show when linguistic metrics help (e.g., ABCD BoW and LM baselines) versus when networks dominate. The reported 10% relative improvement on Cinderella via ensemble/multi-view suggests complementary information across views.
4) Lightweight and potentially language-independent pipeline. Using fastText-like subword embeddings and adjacency networks enables deployment in languages with limited NLP tools. The approach minimizes manual transcript revision, aligning with clinical scalability goals.

Weaknesses
1) Potential subject-level leakage in cross-validation for ABCD. ABCD includes two retellings per subject (46 narratives from 23 MCI, 39 from 20 controls). The paper does not state that folds are grouped by subject. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test folds, accuracy can be inflated. This is critical for clinical validity and may also affect Cookie Theft if multiple recordings per subject exist.
2) Hyperparameter tuning and evaluation protocol details are insufficient. The embedding similarity threshold is “optimized,” but no nested cross-validation or development split is described. Tuning on the same data used for evaluation risks overfitting. Similarly, classifier hyperparameters (e.g., SVM C/γ) and feature standardization are not detailed, limiting reproducibility and possibly biasing results.
3) Embedding training data is unspecified. The paper states training fastText-like embeddings with default parameters but does not specify the corpus (in-domain transcripts vs. large external corpora), per-language training, or whether pre-trained vectors are used. This affects both performance and replicability, especially for Portuguese.
4) Limited evaluation metrics and statistical analysis. Only accuracy is reported; class distributions are not always given; no confidence intervals, variance across folds, or significance testing are provided. Given small datasets (e.g., Cinderella N=40) and potential imbalance, accuracy alone is insufficient to judge robustness.
5) Clarity and dataset description issues. The Cookie Theft description is confusing: it mentions 43 MCI transcripts “sampled from 326 probable AD; 21 possible AD,” which mixes diagnostic categories and leaves the number of controls unclear. Some editorial issues (figure numbering, typos) hinder clarity. Claims of “outperforming linguistic features in all datasets” are not strictly supported (e.g., Cinderella manually revised: LM SVM-RBF 0.725 > CNE 0.675).
6) Novelty is incremental relative to prior network and embedding literature. Constructing adjacency networks and adding embedding-similarity edges follows established ideas (e.g., Perozzi et al. 2014). The application to MCI detection and the specific fusion are useful but technically straightforward. “Multi-view learning” is implemented as majority-vote late fusion rather than principled multi-view learning (e.g., co-training or learned weighting).

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation: For ABCD (and any dataset with multiple narratives per subject), did you use subject-wise splits to prevent leakage? Please report performance with grouped CV if not already done.
- Threshold tuning: How was the embedding similarity threshold selected? Was it tuned within each training fold (nested CV) to avoid test-set influence?
- Embeddings: What corpora and preprocessing were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were vectors trained on the task transcripts only or on large external corpora? Are the embeddings released?
- Dataset details: For Cookie Theft, how many MCI vs. control transcripts were used? Were any AD cases included or filtered? Please clarify selection criteria and class balance.
- Demographic confounds: ABCD shows substantial age and education differences between groups. Did you control for demographics (e.g., matched subsets, covariate adjustment), or assess whether the model exploits these confounds?
- Feature analysis: How many CN features are used in total? Which network measures contribute most? Any ablation of individual metrics or of embedding enrichment?
- ASR robustness: Given the intended clinical automation, have you tested the approach with ASR transcripts (errorful) rather than manual ones?

Overall, the approach is simple, computationally light, and shows promising results across datasets and languages. Addressing the evaluation protocol (especially subject-wise CV), clarifying embeddings and dataset composition, and providing stronger statistical analysis would substantially strengthen the paper.
