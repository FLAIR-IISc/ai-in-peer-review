Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them by adding edges between semantically similar words using word embeddings. It extracts topological features from these enriched graphs (CNE) to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), comparing against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW) on three datasets (DementiaBank Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella narratives, and Portuguese ABCD retellings). Multiple classifiers and simple ensemble/multi-view combinations are explored. Reported best accuracies are ~65% (Cookie Theft), 75% (Cinderella), and 80% (ABCD).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that complex-network features, when enriched with word embeddings, can match or surpass traditional linguistic features for MCI detection in noisy, short transcripts without manual linguistic annotation, across English and Portuguese datasets.
2) A practical adaptation of embedding-enriched co-occurrence graphs to mitigate sparsity in short, disfluent narratives, with a (limited) analysis showing CNE > CN and that multi-view/ensemble strategies can yield additional gains.
3) A cross-dataset evaluation that highlights when linguistic features degrade (raw transcripts) versus improve (manually revised narratives), underscoring the appeal of parser-agnostic features for this application.

Strengths
- Methodological frugality and potential robustness: The network-based approach avoids dependency on high-quality parsing and extensive manual cleanup, a known bottleneck in this domain. The motivation is well grounded: disfluencies harm parser-based features, and language-agnostic representations are desirable for clinical settings and under-resourced languages.
- Sensible enrichment for short texts: Augmenting co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based edges is a reasonable and well-motivated way to inject semantic connectivity into near-linear graphs, and the empirical trend that CNE > CN is consistent and meaningful.
- Cross-linguistic, multi-dataset evaluation: Results on English and Brazilian Portuguese, and on tasks with different elicitation protocols (picture description, story retelling, narrative recall), strengthen the claim that the approach is not language- or task-specific.
- Comparative baselines beyond CN: The inclusion of Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia features and BoW SVM gives context. The observation that BoW linear SVM is strong in some settings (e.g., 75% on revised Cinderella) is honest and informative.
- Practical framing: The focus on fully automatic pipelines (automatic sentence segmentation for ABCD, limited disfluency removal) addresses realistic deployment constraints and highlights where linguistic tools struggle.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage due to cross-validation unit: In the ABCD dataset, each participant contributes two narratives (immediate and delayed recall). Using 5-fold CV over narratives rather than speakers likely leaks subject-specific information across folds, inflating accuracy. The paper does not specify grouped CV by subject. This is a critical flaw; results on ABCD should be recomputed with subject-level splits.
- Hyperparameter tuning and model selection transparency: The cosine similarity threshold is “optimized” with 5-fold CV, but it is unclear whether a nested CV or held-out development set was used. If tuned on the same folds used for evaluation, this induces optimistic bias. Classifier hyperparameters (e.g., C and gamma for SVMs, number of trees for RF, k for KNN) are not reported nor tuned systematically, further muddying reproducibility and fairness across methods.
- Embedding training details missing: The source corpora, tokenization, vocabulary, and language-specific settings for fastText (Bojanowski et al., 2016) are unspecified. If embeddings were trained only on the small task corpora, they are likely underfit; if trained on large external corpora, domain mismatch and potential leakage (e.g., clinical text) need to be clarified. The fixed similarity thresholds (0.7, 0.4) lack sensitivity analysis.
- Demographic confounds and controls: The ABCD groups differ markedly in age and education (e.g., controls average 61 vs. MCI 72). Without controlling for demographics, differences in vocabulary, narrative length, or discourse style may drive classification. No regression or stratified analyses are reported; accuracy alone is insufficient to argue cognitive impairment detection rather than demographic signal capture.
- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor: Only accuracy is reported; no per-class precision/recall, ROC/AUC, confidence intervals, or significance testing. Given small sample sizes (e.g., Cinderella 20/20), variance can be high, and 5-fold CV may be unstable. The multi-view/ensemble gains (e.g., +10% on Cinderella) require statistical backing.
- Clarity and dataset description issues: The Cookie Theft description is confusing (e.g., “43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”), and control group sizes/demographics are not fully specified. Exact counts of transcripts per subject/session, and whether multiple sessions per subject exist, are unclear. Some results tables omit CN on certain datasets; feature dimensionality is not stated.
- Baseline breadth and ablations: Stronger baselines (e.g., tf-idf SVM, averaged word embeddings + logistic regression/SVM, doc2vec) are missing. An ablation on which topological metrics drive performance, and the incremental value of embedding edges (vs. raw co-occurrence), would strengthen the methodological insight.

Questions to authors
- How were cross-validation folds constructed? For ABCD, did you group narratives by speaker to prevent leakage across immediate and delayed retellings? If not, please recompute with grouped CV and report revised results.
- How were embeddings trained? Specify corpora, preprocessing, hyperparameters, vocabulary coverage, and whether embeddings were trained separately for English and Portuguese. Did you try pre-trained fastText vectors? How sensitive are results to the similarity threshold and to embedding dimensionality?
- How were classifier hyperparameters selected? Was nested CV used? Please report the chosen values (e.g., SVM C/gamma, K in KNN, RF trees/depth) and whether feature scaling was applied.
- What is the exact feature dimensionality for CN/CNE, and which measures are global vs. local (and thus aggregated)? Any feature importance analyses?
- For ABCD, can you control for or match demographics (age/education) or include them as covariates? Do results hold when demographics are regressed out or when stratifying by age bands?
- What is the class balance per dataset and per fold? Please report precision/recall/F1 by class and statistical significance of improvements.

Additional comments
- The claim of language independence holds for CN/CNE features, but the pipeline still relies on stopword lists and language-specific embeddings; this distinction could be nuanced.
- Reporting confidence intervals and releasing code/scripts (including data preprocessing and CV splitting) would improve reproducibility.
- A comparison against simple document-level embedding baselines (e.g., mean of word vectors) would help isolate the value of the network topology per se.
