- Paper models transcripts as co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-based semantic edges and uses graph-topology features to classify MCI vs controls on three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, Portuguese ABCD).

- Contribution: Embedding-enriched complex network (CNE) features are competitive and sometimes superior to standard linguistic metrics and BoW across English and Portuguese datasets.

- Contribution: Provides a parser-independent modeling that mitigates short-text sparsity by adding semantic links to co-occurrence graphs, yielding moderate gains over plain co-occurrence networks.

- Contribution: Simple multi-view/ensemble combinations of CNE, LM, and BoW can produce additional accuracy improvements on some datasets.

- Strength: Language- and tool-agnostic representation avoids reliance on fragile syntactic parsing and is suitable for disfluent clinical speech, especially where parsing resources are scarce.

- Strength: Cross-language, cross-task evaluation on three datasets strengthens generalizability claims and the consistent CNE ≥ CN trend supports semantic enrichment.

- Strength: Methodological simplicity and computational lightness make the feature set practical for resource-limited clinical settings.

- Strength: Multi-view combinations show substantial absolute gains on some datasets, suggesting feature-space complementarity.

- Weakness: Possible data leakage and confounds in ABCD—multiple narratives per subject and large demographic imbalances—raise risk of inflated accuracy if folds are not subject-independent and confounds are uncontrolled.

- Weakness: Ambiguity in Cookie Theft labeling and sampling (MCI vs AD) creates uncertainty about ground-truth labels and comparability to prior work.

- Weakness: Weak baselines and incomplete evaluation—BoW limited to tf unigrams, omission of stronger models/features, only accuracy reported, no per-class metrics or statistical tests, and potential non-nested threshold tuning.

- Weakness: Insufficient reproducibility details—embedding training corpora, preprocessing, vocabulary, and exact graph feature extraction procedures are unspecified and no code/embeddings released.

- Weakness: Design choices (removing stopwords and disfluencies) may discard informative signals for cognitive impairment without empirical ablation justification.

- Question: Were cross-validation folds enforced to be subject-independent on ABCD and any other multi-sample datasets; if not, please re-run with subject-wise CV.

- Question: Given ABCD demographics, can you report results controlling for age/education (matched subsets, covariate inclusion, or propensity matching) and show how accuracy changes?

- Question: Please clarify Cookie Theft labeling—how were MCI labels obtained, were AD cases excluded, and what are the exact class counts and diagnostic criteria?

- Question: What corpora, sizes, domains, and preprocessing were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings; did you try off-the-shelf embeddings and how sensitive are results to embedding quality?

- Question: Was the similarity threshold for enrichment tuned within nested CV or on the whole dataset; please provide details and updated results if needed.

- Question: Please provide ablations and error analysis: CN vs CNE across thresholds, impact of keeping stopwords/filled pauses, per-class performance/confusion matrices, and qualitative failure cases.

- Question: Clarify multi-view approach—did you implement learned weighting (regression) or majority voting, and can learned weighting improve results?

- Overall assessment: Promising, resource-light cross-lingual approach, but methodological issues (possible leakage, demographic confounds), weak baselines, and missing details limit confidence; needs label clarification, subject-wise CV, demographic controls, stronger baselines, and fuller reporting to be convincing.
