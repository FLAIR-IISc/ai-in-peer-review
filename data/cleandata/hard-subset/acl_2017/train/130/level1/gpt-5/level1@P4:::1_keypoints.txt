- Summary: Detects MCI from transcripts using word co-occurrence networks enriched by embedding-similarity edges, extracting network topology statistics as features for classifiers, tested on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD) and compared to linguistic and BoW baselines with late fusion explored.

Contributions:
- Shows embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features can be competitive and sometimes superior to linguistic and BoW baselines, especially for short/noisy transcripts.
- Provides cross-lingual evaluation (English and Portuguese) and simple multi-view/ensemble fusion showing additional gains on some datasets.
- Presents a lightweight, parsing-independent pipeline aimed at robustness to disfluencies and minimal preprocessing.

Strengths:
- Methodologically robust to noisy, short, agrammatical transcripts by avoiding POS tagging/parsing and using embedding enrichment to reduce sparsity.
- Consistent, competitive performance across multiple datasets and languages, with CNE often the best individual feature type and fusion improving results.
- Clear ablation among feature families and demonstration that simple late-fusion yields complementary gains.
- Lightweight, potentially language-independent pipeline using subword embeddings and adjacency networks suitable for low-resource settings.

Weaknesses:
- Possible subject-level leakage in CV (ABCD has repeated narratives per subject) risking inflated accuracy if folds are not grouped by subject.
- Insufficient hyperparameter tuning and evaluation protocol details; threshold and classifier tuning procedures (e.g., nested CV) are unclear, risking overfitting.
- Embedding training data and procedure are unspecified (in-domain vs. external corpora, per-language), harming replicability.
- Limited evaluation metrics and statistical analysis: only accuracy reported, no class distributions, confidence intervals, variance, or significance testing.
- Dataset descriptions and clarity issues (e.g., Cookie Theft composition unclear) and some unsupported claims about outperforming linguistic features.
- Novelty is incremental: technique follows established network/embedding ideas and fusion is simple majority voting rather than principled multi-view learning.

Questions to authors (key points):
- Did you use subject-wise grouped CV for ABCD (and any dataset with multiple narratives per subject) to prevent leakage?
- How was the embedding similarity threshold selected, and was it tuned within training folds (nested CV)?
- What corpora and preprocessing were used to train/fine-tune embeddings for English and Portuguese, and are embeddings released?
- What exactly were the class counts and selection criteria for Cookie Theft (MCI vs controls; AD cases included or filtered)?
- Were demographic confounds (age, education) controlled or assessed to ensure models do not exploit them?
- How many CN features are used in total, which network measures contribute most, and is there ablation of individual metrics or enrichment?
- Have you tested robustness to ASR errors by using automatic transcripts rather than manual ones?

Overall:
- Simple, computationally light approach with promising cross-lingual results, but needs stronger evaluation protocol, embedding/dataset transparency, and statistical analysis to be convincing.
