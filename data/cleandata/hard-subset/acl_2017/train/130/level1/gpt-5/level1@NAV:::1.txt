Summary
The paper investigates detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing texts as word co-occurrence complex networks enriched with word embeddings to mitigate sparsity in short, disfluent narratives. It compares complex-network features (with and without embedding enrichment) against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD), and explores ensemble and multi-view combinations. Reported gains include up to 10% improvement on Cinderella via ensemble multi-view and 80% accuracy on ABCD via multi-view CNE–LM–BoW.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features are competitive with, and often outperform, parser-dependent linguistic metrics for MCI detection across English and Portuguese without manual cleanup.
2) Demonstration that simple multi-view combinations of heterogeneous feature spaces (CNE, LM, BoW) provide consistent accuracy gains over the best single-view models, reaching 80% on ABCD.
3) A practical, language-agnostic pipeline for clinical transcripts that is robust to disfluencies and avoids expensive manual normalization.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, clinically relevant evaluation: Three datasets spanning English and Brazilian Portuguese, including institutional clinical data, increase external validity and highlight language-agnostic potential.
- Robustness to noisy, short texts: Enriching co-occurrence graphs with embeddings addresses sparsity (Tables 4–7 show CNE > CN in most cases), aligning with the intended use in disfluent transcripts where parsing fails.
- Sensible baselines and ablations: Comparisons to Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) and BoW, plus multiple classifiers, clarify where the proposed features help; notably, CNE SVM-RBF achieves 0.65 on Cinderella (Table 5), and multi-view reaches 0.8018 on ABCD (Table 7).
- Practical fusion improves performance: Multi-view majority voting yields non-trivial gains (e.g., +10% on Cinderella vs best single model; +4.7% on ABCD), suggesting complementary information across feature spaces.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and overoptimistic CV: The ABCD dataset includes multiple narratives per subject; the paper does not state subject-independent splits for 5-fold CV, risking leakage and inflated accuracy.
- Limited statistical rigor: Results are reported as accuracy only, without confidence intervals, significance tests, or class-wise metrics (sensitivity/specificity), which are crucial for clinical screening tasks with possible class imbalance.
- Unclear embedding training and thresholding: The corpus used to train FastText is not specified; threshold tuning within CV is briefly mentioned but lacks ablation/sensitivity analysis, raising reproducibility concerns.
- Modest absolute performance on some sets: Cookie Theft accuracies hover near chance (0.55–0.65), and RF/Naive Bayes underperform, suggesting limited robustness and the need for stronger baselines (e.g., transformer embeddings).
- Fairness of comparisons: Linguistic feature quality differs across languages/tools; the manuscript-processed Cinderella variant benefited LM substantially, yet fusion strategies remain simple (majority voting) without learned weighting.

Questions to Authors
- Were cross-validation folds constructed to be subject-disjoint, especially for ABCD with repeated narratives per subject?
- What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were pre-trained vectors considered, and how do they affect results?
- Can you report sensitivity/specificity and confidence intervals, and conduct significance testing across folds?
- How sensitive are results to the similarity threshold and embedding dimensionality? Any ablation beyond the selected 0.4/0.7 thresholds?
- How does the approach fare with actual ASR outputs (with recognition errors), not just simulated segmentation? Are code and features available for replication?
