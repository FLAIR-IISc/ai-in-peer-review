Summary
The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (complex) networks and enriches them by adding edges between semantically similar words based on word embeddings. Topological network features are then used for classifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. The approach aims to be lightweight, robust to disfluencies, and less language-dependent than parser-based linguistic features. Experiments on three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD) compare complex-network features (with and without embedding enrichment) against Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) features and BoW, and explore ensemble and “multi-view” combinations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that embedding-enriched complex network (CNE) features provide competitive and sometimes superior performance to standard linguistic metrics and BoW for MCI detection from transcripts, across English and Portuguese datasets.
2) A practical, parser-independent modeling that mitigates short-text sparsity by enriching co-occurrence graphs with semantic links, showing moderate gains over plain co-occurrence networks.
3) Evidence that simple multi-view/ensemble combinations of CNE, LM, and BoW features can yield additional accuracy improvements on some datasets.

Strengths
- Language- and tool-agnostic representation: The use of co-occurrence networks with embedding-based enrichment avoids reliance on fragile syntactic parsing in disfluent speech, addressing a real pain point documented for clinical transcripts. This is especially valuable in Portuguese, where robust parsing resources are scarce.
- Cross-language, cross-task evaluation: Experiments on three datasets spanning two languages and different elicitation protocols add credibility that the method is not dataset-specific. The consistent trend that CNE ≥ CN supports the core modeling idea (semantic enrichment combats short narrative sparsity).
- Methodological simplicity and efficiency: The feature set (graph topology statistics) is compact and computationally light. For clinical settings with limited resources and a desire for automation, this is a practical direction.
- Multi-view improvements: The reported 10% absolute gain on Cinderella and ~4.7% on ABCD when combining views indicates orthogonality among feature spaces; this suggests the network features capture complementary information to LM/BoW.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and confounds: The ABCD set contains multiple narratives per subject (immediate and delayed recall). The paper uses 5-fold CV but does not state that folds are split by subject. If narratives from the same individual appear in both train and test, accuracy can be inflated. Additionally, ABCD demographics are markedly imbalanced (controls: mean age 61, education 16; MCI: mean age 72, education 13.3; sex ratios differ). No attempt is made to control for these confounds (e.g., subject-wise CV, regression/covariate control, matched subsampling). This undercuts the validity of the reported 80% accuracy.
- Ambiguity in labels and sampling: For Cookie Theft, the paper states “we used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD,” which is internally inconsistent (MCI vs AD). It is unclear how MCI labels were obtained and whether AD cases were excluded. This raises concerns about ground truth and comparability to prior DementiaBank studies.
- Weak baselines and incomplete evaluation: The BoW baseline uses tf unigrams only; no tf-idf, n-grams, or regularized linear models beyond SVM are explored. LM baselines in Portuguese omit many strong parser-based features (understandable), but then the claim “CNE outperforms linguistic features” is less meaningful. Results report accuracy only (no precision/recall/F1, no per-class breakdown) and lack statistical significance testing or confidence intervals; tuning of the similarity threshold appears to be done without nested CV, risking optimistic estimates.
- Insufficient detail for reproducibility: The embedding training corpora (size, domain, sources, tokenization, vocabulary) are unspecified for both English and Portuguese, yet embeddings critically affect CNE. Graph feature extraction details are also thin (exact list of local/global stats used, handling of disconnected components, normalization). No code or embeddings are released.
- Design choices may discard signal: Stopwords are removed before graph construction, and filled pauses and some disfluencies are stripped. Yet function words and disfluencies are known to be informative in cognitive impairment. The paper should justify this choice empirically (e.g., ablation showing that keeping them hurts CNE), otherwise the approach may be leaving performance on the table.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you enforce subject-independent folds on ABCD (and any other dataset with multiple samples per subject)? If not, can you re-run with subject-wise CV and report results?
- Demographic control: Given large age and education differences in ABCD, can you (i) report performance on demographically matched subsets or (ii) include age/education as covariates or (iii) perform propensity matching? How does accuracy change?
- Cookie Theft labeling: Please clarify how MCI vs AD labels were established in DementiaBank, and reconcile the statement about sampling MCI from AD cohorts. What are the exact class counts and diagnosis criteria?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings (size, domain, preprocessing)? Did you try off-the-shelf large embeddings (e.g., fastText Wikipedia) and how sensitive are results to embedding quality?
- Threshold selection and nesting: Was the similarity threshold tuned within a nested CV or using only training folds? Please provide details and, if needed, updated results with proper nesting and statistical tests.
- Ablations and error analysis: Can you report (i) CN vs CNE ablation across thresholds, (ii) impact of keeping stopwords and filled pauses, (iii) per-class performance and confusion matrices, and (iv) qualitative errors to understand failure modes?
- Multi-view learning: The text mentions learning weights via regression, but results describe majority voting. Which did you implement? If only majority vote, can you try learned weighting and report gains?

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important clinical NLP problem with a resource-light, cross-lingual method and shows promising trends that embedding-enriched networks can help. However, methodological issues (possible subject leakage, demographic confounds), weak baselines, and missing details currently limit confidence in the conclusions. With clarified labels, subject-wise CV, demographic controls, stronger baselines, and fuller reporting (significance, ablations), this work could become a stronger empirical contribution. As it stands, the novelty is moderate and the experimental rigor needs bolstering to meet ACL standards.
