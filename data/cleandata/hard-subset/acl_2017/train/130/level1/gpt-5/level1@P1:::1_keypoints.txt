- Paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-based semantic edges and uses graph/topological features to classify MCI vs. controls across three datasets.

- Main contribution 1: Embedding-enriched complex network (CNE) features are competitive and often superior to standard linguistic features for MCI detection with minimal preprocessing.

- Main contribution 2: A simple multi-view ensemble (majority vote across feature-space classifiers) yields additional gains (notably +10% on Cinderella, +4.7% on ABCD).

- Main contribution 3: Enriching sparse co-occurrence graphs for short transcripts with semantic edges from word embeddings improves network informativeness.

- Strength: Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation (English and Brazilian Portuguese) with minimal manual preprocessing, demonstrating robustness to noisy/disfluent transcripts.

- Strength: Lightweight, parser-free feature design that performs well in low-resource or noisy settings; CNE outperforms linguistic features on Cookie Theft and ABCD and is competitive on Cinderella.

- Strength: Appropriate baselines (BoW, Coh-Metrix/Dementia) and practical use of ensemble/multi-view combinations that yield notable improvements in some settings.

- Strength: Reasonable solution to short-text sparsity by adding semantic edges to co-occurrence networks.

- Weakness: Potential subject-level leakage and demographic confounds in ABCD (two narratives per subject and unmatched age/education) may inflate results and obscure true signal.

- Weakness: Unclear hyperparameter tuning and evaluation protocol (similarity thresholds allegedly “optimized” without clarity on nested CV or development set), and missing statistical significance and diverse metrics.

- Weakness: Ambiguities in dataset composition and preprocessing (Cookie Theft MCI vs AD confusion, unclear control matching, inconsistent reporting of stopword/disfluency removal across feature spaces).

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty beyond applying known network/embedding ideas to MCI detection and weak analysis of why features work (no detailed feature importance or ablation studies).

- Weakness: Inconsistent claims and missing controls for transcript length effects—network measures may reflect length or vocabulary size rather than topology.

- Question: Did you ensure subject-independent folds for ABCD (no narratives from same subject in both train and test)? If not, please re-run with grouped CV.

- Question: Can you report results controlling for age and education (matching, regression control, or adding as features) to rule out demographic confounds?

- Question: How were cosine similarity thresholds selected—was nested CV used, and can you provide sensitivity analyses over thresholds?

- Question: Which embeddings were used for English and Portuguese, have off-the-shelf fastText been tried, and how sensitive are results to embedding choices (dimensionality/context window)?

- Question: Which network statistics are most discriminative, and are results robust when controlling for transcript length and vocabulary size?

- Additional comment: Clarify preprocessing per feature space (CN/CNE vs LM vs BoW) and consistency of stopword/disfluency removal.

- Additional comment: Recommend reporting ROC-AUC, precision/recall/F1, significance tests, and adding subject-independent evaluation where applicable.
