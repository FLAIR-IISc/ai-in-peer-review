## Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose modeling transcripts as networks and leveraging both topological properties of these networks and machine learning classifiers to differentiate between individuals with and without MCI. They evaluate their approach on three datasets, including one in Portuguese, and demonstrate improvements over linguistic features, particularly when using ensemble and multi-view learning techniques.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the application and evaluation of complex network analysis to the challenging problem of MCI detection from natural language data. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to neuropsychological assessment, especially in the context of potentially noisy speech transcripts, is novel. The enrichment of these networks with word embeddings is a sensible approach to address the limitations of short texts and incorporate semantic information. A second contribution is the evaluation on multiple datasets, including a non-English dataset, which increases the generalizability of the findings. Finally, the demonstration of improved performance through ensemble and multi-view learning provides practical insight into combining different feature types for this task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application:** Applying complex network analysis to MCI detection from speech transcripts is a promising and relatively unexplored area.
*   **Robustness to Noise:** The network-based approach is presented as being more robust to disfluencies in speech, a significant advantage in real-world clinical data.
*   **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The inclusion of three datasets, including one in Portuguese, strengthens the validity and generalizability of the results.
*   **Performance Improvement:** The reported improvements over linguistic features and through ensemble/multi-view learning are encouraging.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Method:** The core idea of using complex networks and word embeddings is not entirely novel in NLP. The specific contribution lies in the application to this domain, but a more thorough discussion of related work in network-based cognitive assessment would be beneficial.
*   **Feature Engineering:** The selection of topological features seems somewhat arbitrary. A more principled feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique could improve performance and interpretability.
*   **Dataset Characteristics:** The datasets vary significantly in size and characteristics. A more detailed discussion of these differences and their potential impact on the results is needed. The automatic segmentation of the Arizona dataset is a potential source of error.
*   **Statistical Significance:** While accuracy improvements are reported, the statistical significance of these improvements is not always clearly stated.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific topological features used? Were any feature selection techniques explored?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embedding model and the cosine similarity threshold?
*   Given the differences in dataset characteristics, could you perform a more rigorous comparison of performance across datasets, perhaps using standardized metrics or normalization techniques?
*   Please provide p-values or confidence intervals to support the reported accuracy improvements.
