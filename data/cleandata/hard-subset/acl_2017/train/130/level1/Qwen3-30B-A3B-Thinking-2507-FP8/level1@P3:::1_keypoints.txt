- Proposes representing speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI, addressing disfluency issues in clinical speech.
- Shows the network-based method outperforms traditional linguistic features across three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, Portuguese ABCD).
- Demonstrates further gains by combining network features with linguistic features via multi-view learning.

- Provides a strong theoretical justification that complex networks naturally handle disfluencies and that semantic enrichment mitigates uninformative linear networks.
- Validates the approach across multiple languages, narrative types, and clinical protocols, showing consistent performance gains and apparent generalizability.
- Addresses a practical clinical need for automated, language-independent MCI screening that reduces manual transcript correction.

- Does not compare with contemporary deep learning approaches (CNNs, RNNs, transformers/BERT), limiting assessment against state-of-the-art methods.
- Lacks detailed analysis of which specific network/topological features drive classification performance (no feature importance or ablation).
- Presents results inconsistently across tables, making fair comparison of ensemble/multi-view and baseline results difficult.
- Omits practical implementation details such as computational cost, sensitivity to embedding choice, and cosine similarity threshold.
- Overstates language independence given reliance on language-specific word embeddings and lack of discussion on low-resource languages.
