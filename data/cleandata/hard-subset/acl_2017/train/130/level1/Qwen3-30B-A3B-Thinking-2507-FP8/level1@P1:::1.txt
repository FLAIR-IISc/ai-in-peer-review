

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using complex network modeling of speech transcripts, enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as complex networks where words are nodes and adjacency represents co-occurrence, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings. They claim this approach is more robust to disfluencies (common in patient speech) than traditional linguistic features, which require manual correction.

The main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A practical, language-independent framework for MCI detection from speech transcripts that avoids the need for manual correction of disfluencies
2. Demonstrated superiority of complex network features (especially when enriched with embeddings) over traditional linguistic features across three diverse datasets
3. Effective combination of complex network features with linguistic features and bag-of-words through ensemble and multi-view learning, achieving significant accuracy improvements

## Strengths

1. **Practical solution to a real clinical problem**: The paper addresses a genuine challenge in clinical assessment - disfluencies in patient speech make traditional linguistic feature extraction unreliable, requiring expensive manual correction. Their approach avoids this problem entirely by using complex networks that are less sensitive to disfluencies. This represents a significant practical contribution to the field of automatic cognitive assessment.

2. **Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets (DementiaBank English, Cinderella Portuguese, Arizona-Battery Portuguese), demonstrating consistent improvements over linguistic features. The improvements (up to 10% in Cinderella dataset) are meaningful in clinical contexts where small accuracy gains can impact diagnosis. The cross-lingual validation (English and Portuguese) strengthens the generalizability claim.

3. **Effective integration of multiple feature spaces**: The paper successfully demonstrates how combining complex network features with linguistic features and bag-of-words through multi-view learning leads to significant accuracy gains (e.g., 4.71% improvement on ABCD dataset). This approach is more elegant than simple feature concatenation, as it allows different classifiers to be trained on different feature types, potentially capturing complementary information.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core methodology**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the complex network approach with word embeddings is largely a direct adaptation of existing techniques (Perozzi et al., 2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate what is new about their specific application beyond the domain shift. A clearer articulation of the specific modifications needed to adapt this approach to clinical speech transcripts would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words, but doesn't compare against more recent approaches for MCI detection from speech, such as deep learning methods that have become prevalent in the field. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess how competitive their approach is with current best practices.

3. **Lack of interpretability analysis**: The paper demonstrates that complex network features work well but doesn't explain *why* they work better than linguistic features. Providing analysis of which network properties (e.g., degree distribution, clustering coefficient) are most discriminative between MCI and control groups would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and provide clinical insights.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that complex networks outperform linguistic features, but doesn't explain why. Could the authors provide an analysis of which specific network properties (e.g., average degree, clustering coefficient) are most discriminative for MCI detection, and how these relate to known linguistic markers of cognitive decline?

2. The authors claim their approach avoids the need for manual transcript correction, but don't quantify the practical impact. Could they provide an estimate of how much time/cost would be saved by using their automated approach versus the manual correction required for linguistic features?

3. The paper uses different threshold parameters (0.7 for Cookie Theft, 0.4 for others). How was this threshold determined, and is it optimized for each dataset or a fixed value? Could the authors clarify this?

4. The paper mentions "a 10% of improvement" in Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier. Could the authors clarify whether this is a relative improvement (10% better than the best individual classifier) or an absolute improvement (accuracy increased by 10 percentage points)?

The paper makes a solid contribution to the application of network theory to clinical language analysis, with promising results. With some strengthening of the novelty argument, more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and additional interpretability analysis, this could be a strong submission.
