- Language-independent method representing speech transcripts as complex (word adjacency) networks that avoids manual correction of disfluencies.  
- Enrichment of complex networks with word embeddings to better represent short texts.  
- Empirical validation showing complex network features outperform linguistic features across multiple datasets, with ensemble and multi-view learning yielding additional gains.  

- Eliminates labor-intensive manual disfluency correction, improving scalability for clinical applications.  
- Robust multi-dataset validation (English and Portuguese) demonstrating generalizability and up to 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset.  
- Language independence enables applicability across languages without language-specific resources.  

- Limited technical novelty, as the approach builds on existing network and embedding methods without clear differentiation.  
- Lacks theoretical explanation for why network features better capture MCI-related signals than linguistic features.  
- Performance gains are modest (max ~10%) and may not be clinically meaningful given similar overall accuracies to prior work.  

- How exactly do complex network features capture MCI-related linguistic patterns and which network properties correlate with MCI?  
- Can you quantify computational efficiency compared to traditional linguistic feature pipelines that require parsers?  
- Are the reported improvements statistically significant (e.g., p-values), particularly small differences like the 5.7% gain on Cookie Theft?
