

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network representations enriched with word embeddings. The authors model transcripts as complex networks and enhance them with semantic information via word embeddings, avoiding the need for manual correction of disfluent speech that plagues traditional linguistic feature extraction. The method is evaluated across three datasets: DementiaBank (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and Arizona-Battery (Portuguese), demonstrating consistent outperformance of linguistic features across all datasets.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) a language-agnostic approach to MCI detection that circumvents the need for manual transcript correction, addressing a critical scalability issue in clinical assessment; (2) empirical validation showing complex network features outperform linguistic features in MCI detection across multiple datasets, with multi-view learning achieving up to 10% improvement in classification accuracy; and (3) a comprehensive evaluation framework that demonstrates the approach's robustness across different linguistic contexts.

Strengths include the practical solution to a real clinical problem (disfluent transcripts), the strong empirical results showing consistent performance improvements across three distinct datasets, and the convincing demonstration that network features are more resilient to disfluencies than linguistic features. The multi-view learning approach that combines different feature spaces represents a particularly valuable contribution to the field.

Weaknesses include insufficient comparison with other graph-based approaches to MCI detection, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their specific network enrichment method. The claim of "language independence" is problematic since word embeddings are language-specific, and the evaluation is limited to English and Portuguese without demonstration of cross-lingual transfer. The paper also lacks discussion of computational complexity compared to simpler methods, which is crucial for clinical deployment.

Questions to authors: 1) How does your approach's computational complexity compare to standard Bag-of-Words methods, and is it feasible for large-scale clinical use? 2) Given that word embeddings are language-specific, how would you address the challenge of applying this method to languages without robust embedding models? 3) Could you clarify which specific disfluencies are most problematic for linguistic features, and why network features are more resilient to them?
