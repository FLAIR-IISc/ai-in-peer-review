

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network features enriched with word embeddings. The authors model transcripts as complex networks (word adjacency networks) and enhance them with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short texts. The main contributions are:

1. A language-independent method for representing speech transcripts as complex networks that avoids the need for manual correction of disfluencies, addressing a significant limitation in prior work.
2. An effective approach to enriching complex networks with word embeddings to handle short text classification, which has been problematic in previous network-based text analysis.
3. Empirical validation showing that complex network features outperform traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese), with ensemble and multi-view learning providing additional improvements.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a critical practical limitation**: The paper successfully tackles a major pain point in MCI detection research - the need for manual correction of disfluencies in speech transcripts. Previous work (e.g., Aluísio et al., 2016) required labor-intensive manual revision to handle disfluencies before applying linguistic features. The authors' approach eliminates this requirement, making the method more scalable for real-world clinical applications. This is a substantial practical contribution that could significantly impact the field.

2. **Robust multi-dataset validation**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft in English, Cinderella and ABCD in Portuguese), showing that the approach generalizes well across different languages and text types. The results show that complex network features outperform linguistic features in all datasets, with the ensemble and multi-view learning approaches providing additional gains (up to 10% improvement in Cinderella dataset). This multi-dataset validation strengthens the claim of the method's generalizability.

3. **Language independence**: Unlike most previous work that focused exclusively on English (Fraser et al., 2015; Lehr et al., 2012), this paper demonstrates the approach works effectively for Portuguese as well. This language independence is a significant advantage, as it means the method can be applied across different linguistic contexts without requiring language-specific resources or manual correction for disfluencies, making it more broadly applicable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the technical approach**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technical approach (complex networks with word embeddings) builds directly on existing work (Perozzi et al., 2014; Amancio et al., 2012a). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from prior work in the NLP literature. The enrichment of networks with word embeddings is a relatively straightforward extension, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this specific application to MCI detection differs significantly from previous network-based text analysis.

2. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper claims complex network features "outperform linguistic features" but provides little explanation for why this might be the case. Without understanding the underlying mechanisms, it's difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach or identify specific aspects that make it effective for MCI detection. The authors should provide more insight into why network features capture MCI-relevant information better than linguistic features.

3. **Modest performance gains**: While the results show consistent improvements, the absolute gains are modest (max 10% improvement over best individual classifier). The accuracy rates (75-80%) are comparable to previous work, and the paper doesn't establish whether these improvements are clinically meaningful. The authors should discuss the clinical relevance of their results, as a 10% improvement might not translate to meaningful clinical utility.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the complex network approach specifically capture MCI-related linguistic patterns that traditional linguistic features miss? Could you provide examples of specific network properties that correlate with MCI symptoms?

2. The paper states that complex networks are "lightweight and language independent." Can you quantify the computational efficiency of your approach compared to traditional linguistic feature extraction pipelines that require parsers?

3. The paper claims "complex networks are suitable to detect MCI and outperform linguistic features in all datasets." However, in the Cookie Theft dataset, the linguistic features (LM) achieved 56.94% accuracy with SVM-Linear, while the best complex network feature (CNE) achieved 62.64%. Given the relatively small difference (5.7%), is this statistically significant? Please provide p-values for the key comparisons.

This paper makes a valuable practical contribution to MCI detection by eliminating the need for manual disfluency correction, and the language independence is particularly noteworthy. While the technical novelty is somewhat limited, the practical implications are significant. With stronger theoretical justification and more discussion of clinical relevance, this work could have a substantial impact in the field.
