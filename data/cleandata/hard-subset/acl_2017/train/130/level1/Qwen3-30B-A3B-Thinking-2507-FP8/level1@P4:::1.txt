

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a method to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel representation of speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with semantic information via word embeddings, (2) empirical demonstration that this approach outperforms linguistic features across three datasets, and (3) the effectiveness of ensemble and multi-view learning for improving classification accuracy.

## Strengths

1. **Practical application with meaningful impact**: The paper addresses an important healthcare challenge - early detection of MCI, which can lead to better intervention opportunities. The approach has potential to contribute to more accessible diagnostic tools for cognitive disorders, particularly given the authors' emphasis on avoiding manual transcript correction.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), representing different languages (English and Portuguese), text lengths, and assessment contexts. This multi-faceted evaluation strengthens the generalizability claims of the method.

3. **Clear technical presentation**: The methodology for constructing and enriching complex networks is well-described, including the preprocessing steps, network characterization metrics, and implementation details. The paper provides sufficient detail to reproduce the core methodology.

4. **Demonstrated performance improvement**: The results consistently show that the proposed complex network approach with word embeddings outperforms linguistic features across all datasets, with the multi-view approach achieving up to 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset compared to the best individual classifier.

5. **Addressing a practical limitation**: The paper successfully addresses the practical limitation of disfluencies in speech transcripts that hinder traditional linguistic feature extraction, which is a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics for healthcare applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty**: The core idea of using complex networks for text representation has been extensively explored in prior work (Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c; de Arruda et al., 2016). The paper fails to clearly articulate how their approach differs from or advances these prior works specifically for MCI detection, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper only compares against linguistic features and BoW representations, omitting comparison to recent deep learning approaches that have become standard for text classification (e.g., CNNs, RNNs, Transformers). This makes it difficult to assess the true competitiveness of the proposed method.

3. **Insufficient justification for key design choices**: The paper does not adequately explain why the specific threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity was chosen, nor does it provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (network construction, enrichment, etc.) to the final performance.

4. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD for Cookie Theft), which limits the statistical significance of the results and may lead to overfitting concerns. The paper does not discuss cross-validation methodology in sufficient detail to address this.

5. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper does not provide any error analysis to understand when the approach succeeds or fails, which would be valuable for understanding the limitations of the method and guiding future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your network enrichment approach specifically differ from the work of Perozzi et al. (2014), which also uses word embeddings to enrich networks? What specific advantages does your adaptation offer for MCI detection?

2. Could you provide more detail about the hyperparameter selection for the network enrichment threshold, including any ablation studies showing how performance changes with different thresholds?

3. The paper claims linguistic features require grammatical texts to perform well (Section 1), but Table 6 shows that with manually revised transcripts, linguistic features performed better. How do you reconcile this apparent contradiction?

4. Have you considered comparing your approach to recent deep learning models for text classification (e.g., BERT, LSTM) that have achieved state-of-the-art results on similar tasks? If not, what are the specific challenges in applying these methods to your task?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the small dataset sizes and the specific types of disfluencies that the method handles well or poorly. The authors should also provide more concrete evidence for their claim about the language independence of their approach, as the datasets are primarily in English and Portuguese.
