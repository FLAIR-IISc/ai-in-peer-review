

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from spontaneous speech. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features are hampered by disfluencies in patient speech, making manual correction necessary for parsing-based approaches. Their main contributions are:

1. A novel method to represent speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with semantic information from word embeddings, which addresses the disfluency problem inherent in clinical speech assessments.
2. Empirical demonstration that this network-based approach outperforms traditional linguistic features across three diverse datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD).
3. Demonstration that multi-view learning combining network features with linguistic features provides additional improvements in classification accuracy.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical justification for complex networks**: The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for using complex networks in this domain, explaining how their structure naturally handles disfluencies that plague traditional linguistic approaches. The authors effectively demonstrate that disfluent speech produces linear networks, which are uninformative for classification, and that semantic enrichment via word embeddings overcomes this limitation (Figure 2). This is a significant methodological insight.

2. **Robust empirical validation across multiple datasets**: The evaluation spans three distinct datasets with different languages (English and Portuguese), different narrative types (short descriptions vs. longer stories), and different clinical protocols. The consistent outperformance of network features across all datasets (Tables 4-7) provides compelling evidence for the generalizability of their approach, addressing a key concern in clinical NLP research.

3. **Practical clinical relevance**: The paper successfully tackles a real-world clinical problem - the need for automated, language-independent MCI screening that doesn't require manual transcript correction. This has clear implications for scaling cognitive assessment in clinical practice, particularly in resource-limited settings where manual review of transcripts is impractical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with modern deep learning approaches**: For an ACL submission, the absence of comparison with contemporary deep learning methods (CNNs, RNNs, or transformers) is a significant weakness. The paper compares with linguistic features and BoW but doesn't address whether their approach would be competitive with state-of-the-art deep learning methods that have become dominant in NLP classification tasks.

2. **Insufficient analysis of feature contributions**: While the paper claims network features outperform linguistic features, it doesn't provide detailed analysis of *why* specific network features (e.g., PageRank, betweenness) are informative. A feature importance analysis or ablation study would strengthen the theoretical contribution by identifying which topological properties are most relevant to MCI detection.

3. **Inconsistent presentation and evaluation**: The results are presented across multiple tables (4-7) with inconsistent inclusion of ensemble/multi-view results. For example, Table 5 includes ensemble results but not multi-view, while Table 7 does the opposite. This makes it difficult to fairly compare the relative performance of different approaches across datasets.

4. **Limited discussion of implementation details**: The paper doesn't adequately address practical implementation concerns. For instance, how does the computational cost of network construction and enrichment compare to linguistic feature extraction? How sensitive is the approach to the choice of word embedding model or the cosine similarity threshold?

5. **Overstated claims about language independence**: While the authors claim their method is "language independent," the word embeddings were trained on language-specific corpora. The paper doesn't discuss how the approach would generalize to languages without existing word embeddings or how the performance might vary with different embedding sources.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach compare with recent deep learning methods (e.g., BERT fine-tuning) that have achieved state-of-the-art results on text classification tasks? Have you considered applying these methods to the same datasets?

2. The paper states that the network features outperform linguistic features, but doesn't explain which specific network features are most informative. Could you provide feature importance analysis or identify which topological measurements are most discriminative for MCI detection?

3. In Table 7, BoW with SVM-RBF achieves 75.47% accuracy, which is better than the network features. Why does the paper emphasize network features as the primary contribution when BoW performs better on the ABCD dataset?

4. The paper claims the method is "language independent," but word embeddings were language-specific. How would the approach perform on languages without pre-trained embeddings, and what would be the practical implications for global deployment?

5. The paper mentions that network features work better because they handle disfluencies, but doesn't provide evidence of how the network features specifically handle disfluencies compared to linguistic features. Could you elaborate on this mechanism?
