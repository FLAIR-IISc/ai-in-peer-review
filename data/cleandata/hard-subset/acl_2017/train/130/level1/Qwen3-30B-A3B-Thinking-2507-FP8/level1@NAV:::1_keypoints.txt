- Proposed method: model speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI, avoiding manual correction of disfluent speech.  
- Implementation: transcripts represented as networks with semantic enrichment via embeddings; evaluated on DementiaBank (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and Arizona-Battery (Portuguese).  
- Empirical result: complex network features consistently outperform linguistic features across all datasets; multi-view learning can boost accuracy by up to ~10%.  
- Contribution 1: language-agnostic approach that mitigates manual transcript correction, addressing scalability in clinical assessment.  
- Contribution 2: empirical validation that network features surpass linguistic features and that multi-view fusion improves classification.  
- Contribution 3: comprehensive evaluation showing robustness across different languages/contexts.  
- Strength: practical solution to handling disfluent clinical transcripts.  
- Strength: strong, consistent performance gains across three diverse datasets.  
- Strength: multi-view learning combining feature spaces is a valuable addition.  
- Weakness: limited comparison with other graph-based MCI detection methods, leaving novelty of enrichment method unclear.  
- Weakness: claim of “language independence” is problematic because word embeddings are language-specific and no cross-lingual transfer is demonstrated.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of computational complexity versus simpler methods, which matters for clinical deployment.  
- Question 1: how does computational complexity compare to standard Bag-of-Words and is the method feasible at large clinical scale?  
- Question 2: how would the approach be applied to languages without robust word embedding models?  
- Question 3: which specific disfluencies most undermine linguistic features, and why are network features more resilient?
