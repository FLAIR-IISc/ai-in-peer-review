- Proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI, aiming to be robust to disfluencies.  
- Main contributions: language-independent framework avoiding manual disfluency correction; superior complex network features (especially with embeddings) over linguistic features across three datasets; effective ensemble/multi-view combination with linguistic and BoW features.  
- Practical clinical relevance: avoids costly manual transcript correction and addresses a real problem in automatic cognitive assessment.  
- Strong empirical validation across three diverse datasets (English and Portuguese) with meaningful accuracy improvements (e.g., up to 10% on Cinderella).  
- Successful integration of multiple feature spaces via multi-view learning, yielding significant accuracy gains (e.g., 4.71% on ABCD).  
- Limited novelty: approach largely adapts existing techniques (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014) and lacks clear articulation of specific methodological innovations for clinical speech.  
- Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art: missing comparisons to recent/deep learning methods for MCI detection.  
- Lack of interpretability analysis: no examination of which network properties drive discrimination or how they relate to linguistic markers of cognitive decline.  
- Question: which specific network properties (e.g., average degree, clustering) are most discriminative and how do they relate to known markers?  
- Question: can authors quantify time/cost savings from avoiding manual transcript correction?  
- Question: how were embedding threshold parameters (0.7 vs 0.4) chosen—dataset-specific or fixed/optimized?  
- Question: clarify whether the reported “10% improvement” is relative or absolute (percentage points).  
- Overall: promising application of network theory to clinical language analysis but needs stronger novelty claims, state-of-the-art comparisons, and interpretability to be a stronger paper.
