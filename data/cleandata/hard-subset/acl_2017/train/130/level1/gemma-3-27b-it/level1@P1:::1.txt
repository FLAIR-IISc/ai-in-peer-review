This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare their approach to traditional linguistic features across three datasets (English and two Portuguese datasets), demonstrating competitive and, in some cases, superior performance.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution lies in successfully applying complex network analysis, a relatively unexplored technique in this specific domain, to the problem of MCI detection from speech. This offers a potentially robust alternative to methods heavily reliant on parsing, which is susceptible to errors from disfluencies common in patient speech.
2. **Effective Combination of Network Features with Word Embeddings:** Enriching the co-occurrence networks with word embeddings significantly improves performance, suggesting that incorporating semantic information is crucial when dealing with short, potentially fragmented, transcripts.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation across Multiple Datasets:** The evaluation across three datasets, including datasets in both English and Portuguese, strengthens the generalizability of the findings and demonstrates the method’s applicability to different languages. The inclusion of a manually revised dataset also provides valuable insight into the impact of data quality on performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The use of complex networks for MCI detection is a novel and promising approach. While complex networks have been used in NLP, applying them to the specific problem of analyzing speech transcripts from patients with cognitive impairment represents a valuable contribution.
2. **Addressing a Practical Challenge:** The method’s ability to function effectively without requiring extensive manual pre-processing (e.g., correcting disfluencies) is a significant advantage. This makes it more scalable and practical for large-scale assessments.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate that the proposed approach is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, traditional linguistic feature-based methods. The improvements achieved through ensemble and multi-view learning further highlight the effectiveness of the approach. Specifically, the 4.71% improvement on the Arizona dataset is noteworthy.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The study includes a comprehensive evaluation on three different datasets, which are described in detail. Comparing results across different datasets and languages is a strength.
5. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the methods are described clearly, making it relatively easy to understand and potentially reproduce the experiments. The inclusion of supplementary material detailing the datasets and features is also appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Feature Selection:** While the paper lists several topological measures used to characterize the networks, it lacks a detailed explanation of *why* these specific measures were chosen and how they are expected to relate to cognitive impairment. A more theoretically grounded justification for the selected features would strengthen the work.
2. **Superficial Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on overall accuracy. A more in-depth error analysis, examining specific cases where the method fails, could provide valuable insights for future improvements. Understanding *which* types of linguistic patterns are missed or misinterpreted would be beneficial.
3. **Dataset Imbalance:** The datasets are imbalanced (e.g. Cookie Theft), which could bias the results. While 5-fold cross-validation is used, it’s unclear if stratified cross-validation was employed to address this imbalance. Reporting performance metrics beyond accuracy (e.g., precision, recall, F1-score) would provide a more complete picture.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Although comparisons are made to some prior work, a more comprehensive comparison to current state-of-the-art methods for MCI detection (especially those using deep learning techniques) would be valuable.
5. **Word Embedding Details:** More detail on the pre-trained word embedding model used (e.g. training data, dimensions) would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding the potential impact of the embeddings on the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical rationale behind choosing the specific network topological features used in this study? How do you hypothesize these features relate to linguistic changes associated with MCI?
2.  Was stratified cross-validation used to address potential class imbalance in the datasets? If not, how might this imbalance have affected the reported results?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific examples where the model fails and discussing potential reasons for these failures?
4.  What specific pre-trained word embedding model was used (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe)? Please provide details regarding its training data and dimensions.
5.  The paper mentions manually revising the Cinderella dataset to improve parsing. How does the performance of your network-based approach *compare* to the results reported in Aluı́sio et al. (2016) *using their manually revised dataset* with traditional linguistic features? This would directly demonstrate the advantage of your method even with cleaner data.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of each dataset (e.g., sample size, average transcript length, demographics).
*   Clarify the meaning of the "-" sign in the results tables. Does it indicate that the calculation was not possible or that the result was not reported?
