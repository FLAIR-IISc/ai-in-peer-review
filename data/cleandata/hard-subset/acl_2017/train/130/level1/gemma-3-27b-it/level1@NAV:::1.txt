## Review of “Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts”

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors explore the performance of this approach alongside traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations across three datasets – one English and two Portuguese – using various classification algorithms, including ensemble and multi-view learning.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the application and demonstration of complex network analysis as a viable method for MCI detection from transcribed speech, particularly in scenarios where transcript quality is compromised by disfluencies. While complex networks have been used in NLP, applying this specifically to the problem of MCI detection, alongside word embeddings to address short text limitations, is a valuable contribution. The inclusion of multiple datasets, including a Portuguese language component, also broadens the scope and potential impact.  A secondary contribution is the empirical comparison demonstrating that complex network features can outperform traditional linguistic features, specifically in the context of noisy speech transcripts. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of complex network analysis to MCI detection from speech transcripts represents a relatively unexplored avenue. The combination with word embeddings is a sensible approach to tackle the challenges posed by short, potentially disfluent narratives.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the method across three datasets, including varying languages (English and Portuguese), increases the robustness and generalizability of the findings. This is particularly strong given the linguistic challenges of applying NLP techniques across languages.
3. **Outperformance of Linguistic Features:** The consistent finding that complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features is significant. This suggests a potential advantage in dealing with the noise inherent in speech transcripts from individuals with cognitive impairment.
4. **Effective Use of Ensemble and Multi-View Learning:** The improvements gained through ensemble and multi-view learning demonstrate a thoughtful approach to leveraging the strengths of different feature sets and classifiers. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Construction:** The paper lacks a deeper discussion of *why* complex networks are effective in this context. A more detailed analysis of the network properties that correlate with MCI could strengthen the contribution.
2. **Superficial Discussion of Word Embeddings:**  The paper briefly mentions the use of word embeddings, but the rationale for selecting the specific embedding model and its impact on performance is not adequately discussed.
3. **Dataset Imbalance:** While the authors acknowledge dataset sizes, the potential impact of class imbalance (varying numbers of MCI and control participants) on the results isn’t fully addressed. This could affect the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Lack of Clinical Significance Discussion:** The reported accuracy improvements (e.g., 4.71% on the Arizona dataset) are presented without discussion of their clinical relevance. Is this improvement meaningful in a practical diagnostic setting?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more insights into the specific network properties (e.g., centrality measures, clustering coefficient) that are most strongly correlated with MCI status in your experiments?
2. What were the considerations for choosing the specific word embedding model used (Bojanowski et al., 2016)? Did you experiment with other embedding approaches, and if so, how did they compare?
3. How did you address the potential impact of class imbalance in your datasets, if at all?
4. Could you discuss the clinical significance of the reported accuracy improvements?  What would a practical improvement of 4.71% in detection accuracy translate to in terms of patient care or diagnosis?
