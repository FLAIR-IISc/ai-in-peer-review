This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare this approach to traditional linguistic features and explore ensemble/multi-view learning techniques. The core idea of leveraging network representations to circumvent the challenges of noisy, disfluent speech is a strong one, and the evaluation across multiple datasets, including Portuguese datasets, is commendable.

**Contributions:**

1. **Application of Complex Networks with Word Embeddings to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex network analysis, specifically enriched with word embeddings, to the task of MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been used in NLP, this is a novel application to the challenging domain of analyzing speech from individuals with cognitive impairment, where traditional parsing-based methods struggle.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation, including Portuguese:** The evaluation across three datasets—DementiaBank (English), Cinderella, and Arizona Battery (both Portuguese)—is a significant contribution. The inclusion of Portuguese datasets is particularly valuable, addressing a gap in research often dominated by English language resources. This broadens the generalizability of the findings.
3. **Demonstration of Improved Performance with Ensemble/Multi-View Learning:** The authors demonstrate that combining the different feature sets (complex networks, linguistic features, BoW) using ensemble and multi-view learning techniques leads to performance improvements, particularly on the Cinderella and Arizona datasets. This indicates a synergistic benefit from integrating different aspects of the data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Robustness to Disfluencies:** The motivation for using complex networks is well articulated: traditional NLP methods relying on parsing are susceptible to errors caused by disfluencies common in MCI patient speech. Complex networks offer a potentially more robust, language-independent representation.  The results, particularly the comparative performance to linguistic features, suggest this is a valid approach.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough evaluation framework, using 5-fold cross-validation and reporting results across multiple datasets and with different classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes). This strengthens the credibility of the findings.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The description of the methodology, including the network construction process, word embedding enrichment, and feature extraction, is relatively clear and well-defined.  The authors provide references to relevant tools (Coh-Metrix, DeepBond).
4. **Practical Relevance:** MCI detection is a crucial area of research, and the development of automated tools for this purpose has significant clinical implications. The potential to reduce reliance on manual transcription and parsing is a practical benefit.
5. **Addressing Dataset Limitations:**  The authors acknowledge and attempt to mitigate the issues of dataset size and noise (disfluencies) and also address that manual revision can impact generalization capability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Network Approach Itself:** While the *application* to MCI detection is novel, the underlying complex network methodology itself is well-established. The paper doesn’t introduce significant new advancements in network analysis; it's the application domain and the combination with word embeddings that are the primary novelty. This weakens the overall contribution.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Word Embedding Implementation:** The description of how word embeddings are integrated into the network is somewhat vague.  Specifically, the cosine similarity threshold used for creating edges between unconnected nodes is not justified with experimentation and may require tuning for optimal performance. A sensitivity analysis of this threshold would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:**  The results are presented as accuracy scores, but there’s no statistical significance testing to confirm whether the observed improvements are statistically significant.  Reporting p-values or confidence intervals would enhance the rigor of the evaluation.
4. **Overly Optimistic Interpretation of Ensemble/Multi-View Results:** While improvements are reported, the gains aren't consistently substantial across all datasets. The authors should temper their claims regarding the significance of these results and acknowledge potential overfitting or dataset-specific effects.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying *which* types of errors the model makes (e.g., misclassifying specific types of disfluencies, struggling with certain semantic concepts) could provide valuable insights for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What was the rationale behind choosing a cosine similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings? Did you experiment with different thresholds, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the network construction and feature extraction process, particularly for larger datasets?
3.  Why was the DeepBond sentence segmenter chosen for the Arizona dataset, and were its performance characteristics assessed for this specific type of transcript?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embedding model? Did you explore alternative embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText)?
5.  Can you provide a qualitative example of a transcript that was correctly classified by your model and one that was incorrectly classified, highlighting the features that contributed to each outcome?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The related work section could be more focused on studies that have specifically addressed the challenges of analyzing disfluent speech in the context of cognitive impairment.
*   The formatting of the tables could be improved for readability (e.g., consistent decimal places, clearer column headings).
