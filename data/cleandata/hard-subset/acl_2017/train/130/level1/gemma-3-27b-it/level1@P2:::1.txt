This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea—modeling transcripts as networks to capture relational information and augmenting them with semantic embeddings—is a reasonable approach, given the challenges of parsing agrammatical speech from MCI patients.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is applying complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, as a feature representation for MCI detection. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application specifically to *this* task and the inclusion of word embeddings to address short text limitations are novel.
2. **Performance Outperforms Linguistic Features:** The authors demonstrate that their network-based features consistently outperform traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets. This is a significant finding, suggesting that the network approach is more robust to the disfluencies inherent in MCI patient speech.
3. **Effective Combination of Methods:** The authors successfully combine complex network features with linguistic features and bag-of-words representations through ensemble and multi-view learning, achieving further performance improvements, particularly on the Cinderella and ABCD datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents clear and compelling experimental results on three datasets (DementiaBank, Cinderella, and ABCD), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The consistent outperformance of network features over linguistic features is particularly noteworthy.
2. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** MCI diagnosis is a challenging clinical problem. The approach presented tackles the difficulty of automatically analyzing imperfect, disfluent speech, a major hurdle in scaling up automated assessments.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors explored a wide range of classification algorithms (GaussianNB, k-NN, SVM, RF) and feature combinations (ensemble, multi-view learning), showcasing a thorough experimental evaluation. This rigorous approach strengthens the validity of their findings.
4. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Testing the approach on multiple datasets, including English and Portuguese, enhances the generalizability of the results and demonstrates the potential for wider application.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Methodology:** While the *application* is novel, the specific complex network construction and feature extraction techniques are relatively standard. The authors don't introduce any significant innovations in network science itself. The enhancements with word embeddings are a logical step, but not fundamentally groundbreaking.
2. **Lack of Deep Linguistic Analysis:** The paper focuses on avoiding linguistic parsing due to disfluencies, but a more in-depth exploration of *what* types of linguistic features are most affected by disfluencies and how network features might implicitly capture that information could be valuable.
3. **Dataset Characteristics and Bias:** The demographic information presented for the datasets is limited. There’s a potential for dataset bias (e.g., differences in the populations represented) to influence the results. A more detailed discussion of dataset characteristics and potential biases would be beneficial.
4. **Hyperparameter Optimization Details:** While the paper mentions optimizing the threshold parameter, more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the various classifiers and embedding models would increase reproducibility and understanding of the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the types of disfluencies present in the Portuguese datasets and how the network-based approach handles them compared to standard linguistic feature extraction?
2.  Were any methods employed to specifically address potential biases in the datasets (e.g., age, education level)? If so, please describe them.
3.  Can you provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and methodology used for each classifier and embedding model?
4.  You state that the lemmatization process can introduce noise. Were any other preprocessing steps, such as stop word removal or stemming, explored and ultimately excluded for similar reasons?



**Additional Comments:**

* The supplementary material is helpful for understanding the datasets and features used.
* The formatting and clarity of the paper are generally good.
* The related work section is reasonably comprehensive.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a promising approach to a challenging problem. While the methodological novelty is somewhat limited, the strong empirical results, practical relevance, and thorough experimentation justify acceptance. The authors adequately address the core challenges of analyzing disfluent speech and demonstrate the effectiveness of their network-based feature representation.
