This paper investigates the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent transcripts as networks, leveraging both word co-occurrence and semantic relationships captured by word embeddings, and then use these network-based features for classification. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, comparing it to linguistic features and exploring ensemble methods.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that complex network features, particularly when enriched with word embeddings, can effectively discriminate between individuals with and without MCI, and often outperform traditional linguistic features. The application of network analysis to this specific problem domain, where speech disfluencies pose challenges for parsing-based approaches, is valuable. A second notable contribution is the evaluation across three datasets (English and Portuguese), providing a broader assessment of the method’s generalizability. Finally, the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning to combine different feature sets (network, linguistic, and bag-of-words) is a practical and insightful addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Feature Representation:** The use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, offers a novel approach to MCI detection from speech. This is particularly significant given the challenges posed by speech disfluencies in this population. Traditional NLP methods heavily rely on accurate parsing, which can be compromised by such disfluencies, whereas the network-based approach is less sensitive to these issues.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show that the complex network features achieve competitive or superior performance compared to traditional linguistic features across all three datasets. The gains, particularly in the Cinderella dataset with the ensemble methods (10% improvement), demonstrate the potential of this approach. The 80% accuracy on the Arizona dataset is also a strong result.
3. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets, spanning different languages (English and Portuguese) and elicitation methods (picture description, narrative retelling), strengthens the robustness and generalizability of the findings.  The inclusion of datasets with varying characteristics (size, manually/automatically segmented) adds to the validity of the study.
4. **Thorough Comparison and Ablation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive comparison of different features (network, linguistic, BoW) and classification algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes).  The exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning further adds to the thoroughness of the evaluation, revealing the benefits of combining different approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Construction Details:** While the paper describes the overall approach of constructing complex networks, the specific parameter choices and rationale behind them are not fully detailed. For instance, the threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity of word embeddings is mentioned but not justified. A more detailed discussion of how these parameters influence performance would be beneficial.
2. **Data Imbalance and Class Distribution:** The datasets exhibit varying degrees of class imbalance (e.g., the Cookie Theft dataset). The impact of this imbalance on the classification results is not explicitly addressed. While 5-fold cross-validation is used, it's unclear if stratified cross-validation was employed to ensure a balanced representation of each class in each fold.
3. **Lack of Baseline Comparison with State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison is primarily against linguistic features. A comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for MCI detection from speech (e.g., those utilizing deep learning approaches, such as recurrent neural networks or transformers) would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed method.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the classification results. Identifying specific types of errors (e.g., false positives, false negatives) and the characteristics of the misclassified transcripts could provide valuable insights for improving the model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for the parameter choices made during the construction of the complex networks, particularly the threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity?
2. Was stratified cross-validation used during the evaluation process? If not, could you comment on the potential impact of class imbalance on the reported results?
3. Could you compare your approach to more recent state-of-the-art methods for MCI detection from speech, such as those based on deep learning?
4. Could you provide an error analysis of the classification results, identifying common patterns among the misclassified transcripts?
