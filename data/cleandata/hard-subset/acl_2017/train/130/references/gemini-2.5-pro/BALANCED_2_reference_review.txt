### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling the texts as complex networks. The core method involves creating word co-occurrence graphs and enriching them with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. This enrichment is designed to make the network representation more robust for the short and disfluent texts common in clinical assessments. The authors evaluate their enriched complex network (CNE) features against traditional linguistic metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) baselines on three different datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) with varying text lengths and elicitation tasks. The results show that the CNE features are competitive and, when combined with other feature sets using multi-view learning, can achieve state-of-the-art or comparable performance in classifying MCI vs. control subjects.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Novel Application of Enriched Complex Networks for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application and adaptation of complex network models for the clinical task of MCI detection. While using networks for text analysis is not new, the specific idea of enriching word co-occurrence graphs with word embedding similarity to overcome the challenges of short, noisy clinical transcripts is a well-motivated and valuable contribution to the field of computational health.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation across Multiple Datasets:** The study provides a thorough comparison of the proposed network-based method against strong baselines (linguistic features from Coh-Metrix, BoW) across three distinct datasets. This evaluation, which spans two languages (English and Portuguese) and different narrative tasks, strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
3.  **Demonstration of a Computationally Robust Method:** The paper effectively argues that the proposed network-based features are less reliant on grammatically clean text than traditional linguistic feature extractors, which often depend on parsers that fail on disfluent speech. This highlights a key practical advantage for building fully automated diagnostic tools.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of identifying a critical weakness in existing NLP methods for clinical text analysis: their fragility when faced with the short, disfluent, and grammatically inconsistent nature of patient speech. The proposed CNE method is presented as a direct and elegant solution to this problem, creating denser, more informative representations from sparse data. The visualization in Figure 2 effectively illustrates this conceptual strength.
2.  **Strong and Diverse Experimental Setup:** The evaluation on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) is a significant strength. It allows the authors to test their method's performance across different languages, elicitation methods (picture description vs. story recall), and text lengths. Comparing against established feature sets like those from Coh-Metrix and the standard BoW model provides a solid benchmark for assessing the performance of the CNE features.
3.  **Practical Implications for Automated Systems:** The research moves towards a more robust, fully-automated system for MCI screening. By showing that CNE features perform well without the extensive manual cleaning (e.g., sentence correction, disfluency removal) often required for robust linguistic feature extraction (as demonstrated by comparing results in Tables 5 and 6), the work presents a method with high practical potential for real-world clinical applications.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Confounding Variables in Datasets:** The validity of the classification results is threatened by potential demographic confounds. In the ABCD dataset, where the highest accuracy (80%) is reported, the control group is on average 11 years younger and has significantly more education than the MCI group (Table 3). Age and education are strongly correlated with both linguistic ability and cognitive health. It is highly plausible that the classifier is learning these demographic differences rather than true linguistic markers of MCI. This issue needs to be addressed, for instance by controlling for these variables or at least discussing their potential impact on the results. The description of the Cookie Theft dataset is also confusing regarding the control group size and characteristics.
2.  **Insufficient Detail and Justification for Methodological Choices:** Several key experimental details are missing. Crucially, the paper does not specify what corpus was used to train the word embeddings. If trained on the small in-domain datasets, the embeddings would likely be of poor quality; if trained on a large external corpus, there could be a domain mismatch. This choice has a major impact on the method's validity and reproducibility. Furthermore, the choice of Term Frequency for the BoW baseline is not justified over the more standard TF-IDF, which might provide a stronger baseline.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper presents classification accuracies but stops short of providing insight into *why* the method works. The network features consist of statistical moments (mean, std, skewness) of 10 different topological metrics, but there is no analysis of which features are most discriminative. An ablation study or feature importance analysis would have been highly beneficial. For example, does the network diameter, clustering coefficient, or assortativity consistently differ between MCI and control groups? Connecting the network properties back to the cognitive phenomena of MCI would have significantly elevated the paper's contribution from a methodological demonstration to a piece of insightful scientific research.
4.  **Overstated Claims and Lack of Statistical Testing:** The conclusion that CNE features "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" is not consistently supported by the results. For example, in Table 7, the BoW baseline achieves a higher accuracy (0.7547) than CNE (0.7173) with an SVM-Linear classifier. In Table 6, LM outperforms CNE with several classifiers. The improvements gained from multi-view learning are often modest and are presented without statistical significance tests, making it difficult to ascertain if they represent a genuine improvement or are artifacts of the cross-validation splits on small datasets. The claims should be toned down to more accurately reflect the nuanced results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the word embeddings (Bojanowski et al., 2016)? Specifically, what corpus were they trained on, and how did you address potential issues of data sparsity (if trained in-domain) or domain mismatch (if using a general pre-trained model)?
2.  The demographic information for the ABCD dataset shows large differences in average age and education between the MCI and control groups. Given that these factors are known to influence linguistic performance and are risk factors for MCI, how can you be sure your classifier is learning linguistic markers of cognitive decline rather than simply discriminating based on these demographic confounds?
3.  Your conclusion makes a strong claim that CNE features outperform linguistic metrics. However, your results appear more mixed, with different feature sets performing best depending on the dataset and classifier. Could you provide a more nuanced discussion of these results and conduct statistical significance tests to validate whether the observed differences between methods are meaningful?
