**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing the text as a complex network. The core idea is to model transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and then "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on a cosine similarity threshold applied to their word embeddings. The authors then extract a set of standard topological graph metrics (e.g., PageRank, clustering coefficient) from these enriched networks and use them as features for a machine learning classifier. The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  An application of complex network analysis, specifically word co-occurrence graphs augmented with word embedding similarity, to the clinical task of MCI detection from speech.
2.  An empirical comparison of these network-based features against established baselines—linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and Bag-of-Words (BoW)—on three small clinical datasets in English and Portuguese.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important clinical problem. The motivation for exploring methods that are less reliant on clean, grammatical text is sound, as clinical speech transcripts are notoriously noisy and filled with disfluencies, which can impair traditional NLP tools like syntactic parsers.
2.  **Inclusion of Standard Baselines:** The authors are to be commended for comparing their proposed feature set against strong, established baselines. The use of a comprehensive set of linguistic metrics (LM) from Coh-Metrix and a standard Bag-of-Words (BoW) model provides a necessary and valuable point of reference for evaluating the performance of the proposed network-based features.
3.  **Evaluation across Multiple Datasets:** The study uses three distinct datasets, covering two different languages and various elicitation tasks (picture description, story recall). This provides a more diverse testing ground than a single dataset would, lending some additional weight to the experimental findings.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Inconsistent Empirical Support for the Central Claim:** The paper's primary assertion is that its proposed complex network features (CNE) are a superior alternative to traditional methods for this task. The results, however, do not consistently support this claim. In two of the four experimental settings, the proposed method is outperformed by simpler, more established baselines.
    *   On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline achieves a substantially higher accuracy than CNE (75.47% vs. 71.73%).
    *   On the manually revised Cinderella dataset (Table 6), the standard linguistic features (LM) outperform CNE (72.50% vs. 67.50%). This particular result is highly problematic, as it seems to contradict the core motivation of the paper. The network approach is justified by its supposed robustness to noise; yet, when the text is cleaned, the traditional linguistic features prove more effective. This suggests that the network features may not be capturing linguistic phenomena as robustly as established methods on cleaner text.
    *   The claim in the conclusion that "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics, in individual classifiers results" is a significant overstatement based on the presented evidence.

2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed method is a straightforward combination of existing techniques. Word co-occurrence networks are a well-known model in NLP. The concept of using word embedding similarity to connect nodes in a graph is not new (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). Furthermore, the features extracted from the network are all standard, off-the-shelf graph metrics. While applying this combination to a new domain is a contribution, the technical novelty of the approach itself is minimal.

3.  **Lack of Analysis and Ablation:** The paper presents the method and results but offers little insight into *why* the network features work (or don't work). The network enrichment depends on a similarity threshold, which is a critical hyperparameter, yet its selection is only briefly mentioned as having been "optimized." There is no analysis of the sensitivity to this threshold or what types of semantic links are being added. It is unclear which of the many topological features are most discriminative. The approach is treated as a black box, which limits its scientific contribution. A more in-depth analysis of the generated graphs or a feature importance analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper.

4.  **Small-Scale Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on very small datasets (N=40 for Cinderella, N=43 subjects for ABCD, N=86 for Cookie Theft). While access to clinical data is often limited, conclusions drawn from such small sample sizes must be treated with extreme caution. The reported differences in accuracy between methods are often only a few percentage points, which may not be statistically significant given the small test sets in a 5-fold cross-validation setup. The paper makes no attempt to assess statistical significance, making it difficult to determine whether observed differences are real or due to chance.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The results on the cleaned Cinderella dataset (Table 6) show that standard linguistic features (LM) are superior to your proposed CNE features. This appears to undermine the primary motivation that network methods are more robust to the noise in raw transcripts. How do you reconcile this finding with the central claims of your paper? Does this suggest that for cleaner text, established linguistic analysis is still the preferred method?
2.  On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the much simpler and more traditional Bag-of-Words baseline outperforms your proposed CNE approach. Given this, what is the justification for the added complexity of building and analyzing enriched complex networks when a simpler method proves more effective on the same data?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the selection of the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment? Was this hyperparameter tuned on a dedicated development set, or within the cross-validation loop? How sensitive is the model's performance to variations in this threshold? An analysis of this sensitivity would be crucial for understanding the robustness of your method.
