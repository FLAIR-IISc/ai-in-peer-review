### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The primary contribution is a technique to enrich standard word co-occurrence networks with semantic edges based on word embedding similarity (CNE), which effectively addresses the problem of network sparsity in short texts common to clinical assessments. The authors extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification. The method is comprehensively evaluated on three datasets in English and Portuguese, with varying text lengths. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Network Enrichment Method:** The introduction of the CNE representation, which augments syntactic adjacency graphs with semantic links from word embeddings, is a well-motivated approach to make graph-based text analysis more viable for short, disfluent clinical transcripts. This reduces dependency on fragile syntactic parsers.
2.  **Extensive Cross-Lingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** The authors validate their approach on three distinct datasets: short English descriptions (Cookie Theft), longer Portuguese narratives (Cinderella), and very short Portuguese story recalls (ABCD). This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the proposed features.
3.  **Effective Use of Multi-View Learning:** The paper shows that the proposed CNE features are complementary to traditional linguistic features (LM) and bag-of-words (BoW). A multi-view learning approach combining these feature sets consistently achieves the best performance, yielding strong results (e.g., 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset).

### Strengths

1.  **Innovative and Well-Motivated Methodology:** The core idea of enriching word adjacency networks with semantic information from word embeddings is a strong contribution. It directly addresses a key limitation of applying graph-based methods to short texts by creating denser, more informative network representations. This approach is particularly suitable for clinical speech transcripts, which are often short and contain disfluencies that break traditional syntactic parsing tools. The method's reduced reliance on complex linguistic preprocessing is a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The evaluation is a major strength. By testing on three datasets with different characteristics (language, task, length), the authors provide convincing evidence for the method's versatility. The inclusion of strong, standard baselines (Coh-Metrix-based linguistic features and BoW) allows for a clear contextualization of the results. The exploration of multiple classifiers and learning paradigms (single-view, ensemble, multi-view) provides a comprehensive picture of the proposed features' utility.

3.  **Strong and Promising Results:** The results consistently demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. The enriched networks (CNE) almost always outperform the basic co-occurrence networks (CN). More importantly, the multi-view models that combine CNE with other feature types achieve the highest accuracies across all datasets. This indicates that the topological features capture a different and complementary signal of cognitive impairment compared to established linguistic metrics, making them a valuable addition to the diagnostic toolkit.

4.  **Clarity and Potential for Impact:** The paper is well-written and the method is explained clearly, with Figure 2 providing an excellent intuitive illustration of the network enrichment process. The work has high potential for practical impact, as it represents a step towards more robust, automated systems for MCI screening that are less dependent on manual data cleaning or fragile NLP pipelines.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Analysis of the Enrichment Method:** The CNE method hinges on a cosine similarity threshold, which is presented as an "optimized" hyperparameter without further analysis. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this threshold. This would provide crucial insight into the method's robustness. Furthermore, there is no discussion on the degree of enrichment (e.g., how many edges are typically added) and whether this property itself correlates with MCI status.

2.  **No Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports improvements in accuracy (e.g., "a 2.3% of improvement") without providing any statistical tests to validate these claims. Given the small sample sizes of the datasets (e.g., N=40 for Cinderella), it is essential to demonstrate that the observed improvements, particularly from the multi-view models over the best single-feature models, are statistically significant. The absence of such tests (e.g., McNemar's test, bootstrapping) makes it difficult to confidently conclude the superiority of the proposed combinations.

3.  **Limited Interpretability of Features:** The work successfully demonstrates that CNE features are useful for classification but stops short of investigating *why*. The paper extracts around 30 topological features but offers no analysis of their relative importance. An ablation study or feature importance analysis (e.g., from the Random Forest model) could reveal which network properties (e.g., centrality, path length, clustering) are most affected by MCI. Such an analysis would provide valuable cognitive and linguistic insights, elevating the paper from a strong engineering contribution to one with deeper scientific implications. For example, does a change in network diameter reflect a less coherent narrative?

4.  **Minor Ambiguities in Experimental Details:** The description of the datasets could be clearer. For the Cookie Theft dataset, the number of control subjects is not explicitly stated in the text, and the caption for Table 1 mistakenly refers to the "Cinderella dataset". The comparison between the automatically processed and manually revised versions of the Cinderella dataset (Tables 5 vs. 6) is interesting but would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the specific revisions and their impact on different feature types.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the number of control and MCI participants for the Cookie Theft dataset? There also appears to be a typo in the caption for Table 1.
2.  The similarity threshold for adding semantic edges is a critical hyperparameter. Could you provide an analysis of the model's sensitivity to this value? For instance, how does accuracy on one of the datasets change as the threshold is varied?
3.  Have you performed any statistical tests to verify that the performance gains from the multi-view learning models are significant compared to the best-performing single-feature models?
4.  Can you provide any insight into which of the ten network metrics (or their summary statistics) were most discriminative? Understanding which topological properties are most indicative of MCI would greatly enhance the interpretability of your findings.
