### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To overcome the problem of sparsity in networks generated from short texts, the authors "enrich" these graphs by adding new edges between non-adjacent words that are semantically similar, based on a cosine similarity threshold applied to their word embeddings. A set of ten topological metrics (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, diameter), summarized by their mean, standard deviation, and skewness, are extracted from these enriched networks. These features are then used to train various machine learning classifiers to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) of varying lengths and elicitation tasks. The performance of the proposed complex network features is compared against traditional linguistic metrics and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations, both individually and in multi-view learning combinations.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings as a feature representation method for the clinical NLP task of MCI detection. This introduces a novel approach to a domain that often relies on either superficial lexical features or complex, brittle linguistic features. The enrichment strategy is a sensible way to adapt network analysis for the short texts common in this setting.
2.  A comparative experimental study across three distinct datasets covering two languages. The paper systematically evaluates the proposed network features against standard baselines (linguistic metrics and BoW) and explores the utility of combining different feature types using multi-view learning, providing a useful benchmark for this feature representation method.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Proposed Feature Representation:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel application of word-embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI detection. This approach moves beyond traditional BoW models by capturing word relationships, and it offers a potential advantage over deep linguistic feature extraction by not relying on syntactic parsers that are often fragile when applied to disfluent, transcribed speech. The specific method of enriching a co-occurrence graph with semantic links from word embeddings to densify the network for short texts is a well-motivated and technically sound contribution.
2.  **Potential for Robustness to Noisy Data:** The proposed method is inherently less dependent on grammatically well-formed text compared to methods that rely on syntactic analysis. Clinical transcripts are often filled with disfluencies, repetitions, and incomplete sentences, which can cause parsers and other linguistic analysis tools to fail. By operating on word co-occurrence and semantic similarity, the network-based approach may be more robust. The authors' results on the Cinderella dataset support this: linguistic features (LM) see a dramatic performance increase on manually cleaned text (from ~50% to 72.5%), while the enriched network features (CNE) are more stable (65% on raw vs. 67.5% on cleaned), suggesting they are less affected by the presence of ungrammatical structures.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation of their method. They use three separate datasets that differ in language (English, Portuguese), task (picture description, story recall), and text length. This helps to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. Furthermore, they compare their proposed features against two strong and distinct baselines (Linguistic Metrics and BoW) and test a variety of machine learning classifiers. The inclusion of multi-view learning experiments, which combine the different feature sets, provides a realistic assessment of how these features might be used in a practical system and shows that they can provide complementary information.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Interpretability and Analysis of Network Features:** While the paper successfully demonstrates that topological network features can be effective for classification, it provides no analysis or intuition as to *why* these features are discriminative. The paper lists ten network metrics but does not connect them to the cognitive or linguistic deficits characteristic of MCI. For example, does a change in the network's diameter or clustering coefficient correspond to a known linguistic phenomenon in MCI, such as reduced semantic coherence or syntactic complexity? Without this link, the features act as a black box. A feature importance analysis or an error analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper by providing insight into which topological properties are most affected by MCI.
2.  **Missing Technical Details Compromise Reproducibility:** Several crucial technical details are omitted, making the work difficult to reproduce and fully assess.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** The paper states it uses pre-trained word embeddings but does not specify the corpus on which they were trained. This is a critical detail, as the quality and domain of the training data heavily influence embedding performance. Were they trained on a large, general-domain corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or on the small, in-domain clinical datasets?
    *   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges to the network is a key hyperparameter. The authors state it was "optimized" to 0.7 or 0.4 for different datasets but do not describe the optimization procedure (e.g., grid search on a held-out validation set). An analysis of the model's sensitivity to this threshold would also have been valuable.
3.  **Under-analyzed and Potentially Contradictory Results:** The analysis of the experimental results could be more insightful and, in some cases, fails to address results that challenge the central claims. Most notably, on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), a simple BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves 75.47% accuracy, which is significantly better than the proposed enriched network (CNE) features alone (71.73% with the same classifier) and only marginally worse than the best-performing multi-view combination (80.18%). The authors dismiss this as "somewhat surprising" but do not discuss its implications. This strong baseline performance raises questions about the added value of the more complex network modeling, suggesting that for certain tasks, simple lexical features may be sufficient or even superior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the corpus used to train the word embeddings for both English and Portuguese? Were they trained on a large external corpus or on the in-domain datasets used in this study?
2.  The paper states that the similarity threshold for network enrichment was "optimized." Could you please describe the optimization procedure in more detail? For instance, was a separate validation set used within the cross-validation folds?
3.  The results for the ABCD dataset show a very strong performance from the Bag-of-Words baseline, outperforming your proposed CNE features in a head-to-head comparison with the same classifier. Could you offer some analysis as to why this might be the case for this specific dataset and what it implies about the limitations or relative strengths of the network-based approach?
4.  Beyond predictive accuracy, have you performed any analysis to connect the topological network features to specific linguistic or cognitive phenomena associated with MCI? For example, does a feature like "average shortest path length" correlate with measures of semantic coherence or lexical diversity? Such an analysis would greatly improve the interpretability of your findings.
