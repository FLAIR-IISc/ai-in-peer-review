### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to create a word co-occurrence network and then "enrich" it by adding edges between non-adjacent words that are semantically similar, as measured by the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The authors then extract a set of topological features from these enriched graphs (e.g., PageRank, centrality, diameter) and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) of varying lengths and styles. The performance of these network-based features is compared against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations, both individually and in combination using multi-view learning.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and practical application of graph-based text representation for a clinical task.** The key contribution is the adaptation of complex network analysis, specifically enriched with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from noisy speech transcripts. This approach cleverly bypasses the need for extensive manual data cleaning or robust syntactic parsers, which are significant bottlenecks for applying standard NLP techniques in this domain.
2.  **A thorough empirical comparison across multiple datasets and languages.** The authors provide a solid evaluation of their proposed method on three different datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness on both English and Portuguese, and on texts of varying length. This multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the claims about the method's potential for generalization.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing.** The paper is very well-motivated. It clearly identifies a critical weakness in existing NLP approaches for analyzing clinical transcripts: their fragility in the face of disfluencies and grammatical errors, and their reliance on high-quality parsers that may not exist for all languages. The proposed complex network approach is presented as a direct and well-justified solution to this problem, offering a more robust representation that does not depend on syntactic correctness.
2.  **Novelty in Application and Method Adaptation.** While using complex networks for text analysis is not new, its application to MCI detection from spontaneous speech is novel. Furthermore, the specific method of enriching a standard co-occurrence network with semantically-derived edges from word embeddings to combat data sparsity in short texts is a sensible and effective adaptation. The visual example in Figure 2 effectively illustrates how this enrichment adds valuable structure to otherwise sparse, linear graphs from short texts.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup.** The evaluation is thorough. The authors test their method on three datasets with different characteristics, which is commendable. They compare their proposed features against two strong and standard baselines (linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and BoW). The exploration of multiple classifiers, as well as classifier ensembles and multi-view learning for feature combination, provides a comprehensive picture of how the proposed features perform in different settings. The finding that multi-view learning often yields the best results is a valuable insight.
4.  **Practical Significance.** The proposed method has high practical potential. By reducing the need for manual transcript correction and specialized NLP tools, it moves towards a more "fully automated system" as stated by the authors. This is a crucial step for making such diagnostic aids scalable and applicable in real-world clinical settings, especially for under-resourced languages like Portuguese where robust parsers are less common.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing.** The paper reports improvements in accuracy, but these are often small (e.g., 2.3% in Table 4, 4.7% in Table 7). Given the small sizes of the datasets (e.g., N=40 for Cinderella), it is crucial to establish whether these differences are statistically significant or could be due to random chance. The absence of any significance tests (e.g., paired t-test, McNemar's test) comparing the CNE-based models to the baselines weakens the claims of superiority. The conclusion that network features "outperform" linguistic metrics feels too strong; the results suggest they are "competitive with," and sometimes better than, the baselines.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning and Word Embeddings.** The model relies on a critical hyperparameter: the cosine similarity threshold for adding new edges. The paper simply states this was "optimized," with no details on the methodology. Was a separate validation set used? Was it tuned within the cross-validation loop? This lack of clarity hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the risk of overfitting. Similarly, crucial details about the word embedding training are missing. Were the embeddings trained on these small, specialized datasets (risking poor quality) or on a larger external corpus (risking domain mismatch)? These details are essential for understanding the model's behavior and for others to build upon this work.
3.  **Potential Overstatement of Technical Novelty.** The paper's primary contribution is in the application of existing techniques to a new domain. The idea of creating networks from word embeddings is acknowledged to be from Perozzi et al. (2014), and using graph metrics for text classification has also been explored before. The specific combination—starting with a co-occurrence graph and adding similarity-based edges—is a nice adaptation, but the paper could be more precise in situating its technical contribution relative to prior work in graph-based NLP, rather than framing it as a fundamentally new modeling approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the training corpus for the word embeddings? Were they trained solely on the clinical datasets used in this study, or on a larger, general-domain corpus (like Wikipedia)?
2.  How was the cosine similarity threshold selected for each dataset? Could you provide more details on the optimization procedure and perhaps an analysis of how sensitive the classification accuracy is to this parameter?
3.  Have you considered performing statistical significance tests to compare the results of the CNE feature set against the Linguistic Metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) baselines? This would significantly strengthen your conclusions about the relative performance of the different feature types.
