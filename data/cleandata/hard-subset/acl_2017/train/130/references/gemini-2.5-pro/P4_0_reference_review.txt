### **Summary**
This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and then "enrich" this network by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on a cosine similarity threshold of their word embeddings. The authors argue that this enrichment step is particularly useful for short, disfluent clinical texts, which would otherwise produce sparse, uninformative networks. They extract a set of ten topological metrics from these enriched networks (e.g., PageRank, degree, clustering coefficient) and use their summary statistics (mean, standard deviation, skewness) as features for machine learning classifiers. The performance of these network-based features is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against two standard baselines: linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) representation. The results show that the enriched network features are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, the baselines. The paper also demonstrates that the highest classification accuracy is achieved by combining the different feature sets using multi-view learning.

### **Contributions**
1.  **A Novel Feature Representation for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to represent speech transcripts for MCI classification. This method is designed to be robust to the short and often agrammatical nature of clinical speech data, which can pose challenges for traditional NLP tools that rely on syntactic parsing or well-formed sentences.
2.  **Comparative Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed method against established linguistic and statistical baselines across three distinct datasets. These datasets vary in language (English and Portuguese), elicitation task (picture description and story recall), and average text length, which strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
3.  **Demonstration of Multi-View Learning Efficacy:** The study effectively shows that combining the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features through multi-view learning yields the best classification performance on all three datasets. This highlights the complementary nature of these different feature types.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The core idea of enriching word adjacency networks with semantic information from word embeddings to analyze short, disfluent texts is a novel contribution to the clinical NLP space. The motivation is clear and well-justified: traditional linguistic feature extractors often fail on such data, and standard complex network models suffer from sparsity when applied to short texts. Figure 2 provides a compelling visual demonstration of how the enrichment process creates a more topologically informative graph from a sparse initial network. This approach provides a promising, processing-light alternative to methods requiring manual transcript cleaning or robust parsing.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is conducted on three different datasets, which is a significant strength. The use of datasets in two languages (English and Portuguese) and with different underlying tasks (Cookie Theft description, Cinderella narrative retelling, and ABCD delayed recall) demonstrates the method's applicability across varied conditions. The comparison against both sophisticated linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and a standard statistical baseline (BoW) provides a solid benchmark for assessing the performance of the proposed network features.
3.  **Clarity and Technical Soundness:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly described. The process of network construction, enrichment, feature extraction, and classification is explained in a logical, step-by-step manner. The choice of classifiers and evaluation protocol (5-fold cross-validation) is standard and appropriate. The results are presented transparently in tables, allowing for straightforward comparison of different methods and feature combinations.

### **Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Interpretive Analysis:** The paper successfully demonstrates that topological network features can be used for classification, but it provides no analysis as to *why* these features are discriminative. For instance, which specific network metrics are most influential in distinguishing MCI from control subjects? Do the networks from MCI participants exhibit consistently different structural properties (e.g., lower clustering coefficient, higher diameter, different degree distribution) compared to controls? Without this analysis, the method remains a "black box," and the paper misses an opportunity to contribute deeper insights into the linguistic or cognitive changes in MCI as reflected in network structure.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on Key Methodological Components:** Several crucial details regarding the experimental setup are missing, which harms reproducibility.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** The paper states that word embeddings were trained using the fastText model (Bojanowski et al., 2016) but does not specify the training corpus. Were the embeddings trained on a large, general-domain corpus (e.g., Wikipedia), or on the small, in-domain clinical datasets? This choice has a major impact on embedding quality and, consequently, on the results.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a critical hyperparameter, yet its optimization process is not described. The paper states that the "best values" were found to be 0.7 and 0.4 for different datasets. It is unclear how these values were determined and whether this was done on a separate validation set within each cross-validation fold to prevent data leakage and overfitting.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Significance Testing:** The performance of the proposed enriched network features (CNE) is not consistently superior to the baselines. For example, on the manually processed Cinderella dataset (Table 6), linguistic features (LM) perform best. On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline is very strong and outperforms CNE in some classifier settings. While the combination of features consistently yields the best results, this slightly weakens the claim of the standalone superiority of the CNE representation. Furthermore, the paper reports differences in accuracy without any statistical significance testing. Given the small sample sizes of the datasets, it is essential to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant or merely due to random variation.

### **Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide an analysis of the feature importance for the network-based classifiers? Specifically, which topological metrics were most discriminative for the MCI classification task across the different datasets? Does this analysis suggest any underlying cognitive differences in discourse production between the groups?
2.  Please clarify the details of the word embedding training. What corpus was used? If it was the clinical data itself, was this done within each fold of the cross-validation to prevent information from the test set influencing the training set's feature representation?
3.  Could you elaborate on the procedure used to select the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks? How was this hyperparameter optimized, and what measures were taken to ensure that this optimization did not use information from the test splits?
4.  Given the small dataset sizes, have you performed any statistical tests (e.g., McNemar's test or a paired t-test on cross-validation results) to confirm that the performance improvements of your proposed method over the baselines, and of the multi-view combinations over individual feature sets, are statistically significant?
