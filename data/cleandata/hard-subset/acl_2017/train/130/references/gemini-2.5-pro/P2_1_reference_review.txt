### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling the text as a complex network. The core of the proposed method is to first construct a word co-occurrence (adjacency) network and then "enrich" it by adding new edges between non-adjacent words based on the semantic similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. This enrichment is designed to make the network representation more robust, especially for short and disfluent clinical texts where standard networks are sparse. The authors extract a wide range of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) of varying lengths and genres, and compared against standard Bag-of-Words (BoW) and linguistic feature baselines.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Method for Clinical Text Representation:** The primary contribution is the application and adaptation of network enrichment using word embeddings for the specific problem of MCI classification from noisy speech transcripts. This is a well-motivated technique that directly addresses the known issue of data sparsity in graph-based text models, particularly for short documents.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation across three different datasets, covering two languages and different elicitation tasks. The comparison between performance on automatically processed versus manually cleaned transcripts offers valuable evidence for the proposed method's robustness to the kind of noise present in real-world clinical data.
3.  **Demonstration of Feature Combination Efficacy:** While the proposed network features are shown to be competitive, the work also highlights that the best performance is consistently achieved through multi-view learning, by combining the network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features. This underscores the complementary nature of these different representations.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Methodology:** The central idea of using semantic similarity from word embeddings to densify a syntactic co-occurrence graph is both novel for this application and strongly motivated. The authors correctly identify that short, disfluent transcripts pose a challenge for both traditional NLP pipelines (which require clean grammar) and basic graph models (which suffer from sparsity). Their proposed enrichment method is an elegant solution that injects semantic information to create a more robust structural representation. The clarity of the explanation, supported by Figure 2, makes the approach easy to understand.
2.  **Thorough and Realistic Experimental Design:** The use of three distinct datasets strengthens the paper's conclusions. By testing on both English and Portuguese, and on different tasks (picture description vs. narrative recall) resulting in different text lengths, the authors provide evidence for the general applicability of their method. The inclusion of the manually-cleaned Cinderella dataset is a particularly strong element, as it allows for a direct comparison that supports their initial hypothesis: traditional linguistic features are brittle and benefit from manual cleaning, whereas their network approach is more resilient.
3.  **Strong Baselines and Honest Reporting:** The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines: a comprehensive set of linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and the standard BoW model. The results are reported transparently, even when the baselines are highly competitive or outperform the proposed method in certain configurations (e.g., BoW in Table 7). The consistent finding that multi-view combinations yield the best results is a mature and practical conclusion, reflecting the reality that different feature types often capture complementary signals.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Performance Gain from the Core Method:** The main weakness is that the standalone performance of the enriched network features (CNE), while often better than basic networks (CN) and linguistic metrics (LM), is not consistently superior to the much simpler Bag-of-Words baseline. For instance, in the Cookie Theft dataset (Table 4), SVM-RBF on CNE scores 0.626, while BoW is comparable at 0.601. In the ABCD dataset (Table 7), BoW with a linear SVM achieves 0.755, significantly outperforming CNE's best of 0.717. The primary performance gains come from combining feature sets. This makes the contribution feel more like the development of a good complementary feature set rather than a new state-of-the-art standalone representation, slightly diminishing the impact of the core technical novelty.
2.  **Lack of Interpretive Analysis:** The paper proposes a complex feature extraction process, deriving dozens of topological metrics (PageRank, betweenness, clustering, etc., each with mean, stdev, and skewness) from the graphs. However, there is no analysis of which of these features are most discriminative. This is a missed opportunity. A feature importance analysis could have provided valuable clinical or linguistic insights, connecting the topological changes in the network (e.g., lower clustering coefficient, higher average path length) to the cognitive deficits characteristic of MCI. Without this, the network features act as a black box, and we don't learn *why* this representation works.
3.  **Insufficient Details on Key Experimental Parameters:** The paper lacks crucial details needed for full reproducibility and for assessing the methodology.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** The authors state they trained word embeddings but do not specify the corpus used. Was it a large, general-domain corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or was it trained/fine-tuned on in-domain clinical data? This is a critical detail, as the choice of training data can significantly impact embedding quality and relevance.
    *   **Threshold Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a key hyperparameter. The paper notes it was "optimized" to 0.7 for one dataset and 0.4 for the others, but the methodology for this optimization (e.g., cross-validation on a held-out set) is not described. The sensitivity of the results to this threshold is also not discussed, leaving it unclear how robust the findings are.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the training data used for the word embeddings? Using general-domain embeddings for this specialized task could be a confounding factor. Did you experiment with different embedding sources or fine-tuning on a relevant corpus?
2.  The Bag-of-Words model proved to be a very strong baseline, particularly on the shortest texts in the ABCD dataset. What is your interpretation of this result? Does it suggest that for very short narratives, specific lexical choices (captured by BoW) are a more powerful diagnostic signal than the complex structural relationships your network model is designed to capture?
3.  To improve the interpretability of your work, have you performed any analysis to identify which of the many topological network features were most important for the classifiers? Gaining insight into which structural properties of language networks are affected by MCI would be a significant contribution.
