### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core of the method is to first build a word co-occurrence network and then "enrich" it by adding edges between non-adjacent words that are semantically similar, as measured by the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. This enrichment step is designed to make the network representation more informative, especially for the short texts common in clinical assessments. The authors extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train various machine learning classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against standard baselines, namely linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW).

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and well-motivated application of complex network analysis for a clinical NLP task.** The adaptation of enriching a co-occurrence graph with semantic information from word embeddings is an intelligent way to address the challenge of applying graph-based methods to short, sparse texts. Applying this to the important problem of MCI detection is a significant contribution.
2.  **A demonstration of a feature extraction technique that is robust to the noisy and disfluent nature of clinical speech.** A key advantage of the proposed method is that it does not require the extensive manual cleaning or robust parsing that traditional linguistic feature extractors depend on. This makes the approach more practical for real-world automated systems.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and languages.** The study validates its approach on three distinct datasets, comparing it against strong baselines (linguistic features, BoW) and exploring feature combinations. This provides solid evidence for the method's potential and generalizability.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Novelty and Significance:** The proposed method of enriching word co-occurrence networks with word embeddings is an elegant solution to a known problem in graph-based text analysis. It effectively combines local syntactic structure (word adjacency) with global semantic relationships (embedding similarity) into a single representation. The application to the early detection of MCI is of high societal importance, and this work presents a promising new direction for feature engineering in this domain.
2.  **Robustness to Noisy Data:** A primary strength of this work is its potential to handle raw, un-preprocessed speech transcripts. Traditional linguistic features often rely on parsers that perform poorly on the ungrammatical and disfluent text common in this setting. The authors' approach bypasses this dependency. The comparison between the raw and manually processed versions of the Cinderella dataset (Tables 5 and 6) provides evidence that the network-based features are effective even on noisy data, which is a major practical advantage.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The evaluation is comprehensive. Using three different datasets, including data in two languages, strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The authors compare their proposed features not only against a simpler variant (non-enriched networks) but also against two strong, standard baselines: a rich set of linguistic features (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The exploration of multiple classifiers and feature combinations using multi-view learning provides a holistic view of the method's performance.
4.  **Strong and Well-Interpreted Results:** The results consistently demonstrate the value of the enrichment step (CNE outperforms CN). The CNE features are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, the established LM and BoW baselines when used individually. Crucially, the best overall performance is achieved by combining CNE with other feature types, indicating that the network features capture complementary signals of cognitive impairment. The final accuracy of up to 80% on the ABCD dataset is promising for this challenging classification task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:** Several crucial experimental details are missing, which hinders the reproducibility of the work.
    *   **Dataset Statistics:** The paper fails to state the number of control participants for the Cookie Theft dataset, making it impossible to assess class balance or interpret the results meaningfully. Table 1 is referenced but appears to be missing.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** The authors state they trained their own word embeddings but do not specify the training corpus. This is a critical detail. Was it a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or in-domain data? If any data from the test folds was used in the training process, it would constitute data leakage and invalidate the results.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a key hyperparameter. The paper simply states the optimized values (0.7 and 0.4) without describing the optimization procedure. It is unclear if a proper validation set was used or if this was tuned on the test folds, which could lead to inflated performance metrics.
2.  **Limited Comparison to Modern Baselines:** While the comparison to LM and BoW is valuable, the paper lacks a comparison to current state-of-the-art text classification models. In recent years, fine-tuned transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) have become the de-facto standard. Acknowledging and, ideally, comparing against such a baseline is necessary to contextualize the performance of the proposed method within the contemporary NLP landscape.
3.  **Absence of Feature Importance Analysis:** The paper extracts dozens of topological features from the networks but provides no analysis of which features are most discriminative for the MCI detection task. An analysis of feature importance would offer valuable clinical insights by connecting the network properties (e.g., centrality, clustering, path length) to the underlying linguistic changes associated with cognitive decline. This would elevate the paper's contribution from a purely engineering result to one with greater scientific and clinical interpretability.
4.  **Inconsistent Gains from Multi-view Learning:** The reported gains from the multi-view learning combination are inconsistent across datasets and the method used (majority vote) is quite simple. A baseline where the different feature vectors are simply concatenated and fed to a single classifier is missing. Without this comparison, it is difficult to determine whether the multi-view framework itself provides an advantage over a more straightforward feature combination strategy.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the size and class distribution of the Cookie Theft dataset, specifically the number of control participants?
2.  What corpus was used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Can you confirm that no data from the test sets of any cross-validation fold was used in this training process?
3.  Could you describe the protocol used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks? Was a separate validation set used, or was it tuned on the training portion of each cross-validation fold?
4.  Have you considered comparing your multi-view combination approach to a simpler baseline, such as concatenating the CNE, LM, and BoW feature vectors into a single vector for classification? This would help isolate the benefit of the multi-view framework.
