**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes the use of complex network (CN) features for the task of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The primary method involves creating a word co-occurrence network for each transcript and "enriching" it by adding edges between non-adjacent words if their word embedding vectors have a cosine similarity above a certain threshold. The authors extract a large set of topological metrics from these enriched networks (CNE) and use them as features for classification. This approach is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compared against two standard baselines: linguistic features (LM) derived from Coh-Metrix and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) representation. The authors find that their CNE features are competitive and that combining all three feature sets using multi-view learning achieves the best performance.

**Contributions**
1.  An empirical evaluation of applying complex network features, augmented with word embeddings, to the clinical task of MCI detection. The work compares this feature set against established linguistic and BoW baselines across three different datasets.
2.  A demonstration that combining the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features via a multi-view learning approach can yield better classification accuracy than any single feature set alone.

**Strengths**
1.  **Evaluation across Multiple Datasets:** The study is conducted on three separate datasets, which differ in language (English and Portuguese) and the nature of the elicitation task (picture description, story retelling). This provides a more robust evaluation than a study on a single corpus and lends some credence to the generalizability of the findings.
2.  **Inclusion of Standard Baselines:** The authors rightly compare their proposed method against two well-established and strong baselines: linguistic features from the Coh-Metrix toolkit and the classic Bag-of-Words model. This comparison is essential for contextualizing the performance of the proposed CNE features and is a methodologically sound aspect of the experimental design.
3.  **Exploration of Feature Combination:** The paper investigates not only the performance of individual feature sets but also their combination through multi-view learning. The results consistently show that a combined approach is superior, highlighting the complementary nature of the different representations, which is a useful finding for practitioners in this domain.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Proposed Method:** The core methodology lacks significant novelty. The use of word co-occurrence networks for text analysis is a well-established field, as cited by the authors themselves. The key modification—enriching the network using word embedding similarity—is also not a new idea and is directly adapted from prior work (Perozzi et al., 2014). The contribution is therefore an application of existing methods to a new task, which tempers the significance of the work.
2.  **Unconvincing Performance of the Proposed Features:** The central argument for the CNE method is undermined by the empirical results. On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the simple and traditional BoW baseline with a linear SVM (0.7547) significantly outperforms the best result from the proposed CNE features (0.7173). The authors' note that this is "somewhat surprising" suggests a lack of deep analysis. If a much simpler, established method performs better, the justification for adopting the more complex network-based approach is severely weakened. In other datasets, the improvements offered by CNE over baselines are modest at best, with the main performance gains coming from multi-view combinations rather than the strength of the CNE features in isolation.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Method:** The paper presents the method as a black box. A critical hyperparameter, the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges, is simply "optimized" with no details provided on the procedure (e.g., was a separate validation set used?) or analysis of its sensitivity. Furthermore, a large number of network metrics are extracted and used for classification without any feature analysis or ablation study to determine which topological properties are actually discriminative. This "kitchen sink" approach lacks methodological rigor and provides little insight into *why* the network features might work.
4.  **Inconsistent Data Processing and Unsupported Claims:** A key motivation for using complex networks is their claimed robustness to disfluencies in transcripts, which can hinder tools like syntactic parsers. However, this claim is not rigorously substantiated. The paper presents results on a manually revised version of the Cinderella dataset (Table 6) to argue that linguistic features require clean text, but this revision involved more than just removing disfluencies (e.g., removing agrammatical sentences, inserting subjects). This is not a controlled experiment and does not directly prove the superior robustness of the network approach. The inconsistent processing across and even within datasets muddles the conclusions that can be drawn.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  In your experiments on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the standard Bag-of-Words baseline outperforms your proposed CNE method. Given that BoW is a much simpler and more established method, how do you justify the additional complexity of the network-based approach when it does not lead to superior performance on its own?
2.  Could you please elaborate on the optimization process for the cosine similarity threshold? Specifically, how was the optimal value determined for each dataset, and how sensitive is the model's performance to variations in this threshold?
3.  Your paper proposes that complex networks are more robust to disfluent, transcribed speech than linguistic feature extractors. Could you suggest a more direct experimental comparison to validate this claim, beyond the analysis on the manually revised Cinderella data which included multiple types of edits?
