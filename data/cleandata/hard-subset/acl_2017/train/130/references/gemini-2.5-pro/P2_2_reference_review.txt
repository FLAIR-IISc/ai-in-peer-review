### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The primary contribution is a novel feature engineering approach where word co-occurrence networks, which can be sparse for short clinical texts, are "enriched" by adding edges between non-adjacent words based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The authors argue this approach is more robust to the disfluencies and grammatical errors common in clinical transcripts and less reliant on high-quality NLP pipelines (like parsers) than traditional linguistic features.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A Novel Feature Representation for Clinical Text:** The core contribution is the method of enriching word adjacency networks with semantic similarity links derived from word embeddings (CNE). This is presented as a way to create more informative graph structures from short and noisy texts, which is a significant challenge in this domain.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct datasets, covering two languages (English and Portuguese) and different elicitation tasks (picture description, story retelling). They compare their proposed features against two strong and standard baselines: a large set of linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) model.
3.  **Demonstration of Robustness to Noise:** A key finding stems from the comparison on two versions of the Cinderella dataset: one with automated, noisy preprocessing and one with manual cleaning. The results effectively demonstrate that while traditional linguistic features excel on clean text, the proposed network-based features are more robust and perform significantly better on the more realistic, noisy transcripts.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Practical Approach:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem: the difficulty of applying standard NLP feature extraction methods to noisy clinical data. The motivation to use complex networks to circumvent the need for robust parsers and manual cleaning is strong, especially in the context of a less-resourced language like Portuguese, as highlighted by the authors. The proposed CNE method is an intelligent solution to the problem of network sparsity in short texts.

2.  **Strong and Insightful Experimental Design:** The evaluation is a major strength. Using three different datasets with varying characteristics (language, length, task) demonstrates the generalizability of the findings. The inclusion of the two versions of the Cinderella dataset is particularly commendable. The results presented in Table 5 (noisy) versus Table 6 (clean) provide compelling evidence for the authors' central hypothesis: CNE is more robust to noise, whereas linguistic features (LM) are brittle but powerful when given clean input. This is an important and nuanced finding for the field.

3.  **Thorough Comparison with Baselines:** The authors don't just present their method in isolation. They compare it rigorously against two widely-used and strong baselines: a comprehensive set of linguistic metrics (LM) and the classic Bag-of-Words (BoW) model. This allows for a clear understanding of the relative strengths and weaknesses of each feature type. The use of multiple classifiers and multi-view learning further strengthens the empirical analysis.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, and the methodology is described in sufficient detail. The choices made (e.g., using fastText for embeddings to handle OOV words) are well-justified. The specific network metrics and classification setup are standard and clearly laid out, which aids in the potential for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Analysis of the Similarity Threshold:** The method's key step is adding edges based on a cosine similarity threshold. The paper states this was "optimized with best values being 0.7 in Cookie Theft dataset and 0.4 in both Cinderella and ABCD dataset." This is a critical hyperparameter, yet there is no discussion of how it was optimized (e.g., via a nested cross-validation on a development set?) or how sensitive the model's performance is to its value. A sensitivity analysis showing how accuracy changes with the threshold would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and provide insight into the method's stability. Without this, it's hard to know if the chosen values are robust or finely tuned to these specific test sets.

2.  **No Interpretation of Network Features:** The paper extracts a large set of topological features (PageRank, Betweenness, Clustering Coefficient, etc.) and uses them for classification, but it stops short of providing any interpretation. Which network features are most discriminative? For example, do MCI patients produce texts that result in graphs with lower clustering coefficients or higher average path lengths? Connecting these topological properties back to the underlying cognitive and linguistic phenomena would elevate the paper from a pure engineering result to one with greater scientific and clinical insight. A simple feature importance analysis (e.g., from the Random Forest classifier) could have provided this.

3.  **Under-explored BoW Performance:** The Bag-of-Words baseline performs very strongly, particularly on the ABCD dataset where it achieves the best individual accuracy (0.7547 with SVM-Linear in Table 7), outperforming the proposed CNE method. The paper notes this result but does not offer a substantive discussion as to why this might be the case. For a very short recall task like ABCD, perhaps specific lexical choices (i.e., the presence or absence of key content words) are a much stronger signal of cognitive impairment than the structural relationships between words. A deeper discussion of this result would provide a more balanced perspective on when and why different feature types are effective.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks? Was this done on a separate validation set, or within the 5-fold cross-validation loop to prevent information leakage from the test folds? Could you provide a brief analysis of how performance varies as this threshold changes?

2.  Did you perform any feature analysis to determine which of the topological network metrics were most predictive for the MCI classification task? It would be highly valuable to understand if specific graph properties consistently differ between the control and MCI groups, as this could offer new linguistic insights into the nature of cognitive decline.

3.  What is your interpretation of the strong performance of the BoW baseline, especially on the very short narratives of the ABCD dataset where it outperformed CNE? Does this suggest that for certain tasks, simple lexical content is a more powerful feature than the syntactic/semantic structure captured by your network model?
