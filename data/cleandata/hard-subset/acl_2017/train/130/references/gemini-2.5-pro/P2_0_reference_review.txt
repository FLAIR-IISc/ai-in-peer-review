### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The primary contribution is a novel technique to enrich these networks, which are based on word co-occurrence, by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar according to pre-trained word embeddings. This enrichment is designed to overcome the problem of network sparsity in short texts, making topological network features more informative. The second key contribution is a comprehensive empirical evaluation on three different datasets (two in Portuguese, one in English) of varying lengths and elicitation tasks. The authors demonstrate that their enriched network features (CNE) are competitive with, and often outperform, traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) baselines, particularly on noisy, minimally-processed transcripts. Finally, the work shows that combining feature sets using multi-view learning yields the best classification performance.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant methodology.** The core problem the authors identify is highly relevant: short clinical transcripts lead to sparse, almost linear co-occurrence networks, rendering standard topological metrics uninformative. The proposed solution—enriching the network with edges based on word embedding similarity—is an elegant and intuitive way to densify the graph with semantic information. This directly addresses the stated limitation and is a clear methodological strength. Figure 2 provides a compelling visual demonstration of this enrichment.

2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental design.** The use of three distinct datasets with different characteristics (Cookie Theft: short English descriptions; Cinderella: longer Portuguese narratives; ABCD: very short Portuguese recall narratives) significantly strengthens the paper's claims. This allows for an evaluation of the method's robustness across languages, text lengths, and cognitive tasks. Furthermore, the inclusion of a manually cleaned version of the Cinderella dataset provides a crucial ablative analysis. It directly supports the authors' hypothesis that their network-based method is less dependent on clean, grammatical text than traditional linguistic features, which perform much better on the manually revised data (Table 6 vs. Table 5).

3.  **Strong comparative analysis and results.** The paper doesn't just present a new method but rigorously compares it against two strong and standard baselines: a rich set of linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words. The results consistently show that the enriched network features (CNE) are a competitive alternative. For instance, on the Cinderella dataset (Table 5), CNE with an SVM-RBF classifier achieves 65.0% accuracy, significantly outperforming the linguistic metrics' 47.5%. The fact that the highest overall accuracy on each dataset is achieved by combining the CNE features with other feature sets (e.g., 80.18% on ABCD) demonstrates that the proposed features capture a different and complementary signal.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of comparison to modern neural baselines.** The baselines used (BoW, linguistic features) are traditional, but the current state-of-the-art in text classification relies heavily on transformer-based models (e.g., BERT). While the datasets are small, a stronger baseline would involve using embeddings from a pre-trained language model (like BERT or a multilingual equivalent) as input features to a classifier, or even attempting to fine-tune such a model. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess where the proposed complex network approach stands relative to the most powerful contemporary methods.

2.  **Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection.** The authors state that the cosine similarity threshold was optimized, yielding values of 0.7 for one dataset and 0.4 for the others. However, the process for this optimization is not described. Was a separate development set used? Or was it tuned within the 5-fold cross-validation loop? If the latter, this could lead to information leakage from the test fold into the model configuration, potentially inflating the reported accuracies. Similarly, the word embedding model was trained with "default parameters." Given the importance of the embeddings to the method, more details on the training corpus and parameters would be necessary for reproducibility.

3.  **Absence of statistical significance testing.** The paper reports improvements in accuracy percentages (e.g., "a 2.3% of improvement"). However, given the small size of the datasets (e.g., Cinderella has only 40 subjects), it is crucial to determine if these differences are statistically significant. Without statistical tests (e.g., McNemar's test or bootstrapping), it is unclear whether the observed performance gains are meaningful or simply due to random variation in the data splits. This makes it difficult to confidently conclude that one feature set or model is truly superior to another.

4.  **Limited interpretation of network features.** The paper extracts 30 topological features (10 metrics × 3 statistics) but does not provide any analysis of which features are most discriminative for MCI detection. Such an analysis could offer valuable insights into the linguistic changes associated with the condition as captured by network topology. For example, does the average clustering coefficient or the network diameter consistently differ between MCI and control groups? This represents a missed opportunity to connect the network model back to the clinical domain.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks? Was this done on a separate validation set or within the cross-validation procedure?

2.  Have you considered comparing your method against features derived from large pre-trained transformer models (e.g., using sentence embeddings from BERT)? This would serve as a powerful contemporary baseline and help situate the performance of your complex network approach in the current NLP landscape.

3.  Were any statistical tests conducted to assess the significance of the performance differences reported between different feature sets and their combinations? This would greatly strengthen your claims of superiority, especially on the smaller datasets.
