### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the use of complex network features for the automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The primary method involves modeling each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and then "enriching" this network by adding edges between non-adjacent words if their word embedding vectors have a cosine similarity above a certain threshold. The authors extract a suite of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The work evaluates this approach on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) of varying lengths and compares its performance against traditional linguistic features and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical study applying complex networks enriched with word embeddings to the clinical task of MCI detection, demonstrating its viability as a feature extraction method.
2.  A comparative analysis of the proposed network-based features against standard linguistic and BoW features across three distinct datasets, showing the approach is competitive, especially when preprocessing is minimal.
3.  An exploration of multi-view learning, which shows that combining the different feature types can lead to modest improvements in classification accuracy over any single feature set.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Application of an NLP Technique to a Clinical Domain:** The paper addresses an important and challenging problem—the early detection of MCI. Applying graph-based text representations, particularly the concept of enriching sparse graphs with semantic information from embeddings, is a novel approach in this specific context and is well-motivated for handling the short and disfluent nature of clinical speech transcripts.

2.  **Well-Motivated Core Idea:** The central premise, that short texts produce sparse, linear-like co-occurrence networks with limited informative topology, is valid. The proposed solution—densifying the graph using semantic similarity from word embeddings to create shortcuts and reveal latent semantic structures—is intuitive and technically sound. Figure 2 provides a good visual illustration of this concept.

3.  **Multi-Dataset and Multi-Lingual Evaluation:** The authors should be commended for evaluating their method on three different datasets, including data in both English and Portuguese. This provides a more robust test of the method's generalizability than a study on a single corpus and strengthens the paper's conclusions. The use of datasets with different elicitation tasks (picture description, story recall) and text lengths is also a strength.

### Weaknesses

My main concerns with this paper are a lack of methodological rigor in critical areas, significant inconsistencies in the reporting of data, and a general lack of polish that undermines the credibility of the results.

1.  **Critical Missing Details in Methodology:** The proposed method's success hinges on two key components whose implementation is inadequately described:
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** The authors state they trained embeddings using the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016) but fail to specify the training corpus. Given the small size of the in-domain datasets (e.g., 40 subjects in the Cinderella dataset), training embeddings on this data alone would yield very poor representations. Were pre-trained embeddings used? Or were they trained on a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia)? This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and for understanding the source of the "semantic information" being used for enrichment.
    *   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold is a critical hyperparameter. The paper states it was "optimized," with different values for different datasets (0.7 and 0.4). However, the procedure for this optimization is not described. Was this done on a separate validation set, or was it optimized on the test data within each cross-validation fold? If the latter, this constitutes data leakage and would inflate the reported results. The lack of clarity on this point is a major methodological flaw.

2.  **Inconsistencies and Errors in Data Reporting:** The paper contains several glaring inconsistencies that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   In Section 4.1.2, the Cinderella dataset is described as having "20 subjects with MCI and 20 subjects without MCI for control." However, Table 2, which purports to show the demographics for this dataset, lists the MCI group as having 29 Males and 14 Females, for a total of 43 subjects. This is a direct and inexplicable contradiction that calls into question the validity of all results reported for this dataset.
    *   Table 1 is incomplete; it provides Male/Female counts but omits the total number of participants in each group (Control and MCI), which is standard and necessary information.

3.  **Ambiguous Preprocessing and Inconsistent Claims:** The description of text preprocessing is vague. Section 3 mentions removing stopwords and punctuation, but Section 4.1.1 details removing specific filled pauses and disfluencies. It is unclear if a standard stopword list was used for all feature sets (especially BoW, where it is critical) or just for the network construction. Furthermore, the central claim that network features are superior for noisy, unrevised text is weakened by the results on the manually processed Cinderella dataset (Table 6). Here, linguistic features (LM) achieve a much higher accuracy (0.7250) than the enriched networks (CNE, 0.6750). This suggests that improving preprocessing for linguistic tools may be a more effective strategy than switching to network models, a point that is understated by the authors.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a significant lack of professional polish. The reference section is riddled with BibTeX compilation errors (e.g., `Aluı́sio et al\.,? \Q2016\E`), which is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference. The presentation of multi-view results in the tables is confusing; it's unclear how a result for a single classifier like "SVM-RBF" is generated for a multi-view combination like "CNE-LM-BoW."

### Questions to Authors

1.  What specific corpus was used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? If pre-trained vectors were used, please specify which ones.
2.  Please provide a detailed description of the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment. Specifically, how did you ensure this optimization was performed without leaking information from the test set in your cross-validation scheme?
3.  Can you please clarify the discrepancy regarding the number of MCI participants in the Cinderella dataset between the text in Section 4.1.2 (20 subjects) and the demographic data in Table 2 (43 subjects)? Which number is correct?
4.  Regarding the multi-view learning results in Tables 4, 5, and 7 (e.g., the `CNE-LM-BoW` column): How were these single-classifier results (e.g., for SVM-Linear) generated? Does this represent a combination of predictions from three separate SVM-Linear models trained on each feature set, or a single classifier trained on a concatenated feature vector? The text mentions "majority vote," which implies an ensemble, making the presentation confusing.
