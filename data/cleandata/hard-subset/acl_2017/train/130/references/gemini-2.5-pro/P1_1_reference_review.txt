### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes using complex networks (CN) to classify speech transcripts from individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) versus healthy controls. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence network. To overcome the issue of sparsity in short texts, which is common in clinical data, the authors "enrich" these networks by adding edges between non-adjacent words if their word embeddings have a high cosine similarity. Topological features are extracted from these enriched networks (CNE) and used in various machine learning classifiers. The authors conduct experiments on three datasets (one in English, two in Brazilian Portuguese) of varying lengths and elicitation tasks. They compare the performance of CNE features against traditional linguistic metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) and show that combining these feature sets via multi-view learning yields the best results.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel application of word-embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI detection.** The method is well-motivated as a way to capture both syntactic (adjacency) and semantic (embedding similarity) relationships in short, disfluent texts, aiming to reduce the need for extensive manual preprocessing that hinders traditional NLP pipelines.
2.  **A thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and languages.** The authors validate their approach on three distinct clinical datasets, demonstrating its applicability to both English and Portuguese and across different text lengths. The systematic comparison against strong baselines (LM, BoW) and the exploration of feature combinations provide a comprehensive picture of the method's utility.
3.  **The demonstration that network-based features provide complementary information to traditional features.** The strongest results are consistently achieved by combining the proposed CNE features with linguistic and BoW features using multi-view learning, suggesting that the network topology captures unique discriminative signals not fully represented by other methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and novel approach for clinical text.** The primary strength is the innovative method for representing clinical transcripts. Standard NLP tools often fail on disfluent speech. Complex networks offer a representation that is inherently more robust to grammatical errors. Furthermore, the enrichment with word embeddings is a clever solution to the critical problem of feature sparsity in the short texts typical of neuropsychological assessments. This makes the network dense enough for topological metrics to be meaningful.
2.  **Reduced dependency on manual preprocessing.** A key advantage of the proposed method is its potential to operate on raw or lightly processed transcripts. Unlike approaches that rely on syntactic parsers or manually annotated features, this method bypasses steps that are both labor-intensive and error-prone, especially for a less-resourced language like Portuguese. This practical consideration significantly increases the potential for real-world application.
3.  **Comprehensive experimental design.** The study is well-designed. The use of three datasets with different characteristics (language, task, length) strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The authors compare against two very relevant and strong feature sets (LM and BoW) and test a variety of classifiers. The inclusion of multi-view learning to combine feature sets is a sophisticated and appropriate choice that ultimately demonstrates the complementary nature of their proposed features.
4.  **Clear validation of the core hypothesis.** The results consistently show that enriched networks (CNE) outperform standard co-occurrence networks (CN), validating the central idea that adding semantic links is beneficial. Furthermore, the fact that CNE features are competitive with, and on the Cinderella dataset superior to, linguistic features highlights the power of this representation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient comparison to the state of the art.** The paper claims its results (accuracies from 65% to 80%) are "comparable" to prior work, but this comparison is too vague. The Cookie Theft dataset is part of the public DementiaBank corpus, and other studies have reported results on it. While tasks might differ slightly (e.g., AD vs. MCI detection), a more direct and detailed comparison with existing benchmarks is necessary to properly contextualize the performance of the proposed method. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the 65% accuracy achieved on this dataset is a significant result.
2.  **Lack of feature analysis and interpretability.** The paper successfully shows that network features work for classification, but it does not provide any insight into *why* they work. The model uses a set of 10 topological measures (and their moments), but there is no analysis of which features are most discriminative. For example, does MCI speech lead to networks with lower clustering coefficients or different centrality distributions? Such an analysis would offer valuable clinical insights into the linguistic markers of MCI that are captured by network topology and elevate the paper beyond a purely engineering contribution.
3.  **Crucial hyperparameter selection is not explained.** The enrichment process hinges on a cosine similarity threshold, which is set to 0.7 for English and 0.4 for Portuguese. This is a key hyperparameter, but the paper provides no details on how these values were chosen. Were they optimized on a separate development set, or tuned within the cross-validation procedure? The latter would risk data leakage and lead to overestimated performance. A sensitivity analysis showing how accuracy varies with this threshold would significantly strengthen the paper's reproducibility and rigor.
4.  **Small dataset sizes limit the robustness of conclusions.** The datasets, particularly for Cinderella (20 vs. 20 subjects) and ABCD (23 vs. 20 subjects), are very small. While this is a common challenge in clinical NLP, it means that performance differences may not be statistically significant and results can have high variance. For example, an accuracy of 75% vs. 65% on the Cinderella dataset corresponds to correctly classifying only 4 more subjects. The conclusions should be stated more cautiously in light of this limitation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the Cookie Theft dataset, could you provide a more direct comparison to previously published results on DementiaBank, even if the experimental settings are not identical? This would help to better position your work within the existing literature.
2.  The cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment is a critical parameter. Could you please clarify how this threshold was selected for each dataset? Was a held-out development set used? Furthermore, could you provide a brief analysis of how sensitive your results are to changes in this threshold?
3.  Have you performed any feature importance analysis to identify which of the ten network topological metrics are most predictive of MCI? Understanding which network properties differ between the groups could provide valuable linguistic and clinical insights.
