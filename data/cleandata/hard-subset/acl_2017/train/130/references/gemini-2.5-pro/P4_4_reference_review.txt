### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for automatically detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To address the challenge of short texts, which typically yield sparse, uninformative graphs, the authors "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between non-adjacent words whose word embeddings have a high cosine similarity. A set of topological metrics (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, degree) are extracted from these enriched networks and used as features for a machine learning classifier to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets of varying length and language (English and Portuguese) and compared against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive empirical study** demonstrating that complex network features, particularly when enriched with semantic information from word embeddings, are a viable and competitive approach for MCI classification from speech transcripts. The evaluation across three different datasets, languages, and narrative types provides evidence for the method's potential generalizability.
2.  **A comparative analysis** showing that the proposed graph-based representation can outperform standard linguistic feature sets (derived from Coh-Metrix) on unprocessed, disfluent speech transcripts. This provides a practical alternative that avoids reliance on potentially fragile NLP pipelines (e.g., parsers) that perform poorly on such noisy data.
3.  **An investigation into feature combination**, showing that multi-view learning, which combines the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features, yields the best overall classification performance, highlighting the complementary nature of these different feature types.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Application of an Existing Technique to a New Domain:** The paper addresses the important clinical problem of early MCI detection. The motivation for using complex networks is strong and clearly articulated: to create a representation that is robust to the disfluencies and grammatical errors common in patient speech, thereby circumventing the need for expensive manual cleaning or reliance on high-performance parsers that may not exist for all languages. While graph-based text representation is not new, its application and thorough evaluation in this specific clinical context is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Thorough and Diverse Experimental Evaluation:** The authors validate their approach on three distinct datasets: the English Cookie Theft descriptions, the Portuguese Cinderella narratives, and the very short Portuguese ABCD recall narratives. This diversity in language, task, and text length strengthens the paper's conclusions. The inclusion of a manually cleaned version of the Cinderella dataset provides a direct test of the hypothesis that the network approach is particularly beneficial for noisy, un-preprocessed text.

3.  **Strong Baselines and Combination Strategies:** The proposed Complex Network Enriched (CNE) features are not evaluated in isolation. They are systematically compared against two widely used and strong baselines: a large set of linguistic metrics (LM) and the Bag-of-Words (BoW) model. Furthermore, the paper explores both classifier ensembles and multi-view learning to combine different feature sets. The finding that a multi-view combination often yields the best performance is significant, suggesting that the network features capture information complementary to that of traditional methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core technical components of the proposed method are built upon existing work. Representing texts as word co-occurrence networks is a well-established technique in NLP, and the idea of using word embedding similarity to add semantic edges to a graph was previously proposed (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main contribution is the novel application and combination of these techniques, rather than a new methodology. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer positioning of its work in relation to these prior methods, highlighting any specific adaptations made for the clinical domain.

2.  **Lack of Critical Implementation Details:** The paper omits several details that are crucial for reproducibility and for fully assessing the technical soundness of the experiments.
    *   **Threshold Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a critical hyperparameter. The paper states it was "optimized" to 0.7 for one dataset and 0.4 for others, but the optimization procedure is not described. It is unclear if a separate validation set was used or if this was tuned on the test set, which would constitute data leakage and inflate the reported performance.
    *   **Word Embedding Training:** The word embeddings are central to the enrichment process. However, the paper does not specify the corpus used to train them. Were they trained on the small, in-domain clinical datasets, or on a large, external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia)? This choice has a major impact on the quality and generalizability of the embeddings, and thus on the results.
    *   **Multi-view Combination:** The multi-view combination is simply described as using "majority vote". For classifiers like SVM that produce continuous scores, it is not clear how this is implemented. A more detailed explanation of the combination function is needed.

3.  **Inconsistent Results and Under-developed Analysis:** The analysis of the results could be more nuanced.
    *   On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves an accuracy of 75.47%, which is considerably higher than the proposed CNE features with the same classifier (71.73%) and close to the best multi-view result (80.18%). This important result, which suggests that for very short texts simple lexical features may be more powerful than complex structural ones, is not adequately discussed.
    *   The paper claims that "Linguistic features depend on grammatical texts to present good results," citing the results on the manually processed Cinderella dataset (Table 6). However, in Table 6, the LM features (0.7250 accuracy) actually *outperform* the CNE features (0.6750) on this cleaned text. While CNE does outperform LM on the *unprocessed* version (Table 5), the argument as stated in the conclusion is not precisely supported by the provided data and requires clearer articulation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the optimization process for the cosine similarity threshold? Specifically, was this hyperparameter tuned using a nested cross-validation scheme or a separate development set to prevent overfitting to the test data?

2.  What corpus was used to train the word embeddings? Given the importance of the embeddings for the network enrichment step, this information is critical for understanding the results and for reproducibility.

3.  On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline performs very strongly, outperforming the CNE features with the same linear SVM classifier. How do you interpret this result in the context of your overall claim about the utility of the network-based approach, particularly for short texts?

4.  Regarding the comparison between the unprocessed (Table 5) and processed (Table 6) Cinderella datasets: your results show CNE outperforming LM on the unprocessed text, while LM outperforms CNE on the processed text. This seems to be the strongest evidence supporting your method's utility for noisy data. Could you please elaborate on this contrast and why you believe the network structure is more robust to the disfluencies present in the unprocessed transcripts?
