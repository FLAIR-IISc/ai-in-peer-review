### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and then "enrich" it by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on a cosine similarity threshold over their word embedding vectors. This enrichment step is designed to make the network representation more robust for the short and often disfluent texts typical of neuropsychological assessments. The authors extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) of varying lengths and genres. The paper's primary contributions are:

1.  **A novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to the clinical task of MCI detection.** The strategy of using semantic similarity from embeddings to densify sparse networks from short texts is a creative and well-motivated solution to a known problem in this domain.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of the proposed network features against standard baselines.** The authors evaluate their method against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and bag-of-words representations across multiple datasets and languages, providing a solid benchmark of its performance.
3.  **Demonstration of a low-preprocessing approach for analyzing clinical language.** A key strength of the proposed method is its reduced reliance on complex NLP pipelines (e.g., syntactic parsing) that often fail on the noisy, agrammatical text found in speech transcripts. This makes the approach practically valuable and more easily adaptable to new languages or domains.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Sound Motivation:** The application of complex networks to this specific clinical task is not entirely new, but the method of enriching the networks with word embeddings to handle short, sparse transcripts is a significant and novel contribution. This approach directly addresses the limitations of standard network models, which are often uninformative for short texts. The motivation to bypass fragile, high-preprocessing NLP pipelines is compelling and highly relevant for real-world clinical applications.

2.  **Thorough and Multi-faceted Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors validate their approach on three different datasets that vary in language (English, Portuguese), elicitation task (picture description, story retelling), and text length. This demonstrates the method's versatility and generalizability. Furthermore, they compare their proposed features not only against strong, standard baselines (linguistic metrics, BoW) but also show the benefit of combining them in a multi-view learning framework, which is an appropriate way to handle heterogeneous feature sets.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Practical Significance:** The proposed enriched complex network (CNE) features are shown to be competitive with, and in several cases superior to, traditional linguistic features on their own. The best results overall are achieved by combining CNE features with other types, highlighting their complementary nature. The success of the method on automatically processed transcripts (e.g., the main Cinderella experiment) supports the claim that this is a viable approach for building fully automated systems for MCI screening, which has significant clinical importance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper presents numerous accuracy results in tables, but no statistical tests are used to validate the claims. For example, on the Cookie Theft dataset, the best individual classifier achieves 62.6% accuracy, while the best combined model reaches 65.0%. Is this ~2.4% improvement statistically significant, or could it be due to chance? Without significance testing, it is difficult to confidently conclude that the proposed enrichment method or the multi-view combinations provide a meaningful advantage over the baselines.

2.  **Clarity and Missing Details in Experimental Setup:** There are several ambiguities regarding the datasets and methodology.
    *   Table 1, which is supposed to describe the Cookie Theft dataset demographics, is missing from the paper. The number of control participants for this dataset is never explicitly stated, making it difficult to assess class balance.
    *   The disfluency removal for the Cookie Theft data is described as being "like (Fraser et al., 2015)". It is unclear if this was an automated process (which would support the paper's claims) or a manual one (which would weaken the low-preprocessing argument).
    *   The corpus used to train the word embeddings is not specified. The choice of training data (e.g., general domain like Wikipedia vs. in-domain clinical texts) can significantly impact embedding quality and downstream performance. This is a critical detail for reproducibility and interpreting the results.

3.  **Limited Interpretability and Analysis:** The paper successfully demonstrates that the network features are predictive, but it offers little insight into *why* they are predictive. A feature importance analysis could have revealed which of the ten topological metrics are most discriminative. For example, do MCI patients exhibit networks with systematically lower clustering coefficients or higher diameters? Such an analysis would elevate the paper from a demonstration of a predictive method to a study that offers potential clinical and linguistic insights into the nature of cognitive decline as reflected in language production. The lack of this analysis is a missed opportunity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the missing details for the Cookie Theft dataset, including the number of control subjects and whether the disfluency removal was automated or manual?
2.  What corpora were used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Have you considered the potential impact of using a general-domain versus an in-domain corpus for this clinical task?
3.  Have you conducted any statistical significance tests to compare the performance of different feature sets (e.g., CNE vs. LM) and to validate the improvements gained from your multi-view learning approach?
4.  Beyond predictive accuracy, have you performed any analysis to determine which topological network properties are most discriminative between the MCI and control groups? Such an analysis could provide valuable insights into the linguistic structural changes associated with MCI.
