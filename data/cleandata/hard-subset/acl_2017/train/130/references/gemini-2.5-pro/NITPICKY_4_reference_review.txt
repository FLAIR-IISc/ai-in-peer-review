### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to enrich standard word co-occurrence networks with additional edges based on the semantic similarity of words, derived from pre-trained word embeddings. This enrichment is intended to make the network representation more robust, particularly for the short and often disfluent texts common in clinical assessments. The authors extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification, comparing and combining them with standard linguistic and Bag-of-Words (BoW) features. The evaluation is conducted on three datasets: one in English (Cookie Theft) and two in Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD).

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical investigation into the use of word-embedding-enriched complex networks for the clinical NLP task of MCI detection, motivated by the challenge of representing short, noisy texts.
2.  An evaluation across three distinct datasets and two languages, which provides a broader basis for the findings than a single-dataset study.
3.  A demonstration that combining feature sets (network, linguistic, BoW) through multi-view learning can improve classification accuracy over individual feature sets.

### Strengths
1.  **Novelty of Application:** While complex networks and word embeddings are not new to NLP, their combined application to enrich representations for MCI detection from transcripts is a novel approach. The motivation to better capture information in short clinical texts, which often result in sparse, uninformative graphs, is well-founded.
2.  **Multi-dataset Evaluation:** The use of three datasets with different characteristics (language, task, length) provides a more robust evaluation of the proposed method. The inclusion of both English and Portuguese data is a commendable effort.
3.  **Effectiveness of Multi-view Learning:** The experiments clearly show that combining different feature spaces using multi-view learning consistently improves performance. For instance, on the Cinderella dataset (Table 5), the CNE-LM combination boosts accuracy by 10% over the best individual classifier. This highlights the complementary nature of the different feature types.
4.  **Analysis of Text Quality:** The comparison between the automatically processed and manually revised versions of the Cinderella dataset (Tables 5 and 6) provides compelling evidence for one of the paper's central claims: that linguistic features are sensitive to text quality. The accuracy of the LM feature set improves from 47.5% (SVM-RBF) on the noisy text to 72.5% on the clean text, whereas the CNE features are more stable. This is a strong argument for the utility of their approach in real-world scenarios with uncurated data.

### Weaknesses
My arguments against acceptance are primarily rooted in significant methodological omissions, overstated claims, and a notable lack of care in the paper's preparation, which collectively undermine the credibility of the work.

1.  **Critical Methodological Flaws and Omissions:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details, raising serious concerns about the validity and reproducibility of the results.
    *   **Word Embedding Training Corpus:** The paper fails to specify the corpus used to train the word embeddings. This is a fundamental detail. Were they trained on the small experimental datasets (which would be invalid due to data leakage and insufficient data) or on a large, external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia)? Without this information, the entire "enrichment" process is unreproducible and its contribution cannot be properly assessed.
    *   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold, a key parameter, was "optimized" to specific values (0.7 and 0.4). The paper does not describe the procedure. If this optimization was performed on the entire dataset before 5-fold cross-validation, it represents a classic case of data leakage, which would inflate the reported accuracies and invalidate the conclusions. Proper validation requires tuning within a nested cross-validation loop or on a held-out development set.
2.  **Overstated and Inconsistent Claims:** The paper's framing and conclusions are not consistently supported by its own data and methods description.
    *   The authors claim their method "avoid[s] a manual preprocessing step." However, Sections 4.1.1 and 4.1.2 explicitly state that they "automatically removed filled pauses," "short false starts," and "repetition." This is a non-trivial, rule-based preprocessing step. The claims are therefore misleading.
    *   The conclusion asserts that network features "outperfom traditionally linguistic metrics, in individual classifiers results." This is demonstrably false according to their own tables. In Table 6 (Cinderella-manual), the LM features (72.5%) outperform CNE (67.5%). More strikingly, in Table 7 (ABCD), the simple BoW baseline (75.5%) significantly outperforms CNE (71.7%). The contribution of the proposed method is not as clear-cut or dominant as the authors claim.
3.  **Profound Lack of Attention to Detail:** The manuscript is replete with errors, suggesting a rushed and careless preparation that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Missing Content:** Figure 1 and Table 1 are referenced in the text but are completely absent from the paper.
    *   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is exceptionally poor. It contains malformed entries (e.g., `Aluı́sio et al\.,? \Q2016\E`), incorrect character encoding (`Solé`), inconsistent formatting, and clear typos (e.g., "Googlass" instead of "Goodglass"). Such a lack of care in the bibliography casts doubt on the scholarly diligence of the entire work.
    *   **Table Formatting:** The headers in Tables 2 and 3 are improperly formatted, breaking across lines. Numerical values use an inconsistent decimal separator (`72,0`).
4.  **Weak Baselines and Insufficient Analysis:** The analysis of the results feels incomplete. The BoW baseline uses simple term frequency (tf), rather than the more standard and often stronger tf-idf weighting scheme. Even with this potentially weaker baseline, BoW achieves a remarkable 75.5% accuracy on the ABCD dataset. The paper fails to provide any meaningful discussion as to why this simple model is so effective, and what this implies about the added value of the far more complex network-based methodology in that context.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Please specify the exact corpus and its size used to train the word embeddings for both English and Portuguese.
2.  Please describe in detail the protocol used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment. Was this optimization performed within each fold of the cross-validation to prevent information leakage from the test set?
3.  Given that the BoW model achieves 75.5% accuracy on the ABCD dataset, outperforming the CNE model (71.7%), could you provide a more detailed analysis of this result? What linguistic phenomena might the BoW model be capturing so effectively in this specific dataset, and what does this suggest about the limitations or comparative disadvantages of the network-based features in this scenario?
