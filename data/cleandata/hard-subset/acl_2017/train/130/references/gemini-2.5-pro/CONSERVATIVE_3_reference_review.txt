### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks for the task of classifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The primary novelty is a method to "enrich" standard word co-occurrence networks by adding edges between non-adjacent words based on the cosine similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The authors evaluate this approach (CNE) against standard complex networks (CN), a large set of linguistic features (LM), and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline on three small datasets.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical evaluation of complex network-based features, both standard and enriched with word embeddings, for MCI classification from speech.
2.  A comparison of these network features against established baselines (linguistic metrics and BoW) across three different datasets, including two languages.
3.  A demonstration that combining the proposed network features with traditional features via multi-view learning can lead to improved classification performance over any single feature set.

### Strengths

1.  **Clinically Relevant Problem:** The paper addresses the important clinical problem of early MCI detection, a task where automated linguistic analysis holds significant promise. This is a valuable application area for NLP.
2.  **Inclusion of Standard Baselines:** The authors rightly compare their proposed method against strong and well-established baselines, namely a comprehensive set of linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and a standard Bag-of-Words representation. This provides a necessary context for interpreting the performance of the network-based models.
3.  **Exploration of Multiple Datasets:** The evaluation is conducted on three distinct datasets, covering two different languages (English and Portuguese) and different elicitation tasks. This adds some breadth to the experimental setup, although the datasets themselves are quite small.
4.  **Sound Combination Strategy:** The use of multi-view learning to combine heterogeneous feature sets (networks, linguistic, BoW) is a methodologically appropriate choice, acknowledging that different feature types may capture complementary information and may not be optimally represented in a single concatenated vector.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent and Unconvincing Results:** The paper's central claim—that complex network features outperform traditional methods—is not consistently supported by its own results.
    *   On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the simple BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves an accuracy of 0.7547, which is substantially better than the best-performing proposed CNE model (0.7173). The most established method wins here.
    *   On the manually processed Cinderella dataset (Table 6), the linguistic metrics (LM) achieve the highest accuracy (0.7250) among individual feature sets, again outperforming CNE (0.6750).
    *   Only on the original Cinderella dataset (Table 5) does the proposed CNE method achieve the best performance among individual feature sets.
    *   The gains from CNE over baselines, where they exist, are modest. The paper claims superiority for a new method, but the evidence is mixed at best and contradictory at worst. The overall best results are consistently achieved only by combining all feature types, which undermines the argument that the network approach is a standalone improvement.

2.  **Critically Small Datasets:** The conclusions are drawn from experiments on extremely small datasets (e.g., 20 MCI vs. 20 controls for the Cinderella dataset). With such low sample sizes, the reported accuracy figures have very wide confidence intervals, and small differences between models are unlikely to be statistically significant. It is difficult to accept claims of a novel method's superiority without more robust validation on larger corpora. The performance could easily be an artifact of the specific data splits in the cross-validation.

3.  **Methodological Concerns Regarding Hyperparameter Tuning:** The method for enriching the network introduces a critical hyperparameter: the cosine similarity threshold. The paper states, "the threshold parameter was optimized with best values being 0.7... and 0.4...". This statement is concerning as it lacks detail. Was this optimization performed properly within each fold of the cross-validation using a separate validation set? Or was a single threshold chosen that maximized performance on the entire dataset (including the test folds)? The latter would constitute data leakage and would artificially inflate the reported performance. This lack of methodological clarity is a significant flaw.

4.  **Ad-Hoc Nature of the "Enrichment" Method:** The proposed method of adding edges based on a hard similarity threshold feels arbitrary. There is little theoretical justification for why this specific mechanism is optimal for capturing information relevant to MCI. Why cosine similarity and not another distance metric? Why a hard threshold instead of weighted edges? The contribution feels more like an engineering trick than a principled methodological advance, and its effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please clarify the exact procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for the CNE models. Was a validation set partitioned from the training data within each cross-validation fold to select this hyperparameter? If not, the reported results may be invalid. Please also comment on the sensitivity of your model's performance to this threshold.
2.  Given that a standard Bag-of-Words model outperforms your proposed CNE method on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), and linguistic features outperform CNE on the cleaned Cinderella dataset (Table 6), how do you justify the conclusion that "the topological properties of CN outperfom traditionally linguistic metrics"? The evidence presented seems to directly contradict this strong claim.
3.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to compare the performance of the different feature sets? On datasets of this size, an accuracy difference of 2-5% could easily be due to random variation. Without significance testing, it is difficult to conclude that any observed differences are meaningful.
