### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. Recognizing that transcripts from neuropsychological assessments are often short, leading to sparse and topologically simple graphs, the authors "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on a cosine similarity threshold over their word embeddings. A rich set of topological features (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering coefficient) are extracted from these enriched graphs. The efficacy of these features is evaluated on three datasets (two in Portuguese, one in English) for the task of classifying MCI vs. healthy controls. The proposed complex network features (especially the enriched version, CNE) are compared against traditional linguistic features (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. The results show that CNE is competitive with, and sometimes superior to, the baselines. Furthermore, combining feature sets using multi-view learning yields the best performance, achieving up to 80% accuracy on one of the datasets.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Engineering Approach for a Clinical Task:** The primary contribution is the application and adaptation of complex network analysis for MCI detection. The method of enriching sparse co-occurrence graphs with semantic information from word embeddings is a clever way to make graph-based text analysis viable for short, noisy clinical texts. This provides a new set of features that are less dependent on traditional syntactic parsers, which often struggle with disfluent speech.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct datasets that vary in language, elicitation task, and text length. This multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the claims of the method's potential. The paper also provides a solid comparison against standard and relevant baselines (linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and BoW).
3.  **Demonstration of Multi-View Learning Efficacy:** The paper effectively shows that combining the proposed topological features with traditional linguistic and lexical features through multi-view learning consistently improves classification performance. This highlights the complementary nature of these different information sources.

### Strengths

1.  **High-Impact Problem and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper addresses the important clinical problem of early dementia detection. The motivation for using complex networks is compelling: they offer a way to capture structural and relational aspects of language that might be robust to the disfluencies and agrammaticality common in MCI speech, thus bypassing the brittleness of traditional NLP pipelines that rely on clean syntax.
2.  **Methodological Clarity:** The proposed method is explained clearly, and the use of diagrams (especially Figure 2) is effective in illustrating the core idea of network enrichment. The feature extraction process is well-defined, drawing upon standard measures from graph theory.
3.  **Thoroughness of Experiments:** The evaluation is extensive. Using three different datasets with varying characteristics is a significant strength, suggesting the approach is not tailored to a single data type. The inclusion of multiple classifiers (SVM, k-NN, RF, etc.) and the exploration of both ensemble and multi-view combinations demonstrate a rigorous experimental process. The comparison against Coh-Metrix features, a strong and widely used baseline in this domain, is particularly valuable.
4.  **Promising Results:** The results demonstrate that the proposed CNE features are competitive on their own and provide significant value when combined with other feature sets. The fact that CNE outperforms standard linguistic metrics in several instances (e.g., Table 5, SVM-RBF) supports the central claim that this approach can effectively capture signals of cognitive impairment.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Crucial Missing Details on Network Enrichment:** The paper's core novelty lies in enriching networks with word embeddings, but this process lacks crucial details.
    *   **Embedding Training:** The paper states that fastText embeddings (Bojanowski et al., 2016) were used, but not what corpus they were trained on. Were pre-trained, general-domain embeddings used (e.g., trained on Wikipedia)? Or were they trained/fine-tuned on a specific clinical or in-domain corpus? This choice has a massive impact on the "semantic" relationships captured and is a critical detail for reproducibility and interpretation.
    *   **Threshold Selection:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges (0.7 for English, 0.4 for Portuguese) appears arbitrary. How was this crucial hyperparameter determined? Was it optimized on a development set through nested cross-validation, or was it an ad-hoc choice? This decision fundamentally alters the graph topology and, consequently, all derived features. Without proper justification, the method's robustness is questionable.
2.  **Lack of More Modern Baselines:** While BoW and Coh-Metrix are important baselines, the paper would be significantly stronger if it compared its approach to other modern methods that also leverage word embeddings but do not use complex networks. For example, a simple but strong baseline would be to average the embeddings of all words in a transcript to form a single document vector, which is then fed to a classifier. More sophisticated baselines could involve using a simple CNN or an LSTM/RNN-based model over the sequence of word embeddings. These comparisons would help isolate whether the performance gain comes from the embeddings themselves or specifically from the *topological structure* imposed by the network model.
3.  **Limited Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper successfully shows *that* the method works, but offers little insight into *why* it works. Which of the many topological features are most discriminative? For instance, do MCI patients produce texts that result in graphs with lower assortativity or a higher diameter? A feature importance or ablation analysis would elevate the paper from a purely engineering contribution to one that also offers potential clinical and linguistic insights into the nature of language breakdown in MCI. This would be a major addition to the paper's impact.
4.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper compares accuracy scores across different models and feature sets, but no statistical tests are reported to confirm if the observed differences are significant. Given the small sizes of the datasets (e.g., N=40 for Cinderella), a difference of a few percentage points may not be statistically meaningful. Reporting results of tests like McNemar's or paired t-tests over the cross-validation folds would be essential to substantiate claims of superiority (e.g., that CNE-LM-BoW is significantly better than BoW alone).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the word embeddings? Specifically, what corpora were used to train the English and Portuguese fastText models?
2.  How was the cosine similarity threshold for adding enrichment edges selected for each dataset (0.7 and 0.4)? Was this parameter tuned on a held-out development set, and if so, what was the tuning procedure? How sensitive are the final results to this threshold?
3.  Have you considered comparing your method to a simpler embedding-based baseline, such as classifying based on the averaged word embeddings of each transcript? This would help to disentangle the contribution of the graph topology from the general semantic information provided by the embeddings.
