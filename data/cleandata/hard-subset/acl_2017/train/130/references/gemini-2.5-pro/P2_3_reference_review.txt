### **Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To overcome the sparsity of these graphs, especially for short texts typical of neuropsychological assessments, the authors "enrich" the networks by adding new edges between words based on their semantic similarity, calculated from pre-trained word embeddings. From these enriched networks, a set of ten topological metrics (e.g., PageRank, Degree, Clustering Coefficient) and their statistical moments are extracted as features. The authors evaluate this approach on three different datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) and compare the performance of their network-based features against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. They demonstrate that the enriched complex network (CNE) features are competitive and, in some cases, superior to linguistic features. Finally, they show that the best classification accuracy is achieved by combining all three feature sets using a multi-view learning approach.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Application of Semantically Enriched Networks for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the adaptation and application of complex network analysis to the clinical task of MCI classification from short, disfluent speech transcripts. The key novelty lies in enriching the standard word co-occurrence network with semantic links derived from word embeddings, providing a principled way to add informative structure to otherwise sparse graphs.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The authors provide a solid empirical study across three distinct datasets, varying in language (English, Portuguese), elicitation task (picture description, story recall), and text length. This multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the generalizability of their findings.
3.  **Comparative Analysis of Different Feature Types:** The paper presents a valuable comparison between the proposed network features, established linguistic metrics, and a standard BoW baseline. The analysis showing the complementarity of these feature sets through multi-view learning is a significant finding.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Innovative Approach to a Challenging Problem:** The paper addresses the well-known challenge of applying NLP to noisy, disfluent, and often short clinical speech transcripts. Traditional methods relying on syntactic parsing (which informs many linguistic features) often fail in this domain. The proposed network-based approach elegantly sidesteps the need for robust parsers by capturing word relationships directly. The enrichment with word embeddings is a clever solution to the data sparsity problem inherent in modeling short texts as graphs.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The use of three datasets adds credibility to the results. The authors compare their method against strong, relevant baselines (linguistic features via Coh-Metrix and BoW). The inclusion of multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, etc.) and the exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques provide a comprehensive picture of the features' utility. The experiment on the manually cleaned Cinderella dataset (Table 6) provides insightful evidence for their claim that linguistic features depend on clean text, thus motivating their network-based approach for raw transcripts.
3.  **Clinically Relevant and Impactful Research Direction:** The early detection of MCI is a critical healthcare goal. This work contributes a novel, fully-automated method that could potentially be integrated into low-cost, non-invasive screening tools. The focus on avoiding manual preprocessing steps is particularly valuable for real-world applicability.
4.  **Clear Results and Analysis:** The results are presented clearly in tables, and the authors' analysis is generally easy to follow. They correctly identify that while their CNE features are strong on their own, the main benefit comes from combining them with other feature types. The 80% accuracy achieved on the ABCD dataset through multi-view learning is a very strong result.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Critical Methodological Details:** The core novelty of the paper—enriching the network—hinges on a similarity threshold hyperparameter. The authors state this was set to 0.7 for one dataset and 0.4 for the others, but they provide no explanation of how these values were chosen. If they were tuned on the test set, this constitutes a data leak and inflates the reported results. If they were tuned on a separate validation set, this procedure must be described. This omission significantly impacts the reproducibility and credibility of the core method. Similarly, details about the word embedding training (e.g., the corpus used for the fastText model) are sparse, which is a critical factor in determining the quality of the semantic enrichment.
2.  **Overstated Superiority of the Network Features:** The conclusion that CNE features "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" is not consistently supported by the data. On the Cookie Theft dataset (Table 4), the best CNE model (SVM-RBF, 0.6264) is only marginally better than the best BoW model (SVM-Linear, 0.5917). On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW model with a linear SVM achieves 75.47% accuracy, which is substantially higher than the best CNE model (71.73%). The results suggest that CNE is a *valuable and complementary* feature set, but not necessarily a superior replacement for other methods in all scenarios. The paper's framing could be more nuanced.
3.  **Absence of Feature Ablation or Analysis:** The paper extracts features from ten different network metrics, each summarized by its mean, standard deviation, and skewness. This results in approximately 30 features per transcript. However, there is no analysis of which of these topological properties are most discriminative. Is the performance driven by simple metrics like degree distribution, or do more complex measures like assortativity or clustering coefficient provide unique information? A feature importance analysis or an ablation study would provide much deeper insight into *why* this network-based representation is effective and would strengthen the paper's scientific contribution beyond just an engineering result.
4.  **Potential Confounding Variables and Dataset Limitations:** The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 40 subjects in the Cinderella dataset), which can lead to high variance in cross-validation results. More importantly, the paper does not discuss potential demographic confounders. For instance, in the ABCD dataset (Table 3), there is a large average age difference between the Control (61 years) and MCI (72 years) groups. This difference could be a powerful confounding variable that the classifiers are learning, rather than the linguistic features themselves. A discussion of these limitations and potential confounders is missing.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The cosine similarity threshold for enriching the network is a critical hyperparameter. Could you please clarify exactly how this threshold was selected for each dataset? Was a separate development/validation set used for tuning, or was it optimized based on performance on the test folds?
2.  Could you provide more details on the training corpus used for the fastText word embeddings? Was it a general-domain corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or a more specific in-domain corpus (e.g., clinical texts)? This choice has a significant bearing on the quality of the semantic similarity measures.
3.  Your results demonstrate the power of combining different feature sets. To better understand the contribution of your proposed network features, have you performed any analysis (e.g., feature importance, ablation) to identify which of the specific topological metrics are most predictive of MCI?
