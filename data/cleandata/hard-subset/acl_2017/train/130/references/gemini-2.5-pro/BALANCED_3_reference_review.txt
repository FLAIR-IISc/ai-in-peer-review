### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To address the problem of data sparsity in short clinical texts, the authors "enrich" these graphs by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, determined by a cosine similarity threshold on their word embeddings (fastText). The authors then extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks (CNE) and use them in a machine learning pipeline. The performance of CNE features is compared against traditional linguistic metrics (LM) from Coh-Metrix and a Bag-of-Words (BoW) baseline. The evaluation is conducted on three datasets: the English Cookie Theft descriptions, and two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella story retelling and ABCD narrative recall), which vary in text length and task. The results show that the CNE features are competitive, often outperforming the linguistic metrics, and that combining all three feature sets using multi-view learning yields the highest classification accuracy.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Application of an Enriched Network Model to a Clinical NLP Task:** The primary contribution is the novel application and evaluation of word embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI detection. While the enrichment technique is adapted from prior work, its use to mitigate the short-text problem in noisy clinical transcripts is a valuable and well-motivated contribution. The paper demonstrates that this approach, which avoids reliance on fragile parsing tools, is a competitive alternative to standard linguistic feature sets.
2.  **A Multi-faceted Comparative Study:** The paper provides a thorough comparative analysis across three different feature paradigms (network-based, linguistic, and BoW) on three distinct datasets spanning two languages and various text lengths. This comprehensive evaluation serves as a useful benchmark, highlighting the relative merits and trade-offs of each approach under different conditions (e.g., text length, presence of disfluencies).

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Practical Approach:** The paper addresses a clear and important problem in clinical NLP: the difficulty of applying sophisticated linguistic analysis to short, noisy, and disfluent speech transcripts. The proposed method of using complex networks enriched with semantic information is an elegant way to bypass the need for heavy preprocessing, manual correction, or robust parsers, which are often unavailable or unreliable for such data. This makes the approach highly practical for building automated diagnostic aids.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is a significant strength. By testing on three different datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD), the authors provide evidence for the method's applicability across different languages, elicitation tasks, and average text lengths. The inclusion of two strong baselines—a rich set of linguistic features (LM) and a standard Bag-of-Words model (BoW)—provides a solid foundation for comparing and contextualizing the performance of the proposed network features.
3.  **Sound Methodological Choices:** The paper demonstrates thoughtful design. The decision to use fastText embeddings to handle out-of-vocabulary words, a common issue in clinical data, is appropriate. Similarly, the justification for omitting lemmatization to avoid cascading errors from POS taggers on atypical language is well-reasoned. The exploration of both individual classifiers and more complex multi-view and ensemble models adds a valuable layer to the analysis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper makes several comparative claims based on differences in accuracy (e.g., CNE is better than LM, multi-view is better than a single feature set). However, no statistical significance tests are reported. Given the relatively small sizes of the datasets (N=40 for Cinderella, N=43 for ABCD), it is crucial to establish whether observed performance differences are statistically significant or could be due to random variation. Without this, the reliability of the paper's conclusions is diminished.
2.  **Inconsistent Results and Unexplored Contradictions:** The paper's central narrative—that CNE is particularly effective for short texts—is not consistently supported by the results. On the ABCD dataset, which contains the shortest texts, the BoW model is the best-performing individual feature set, outperforming CNE (75.5% vs. 71.7% with SVM-Linear). Furthermore, on the manually cleaned Cinderella data, the traditional linguistic features (LM) outperform CNE (72.5% vs. 67.5% with SVM-RBF). While the authors briefly acknowledge this, they do not sufficiently discuss the implications. This suggests that the advantages of the CNE method are highly conditional and not as universally applicable as implied.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis of Features and Hyperparameters:** The paper treats the network features largely as a black box. A large set of topological metrics are extracted, but there is no analysis of which features are most discriminative for MCI detection. An analysis of whether, for instance, MCI patients' graphs exhibit significantly different clustering coefficients or diameters would connect the methodology back to the cognitive phenomena being studied and elevate the paper beyond a simple feature engineering exercise. Additionally, the process for optimizing the crucial cosine similarity threshold is not detailed, raising concerns about potential information leakage from the test set if not performed using a properly nested cross-validation scheme.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The presentation of results in Section 5 is somewhat difficult to follow, jumping between datasets, classifiers, and feature combinations. A more structured discussion, perhaps organized by research question (e.g., "How does CNE compare to baselines?", "What is the effect of text length?"), would improve readability. Furthermore, the claims in the conclusion, such as CNE "outperform[ing] traditionally linguistic metrics, in individual classifiers results," are overstatements that are not universally supported by the tables.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the apparent contradiction in your results for the shortest texts (ABCD dataset), where the BoW model outperformed your proposed CNE model? Why do you believe this was the case, and how does this affect the primary motivation for using enriched networks on short texts?
2.  Could you provide the results of statistical significance tests for your main comparisons (e.g., CNE vs. LM, CNE vs. BoW, and the best multi-view model vs. the best single-feature model on each dataset)? This would greatly strengthen the validity of your claims.
3.  Please provide more detail on how the cosine similarity threshold was optimized for each dataset. Specifically, was this tuning performed within each fold of the cross-validation to prevent any leakage of information from the validation/test data?
4.  Did you perform any analysis to understand which of the topological network features were most predictive of MCI? For example, are there specific structural properties of the language graphs that consistently differ between MCI and control participants? Such an analysis would provide valuable insight into the linguistic markers of MCI from a network perspective.
