### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The primary contribution is a technique to address the sparsity of such networks for short texts by "enriching" the standard word co-occurrence graph with additional edges. These new edges connect non-adjacent words that are semantically similar, as determined by a cosine similarity threshold on their word embeddings. The authors extract topological features from these enriched graphs and use them for classification. A secondary contribution is an empirical evaluation on three datasets (in English and Portuguese), demonstrating that these network-based features are competitive with and complementary to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words models.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper's core idea is a significant and welcome departure from the incremental improvements often seen in this domain. Instead of engineering a few more linguistic features, the authors propose a fundamentally new representation for transcripts that captures both local syntactic structure (co-occurrence) and global semantic relationships (embedding similarity). The enrichment of a structural graph with semantic information is a bold and creative approach that directly tackles a well-known weakness of graph-based text analysis: its ineffectiveness on short documents. This conceptual novelty is the paper's greatest strength.

2.  **Elegant Solution to a Practical Problem:** The proposed method offers a promising way to bypass the need for complex, language-specific, and often brittle NLP pipelines. Traditional linguistic features rely heavily on tools like syntactic parsers, which are known to perform poorly on the disfluent and often agrammatical speech produced by patients. The complex network approach, especially one that doesn't require lemmatization or parsing, is inherently more robust to such noise. This makes the method more practical, especially for less-resourced languages like the Portuguese examples used here.

3.  **Well-Motivated Methodology:** The motivation for enriching the networks is clear and compelling. A simple co-occurrence network from a short transcript is often just a simple path graph with little informative topology. The authors correctly identify this limitation and propose using word embeddings as a principled way to add meaningful structure. Figure 2 provides a clear and intuitive illustration of the benefit of this enrichment, transforming a nearly linear graph into a much richer structure from which meaningful features can be extracted.

4.  **Strong Empirical Signal:** Across three different datasets, the features derived from the enriched complex networks (CNE) consistently provide a strong classification signal. While not always the single best feature set, they are highly competitive with established baselines like linguistic metrics (LM) and bag-of-words (BoW). Crucially, the multi-view learning experiments show that the CNE features are complementary to these other methods, leading to the best overall performance when combined. This demonstrates that the novel representation is capturing unique and relevant information about the speaker's cognitive state.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Claims of Superiority:** The paper's conclusion that the topological properties "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" is an overstatement not fully supported by the provided results. In several instances, particularly on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), a simple BoW model with a linear SVM is the best-performing individual model. The results more accurately support the claim that the CNE features are a *competitive and complementary* source of information, which is still a strong result. The authors should moderate their claims to better reflect the data.

2.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper presents the extraction of network features as a successful but somewhat "black box" process. There is no attempt to connect the specific topological metrics back to the cognitive or linguistic phenomena associated with MCI. For example, do the graphs of MCI patients exhibit a lower clustering coefficient or a different degree distribution? Does this relate to known issues like loss of semantic coherence or circumlocution? Providing even a brief qualitative analysis or showing examples of graph differences between patient groups would immensely increase the paper's insightfulness and explanatory power.

3.  **Unexplored Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The method's performance appears highly dependent on two key components: the quality of the word embeddings and the choice of the cosine similarity threshold. The paper uses generic, pre-trained embeddings without discussing their suitability for this specific clinical domain, where word usage can be idiosyncratic. Furthermore, the similarity threshold is optimized but its sensitivity is not analyzed. A brief discussion of how performance varies with different thresholds or a comparison between generic and domain-adapted embeddings would strengthen the validation of the method. While not a fatal flaw, it leaves important questions about the method's stability unanswered.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The strong performance of the BoW baseline on the ABCD dataset (Table 7) is surprising, as it seems to contradict the motivation for moving to more complex structural representations. Do you have any hypotheses for why simple lexical frequency was so effective for this particular dataset? Could it be related to the specific narrative recall task used?

2.  Could you provide a more qualitative intuition for what the network enrichment is capturing? For instance, could you show a small snippet of a transcript and the corresponding graph, highlighting a few key semantic links that were added and explain how they might be relevant for distinguishing MCI from control subjects? This would help bridge the gap between the topological features and the underlying linguistic behavior.
