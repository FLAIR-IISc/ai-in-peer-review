### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to create word co-occurrence networks and enrich them with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. This enrichment is designed to make the network representation more informative, especially for the short and disfluent texts common in clinical assessments. The authors extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification. They conduct experiments on three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) of varying text lengths, comparing the network-based features against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW). They also demonstrate that combining all three feature types using multi-view learning yields the best performance.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Representation for MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks to this clinical task. Specifically, the method of enriching a standard word adjacency graph with semantic edges from word embeddings is a novel and well-motivated approach to handle the challenges of short and noisy clinical transcripts.
2.  **A Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their method across three distinct datasets, covering two languages (English and Portuguese) and different elicitation tasks (picture description, story retelling). This multi-faceted evaluation provides strong evidence for the generalizability and robustness of the proposed features.
3.  **An Effective Feature Combination Strategy:** The paper shows that the proposed network features are complementary to established linguistic and BoW features. Their results demonstrate that a multi-view learning approach, which combines classifiers trained on these different feature sets, consistently outperforms any single feature type, leading to state-of-the-art results on the datasets used.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The paper introduces a novel and sensible method for a challenging clinical application. The motivation is clear: traditional linguistic features often require clean, syntactically well-formed text, which is rare in spontaneous speech transcripts from patients with cognitive decline. The authors’ proposed solution—enriching sparse co-occurrence networks with semantic information from word embeddings—is an elegant way to create a more robust representation that is less dependent on grammatical structure and more resilient to the challenges of short texts.
2.  **Rigorous Experimental Design:** The evaluation is thorough and well-structured. By testing on three different datasets, the authors demonstrate the versatility of their method across languages, text genres, and lengths. The inclusion of two strong and distinct baselines (linguistic features via Coh-Metrix and a standard BoW model) provides a solid point of comparison. Furthermore, the exploration of multiple classifiers and combination strategies (ensemble and multi-view learning) adds to the credibility of the results.
3.  **Insightful Results and Analysis:** The results are promising and support the paper's core claims. The enriched network features (CNE) are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, the individual baselines. The finding that performance on the manually-cleaned Cinderella dataset (Table 6) improves more for linguistic features than for network features provides compelling evidence for the authors' hypothesis that network-based methods are more robust to the noise and disfluencies present in raw transcripts. The consistent performance boost from combining feature sets via multi-view learning is a key takeaway, highlighting the complementary nature of the proposed features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The manuscript suffers from several presentation errors that detract from its quality and make it difficult to fully understand the experimental setup.
    *   Table 1 is referenced in the text (Section 4.1.1) but is missing from the paper.
    *   The caption for Table 2 incorrectly identifies it as describing the Cinderella dataset, while the preceding text clearly discusses the Cookie Theft dataset.
    *   The number of control participants for the Cookie Theft dataset is never explicitly stated, making it impossible to assess the class balance for that experiment.
    These errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading and should be addressed.
2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** Key details required for reproducibility are missing.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The authors state they trained fastText embeddings but do not specify the training corpus. Were they trained on a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or only on the small, in-domain clinical datasets? This is a critical detail, as the quality and domain relevance of the embeddings directly impact the network enrichment process.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The similarity threshold for adding edges is a crucial hyperparameter. The paper simply states it was "optimized" and provides the final values. To ensure the validity of the results, the authors should describe this optimization process. Specifically, was a separate development set used, or was tuning performed within each cross-validation fold to prevent data leakage from the test set?
3.  **Interpretation of Results:** While generally positive, the results warrant a more nuanced discussion.
    *   On the ABCD dataset, the simple BoW baseline with a linear SVM (75.5% accuracy) outperforms the proposed CNE features (71.7%). The final combined model improves performance to 80.2%, but the strength of the BoW baseline on these very short texts slightly weakens the argument that complex networks are inherently superior for this setting. This finding deserves more discussion.
    *   The baseline for comparison is not fully established. Providing the majority class baseline accuracy for each dataset would help contextualize the reported performance and make the significance of the improvements clearer. For example, a jump from 51% to 65% is more impressive than a jump from 60% to 65%.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the fastText word embeddings? Specifically, what corpus was used for the English and Portuguese models?
2.  How was the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment optimized? Was this done using a nested cross-validation scheme or a separate development set to avoid information leakage from the test folds?
3.  Could you please provide the missing demographic information for the Cookie Theft dataset (what should have been in Table 1), including the number of participants in the control group? Additionally, what is the majority class baseline accuracy for each of the three datasets?
