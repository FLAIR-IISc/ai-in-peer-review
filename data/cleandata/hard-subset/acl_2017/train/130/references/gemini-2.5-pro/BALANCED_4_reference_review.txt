### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling the text as a complex network. The primary novelty lies in enriching a standard word co-occurrence network with additional edges based on the semantic similarity of words, as measured by word embeddings. This enrichment is designed to make the network representation more informative, especially for the short and disfluent texts common in neuropsychological assessments. The authors evaluate their proposed features (Complex Network Enriched, CNE) on three datasets (one in English, two in Brazilian Portuguese) and compare their performance against traditional linguistic metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. They also explore combining these feature sets using multi-view learning.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel application of enriched complex networks for a clinical task.** The paper introduces a method that combines word co-occurrence graphs with semantic information from word embeddings to tackle the specific challenge of data sparsity in short clinical transcripts. This is a well-motivated approach to an important problem.
2.  **A comparative empirical study on multiple diverse datasets.** The authors provide a valuable evaluation of their proposed method against strong, standard baselines (linguistic features, BoW) across three datasets that vary in language, elicitation task, and text length.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and novel methodology.** The core idea of enriching sparse co-occurrence networks with semantic links from word embeddings is clever and directly addresses a key challenge in analyzing short texts. Standard graph metrics often yield uninformative values for nearly linear graphs, which are common for short utterances. The proposed enrichment method provides a principled way to densify these graphs, potentially capturing long-distance semantic relationships that are missed by simple adjacency. The visualization in Figure 2 effectively demonstrates the conceptual benefit of this approach.
2.  **Thorough experimental design and evaluation.** The study's credibility is significantly strengthened by its evaluation on three distinct datasets, including two different languages (English and Portuguese). This demonstrates that the approach is not tailored to a single dataset or language. Furthermore, the comparison against widely-used and strong baselines—linguistic features from Coh-Metrix and a standard BoW model—provides a solid benchmark for assessing the performance of the proposed CNE features. The inclusion of multi-view learning to combine feature sets is also a sensible extension.
3.  **Focus on a practical and impactful application.** The paper tackles the important clinical problem of early MCI detection. The authors' motivation to develop a fully automated system that is robust to the disfluencies present in real-world speech transcripts and requires less manual preprocessing is a significant practical strength. This goal contrasts favorably with prior work that relied on manually cleaned-up text to achieve good performance, making the proposed approach more scalable and applicable in a clinical setting.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent performance gains and overstated claims.** The central claim that CNE features outperform traditional methods is not consistently supported by the results.
    *   On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW model achieves the highest accuracy for a single feature set (75.5% vs. CNE's 71.7%).
    *   On the manually revised Cinderella dataset (Table 6), the linguistic metrics (LM) perform best (72.5% vs. CNE's 67.5%).
    *   On the Cookie Theft dataset (Table 4), the best CNE model (62.6%) offers only a marginal improvement over the best BoW model (60.1%).
    The conclusion that CNE features "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics, in individual classifiers results" is an overstatement. The performance gains are modest and context-dependent, which weakens the paper's main argument. The overall accuracy scores (e.g., 65% on Cookie Theft) are also relatively low, raising questions about the immediate clinical utility.
2.  **Missing implementation details crucial for reproducibility and interpretation.** Several key methodological details are either missing or underspecified, which is a significant weakness for a computational paper.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The paper does not specify the corpus used to train the word embeddings. Were pre-trained vectors used (e.g., trained on Wikipedia), or were they trained on the small in-domain datasets? This choice has massive implications for the quality and nature of the semantic information being injected into the networks.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The cosine similarity threshold is a critical hyperparameter, yet its optimization process is not described. Was it tuned using a separate development set, or within the cross-validation loop (which could lead to information leakage)? A sensitivity analysis showing how results vary with this threshold would have been valuable.
    *   **Multi-view Learning:** The combination of classifiers is described simply as "majority vote." For combining three feature sets (CNE-LM-BoW), it is unclear how this is implemented. A more detailed description of the fusion strategy is necessary.
3.  **Lack of error analysis and feature interpretation.** The paper presents performance tables but provides no insight into *why* the method works or what linguistic phenomena it captures. An analysis of the most predictive topological features (e.g., via feature importance scores from the Random Forest model) is absent. Do MCI patients' networks exhibit lower clustering coefficients or different assortativity? Answering such questions would connect the abstract graph metrics back to cognitive hypotheses about language breakdown in MCI (e.g., disorganized semantic fields, topic wandering) and would elevate the paper from a purely engineering result to a more insightful piece of computational linguistics research. Without this analysis, the features remain a black box.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the word embeddings? Specifically, were they pre-trained on a large external corpus, or were they trained on the experimental datasets themselves?
2.  How was the cosine similarity threshold (0.7 for English, 0.4 for Portuguese) selected for enriching the networks? Was this parameter tuned on a held-out development set, or was it optimized within the 5-fold cross-validation procedure?
3.  The paper's conclusion states that CNE features outperform linguistic metrics, but the results in Tables 6 and 7 show that LM and BoW, respectively, achieve the best performance for a single feature set on those datasets. Could you comment on this and perhaps provide a more nuanced interpretation of the relative strengths of the different feature types?
4.  Could you provide any analysis on which of the ten network topological metrics were most predictive of MCI status? For example, did you find consistent differences in measures like average clustering coefficient or network diameter between the MCI and control groups across datasets? Such an analysis would offer valuable insight into what aspects of discourse structure your model is capturing.
