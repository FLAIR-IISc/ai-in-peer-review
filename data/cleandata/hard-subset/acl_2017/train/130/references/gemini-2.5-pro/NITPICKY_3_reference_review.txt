### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The primary contribution is a novel text representation technique where standard word co-occurrence networks are "enriched" by adding edges between non-adjacent words based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. This is motivated by the challenge of applying network analysis to short texts, which typically result in sparse, linear graphs. The authors extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them in a machine learning classification pipeline. A secondary contribution is an empirical comparison of this network-based approach against traditional linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations across three different datasets, including texts in both English and Portuguese. The study also explores combining these different feature sets using multi-view learning.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Core Idea:** The central proposal to enrich sparse co-occurrence networks with semantic edges derived from word embeddings is a clever solution to a well-known problem: the limited utility of network metrics on very short texts. This addresses a specific challenge in the clinical domain, where patient narratives can be brief. The visualization in Figure 2 effectively illustrates how this enrichment process can create a more topologically informative graph from a simple linear chain.

2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their method on three distinct datasets that vary in language (English, Portuguese), task (picture description, story retelling), and length. The inclusion of two versions of the Cinderella dataset (one with automated preprocessing and one with manual cleaning) provides a valuable ablation-like study on the impact of text quality, strengthening the claim that their method is robust to the disfluencies present in raw transcripts.

3.  **Systematic Comparison of Feature Types:** The paper does not just present its novel feature set in isolation. It provides a direct and systematic comparison with two strong and widely-used baselines: sophisticated linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix) and standard BoW. Furthermore, it investigates combinations of these feature sets, which is a practical and important step for building a robust classifier. This comprehensive comparison provides a clear context for judging the performance of the proposed network features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Severe Lack of Professionalism in Presentation:** The paper is marred by an unacceptable number of formatting, referencing, and typesetting errors that severely undermine its credibility.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference section is completely broken. It contains unresolved BibTeX fields (e.g., `\Q2016\E`), question marks, inconsistent formatting, and repeated, malformed entries. This is not a minor issue; it suggests a profound lack of care in the paper's preparation.
    *   **Tables and Figures:** There are significant inconsistencies between the text and the tables. The text in Section 4.1.1 refers to "Table 1," but no table is labeled as such. The first unlabeled table presumably corresponds to the Cookie Theft dataset. The caption for Table 2 refers to the Cinderella dataset, but its data appears incomplete or incorrectly formatted. Figure 1 is explicitly mentioned in the text but is not included in the paper. These errors make the experimental setup difficult to follow and verify.

2.  **Omission of Critical Methodological Details:** The methodology lacks crucial details required for reproducibility and for a full assessment of the results.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The paper fails to state how the word embeddings were trained. Were they trained on the small, in-domain datasets, which would likely yield poor-quality vectors? Or were they pre-trained on a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia, Common Crawl)? This is arguably the single most important detail of the method, and its omission is a major flaw.
    *   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The cosine similarity threshold is a key hyperparameter for the network enrichment process. The paper states it was "optimized," but provides no details on the procedure (e.g., what was the search range? was it optimized via a nested cross-validation on the training set?). Similarly, the choice of using term frequency (tf) for BoW instead of the more standard tf-idf is not justified.

3.  **Inconsistencies and Overstated Claims in Results Analysis:** The paper's conclusions are not fully supported by its own results, and key findings are left unexamined.
    *   **Contradictory Performance:** The conclusion claims the proposed network features "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics." However, the results are inconsistent. In Table 4 (Cookie Theft), CNE is better than LM. In Table 5 (Cinderella), CNE is much better. But in Table 7 (ABCD), the simple BoW baseline (75.5%) is substantially better than CNE (71.7%), and LM (70.6% with KNN) is competitive. The claim of clear superiority is an overstatement.
    *   **Discrepancy in Multi-view Learning:** Section 4.3 suggests a sophisticated multi-view approach where weights for each feature space "can be learned by a regression method." However, the experiments only employ a simple majority vote. This is a significant disconnect between the proposed methodology and the actual implementation.
    *   **Unexplained Baseline Behavior:** The performance of the BoW baseline fluctuates wildly between datasets, from an extremely poor 37.5% on the Cinderella dataset (Table 5) to being the best-performing single feature set on the ABCD dataset (Table 7). This massive variance is highly unusual and is not discussed or analyzed, raising questions about the stability and implementation of the baselines.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please provide the full details of the word embedding models used. Specifically, on what corpus were they trained, and what was the vocabulary size? If you used a pre-trained model, please cite it.

2.  Could you clarify the discrepancy between the description of multi-view learning in Section 4.3 (which mentions learning weights) and the use of a simple majority vote in the experiments? Did you experiment with any weighted combination schemes?

3.  The performance of the Bag-of-Words baseline is exceptionally low on the Cinderella dataset (Table 5) but is the strongest individual feature set on the ABCD dataset (Table 7). Can you offer any explanation for this dramatic performance difference? Could it be related to text length, vocabulary overlap, or other dataset characteristics?

4.  Given the extensive and severe errors in the bibliography and table/figure referencing, can you assure the reviewers that the paper will undergo a thorough and professional copy-editing process prior to publication? The current state is far below the expected standard for an ACL venue.
