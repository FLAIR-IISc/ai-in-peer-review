### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The primary novelty lies in "enriching" word co-occurrence networks with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors argue this enrichment helps overcome the problem of feature sparsity in short texts. The paper evaluates features extracted from these networks against, and in combination with, two established baselines: standard linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The evaluation is conducted on three datasets across two languages.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of word co-occurrence networks, enriched via word embedding similarity, as a feature extraction method for the clinical task of MCI detection.
2.  An empirical comparison of this network-based approach against strong, standard baselines (linguistic features and BoW) on multiple datasets.
3.  A demonstration that combining the proposed network features with baseline features using a multi-view learning approach can, in some cases, improve classification accuracy.

### Strengths

1.  **Clinically Relevant Problem:** The paper addresses the important and challenging task of early MCI detection, a problem where automated linguistic analysis holds significant promise. The motivation to develop methods that are robust to the noisy and disfluent nature of clinical speech transcripts is sound.
2.  **Evaluation across Multiple Datasets:** The authors validate their approach on three distinct datasets, which vary in language (English, Portuguese), elicitation task (picture description, story recall), and text length. This provides a more comprehensive evaluation than a study on a single dataset and lends more credibility to the findings.
3.  **Inclusion of Strong Baselines:** The paper correctly compares the proposed network-based features (CNE) against well-established and powerful baselines, namely linguistic metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). This comparison is essential for contextualizing the performance of the proposed method and is a methodologically sound aspect of the experimental design.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Superiority of the Proposed Method Is Not Convincingly Demonstrated:** A central claim of the paper is that the enriched complex network (CNE) representation is a powerful tool for this task, potentially outperforming traditional methods. The results, however, do not consistently support this assertion. In many cases, standard baselines perform as well or better.
    *   On the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves 75.47% accuracy, which is substantially better than the best result from the proposed CNE method (71.73%). This suggests that for very short texts, a simple word frequency model is more effective.
    *   On the manually revised Cinderella dataset (Table 6), the established linguistic features (LM) achieve 72.50% accuracy, outperforming CNE's best of 67.50%. This result directly contradicts the motivation that network methods are better suited for this data, as cleaning the text makes the traditional method superior.
    *   While CNE shows some promise on the Cookie Theft and the original Cinderella datasets, its performance gains over baselines are often marginal. The primary gains in the paper come from combining feature sets, which suggests that CNE offers complementary information rather than being a superior standalone representation. The claim in the conclusion that CNE "outperfom traditionally linguistic metrics" is an overstatement not fully supported by the evidence presented.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Core "Enrichment" Method:** The novel contribution of enriching networks via word embeddings is not analyzed with sufficient rigor. The method relies on a cosine similarity threshold, a critical hyperparameter, yet its selection and impact are not explored.
    *   The paper states the threshold was "optimized" to 0.7 for one dataset and 0.4 for others, but provides no details on this process. Was a separate development set used? How was this search conducted? Without this information, it is difficult to assess the risk of overfitting or to understand the robustness of the method.
    *   A sensitivity analysis is missing. How does classification performance change as this threshold is varied? A method that is highly sensitive to one of its core parameters is less likely to be robust and generalizable.
    *   The source of the word embeddings is also unclear. The paper states "we trained the model," but does not specify the training corpus. Training on the small in-domain datasets would result in poor-quality embeddings, undermining the entire enrichment premise. If a large external corpus was used, this should be stated explicitly.

3.  **Lack of Clarity in Evaluation and Reporting:** Some aspects of the experimental setup and results are ambiguous, making them difficult to interpret.
    *   The substantial performance increase from multi-view learning in Table 5 is surprising and not adequately explained. Combining two views with individual ensemble accuracies of 60.0% (CNE) and 50.0% (LM) to achieve 75.0% suggests an unusually strong synergistic effect that warrants further analysis or could indicate a methodological flaw.
    *   The description of how the "Ensemble" results in the multi-view columns are generated is confusing. Clarification is needed on how the ensemble of classifiers and the multi-view combination interact.
    *   The number of control subjects for the Cookie Theft dataset is not explicitly stated in the text, which is a minor but important detail for understanding the dataset composition.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the corpus used to train the word embeddings? Was a large, general-purpose corpus used, or were they trained only on the small, task-specific datasets? The validity of the semantic enrichment depends heavily on the quality of these embeddings.
2.  The cosine similarity threshold is a crucial component of your proposed method. Could you elaborate on the optimization procedure used to select the values of 0.7 and 0.4? Specifically, was a held-out validation set used within the cross-validation folds to prevent information leakage?
3.  Given that standard baselines like BoW and LM outperform your proposed CNE method on two of the four experimental conditions (ABCD and manually-cleaned Cinderella), do you agree that the primary value of CNE may be as a source of complementary features rather than as a superior standalone representation? If so, the paper's central claims might need to be recalibrated.
4.  In Table 5, the multi-view combination of CNE and LM features results in a 75% accuracy, a very large improvement over the individual ensemble accuracies of 60% and 50%. Can you provide more insight into why this combination yields such a strong synergistic effect?
