**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph. To address the issue of short texts yielding sparse and uninformative graphs, the authors "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, determined by a cosine similarity threshold on their word embeddings. Topological features are then extracted from these enriched networks and used in a machine learning pipeline to classify transcripts from MCI patients versus healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets (two in Portuguese, one in English) and compared against baselines using linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. The authors claim that their enriched network features outperform traditional methods and that performance can be further improved by combining feature sets.

**Contributions of this work (as I see them)**
1.  The primary contribution is an empirical study applying an embedding-enriched complex network model to the clinical task of MCI detection from transcribed speech. This adapts existing graph-based text representation methods to a challenging domain characterized by short and disfluent language.
2.  A secondary contribution is the evaluation across three datasets in two languages, providing evidence for the method's potential applicability in different contexts.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty of Application:** The paper applies a relatively novel text representation technique (embedding-enriched graphs) to an important and challenging clinical problem. The motivation to find methods that are robust to the disfluencies and syntactic irregularities of patient speech, thereby avoiding reliance on brittle parsing tools, is compelling and well-argued.
2.  **Addressing Data Sparsity:** The strategy of enriching sparse co-occurrence graphs with semantic edges from word embeddings is a methodologically sound and clever way to handle the short length of clinical transcripts. It allows for a richer topological characterization than would be possible from simple adjacency graphs, as effectively illustrated in Figure 2.
3.  **Multi-Faceted Evaluation:** The authors are to be commended for evaluating their approach on three distinct datasets, spanning two languages and different elicitation tasks. This provides a more robust test of the method's utility than a single-dataset evaluation.
4.  **Sound Combination of Features:** The use of multi-view learning to combine the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features is a sensible approach. The results showing that this combination often leads to the best performance correctly suggest that the network features capture complementary information.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Critical Methodological Omissions:** The paper's experimental methodology is insufficiently described, making the results impossible to reproduce and their validity uncertain.
    *   **Word Embedding Training Corpus:** The entire "enrichment" process depends on word embeddings, yet the paper fails to specify the corpus used to train them. Section 5 mentions training the model but omits the most critical detail: the training data. Were embeddings trained on the small, task-specific datasets (which would be methodologically unsound) or a large, external corpus? Without this information, the core of the proposed method is a black box.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a key hyperparameter. The paper states it was "optimized" but provides no details on the optimization procedure. This must be clarified. Was a separate development set used? Was optimization performed via nested cross-validation? If the threshold was tuned using the full dataset (including the test folds), this would constitute data leakage and inflate the reported accuracies.

2.  **Inconsistent and Overstated Claims:** The paper's conclusions are not fully supported by its own results tables, and some interpretations reveal a potential misunderstanding of the baselines.
    *   The conclusion in Section 6 states: "We have shown that the topological properties of CN outperfom traditionally linguistic metrics, in individual classifiers results." This is verifiably false according to their own data. In Table 6, the best Linguistic Metric (LM) accuracy is 0.7250, far exceeding the best enriched network (CNE) accuracy of 0.6750. In Table 7, the best Bag-of-Words (BoW) accuracy is 0.7547, which is also higher than the best CNE accuracy of 0.7173. The central claim of the paper is therefore contradicted by its own evidence.
    *   The authors describe the strong performance of a linear SVM on BoW features (0.7547 in Table 7) as "Somewhat surprising." This is a standard and powerful baseline in text classification, not a surprising result. This comment suggests a lack of awareness of the strength of established baselines.

3.  **Unaddressed Confounding Variables:** The analysis of the ABCD dataset (Section 4.1.3 and Table 3) overlooks a critical confounding variable. There is a large and statistically significant age difference between the groups (Control Avg. Age: 61, MCI Avg. Age: 72). Since age is a primary risk factor for MCI, the classifier may be learning to distinguish between the language of 60-year-olds and 70-year-olds, rather than detecting cognitive impairment. This potential confound is not acknowledged, let alone controlled for, seriously threatening the validity of the results on that dataset.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript is marred by an unacceptable number of formatting and presentation errors, which severely detract from its professional quality and credibility.
    *   **Missing Figures and Tables:** Table 1 is explicitly referenced in the text but is missing from the paper. Figures 1, 3, and 4 are also referenced but are not included.
    *   **Bibliography Errors:** The reference list is corrupted with BibTeX processing artifacts (e.g., `? \Q2016\E` appears repeatedly), indicating a lack of proofreading.
    *   **General Sloppiness:** The text referring to the Cookie Theft dataset demographics points to the non-existent Table 1, while the caption for Table 2 identifies it as the Cinderella dataset, creating confusion for the reader.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Please specify the exact corpus (including its size, source, and language) used to train the word embeddings for each of the three datasets.
2.  Can you provide a precise description of the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks? Was this done in a way that prevents information from the test sets from influencing the parameter choice?
3.  Given the significant age difference between the control and MCI groups in the ABCD dataset, how can you be sure your classifier is identifying MCI-related linguistic changes rather than simply modeling age-related language differences? Have you performed any analysis to control for this potential confounder?
4.  Your main conclusion states that your enriched network features (CNE) outperform linguistic metrics (LM). How do you reconcile this with your results in Tables 6 and 7, where LM and BoW features, respectively, achieve higher classification accuracy than CNE features? Would it be more accurate to claim that CNE features are a competitive, complementary signal rather than a superior one?
