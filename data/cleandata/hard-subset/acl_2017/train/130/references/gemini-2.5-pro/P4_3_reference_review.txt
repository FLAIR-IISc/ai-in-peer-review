### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core of the method involves representing each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and then "enriching" it by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, with similarity measured by the cosine distance of their word embeddings. This enrichment step is designed to address the problem of network sparsity, which is common when modeling short texts like those from neuropsychological assessments. The authors extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three datasets—one in English and two in Brazilian Portuguese—of varying lengths and tasks. The performance of the enriched complex network (CNE) features is compared against standard baselines, including traditional linguistic metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW). The results demonstrate that the CNE features are competitive and that combining all three feature types using a multi-view learning approach yields the highest classification accuracy.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel feature representation for MCI detection from text.** The primary contribution is the application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to model speech transcripts for a clinical classification task. This method provides a new set of topological features that are shown to be effective for distinguishing between MCI and control participants, offering an alternative to traditional linguistic analysis that may be less dependent on clean, grammatical text.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and languages.** The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on three distinct datasets that vary in language (English, Portuguese), elicitation task (picture description, story recall), and average transcript length. This evaluation provides strong evidence for the potential generalizability of the proposed features.
3.  **Demonstration of effective feature combination through multi-view learning.** The study systematically shows that while the proposed network features are strong on their own, classification performance is consistently improved by combining them with traditional linguistic and Bag-of-Words features in a multi-view learning framework.

### Strengths
1.  **Methodological Novelty for the Target Application.** While complex networks and word embeddings are established techniques in NLP, their combination to enrich representations of short, disfluent speech transcripts for MCI detection is a novel contribution. The proposed enrichment strategy is a technically sound and well-motivated solution to the inherent problem of data sparsity in network models of short texts. This is a significant challenge in many clinical NLP applications, and the paper's approach presents a viable solution.
2.  **Potential Robustness to Noisy Clinical Data.** A key advantage of the proposed method is its reduced reliance on grammatically perfect text, unlike many traditional linguistic feature extractors that depend on accurate parsing. Speech transcripts from clinical settings are often rife with disfluencies, revisions, and agrammatical constructions. By modeling word adjacency and semantic similarity rather than complex syntactic structures, the method is potentially more robust. This is a crucial practical advantage for developing automated diagnostic tools that can operate on raw or minimally processed transcripts.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Design and Evaluation.** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation of their method. The use of three different datasets strengthens the paper's claims. The method is benchmarked against two appropriate and widely-used baselines (linguistic features via Coh-Metrix and Bag-of-Words). The study also explores a variety of machine learning classifiers and demonstrates the utility of both classifier ensembling and multi-view learning, showing a systematic effort to achieve the best possible performance and understand the interplay between different feature sources.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing.** The paper reports improvements in accuracy, but many of these gains are modest (e.g., a 2.3% improvement on the Cookie Theft dataset). Without statistical significance tests, it is impossible to determine whether these differences are meaningful or simply a result of chance given the specific cross-validation splits. This omission is a critical flaw that weakens the conclusions drawn from the results, particularly when comparing the proposed method to strong baselines.
2.  **Limited Interpretability and Feature Analysis.** The paper introduces a large set of topological network features (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness, Clustering Coefficient, each summarized by mean, standard deviation, and skewness) but provides no analysis of which features are most discriminative. An analysis of feature importance would offer valuable insight into which graph properties are most affected by MCI-related language changes. This would elevate the work beyond a purely engineering contribution by providing a potential link between the network topology and the underlying cognitive-linguistic phenomena, thereby increasing its scientific impact.
3.  **Inconsistencies in Experimental Reporting.** The presentation of results across different tables is inconsistent, which complicates direct comparisons. For instance, Table 6, which reports on the manually processed Cinderella dataset, omits results for the standard complex network (CN) and several multi-view combinations present in Table 5. This makes it difficult to fairly assess the authors' claim that linguistic features are more sensitive to noisy text than network features. Furthermore, on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the best-performing individual feature set is BoW, not the proposed CNE, which slightly undermines the central claims about the superiority of the network-based approach in isolation.
4.  **Insufficient Detail on Word Embedding Models.** The word embeddings are a crucial component of the network enrichment process, yet their training is described with minimal detail. Information regarding the corpora used for training the English and Portuguese models (e.g., source, domain, size) is absent. The quality and domain-relevance of the embeddings can significantly impact the performance of the proposed method, and this critical dependency is not explored or discussed.

### Questions to Authors
1.  To strengthen the claims of performance improvement, could you please conduct statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar's test, bootstrap testing) for your key comparisons, such as CNE vs. LM/BoW and the multi-view models vs. the best individual models?
2.  Could you provide a feature importance analysis to identify which topological metrics are most predictive of MCI? Understanding which network properties are most discriminative would greatly enhance the interpretability of your model and its potential clinical relevance.
3.  Please provide more details on the word embedding training process. What specific corpora were used to train the English and Portuguese embeddings? Have you considered how the choice of a general-domain vs. a domain-specific (e.g., clinical or spoken language) corpus might affect your results?
4.  The comparison between the automatically processed (Table 5) and manually revised (Table 6) Cinderella datasets is key to your argument about robustness. Could you present these results in a more unified format that allows for a direct, side-by-side comparison of all feature sets (CN, CNE, LM, BoW) on both data versions?
