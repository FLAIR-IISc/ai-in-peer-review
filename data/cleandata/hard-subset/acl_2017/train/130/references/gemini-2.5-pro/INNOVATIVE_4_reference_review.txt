**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and then "enrich" this graph by adding new edges between non-adjacent words based on the semantic similarity of their word embeddings. The authors argue that this enrichment step is crucial for overcoming data sparsity issues inherent in the short, often disfluent texts produced in clinical assessments. Topological features are extracted from these enriched networks and used for classification.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of semantically-enriched complex network analysis to the clinical task of MCI detection. This represents a bold and creative departure from standard approaches that rely on heavily engineered linguistic features, offering a method that is potentially more robust to the noisy and agrammatical nature of patient speech.
2.  The paper introduces a conceptually interesting method for fusing structural (syntactic adjacency) and semantic (embedding similarity) information into a single graph representation. This technique for densifying sparse graphs from short texts is a valuable contribution that could have implications for other short-text classification tasks beyond the clinical domain.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's central idea is highly novel and thought-provoking. Instead of extracting a laundry list of linguistic features, it abstracts the text into a topological space where patterns of impairment might manifest differently. This shift in perspective is precisely the kind of innovative thinking that can push the field forward. The fusion of co-occurrence structure with distributed semantics via embeddings is an elegant way to build a richer representation of discourse.
2.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The authors provide a compelling rationale for their work. They correctly identify a major bottleneck in the current paradigm for this task: the fragility of standard NLP tools (e.g., parsers) and the need for extensive manual cleaning when dealing with the disfluent, fragmented language common in MCI patients. Their approach is explicitly designed to bypass these issues, aiming for a more robust, end-to-end automated system. The experiment on the manually revised Cinderella dataset, which shows linguistic features improving while the network features do not, provides strong empirical support for this motivation.
3.  **Effective Handling of Short Texts:** The enrichment of sparse co-occurrence networks is a clever solution to a fundamental and practical problem. Short clinical transcripts often result in trivial, near-linear graphs from which few meaningful topological features can be extracted. By adding semantic "shortcuts," the authors create a much more informative structure. This is a significant methodological advance for applying graph-based methods to short documents.
4.  **Thorough Evaluation Across Diverse Datasets:** The authors validate their method on three distinct datasets, varying in language (English, Portuguese), elicitation task, and average text length. This demonstrates the versatility and generalizability of the approach. The consistent outperformance of the enriched network (CNE) over the basic co-occurrence network (CN) validates the core technical contribution, while its competitiveness with established linguistic and BoW features proves its viability.

**Weaknesses**
While I am very enthusiastic about the core ideas in this paper, there are areas where the methodology could be strengthened. I view these as minor flaws in the context of a highly novel proposal, rather than reasons for rejection.
1.  **Ambiguity in Word Embedding Training:** The paper is vague about the corpus used to train the word embeddings. It is a critical detail, as the quality and domain-specificity of the embeddings directly determine the quality of the network enrichment. Were they trained on large, general-purpose corpora (e.g., Wikipedia) or on the small, in-domain datasets? The former may miss clinical nuances, while the latter risks being undertrained. This lack of clarity hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to fully interpret the source of the model's performance.
2.  **Limited Analysis of the Enrichment Threshold:** The cosine similarity threshold is the central hyperparameter of the enrichment process, yet its impact is not analyzed. The paper reports optimal values found via grid search but provides no sensitivity analysis. It would be insightful to see how classification accuracy and graph density change as this threshold is varied. This would provide a better understanding of how much semantic information is optimal to inject into the network.
3.  **Performance Gains are Complementary, Not Substitutive:** The results, while strong, show that the best performance is often achieved by combining the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features. For example, in the ABCD dataset, BoW alone outperforms CNE alone, and the combination of all three is best. This suggests the topological features are capturing a complementary signal rather than offering a complete replacement for existing methods. While this is still a valuable contribution, the paper could be clearer about positioning the method as a powerful new component in an ensemble rather than a standalone substitute.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please clarify the training details for the word embeddings? Specifically, what corpora were used to train the English and Portuguese embeddings, and what was their size? Have you experimented with embeddings trained on different corpora (e.g., general vs. clinical) to see how it affects performance?
2.  The similarity threshold for adding semantic edges is a key component of your method. Could you provide a brief analysis of how sensitive your results are to this parameter? For example, how does accuracy change as the threshold varies from a low to a high value on one of the datasets?
