### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core idea is to address the sparseness of networks derived from short texts by "enriching" a standard word co-occurrence graph. This enrichment involves adding new edges between non-adjacent words if their word embeddings exhibit a cosine similarity above a certain threshold. The authors then extract a set of topological features from these enriched graphs and use them in a machine learning classifier to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets (two in Portuguese, one in English) and compared against standard linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A novel graph enrichment method for short-text representation:** The primary contribution is the technique of using word embedding similarity to add semantic links to a syntactic co-occurrence graph. This is a clever approach to mitigate the problem of information sparsity in graph-based representations of short, clinically-derived texts.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of the method's utility for MCI detection:** The paper shows that features derived from these enriched networks are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, strong baselines like linguistic features and BoW for the task of MCI classification.
3.  **Evaluation of robustness to noisy data:** The experiments suggest that the proposed network-based method is less dependent on extensive manual preprocessing and cleaning of transcripts compared to methods that rely on syntactic parsing, which is a significant practical advantage for real-world clinical applications.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Novelty and Practical Relevance:** The proposed method of enriching a co-occurrence network with semantic information from word embeddings is both novel and well-motivated. It directly tackles a known weakness of graph-based text models—their poor performance on short documents which result in sparse, almost linear graphs. This is particularly relevant for the clinical domain, where patient utterances can be brief. The approach provides a principled way to incorporate deeper semantic relationships into the graph structure beyond simple word adjacency.
2.  **Focus on Automation and Reduced Preprocessing:** A significant strength of this work is its potential to reduce the reliance on manual data cleaning. Traditional linguistic analyses for MCI detection often require painstaking manual correction of disfluencies, sentence segmentation, and other artifacts to enable parsers to function correctly. By operating on minimally processed text and not relying on parsing, the proposed method is more aligned with the goal of creating a fully automated diagnostic pipeline. The comparison between the automatically processed (Table 5) and manually revised (Table 6) Cinderella datasets, while not fully elaborated, hints at this strength.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct datasets, varying in language (English, Portuguese), length (short descriptions to longer narratives), and elicitation task (picture description, story retelling). The inclusion of multiple strong baselines (Linguistic Features, BoW), several classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, NB), and combination strategies (ensemble, multi-view learning) provides a robust assessment of the proposed features' performance and complementarity.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Ablation and Hyperparameter Analysis:** The core of the proposed method is the graph enrichment step, which hinges on a critical hyperparameter: the cosine similarity threshold. The paper simply states this was "optimized" with final values given, but provides no analysis of how this choice impacts the resulting graph structure or classification performance. A sensitivity analysis showing how accuracy changes with the threshold would be essential for understanding the model's behavior and robustness. Furthermore, details on the training of the word embeddings are sparse (e.g., training corpus size and domain), leaving questions about their influence on the final results.
2.  **Overstated Claims of Superiority:** The conclusion claims that the topological properties of the networks "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics." The results, however, present a more nuanced picture. On the Cookie Theft and Cinderella datasets, the enriched network (CNE) features are the best *single* feature set. However, on the ABCD dataset, BoW is the strongest single feature set by a notable margin (75.5% vs. 71.7% for CNE). In all cases, the highest accuracy is achieved by *combining* the CNE features with the other feature sets. This suggests that the network features are a valuable and complementary source of information, but not necessarily a superior replacement for traditional methods in all scenarios. The claims should be tempered to more accurately reflect these findings.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation of Results:** The paper could be improved in terms of clarity. For instance, the exact number of participants in the control groups for each dataset is not always explicitly stated in the text and must be inferred from tables. More importantly, the comparison between the results on the raw Cinderella dataset (Table 5) and the cleaned version (Table 6) is a powerful piece of evidence for the method's robustness, yet it is presented with minimal discussion. Table 6 is also curiously incomplete, omitting the multi-view combinations that are present in all other result tables. This makes a direct and complete comparison of how different feature types benefit from cleaning difficult and weakens one of the paper's key selling points.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the process for selecting the cosine similarity threshold? A graph showing classification performance as a function of this threshold for one of the datasets would significantly strengthen the paper by demonstrating the sensitivity of the method to this key hyperparameter.
2.  Your results show that BoW is the strongest individual feature set on the very short narratives of the ABCD dataset. Do you have a hypothesis for why the network-based features, even when enriched, are less effective than a simple word frequency model on this specific type of short-text data?
3.  The comparison between the raw (Table 5) and manually processed (Table 6) Cinderella datasets is very compelling. Could you please clarify why the multi-view learning results (e.g., CNE-LM, CNE-BoW) were not included in Table 6? Providing these results would allow for a more complete analysis of how each feature set, and their combinations, are affected by manual preprocessing.
