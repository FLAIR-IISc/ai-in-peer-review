**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes a novel method for representing speech transcripts for the task of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) detection. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence network and then "enrich" this network by adding new edges between words that are semantically similar, based on pre-trained word embeddings. The authors argue this enrichment is crucial for short and disfluent clinical transcripts, which would otherwise form sparse, uninformative linear graphs. They extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel method for representing short, noisy texts by enriching word co-occurrence networks with semantic similarity links derived from word embeddings. This is a creative approach to mitigate the data sparsity problem inherent in applying network analysis to short documents.
2.  An empirical demonstration that features derived from these enriched networks are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, strong baselines like traditional linguistic features (Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words for MCI detection.
3.  The evaluation is conducted across three different datasets, including two languages (English and Portuguese), showcasing the potential generalizability of the approach.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and a Bold Approach:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty. The idea of using word embeddings to densify a co-occurrence graph is a genuinely new and clever way to tackle the well-known problem of applying complex network analysis to short texts. Instead of relying on complex, error-prone syntactic parsing or handcrafted linguistic features, the authors propose a representation that fuses local word order (co-occurrence) with global semantics (embeddings). This is precisely the kind of bold, paradigm-shifting idea that the field needs. It opens up a new direction for text representation that could be applicable beyond the clinical domain.

2.  **Strong Problem Motivation:** The method is not just a technical exercise; it is directly motivated by a significant real-world problem. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of using traditional NLP pipelines on disfluent, transcribed clinical speech, particularly the expense and fragility of manual preprocessing. Their proposed network-based method is designed to be more robust to such noise, representing a valuable step towards fully automated diagnostic aids. The results on the non-manually-revised Cinderella dataset (Table 5 vs. Table 6) provide compelling evidence for this robustness.

3.  **Creative Solution to Sparsity:** The "enrichment" step is the key innovation. Simply creating a word adjacency graph for a short description results in little more than a linear chain, from which topological measures are mostly meaningless. By adding semantic shortcuts between related words (e.g., 'dishes' and 'sink', 'cookie' and 'jar'), the graph becomes a much richer representation of the text's conceptual space. This is an elegant way to incorporate semantic information into a structural model.

4.  **Thorough Baseline Comparison:** The authors validate their approach against two very appropriate and strong baselines: a large set of linguistic features from the established Coh-Metrix tool and the standard Bag-of-Words model. The experiments are comprehensive, testing multiple classifiers and exploring multi-view learning combinations. The fact that the enriched network features (CNE) perform on par with or better than these baselines in several conditions strongly supports the viability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Interpretability:** While the proposed method shows promising classification accuracy, it is a significant step back in terms of interpretability, which is critical for clinical applications. A clinician can understand why reduced lexical diversity or syntactic complexity might indicate cognitive decline. It is far less clear what a change in the average eigenvector centrality or assortativity degree of a patient's speech graph signifies clinically. The paper demonstrates *that* the method works, but offers no insight into *why* it works in terms of the underlying cognitive processes. This is a major limitation for the adoption of such a model in a real-world diagnostic setting.

2.  **Methodological Nuances are Under-explored:** The process of enriching the graph relies on a cosine similarity threshold, which is described as being "optimized" for each dataset. This feels somewhat arbitrary and raises questions about its stability and generalizability. How sensitive are the results to this threshold? A more principled method of integrating embedding information, perhaps by creating weighted edges based on similarity rather than using a hard threshold, might be more robust. While I appreciate the novelty of the core idea, the implementation presented is a first-pass, and its parameters are not fully justified.

3.  **Inconsistent Performance Gains:** The results, while positive, are not a slam dunk. In the ABCD dataset, the simple SVM-Linear on BoW features (75.47%) outperforms the same classifier on the proposed CNE features (71.73%). The main performance gains are consistently achieved only when CNE features are combined with other feature sets in a multi-view model. This suggests that the enriched network representation captures a useful, but perhaps not complete, signal and works best as a supplement to, rather than a replacement for, existing methods.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate on the optimization process for the cosine similarity threshold? Specifically, was this done on a separate validation set within the cross-validation folds? How much did the optimal threshold vary across datasets (0.7 for English, 0.4 for Portuguese is a large difference) and what might this imply?

2.  While a full clinical validation is beyond the scope of this paper, could you offer any hypotheses connecting specific topological changes in the speech graphs to known linguistic symptoms of MCI? For example, does a graph with a larger diameter or lower clustering coefficient correspond to less coherent or more tangential speech? Exploring the feature importance from your Random Forest models might provide some initial clues.

3.  The quality of the word embeddings is fundamental to your enrichment step. Could you please specify the corpora used to train the fastText models for both English and Portuguese? Did you experiment with or consider the impact of using embeddings trained on more domain-specific corpora (e.g., from medical or geriatric sources)?
