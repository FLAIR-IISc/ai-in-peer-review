### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core method involves creating word co-occurrence networks and then "enriching" them by adding new edges between words based on the semantic similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. This enrichment step is designed to make the network representation more informative, especially for the short and disfluent texts common in clinical assessments. The authors extract a set of topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train machine learning classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) and compared against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) baselines. The paper also explores combining these different feature sets using multi-view learning.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of a novel feature extraction method for MCI detection, where word co-occurrence networks are enriched with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short clinical transcripts.
2.  An empirical comparison of the proposed network-based features against standard linguistic and BoW features across three different datasets, demonstrating the competitiveness of the proposed method.
3.  A demonstration that combining the proposed network features with traditional linguistic and BoW features using a multi-view learning approach can lead to improved classification performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Method for Clinical Text Analysis:** The proposed method of enriching word co-occurrence networks with word embeddings is a novel approach for the specific task of MCI detection from speech transcripts. This technique directly addresses a well-known limitation of graph-based text models: their sparsity and lack of informative structure when applied to short texts (Amancio, 2015c). By adding edges based on semantic similarity, the method creates denser, more topologically complex graphs from which meaningful features can be extracted, representing a non-trivial adaptation of existing NLP techniques to a challenging clinical application.

2.  **Potential for Robustness to Unstructured Transcripts:** A significant advantage of the proposed network-based approach is its reduced reliance on grammatically well-formed text. Methods based on syntactic parsing or complex linguistic features often require significant manual pre-processing and cleaning of clinical transcripts to perform well, as noted by the authors in their discussion of prior work (Aluísio et al., 2016). The proposed method, based on word adjacency and semantic similarity, can operate on less structured input, making it more suitable for a fully automated pipeline that processes raw or ASR-generated transcripts containing disfluencies. The comparison between results on the raw (Table 5) and manually revised (Table 6) Cinderella datasets provides evidence supporting this claim.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The study is grounded in a thorough empirical evaluation across three distinct datasets, which vary in language (English, Portuguese), elicitation task (picture description, story retelling), and text length. This multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The authors compare their method against two widely used baselines (linguistic features via Coh-Metrix and BoW) and systematically test several classification algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF, GaussianNB) as well as feature combination strategies (multi-view learning) and classifier ensembles. This rigorous setup provides a clear context for interpreting the performance of the proposed features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The process of enriching the network depends on a critical hyperparameter: the cosine similarity threshold for adding new edges. The paper states that this threshold was "optimized with best values being 0.7 in Cookie Theft dataset and 0.4 in both Cinderella and ABCD dataset." However, the methodology for this optimization is not described. It is unclear if a validation set was used, if tuning was performed within the cross-validation folds to prevent data leakage, or how sensitive the model's performance is to this parameter. This omission hinders the reproducibility of the work and leaves open questions about the robustness of the chosen threshold values.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Feature Informativeness:** The paper extracts a large set of topological network metrics (10 base metrics, each summarized by mean, standard deviation, and skewness). However, the work provides no analysis of the relative importance of these features. It is therefore impossible to know which topological properties of the enriched networks are actually contributing to the classification. An ablation study or feature importance analysis would provide valuable insight into *why* the method works and which linguistic phenomena (e.g., connectivity patterns, centrality of certain words) are captured by the network metrics that distinguish MCI from control participants. Without this, the feature set appears arbitrary and the model remains a black box.

3.  **Inconsistent Performance and Overstated Claims:** The paper claims that the topological properties of enriched complex networks (CNE) "outperfom traditionally linguistic metrics, in individual classifiers results." This claim is not consistently supported by the reported results. For the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves an accuracy of 0.7547, which is substantially higher than the best result for the CNE features (0.7173 with the same classifier) and the linguistic features (0.7056). While CNE features perform well on the other two datasets, their failure to outperform a simpler baseline on the dataset with the shortest texts raises questions about their effectiveness in the very scenario they were designed for. The lack of statistical significance testing makes it difficult to ascertain whether the observed performance differences between methods are meaningful.

4.  **Potential Confounding Demographic Variables:** The demographic information provided for the datasets reveals potential confounding factors that are not addressed in the analysis. For the ABCD dataset (Table 3), the control group has a mean age of 61, while the MCI group has a mean age of 72. There are also notable differences in years of education and gender distribution between the groups. Given that age is a primary risk factor for cognitive decline, it is possible that the classifiers are learning to distinguish groups based on demographic proxies reflected in language rather than specific linguistic markers of MCI. The paper does not state whether these demographic variables were controlled for or included as features in the models, which is a significant threat to the validity of the conclusions drawn.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the procedure used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment? Specifically, was this hyperparameter tuned on a separate validation set or within each fold of the cross-validation procedure? A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this threshold would also be informative.
2.  On the ABCD dataset, the BoW baseline outperforms the proposed CNE feature set when using a linear SVM. Given that the network enrichment method was designed to improve performance on short texts, how do you explain its lower performance on this particular dataset compared to a simpler baseline?
3.  The control and MCI groups in the ABCD dataset exhibit significant differences in mean age, education, and gender balance. Were these demographic variables controlled for in the classification experiments? If not, how can you ensure that the models are learning features indicative of MCI rather than linguistic patterns correlated with these demographic differences?
