### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network analysis. The core contribution is a novel text representation technique where each transcript is first modeled as a word co-occurrence graph. To address the problem of data sparsity in short clinical texts, which results in topologically simple graphs, the authors "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between non-adjacent words if their pre-trained word embeddings have a cosine similarity above a specified threshold. A set of standard graph-theoretic metrics is extracted from these enriched networks and used as features for machine learning classifiers. The method is evaluated on three datasets—one in English (Cookie Theft descriptions) and two in Portuguese (Cinderella and ABCD narratives)—of varying lengths and transcription quality. The performance of these network features is compared with traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations, and they are also combined in a multi-view learning framework.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Text Representation for Clinical NLP:** The paper introduces a method that intelligently combines syntactic information (word adjacency) with semantic information (word embedding similarity) within a single graph structure. This enrichment strategy is a well-motivated and effective approach to creating more informative representations from short, noisy clinical texts, which are often challenging for traditional NLP pipelines.
2.  **A Robust Empirical Evaluation across Multiple Datasets:** The authors provide a valuable empirical study across three datasets covering two languages, different narrative tasks, and varying levels of preprocessing. This demonstrates the method's versatility and, crucially, provides evidence (Table 6) that its advantages are most pronounced on raw, disfluent transcripts where traditional linguistic analysis tools may be less reliable.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Solution:** The paper addresses the important clinical task of early MCI detection and identifies a key technical challenge: the difficulty of applying standard NLP methods to disfluent, non-canonical speech transcripts. The proposed solution—enriching sparse co-occurrence graphs with semantic information from word embeddings—is an elegant and direct response to this problem. It offers a way to capture rich linguistic patterns without depending on fragile tools like syntactic parsers or requiring extensive manual data cleaning.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The evaluation is comprehensive. By using three different datasets, the authors show the generalizability of their approach across languages (English, Portuguese) and task types (picture description, story retelling). The comparison between automatically and manually processed versions of the Cinderella dataset is a particularly strong piece of evidence supporting the authors' core claim about the robustness of their method to noise.
3.  **Systematic Comparison and Combination of Features:** The paper does not analyze the proposed network features in isolation. It provides a rigorous comparison against two strong and widely-used feature sets: complex linguistic metrics from Coh-Metrix and a standard BoW model. The subsequent use of multi-view learning to combine these feature sets is a significant strength. It demonstrates that the different feature types capture complementary signals and that their combination leads to the best overall performance, achieving a strong accuracy of 80.18% on the ABCD dataset.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Claims Regarding Performance:** The conclusion claims that the network features "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics." However, the results tables do not consistently support this. For instance, on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), the best single-feature result comes from BoW (75.47%), which is considerably better than the best enriched network (CNE) result (71.73%). On the cleaned Cinderella data (Table 6), linguistic metrics (LM) outperform CNE (72.50% vs. 67.50%). A more nuanced interpretation is that CNE is a competitive and complementary feature set, rather than a universally superior one. The paper's impact would be strengthened by a more balanced discussion of these results.
2.  **Lack of Interpretive Analysis:** The paper successfully uses graph metrics as features for a "black box" classifier but misses an opportunity to provide deeper insight. It extracts dozens of features (10 metrics × 3 statistics) without analyzing which ones are most discriminative. An analysis of the specific topological differences between the graphs of MCI patients and controls (e.g., are their graphs more or less clustered? Do they have a higher diameter?) would provide valuable scientific insight into the nature of linguistic decay in MCI. This would elevate the work from a feature engineering paper to one that contributes to our understanding of the condition itself.
3.  **Missing Methodological Details Hinder Reproducibility:** Several crucial experimental details are ambiguous or missing, which makes the work difficult to reproduce and assess.
    *   **Dataset statistics:** The size of the control group for the Cookie Theft dataset is unclear from the text and Table 1.
    *   **Word embeddings:** The paper states that embeddings were trained using the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016), but does not specify the training corpus. The performance of embedding-based methods is highly dependent on the domain, size, and quality of the training data.
    *   **Hyperparameter tuning:** The similarity threshold for adding edges is a critical hyperparameter that was "optimized." It is not explained *how* this was done. If tuning was performed on the test data within the cross-validation folds, this would represent data leakage and could lead to inflated performance estimates. A clear description of the validation process is needed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the process used to tune the cosine similarity threshold for graph enrichment? Was a separate, held-out development set used, or was this parameter tuned within the cross-validation loop?
2.  What corpora were used to train the fastText word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were these general-purpose corpora (e.g., Wikipedia) or were they adapted to the clinical or narrative domain?
3.  The results on the ABCD dataset show that BoW is a surprisingly strong baseline. Could you offer some hypotheses as to why this simple model works so well for this specific task (short narrative recall) and how your CNE features capture complementary information that leads to the significant performance boost in the multi-view setting?
4.  Have you performed any feature importance analysis to identify which of the network topological metrics are most predictive of MCI? Such an analysis could provide valuable clinical insights into the linguistic markers captured by your graph representation.
