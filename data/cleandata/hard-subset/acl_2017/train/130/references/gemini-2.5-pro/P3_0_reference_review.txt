### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing them as complex networks. The core contribution is a technique to "enrich" word co-occurrence networks, which are often sparse for short clinical texts, by adding new edges between words based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The authors extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them to train classifiers. They conduct a comprehensive evaluation across three datasets (one English, two Portuguese) of varying lengths and elicitation tasks. The proposed enriched network features (CNE) are compared against standard linguistic metrics (LM) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) features, and combinations are explored using multi-view learning. The results show that the CNE approach is competitive with, and often superior to, traditional methods, particularly as it requires less manual text preprocessing.

### **Contributions**

1.  **Primary Contribution:** The novel application and formulation of semantically-enriched complex networks for the clinical task of MCI detection. The method of adding edges based on word embedding similarity is a clever and well-motivated approach to overcome the known problem of applying network analysis to short, sparse texts, which are characteristic of this domain.
2.  **Secondary Contribution:** A thorough empirical comparison of the proposed network-based features against strong baselines (linguistic features from Coh-Metrix, BoW) across multiple datasets and two languages. This demonstrates the viability of the proposed method and its complementarity to existing approaches.
3.  **Tertiary Contribution:** The paper makes a strong practical argument for a methodology that is more robust to the disfluencies and grammatical errors common in spontaneous speech transcripts. By avoiding reliance on fragile syntactic parsers, the proposed system is better suited for fully automated, real-world clinical deployment.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Methodology:** The central idea of enriching a syntactic co-occurrence graph with semantic information from word embeddings is both novel for this application and well-justified. It directly tackles a key weakness of graph-based text analysis—data sparsity in short documents—and provides an elegant way to incorporate modern distributional semantics into a graph-theoretic framework. The illustrations in Figures 1 and 2 effectively convey the intuition behind this enrichment.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The evaluation is a significant strength. By using three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD), the authors test their method on different languages, tasks (picture description, story narrative, story recall), and text lengths. The inclusion of both automatically and manually processed versions of the Cinderella dataset provides compelling evidence for their claim that linguistic feature extractors are brittle while their network approach is more robust.
3.  **Strong Practical Implications:** The work is grounded in the real-world need for scalable, low-cost MCI screening tools. The authors' focus on creating a method that minimizes the need for manual data cleaning or annotation is a crucial step towards this goal. This focus on automation and robustness makes the work particularly relevant and impactful for the clinical NLP community.
4.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and experiments are explained clearly, making the work accessible and its contributions understandable.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Detail on Word Embedding Training:** A critical methodological detail is underspecified. The authors state they trained a fastText model with default parameters but do not specify the training corpus. The performance of any embedding-based method is highly dependent on this corpus (e.g., general domain like Wikipedia vs. in-domain clinical data). If in-domain data was used, it is crucial to clarify how data leakage was avoided during cross-validation (i.e., ensuring embeddings were trained only on the training folds for each split). This lack of detail makes the results difficult to reproduce and interpret fully.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Results and Baselines:** The analysis of the results could be deeper. For example, on the very short ABCD narratives, the simple BoW baseline achieves a very high accuracy of 75.5%, nearly matching the final multi-view system's 80.2%. This surprising strength of BoW warrants discussion. What specific lexical choices distinguish the groups in this task? Does the CNE model capture different phenomena, or is there significant overlap? An error analysis or feature importance study would provide valuable insights into *why* the models work and what linguistic markers are most salient in each task.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The datasets are relatively small, which is common in clinical research but makes the results prone to variability. The authors report mean accuracies from 5-fold cross-validation but do not include standard deviations or conduct any statistical tests to determine if the performance differences between their proposed CNE model and the baselines are significant. Without this, it is difficult to confidently conclude that the observed improvements are meaningful rather than due to chance.
4.  **Potential Overfitting of Hyperparameters:** The cosine similarity threshold is a key hyperparameter that was "optimized" for each dataset. The paper does not specify the optimization procedure. If this was tuned on the entire dataset before cross-validation, or tuned within the CV loop on the test folds, it could lead to an overestimation of performance. A more rigorous approach would involve a nested CV or a separate development set. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this threshold, which would speak to the robustness of the enrichment method.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the training data and procedure for the fastText embeddings? Was a general-domain corpus used for each language? Most importantly, can you detail the steps taken to ensure that no information from the test sets (within the cross-validation folds) was used to train the word embeddings?
2.  The BoW baseline performed remarkably well on the ABCD dataset. Do you have any hypotheses as to why this simple lexical model is so effective for that specific short-narrative recall task? Does this suggest that lexical choice is a more powerful marker than structural complexity for this particular task?
3.  Could you provide more detail on how the cosine similarity threshold was optimized for each dataset? Was a separate, held-out validation set used, or was it optimized within the cross-validation procedure?
