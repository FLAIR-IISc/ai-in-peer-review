### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing the transcripts as complex networks. The core idea is to address the challenge of short and noisy (disfluent) texts, which yield sparse and uninformative standard word co-occurrence networks. The authors' primary contribution is a novel method to "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between non-adjacent words based on their semantic similarity, calculated using word embeddings. The resulting enriched networks are then characterized by a set of topological metrics, which are used as features for a machine learning classifier.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Extraction Method:** The proposal to enrich word co-occurrence networks with edges based on word embedding similarity is a clever approach to mitigate the data sparsity problem inherent in analyzing short clinical transcripts. This fusion of a structural (co-occurrence) and semantic (embedding) representation is a valuable contribution to feature engineering for this task.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their method across three distinct datasets, covering two languages (English and Portuguese) and different text lengths (short descriptions, longer narratives, and very short recall tasks). They compare their method (CNE) against several baselines, including standard complex networks (CN), traditional linguistic features (LM), and bag-of-words (BoW), demonstrating its competitiveness.
3.  **Analysis of Feature Combination:** The paper systematically explores the combination of different feature sets using multi-view learning. The finding that combining their network-based features with linguistic and BoW features consistently improves performance, achieving up to 80% accuracy, is a significant practical result.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Solution:** The paper clearly identifies a real-world problem: standard NLP tools and graph-based methods struggle with the short, disfluent, and sometimes agrammatical nature of speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments. Their proposed solution of enriching sparse co-occurrence networks with semantic information from word embeddings directly and intelligently addresses this problem. The comparison between Table 5 (raw Cinderella data) and Table 6 (cleaned Cinderella data), which shows linguistic features improving on cleaner text, effectively validates their initial motivation.
2.  **Strong Experimental Design:** The use of three different datasets with varying characteristics strengthens the generalizability of the findings. Testing on both English and Portuguese demonstrates the language-agnostic potential of the approach. Furthermore, the inclusion of multiple strong baselines (Linguistic Features via Coh-Metrix and Bag-of-Words) and a variety of classifiers (SVM, k-NN, RF, Naive Bayes) provides a robust context for evaluating the proposed method's performance.
3.  **Clear and Interpretable Method:** The proposed method is explained clearly, and the intuition behind it is easy to grasp. Figure 2 provides an excellent visualization of how the enrichment process transforms a simple linear graph into a more informative, densely connected network, making the contribution tangible.
4.  **Promising and Competitive Results:** The enriched network features (CNE) consistently outperform the non-enriched version (CN) and are shown to be competitive with, and in several cases superior to, traditional linguistic features and BoW models when used individually. The state-of-the-art results achieved through multi-view learning (e.g., 80.18% accuracy on the ABCD dataset) demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed features as a valuable component in a larger MCI detection system.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Detail on Crucial Hyperparameters and Resources:** The method's performance is highly dependent on two key elements that are not sufficiently detailed:
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The authors state they trained word embeddings using the model from Bojanowski et al. (2016) with default parameters. However, they critically omit what text corpus these embeddings were trained on. Were they trained on a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia), or on the in-domain task data? This decision has a massive impact on the quality of the semantic information and the reproducibility of the work. If trained on the task data, there's a risk of overfitting, especially with small datasets.
    *   **Similarity Threshold:** A cosine similarity threshold is used to decide whether to add an edge (0.7 for Cookie Theft, 0.4 for the others). There is no explanation of how these values were chosen. Were they optimized using a separate validation set, or tuned within the 5-fold cross-validation loop? If the latter, it constitutes data leakage and could inflate the reported results. The sensitivity of the network topology, and thus the final classification accuracy, to this threshold is likely high and warrants more discussion.
2.  **Absence of Modern Baselines:** While the baselines used (BoW, Coh-Metrix) are relevant, the paper lacks a comparison to more modern and powerful neural text classification models. Even a simple model like a BiLSTM with attention over pre-trained embeddings, or a fine-tuned BERT-based classifier, would serve as a much stronger baseline today. While the paper's references suggest it may have been written before the widespread adoption of Transformers, for a top-tier conference, a comparison against stronger contemporary methods is expected.
3.  **No Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper makes several claims about performance improvements (e.g., "a 2.3% of improvement"). However, no statistical tests are performed to determine if these differences are significant. On the Cookie Theft dataset, for example, the best CNE result (62.64%) is quite close to the best BoW result (60.14%). Without significance testing, it is difficult to confidently conclude that one method is truly superior to another.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training corpus used for the word embeddings? Was it a general-purpose corpus (like Wikipedia or Common Crawl) or was it the specific datasets used in this study?
2.  How were the cosine similarity thresholds (0.7 and 0.4) for enriching the networks determined? Was a development set used for tuning this hyperparameter, or was it selected via another method? Could you comment on the sensitivity of the model's performance to this threshold?
3.  Have you considered comparing your approach to more recent deep learning-based text classification models, such as those based on LSTMs or Transformers (e.g., BERT), which have become the state-of-the-art for many NLP tasks? How do you expect your graph-based method to compare, particularly in its robustness to the disfluencies present in the transcripts?
