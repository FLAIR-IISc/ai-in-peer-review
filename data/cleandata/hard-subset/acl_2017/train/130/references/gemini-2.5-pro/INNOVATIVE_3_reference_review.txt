### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) by representing speech transcripts as complex networks. The core idea is to first construct a standard word co-occurrence network and then "enrich" it by adding new edges between non-adjacent words that are semantically similar, as measured by the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. This enrichment step is designed to overcome the sparsity inherent in the short texts typical of neuropsychological assessments. A set of topological features (e.g., PageRank, clustering coefficient) are extracted from these enriched networks and used with machine learning classifiers. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (in English and Portuguese) and compared to standard linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) baselines.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel conceptual framework for feature extraction in clinical NLP:** The primary contribution is the application of word-embedding-enriched complex networks to the problem of MCI detection. This is a bold departure from the dominant paradigms of hand-crafted linguistic features or purely statistical models. The idea of using embeddings to densify a graph representation of a short text is particularly creative and addresses a genuine challenge in this domain.
2.  **Demonstration of a potentially more robust feature set:** The paper provides evidence that this network-based approach can work effectively on transcripts with minimal preprocessing, potentially obviating the need for the expensive manual correction and annotation often required for methods that rely on syntactic parsers.
3.  **A cross-lingual evaluation of the proposed method:** The authors validate their approach on datasets in both English and Portuguese, suggesting the method's potential for broader applicability beyond a single language.

### Strengths

1.  **High Degree of Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty. It proposes a fundamentally different way to represent and analyze clinical language data. Instead of counting n-grams or measuring syntactic complexity, it models the text as a topological object and analyzes its structure. The specific method of enriching the co-occurrence graph with semantic links from word embeddings is an elegant solution to the problem of network sparsity in short texts. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that can open up new avenues for investigation in computational health.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance of the Novel Features:** The results convincingly show that the proposed Complex Network Enriched (CNE) features are not just a theoretical curiosity; they are highly effective in practice. Across the datasets, CNE is often the best-performing individual feature set (Tables 4, 5). More importantly, the best overall results are consistently achieved when CNE features are combined with other feature types in a multi-view model. This strongly suggests that the network topology captures unique and discriminative information that is complementary to traditional linguistic and lexical features.
3.  **Potential for Practical Impact:** A significant challenge in applying NLP to clinical data is the noisy and disfluent nature of spoken language, which often breaks tools like syntactic parsers. The authors argue their method is more robust to such noise. The results on the Cinderella dataset before and after manual revision (Tables 5 vs. 6) provide compelling evidence for this. While linguistic features improve significantly on the cleaned text, the CNE features are already highly competitive on the noisier, more realistic version. This robustness could lower the barrier to deploying such diagnostic aids by reducing the need for costly manual data preparation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Interpretability and Analysis:** While the paper successfully demonstrates *that* the method works, it offers very little insight into *why* it works. The network features are treated as a black box. Which of the ten topological metrics are most discriminative for MCI? What are the characteristic structural differences between the speech networks of MCI patients and healthy controls? For instance, do MCI networks exhibit lower average path length, a different degree distribution, or less clustering? Adding even a basic feature importance analysis or a qualitative comparison of example graphs would have significantly strengthened the paper by connecting the topological features back to the underlying clinical phenomenon.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on Key Methodological Steps:** The process for generating and using the word embeddings is under-specified. The paper states default parameters were used, but it is unclear if the embeddings were pre-trained on a large external corpus (which is standard practice) or trained only on the small in-domain datasets (which would be problematic). This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding the source of the semantic information. Similarly, the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a crucial hyperparameter, but its optimization process is not described. Was it tuned on a separate development set or within the cross-validation loop? The latter could lead to information leakage and overestimated performance.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance Against a Simple Baseline:** On the ABCD dataset, a simple BoW model with a linear SVM outperforms the proposed CNE method as a standalone feature set (0.75 vs. 0.72). The authors acknowledge this is "somewhat surprising" but do not investigate it further. This result slightly weakens the paper's claims about the general superiority of the network approach. An analysis of why BoW excels on this particular task (e.g., are the narratives extremely short? Is there a very strong lexical signal?) would have been valuable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training protocol for the word embeddings? Specifically, were they pre-trained on a large, general-domain corpus (e.g., Wikipedia), or were they trained from scratch on the clinical data used in this study?
2.  The cosine similarity threshold seems to be a key hyperparameter. Could you please provide more detail on how this was selected for each dataset? Was a separate validation set used for tuning, or was it optimized as part of the cross-validation procedure?
3.  Your work presents a fascinating new feature set. Have you performed any exploratory analysis to understand which topological features are most discriminative? For example, could you provide a feature importance ranking for one of the datasets? This could offer valuable insights into how MCI manifests as a structural change in language networks.
