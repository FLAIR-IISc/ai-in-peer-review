### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks. The core idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and then "enrich" this graph by adding new edges between non-adjacent words that are semantically similar, as measured by the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The authors then extract a suite of topological features from these enriched networks (e.g., centrality, degree, clustering coefficient) and use them in a machine learning pipeline to classify subjects as either MCI or healthy controls. The approach is evaluated on three distinct datasets (one English, two Portuguese) of varying text lengths and is compared against standard Bag-of-Words (BoW) and linguistic feature (LM) baselines.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel application of enriched complex networks for a clinical NLP task.** The method of augmenting sparse co-occurrence graphs from short, noisy texts with semantic information from word embeddings is a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
2.  **An empirical demonstration that network-based features can be competitive with, and complementary to, traditional linguistic and BoW features for MCI detection.** The study shows that combining these different feature types via multi-view learning often yields the best performance.
3.  **An evaluation across multiple datasets and languages (English and Portuguese),** which suggests the potential generalizability of the proposed method and provides valuable results for a less-represented language in this domain.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Novelty:** The paper effectively argues for the need for methods that are robust to the disfluencies and noise inherent in spontaneous speech transcripts, which can be a significant challenge for traditional NLP pipelines that rely on clean, grammatical text. The proposed graph-based representation is a novel and promising alternative for this specific problem domain. The enrichment via word embeddings is a clever solution to the known problem of applying network analysis to short texts.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors evaluate their method on three datasets with different characteristics (language, elicitation task, length of transcript), which strengthens the credibility of their findings. The comparison against both linguistic features (from Coh-Metrix) and a standard BoW baseline is appropriate. Furthermore, the exploration of multiple classifiers and the use of multi-view learning to combine feature sets demonstrate a rigorous approach to model evaluation.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the proposed methodology is explained clearly. Figure 2, in particular, provides an excellent visual intuition for how the network enrichment process adds valuable structure to an otherwise sparse, linear graph derived from a short text.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Contextualization and Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper's primary weakness is its failure to properly contextualize its results. The authors state that their accuracies are "comparable" to prior work but do not provide any direct comparisons on the same datasets. The DementiaBank Cookie Theft dataset, in particular, has been used in numerous publications (e.g., Fraser et al., 2015, cited by the authors). Without a clear comparison to established results on this benchmark, it is difficult to assess the true significance of the 65% accuracy achieved. Are the LM and BoW baselines implemented here strong? Are the reported CNE results an improvement over prior work? The paper leaves these critical questions unanswered.
2.  **Crucial Methodological Details are Missing:** The process of generating and using the word embeddings is under-specified.
    *   **Training Corpus:** The paper does not state what text corpora were used to train the fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese. This is a critical detail, as the quality, size, and domain of the training data fundamentally determine the quality of the semantic relationships captured by the embeddings, and thus the entire network enrichment process.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The cosine similarity threshold is a key hyperparameter, but its selection process is not described. The authors state it was "optimized," but it is unclear if this was done on a separate validation set or within the cross-validation loop (which would risk data leakage). A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this threshold would have significantly strengthened the paper.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper presents classification accuracies but misses the opportunity to provide insight into *why* the method works. A feature importance analysis could reveal which topological metrics are most discriminative. For example, do MCI patients exhibit networks with significantly different average path lengths or clustering coefficients? This type of analysis could connect the network properties back to cognitive or linguistic theories of language breakdown in MCI (e.g., impaired semantic association leading to a less clustered graph), elevating the paper from a purely engineering result to one with potential clinical and scientific insight.
4.  **Inconsistent Performance Gains over Baselines:** The superiority of the proposed Complex Network Enriched (CNE) features is not consistently demonstrated. In Table 7 (ABCD dataset), the simple BoW baseline (75.5%) outperforms the best individual CNE model (71.7%). In Table 6 (manually cleaned Cinderella dataset), the linguistic features (LM) outperform CNE (72.5% vs. 67.5%). This latter result seems to challenge the paper's core motivation that network features are better suited for this task, especially on cleaner text. While the combination of features often performs best, the results suggest that CNE features are more complementary than strictly superior. This should be discussed more transparently.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please specify the corpora used to train the English and Portuguese word embeddings? How does the domain of the training corpus relate to the domain of the speech transcripts?
2.  Could you elaborate on the optimization process for the cosine similarity threshold? Specifically, was a separate validation set used to prevent information leakage during cross-validation?
3.  On the manually cleaned Cinderella dataset (Table 6), linguistic features outperform your proposed CNE features. How do you interpret this finding in light of your initial hypothesis that the network approach is more robust and better suited for this type of data than methods requiring parsing?
4.  Have you performed any feature analysis to identify which of the topological network metrics contribute most to the classification? Such an analysis could provide valuable insights into the structural differences between the language networks of MCI patients and healthy controls.
