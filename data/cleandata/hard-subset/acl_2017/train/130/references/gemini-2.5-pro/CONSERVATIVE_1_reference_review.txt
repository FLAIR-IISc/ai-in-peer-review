### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing the text as a complex network. The core idea is to model each transcript as a word co-occurrence graph and then "enrich" this graph by adding new edges between non-adjacent words based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The authors then extract a set of standard topological features from these enriched networks (e.g., PageRank, clustering coefficient) and use them in a machine learning classifier. The work compares this network-based approach against two established baselines: a set of linguistic features (LM) extracted using Coh-Metrix and a standard Bag-of-Words (BoW) representation. Experiments are conducted on three small datasets, one in English and two in Portuguese.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of a complex network model, augmented with semantic information from word embeddings, to the clinical NLP task of MCI detection.
2.  An empirical study comparing the performance of features derived from this network representation against traditional linguistic and BoW features on multiple datasets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clinically Relevant Problem:** The paper addresses the important and challenging task of automating the detection of MCI, a potential precursor to Alzheimer's disease. The motivation to develop methods that can handle the noisy and disfluent nature of clinical speech without relying on fragile, high-level NLP tools like syntactic parsers is sound and practical.
2.  **Systematic Experimental Design:** The authors perform a reasonably systematic comparison of three distinct feature paradigms (network, linguistic, BoW) using multiple standard classification algorithms. Furthermore, they explore combinations of these feature sets using multi-view learning, which is a sensible approach for integrating heterogeneous information sources.
3.  **Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The use of three datasets that vary in language, elicitation task, and average text length lends more credibility to the findings than an evaluation on a single corpus. This demonstrates that the authors have considered the generalizability of their approach to some extent.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Results:** The central claim of the paper is that the proposed complex network features are a competitive or superior alternative to established methods. However, the results do not consistently support this. On the ABCD dataset, the strongest single feature set is the BoW baseline, which achieves 75.5% accuracy with a linear SVM, outperforming the proposed enriched network (CNE) features at 71.7%. The highest reported accuracy (80.2%) is only achieved by combining the proposed CNE features with *both* of the baseline feature sets (LM and BoW). This suggests the network features may provide a complementary signal but are not a stronger standalone representation than simpler, well-established methods. Furthermore, overall accuracies are modest, and without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to determine if the small improvements from multi-view combinations are meaningful or simply due to chance on these small datasets.
2.  **Weak and Outdated Baselines:** While BoW and Coh-Metrix features are reasonable baselines, the paper fails to compare its method against the current state-of-the-art in text classification. Modern neural network models (e.g., BiLSTMs with attention, BERT-based fine-tuning) have become the standard for such tasks. These models operate directly on word sequences, do not require syntactic parsing, and typically offer much stronger performance than the baselines used here. The absence of a comparison to these established neural approaches makes it impossible to properly situate the performance of the proposed method within the context of contemporary NLP.
3.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Rigor:** Critical details about the experimental setup are missing, raising concerns about reproducibility and soundness.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The paper is vague about the corpus used to train the word embeddings. If they were trained on the very small in-domain datasets, their quality would be questionable. This is a crucial detail that significantly impacts the validity of the "enrichment" process.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The cosine similarity threshold for adding edges is a key hyperparameter. The paper simply states it was "optimized" with no mention of the procedure (e.g., using a held-out development set within each cross-validation fold). This lack of transparency makes it unclear if the tuning was performed correctly without leaking information from the test set. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with this threshold is also absent.
4.  **Insufficient Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper is primarily a report of experimental results with very little analysis or insight. It does not explore *why* the network features might be effective or what specific topological properties are most discriminative for MCI. For example, do metrics like average path length or clustering coefficient capture phenomena like semantic incoherence or circumlocution known to be associated with cognitive decline? The claim in the conclusion that "topological properties of CN outperfom traditionally linguistic metrics" is a generalization not fully supported by the presented results (e.g., Table 6, SVM-RBF shows LM outperforming CNE). The work would be much stronger if it moved beyond reporting accuracy scores to provide a linguistic or cognitive interpretation of the features.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the specific training corpus and its size used to generate the word embeddings? Were these pre-trained embeddings from a large, general-domain corpus, or were they trained specifically for this work?
2.  Please detail the procedure used to "optimize" the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment. Specifically, was this hyperparameter tuned on a dedicated validation set separate from the test set in each fold of your cross-validation to prevent data leakage?
3.  Given that a standard BoW model outperforms your proposed CNE model on its own in the ABCD dataset (Table 7), how do you justify the additional complexity of the network construction and feature extraction pipeline as a superior alternative to established statistical methods?
4.  Why did you not include comparisons to standard neural text classification models (e.g., LSTM, BERT), which are the current state-of-the-art for many classification tasks and also do not require the explicit parsing pipeline that you argue against?
