### **Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"**

**Summary and Contributions:**

This paper proposes a novel method for classifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing each transcript as a complex network. The core contribution is a technique to "enrich" standard word co-occurrence networks with additional edges based on semantic similarity, calculated using word embeddings. This enrichment is designed to overcome the problem of network sparsity in short texts, which are common in clinical assessments. The authors evaluate their network-based features on three datasets (two in Portuguese, one in English) of varying lengths and compare them against traditional linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) representations. A secondary contribution is showing that a multi-view learning approach, combining their proposed network features with other feature types, yields the best classification performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Method:** The paper effectively identifies a key challenge in applying graph-based NLP methods: short texts result in sparse, often linear, networks with limited informative topological properties. The proposed solution of enriching the network with semantic links from word embeddings is an elegant and intuitive way to add density and capture non-local semantic relationships. This directly addresses the stated problem and is a novel application in the clinical domain of MCI detection.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is conducted on three distinct datasets, which strengthens the paper's claims. The use of datasets in two different languages (English and Portuguese) and with varying text lengths (short descriptions in Cookie Theft, longer narratives in Cinderella, very short narratives in ABCD) demonstrates the method's potential for broader applicability.

3.  **Strong Baselines and Feature Comparisons:** The authors provide a robust comparison by evaluating their method against two strong and widely-used baselines: a comprehensive set of linguistic features (extracted via Coh-Metrix) and the standard Bag-of-Words model. This allows for a clear contextualization of the performance of the proposed complex network features (CNE). The experiment on the manually cleaned Cinderella dataset (Table 6) provides compelling evidence for their hypothesis that network-based methods are more robust to the disfluencies present in raw transcripts compared to traditional linguistic analysis tools.

4.  **Demonstration of Multi-view Learning Efficacy:** The paper successfully shows that combining different feature sets through multi-view learning consistently improves performance across all datasets. While combining features is a common practice, the authors' systematic application and the significant performance boosts (e.g., a 10% absolute improvement on the Cinderella dataset) highlight the complementary nature of the topological, linguistic, and lexical information for this task.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Inconsistent Performance of the Core Method:** The central claim is that the enriched complex network (CNE) representation is superior for this task. However, the results do not consistently support this. In Table 4 (Cookie Theft), the CNE's best accuracy (62.6%) is only marginally better than BoW (60.1%). More strikingly, in Table 7 (ABCD), the simple BoW baseline with a linear SVM achieves 75.5% accuracy, significantly outperforming the CNE's best result of 71.7%. The authors acknowledge this as "somewhat surprising" but offer no analysis as to why this might be the case. This result weakens the central argument that the complex network representation is inherently better than simpler, traditional methods for these types of short, noisy texts. The best results are always achieved by combining features, which suggests the CNE features are useful but not necessarily superior on their own.

2.  **Lack of Critical Experimental Details:** Key details regarding the training of the word embeddings and hyperparameter tuning are missing.
    *   **Embedding Training Corpus:** The paper states they trained a model based on Bojanowski et al. (2016), but does not specify the corpus used for training. Was it a large external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia for each language) or was it trained on the small in-domain datasets? The latter would likely result in poor quality embeddings, severely impacting the enrichment step. This is a critical detail for reproducibility and understanding the results.
    *   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The authors report optimizing the cosine similarity threshold but do not describe the procedure. Was this tuning performed on a separate validation set, or was it optimized within the 5-fold cross-validation loop? If the latter, it risks information leakage from the test folds and could lead to inflated performance metrics.

3.  **Absence of Feature Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper presents classification accuracies but provides no analysis of the network features themselves. Which of the ten topological metrics were most discriminative between MCI and control participants? For instance, did the networks from MCI patients consistently exhibit a lower clustering coefficient or a different degree assortativity? Providing such an analysis would offer valuable clinical and linguistic insights into *how* the network structure captures cognitive decline, moving the contribution from a purely engineering result to one with greater scientific impact. Without this, the method remains a black box.

4.  **No Statistical Significance Testing:** The performance differences between methods are often only a few percentage points. Without statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar's test or bootstrapped confidence intervals), it is difficult to determine if the reported improvements from the CNE method or the multi-view models are genuine or simply due to random variation in the cross-validation splits. This is especially important when comparing CNE against the strong BoW baseline in Table 7.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the strong performance of the BoW baseline on the ABCD dataset (Table 7), where it outperformed the proposed CNE method? Does this challenge the core hypothesis that network-based representations are more suitable for short, noisy transcripts, or does it suggest something specific about that particular dataset or task (e.g., simple vocabulary differences were highly predictive)?

2.  Please clarify the corpus used to train the word embeddings for both English and Portuguese. If an external corpus was used, which one? If it was trained on the task datasets, how did you mitigate the risk of poor embedding quality from such small corpora?

3.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter optimization process for the cosine similarity threshold? Specifically, was a separate, held-out validation set used to prevent data leakage during cross-validation?

4.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to validate that the observed performance gains of the CNE method over baselines, and of the multi-view models over the individual feature sets, are statistically significant?
