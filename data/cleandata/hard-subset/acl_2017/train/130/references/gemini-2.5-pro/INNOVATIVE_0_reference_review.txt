**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a novel method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The central idea is to represent each transcript as a word co-occurrence complex network. Recognizing that transcripts from neuropsychological assessments are often short, leading to sparse and uninformative networks, the authors "enrich" these networks by adding new edges between non-adjacent words. These new edges are created if the cosine similarity of the words' pre-trained embeddings exceeds a certain threshold. The topological features of these enriched networks are then used, alone or in combination with traditional linguistic and bag-of-words features, to train machine learning classifiers to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. The method is evaluated on three datasets (one in English, two in Portuguese) with varying characteristics.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel method of enriching sparse word co-occurrence networks with semantic edges derived from word embeddings. This is a creative and well-motivated technique to make graph-based text representation viable for short and noisy texts, a common challenge in clinical and other real-world domains.
2.  The paper demonstrates the successful application of this enriched network representation to the important clinical task of MCI detection. The results show that this approach is competitive with, and in some cases superior to, established methods based on complex linguistic feature extraction, particularly on unprocessed, disfluent text.

**Strengths**
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The core idea of using word embedding similarity to densify and add semantic structure to word co-occurrence graphs is highly innovative. It is a bold departure from the standard NLP pipeline for this task, which often relies on brittle syntactic parsers and extensive feature engineering. This work doesn't just tweak an existing method; it proposes a fundamentally new way to represent textual data for classification, which is precisely the kind of conceptual leap our community should encourage.
2.  **Elegantly Addresses a Key Methodological Problem:** The paper correctly identifies a major weakness of applying network science to short texts—the resulting graphs are often too sparse and linear to yield meaningful topological measures. The proposed enrichment strategy is a principled and elegant solution to this problem, injecting valuable semantic information that a simple co-occurrence model misses. The visual example in Figure 2 powerfully illustrates this transformation.
3.  **Potential for Robustness in Real-World Scenarios:** A significant advantage of this method is its independence from complex linguistic preprocessing like syntactic parsing. Parsers often fail or perform poorly on the disfluent, fragmented, and ungrammatical language characteristic of spontaneous speech transcripts. By sidestepping this dependency, the proposed method offers a more robust and practical pathway toward fully automated diagnostic tools. The authors' analysis comparing performance on raw versus manually-cleaned versions of the Cinderella dataset (Tables 5 and 6) provides compelling evidence for this claim.
4.  **Strong Empirical Grounding:** The authors validate their approach across three distinct datasets, covering two languages and different narrative tasks. This multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the claim that the method is not just tailored to one specific setup but has the potential for broader applicability.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Comparison to Modern Neural Baselines:** While the comparison against traditional linguistic features and BoW is informative, the evaluation feels incomplete without a comparison to current state-of-the-art text classification methods. Fine-tuning a transformer-based model (e.g., BERT) directly on the transcripts would serve as a powerful modern baseline. Such models are also known to be relatively robust to noise and would provide a clearer picture of where this novel graph-based approach stands in the current landscape. While the paper's contribution is primarily conceptual, a stronger baseline would better contextualize its performance.
2.  **Lack of Interpretability Analysis:** A missed opportunity lies in the analysis of the network features themselves. For a clinical application like MCI detection, understanding *why* the model works is nearly as important as its accuracy. The paper does not investigate which specific topological features (e.g., clustering coefficient, average path length, degree assortativity) are most discriminative. An analysis of this kind could yield fascinating insights into the structural changes in language production associated with MCI, significantly elevating the paper's clinical and scientific impact.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The enrichment process hinges on a cosine similarity threshold, which is a critical hyperparameter. The authors state this was "optimized" to specific values for different datasets, but the methodology for this optimization is not described. It is unclear if this was done on a held-out validation set or within the cross-validation loop, raising potential concerns about data leakage. A more detailed sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with the threshold would also have strengthened the paper's methodological rigor.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The choice of the similarity threshold is central to your method. Could you elaborate on the optimization process used to select the values of 0.7 and 0.4? Specifically, was this done using a separate validation set, or was it tuned within the main cross-validation procedure?
2.  Your method's strength lies in avoiding brittle parsing pipelines. Transformer-based models like BERT also bypass this dependency. Have you considered comparing your approach to a fine-tuned BERT-style model as a more modern and robust baseline for this classification task?
3.  Beyond the final classification accuracy, did you analyze which of the ten network metrics were the most influential in distinguishing the MCI group from controls? Such an analysis could provide valuable clinical insights into how cognitive impairment manifests as a change in the topological structure of discourse.
