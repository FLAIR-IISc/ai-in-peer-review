Summary
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and augments them by adding edges between semantically similar words (cosine similarity from word embeddings), then characterizes the resulting graphs with standard topological measures for MCI detection. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD recalls) and compared against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and BoW; simple multi-view ensembles are also reported.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- An empirical study showing that network features enriched with word-embedding similarities can be competitive with simple BoW and off-the-shelf linguistic feature sets for MCI detection on small transcript datasets, sometimes improving over pure co-occurrence networks and matching/approaching linguistic metrics.
- A multi-dataset, bilingual evaluation demonstrating that the approach can be applied without heavy manual preprocessing or dependency on robust parsers (relevant for noisy transcripts).

Strengths
- Clinically relevant task with practical constraints: The focus on methods that avoid heavy manual cleanup and robust parsing is well-motivated given noisy, disfluent transcripts typical in neuropsychological assessments, especially for Portuguese where tooling is limited.
- Bilingual, multi-dataset evaluation: The paper evaluates on three datasets and two languages, including a version with manual cleanup, which helps probe sensitivity to preprocessing.
- Conceptually reasonable augmentation: Adding embedding-based similarity edges is a sensible way to mitigate sparsity/linearity of short-text co-occurrence graphs and is grounded in prior work (Perozzi et al.).
- Some empirical gains: Enriched complex networks (CNE) generally outperform plain CN and are sometimes competitive with or better than the linguistic feature baselines. Simple multi-view combinations yield the best reported accuracies on two datasets (65% on Cookie Theft, 80.2% on ABCD).
- Clear description of network measures: The set of topological metrics and how they are aggregated is described sufficiently for reproduction in principle.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established baselines: Modeling texts as co-occurrence networks is well-established, and augmenting graphs with embedding-based edges closely follows prior art (Perozzi et al.). The methodological contribution is incremental; the paper mainly repurposes existing components for MCI detection without introducing new algorithms or theoretical insights.
- Inadequate baseline strength and evaluation rigor: BoW uses raw term frequency only; tf-idf and strong linear SVM logistic baselines are standard and often hard to beat. No comparison to modern pretrained representations (e.g., averaged word embeddings, sentence embeddings, or fine-tuned transformer baselines), which are established and accessible. There is no statistical significance testing, no reporting of variance across folds, and only accuracy is reported despite potential class imbalance; F1/AUC/confidence intervals are expected.
- Potential data leakage and CV protocol concerns: The ABCD dataset contains two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed). The paper does not state if cross-validation was performed with subject-disjoint folds; if not, near-duplicate narratives from the same speaker may appear in train/test, inflating accuracy. Similarly, the similarity threshold for adding edges appears “optimized” without a clear nested CV protocol, risking hyperparameter overfitting.
- Embedding training/data unspecified: It is unclear on what corpora the fastText embeddings were trained (only “default parameters” are given). Training embeddings on the tiny task data would be inadequate; training on large external corpora may introduce domain/lexicon mismatch. The paper also does not clarify whether distinct English/Portuguese models were used and how OOV handling impacted each dataset.
- Confounds and preprocessing choices: Removing stopwords and punctuation discards function-word and fluency signals known to be informative for cognitive impairment. Disfluency removal is inconsistently described (some are removed; others retained), and segmentation differs across datasets (manual vs DeepBond). Coh-Metrix vs Coh-Metrix-Dementia features differ across languages. These factors introduce confounds that could explain performance differences independently of the proposed method.
- Mixed and sometimes unfavorable results vs established features: On the manually revised Cinderella dataset, the LM baseline with SVM-RBF reaches 72.5%, higher than CNE (67.5%), and in ABCD the linear SVM on BoW achieves 75.5%, close to the best multi-view result (80.2%). The claim that CN “outperform” linguistic metrics is not consistently supported across datasets and settings.
- Reporting gaps and clarity: Key demographic and class balance details are missing for some datasets; it is unclear if demographic covariates (age, education) were controlled for or used as features. The rationale for different similarity thresholds per dataset is not justified beyond empirical tuning. No ablation isolating which topological measures matter is provided.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you ensure subject-disjoint folds, especially for ABCD where each subject has two narratives? If not, can you re-run with subject-level splits and report results?
- Hyperparameter tuning: How were similarity thresholds (0.7, 0.4) selected? Was nested CV used to avoid overfitting within the same folds?
- Embeddings: On which corpora (size, domain) were the fastText embeddings trained for English and Portuguese? Were they trained solely on your datasets, or external corpora? Please quantify OOV rates and the impact of subword modeling.
- Baselines: Can you add tf-idf linear SVM/logistic regression baselines and a simple pretrained embedding baseline (e.g., averaged word2vec/fastText or sentence-BERT), and report significance tests?
- Metrics and variance: Please report F1/AUC and per-fold variance or confidence intervals. What is the class balance per dataset? Any stratification?
- Confounds: Did you control for age/education or include them as features? If not, could performance be confounded by demographics?
- Preprocessing: Given the importance of function words and disfluencies in this domain, what is the effect of retaining stopwords/punctuation and not removing disfluencies? Can you provide an ablation?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessing scripts, and exact data splits to enable replication?

Overall, while the application is important and the idea is plausible, the contribution is incremental and the evaluation lacks the rigor and baseline breadth expected for acceptance. Strengthening the baselines, clarifying protocols, and addressing potential leakage/confounds are necessary.
