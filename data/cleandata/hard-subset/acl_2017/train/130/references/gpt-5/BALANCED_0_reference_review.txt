Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks and enriches those networks with semantic links derived from word embeddings. It extracts topological features from these enriched networks to classify mild cognitive impairment (MCI) vs. controls across three datasets in English and Brazilian Portuguese. The authors compare complex-network features (with and without embedding enrichment) against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW), and explore ensemble and multi-view combinations.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evaluation showing that complex-network features enriched with word embeddings are competitive and often superior to traditional linguistic features for MCI detection from short, noisy transcripts, across multiple datasets and languages.
2) A practical, language-agnostic pipeline that avoids heavy manual preprocessing and parsing, thereby better accommodating disfluency-laden clinical transcripts.
3) A study of multi-view combinations (CNE, LM, BoW) indicating that combining feature families can yield further gains.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation with minimal manual preprocessing. The approach is applied to English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD), with both short descriptions and longer narratives, and with both manually and automatically segmented variants. This increases external validity and suggests robustness to transcription idiosyncrasies.

- Clear added value of embedding-enriched networks for short texts. The motivation (sparsity and near-linear structures in short co-occurrence networks) is sound; empirically, CNE improves over plain CN in most cases (e.g., Cookie Theft: SVM-RBF 0.626 vs. 0.569; Cinderella: SVM-RBF 0.650 vs. 0.575; ABCD: SVM-Linear 0.717 vs. CN not reported in Table 7 but consistently lower elsewhere), indicating the semantic links help counteract small-text limitations.

- Competitive results versus established baselines and gains from multi-view learning. The best accuracies are achieved by combining feature families (e.g., Cinderella: Ensemble + CNE-LM 0.75; ABCD: SVM-Linear with CNE-LM-BoW 0.802). This supports the claim that these feature spaces are complementary.

- Practicality: avoids reliance on high-quality parsers and extensive manual cleaning. Given the difficulties of parsing disfluent clinical speech and the cost of manual normalization, a method that remains effective without heavy linguistic tooling is attractive.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject-level leakage and inadequate validation protocol. The ABCD dataset contains two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed). The paper applies 5-fold CV at the narrative level; if folds are not stratified by subject, the same speaker may appear in both train and test, inflating performance. The paper does not state that splits are subject-disjoint for any dataset. A subject-level CV (or leave-one-subject-out) is necessary for a fair estimate.

- Confounds and dataset reporting issues. In ABCD, the control and MCI groups differ markedly in age (61 vs. 72 on average) and education (16 vs. 13.3 years). Without controlling for these covariates, classifiers might pick up demographic signals rather than cognitive impairment. Demographic matching, inclusion as covariates, or partialing out effects is needed. Additionally, the Cookie Theft description is confusing: it mentions 43 MCI transcripts sampled from AD cohorts, but does not clearly report the number of controls or total transcripts used, raising reproducibility concerns.

- Hyperparameter tuning and risk of test contamination. The cosine threshold for adding embedding edges (0.7 for Cookie Theft; 0.4 for Cinderella/ABCD) is “optimized,” but the protocol (nested CV vs. tuning on the whole data) is not specified. Similarly, classifier hyperparameters and selection criteria for multi-view/ensemble combinations are unclear. Without nested CV or a held-out development set, reported accuracies may be optimistic.

- Baselines and evaluation metrics are limited. Using term-frequency BoW is a relatively weak baseline; tf-idf with a linear SVM or regularized logistic regression is standard and often stronger. No report of AUROC, F1, sensitivity/specificity, or confidence intervals, and no statistical significance testing of improvements, despite small datasets and modest margins (e.g., Cookie Theft gains of ~2–6 pp). This limits interpretability and the strength of claims.

- Reproducibility details are insufficient. The embeddings training corpus is not specified (on-domain transcripts vs. large external corpora), nor are language-specific resources for Portuguese vs. English. Important preprocessing choices differ across datasets (e.g., which disfluencies are removed), and the rationale for removing stopwords (which can carry structural information in CNs) is not discussed. Tables mix ensemble and multi-view settings in a way that is hard to follow (“–” entries and where voting is applied).

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- How were cross-validation folds constructed? Were they subject-disjoint for all datasets, especially ABCD (two narratives per subject) and Cinderella (one per subject)? If not, can you re-run with subject-level splits?

- What corpora were used to train the word embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were they trained solely on the task transcripts or on large external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, news)? Please provide training data sizes and any preprocessing.

- How was the similarity threshold tuned? Was nested CV used? What other hyperparameters were tuned for classifiers, and what were their ranges?

- Can you report per-class precision/recall/F1 and AUROC, along with variance across folds or confidence intervals, and significance tests vs. strong baselines?

- For Cookie Theft, what is the exact number of control transcripts and subjects used, and what are the demographics? For ABCD and Cinderella, can you demonstrate that age/education differences are not driving the results (e.g., matched subsamples, including demographics as covariates, or regression controls)?

- In multi-view and ensemble configurations, how are votes combined exactly? Are the base models trained on disjoint feature sets per view with identical training folds? Could you provide an ablation clarifying which combinations add value beyond linear SVM on tf-idf?

Additional suggestions for improvement
- Add a strong linear baseline (tf-idf + SVM/LogReg), and consider simple neural baselines (e.g., averaged embeddings) for completeness.

- Provide an ablation of the network enrichment (varying thresholds, number of added edges), and discuss the effect of keeping/removing stopwords.

- Include feature importance analyses (e.g., which CN statistics drive performance), and visualize how enrichment changes graph topology for representative cases.

- Ensure reproducibility by releasing code, exact preprocessing scripts, and trained embeddings or clear instructions to reproduce them.
