Summary
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word-embedding-based similarity edges to better handle short, noisy clinical texts. From the resulting graphs, standard topological measures are computed and used as features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. control. The approach is compared against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words across three datasets: English Cookie Theft, and two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella and ABCD). Multi-view combinations (majority vote) across feature spaces and ensemble classifiers are also explored. Reported accuracies range from roughly 55–65% on Cookie Theft, 50–75% on Cinderella (higher on the manually revised version), and up to 80% on ABCD when combining features.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that enriching word co-occurrence networks with embedding-based edges improves graph-based features for short clinical transcripts and can be competitive with BoW/linguistic metrics across English and Portuguese datasets.
2) A multi-view combination of complex-network, linguistic, and BoW feature spaces yields the best performance (notably on ABCD).
3) Practical evidence that such graph features avoid heavy reliance on parsing and are somewhat robust to disfluencies and low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation: The paper goes beyond a single corpus/language and evaluates on English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and two Portuguese datasets with different elicitation protocols. This breadth is valuable for clinical NLP, where datasets are often small and heterogeneous.
- Methodological simplicity and interpretability: The complex-network features are standard topological metrics, easy to compute and interpret, and do not require fragile syntactic parsing in noisy transcripts. This is well aligned with the goal of fully automated pipelines.
- Solid baseline coverage for transcripts: The paper compares against BoW and Coh-Metrix(-Dementia), two strong, widely used baselines in this domain. It also explores multiple classifiers and ensemble/multi-view strategies.
- Empirical finding that embedding-enriched networks help short texts: The enrichment addresses a known limitation of graph features on short documents (nearly linear graphs). Showing consistent gains of CNE over CN across datasets is a useful result.
- Practical relevance: Early, non-invasive indicators of MCI from spontaneous speech transcripts are clinically meaningful; the approach avoids expensive manual preprocessing that prior Portuguese work required.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and non-independent evaluation: The paper optimizes the similarity threshold (0.7/0.4) but does not specify whether this tuning is done within the training folds (nested CV). Similarly, SVM and KNN hyperparameters appear untuned or tuned outside CV. Most critically, the ABCD set contains two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed); using narrative-level 5-fold CV likely places the same speaker in train and test, inflating performance. Subject-wise CV is essential and must be reported.
- Limited metrics and statistical rigor: Accuracy alone is inadequate for imbalanced clinical datasets; report precision/recall/F1 per class and AUC, with confidence intervals or statistical significance tests across folds. Without this, many gains (often 2–5%) may not be meaningful.
- Ambiguous data descriptions and preprocessing: The Cookie Theft section is confusing (e.g., “43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”). Clarify class sizes, inclusion criteria, and whether controls are included and how many. The automatic removal of disfluencies is underspecified (rules, robustness across languages). For embeddings (fastText), the training corpus is not specified; training on these small datasets would be inappropriate, while external corpora should be detailed.
- Modest and inconsistent gains: On Cookie Theft, best accuracy is 65% (multi-view), which is near the lower bound of prior literature. On Cinderella (original), gains are limited (≤65%); on the revised Cinderella version, linguistic features or BoW often match/exceed CNE; on ABCD, BoW with linear SVM is already strong (75%), and the multi-view gain to 80% may be within variance. The contribution would be stronger with systematic ablations, sensitivity to the similarity threshold, and clearer demonstration of robustness.
- Missing controls and analyses: No subject-wise CV (as noted), no demographic control (age, education) despite known confounds, no feature importance analysis (which topological measures matter?), and no comparison to more modern representation baselines (e.g., tf-idf + linear model; simple sentence embeddings; or contextual encoders). Also, no error analysis or per-task breakdown (e.g., immediate vs delayed in ABCD).

Questions to authors
- How did you prevent subject leakage, particularly in ABCD where each subject produces two narratives? Did you use subject-wise folds? If not, please re-run with subject-wise CV and report results.
- How were thresholds and classifier hyperparameters selected? Was selection nested within the CV folds? If not, please provide nested CV results.
- On what corpora were the fastText embeddings trained (language, size, domain)? Were training transcripts included? If external, please specify sources and preprocessing.
- Please report per-class precision/recall/F1 and AUC, along with variance/confidence intervals, and conduct significance tests for key comparisons (CN vs CNE, CNE vs BoW/LM, and multi-view vs best single view).
- What is the exact class distribution in each dataset (subjects and narratives)? The Cookie Theft description seems inconsistent; please clarify counts per class.
- How sensitive are results to the similarity threshold and to the set of topological measures? Any ablations showing which metrics contribute most?
- For ABCD, are immediate and delayed recalls pooled in training/testing? Can you report separate results or control for task type?

Additional comments
- Consider a stronger BoW baseline with tf-idf and calibrated linear models, and simple modern sentence-level baselines (e.g., averaged word embeddings or sentence transformers) for fairness.
- Please release code and detailed preprocessing scripts to improve reproducibility.
- The motivation for removing filled pauses should be discussed; such disfluencies can be diagnostic, and removing them might discard useful signal.
