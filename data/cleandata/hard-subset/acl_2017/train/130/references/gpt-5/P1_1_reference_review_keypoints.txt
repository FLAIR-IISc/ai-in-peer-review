- Paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-based semantic links and uses topological features to classify MCI vs control across three datasets, comparing CN, CNE, LM, and BoW with multi-view ensembles.  
- Claimed contributions: empirical evidence that CNE is competitive/superior to CN and linguistic metrics across datasets, a simple language-agnostic low-resource pipeline, and that ensembling CNE with LM/BoW can improve accuracy.  

Strengths:
- Cross-lingual, low-resource applicability: avoids parsers/annotations and works on noisy English and Portuguese clinical transcripts, including automatically segmented data.  
- Better modeling of short texts: embedding enrichment addresses CN brittleness on short texts and yields consistent gains.  
- Broad comparisons and ablations: contrasts CNE, LM, BoW across multiple classifiers and ensemble/multi-view setups.  
- Clinical relevance and accessibility: simple off-the-shelf features useful for early MCI detection and potential deployment.  
- Evaluation under two preprocessing regimes: tests both raw and manually revised Cinderella transcripts to show robustness differences.

Weaknesses:
- Potential data leakage and confounds: 5-fold CV may not be subject-disjoint (ABCD has two narratives per subject) and demographics are imbalanced, risking inflated or confounded results.  
- Incomplete baselines and metrics: BoW uses term frequency only; missing tf-idf, regularized linear models, transformer features, and reporting lacks precision/recall/F1/AUC and statistical tests.  
- Insufficient reproducibility details: embedding training corpora, stopword/tokenization choices, handling of multiword tokens/case, and code/data availability are underspecified.  
- Hyperparameter tuning may leak test information: cosine similarity threshold selection procedure (nested CV vs full-dataset tuning) is unclear.  
- Heterogeneous preprocessing across datasets: inconsistent disfluency removal and manual revisions complicate cross-dataset comparisons.  
- Clarity and presentation issues: unclear Cookie Theft composition, figure/table typos, and formatting errors hinder comprehension.

Questions to authors (concise):
- Are CV folds subject-disjoint for all datasets (especially ABCD)?  
- Clarify Cookie Theft class counts and inclusion criteria (text says 43 MCI “sampled from AD” — ambiguous).  
- What corpora (size/domain) were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings; what were OOV rates and did subword help?  
- How was the cosine similarity threshold selected; was nested CV used and what grid/criterion?  
- Detailed preprocessing: stopword lists, tokenization, normalization, punctuation and filled-pause handling per language.  
- Are CN/CNE features computed at narrative or sentence level and how sensitive are results to sentence segmentation errors?  
- Can you add baselines: tf-idf + linear SVM/logistic regression, n-grams, and pooled sentence-transformer/ClinicalBERT/XLM-R embeddings?  
- Please report F1 per class, AUC, confidence intervals, and statistical tests across folds for key comparisons.

Additional suggestions:
- Consider calibrated stacking to weight views instead of simple majority voting.  
- Perform control analyses to regress out or match demographics (age/education/length) to rule out confounds.  
- Release code and detailed configs to improve reproducibility.

Overall conclusion:
- Enriching co-occurrence networks with embeddings is a reasonable, practical idea, but methodological gaps in splits, baselines, reproducibility, and reporting must be addressed to support the claimed findings.
