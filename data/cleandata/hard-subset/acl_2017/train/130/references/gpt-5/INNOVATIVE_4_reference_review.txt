Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks enriched with semantic links derived from word embeddings, then using topological network measures as features to detect mild cognitive impairment (MCI). The approach aims to mitigate the sparsity/linearity of networks induced from very short, noisy clinical transcripts. Experiments on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD with automatic sentence segmentation) compare plain co-occurrence networks (CN), enriched networks (CNE), Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features (LM), and Bag-of-Words (BoW), as well as multi-view majority-vote combinations.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Introduction of embedding-enriched word adjacency networks for clinical speech transcripts and demonstration that their topological descriptors can outperform standard CN and rival or surpass linguistic metrics across languages. To my knowledge, this graph-with-embedding modeling has not been applied to MCI detection from speech transcripts.
2) Evidence that the approach is robust under low-resource and noisy conditions (automatic segmentation, disfluencies, absence of parsing), with cross-lingual applicability (English and Portuguese), and that multi-view fusion with LM/BoW yields complementary gains.
3) An automation-oriented pipeline for MCI detection that avoids costly manual revision common in prior work, with competitive accuracies (up to 80% on ABCD) using simple, interpretable graph features.

Strengths
- Novel modeling tailored to short, disfluent transcripts: Enriching co-occurrence networks with semantic similarity edges is a neat, bold idea that directly addresses the “near-linear network” problem for short texts. The consistent gains of CNE over CN across datasets (e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF: 0.626 vs 0.569; Cinderella: 0.650 vs 0.575) support the effectiveness of the enrichment.
- Cross-lingual, low-resource robustness: The method works with minimal linguistic preprocessing, no dependency parsing, and with automatic sentence segmentation (ABCD), which is highly relevant in clinical settings where clean transcripts and robust parsers are scarce. Results show competitiveness with LM even when LM benefits from manual revision (Cinderella processed: CNE 0.675 ensemble vs LM 0.675; and CNE-BoW 0.750).
- Complementarity and simplicity: Multi-view majority voting yields further improvements (e.g., ABCD: CNE-LM-BoW 0.802 with linear SVM; Cookie Theft: best 0.650), indicating complementary signals among CNE/LM/BoW. The approach is simple, interpretable, and computationally light.
- Practical clinical relevance: Avoids manual clean-up and relies on features that can be extracted reliably from noisy transcripts—this increases translational potential compared to parser-dependent pipelines.
- Broad experimental scope: Three datasets, two languages, differing elicitation tasks and preprocessing conditions provide a useful breadth of evidence for generality.

Weaknesses
- Possible evaluation leakage and insufficient validation rigor: Cross-validation appears to be at narrative level, not subject level. In ABCD, subjects produce two retellings; without subject-wise splits, the same speaker may appear in train and test, inflating accuracy. The similarity threshold is “optimized” but no nested CV is reported, risking tuning leakage. No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals are provided.
- Missing strong baselines: Comparisons lack modern, powerful baselines such as sentence/utterance embedding classifiers (e.g., average word2vec/fastText, SIF/SimCSE), or fine-tuned transformer encoders (e.g., BERT/mBERT/PT-BR BERT) on sentence-level or document-level representations. BoW with TF only (no tf-idf) is weak; hence the relative advantage of CNE may be overstated.
- Dataset clarity and size: Cookie Theft set description is confusing (e.g., “We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”), and class distributions for all datasets are not clearly reported. The datasets are small, and no held-out test set is used; results only report accuracy under 5-fold CV.
- Limited metrics and analysis: Only accuracy is reported; for potentially imbalanced data, F1/AUC and per-class metrics are necessary. No ablation on the enrichment threshold, embedding dimensionality, or individual topological measures; little analysis of feature importance or error types. Robustness to stopword removal and to disfluency handling is not studied.
- Embedding training unspecified: It is unclear on which corpora fastText models were trained (only parameters are given). Training on the tiny task transcripts would be inadequate; training corpora, size, and domain mismatch matter for semantic enrichment quality.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you enforce subject-wise splits for all datasets (especially ABCD where each subject contributes two narratives)? If not, please re-run with subject-level CV and report results.
- Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold chosen (0.7 Cookie Theft, 0.4 others)? Was selection nested within CV folds? Please provide sensitivity curves or justify the values.
- Embedding details: What corpora were used to train fastText for English and Portuguese? Corpus sizes, domains, tokenization, and whether OOV subword modeling sufficed for disfluent tokens?
- Class balance and metrics: Please report positive/negative class counts per dataset and provide F1, precision/recall, AUC, and confidence intervals.
- Baselines: Did you try (i) tf-idf BoW, (ii) averaged word embeddings + SVM, (iii) sentence embeddings (e.g., SBERT/mSBERT) + linear classifier, or (iv) fine-tuned BERT variants? How do CNE and multi-view fare against these?
- Analysis: Which topological metrics contribute most? How sensitive are results to stopword removal, embedding dimension, and network density? Any qualitative examples where CNE helps over CN/LM?

Overall assessment
This is a creative, promising direction: using embedding-enriched complex networks to stabilize topology for short, noisy clinical transcripts is novel and compelling, and the cross-lingual, low-resource angle is valuable. Despite methodological gaps (potential leakage, weak baselines, limited analysis), the idea itself feels fresh and impactful for clinical NLP. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the evaluation protocol and adding stronger baselines/metrics; even if some numbers drop, the conceptual contribution warrants visibility and discussion at ACL.
