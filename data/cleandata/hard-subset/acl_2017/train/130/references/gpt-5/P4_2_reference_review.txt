Summary
The paper investigates detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing each transcript as a word co-occurrence (adjacency) network and enriching that network with semantic links derived from word embeddings. Topological network metrics (with aggregated statistics), linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia), and Bag-of-Words (BoW) are used as feature spaces. Multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes) and simple ensemble/multi-view combinations are evaluated under 5-fold cross-validation on three datasets: English Cookie Theft picture descriptions (DementiaBank), Portuguese Cinderella narratives (raw and manually revised versions), and Portuguese ABCD recall narratives. Enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges yields higher accuracies than plain co-occurrence networks and is competitive with linguistic and BoW baselines; multi-view combinations further improve performance, with a reported 80.18% accuracy on ABCD.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Methodological: augmenting co-occurrence text networks with embedding-based similarity edges to improve network topology informativeness on short/disfluent clinical transcripts, and using topological metrics as features for MCI detection.
2) Empirical: a cross-lingual evaluation (English and Portuguese) across three clinical narrative settings, showing that enriched complex-network features are competitive with or better than linguistic metrics, and that multi-view combinations can improve accuracy.
3) Practical pipeline: a feature extraction approach that minimizes reliance on fragile parsing and extensive manual preprocessing, making it better suited to noisy clinical transcripts.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation: Results are reported on three datasets spanning two languages and different elicitation protocols (picture description, story recall), enhancing external validity. For example, enriched networks (CNE) improved over plain CN on Cookie Theft (SVM-RBF 0.626 vs 0.569), Cinderella (0.650 vs 0.575), and ABCD (SVM-Linear 0.717 vs CN not shown for ABCD).
- Robustness to disfluency and limited NLP tools: The approach avoids dependency/constituency parsing and lemmatization, which are brittle on clinical transcripts and under-resourced languages. The comparison between raw and manually revised Cinderella data shows linguistic metrics improve markedly with cleaned text (LM SVM-RBF 0.475 to 0.725), whereas CNE remains usable without heavy manual revision.
- Simple, interpretable feature spaces with modest gains from multi-view: Combining CNE with LM and/or BoW via majority voting yields consistent improvements: Cookie Theft multi-view reaches 0.650 (vs best single 0.626), Cinderella 0.750 (vs 0.650), and ABCD 0.802 (vs 0.755 BoW SVM-Linear). This indicates complementary information across feature views.
- Consideration of OOV through subword embeddings: Using FastText-style embeddings helps mitigate OOV in noisy transcripts, a practical detail often overlooked in clinical NLP.
- Baselines cover common families: Comparisons include BoW and widely used Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) features, alongside multiple standard classifiers, giving readers a reasonable performance context.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete methodological details and potential evaluation leakage:
  - Embeddings: The training corpora (language, size, domain) are not specified. Training only on the small task data would be inadequate; using large external corpora would need to be stated.
  - Threshold tuning: The similarity threshold (0.7 for Cookie Theft; 0.4 for others) is “optimized” but the procedure is not described. Without nested CV, tuning on the whole dataset risks overfitting.
  - Cross-validation unit: In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives; it is unclear whether folds are subject-wise. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test, accuracy is inflated.
- Potential demographic confounds: ABCD shows large age differences between groups (controls avg 61, MCI avg 72); education also differs. No control for demographics (matching, covariates, or stratified CV) is reported. Classifiers may capture demographic correlates rather than cognitive status.
- Limited baselines and metrics: BoW uses term frequency only; tf-idf and n-grams are strong, cheap baselines not reported. Only accuracy is provided; no precision/recall, ROC-AUC, confidence intervals, or significance testing, limiting interpretability of gains that are often modest (e.g., 2–5% absolute).
- Ambiguities in data description and preprocessing:
  - Cookie Theft cohort composition is unclear (numbers for controls not reported; reference to AD subjects confuses the MCI subset).
  - The exact set and dimensionality of network features are not fully specified (e.g., which metrics are global vs per-node with aggregation, normalization for network size).
  - Claims of “fully automated” are overstated: transcripts are manual; only sentence segmentation is automated in some datasets.
- Generalization and sensitivity analysis: No ablations for the enrichment component (e.g., varying thresholds systematically, effect on graph density and metrics). The dependence of performance on transcript length (a known issue for CN) is not analyzed.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Embeddings:
  - What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese (source, size, domain), and were they pre-trained or trained from scratch? Were the same embeddings used across folds?
- Cross-validation protocol:
  - In ABCD, did you enforce subject-wise splits so that both narratives of a subject are in the same fold? For Cookie Theft and Cinderella, were there multiple samples per subject, and if so, how was leakage prevented?
- Threshold selection:
  - How was the similarity threshold tuned? Was tuning nested within the 5-fold CV for each training split? Please provide sensitivity to threshold values and the induced average edge density.
- Network features:
  - Please list the exact feature vector: which metrics are aggregated by mean/std/skewness and which are global; any normalization by network size/degree distribution?
- Demographics:
  - Given clear age/education differences (e.g., ABCD), did you control for these (e.g., matched subsets, including demographics as covariates, or stratified CV)? Can you report performance on matched cohorts?
- Baselines and metrics:
  - Why not include tf-idf and character/word n-gram baselines? Please report additional metrics (ROC-AUC, F1) and confidence intervals or significance tests across folds.
- Reproducibility:
  - Will you release code, feature extraction scripts, embedding models, and (where allowed) processed data splits?

Overall, the approach is reasonably clear and technically sound at a high level, with moderate novelty (embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks applied to MCI detection) and promising cross-lingual applicability. However, important methodological and evaluation details are missing, and potential leakage and confounds could inflate reported gains. Clarification and additional analyses would substantially strengthen the submission.
