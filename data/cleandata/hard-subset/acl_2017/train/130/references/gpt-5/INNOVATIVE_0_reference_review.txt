Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks enriched with semantic edges derived from word embeddings, then using topological network measures as features for classifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. The approach aims to be robust to disfluencies and minimal in preprocessing, making it suitable for noisy, short clinical transcripts and lower-resource languages. Experiments on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Brazilian Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD) compare plain CN vs. embedding-enriched CN (CNE) vs. linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix variants) vs. BoW, and multi-view/ensemble combinations.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Introduces embedding-enriched word-adjacency networks for MCI detection from speech transcripts and shows they can match or outperform standard linguistic metrics with minimal reliance on fragile NLP tools.
2) Multi-dataset, cross-lingual evaluation (English and Portuguese) demonstrating that CNE features are particularly effective for short/noisy narratives and combining views can further improve accuracy (up to ~80% on ABCD).
3) A practical, tool-light pipeline that avoids dependency parsing, suggesting better portability to low-resource clinical settings.

Strengths
- Novel representation for this task with clear motivation: Enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based semantic edges to overcome sparsity in short transcripts is a clever, low-cost idea. To my knowledge, complex networks (especially embedding-enriched) have not been applied to MCI detection, and the method aligns well with the domain’s constraints (disfluencies, short text, limited annotated resources).
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset validation: The method is tested on three datasets across two languages and tasks (picture description, story retelling, immediate/delayed recall), increasing external validity. The approach is not tied to language-specific syntax/semantics tools, which is an important practical advantage for clinical deployment.
- Competitive results with simple models: CNE often outperforms both plain CN and Coh-Metrix metrics and is competitive with BoW; on ABCD, a multi-view combination achieves 80.2% accuracy, which is in the range of prior work despite minimal preprocessing. Notably, SVM with linear kernel over BoW is strong on ABCD, and CNE holds its own, indicating the proposed features are genuinely informative.
- Minimal, automated preprocessing: Avoids lemmatization and dependency parsing; uses FastText for OOV handling. This practicality matters for noisy clinical transcripts and for scaling beyond curated corpora.
- Multi-view combination: While simple (majority vote), combining CN/CNE, LM, and BoW shows consistent gains on some datasets and supports the claim that the views are complementary.

Weaknesses
- Potential leakage and confounds: It is unclear whether cross-validation is subject-level stratified, especially in ABCD where subjects contribute multiple narratives (immediate/delayed recall). If the same speaker appears in train/test folds, reported accuracies may be inflated. Also, Table 3 shows demographic imbalances (e.g., age and gender differences) that may confound results; no control or analysis is reported.
- Incomplete experimental clarity and rigor: Only accuracy is reported, with no statistical significance tests, confidence intervals, or per-class metrics. There is no error analysis. The similarity threshold for enrichment is “optimized” but the protocol is unclear; embedding training data and folding protocol for embeddings are unspecified, raising overfitting/leakage concerns. Without these clarifications, it’s hard to assess robustness.
- Heterogeneous and inconsistent preprocessing: Some datasets remove certain disfluencies, others keep them; Cinderella has both raw and manually “cleaned” variants. This variability complicates comparisons and may mask the contribution of the proposed features vs. preprocessing choices. Moreover, removing filled pauses/false starts may discard clinically relevant signals for MCI.
- Limited method analysis/ablation: The paper does not analyze how enrichment affects network properties (e.g., edge density, degree distributions) or provide ablations on the threshold, edge count normalization, or which topological measures drive performance. No comparison to other simple enrichment strategies (e.g., k-NN in embedding space with fixed k) or to contextual embeddings (ELMo/BERT) is provided.
- Modest, sometimes inconsistent gains: While CNE is often best among single feature sets, BoW with linear SVM is very strong on ABCD (75%), and best overall results often require multi-view majority voting. The novelty is more on the representation than on groundbreaking performance. Given the persona I value novelty, I’m sympathetic, but stronger evidence of generalizable gains would help.

Questions to authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: Was CV stratified by subject so that all narratives from the same participant are confined to a single fold (especially in ABCD with immediate/delayed recalls)? If not, please re-run with subject-level splits.
2) Embeddings: On what corpus were FastText embeddings trained? Were embeddings trained only on training folds or using all data (risking leakage)? If external corpora were used, please specify and report vocabulary coverage.
3) Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold chosen per dataset? Was it tuned within training folds only? Did you consider normalizing for edge count or fixing k nearest neighbors per node to control density?
4) Demographics: Given age/education/gender imbalances (e.g., ABCD), did you control for or include these as features? Can you report performance matched for demographics or include a regression/propensity control?
5) Robustness to ASR: Since the goal is a fully automated pipeline, do you have results on ASR transcripts (not just simulated segmentation)? How sensitive is performance to WER?
6) Statistical testing: Can you provide CIs and significance tests between CNE and baselines, and per-class metrics (precision/recall/F1)?
7) Feature analysis: Which topological measures contribute most? Any stability analysis across folds/datasets? How does performance vary with the enrichment threshold and network sparsity?
8) Modern baselines: Did you compare to simple classifiers over sentence/utterance-level embeddings (e.g., averaging word2vec/FastText) or to pretrained contextual models? Even small-sample protocols (e.g., frozen embeddings with linear probes) would be informative.

Overall, I find the core idea novel for this problem and practically appealing. If the authors can rule out leakage, clarify training protocols, and add minimal statistical rigor and analysis, I would lean toward acceptance based on conceptual contribution and cross-lingual evidence despite some methodological incompleteness.
