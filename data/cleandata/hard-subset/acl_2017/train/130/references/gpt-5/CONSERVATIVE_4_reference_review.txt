Summary and contributions
The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word-embedding-based similarity edges. It extracts standard graph-theoretic measures (with mean/std/skew per transcript) and uses these features to classify MCI vs control across three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD retellings). Comparisons are made against Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features and BoW; classifiers include SVM, kNN, RF, NB, with simple ensemble and multi-view majority voting. Reported best accuracies are ~0.65 (Cookie Theft), ~0.75 (Cinderella), and ~0.80 (ABCD).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features are competitive (sometimes superior) to hand-crafted linguistic metrics and BoW on small MCI datasets across two languages.
2) A simple multi-view combination of feature spaces that yields modest gains over single-view classifiers.
3) A practical orientation toward minimizing reliance on language-specific parsing, with some automation in preprocessing.

Strengths
- Cross-language evaluation on three datasets. Testing on English and Brazilian Portuguese increases the potential applicability and reduces overfitting to a single language/toolchain.
- Parser-light approach. The feature extraction avoids dependency on fragile constituency/dependency parsing in disfluent speech, addressing a known pain point in clinical transcripts.
- Reasonable attempt to mitigate short-text network sparsity. Enriching co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based similarity edges directly targets the near-linear topology of short texts; the paper shows consistent gains of CNE over plain CN across datasets.
- Comparisons to multiple feature families. Including Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) and BoW offers some perspective on where the proposed features stand relative to established, simpler baselines.
- Transparent, standard classifiers and 5-fold CV. Using off-the-shelf models facilitates replication at a high level (though details are missing; see weaknesses).

Weaknesses
- Potential subject-level leakage in cross-validation. In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate/delayed). The paper does not state that folds were grouped by subject, so narratives from the same person may appear in both train and test, inflating accuracy. Similar concerns may apply to other datasets if multiple samples per speaker exist. Results are not reliable without subject-wise CV.
- Ambiguous dataset composition and preprocessing. The Cookie Theft description of groups is unclear (e.g., “we used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD…”), with control counts and selection not specified. Preprocessing varies across datasets (manual vs automatic segmentation; different disfluency removal), confounding comparisons and undermining claims of full automation.
- Weak and incomplete baselines. BoW uses raw term frequency only; no tf-idf with linear SVM/logistic regression (a strong, established baseline). No simple embedding baselines (e.g., averaged word vectors), and no modern pretrained language models. Given the modest reported gains and small datasets, stronger baselines are necessary to justify novelty and utility.
- Insufficient methodological detail and rigor. The embeddings (fastText-like) are “trained with default parameters,” but the training corpora are unspecified (size, domain, language-specific sources). Thresholds for adding edges (0.7/0.4) are “optimized,” but it is unclear whether this was done on held-out validation or via CV on the test folds (risking optimistic bias). No normalization details for features, no hyperparameter search for SVM/RBF, and no discussion of class imbalance handling.
- Limited evaluation reporting. Only accuracy is reported; no F1, AUC, or confidence intervals. No statistical significance testing or variance analyses, despite small sample sizes. No ablations isolating the effect of enrichment vs. simply increasing edge density, and no sensitivity analysis over the similarity threshold.
- Reproducibility concerns. The paper omits key implementation details (embedding corpora, stopword lists, vocabulary thresholds, exact graph construction rules, feature scaling, CV stratification, random seeds) and does not provide code or data splits.

Questions to authors
- Did you perform subject-wise cross-validation for datasets where a subject contributed multiple narratives (ABCD immediate/delayed; any others)? If not, can you re-run with subject-grouped folds and report results?
- What corpora were used to train the word embeddings in English and Portuguese (size, domain, token counts)? Were embeddings trained only on your datasets or on large external corpora?
- How were similarity thresholds chosen? Was threshold tuning nested inside CV, or selected using the full dataset? Please include a sensitivity analysis.
- What are the per-class metrics (precision/recall/F1) and confusion matrices? Are results statistically significant versus baselines?
- Can you include stronger baselines (tf-idf + linear SVM/logistic regression; averaged embeddings; a simple pretrained LM where feasible) and report comparisons?
- Were folds stratified by class and balanced by speaker demographics? Did you control for age/education differences?

Additional comments
- Clarify the Cookie Theft dataset composition (numbers of MCI and control participants/samples) and the selection criteria for controls.
- Standardize preprocessing across datasets or, at minimum, report how differences affect performance (e.g., manual vs automatic sentence segmentation, disfluency handling).
- Provide ablations on feature scaling and on individual graph measures, and analyze correlations/redundancies.
- Consider releasing code and exact data splits to enable verification.

Overall, while the idea of embedding-enriched text networks is interesting and the cross-language evaluation is appreciated, the current experimental protocol and baselines are not sufficiently rigorous to support the claims. Addressing subject leakage, baseline strength, and evaluation completeness is essential.
