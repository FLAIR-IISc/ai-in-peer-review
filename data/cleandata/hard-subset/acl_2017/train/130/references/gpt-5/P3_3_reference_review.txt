Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them with edges induced by word embedding cosine similarity, then characterizes each network via standard topological metrics. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed recall), comparing complex-network (CN) features, embedding-enriched CN (CNE), Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic metrics (LM), and Bag-of-Words (BoW), with several classifiers and simple multi-view voting.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks (CNE) provide competitive and often better performance than plain CN and (in two datasets) than Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) metrics for MCI vs control classification from transcripts, with minimal reliance on heavy linguistic preprocessing.
2) Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation: results across English and Portuguese datasets of varying length and segmentation conditions, including one with automatic sentence segmentation, suggesting some robustness to transcript noise.
3) Demonstration that simple multi-view ensembling of CNE with LM and/or BoW yields further gains on two datasets.

Strengths
- Relevance and practicality: Language-based screening for MCI is an important, clinically meaningful task. The proposed features are language-agnostic and avoid brittle parsing, which is attractive for noisy clinical transcripts and lower-resource languages like Brazilian Portuguese.

- Conceptual simplicity with empirical gains: Enriching co-occurrence networks with fastText-based similarity edges is simple and computationally light. Across datasets, CNE consistently outperforms plain CN, and in two datasets it matches or exceeds LM features. The use of fastText for OOV handling is appropriate in this setting.

- Multi-dataset, bilingual evaluation: Including three datasets spanning different task paradigms (picture description, narrative recall), languages, and transcript lengths increases external validity relative to many single-dataset studies. The inclusion of a setting with automatic sentence segmentation is also valuable.

- Comparisons across feature families and classifiers: The paper compares CN/CNE vs BoW vs LM across k-NN, SVM (linear/RBF), RF, GNB, and simple multi-view voting. This breadth helps situate the proposed features among common baselines.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Modeling texts as word adjacency networks is established, and enriching graphs using embedding similarities follows prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The methodological contribution is largely an application of known ideas to MCI detection, with no new graph construction, metrics, or learning methods. The novelty is thus incremental.

- Methodological rigor and potential leakage: The evaluation uses 5-fold CV on narratives, but in the ABCD dataset each subject contributes two narratives (immediate/delayed). Unless folds are grouped by subject, this risks train–test contamination and overoptimistic accuracy. The similarity threshold for adding edges is “optimized” (0.7 vs 0.4) without describing whether tuning was nested within CV; tuning on the full data would leak information. These issues must be clarified and, if present, corrected.

- Demographic confounds: The ABCD dataset shows large age and education mismatches between MCI (older, less educated) and controls. Without controlling for or including demographics, classifiers may pick up demographic effects rather than MCI-specific linguistic signatures. No matched analyses or covariate controls are reported.

- Incomplete evaluation metrics and statistical analysis: The paper reports only accuracy. Clinical screening requires sensitivity/specificity, ROC-AUC, and macro-F1, with confidence intervals and significance testing. Class imbalance is not addressed beyond counts, and no per-class performance is given. Gains of a few points may not be statistically meaningful.

- Baseline coverage and ablations: BoW uses tf only (tf-idf would be a stronger baseline) and no comparison to simple embedding baselines (e.g., averaged word embeddings with logistic regression), doc2vec, or coherence-based measures from modern sentence/paragraph embeddings. Given the hypothesis about semantic enrichment, a baseline that uses only embedding-derived text-level features is essential. The multi-view method is a simple majority vote; more principled stacking with calibrated probabilities would be informative.

- Length/density confounds in graph features: Many topological measures correlate with network size and density. Given substantial variation in narrative length across subjects and datasets, it is important to control for basic length and type-token confounds, normalize graph metrics, or at least show that CNE adds predictive value beyond simple length/lexical diversity. No such controls or partial correlation analyses are reported.

- Reproducibility details: The source corpora used to train the fastText embeddings (for English and Portuguese) are not specified. If embeddings were trained only on the small clinical transcripts, quality is doubtful; if trained on external corpora, their size/domain and tokenization are needed. Rules for disfluency removal are only broadly described. No code or data release is mentioned.

- Interpretation and analysis: The paper does not analyze which network metrics are most discriminative, nor provide feature importance or error analysis. Understanding what aspects of the enriched topology drive decisions would strengthen both scientific and clinical implications.

Questions to authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: In ABCD, did you ensure that narratives from the same subject never appear in both training and test folds (i.e., grouped CV)? If not, please re-run with subject-level grouping. Likewise, was the similarity threshold tuned within each training fold (nested CV), or on the full dataset?

2) Embeddings: What corpora and preprocessing were used to train fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese? What was the OOV rate on the transcripts? Have you tried off-the-shelf larger embeddings, and how sensitive are results to embedding choice?

3) Graph normalization: How do you control for network size/density effects given varying transcript lengths? Can you report performance after including simple length/lexical diversity features and/or normalizing graph metrics?

4) Metrics and significance: Please report sensitivity, specificity, ROC-AUC, and macro-F1 with confidence intervals, and test whether differences between CN, CNE, LM, and BoW are statistically significant.

5) Demographics: Given the age/education mismatch in ABCD, can you match subjects or include demographics as covariates/features, and report results? Similarly, for Cookie Theft and Cinderella, please report demographic comparability and any controls.

6) Baselines and ablations: Please add tf-idf BoW; averaged word embeddings + linear classifier; and a “coherence” baseline (e.g., mean adjacent-sentence cosine using sentence embeddings). Also compare multi-view majority vote to stacked logistic regression on calibrated probabilities.

Overall, this work addresses an important problem with a simple, language-agnostic method and promising cross-dataset evidence. However, concerns about novelty, evaluation rigor (possible leakage), demographic confounding, and incomplete metrics/ablations currently limit confidence in the conclusions. Addressing the methodological issues and strengthening baselines and analyses would substantially improve the submission.
