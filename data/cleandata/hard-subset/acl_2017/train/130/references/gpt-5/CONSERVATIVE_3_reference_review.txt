Summary and contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks augmented with additional edges derived from word-embedding cosine similarity, then uses standard graph-topology statistics as features for MCI detection. It compares this representation against Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features and BoW across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD), and explores multi-view and ensemble combinations.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) An empirical study showing that simple complex-network (CN) features enriched with word-embedding links can be competitive with established baselines (Coh-Metrix and BoW) for MCI detection from transcripts, across English and Portuguese datasets, and that multi-view combinations can further improve accuracy.
  2) A practical emphasis on avoiding heavy linguistic preprocessing (e.g., parsing) that is brittle on disfluent clinical speech, with cross-language experiments and an automatically segmented variant.
  3) A small method adaptation: adding similarity-based edges to co-occurrence graphs to mitigate sparsity in short texts.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual evaluation and multiple datasets: The paper evaluates on three datasets spanning English and Portuguese, including two narrative types and an automatically segmented variant. This is valuable given the scarcity of resources outside English and the brittleness of parsing on disfluent speech.
- Pragmatic modeling without deep parsing: The focus on representations that avoid dependency on high-quality parsers or hand annotation is well-motivated for clinical data (disfluencies, short texts). The empirical results suggest these lightweight features are competitive.
- Solid classical baselines and methods: The inclusion of Coh-Metrix(-D), BoW, and standard classifiers (SVM linear/RBF, KNN, RF, NB) is appropriate, and the linear SVM BoW baseline performs strongly (e.g., 75.5% on ABCD), providing a reasonable reference point.
- Multi-view combinations: The paper shows that combining views (CNE, LM, BoW) can yield improvements (e.g., 80.2% on ABCD), which is a sensible and established technique for heterogeneous features.

Weaknesses
- Potentially invalid evaluation due to leakage/subject split: In ABCD, each subject produces two narratives. The paper does not state that folds are grouped by subject. If folds split narratives from the same subject across train/test, results are inflated. Similarly, the embedding-edge threshold is “optimized” but no nested CV is reported; tuning on the same CV loop risks leakage. Clarify grouping by subject and use nested CV for threshold and model selection.
- Confounding demographics and missing controls: The ABCD demographics show a large age gap (controls mean 61 vs MCI 72), and different education and sex distributions. Without controlling for these (e.g., matching, covariate regression, or stratified evaluation), classifiers may exploit demographic proxies rather than cognitive-linguistic deficits. Length differences are also present and could drive accuracy. Include demographic-only and length-only baselines and stratified analyses.
- Metrics and statistical rigor: Only accuracy is reported. No per-class precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, confidence intervals, or statistical significance tests are provided, which is problematic with small N and 5-fold CV. The reported gains (often 2–5 points) may not be significant. Report variance/CI across multiple CV runs and significance against strong baselines.
- Limited novelty and weak methodological justification: Complex network modeling and embedding-enriched graphs (Perozzi et al., 2014) are known; the addition of edges by cosine threshold is incremental. There is little ablation (e.g., pure co-occurrence vs enriched; threshold sensitivity; weighted vs unweighted edges; contribution of each topological feature). Without such analysis, it is hard to attribute gains to the proposed enrichment rather than generic feature stacking.
- Incomplete and confusing experimental details: It is unclear what corpora were used to train embeddings (on tiny task data vs large external corpora), whether vectors are shared across languages, whether stopwords were removed for BoW (they are removed for CN, which may discard clinically informative function words), and how disfluency removal was implemented in Portuguese. Cookie Theft group composition is unclear (controls vs MCI counts; Table 1 missing). Figures are referenced but not shown. These omissions hinder reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Data splitting: For ABCD (two narratives per subject) and any dataset with multiple samples per subject, did you group folds by subject to prevent leakage? If not, please re-run with subject-wise CV. For Cookie Theft and Cinderella, confirm one sample per subject and any grouping.
- Hyperparameter tuning: How was the cosine similarity threshold selected? Was nested CV used within each training fold, or was the threshold chosen on the full dataset? Please provide a sensitivity analysis over thresholds and report variance.
- Embeddings: What training corpora were used for English and Portuguese embeddings? Corpus sizes, token counts, preprocessing, and whether pre-trained vectors were used. If trained on the task transcripts alone, how do you mitigate data sparsity and overfitting?
- Confounds: Given the age/education/sex mismatches (e.g., ABCD), can you report results with demographic matching or as covariates (e.g., logistic regression with features plus demographics), and provide a demographic-only baseline? Also include length-only baselines (e.g., words/sentences).
- Metrics and significance: Please report per-class precision/recall/F1 and ROC-AUC, along with confidence intervals over multiple random 5-fold splits. Are your multi-view gains statistically significant vs linear SVM on BoW?
- Baselines and preprocessing: Did BoW include stopwords and use tf-idf with a linear classifier (a very strong baseline), and did you try simple regularized logistic regression? For CN, what is the effect of including function words (which can be diagnostic) vs removing them?
- Ablations: Provide ablations isolating (i) co-occurrence CN vs enriched CN; (ii) weighted vs binary edges; (iii) individual topological features’ contributions; and (iv) using pre-trained embeddings vs random or bag-of-subwords only.
- Reproducibility: Can you release code, configuration, and exact train/test splits? For privately held datasets, can you at least release the feature matrices and splits?

Overall, while the goal is important and the cross-language perspective is welcome, the current evaluation lacks the rigor required to support the claimed improvements. Stronger controls against leakage/confounds, clearer experimental details, and thorough ablations are necessary before I could recommend acceptance.
