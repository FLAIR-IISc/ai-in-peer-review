Summary and contributions
This paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity links, and using topological features from these networks to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture description; Brazilian Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed recalls), compared against linguistic (Coh-Metrix) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) features, and combined via multi-view and ensemble learning.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical finding: Complex network features enriched with word embeddings (CNE) are competitive with, and sometimes outperform, traditional linguistic metrics and BoW on noisy, short neuropsychological transcripts across English and Portuguese (e.g., ABCD multi-view 0.80 accuracy; CNE often best among single feature sets).
2) Methodological instantiation: An application-oriented protocol for enriching word adjacency networks with embedding-based edges (cosine threshold) to mitigate the “linear network” issue in short texts, and extracting a standardized set of topological statistics for classification.
3) Multilingual evaluation and automation focus: Demonstration that this approach requires minimal language-specific NLP and can work with automatic sentence segmentation and limited preprocessing, making it attractive where mature parsing tools are lacking.

Strengths
- Addresses a real and clinically relevant problem with language-only signals. The focus on transcript-based detection of MCI is timely, and the approach aims to reduce dependence on costly/fragile annotations and high-precision parsing (important for disfluent neuropsychological speech).
- Language- and tool-agnostic modeling. The complex-network representation with embedding enrichment relies on tokenization and stopword removal only, and it transfers across English and Portuguese without language-specific engineering. This is a practical advantage over Coh-Metrix-like features.
- Empirical competitiveness on multiple datasets. The method yields competitive accuracies: Cookie Theft best 0.65; Cinderella 0.75 (multi-view/ensemble); Cinderella (manually revised) 0.75; ABCD 0.80 (multi-view). CNE often improves over plain CN and over LM on noisier transcripts, supporting the central claim that embedding enrichment helps short/disfluent inputs.
- Clear, reproducible feature definition for the CN side. The paper enumerates the ten network metrics and summarizes the statistics used (mean, std, skewness), making the feature extraction conceptually straightforward to replicate.
- Sensible comparison to reasonable baselines. The paper contrasts CNE with BoW and Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) and explores multiple classifiers (linear/RBF SVM, KNN, RF, NB), plus multi-view/ensemble combinations.

Weaknesses
- Risk of data leakage and over-optimistic estimates due to cross-validation design. The ABCD set includes two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed recalls). The paper does not state that folds are split by subject. If narratives from the same speaker appear in both train and test, accuracy can be inflated by idiosyncratic lexical content or style. Similarly, it is unclear for Cookie Theft and Cinderella whether multiple samples per subject exist and whether splits are subject-stratified. This is a critical threat to validity.
- Incomplete experimental rigor: no statistical significance, no confidence intervals, and limited metrics. Only accuracy is reported, with no per-fold variance, no statistical tests, and no class distribution or chance baseline. Given small sample sizes (e.g., 40 subjects for Cinderella), this makes it hard to assess robustness. Additional metrics (F1, AUROC) and repeated/nested CV would strengthen claims.
- Ambiguity in embedding training and threshold tuning. The paper says FastText-like embeddings were “trained with default parameters” but does not specify the training corpora, vocabulary size, or whether models were trained on the same small datasets (which would be problematic) vs. large external corpora. The cosine threshold is “optimized” (0.7 vs. 0.4) without describing a validation protocol; tuning on the full data risks leakage.
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. Word adjacency networks are established in NLP, and adding embedding-similarity edges follows Perozzi et al. (2014). The novelty is primarily the application to MCI detection and the empirical observation that it fares well on noisy transcripts. Methodologically, both the feature family and the multi-view/ensemble combination are incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Missing ablations and controls. The network enrichment likely correlates with lexical-semantic similarity and length. There are no controls for text length, no ablation on which topological measures matter, and no comparison to simpler embedding baselines (e.g., average word embeddings + linear SVM) or tf-idf with regularization. BoW tf was used; tf-idf is a standard and often stronger baseline. Without these, it’s hard to attribute gains specifically to the CN formalism.
- Clarity issues and incomplete dataset reporting. Some tables lack full context (e.g., exact N per class in Cookie Theft), and references are sometimes malformed. Preprocessing steps (stopword lists, handling of disfluencies) are only partly specified. It is unclear whether class balance was controlled and whether demographic variables were matched or used.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds stratified by subject in all datasets, especially ABCD where each subject has two narratives? If not, please re-run with subject-level splits and report updated results.
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train the FastText models (for English and Portuguese), and were test transcripts included? What vocabulary sizes and token counts? Please clarify whether pre-trained public embeddings were used or custom-trained on large external corpora.
- Threshold selection: How were similarity thresholds (0.7, 0.4) chosen? Was a held-out validation set used within each training fold (nested CV), or were thresholds tuned on the full dataset?
- Baselines: Can you report tf-idf + linear SVM, and a simple bag of averaged word embeddings + linear classifier, with subject-level CV? Also include a length-only baseline to check whether CN gains are orthogonal to length.
- Metrics and statistics: Please provide class distributions, majority-class baselines, mean±std over repeated CV, and statistical significance tests vs. the best baseline.
- ABCD evaluation unit: Are accuracies reported per narrative or per subject? If per narrative, can you also aggregate per subject (e.g., majority vote over immediate/delayed) to reflect a clinical decision?

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem with a practical, language-agnostic approach and promising results. However, concerns about potential leakage, underspecified embedding/training/tuning protocols, lack of statistical rigor, and limited novelty weaken the case at the ACL bar. Addressing the methodological issues and strengthening baselines/ablations would substantially improve the work. My current inclination is to reject, but I see a plausible path to acceptance with clarified and strengthened evaluation.
