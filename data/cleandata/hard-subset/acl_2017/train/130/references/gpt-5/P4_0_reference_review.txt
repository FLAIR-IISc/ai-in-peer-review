Summary
The paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks enriched with distributional semantics (word embeddings) to detect mild cognitive impairment (MCI). Nodes are words; edges include standard adjacency plus added links between semantically similar words (cosine similarity above a threshold). From these networks, the authors compute standard topological measures (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering, assortativity) and use them as features in classifiers. They compare (i) plain complex network (CN) features, (ii) enriched complex network (CNE) features, (iii) linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia), and (iv) bag-of-words (BoW), as well as multi-view combinations via majority vote. Experiments span three datasets: English Cookie Theft picture descriptions (DementiaBank), Portuguese Cinderella narratives, and Portuguese ABCD story retelling. Reported accuracies indicate CNE features are competitive and that multi-view combinations yield the best results on two datasets.

Contributions (as assessed)
- Demonstration that complex network features enriched with word embeddings (CNE) can discriminate MCI vs. controls from speech transcripts and are competitive with (and sometimes outperform) standard linguistic metrics and BoW baselines across datasets and languages.
- Empirical evidence that simple multi-view combination (majority voting across feature spaces/classifiers) can improve accuracy over individual feature spaces, reaching up to 80% on the ABCD dataset.
- Practical pipeline choices for noisy, short transcripts (no lemmatization, minimal preprocessing) and handling out-of-vocabulary via subword embeddings.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset, cross-lingual evaluation: Results are provided on three datasets in two languages, including both picture description and narrative retelling tasks. This improves the external validity of the findings compared to single-dataset studies.
- Clear ablations: The paper contrasts CN vs. CNE vs. linguistic vs. BoW features and includes multi-view combinations. The consistent improvement from CN to CNE supports the value of adding semantic links to mitigate sparsity/linearity in short-text networks.
- Competitive performance: Best accuracies are in the range 65% (Cookie Theft), 75% (Cinderella), and 80% (ABCD) using multi-view/ensemble approaches, comparable to prior work cited on related tasks. Notably, simple BoW with linear SVM is a strong baseline and is appropriately included.
- Task-relevant design choices: Avoidance of lemmatization and reliance on robust subword embeddings are justified for disfluent transcripts where POS/lemmatization may be unreliable. The approach does not require manual annotation at inference time and is compatible with automatic sentence segmentation.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and confounding in evaluation: The ABCD dataset includes two retellings per subject; it is unclear whether folds were created at the subject level. Without grouping by speaker, the 5-fold cross-validation risks training/test contamination. Moreover, Table 3 shows a large age difference between controls (mean 61) and MCI (mean 72), which could confound results; no control for demographics (e.g., matched sampling, covariate adjustment) is reported.
- Insufficient detail on embedding training and threshold selection: The source corpus, vocabulary size, and training data for the fastText-style embeddings are not described. If trained only on the small task transcripts, embedding quality is likely limited; if trained on large external corpora, this should be stated. The similarity threshold is “optimized” with best values per dataset, but the procedure (nested CV vs. using test folds) is not specified, risking overfitting.
- Limited metrics and statistical analysis: Evaluation uses accuracy only, with no class distribution reporting, no confidence intervals, and no statistical significance tests. With small sample sizes (e.g., 40 subjects in Cinderella) and potential imbalance, accuracy can be misleading; precision/recall/F1 and ROC-AUC would be informative.
- Dataset description clarity: The Cookie Theft cohort description is internally inconsistent (“We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”). The exact number of controls, per-subject transcript counts, and class balance per fold are not clearly stated.
- Methodological simplifications not fully justified: Majority-vote multi-view fusion is basic; weighting or learning-to-combine could be stronger and more interpretable. Complex network construction is unweighted and undirected; given similarity magnitudes, weighted edges could carry more information. The effect of removing stopwords and disfluencies is not analyzed; some function words/disfluencies may carry diagnostic signal. Claims that linguistic metrics “depend on grammatical texts” are asserted, but in Table 6 LM with SVM-RBF achieves 0.725, competitive with CNE, and the best result is from a CNE-BoW ensemble, so the conclusion needs nuance.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds created at the subject level for all datasets (especially ABCD with two retellings per subject)? If not, please re-run with subject-wise CV to rule out leakage.
- Embeddings: What corpus was used to train the subword embeddings (size, domain, language)? Were pre-trained fastText vectors used or trained from scratch? Are the same embeddings used across all folds (trained externally) to avoid training on test data?
- Threshold tuning: How was the cosine similarity threshold selected (grid, continuous search)? Was tuning nested within cross-validation? Please report sensitivity analyses for the threshold and the proportion of added edges.
- Class distributions and metrics: What are the positive/negative counts per dataset? Please report precision/recall/F1 and ROC-AUC, plus confidence intervals or variance across folds, and conduct significance testing between key conditions (e.g., CNE vs. LM vs. BoW).
- Demographic confounds: Given the age and education differences (notably in ABCD), did models inadvertently capture demographics? Have you controlled for or matched on these variables, or tested models with/without demographic features to assess confounding?
- Network construction specifics: Are similarity-induced edges unweighted (binary) and undirected? Have you tried weighted edges or combining co-occurrence weights with similarity weights? What is the average increase in edge density after enrichment, and how do topological measures change?
- Reproducibility: Will code, preprocessing scripts, trained embeddings, and data splits be released to enable replication?

Overall, the idea of enriching short-text co-occurrence networks with embeddings for MCI detection is plausible and empirically supported here, but the evaluation methodology and missing details need clarification to assess technical soundness and robustness.
