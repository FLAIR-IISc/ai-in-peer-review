Summary
This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks, then enriching those networks with semantic links derived from word embeddings (cosine similarity above a threshold). Topological features (centralities, clustering, diameter, assortativity, etc.) computed over these enriched graphs are used to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs controls. The approach aims to reduce reliance on fragile linguistic preprocessing (e.g., parsing) and cope better with disfluent speech. The authors evaluate on three datasets: Cookie Theft picture descriptions in English (DementiaBank), and two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella narratives and ABCD recall tasks), comparing complex-network features (with/without embedding enrichment), Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features, and bag-of-words (BoW), with multiple classifiers and simple multi-view majority voting. The enriched networks generally outperform plain network features, and multi-view combinations yield further gains (up to 80% accuracy on ABCD).

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Applying embedding-enriched co-occurrence network topology to detect MCI from speech transcripts across English and Portuguese, demonstrating competitive performance without heavy manual linguistic preprocessing.
- Empirical evidence that combining enriched network features with BoW and/or linguistic features via a simple multi-view scheme can improve accuracy on small clinical datasets.
- A practical modeling/feature-extraction pipeline that is potentially robust to disfluencies and limited NLP resources, especially for Portuguese.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practical relevance: Early MCI detection from language is impactful, and the proposed approach avoids dependence on fragile tools (e.g., parsers) that are often unavailable or unreliable for disfluent speech and lower-resourced languages.
- Methodological plausibility: Enriching adjacency networks with embedding-based similarity is a sensible way to mitigate the near-linearity of graphs from short texts and inject semantic cohesion; the chosen topological measures are standard and interpretable.
- Cross-lingual evaluation: Results across English (short descriptions) and Portuguese (longer narratives and short retellings) suggest the approach generalizes to different elicitation tasks and languages.
- Competitive baselines and combinations: The paper compares against BoW and Coh-Metrix(-Dementia), includes multiple classifiers, and explores (albeit simply) ensemble and multi-view combinations, showing consistent improvements from enrichment and combinations.
- Practical insight: The Cinderella “manually revised” variant highlights how linguistic metrics benefit from curated text, while network features remain competitive without such curation—supporting the paper’s central claim about robustness.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject leakage and evaluation bias: In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate and delayed). The paper uses 5-fold CV seemingly at the narrative level. If folds are not stratified by subject, the same subject may appear in both train and test, inflating accuracy. Similarly, the Cookie Theft and Cinderella setups do not explicitly ensure subject-wise splits. This is a critical threat to validity and must be addressed with subject-level CV or leave-one-subject-out.
- Confounding demographics: In ABCD, the MCI group is substantially older than controls (72 vs 61). Without controlling for age/education (matching, covariates, or residualization), models may capture demographics rather than cognitive impairment. No analyses controlling for confounds are reported.
- Incomplete details on embeddings and threshold tuning: Embedding training corpora, tokenization/stopword lists per language, and whether pre-trained fastText vectors were used are unspecified. The semantic-edge threshold is “optimized” per dataset, but it is unclear whether this is tuned on held-out data or within the same CV folds (no nested CV), risking overfitting. No sensitivity analysis is provided for the threshold or embedding choices.
- Limited baselines and metrics: Evaluation relies on accuracy only; no F1, AUC, or confidence intervals are reported, and no statistical significance testing is provided. Stronger baselines (e.g., L2-regularized logistic regression on BoW/TF-IDF, linear SVM on character n-grams, gradient boosted trees, or simple neural models) are absent. The multi-view method is an unweighted majority vote; calibrated stacking or learned weighting could be more informative.
- Dataset clarity and reproducibility: The Cookie Theft cohort description is confusing (e.g., “43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”), and Table 1 is missing. It is unclear how controls are defined and matched. Code/data availability and preprocessing details (e.g., the specific disfluency removal rules, how CHAT annotations were stripped, how sentence segmentation errors affect networks) are insufficient for replication.
- Design choices may discard useful signal: Removing stopwords before building networks may lose function-word cues known to be informative in dementia detection. An ablation on stopword removal would be helpful.

Questions to authors
- How did you partition data for cross-validation? Specifically, did you ensure subject-wise splits (so that all narratives from a subject reside in a single fold)? If not, please re-run with subject-level CV and report the revised results.
- How did you control for demographic confounds (age, education, sex), especially in ABCD where group means differ substantially? Can you report results with demographics included as features or matched subsamples?
- What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were pre-trained fastText vectors used, or did you train on your small datasets? Please specify tokenization, preprocessing, and OOV handling. Can you include a sensitivity analysis over different embeddings and semantic thresholds (including nested CV)?
- For Cookie Theft, please clarify the composition of the MCI and control sets (counts, diagnostic criteria, and whether AD or other dementia subjects were excluded). Provide the missing table and ensure the classes are balanced or report balanced metrics.
- Can you provide additional baselines (e.g., logistic regression with TF-IDF BoW, char n-gram SVM) and report F1/AUC with confidence intervals? Also, have you tried learned multi-view fusion (stacking) or simple feature concatenation with normalization?

Overall, the idea of enriching word-adjacency networks with embeddings for clinical language classification is interesting and practically motivated, and the preliminary results are promising. However, serious issues around experimental protocol (subject leakage), demographic confounds, missing methodological details, and limited baselines/metrics currently undermine the evidential strength. Addressing these concerns would substantially improve the paper.
