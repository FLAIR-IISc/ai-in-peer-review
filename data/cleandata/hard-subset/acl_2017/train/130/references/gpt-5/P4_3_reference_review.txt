Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them with semantic links derived from word embeddings (cosine similarity above a threshold). It then extracts standard network topology metrics and uses them as features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. The approach is compared to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW) features, and combined via multi-view and ensemble learning. Experiments are run on three datasets spanning English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella and ABCD), with both short descriptions and longer narratives.
- Contributions as assessed:
  1) Empirical evidence that embedding-enriched complex network features provide competitive and often superior performance to traditional linguistic metrics for MCI detection across two languages and different elicitation tasks, without the need for manual, resource-heavy preprocessing.
  2) Multi-view combinations of complex network, linguistic, and BoW features systematically improve accuracy (up to 80.18% on ABCD), demonstrating complementarity of feature spaces.
  3) An adaptation of embedding-enriched network modeling to short, noisy neuropsychological transcripts, showing gains over plain co-occurrence networks.

Strengths
- Language- and resource-agnostic feature design: The network approach avoids reliance on high-accuracy parsers and extensive manual cleanup, which is a known bottleneck in Portuguese. The results show that CN features remain effective without the manually revised transcripts required by prior work (e.g., Aluísio et al., 2016), where linguistic metrics degrade on raw transcripts (Table 6 vs. Table 5), underscoring robustness to disfluencies.
- Cross-lingual and cross-task evaluation: The method is tested on three datasets with different languages (English, Portuguese), sample lengths (short picture descriptions vs. longer narratives), and segmentation regimes (manual vs. automatic). Enriched CN (CNE) is consistently better than plain CN (e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF 0.626 vs. 0.569; Cinderella SVM-RBF 0.650 vs. 0.575), supporting the general utility of enrichment for short/noisy texts.
- Complementarity via multi-view learning: Combining feature spaces yields the best accuracies (Cookie Theft 0.650 with CNE-LM-BoW; Cinderella 0.750 with CNE–LM ensemble; ABCD 0.8018 with CNE-LM-BoW SVM-Linear). This indicates the proposed features add independent signal beyond BoW and Coh-Metrix.
- Clear feature engineering and reproducible feature sets: The paper enumerates standard network metrics and outlines preprocessing choices (e.g., stopword removal, no lemmatization), enabling independent replication of the feature engineering stage.

Weaknesses
- Risk of data leakage and non-independent folds: In ABCD, each subject contributes multiple narratives (46 vs. 39). The use of 5-fold cross-validation without explicit subject-level grouping risks having narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test, inflating accuracy. The paper does not state that folds are stratified by subject across all datasets (particularly important for ABCD and any multi-session data).
- Insufficient control for demographic confounds: In ABCD, groups differ substantially in age, education, and sex (e.g., Control mean age 61 vs. MCI 72; sex distribution differs). Without matching, covariate adjustment, or analyses controlling for these variables, classifiers may learn demographic correlates rather than cognitive impairment. Similar concerns apply to the Cookie Theft subset, where the description of group composition is unclear (MCI vs. AD vs. controls).
- Incomplete and potentially weak baselines: BoW is limited to term frequency; stronger text baselines such as TF-IDF with linear models (e.g., logistic regression/SVM) and simple neural bag-of-embeddings are omitted. Notably, even the provided BoW SVM-Linear is the strongest single baseline on ABCD (0.7547), underscoring the need for competitive baselines. Acoustic features, common in prior MCI/AD work, are also absent.
- Hyperparameter selection and evaluation protocol lack rigor: The embedding similarity threshold is “optimized” but the procedure (grid, search space, and whether nested CV was used) is not described. Classifier hyperparameters appear to be defaults; no tuning or regularization analysis is reported. Only accuracy is presented; no confidence intervals, standard deviations, or statistical significance tests are provided, limiting the robustness of conclusions.
- Ambiguities and missing details impacting reproducibility: The corpus used to train fastText embeddings (size, domain, language-specific models) is unspecified; training embeddings solely on the small task datasets would be suboptimal. The exact stopword lists, handling of OOV across languages, and the rationale for removing stopwords (which can carry structural information in networks) are not justified. The Cookie Theft cohort description is inconsistent (MCI “sampled from AD” vs. presence of controls), and Table 1 is referenced but not shown.
- Novelty is incremental: Using complex networks for text classification and enriching with word embeddings follows prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main novelty is the application to MCI detection with cross-lingual evaluation. The methodological contribution beyond adaptation is limited.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you enforce subject-level folds for all datasets, especially ABCD with multiple narratives per participant, to prevent train-test contamination? If not, can you re-run with grouped CV and report revised metrics?
- Cookie Theft cohort: How many MCI and control transcripts were used, and how were they selected? The text suggests MCI were “sampled from AD” cohorts—please clarify diagnostic criteria and group composition, and provide the full demographics (Table 1).
- Embeddings: What corpora and training data were used for English and Portuguese embeddings (size, domain, token counts)? Were they trained only on the task transcripts or on larger external corpora? Please report OOV rates and sensitivity to dimensionality and similarity thresholds, ideally with nested CV.
- Confound control: Did you attempt age/education/sex matching or include these as covariates? If not, can you provide analyses showing performance persists in matched subsets or after regressing out demographic effects?
- Baselines and ablations: Can you report results with TF-IDF + linear models, and a simple averaged word-embedding baseline? Also provide ablations isolating the impact of each network metric and the effect of stopword removal on network performance.
- Statistical robustness: Please report variance across folds and significance testing for key comparisons (CNE vs. CN; CNE vs. LM; multi-view vs. best single view).
