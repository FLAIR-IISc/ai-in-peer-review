Summary
This paper investigates detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges. From these enriched graphs, the authors compute standard topological metrics (e.g., PageRank, betweenness) aggregated over nodes and use them as features for standard classifiers. They compare against Coh-Metrix-based linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) and also explore multi-view ensembles. Experiments span three datasets: English Cookie Theft descriptions (DementiaBank), Portuguese Cinderella narratives (two variants, including a manually revised version), and Portuguese ABCD retellings with automatic sentence segmentation. Results using 5-fold cross-validation show that embedding-enriched network features are competitive with, and sometimes outperform, linguistic and BoW features; multi-view ensembles often yield the best accuracies.

Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
- Demonstration that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features are a viable, often competitive representation for MCI detection from transcripts, reducing dependence on manual preprocessing and parsing.
- A bilingual, multi-dataset empirical study (English and Brazilian Portuguese; picture description, story narration, and retelling) including automatically segmented transcripts, indicating some robustness across settings.
- Evidence that multi-view ensemble learning across heterogeneous feature spaces (CNE, LM, BoW) can improve accuracy over single-view models on several datasets.

Strengths
- Practicality and robustness to noisy transcripts: The approach avoids lemmatization and heavy parsing, which are error-prone on disfluent speech. This aligns with the goal of a fully automated pipeline and addresses a real bottleneck in prior Portuguese work requiring manual revision.
- Cross-lingual, multi-task evaluation: The study covers three datasets across two languages and tasks (picture description, storytelling, immediate/delayed retelling), including an automatically segmented corpus (ABCD). This breadth supports claims of generality beyond a single dataset or language.
- Clear modeling and feature design: The enriched network construction (adding edges when cosine similarity exceeds a threshold) builds on known methods and is well specified. Use of subword embeddings (Bojanowski et al.) addresses OOV issues common in speech transcripts.
- Empirical gains from enrichment and multi-view: Enriching co-occurrence networks with embeddings consistently improves over plain CN in most settings (e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF: 0.5694→0.6264; Cinderella SVM-RBF: 0.5750→0.6500). Multi-view ensembles yield the top results in several cases (Cookie Theft: 0.6500; Cinderella: 0.7500 on the manually revised set via CNE-BoW ensemble; ABCD: 0.8018 with CNE-LM-BoW).
- Baseline diversity and transparency: The paper compares three feature families (CNE, LM via Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia, BoW) and multiple classifiers (SVM variants, KNN, RF, GNB), which helps contextualize the proposed features.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Complex networks for text and embedding-based network enrichment are established; the primary novelty is their application to MCI detection. The contribution is incremental rather than conceptual, and several design choices (e.g., standard topological metrics, majority-vote ensembles) are straightforward.
- Evaluation rigor and metrics: Only accuracy is reported, with small sample sizes (e.g., Cinderella 20+20). No confidence intervals, variance estimates, or statistical significance tests are provided. Class distributions and per-class performance (precision/recall/F1) are absent. Given the dataset sizes, the reported differences may not be robust.
- Potential confounds and cohort imbalance: In the ABCD dataset, groups differ markedly in age (61 vs. 72) and education (16 vs. 13.3 years). Without controlling for demographics (e.g., stratified sampling, covariates, matched subsamples), classifiers may exploit demographic proxies rather than cognitive markers. No analysis is provided to rule out this confounding.
- Hyperparameter tuning and validation clarity: The similarity threshold is “optimized,” but the procedure is not specified (e.g., nested cross-validation vs. tuning on the same folds), raising overfitting concerns. Details of multi-view combination (only majority vote? any calibration?) and model selection across folds are sparse.
- Baseline coverage and comparability: BoW uses raw term frequency only (no tf-idf). There are no comparisons to simple embedding-based document representations (e.g., averaged embeddings), or to acoustic features common in MCI/AD detection. For Portuguese, linguistic feature subsets differ due to parser limitations, complicating direct comparisons. Some paper claims seem overstated relative to tables (e.g., BoW SVM-linear outperforms CNE on ABCD; LM SVM-RBF is higher than CNE on Cinderella manual).
- Reporting gaps and clarity: For Cookie Theft, the control cohort size and selection criteria are unclear, although averages per narrative are reported. Some figure references (Figure 1 vs. Figure 2) and references are inconsistent. Embedding training corpora (language, size, domain) are unspecified, which impacts reproducibility and comparability.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Embeddings: On what corpora were embeddings trained for English and Portuguese (size, domain, preprocessing)? Were separate models trained per language? Were pre-trained FastText models considered?
- Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold tuned? Was nested cross-validation used to avoid train/test leakage? Please provide sensitivity analysis across thresholds.
- Confounding variables: How did you control for age and education differences, especially in ABCD? Were folds stratified by demographics? Can you report results on matched subsets or with demographic covariates?
- Class balance and metrics: What are the exact class counts per dataset and fold? Please report precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals, and provide significance tests for key comparisons.
- Multi-view ensemble: Beyond majority vote, did you try probability averaging or learned weighting? How were classifier outputs calibrated?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, feature extraction scripts, and detailed preprocessing steps (including stopword lists and sentence segmentation settings)? Can you clarify the Cookie Theft control cohort composition and sample sizes used?

Overall, the paper is clear in its high-level methodology and presents promising, practical results across multiple datasets. However, the novelty is modest, and the evaluation would benefit from stronger statistical rigor, clearer cohort reporting, and controls for demographic confounds.
