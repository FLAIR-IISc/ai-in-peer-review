Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges, extracts topological metrics, and uses them to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs controls in English and Brazilian Portuguese datasets. It compares complex network features (CN, CNE) to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag of Words (BoW), using several classifiers and simple ensemble/multiview majority voting, with 5-fold CV.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Application of embedding-enriched word co-occurrence networks to MCI detection from transcripts across two languages and several tasks, showing competitive performance to BoW and linguistic baselines (sometimes modest gains).
2) Empirical study suggesting that enriching co-occurrence graphs with semantic links improves network-based classification for short noisy transcripts.
3) Practical exploration of multiview majority voting across heterogeneous feature spaces.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-task evaluation: The paper evaluates on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD), including two versions of Cinderella (raw-like vs manually revised). This breadth is valuable and demonstrates method portability to different elicitation protocols and preprocessing qualities.

- Simple, language-agnostic features that avoid heavy parsing: The network features (especially when enriched by embeddings) are appealing for low-resource, disfluent clinical transcripts where robust parsing is difficult. The choice to avoid lemmatization and depend minimally on manual cleanup is practical.

- Competitive baselines and results: Linear SVM on BoW is a reasonable strong baseline; network+embedding features are often competitive or slightly better (e.g., ABCD 0.8018 with CNE-LM-BoW vs BoW 0.7547). The finding that CNE generally outperforms bare CN is consistent and meaningful for short-text settings.

- Clear feature description and standard graph metrics: The topological features are well enumerated (centralities, clustering, assortativity, etc.), and the aggregation strategy (mean, std, skewness) is straightforward, aiding interpretability.

- Inclusion of an automatic segmentation pipeline (DeepBond) for ABCD: It aligns with the stated goal of fully automated processing and increases the ecological validity of the approach.

Weaknesses
- Critical experimental design ambiguities and likely leakage:
  - Cross-validation appears narrative-level, not subject-level. In ABCD, each subject produced two narratives; without subject-wise splits, the same subject likely appears in training and test, inflating accuracy. Similarly for other datasets, the unit of CV split is not stated.
  - The embedding training corpus is unspecified. If embeddings are trained on the full set of transcripts, fold leakage is possible; if trained on tiny in-domain data, quality is questionable; if trained on external corpora, sources must be described for each language.
  - The similarity threshold “optimized” per dataset (0.7, 0.4) lacks a clear validation protocol. If tuned on the full data or test folds, this biases results.

- Demographic confounds and insufficient controls:
  - ABCD demographics differ markedly (controls younger by ~11 years; gender distributions inverted; education differs). No matching, covariate control, or stratified analysis is reported. Accuracy may reflect demographic differences rather than language deficits.
  - Cookie Theft counts are unclear; Table 1 is missing; the text oddly states MCI sampled from probable/possible AD cohorts, creating diagnostic confusion. Without transparent class sizes and matching, reported metrics are hard to interpret.

- Incomplete and uneven baselines and metrics:
  - Only accuracy is reported. No precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, or confidence intervals; no statistical significance tests across folds. With small sample sizes, differences (e.g., 0.05) may not be meaningful.
  - BoW uses plain term frequency; tf-idf and character n-gram baselines are standard and often stronger for short, noisy text. No simple embedding-average baseline is included.
  - No ablation to test the additive value of semantic edges vs co-occurrence, or sensitivity to the threshold, network directionality/weights, or edge weighting.

- Methodological detail gaps undermine reproducibility:
  - Graph construction: directed vs undirected is specified, but co-occurrence window length is fixed to adjacency only; stopword removal before edge creation changes adjacency semantics. No justification or ablation for these choices, and no handling of disconnected components.
  - Classifier hyperparameters are unspecified (C/gamma for SVMs, k for KNN, tree counts for RF), beyond “default”; no validation protocol described.
  - No normalization of graph metrics for text length. Many network measures are size-sensitive; group differences in length (reported) may drive results.

- Numerous presentation and formatting issues:
  - Figures/tables/references inconsistencies: “Figure 1” vs “Figure 2”; missing Table 1; decimal commas in Table 3; multiple typos (“parametrs”, “vertice”, “agramatical”), reference list contains LaTeX artifacts and inconsistent styles; footnotes/URLs embedded awkwardly; section cross-references off. These reduce clarity and raise concerns about care in execution.

Questions to authors
1) Was cross-validation performed at the subject level for all datasets? If not, please re-run with subject-wise splits and report all metrics with confidence intervals.
2) On what corpora were the fastText embeddings trained for English and Portuguese? Were embeddings trained solely on training folds to avoid leakage? Please detail tokenization, vocabulary size, and OOV rates.
3) How was the similarity threshold selected? Provide the search space, validation procedure, and sensitivity analysis.
4) What are the exact class sizes (n subjects, n narratives) per group for each dataset? For Cookie Theft, clarify the diagnostic labels and how MCI vs controls were defined.
5) Please report tf-idf and character n-gram baselines (linear SVM), and a simple averaged-embedding baseline. Also report F1/ROC-AUC and statistical tests.
6) Did you control for length and demographics (age, education, sex)? At minimum, include them as covariates or features and report stratified results.

Overall assessment
The idea of enriching co-occurrence networks with embeddings for clinical transcript classification is sensible and practically attractive, and the cross-lingual evaluation is commendable. However, substantial methodological ambiguities (likely subject-level leakage, threshold tuning without proper validation), demographic confounds, incomplete baselines/metrics, and numerous presentation issues significantly weaken the validity and impact. I recommend rejection in its current form; a revised version addressing the above concerns, with rigorous leakage-free evaluation and stronger baselines, could be much more compelling.
