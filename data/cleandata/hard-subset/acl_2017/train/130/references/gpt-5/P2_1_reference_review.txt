Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks and enriching them with semantic links derived from word embeddings (cosine similarity threshold), then classifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs control using topological features. It compares this approach against linguistic (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW) baselines across three datasets (English and Brazilian Portuguese), and explores ensemble and multi-view learning.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical evidence that complex-network features enriched with word embeddings (CNE) are competitive and sometimes superior to traditional linguistic features/BoW for MCI detection from transcripts, including under low-resource, noisy settings (Portuguese, disfluencies, automatic segmentation).
  2) A multi-view combination of CNE, LM, and BoW that yields the best accuracies on two datasets, suggesting complementary signals.
  3) A practical pipeline avoiding heavy manual annotation/robust parsing, making the approach potentially portable across languages and settings.

Strengths
1) Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation. The method is tested on three datasets spanning English Cookie Theft descriptions, Portuguese Cinderella narratives (raw and cleaned), and Portuguese ABCD retellings, with both manual and automatic sentence segmentation. This supports claims of robustness to different elicitation tasks, lengths, and languages, which is valuable for clinical NLP where resources are uneven.

2) Addresses short-text limitations in network modeling. Enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based edges is a principled way to mitigate the near-linear topology of short transcripts. The reported consistent gains of CNE over plain CN (e.g., Cookie Theft CN 0.569 vs CNE 0.626 SVM-RBF; Cinderella CN 0.575 vs CNE 0.650 SVM-RBF; ABCD CNE 0.717 SVM-Linear) support the motivation.

3) Low-dependency pipeline suitable for noisy clinical transcripts. The approach avoids lemmatization and heavy parsing, explicitly tackling disfluency-laden speech transcripts where robust tools are scarce (especially in Portuguese). This practical angle is important for deployability.

4) Evidence of complementarity. Multi-view combinations often improve over single views (e.g., ABCD CNE-LM-BoW SVM-Linear 0.802 vs best single-view 0.755 BoW; Cinderella ensemble CNE-LM 0.750 vs best single 0.650). This suggests the proposed features add independent signal beyond standard text representations.

5) Transparent feature set. The network features are standard and interpretable (centralities, clustering, assortativity, etc.), facilitating future analysis and clinical interpretability compared to opaque end-to-end models.

Weaknesses
1) Insufficient methodological detail and risk of overfitting in hyperparameter selection. The embedding similarity threshold is “optimized” per dataset (0.7 vs 0.4), but it is unclear if this tuning was nested within the cross-validation folds. If tuned on full data, accuracy is optimistic. Similarly, embedding training is underspecified (training corpus, tokenization, vocabulary size, OOV rate), which affects the CNE graph and reproducibility.

2) Baselines under-optimized and potentially weak. BoW uses raw term frequency only (no tf–idf, no n-grams), and SVM/RF parameters appear to be defaults. Given that a simple BoW SVM-Linear achieves 0.755 on ABCD even with these limitations, stronger baselines might close or surpass the proposed method (e.g., tf–idf, char/word n-grams, regularized logistic regression, calibrated SVMs, shallow neural models, or pre-trained multilingual embeddings). The comparison omits acoustic features, which are known to be strong for MCI/AD detection, and prior clinical NLP methods (e.g., richer lexical-syntactic signals, topic features).

3) Dataset composition and evaluation clarity. For Cookie Theft, the control group size and exact selection protocol are not clearly reported, and the description (“43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”) is confusing and could imply label noise. Class balance, stratification in folds, and whether repeated narratives per subject are split between train/test (ABCD has immediate/delayed retells) are not described; subject leakage would inflate results. Only accuracy is reported, with no confidence intervals or statistical significance tests, which is problematic given small sample sizes.

4) Limited analysis and ablations. The paper does not analyze which topological measures carry signal, how network density/threshold selection affects performance beyond a single best value, or how much enrichment vs co-occurrence contributes across text lengths. There is no error analysis (e.g., confusion matrices, typical failure cases), nor control for confounders (age/education differences are nontrivial in ABCD). Without these, it’s hard to assess robustness and interpretability.

5) Reproducibility and clarity issues. Important implementation details are missing (feature scaling/normalization, graph weighting, directed vs undirected treatment, handling multiword expressions, tokenization specifics for Portuguese). Some writing/formatting issues (e.g., figure numbering, table references) and minor typos reduce clarity. No code or embeddings are released.

Questions to authors
- How was the similarity threshold selected relative to cross-validation? Was tuning nested within each training fold? Please report performance as a function of threshold and provide variance across folds.
- On what corpus were the subword embeddings trained (size, domain, language(s))? Were embeddings trained separately per language? Were test folds included in embedding training?
- For ABCD, are immediate and delayed retells from the same subject kept within the same CV fold to avoid subject leakage? Were CV splits stratified by class?
- Please report class distributions per dataset and per fold, as well as precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals. Are gains statistically significant?
- Did you try stronger text baselines (tf–idf, word/char n-grams) and hyperparameter tuning for SVM/RF? How do results change?
- How are edges weighted (binary vs weighted by frequency/similarity)? Are networks directed? Are topological features normalized for graph size to control length effects?
- Which topological features contribute most? Can you provide feature importance or ablations?
- How were demographic confounders (age, education) handled, especially in ABCD where groups differ? Any covariate adjustment or matching?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important clinical NLP task with a practical, language-agnostic approach and provides encouraging cross-lingual results. However, methodological and reporting gaps (tuning protocol, baselines, dataset clarity, significance) limit confidence in the claimed improvements and in the generality of the approach. With clearer experimental rigor, stronger baselines, and deeper analysis, the contribution could be solid; as is, I view the novelty as moderate and the evidence as promising but not yet conclusive.
