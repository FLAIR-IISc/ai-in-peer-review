Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks and enriches them with word-embedding-based similarity edges to mitigate sparsity in short texts. It computes a set of topological graph features and evaluates them for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD). The approach is compared to linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW), and multi-view ensembles are explored.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that enriching co-occurrence networks with word-embedding similarity yields competitive (sometimes superior) performance to standard linguistic and BoW features for MCI detection across two languages and multiple tasks, and that multi-view combinations can further improve accuracy.
2) A simple and generalizable enrichment scheme for co-occurrence graphs that adds edges between semantically similar words (cosine above a threshold), addressing the known limitation of network methods on short texts.
3) A cross-dataset, cross-lingual evaluation highlighting the feasibility of low-preprocessing graph-based features on noisy clinical transcripts.

Strengths
1) Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation: The method is evaluated on English and Brazilian Portuguese, with both picture description and story recall tasks, and with varying transcript lengths. This breadth strengthens the claim that the approach is language- and task-agnostic. For example, on ABCD, the multi-view SVM-linear with CNE-LM-BoW achieves 0.8018 accuracy; on Cinderella (automatic), the ensemble multi-view CNE-LM reaches 0.75.

2) Practical modeling for noisy clinical transcripts: The approach avoids heavy manual preprocessing and dependency on high-quality parsing, which is particularly valuable for disfluent speech. The decision to forgo lemmatization/POS tagging and to rely on subword embeddings lowers the barrier to deployment in real clinical settings and low-resource languages.

3) Addressing short-text sparsity in network modeling: The embedding-enrichment step directly tackles the “almost linear network” problem in short texts. Empirically, enriched networks (CNE) tend to outperform plain co-occurrence networks across datasets (e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF: CN 0.5694 vs CNE 0.6264).

4) Multi-view combination improves over single views: The paper shows consistent gains when combining feature spaces by majority vote. On ABCD, CNE-LM-BoW with SVM-linear yields 0.8018 vs best single-view 0.7547 (BoW with SVM-linear). This suggests complementary information across representations.

Weaknesses
1) Potential subject leakage and confounds in evaluation: On ABCD, each subject produces two narratives (immediate and delayed recall). The paper does not state that folds are split by subject rather than by narrative. If the same speaker appears in training and test folds, results can be inflated. Additionally, ABCD demographics are imbalanced (Controls avg age 61 vs MCI 72; education also differs), so accuracy may reflect age/education confounds rather than cognitive status. No controls (e.g., matching, covariate-adjusted models, or stratified CV) are reported.

2) Incomplete and potentially over-optimistic model selection: Only accuracy is reported; no precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, or confidence intervals. The similarity threshold (0.7, 0.4) is “optimized,” but it is unclear whether tuning is nested within CV or performed on the full data, risking overfitting. Classifier hyperparameters (e.g., SVM C/γ) appear to use defaults, which may disadvantage some baselines (especially BoW/LM).

3) Unclear embedding training and reproducibility: The paper says it trained subword embeddings with default parameters but does not specify the training corpus (size, domain, language-specific corpora) or whether embeddings were trained on the evaluation transcripts (risking leakage). There is no ablation on embedding quality or coverage, and no release of code, features, or trained models to enable replication.

4) Overgeneralized claims and limited analysis: The conclusion that “topological properties of CN outperform linguistic metrics in individual classifiers” is not consistently supported (e.g., Cinderella manually processed: LM SVM-RBF 0.725 > CNE 0.675; ABCD: BoW SVM-linear 0.7547 > CNE 0.7173). There is no significance testing, feature importance analysis, or error analysis to explain when/why CNE helps. The impact of enrichment on network density/topology (e.g., number of added edges) is not quantified.

5) Baselines and fair comparison: BoW is implemented with term frequency only; tf-idf or regularized linear models with tuned hyperparameters are standard strong baselines and might perform better. For linguistic features, the choice to remove some disfluencies and keep others, and the use of different preprocessing across datasets, may confound comparisons. It is unclear whether stopwords are kept/removed consistently across feature types, which can affect BoW and graph construction differently.

Questions to Authors
- How were cross-validation folds constructed for ABCD and Cinderella? Were they split by subject (ensuring all narratives from a subject are in the same fold)? Please clarify to rule out subject leakage.
- What corpora were used to train the embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were the evaluation transcripts included? If so, please justify and, ideally, re-run with external corpora.
- How was the similarity threshold tuned? Was tuning nested within CV, or done once on the whole dataset? What is the sensitivity of performance to this threshold?
- Can you report additional metrics (F1, ROC-AUC) and confidence intervals, and conduct significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., CNE vs LM, CNE vs BoW)?
- What is the dimensionality of the network feature set (how many features total), and how many similarity edges are typically added per document at the chosen thresholds? Any risk of over-densification?
- For ABCD, given age/education differences, can you control for covariates (e.g., matched subsampling, regression-adjusted models) or at least report results on balanced subsets?
- Did you tune SVM/RF/KNN hyperparameters? If not, could you provide results with standard hyperparameter search and a tf-idf baseline?

Overall recommendation
Reject. The application is timely and the idea of embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks for clinical transcripts is interesting and potentially useful. However, serious methodological concerns (possible subject leakage, strong demographic confounds, lack of nested tuning and statistical analysis) undermine the validity of the reported gains. Clarifying and addressing these issues, strengthening baselines, and improving reproducibility would substantially improve the paper.
