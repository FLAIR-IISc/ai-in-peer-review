Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with semantic edges derived from word embeddings; graph-theoretic features (centrality, clustering, etc., summarized by mean/std/skew) are used for classifying MCI vs. control. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD), and compared against Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features and BoW, with additional multi-view majority-vote ensembles.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) An empirical study showing that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features can be competitive with standard text features for MCI detection across English and Portuguese datasets.
  2) A multi-view combination of graph, linguistic, and BoW features yielding modest gains in accuracy on some datasets.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation: The study covers English and Brazilian Portuguese, with tasks of picture description, storytelling, and immediate/delayed recall. This breadth is useful for understanding generality across elicitation protocols and languages.
- Comparisons to standard baselines: The inclusion of BoW and Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) provides a reference against established methods. Notably, linear SVM on BoW is strong in ABCD, anchoring the evaluation.
- Practical motivation: Avoiding reliance on brittle full syntactic parsing for disfluent transcripts is a sensible goal; topological features are simple to compute and potentially robust to noisy transcriptions.
- Transparent feature families and classifiers: The study uses widely known graph metrics and standard classifiers (SVM, kNN, RF, NB) with scikit-learn, which aids interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Word adjacency networks are well known, and adding embedding-based similarity edges follows prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main new element is applying this to MCI detection; methodologically this is incremental rather than conceptually novel.
- Methodological rigor concerns:
  • Cross-validation granularity: For ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives; it is unclear whether folds are subject-wise. If narratives from the same subject appear in train and test folds, results are inflated. Similarly, Cookie Theft and Cinderella might have multiple recordings per participant; the paper does not state subject-wise CV.  
  • Hyperparameter tuning: The similarity threshold (0.7/0.4) is “optimized,” but no nested CV/validation split is described. Tuning on the same CV folds can overfit.  
  • Embeddings training: The corpus used to train word vectors is unspecified (task transcripts vs. large external corpora, per language). If trained on the full dataset (including test folds), there is information bleed; if trained only on tiny task data, the vectors may be poor and unstable. Details are critical for reproducibility and validity.  
  • Only accuracy is reported, without confidence intervals or statistical significance tests. Given very small sample sizes (e.g., Cinderella 20/20; Cookie Theft counts are unclear), reported differences (often 2–5%) may be noise.
- Confounds not controlled: In ABCD, Control vs. MCI differ substantially in age (61 vs. 72) and education. Without controlling (matching, regression, or including demographics as features with appropriate baselines), classifiers may capture demographic differences rather than cognitive impairment. No analyses control for transcript length/network size, which can strongly affect graph metrics.
- Incomplete dataset and preprocessing description:
  • Cookie Theft: The number of controls is not stated; Table 1 is referenced but missing. Demographics and exact sample counts are unclear.  
  • Preprocessing is inconsistent across datasets, with some disfluencies automatically removed and others retained. Since disfluencies are often diagnostic, removing them risks discarding signal; the rationale and consistency relative to baselines need clarification.  
  • Feature dimensionality for each view (especially CNE) is not reported; no feature selection is described despite small n, increasing overfitting risk.
- Evaluation design of multi-view/ensembles: Majority voting across views/models is presented, but no ablation of calibration, weighting, or validation of ensemble design is provided. Gains are modest and could reflect variance rather than true synergy.

Questions to authors
- Splits: Were all CV splits strictly subject-wise for every dataset (especially ABCD with two narratives per subject)? If not, please re-run with subject-wise CV and report results.
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were vectors trained only on the task transcripts, or on large external text? Were embeddings fitted within training folds (nested) or once on the full corpus (including test data)?
- Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold chosen? Was nested CV used? Please report sensitivity to this parameter and average number of added edges per document.
- Controls and demographics: For Cookie Theft, how many controls were included, and are multiple visits per subject present? For ABCD, can you control for age/education (e.g., include them as features, match cohorts, or regress out confounds) and report adjusted performance?
- Size effects: Since graph metrics are sensitive to network size, can you normalize or at least report performance stratified by transcript length, and include length-only baselines?
- Metrics and significance: Please report per-class precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals; run statistical significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., CNE vs. BoW).
- Reproducibility: Provide exact feature counts per view, stopword lists, OOV handling, and code or sufficient pseudo-code for graph construction.

Overall recommendation: I am not convinced of acceptance at this stage. The methodological issues (potential leakage, confounds, lack of nested tuning/significance) and limited novelty outweigh the empirical findings. A revision with subject-wise CV, confound control, rigorous tuning, stronger statistical analysis, and clearer dataset/embedding details would substantially strengthen the work.
