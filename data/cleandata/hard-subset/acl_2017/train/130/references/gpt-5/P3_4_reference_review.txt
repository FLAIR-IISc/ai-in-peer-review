Summary and contributions
This paper tackles automatic detection of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing each transcript as a word co-occurrence (adjacency) network and enriching the network with semantic edges derived from word embeddings. The authors compute a set of graph-theoretic features and use them for binary classification (MCI vs control), comparing against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix / Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and a bag-of-words (BoW) baseline, and exploring ensemble and multi-view voting. Experiments span three datasets: English picture descriptions (Cookie Theft/DementiaBank) and two Portuguese datasets (Cinderella narratives and ABCD recall).

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple, language-agnostic variant of word adjacency networks enriched with embedding-based similarity edges, applied to MCI detection on noisy, short transcripts in two languages.
2) An empirical comparison showing that such “CNE” features are competitive with, and complementary to, Coh-Metrix-style linguistic metrics and BoW, with multi-view voting sometimes yielding the best accuracies.
3) A practical emphasis on reduced reliance on high-precision NLP preprocessing (no parsing/lemmatization), relevant for low-resource languages and noisy clinical transcripts.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, clinically relevant evaluation: The method is tested on English and Brazilian Portuguese, across different elicitation paradigms (picture description, story narration, immediate/delayed recall). This breadth is valuable and demonstrates potential generality beyond a single dataset or language.
- Low-dependency pipeline: Avoiding lemmatization and heavy parsing addresses real constraints in clinical settings (disfluencies; low-resource toolchains). This is a compelling practical angle relative to earlier work that required manual cleanup for parsers to succeed.
- Competitive and complementary performance: Enriched networks (CNE) outperform Coh-Metrix features on two datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella raw) and complement BoW and LM in multi-view voting, yielding the top accuracies in two datasets (65% Cookie Theft; 80% ABCD) and strong performance elsewhere. The comparison to the manually revised Cinderella set (where LM shines) is an informative sanity check.
- Methodological simplicity and interpretability potential: Graph features such as degree, clustering, betweenness, etc., are standard and interpretable, opening room for clinical insight about discourse structure differences, even though the paper does not fully exploit this yet.

Weaknesses
- Risk of overestimation due to data splitting: The ABCD dataset contains two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed). The paper uses 5-fold CV but does not state whether splits are by subject (speaker-independent). If the two narratives from the same speaker are split across folds, results are inflated. A subject-level CV (and reporting both settings) is essential. A similar concern may apply to other datasets if multiple samples per subject exist.
- Evaluation and reporting are limited: Accuracy alone (with possibly imbalanced classes) is insufficient. Report macro-F1, AUC, and confidence intervals, and conduct significance testing. No variance across folds is reported. Thresholds for adding embedding edges are “optimized” but the procedure (and whether nested CV was used) is unclear; tuning on the same folds risks optimistic bias.
- Baselines and ablations are incomplete: BoW uses raw term frequency; TF–IDF with linear SVM is a strong standard baseline and should be included. Likewise, simple embedding baselines (e.g., average fastText vectors + linear classifier), doc2vec, and modern pretrained sentence embeddings would contextualize the added value of graph topology versus pure semantics. An ablation on the enrichment threshold (and k-nearest neighbor vs global cosine threshold) and on stopword removal would clarify the mechanism. Document length is a major confound for graph size/topology; control for it (e.g., include length as a covariate or match lengths).
- Dataset description inconsistencies and potential confounds: Cookie Theft: “We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD” is confusing. How many controls were used and what were the exact labels? Demographic tables are incomplete/missing in the paper text. ABCD demographics show large age and sex differences across groups; these can drive differences in language use. Either match groups or include demographics as features/covariates and analyze their impact.
- Methodological clarity of graph construction: Key details are missing/ambiguous: exact number of graph features (local vs global), whether local clustering is aggregated by mean/std/skew or global transitivity used, whether edges are weighted, and the computational effect of adding all pairs above a cosine threshold (graphs may become dense, collapsing topology to length/lexical content effects). The rationale for removing stopwords is at odds with adjacency networks aimed at capturing syntactic relations; a comparison with/without stopwords is needed.
- Claims outpace evidence: The statement that CN “outperform linguistic metrics” is not consistently borne out (e.g., in ABCD, BoW linear SVM is best among single views; in Cinderella manual, LM is best). Multi-view gains are sometimes modest and could be due to ensembling rather than unique virtue of CN features. Without stronger baselines and significance testing, the incremental value of CNE remains suggestive but not conclusive.

Questions to authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: Were folds constructed at the subject level for ABCD (and any dataset with multiple samples per subject)? If not, please redo with speaker-independent CV and report both results and deltas.
2) Threshold tuning: How were similarity thresholds (0.7; 0.4) selected? Was there a held-out dev set or nested CV? Please provide sensitivity analyses and justify the choice versus k-NN graph construction.
3) Embeddings: On what corpora were the fastText embeddings trained for English and Portuguese? Only the small task corpora, or larger external corpora? Did you try publicly available pretrained fastText models? Please report vocabulary coverage and OOV rates.
4) Graph details: Are edges weighted by frequency/similarity? Which features are local vs global, and how many feature dimensions in total? Do you normalize features by graph size? Did you try keeping stopwords?
5) Baselines: Please include TF–IDF + linear SVM, averaged fastText/doc2vec, and a pretrained sentence embedding baseline. Also add macro-F1/AUC and significance tests.
6) Dataset clarity: Clarify the Cookie Theft cohort composition (numbers of MCI vs controls, selection criteria) and reconcile the “probable/possible AD” mention with the MCI label.
7) Confounds: Given age/education/sex differences (e.g., ABCD), did you control for these or assess their predictive power? Can you report results with demographics included/excluded?

Additional comments
- The interpretability angle is promising. A post-hoc analysis of which topological metrics drive decisions (and how enrichment changes them) would strengthen the paper’s clinical impact.
- Consider releasing code and graph feature extraction scripts for reproducibility.
