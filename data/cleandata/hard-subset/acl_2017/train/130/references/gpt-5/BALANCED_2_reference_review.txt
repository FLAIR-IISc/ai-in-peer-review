Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes representing speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments as word co-occurrence networks enriched with semantic links derived from word embeddings, and using topological network measures as features to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors evaluate across three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture descriptions from DementiaBank; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; and Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed recall) and compare complex network (CN) features to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag-of-Words (BoW), including combinations via multi-view/ensemble learning.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding that enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges (CNE) enables network topology features to be competitive with, and sometimes superior to, Coh-Metrix features and BoW on MCI detection across multiple datasets/languages.
2) Multi-view combination of CNE, linguistic metrics, and BoW improves performance over individual views on all datasets, with best accuracies of 0.65 (Cookie Theft), 0.75 (Cinderella), and 0.80 (ABCD).
3) A practical pipeline that avoids heavy linguistic preprocessing (no parsing/lemmatization), potentially increasing robustness to noisy clinical transcripts.

Strengths
1) Cross-dataset and cross-lingual evaluation. The method is tested on three distinct datasets spanning English and Brazilian Portuguese, with different elicitation tasks (picture description, story retell, immediate/delayed recall). This breadth strengthens external validity and shows that the approach is not tied to a single corpus or language.

2) Sensible feature comparisons with plausible baselines. The paper compares CNE to standard linguistic measures (Coh-Metrix[‑Dementia]) and BoW, and also to “plain” CN. The finding that CNE generally improves over CN supports the motivation that semantic enrichment mitigates issues of short, linear transcripts.

3) Clinically motivated, low-dependency preprocessing. Avoiding parsers and lemmatizers addresses known brittleness to disfluencies and reduces manual intervention. This is valuable for real-world deployment, where high-quality ASR/parsers may be unavailable, especially for Portuguese.

4) Transparent, replicable topological measures. The selected set of network metrics (degree, clustering, path length, assortativity, centralities, etc.) is standard and well-documented; reporting mean/std/skewness per metric is reasonable for small graphs.

Weaknesses
1) Potential data leakage and confounds in evaluation design. In the ABCD dataset, there are two narratives per subject (immediate/delayed). It is not stated whether cross-validation was performed at the subject level. If immediate and delayed narratives from the same subject appear in different folds, this can inflate accuracy via subject-specific lexical idiosyncrasies. Similarly, threshold tuning for semantic edges (“optimized with best values being …”) appears to use the same cross-validation without an inner validation loop, risking overfitting. Please clarify and, if needed, re-run with subject-wise nested CV.

2) Strong demographic confounds not controlled. The ABCD cohort shows large age differences (Control 61 vs. MCI 72 years) and different sex/education distributions. Without controlling for these variables (or matching), reported improvements may reflect demographic differences rather than cognitive status. At minimum, provide analyses that (a) report performance after regressing out demographics, (b) match subsamples, or (c) include demographics as covariates and show incremental value of CNE beyond them.

3) Insufficient detail on word embedding training and reproducibility. The corpus used to train fastText embeddings (size, domain, language-specific corpora), tokenization, and training details are unspecified beyond default hyperparameters. Given embeddings directly determine the added edges, this is central to reproducibility and fairness across languages. Also, no code or detailed feature extraction settings (stopword lists, thresholds per language) are provided.

4) Limited evaluation metrics and significance testing. The paper reports only accuracy. For imbalanced datasets and clinical use-cases, report precision/recall/F1 per class and ROC‑AUC, with confidence intervals. Include statistical significance testing or at least paired comparisons to support claims that CNE outperforms LM/BoW or that multi-view improves over best single view.

5) Incomplete methodological clarity. Several critical choices are under-specified: how stopwords were selected for each language; whether networks are built per sentence or per transcript; handling of OOVs and rare words; whether edges are weighted; how many topological features are ultimately used (10 measures × 3 moments?) and whether feature selection/normalization was applied. The Cookie Theft cohort composition is unclear (controls count, selection of 43 MCI “sampled from 326 probable AD” seems inconsistent).

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Was CV subject-wise for ABCD (ensuring both narratives from a subject remain in the same fold)? For Cookie Theft and Cinderella, was there any per-participant multiplicity? Please clarify and, if necessary, provide corrected results.
- Hyperparameter selection: How were similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) chosen? Was there an inner validation split within each training fold (nested CV), or were thresholds tuned on the full data?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were clinical transcripts included? Are the embeddings shared for release?
- Demographics: How do models perform when controlling for age/sex/education (e.g., via matching or adding as covariates)? Do CNE features add predictive value beyond demographics alone?
- Metrics: Please report class-wise precision/recall/F1 and ROC‑AUC, along with confidence intervals, and conduct significance testing between key conditions (CN vs. CNE; best single view vs. multi-view).
- Leakage via preprocessing: For ABCD, were sentence segmentation and disfluency removal trained on or tuned with any part of the evaluation data?
- Feature importance: Which topological features contribute most? Any ablation of mean vs. std vs. skewness, or of specific centralities?

Additional comments
- Clarify counts and demographics for Cookie Theft (numbers of controls vs. MCI; the current wording suggests sampling MCI from AD cohorts).
- Consider tf‑idf and n‑grams for BoW; the linear SVM BoW baseline is already strong on ABCD—tf‑idf could be even stronger and a fairer comparator.
- Include standardization of features and any feature selection details; small datasets benefit from regularization (e.g., L2‑logistic regression) that could be added as a baseline.
- Figures: there is a mismatch in Figure numbering (refers to Figure 1 then Figure 2 for the example).
- Ethical note: briefly discuss intended clinical use, risks of false positives/negatives, and need for human oversight.

Overall assessment
Promising direction and solid cross-corpus evaluation, but current methodological ambiguities (possible subject leakage, lack of demographic controls, and non-nested tuning) and missing details on embeddings undermine the reliability of the reported gains. I encourage a revision addressing these issues, with subject-wise nested CV, demographic analyses, richer metrics, clearer descriptions of embeddings and preprocessing, and stronger baselines.
