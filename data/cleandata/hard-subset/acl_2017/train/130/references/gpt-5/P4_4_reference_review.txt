Summary
The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks and enriches these networks with semantic links derived from word embeddings. From these enriched graphs, the authors compute standard topological measures (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, clustering, assortativity) and use their distributional statistics as features for classifying mild cognitive impairment (MCI) vs. control. They compare against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW) on three datasets spanning two languages (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD). Classification uses several standard algorithms and simple ensemble/multi-view voting. Across datasets, enriched network features are competitive with or better than traditional features, and multi-view combinations sometimes yield further gains.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Methodological application: Demonstration that complex network features enriched with word embeddings provide a robust, language-agnostic representation for classifying MCI from short, noisy transcripts, outperforming plain co-occurrence networks and often matching or surpassing linguistic features.
2) Empirical evaluation across languages and tasks: Consistent experiments on three datasets (English and Portuguese; picture description and story retelling), showing competitive accuracy and some improvements via multi-view combinations.
3) Practical angle: An approach that reduces reliance on parsing and manual preprocessing (unlike prior work), potentially facilitating deployment where high-quality tools or annotations are unavailable.

Strengths
- Novelty in this application domain: While complex network features have been used for various NLP tasks, their enrichment with word-embedding-based semantic links and application to MCI detection from transcripts appears new. The paper clearly states the motivation (short, disfluent texts) and provides a principled way to densify sparse networks.
- Cross-lingual, multi-task validation: Results are reported on three datasets spanning English and Portuguese, with differing elicitation tasks and transcript lengths. This breadth supports claims of generality and language/tool independence (e.g., no dependency on accurate parsers).
- Competitive performance and clear empirical trend: Enriched networks (CNE) consistently improve over plain co-occurrence networks (CN). On Cookie Theft, CNE improves best individual accuracy to 62.6% (SVM-RBF) and to 65.0% with multi-view. On Cinderella (raw), best individual is 65.0% (CNE + SVM-RBF) and 75.0% with multi-view ensemble. On ABCD, BoW (SVM-linear) reaches 75.5%, and the combined multi-view yields 80.2%. The steady gains from enrichment and fusion are informative.
- Reduced reliance on fragile preprocessing: Avoiding lemmatization and heavy parsing is reasonable for disfluent transcripts and aligns with the aim of automating screening where robust tools are lacking, especially for Portuguese.
- Clear articulation of topological features: The chosen network measures and how they are aggregated (mean, std, skewness) are explicitly listed, aiding conceptual reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation protocol clarity and potential leakage: The ABCD dataset includes two narratives per subject (immediate/delayed). The paper does not state whether cross-validation was conducted with subject-level splits. If narratives from the same individual appear in both train and test folds, results can be inflated. This must be clarified for all datasets (especially ABCD and DementiaBank where multiple samples per subject are common).
- Threshold tuning and hyperparameter selection: The semantic-edge threshold is “optimized” (0.7; 0.4) but the procedure is unspecified. Without nested cross-validation or a held-out validation set, threshold/algorithm choices may be tuned to test folds, risking optimistic estimates. Similarly, the choice among many algorithms and feature-space combinations without explicit model selection control increases overfitting risk given small sample sizes.
- Limited metrics and statistical analysis: Only accuracy is reported. Class balance, variance across folds, confidence intervals, or statistical significance tests are omitted. In small datasets (e.g., 20 vs. 20), reporting F1, ROC-AUC, per-fold variance, and significance is crucial to assess robustness.
- Reproducibility gaps: The embedding training details are insufficient. The paper states the use of Bojanowski et al. (fastText) with default parameters but does not specify training corpora, size, domain, or whether pre-trained vectors were used. Performance of semantic enrichment depends strongly on embedding quality. Additionally, no sensitivity analysis to the semantic threshold, embedding dimensions, or preprocessing (e.g., stopword removal) is provided.
- Dataset description inconsistencies: The Cookie Theft section contains confusing wording about controls vs. AD participants, and demographic tables/labels are incomplete in the excerpt. Some figure/table references and citations are inconsistent. These clarity issues hinder interpretability and replication.
- Baseline strength and potential confounds: On ABCD, BoW with a linear SVM already reaches 75.5%. Since ABCD involves retells, topic content and lexical overlap might drive classification; it is unclear whether immediate and delayed retells are balanced across classes, or whether topic effects confound labels. Stronger baselines (e.g., tf-idf with regularization, length-only features) and topic-control checks would contextualize the added value of CNE features.

Questions to Authors
1) Cross-validation splits: Were folds created at the subject level for all datasets, ensuring no subject appears in both train and test? If not, please re-evaluate with subject-level CV and report changes.
2) Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold tuned? Was tuning nested within cross-validation or done on the full data? Please provide a sensitivity analysis of accuracy vs. threshold.
3) Embeddings: What corpora were used to train fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese? Report corpus size, domain, tokenization, and whether any pre-trained vectors were used. Have you tested off-the-shelf fastText embeddings, and how do results compare?
4) Class balance and metrics: What are class distributions per dataset and per fold? Please add F1 (macro/micro), ROC-AUC, and confidence intervals, and conduct significance tests comparing CNE to baselines.
5) Subject/task confounds (ABCD): Are immediate and delayed retells balanced across MCI/control, and are both included per subject? Any measures taken to mitigate topic or prompt-induced lexical confounds? Could you report performance controlling for simple length-based features?
6) Feature ablations: Which topological measures contribute most? Provide ablation results (e.g., removing diameter, betweenness, etc.) and an analysis of length sensitivity.
7) Preprocessing: Did you lowercase, and how did stopword removal affect both CN and linguistic features? Given that filled pauses can be diagnostic, what is the impact of retaining vs. removing them?

Overall, the approach is promising and appears novel in this specific application, but the evaluation and reproducibility details need clarification and strengthened methodology to support the reported gains.
