- Models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-based edges to detect MCI in English and Brazilian Portuguese.
- Compares complex-network features (with/without enrichment) to linguistic metrics and BoW across three datasets, using multi-view learning and simple ensembles.
- Main empirical finding: embedding-enriched networks are competitive with or outperform linguistic metrics; multi-view combinations yield best accuracies (up to ~80% on ABCD).
- Contribution: Demonstrates embedding-enriched adjacency networks as useful, language-agnostic features for MCI detection from short, noisy transcripts.
- Contribution: Provides cross-dataset, cross-language evaluation showing multi-view fusion of networks, linguistic metrics, and BoW improves accuracy under limited resources.
- Contribution: Presents a practical pipeline that avoids heavy manual annotation or robust parsing, increasing applicability in low-resource settings.
- Strength: Targets an important clinical task and evaluates on both English and Brazilian Portuguese, increasing external validity.
- Strength: Enrichment strategy is well motivated for noisy, short, disfluent transcripts and CNE improves over plain CN in most settings.
- Strength: Reduces dependence on fragile NLP preprocessing (parsing/lemmatization), showing robustness to disfluencies.
- Strength: Uses simple, interpretable topological features and standard classifiers, making the approach transparent and reproducible in principle.
- Strength: Multi-view fusion yields the best results across datasets (Cookie Theft 65%, Cinderella ~75%, ABCD ~80%).
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—embedding-enriched text networks and fusion methods are largely known; novelty is mainly application-focused.
- Weakness: Evaluation rigor issues and potential leakage—subject-level split handling unclear, thresholds tuned without nested CV, no statistical tests or confidence intervals reported.
- Weakness: Dataset confounds and clarity problems—Cookie Theft composition unclear; ABCD shows large age mismatch that may confound results.
- Weakness: Insufficient baselines and ablations—BoW limited to raw TF, no tf-idf/averaged embeddings/doc2vec/PLM baselines, no acoustic features, no feature importance or sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—unclear embedding training corpora, missing code/preprocessing scripts, and incomplete table/figure details.
- Question: Were cross-validation folds created at the subject level to prevent speaker leakage (especially for ABCD with two narratives per subject)?
- Question: What corpora (sizes/domains) were used to train the fastText embeddings per language, and were pretrained vectors used or were transcripts included?
- Question: How were cosine similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) chosen and was nested CV used to avoid tuning on test folds?
- Question: Please clarify dataset compositions (exact MCI/control counts) and how demographic confounds (age/education) were mitigated.
- Question: Can you report precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, confidence intervals, and confusion matrices to assess statistical significance?
- Question: Did you try stronger lexical baselines (tf-idf, averaged word embeddings, doc2vec, PLMs) or include acoustic features?
- Question: Which topological measures drive performance and how robust are results to stopword removal, lemmatization, and varying enrichment thresholds?
- Overall assessment: Moderate originality and clinical significance with promising cross-language results, but mixed rigor, potential confounds, and reproducibility gaps that need addressing.
