Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies automatic detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by representing each transcript as a word co-occurrence network, then enriching this network with word-embedding similarity edges, and extracting topological features for classification.
- It evaluates the approach on three datasets (English Cookie Theft descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD retellings), compares against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and bag-of-words (BoW), and explores simple multi-view ensembling across feature spaces.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Application-focused: a simple, largely language/tool-agnostic featureization (embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks) that avoids heavy preprocessing, shown to be competitive across two languages and different tasks.
  2) Empirical finding: embedding-enrichment materially improves network features for short, disfluent transcripts; combining feature views via simple voting further improves accuracy.
  3) Practical insight: linguistic metrics can underperform without manual cleaning, while BoW with linear SVM is a strong baseline on some datasets.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation: The paper tests on English and Brazilian Portuguese with heterogeneous elicitation protocols (picture description, story narration, prompted retellings). This improves external face validity compared to single-corpus studies and surfaces interesting dataset-dependent behaviors (e.g., BoW dominating in ABCD).
- Robust, low-dependency featureization: Modeling transcripts as networks with embedding-based edges is attractive for noisy, disfluent speech, requiring minimal manual annotation and avoiding reliance on high-quality parsers (not always available for Portuguese). The design goal is well motivated by prior work and the realities of clinical transcripts.
- Clear empirical trend: In multiple tables, enriched networks (CNE) consistently outperform plain co-occurrence networks (CN), aligning with the stated motivation that short transcripts yield near-linear networks that benefit from semantic links. This is a coherent, well-argued result.
- Simple multi-view combination helps: Majority-vote ensembles across CNE, LM, and BoW give the best accuracies in several settings (e.g., 0.80 on ABCD), suggesting complementary information across feature spaces.
- Relevance and potential impact: Early MCI detection is clinically important; demonstrating workable methods that are less resource-intensive than full parsing makes the work appealing for lower-resource settings.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject leakage and evaluation validity: ABCD contains two retellings per subject; the paper uses 5-fold CV but does not state that folds are made at the subject level. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test folds, accuracy is likely inflated. Similarly, Cookie Theft and Cinderella split protocols are not described with subject-level grouping. This is a critical methodological gap that must be addressed.
- Confounding demographics: In the ABCD dataset, the control group is much younger (61) than the MCI group (72). Without controlling for age/education (e.g., matching, regression adjustment, or including demographics as covariates with careful analysis), reported gains may partially reflect demographic signals rather than cognitive status. No stratified analyses or confound checks are provided.
- Limited novelty: Using word co-occurrence networks for NLP is established, and enriching networks with embedding-based similarity edges follows prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main novelty is the application to MCI detection/transcripts and the empirical synthesis, which is incremental methodologically.
- Incomplete and potentially optimistic model selection: The cosine thresholds (0.7, 0.4) are said to be “optimized,” but it is unclear whether tuning was nested within CV. The same concern applies to classifier hyperparameters (SVM C/gamma, k in KNN, RF depth/trees). Non-nested tuning risks optimistic estimates on small datasets.
- Reporting and baselines: Only accuracy is reported, without class counts per fold, confidence intervals, or significance testing. F1, AUC, and variance across folds would make conclusions more reliable. Baselines omit modern approaches (e.g., pretrained fastText/word2vec/GloVe embeddings with linear models; simple neural sequence models; acoustic features commonly used in this domain). The strong BoW baseline in ABCD highlights the need for stronger comparisons.
- Data clarity and reproducibility: Counts for Cookie Theft controls are unclear (Table 1 is missing here), and the provenance/training corpora for embeddings are unspecified. If embeddings are trained on the full dataset (including test folds), this introduces representation leakage; using large external corpora or training only on training folds is preferable. No code, feature lists, or fixed splits are provided, limiting reproducibility.
- Presentation issues: Some figure/table references are inconsistent; details like the number of features per view, average network density before/after enrichment, or sensitivity to the similarity threshold are missing. These would help interpretability.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you ensure subject-level separation in all datasets, especially ABCD where each subject contributes two narratives? If not, please re-run with grouped CV and report updated results.
- Hyperparameter tuning: Were the similarity threshold and classifier hyperparameters tuned within a nested CV loop? If not, please provide nested CV results or a held-out validation protocol.
- Embeddings: On what corpora were the embeddings trained (English and Portuguese)? Were embeddings fit on the full dataset including test folds, or only the training folds, or external corpora? Please quantify OOV rates and report whether using pretrained fastText improves results.
- Demographics and confounds: How do results change when controlling for age/education (e.g., matched subsampling, including demographics as covariates, or regression-based residualization)? Can you report performance stratified by age/education bins?
- Class distributions and metrics: Please report exact class counts per dataset and provide precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals. Are the improvements of CNE over CN and LM statistically significant?
- Leakage/overlap in Cookie Theft/Cinderella: Are there repeated narrations or sessions per subject, and how did you avoid overlap across folds? Also, please clarify the control sample size in Cookie Theft and the final counts used.
- Sensitivity/ablation: How sensitive are results to the similarity threshold and to the choice of embeddings? How many edges are typically added by enrichment, and how does network density relate to performance? Can you ablate stopword removal or include function words, which are often informative in MCI?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem with an appealing, low-dependency approach and produces competitive results across datasets and languages. However, potential subject leakage, demographic confounds, non-nested tuning, and limited baselines weaken the strength of the claims. With clarified protocols, stronger baselines, and more rigorous reporting, the work could be a useful empirical contribution; as is, its methodological rigor and novelty fall short of the bar for acceptance.
