Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with semantic links derived from word embeddings (cosine similarity threshold), then uses topological features of these networks to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs control. It compares complex networks (CN), enriched complex networks (CNE), linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia), and bag-of-words (BoW) across three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture descriptions; Brazilian Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Brazilian Portuguese ABCD recall tasks), using several classifiers and majority-vote ensembles and multi-view combinations.
- My assessment of contributions (strongest first):
  1) Empirical evidence that embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks provide competitive and sometimes superior performance to traditional linguistic metrics and plain CN for MCI detection across three datasets, including Portuguese data with noisy transcripts.
  2) A simple, language-agnostic modeling pipeline that reduces reliance on parsers/annotated resources, evaluated across English and Brazilian Portuguese, including an automatically segmented setting.
  3) An exploration showing that combining CNE with LM and BoW via simple multi-view ensembling can further improve accuracy on some datasets.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, low-resource applicability: The approach avoids parsing/annotation and works on noisy clinical transcripts in English and Portuguese, including an automatically segmented dataset (ABCD). This is practically valuable where robust tools are lacking.
- Stronger modeling of short texts: Enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based edges directly targets the known brittleness of CN on short texts and shows consistent gains of CNE over CN across datasets.
- Broad comparison and ablations: The paper contrasts three distinct feature families (CNE, LM, BoW) and reports multiple classifiers, plus multi-view and ensemble variants. Results consistently highlight the added value of enrichment and, in ABCD, the benefit of combining views.
- Clinical relevance and accessible method: Early detection of MCI is impactful; the proposed features are simple to compute with off-the-shelf tools, potentially easing deployment.
- Evaluation under two preprocessing regimes: For Cinderella, testing both raw (no manual clean-up) and manually revised transcripts illustrates the brittleness of linguistic features and the relative robustness of CNE.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and confounds: Cross-validation is described as 5-fold at the narrative level, but ABCD includes two narratives per subject (immediate/delayed recalls). If folds are not made subject-disjoint, there is a risk of subject leakage, inflating accuracy. Additionally, the ABCD demographics are highly imbalanced (mean age 61 vs 72; gender imbalance). Without controlling for subject-level splits and confounds, observed gains may reflect subject characteristics rather than language markers of MCI.
- Incomplete baselines and metrics: The BoW baseline uses term frequency only, not tf-idf, and omits strong, simple baselines (e.g., logistic regression on tf-idf) and modern text classifiers (e.g., regularized linear models with n-grams, or transformer-based features). Reporting only accuracy (no precision/recall/F1/AUC) and no statistical significance testing makes it hard to judge the reliability of small differences (often 2–5%).
- Insufficient detail for reproducibility: The source corpora used to train word embeddings (English and Portuguese) are not specified; training only on small clinical transcripts would be inadequate, while using large external corpora could shift performance. Stopword lists, tokenization, vocabulary handling, and network construction details (e.g., handling multiword tokens, case, thresholds selection protocol) are underspecified. Code and data availability are not addressed.
- Hyperparameter selection may leak test information: The similarity threshold is “optimized with best values” reported per dataset; it is unclear if this is tuned on held-out data or via nested CV. Tuning on the full dataset prior to CV would bias results.
- Heterogeneous preprocessing across datasets: Different disfluency removal policies are applied to Cookie Theft vs Cinderella, and manual revisions are used in one variant. This complicates cross-dataset comparison and could affect which feature family appears stronger.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Inconsistent descriptions of the Cookie Theft selection (MCI “sampled from AD”), unclear control counts, figure references/typos, and table formatting errors hinder comprehension.

Questions to authors
- Splits: Are CV folds subject-disjoint in all datasets (especially ABCD with two narratives per subject)? If not, can you re-run with subject-level CV and report results?
- Cookie Theft composition: How many controls vs MCI? The text says 43 MCI “sampled from AD” which is confusing. Please clarify inclusion criteria and counts for both classes.
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train English and Portuguese embeddings (size, domain)? Did you train only on your transcripts or on large external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, news)? Please report OOV rates and whether subword alleviated them in practice.
- Threshold tuning: How was the cosine similarity threshold selected? Was nested CV used per dataset? Please provide the search grid and selection criterion.
- Preprocessing: Precisely which stopword lists, tokenization, and normalization (casing, numbers) were used per language? Were punctuation and filled pauses handled consistently across datasets?
- Features: Are CN/CNE features computed over entire narratives or per sentence then aggregated? How sensitive are results to sentence segmentation errors (e.g., using DeepBond vs gold)?
- Baselines: Can you include tf-idf + linear SVM/logistic regression and n-gram features? Also, a simple sentence-transformer or ClinicalBERT/XLM-R pooled embedding + linear classifier would contextualize the gains.
- Significance and metrics: Please report F1 (per class), AUC, and confidence intervals or paired tests across folds for key comparisons (CN vs CNE, best single-view vs multi-view).

Additional comments
- The multi-view combination is a simple majority vote; consider a calibrated stacking approach to learn per-view weights.
- Given demographic imbalances, a control analysis regressing out length and basic counts or matching subsets by age/education would strengthen claims that linguistic markers, not confounds, drive performance.
- Releasing code and detailed configs would substantially improve reproducibility.

Overall, the idea of enriching co-occurrence networks with embeddings for clinical speech classification is reasonable and practically appealing, but the current evaluation has methodological gaps (splits, baselines, reporting) that need to be addressed to support strong claims.
