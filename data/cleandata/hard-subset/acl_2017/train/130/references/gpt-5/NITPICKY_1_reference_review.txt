Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes representing speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks enriched with semantic links derived from word-embedding cosine similarity, and using topological network metrics as features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls.
- It evaluates this approach on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed recalls), comparing: (i) plain complex-network (CN) features, (ii) embedding-enriched CN (CNE), (iii) Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features, (iv) bag-of-words (BoW), and (v) simple majority-vote multi-view combinations.
- Claimed contributions are: (1) using embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI detection from transcripts; (2) showing CNE is competitive with standard features and sometimes improved with simple multi-view ensemble; (3) suggesting a minimally parser-dependent pipeline for noisy/disfluent transcripts.

Strengths
- Cross-language, cross-task evaluation: The paper evaluates on three datasets and two languages with different elicitation protocols (picture description, story retelling, immediate/delayed recall). This increases ecological validity beyond a single dataset/setting.
- Parser-light approach: The network-based features avoid dependency on fragile parsing for disfluent transcripts, relevant for clinical speech where ASR/transcription noise is common. The Portuguese Coh-Metrix-Dementia subset selection is also carefully motivated.
- Simple baseline comparisons: The study compares CNE against CN, linguistic metrics, and BoW with multiple classic classifiers, and shows that adding embedding-derived edges often helps over plain CN.
- Modest gains with multi-view voting: On two datasets the majority-vote combination across feature families yields the best reported accuracy (e.g., 80.18% on ABCD), suggesting complementary information.

Weaknesses
- Serious methodological clarity and rigor issues:
  - Subject-level data leakage risk: ABCD includes two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed). The paper does not state that cross-validation is stratified by subject; if folds mix a subject’s two narratives across train/test, results are inflated. Similar concerns apply to DementiaBank if multiple sessions per subject exist. This must be clarified and, if necessary, re-evaluated with subject-exclusive folds.
  - Threshold tuning for similarity links: The “best” cosine thresholds (0.7, 0.4) appear selected on the evaluation data without nested cross-validation. This risks optimistic bias. Provide details of the tuning protocol; redo with nested CV or separate dev sets.
  - Embedding training corpus unspecified: It is unclear what corpora were used to train the fastText-style embeddings for English and Portuguese. If embeddings were trained on the task transcripts (small, label-correlated domain), that introduces leakage/overfitting and poor embedding quality. If trained externally, specify corpora, tokenization, and language-specific models.
  - No control for length and demographics: Network topology is highly sensitive to graph size; cohorts differ in age/education (e.g., ABCD shows a notable age gap), and narratives differ in length. There is no normalization by number of nodes/edges, nor regression/control for age/education. Accuracy gains may reflect confounds rather than cognitive impairment per se.
  - Evaluation metrics and significance: Only accuracy is reported, despite potential class imbalance and small sample sizes. No confidence intervals or statistical significance testing (e.g., McNemar, permutation tests, bootstrap) is provided. Improvements of a few points may not be statistically meaningful.

- Baselines and modeling choices are weak/outdated for text classification:
  - BoW uses raw term frequency; tf–idf and tuned linear SVM (with C tuning) are standard strong baselines; here BoW-linear SVM already reaches 75% on ABCD. More modern baselines (e.g., averaged word vectors, doc2vec, or (frozen) sentence-transformer/BERT embeddings with linear classifiers) are absent, limiting claims of competitiveness.
  - “Multi-view learning” is a misnomer here; the method is simple majority vote across feature spaces without learned view weights or co-training. Claims should be toned down or a principled multi-view method provided.

- Preprocessing inconsistency across datasets:
  - The paper removes different disfluency types in different corpora (e.g., Cookie Theft vs. Cinderella), and for Portuguese also evaluates a manually “revised” version. These inconsistencies confound comparisons and claims about automation. A uniform preprocessing pipeline should be enforced for core results; the “revised” variant can be an auxiliary analysis.
  - Stopword removal for CN may discard syntactic information particularly relevant in clinical language; this choice is not justified empirically (no ablation).

- Reporting and reproducibility gaps:
  - No code or precise feature counts (for CN features: which metrics are local vs global? Are means/SD/skew computed for all? Are graphs weighted?), no random seeds, no classifier hyperparameter grids, no fold definitions, and no data splits released.
  - Key dataset details are missing/unclear: Cookie Theft controls count; whether longitudinal samples per subject exist; exact transcript filtering.

- Numerous formatting, citation, and editorial issues detract from professionalism:
  - Figure numbering mismatch (“Figure 1 shows … Figure 2: …”), missing Table 1, inconsistent decimal separators (72,0), typos (“parametrs”, “syntatic”, “vertice”, “cooccurrence”), inconsistent hyphenation and capitalization, malformed references with backslashes and placeholders (e.g., “Aluı́sio et al.,? \Q2016\E”), stray URLs/footnotes, author names with punctuation errors. These need substantial cleanup.

Questions to authors
- Splitting protocol: Were cross-validation folds constructed at the subject level for all datasets (especially ABCD immediate/delayed retells)? If not, please re-run with subject-exclusive folds and report updated results.
- Threshold selection: How was the cosine similarity threshold tuned? Was tuning nested within the CV or on a held-out dev set? Provide the search space and procedure.
- Embeddings: What corpora (size, domain) and preprocessing were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese? Did training include the target transcripts? If external, please specify models or provide links.
- Normalization and confounds: How do results change when controlling for narrative length (e.g., by matching lengths, using length-normalized features, or including length as a covariate) and for age/education via stratification or regression?
- Baselines: What is the performance of tf–idf with tuned linear SVM, and of simple averaged word vectors or sentence embeddings with a linear classifier? Please add these to contextualize the competitiveness of CNE.
- Reproducibility: Can you release code, full preprocessing scripts, feature extraction settings, and exact train/dev/test splits?
- CN feature details: Which metrics are treated as local vs global? Are edges binary or weighted by co-occurrence/similarity? Any sensitivity analysis over the threshold and over inclusion/exclusion of stopwords?
