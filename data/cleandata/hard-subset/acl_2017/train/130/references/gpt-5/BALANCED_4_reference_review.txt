Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with semantic edges derived from word embeddings to mitigate sparsity in short/disfluent clinical narratives. It extracts topological network metrics as features, compares them to linguistic (Coh-Metrix variants) and BoW features, and evaluates across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Brazilian Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD). It also explores multi-view ensembles over feature families.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Methodological: Enriching word co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges and demonstrating their utility for MCI detection in short, noisy transcripts across two languages.
2) Empirical: Multi-dataset evaluation indicating that enriched network (CNE) features are competitive with or better than traditional linguistic features on several settings, and that multi-view learning can yield further gains.
3) Practical insight: The network approach reduces reliance on heavy linguistic preprocessing/resources and appears robust to some transcript disfluencies and short text length.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation with realistic, noisy clinical data. The study spans English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD), which strengthens the generality of the findings and tests robustness to different elicitation protocols and transcript properties.
- Sensible modeling innovation tailored to short, disfluent texts. Adding semantic edges based on cosine similarity of embeddings directly addresses the near-linear topology of short text networks, a documented limitation of pure co-occurrence networks. The reported gains of CNE over CN in most settings support this design choice.
- Competitive performance and complementary information. On Cookie Theft and the raw Cinderella dataset, CNE features outperform linguistic metrics in several classifiers, and multi-view combinations (e.g., CNE+LM, CNE+LM+BoW) improve accuracy further (up to 75% on Cinderella and 80% on ABCD). This suggests the network topology captures information not fully covered by Coh-Metrix or BoW.
- Reduced dependence on brittle NLP preprocessing. The approach avoids parsing and lemmatization, which are problematic on disfluent speech and for Portuguese. This makes the method attractive for low-resource clinical settings.
- Clear articulation of feature design and network measures. The paper enumerates ten network metrics and summarizes per-node statistics (mean, std, skewness), making the feature construction relatively transparent.

Weaknesses
- Potential evaluation leakage and confounds. ABCD includes two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed recall); the paper does not state that cross-validation is grouped by speaker. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test folds, accuracy can be inflated. Additionally, ABCD demographics are markedly different across groups (e.g., age 61 vs 72, education 16 vs 13.3), creating strong confounds. No controls (matching, covariate adjustment, or stratification) are described.
- Limited reporting and statistical rigor. Results are reported as accuracy only, with no confidence intervals, variance across folds, or significance testing. Given small sample sizes (e.g., 40 subjects in Cinderella), effect sizes and uncertainty matter. Class balance is not consistently reported, nor are per-class metrics (F1, AUC, sensitivity/specificity), which are important in clinical screening.
- Incomplete baseline coverage and overclaims. While Coh-Metrix and BoW are reasonable baselines, stronger modern baselines are missing (e.g., sentence/document embeddings, pretrained transformer-based representations, or simple averaged word vectors). Moreover, the general claim that “among the three types, CNE provided the highest accuracies” is contradicted on ABCD, where BoW + linear SVM (75.5%) outperforms CNE (71.7%) in single-view.
- Hyperparameter tuning and reproducibility details are under-specified. The similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) are “optimized” but the tuning protocol is unclear (nested CV vs. tuning on the same folds). SVM kernels and C values, K in KNN, RF settings, and feature scaling/normalization are not detailed. Embedding training corpora are unspecified; stating only “default parameters” is insufficient for replication, especially across languages.
- Limited analysis/interpretability. The paper does not analyze which network metrics drive performance, how enrichment changes graph properties across classes, or provide qualitative error analysis. Without this, it is hard to assess the clinical relevance or understand failure modes.
- Data preprocessing inconsistencies and potential biases. Different datasets undergo different preprocessing (manual vs. automatic segmentation, disfluency removal policies). This complicates comparability and may favor certain feature families.

Questions to authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: For ABCD (two narratives per subject), did you perform subject-level (grouped) CV to avoid train-test contamination? If not, can you re-run with grouped splits and report the change?
2) Confound control: How did you address large age/education differences in ABCD? Can you report results with matched subsamples, stratified CV, or models that include demographics as covariates to test robustness?
3) Threshold tuning: Was the cosine similarity threshold selected via inner (nested) CV per training fold, or tuned on the full data? Please clarify and, if needed, provide nested-CV results.
4) Embeddings: What training corpora were used for English and Portuguese embeddings (size, domain)? Were embeddings trained solely on the small task data or on large external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, Common Crawl)? Are embeddings shared for reproducibility?
5) Feature processing: Were features standardized within folds? What were the SVM C and gamma ranges, K in KNN, and RF hyperparameters? Any feature selection or dimensionality reduction?
6) Subject-level metrics: Can you report sensitivity/specificity, macro-F1, and AUC, and provide per-fold variance or confidence intervals?
7) Baselines: Have you compared against simple neural text representations (e.g., averaging pretrained word embeddings, doc2vec, or sentence-BERT/other sentence encoders) or fine-tuned transformers on the transcript text? If not, can you add such baselines?
8) Interpretability: Which network metrics most distinguish MCI vs control? Does enrichment mainly increase clustering, reduce diameter, or change assortativity differently across classes?

Decision
Reject (borderline). The idea of embedding-enriched text networks for clinical transcript classification is interesting and the multi-dataset evaluation is promising. However, methodological concerns (possible subject leakage, demographic confounds), limited statistical rigor, and missing strong baselines undermine the reliability of the reported gains. Addressing these issues, clarifying/tightening the experimental protocol (grouped/nested CV, confound control), adding modern baselines, and providing more analysis would substantially strengthen the work.
