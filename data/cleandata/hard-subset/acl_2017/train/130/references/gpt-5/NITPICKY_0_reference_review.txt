Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding-based similarity edges, extracts topological features, and classifies MCI vs control across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD). It compares complex-network (CN/CNE) features to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix variants) and bag-of-words (BoW), and explores ensemble and multi-view majority voting.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Application of complex network features enriched with word embeddings to the clinical task of detecting MCI from speech transcripts in English and Portuguese, with cross-dataset comparisons.
2) Empirical finding that enriched CN features can be competitive with linguistic metrics and BoW, and that simple multi-view majority voting can improve accuracy in some settings.
3) A methodological variant of co-occurrence networks by adding cosine-similarity edges between words within each transcript.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation. Testing on three datasets across two languages (with both picture description and narrative recall) is valuable and relatively rare in this area, adding external validity beyond a single corpus.
- Simple, resource-lean approach. The CN/CNE pipeline avoids heavy reliance on high-accuracy syntactic parsing, which is a practical advantage for noisy speech transcripts and under-resourced languages.
- Clear, intuitive idea to densify short-text networks. Enriching sparse co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based similarity edges is a reasonable way to mitigate the near-linear structure of very short texts; the idea is easy to reproduce in principle.
- Inclusion of multiple feature families and learners. The side-by-side comparison of CNE vs CN vs Coh-Metrix vs BoW, and several classifiers, gives a broad picture of where the proposed features sit relative to familiar baselines.
- Some positive results. The best accuracies (e.g., ABCD multi-view 0.80; Cinderella manual 0.75) are within the range of prior work and indicate potential utility.

Weaknesses
- Serious evaluation design concerns and likely leakage. For ABCD, two narratives per subject are included; for Cookie Theft/DementiaBank there are often multiple sessions per participant. The paper does not state that cross-validation is grouped by subject. If narratives from the same speaker appear in train and test folds, results are inflated. This must be fixed (subject-level CV) and reported. Similarly, selection of the similarity threshold (“optimized with best values being 0.7/0.4”) is not described; if tuned on the whole dataset before CV, that is also leakage.
- Insufficient metrics and statistics. Only accuracy is reported, no class distributions (some are imbalanced), no precision/recall/F1, no AUC, no confidence intervals, no significance tests, and no variance across folds. Many differences are small (2–3%) and may be non-significant.
- Confounds and inconsistent preprocessing. Demographics in ABCD differ markedly (e.g., age 61 vs 72; M/F imbalance). Preprocessing differs across datasets (e.g., types of disfluencies removed/kept), undermining comparability. No analysis controls for transcript length, which strongly affects network topology; length-normalized baselines are not provided.
- Methodological underspecification. Key details are missing: embeddings were “trained with default parameters”—on what corpora, per language, with what vocabulary size? For the network: undirected vs directed rationale; were edges weighted or binary (Aij=1 loses frequency info); handling of disconnected graphs for diameter/shortest paths; whether global measures (assortativity, diameter) were included in the mean/std/skew summaries; total feature dimensionality; whether features were normalized. Computational aspects are unclear: checking all pairs for cosine > threshold is O(n^2)—was k-NN pruning used?
- Limited novelty and weak baselines. Adding embedding-similarity edges follows prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al.). The primary novelty is the application to MCI. Strong modern baselines (e.g., TF–IDF with regularized linear models, doc2vec, sentence/paragraph embeddings, transformer-based representations, simple length-controlled features) are absent. Using raw TF rather than TF–IDF is a weak text baseline; yet linear SVM on BoW performs very well, suggesting the proposed method’s edge is limited.
- Reproducibility and clarity issues. Figures/tables are inconsistently referenced (e.g., Figure 1/2 mismatch); typos and formatting issues abound; some tables (e.g., Cookie Theft demographics) are missing; reference formatting is broken in places. Code, splits, and detailed feature definitions are not provided.

Questions to authors
1) Did you perform subject-level cross-validation for all datasets (i.e., no subject appears in both train and test folds)? If not, please re-run with grouped CV and report updated metrics.
2) How were cosine similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) selected? Was tuning performed inside each training fold only? Provide sensitivity analysis of threshold and of embedding dimensionality/window size.
3) On what corpora were fastText embeddings trained for English and Portuguese? Corpus sizes, token counts, vocabulary coverage? Did you pretrain on large external corpora or only on your datasets (which would be too small)?
4) How many CNE features are used in total? For local measures, do you concatenate mean/std/skew? For global measures (e.g., diameter, assortativity), how are they handled? Are features normalized by graph size (nodes/edges) to mitigate transcript length effects?
5) For ABCD, are the two retellings from each subject both included? If so, how did you prevent fold leakage at the subject level?
6) Why remove stopwords in building adjacency networks, given they stabilize network topology in short texts? Please report results with and without stopword removal.
7) Why use undirected, unweighted adjacency (Aij=1) instead of directed/weighted edges that preserve order and frequency? Please justify or provide ablation.
8) Please report class distributions per dataset, and add F1 (macro/micro), AUC, and per-fold variance or confidence intervals. Are improvements significant (e.g., McNemar/Wilcoxon across folds)?

Given the potential leakage, lack of statistical rigor, missing methodological detail, and limited novelty beyond application, I cannot recommend acceptance in current form, despite the promising cross-lingual application and some encouraging accuracies. Addressing the evaluation flaws and strengthening baselines/analysis would substantially improve the work.
