Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them with semantic edges derived from word embeddings (fastText) to mitigate the sparsity/linearity of short texts. It extracts topological features (e.g., PageRank, betweenness, degree, clustering, etc., aggregated via mean/std/skewness) and compares them to linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and BoW on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed recall), using several classifiers and simple ensemble/multi-view majority voting. The authors report 60–80% accuracies, with the best results typically achieved by multi-view combinations.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Application and adaptation of complex network features enriched with embedding-based similarity edges to MCI detection from transcripts in English and Portuguese, including datasets with limited preprocessing.
2) Empirical finding that embedding-enriched complex network features are competitive with or better than conventional linguistic metrics across some datasets, and that multi-view combinations can improve accuracy.
3) Practical insight into modeling short, disfluent clinical transcripts without heavy manual normalization, leveraging fastText subword embeddings to handle OOV.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant, underexplored clinical NLP setting: The focus on fully-automated analysis of disfluent, short clinical transcripts (no dependency on robust parsers or extensive manual normalization) is practically important, especially for Portuguese where NLP resources are less mature. The use of fastText to reduce OOV effects is a sensible design choice in this context.
- Methodological adaptation for short texts: Enriching sparse co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges is a reasonable and well-motivated way to make network topology informative for very short/disfluent narratives, consistent with prior observations that plain adjacency networks on short texts are near-linear.
- Multi-dataset, bilingual evaluation: Results on three datasets spanning two languages and two elicitation paradigms (picture description and story recall) improve external face validity. The inclusion of both manually revised and non-revised versions for Cinderella offers an informative comparison of reliance on preprocessing.
- Competitive baselines and feature families: The paper compares three distinct feature families (network, linguistic, BoW) across several classifiers, and the BoW baseline is non-trivial (indeed strong on ABCD), which grounds the empirical claims.
- Clarity of the core modeling idea: The construction of CNE (adjacency plus similarity edges via cosine threshold) and the suite of ten graph measures with aggregation statistics is clearly described, making the approach understandable and potentially reproducible.

Weaknesses
- Potential confounds and split leakage: The ABCD dataset has large demographic imbalances (age differs by ~11 years; gender distribution differs markedly; education differs). No controls (covariate matching, including demographics as features, or stratified subject-level CV) are reported. Moreover, ABCD includes two narratives per subject (immediate/delayed recall); it is unclear whether folds were split by subject, risking leakage. These issues can inflate accuracies by capturing age/subject idiosyncrasies rather than MCI.
- Insufficient experimental rigor and reporting: Only accuracy is reported; no per-class F1, sensitivity/specificity (critical in clinical screening), confidence intervals, or significance tests. Hyperparameter selection (e.g., SVM C/γ), feature scaling, and model selection protocols (nested CV) are unspecified. The cosine similarity threshold is “optimized” but the procedure and validation regime are unclear (risking test fold contamination).
- Overstated claims about CNE superiority: The statement that “enriched networks provided the highest accuracies” is not consistently supported. For ABCD, BoW with linear SVM (0.7547) exceeds CNE (0.7173); for Cinderella (manually processed), LM with SVM-RBF (0.7250) exceeds CNE (0.6750). The paper should temper claims and provide statistical comparisons.
- Limited novelty beyond application: Constructing networks augmented with embedding-based similarity has prior art (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The technical novelty here is limited; the contribution is mainly an application to MCI detection with empirical evaluation. Stronger methodological novelty (e.g., principled edge weighting/normalization, learned thresholds, or graph neural encoders) is lacking.
- Ambiguities and inconsistencies in datasets and evaluation: The Cookie Theft description is confusing (e.g., “43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”). The number of controls and their selection are not fully specified. The multi-view tables use dashes under individual classifiers yet report multi-view performance only in the “Ensemble” row, which is conceptually inconsistent and needs clarification. The choice of tf (vs. tf–idf) for BoW is not justified.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: No ablation quantifies the marginal gain from enrichment over plain CN across thresholds; no analysis of stopword removal (function words can be discriminatory in cognitive impairment); no comparison to modern embedding baselines (e.g., averaging sentence/utterance embeddings, doc2vec, or transformer-based pooled embeddings); no robustness checks to ASR errors.

Questions to authors
- Splits and leakage: For ABCD (two narratives per subject), did you enforce subject-exclusive folds? For all datasets, were folds stratified by subject? If not, please re-run with subject-level CV.
- Demographics: Given the strong age, gender, and education imbalances (especially ABCD), did you control for these covariates (e.g., matching, including as features, or stratified CV)? How do results change when controlling for demographics?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train fastText embeddings (size, domain, language)? Were embeddings trained solely on the small task transcripts (which would be problematic) or on large external corpora? Did you freeze embeddings?
- Threshold selection: How was the cosine threshold tuned (grid, validation protocol)? Was tuning nested within CV to avoid test fold peeking? Any sensitivity curve showing performance vs. threshold?
- Feature engineering details: What is the final dimensionality of the network feature vector (which measures included as global vs. aggregated local)? Were features standardized within training folds? What SVM and RF hyperparameters were used and how tuned?
- Baselines and metrics: Why use tf rather than tf–idf for BoW? Can you report macro-F1, sensitivity, specificity, and confidence intervals? Any statistical tests comparing CNE vs. BoW/LM?
- Dataset clarifications: For Cookie Theft, please clarify the exact composition of MCI vs. control and the selection from the larger AD cohort. Were the TalkBank disfluency removal rules applied uniformly across languages?

Overall recommendation
The application is timely and the idea of enriching word co-occurrence networks with embeddings for short clinical transcripts is sensible. However, the current experimental design has serious confounding risks (demographics, subject-level leakage), limited reporting, and overstated claims relative to the presented evidence. I encourage a substantial revision addressing split protocols, confound control, evaluation metrics, and ablations, and adding stronger baselines before consideration.
