Summary
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word embeddings to mitigate sparsity and the near-linearity of networks in short, noisy clinical narratives. It extracts topological graph features and combines them with linguistic metrics and bag-of-words in a multi-view/ensemble setup to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. control across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD). Enriched network features are generally competitive or superior to linguistic metrics; multi-view fusion achieves the best results (up to 80% accuracy on ABCD), while avoiding costly manual preprocessing.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Conceptual/architectural: Enriching word co-occurrence networks with distributional semantics (fastText-like embeddings) for clinical speech analysis, addressing the well-known weakness of network topology on short texts. This is a clear, bold idea that bridges complex networks and embeddings in an underexplored clinical setting.
2) Empirical evidence across languages and tasks that network topology features (with embedding enrichment) can rival or synergize with traditional linguistic metrics without heavy parsing or manual cleanup, enabling a more automation-friendly pipeline.
3) A practical multi-view fusion of heterogeneous feature families (graph metrics, linguistic metrics, BoW), showing robust gains over any single view.

Strengths
- Novel and promising direction: The embedding-enriched network representation is an elegant, minimally engineered way to inject semantics into sparse co-occurrence graphs, directly targeting the “small text linearity” problem. To my knowledge, applying this to MCI detection from clinical narratives is new and impactful.
- Automation-friendly and language-agnostic: The approach avoids dependency on high-precision parsers or manual revision, which is crucial for noisy transcripts with disfluencies and for languages lacking robust NLP tools. Demonstrations on English and Brazilian Portuguese strengthen the generality claim.
- Consistent competitive performance: Enriched CN (CNE) often surpasses plain CN and is competitive with (and sometimes better than) linguistic metrics. Multi-view fusion delivers the best accuracies: 65% (Cookie Theft), 75% (Cinderella), 80% (ABCD), with clear, interpretable gains over single-view baselines.
- Sensible engineering choices: Use of subword embeddings (fastText-style) for OOV robustness, and summarizing local graph measures via mean/std/skewness makes the feature space simple and relatively interpretable—appealing for clinical stakeholders.
- Clear motivation and analysis of the limitation: The paper explicitly diagnoses why standard CNs underperform on short transcripts and justifies the embedding enrichment with intuitive illustrations.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation rigor and potential leakage: The 5-fold cross-validation protocol is underspecified. Are splits speaker-independent? With multiple narratives per subject (ABCD), per-narrative CV risks identity leakage. Thresholds for similarity edges appear tuned on the same data; clarify whether tuning is nested to avoid overfitting.
- Confounders and demographics: ABCD groups differ notably in age and education. No control for covariates (e.g., regression/stratification) is reported. Accuracy could reflect demographic confounds rather than cognitive status; at minimum, report models controlling for demographics or matched subsets.
- Limited baselines and metrics: BoW uses tf only; tf-idf and simple regularized linear models (e.g., logistic regression) are standard strong baselines. No comparison to modern pretrained text encoders (even frozen-BERT sentence averages on such small data) or simple neural baselines. Only accuracy is reported; provide precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals or significance tests.
- Missing modeling details/reproducibility: Embedding training corpora and language-specific training details are vague (size, domain match, tokenization, stopword policy). Graph construction choices are under-specified (directed vs. undirected, weighted vs. unweighted, how semantic and co-occurrence edges are combined, and whether features are size-normalized to control for narrative length).
- Fusion method is simplistic: Multi-view fusion is by majority vote, despite text stating weights “can be learned by a regression method.” A learned late-fusion or simple stacking (with proper CV) could be stronger and more principled. An ablation of fusion choices would strengthen the case.

Questions to authors
- Data splits: Are CV folds speaker-independent across all datasets, particularly ABCD where each subject has two narratives? If not, please re-run with subject-level splits.
- Threshold tuning: How were similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) selected? Was tuning nested within CV? How sensitive are results to this hyperparameter?
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train the embeddings for English and Portuguese (size, domain, preprocessing)? Did you try off-the-shelf fastText vectors, and how do results compare?
- Graph construction: Are graphs weighted and/or directed? How are co-occurrence and semantic edges combined (union, weights, normalization)? Do you normalize topological measures by graph size or degree distributions to control for transcript length?
- Baselines: How do results change with tf-idf + linear classifier, and with a simple pretrained encoder (e.g., averaging multilingual transformer embeddings per sentence and pooling per narrative)?
- Metrics/significance: Please report F1, per-class recall, and confidence intervals; include permutation tests or paired tests across folds.
- Confounds: Can you report performance after controlling for age/education (e.g., adding them as covariates, matching, or residualizing features)?

Overall, I find the core idea original and promising for clinical NLP under realistic constraints. With clarifications and stronger evaluation controls, this work would make a valuable contribution.
