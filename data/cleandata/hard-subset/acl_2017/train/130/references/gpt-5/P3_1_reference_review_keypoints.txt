- Paper models speech transcripts as adjacency (co-occurrence) networks enriched with fastText-based semantic edges and extracts aggregated topological features for MCI detection.
- Evaluates CNE features against Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia linguistic features and BoW on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed) using several classifiers and simple ensemble voting.
- Main claimed contributions: applying embedding-enriched complex network features to MCI detection in English and Portuguese, showing competitive performance versus linguistic metrics, and demonstrating practicality for noisy short clinical transcripts using fastText to reduce OOV.
- Strength: Focuses on a relevant, underexplored clinical NLP setting—fully automated analysis of short, disfluent transcripts without heavy preprocessing, especially valuable for Portuguese.
- Strength: Methodologically sensible adaptation—adding embedding-based similarity edges makes network topology informative for very short/disfluent texts.
- Strength: Multi-dataset, bilingual evaluation across different elicitation paradigms and inclusion of both manually revised and non-revised transcripts improves external face validity.
- Strength: Compares three distinct feature families and uses reasonable baselines; BoW baseline is non-trivial and strong on some datasets.
- Strength: CNE construction and the set of graph measures (with aggregation statistics) are clearly described and reproducible.
- Weakness: Potential confounds and leakage—ABCD has major demographic imbalances and may have subject-level leakage since each subject contributes two narratives; no controls or subject-exclusive folds reported.
- Weakness: Insufficient reporting—only accuracy reported; missing per-class F1, sensitivity/specificity, confidence intervals, significance tests, and unclear hyperparameter/model selection protocols.
- Weakness: Unclear and possibly improper threshold tuning for cosine similarity (procedure and validation regime not specified), risking test contamination.
- Weakness: Claims of CNE superiority are overstated—BoW or linguistic metrics outperform CNE on some datasets and no statistical comparisons are provided.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—embedding-augmented networks exist in prior work; technical novelty is mainly application rather than new methodology.
- Weakness: Ambiguities and inconsistencies in dataset descriptions and evaluation reporting (Cookie Theft composition unclear, inconsistent table reporting, unexplained BoW tf choice).
- Weakness: Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses—no direct comparison to plain CN, no stopword analysis, no modern embedding baselines (doc2vec/transformers), and no robustness checks to ASR errors.
- Key question: Were folds split by subject (especially for ABCD) to avoid leakage, and were folds stratified by subject across datasets?
- Key question: Were demographic covariates controlled (matching, included as features, or stratified CV), and how do results change when controlling for them?
- Key question: What corpora were used to train fastText embeddings, were they trained on task transcripts or external corpora, and were embeddings frozen?
- Key question: How was the cosine threshold selected and was tuning nested within CV; can you provide sensitivity curves for threshold vs. performance?
- Key question: What is the final dimensionality of the network feature vector, which measures were aggregated, and were features standardized within training folds?
- Key question: What hyperparameters were used for SVM/RF and how were they tuned?
- Key question: Why use term frequency (tf) instead of tf–idf for BoW, and can you report macro-F1, sensitivity, specificity, confidence intervals, and statistical tests comparing methods?
- Overall recommendation: Idea is sensible and timely but current experiments have serious confounding/leakage risks, limited reporting, and overstated claims—substantial revision required (split protocols, confound control, metrics, ablations, stronger baselines).
