Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks and enriches them by adding edges between semantically similar words based on word embeddings (fastText-like), to address sparsity in short texts. It then extracts a set of topological features (and their distributional stats) for MCI vs Control classification across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD). It compares against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and BoW, and explores ensemble and multi-view (majority vote) combinations.
- Claimed contributions: (1) Use of complex network features enriched with embeddings for MCI detection from transcripts; (2) empirical comparison showing these features are competitive or superior to linguistic metrics; (3) practical pipeline aiming to avoid heavy manual preprocessing and demonstrating multi-view/ensemble gains.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual evaluation on three datasets, including two Portuguese corpora where NLP tool support is limited. This broadens applicability beyond DementiaBank English studies and shows attention to practical settings.
- Focus on minimally supervised features that avoid dependence on brittle parsing seems prudent for disfluent transcripts. The attempt to mitigate short-text network linearity by enrichment is sensible in principle.
- Inclusion of multiple baselines (Coh-Metrix variants, BoW, several classifiers) and reporting across views; SVM linear on BoW performing strongly is a useful reality check for this domain.
- The paper highlights operational considerations (ASR-like sentence segmentation; automatic disfluency removal for some datasets), which is relevant for building deployable systems.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and rigor are insufficient, risking inflated or uninterpretable results.
  - Data leakage risk: ABCD has two narratives per subject; 5-fold CV without subject-wise splitting can leak speaker identity across folds. Similarly, it is unclear whether Cookie Theft and Cinderella folds are subject-independent. This can substantially overestimate accuracy.
  - Threshold tuning: “threshold parameter was optimized with best values being…” but no description of search space, and whether tuning was nested inside CV. Tuning on the full data (or on test folds) would leak information.
  - Metric choice: only accuracy is reported. Given potential class imbalance and clinical relevance, report per-class precision/recall, macro-F1, AUC, and confidence intervals or significance tests (e.g., McNemar/permutation). Many reported gains are small (e.g., 2–4%), with no significance established.

- Data description inconsistencies and missing critical details compromise reproducibility and validity.
  - Cookie Theft: counts unclear. You state 43 MCI transcripts “sampled from 326 probable AD; 21 possible AD.” How are controls defined and how many control transcripts were used? Table 1 is referenced but not present. What is the total number per class used in classification?
  - Embeddings: You “trained the model proposed by Bojanowski et al. with default parameters.” On what corpora (size, domain, language(s))? Training embeddings on tiny clinical transcripts would be inadequate; using large external corpora raises domain shift questions. Reproducibility requires corpora and training details.
  - Preprocessing: Stopword and punctuation removal is applied before network construction, yet function words are highly informative in dementia. Justify this choice and quantify its impact (ablation). Disfluency removal is inconsistently applied across datasets; exact rules/tools for removal are not provided.

- Novelty is limited relative to prior work. The word-embedding-based network enrichment closely mirrors Perozzi et al. (2014). Using complex networks for text classification is established; the primary novelty seems the application to MCI detection and a simple hybrid of co-occurrence plus similarity edges. Multi-view via majority vote is a basic combination scheme without principled weighting/learning. The contribution is more empirical than methodological.

- Analysis is shallow; confounds are not controlled.
  - Network features are strongly correlated with document length and density. Groups differ in mean sentences/words; gains may reflect length differences rather than cognitive markers. No length-controlled experiments or normalization are reported.
  - No sensitivity analysis to the similarity threshold, number of added edges, or alternative enrichment schemes (e.g., k-NN per node rather than global threshold). The potential densification effect on centrality measures is unexamined.
  - No ablation of individual topological measures or summary statistics (mean/std/skewness), nor comparison to trivial baselines (length-only, sentence count, type-token ratio). BoW linear SVM outperforms in ABCD, suggesting simple lexical content dominates.

- Presentation and formatting issues are pervasive.
  - Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “i Cancho,” “vertice,” “parametrs,” inconsistent figure/table numbering (Figure 1 vs Figure 2), duplicated table labels (Tables 5/6 both for Cinderella), missing Table 1, awkward spacing, and broken references with placeholders (question marks, escaped tokens). Figures are referenced but not shown. This reduces readability and confidence.

Questions to authors
- Splits and leakage: For each dataset, were folds stratified by subject (i.e., all narratives from a subject in one fold)? If not, please redo with subject-wise nested CV and report updated metrics. Also clarify class distributions per fold.
- Threshold tuning: Specify the tuning protocol (search grid, objective, and whether nested CV was used). Provide sensitivity curves of accuracy vs threshold for each dataset.
- Embeddings: What corpora (size, domain) did you use per language? Did you train from scratch or use pre-trained fastText? Provide training details and release embeddings for reproducibility. How do results change if embeddings are fixed vs fine-tuned vs trained only on transcripts?
- Preprocessing: Precisely list which disfluencies were removed and how (rules/tooling). Did you remove stopwords for all views (including BoW and Coh-Metrix)? Provide ablations with/without stopword/punctuation removal and with/without disfluency removal.
- Controls and counts: For Cookie Theft, how many controls were included, and by what inclusion criteria? Please include the missing demographic Table 1 and exact per-class sample sizes used in experiments.
- Evaluation metrics: Please report macro-F1, per-class precision/recall, and statistical significance tests for key comparisons. Also provide a trivial length-based baseline and a subject-level majority baseline.

Given the methodological ambiguities (potential leakage), limited novelty, and presentation problems, I cannot recommend acceptance in the current form despite the promising cross-lingual empirical angle.
