Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with semantic edges derived from word embeddings, and uses topological graph features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs control. It evaluates English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella narratives; ABCD recall) datasets, compares complex network features (CN/CNE) against linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix(-Dementia)) and Bag-of-Words (BoW), and explores multi-view and ensemble combinations. Reported 5-fold CV accuracies peak at 0.65 (Cookie Theft), 0.75 (Cinderella), 0.75 (Cinderella revised), and 0.80 (ABCD).

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Application of embedding-enriched word co-occurrence networks to model short, noisy clinical transcripts and use their topology for MCI detection, showing these features are competitive with standard linguistic features.
2) Empirical evidence across two languages and three tasks that multi-view fusion of CNE, linguistic metrics, and BoW can improve classification accuracy.
3) Practical insight that embedding-enriched networks mitigate the limitations of network topology on very short texts.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, low-resource friendly approach: The network-based features require minimal linguistic preprocessing and no syntactic parsing, making them appealing in noisy, disfluent clinical transcripts and for languages with limited NLP tools. The method shows utility in both English and Portuguese settings.
- Addressing short-text sparsity: Enriching co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based edges is a simple, effective way to add semantic structure when co-occurrence graphs devolve into chains on short texts. CNE consistently improves over CN across datasets (e.g., Cookie Theft SVM-RBF: 0.626 vs 0.569).
- Competitive performance and useful fusion: CNE matches or exceeds linguistic metrics in several settings and multi-view combinations yield the best scores (e.g., ABCD SVM-linear CNE-LM-BoW 0.802; Cookie Theft multi-view 0.650; Cinderella ensemble CNE-LM 0.750).
- Methodological simplicity and interpretability: Topological measures (PageRank, betweenness, clustering, assortativity, etc.) offer interpretable signals about structural properties of discourse and may be more explainable to clinicians than opaque neural models.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject-level data leakage: In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate and delayed recall). The paper uses 5-fold CV but does not state whether folds are partitioned by subject. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test folds, accuracy can be substantially inflated. This is a critical validity threat that must be addressed. The same concern applies to any dataset with multiple samples per subject.
- Incomplete dataset description and class balance reporting: Cookie Theft counts for controls vs MCI are unclear (only “43 MCI transcripts” are specified), while Table 1 is not included here. Precise per-dataset sample sizes per class, number of narratives per subject, and whether multiple sessions per subject exist are necessary to assess difficulty and balance. Without this, accuracy is hard to interpret.
- Weak and inconsistent baselines: BoW uses term frequency only; tf-idf and n-gram features (often strong baselines for short clinical texts) are not reported. No neural text baselines (e.g., averaged word embeddings, doc2vec, or lightweight transformer fine-tuning) are included. Given that BoW SVM-linear already yields 0.755 on ABCD, stronger standard baselines could change the conclusions about relative gains.
- Evaluation methodology and statistics: Only accuracy is reported. Clinical datasets are small and slightly imbalanced; precision/recall/F1 per class, ROC-AUC, and confidence intervals or significance tests are needed. Hyperparameters (similarity threshold .7/.4) appear tuned on the same data used for evaluation without nested CV, risking overfitting. No ablations quantify the individual contribution of embedding enrichment vs plain CN vs feature statistics (mean/std/skew).
- Reproducibility and missing training corpus details: The paper trains subword embeddings “with default parameters,” but the training corpora (size, domain, language sources) are not specified. For small clinical corpora, in-domain training is insufficient; out-of-domain corpora may introduce mismatch. Code, preprocessing recipes (exact disfluency removal rules), and exact feature lists per dataset are not provided, limiting reproducibility.
- Limited novelty relative to prior network/embedding work: Constructing networks from word embeddings or enriching co-occurrence graphs by similarity follows prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The primary novelty is the clinical application and empirical findings rather than a methodological advance.

Questions to authors
1) Did you ensure subject-wise cross-validation, especially in ABCD (two narratives per subject)? If not, please re-run with subject-disjoint folds and report results.
2) What corpora were used to train the fastText-style embeddings (size, domain, language)? Were embeddings trained separately per language? Were embeddings trained solely on the small clinical datasets or larger external corpora?
3) How was the similarity threshold chosen (0.7/0.4)? Was threshold selection nested within CV to avoid test fold peeking? Please report sensitivity analyses.
4) What is the exact class distribution for each dataset (n subjects, n narratives per class)? For Cookie Theft, how many controls vs MCI, and how many samples per subject?
5) Please report additional metrics (per-class precision/recall/F1; ROC-AUC) and confidence intervals, and perform significance testing for key comparisons.
6) Can you add stronger baselines (tf-idf with 1-2/3-grams + linear SVM; averaged word embeddings; doc2vec), and compare to CNE fairly (same CV protocol)?
7) Provide ablation studies: CN vs CNE, with/without skewness/variance features, and an analysis of graph density as threshold varies to support the “short-text mitigation” claim.

Additional comments
- Clarify preprocessing consistency across datasets (which disfluencies removed/kept; segmentation sources).
- Consider reporting feature counts and normalization. Given small datasets, consider feature selection or regularization details.
- Releasing code and data splits (subject-wise) would substantially improve impact.

Overall, the application focus is timely and the approach is simple and promising, but methodological rigor (subject-wise CV, stronger baselines, fuller reporting) needs to be strengthened to support acceptance.
