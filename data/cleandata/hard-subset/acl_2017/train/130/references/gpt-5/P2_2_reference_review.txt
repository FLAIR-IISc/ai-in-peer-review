Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with embedding-based similarity edges to mitigate sparsity in short, noisy texts. It extracts standard topological metrics (with mean/std/skew) as features and evaluates classification of MCI vs. controls across three datasets (English Cookie Theft descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD retells), comparing: (i) plain complex networks (CN), (ii) embedding-enriched networks (CNE), (iii) Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic metrics (LM), and (iv) bag-of-words (BoW). It also combines feature spaces via multi-view majority vote and ensembles of classifiers.

Contributions (ordered by strength)
1) Empirical finding that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network topology (CNE) is a competitive, often strongest single-view feature set for MCI detection in short/noisy transcripts, across English and Portuguese, and complements LM/BoW in multi-view fusion.
2) Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation showing a language-agnostic pipeline that does not require manual clean-up or deep parsing can reach accuracies comparable to prior work.
3) A simple enrichment mechanism (adding edges for high-cosine pairs) demonstrating a practical way to make network features informative for short texts.

Strengths
1) Cross-lingual, multi-task evaluation with consistent trends: CNE typically outperforms plain CN and is competitive with LM; multi-view (CNE+LM+BoW) yields the best scores on two datasets (e.g., 0.80 accuracy on ABCD). This supports the claim that network topology enriched with distributional semantics adds value beyond content-only features.
2) Language- and tool-agnostic approach appropriate for noisy clinical transcripts: avoids reliance on error-prone or unavailable parsers, yet leverages subword embeddings (fastText-style) to handle OOVs and morphological variation, which is sensible for Portuguese and for disfluent ASR-like text.
3) Comparisons to multiple baselines and classifiers: the paper situates CNE against LM (Coh-Metrix/CM-Dementia) and BoW and reports several learning algorithms, indicating the effect is not tied to a single classifier.
4) Practical motivation and relevance: Early MCI detection from language is important, and the method’s simplicity and robustness to minimal pre-processing make it attractive for eventual deployment with ASR transcripts.

Weaknesses
1) Threats to validity in experimental design and confounds:
   - Cross-validation appears to be document-level, not subject-level. In ABCD, each subject contributes two retells; placing narratives from the same subject across folds would inflate accuracy via subject leakage. The paper does not state subject-wise CV. Similarly, Cookie Theft and Cinderella subject leakage risk is not addressed.
   - Strong demographic confounds, especially in ABCD: controls are much younger (61±7.5) than MCI (72±7.4). Classifiers may be picking up age-correlated proxies rather than impairment. No matching, covariate control, or ablation with demographics is reported.
   - Hyperparameter selection (cosine threshold 0.7/0.4) appears to use CV without a separate dev set; selection on test folds can bias results.
2) Baselines are underpowered; comparisons may overstate gains:
   - BoW uses raw term frequency only; no tf-idf, no n-grams, no regularized logistic regression tuning, which are strong standard baselines for short texts.
   - No comparison to simple embedding baselines (e.g., averaged word vectors, sentence transformers) or shallow neural models, which are widely used and might rival/surpass CNE.
   - SVM hyperparameters (C, γ) and feature scaling are not detailed; lack of tuning may disadvantage baselines.
3) Statistical analysis is limited:
   - Only accuracy is reported; no precision/recall/F1, no confidence intervals, no significance tests (e.g., McNemar, permutation). Many models and combinations are tried; without correction or uncertainty estimates it’s hard to assess robustness.
4) Methodological details and reproducibility gaps:
   - Embedding training data is unspecified (corpus size, domain, language, pretraining vs. in-domain). Training fastText “with default parameters” on tiny transcripts would be inadequate; using public pre-trained vectors could yield different outcomes.
   - The enrichment thresholding strategy is not justified or analyzed (no sensitivity analysis, no normalization for network density/size). Global thresholds can confound text-length effects and topological metrics.
   - Preprocessing choices (stopword removal, sentence segmentation simulation) may materially affect network topology; details are insufficient to reproduce.
5) Clarity/presentation issues:
   - Dataset descriptions are confusing (Cookie Theft: “43 MCI sampled from 326 probable AD; 21 possible AD”), and several tables/figures are missing or poorly formatted; references have numerous formatting errors. These hinder assessment.

Questions to authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: Was CV subject-wise for all datasets? In ABCD, were both retells from each subject kept in the same fold? If not, please re-run with subject-wise CV and report results.
2) Demographic confounds: Given the substantial age difference in ABCD, can you (a) match groups on age/education, (b) include demographics as covariates and report incremental gains from language features, and/or (c) perform stratified analyses to show robustness?
3) Embeddings: What corpora and sizes were used to train embeddings? Did you try publicly available pre-trained fastText for English/Portuguese? How sensitive are results to embedding dimension and to using pre-trained vs. in-domain vectors?
4) Thresholding and network density: How was the cosine threshold selected (held-out dev vs CV)? Did you control for the number of added edges (e.g., k-NN per node) to normalize density across texts? Please provide a sensitivity analysis of threshold/density and report average network sizes/densities per dataset.
5) Baselines and metrics: Please compare against stronger text baselines (tf-idf unigrams+bigrams with tuned logistic/SVM; averaged word vectors; sentence-level embeddings). Report precision/recall/F1 and confidence intervals, and run significance tests vs. the strongest baseline.
6) Reproducibility: Will you release code, feature extraction scripts, and exact preprocessing settings (stopword lists, tokenization), and specify whether networks were weighted or unweighted and how disconnected components were handled?

Overall assessment
The idea of enriching co-occurrence networks with embeddings for clinical speech classification is interesting and practically motivated, and the cross-lingual evidence is encouraging. However, the current evaluation has substantial validity and baseline gaps that limit confidence in the conclusions. With subject-wise CV, confound control, stronger baselines, and clearer methodology, this work could make a solid empirical contribution; as written, it falls short of ACL acceptance standards.
