Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them by adding edges between semantically similar words based on cosine similarity of word embeddings (fastText). It extracts standard topological measures (degree, betweenness, PageRank, clustering, etc.), summarizes them per transcript, and uses these as features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. control. It compares complex network (CN) features, enriched CN (CNE), linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia), and bag-of-words (BoW), and explores multi-view majority voting ensembles.
- Empirical evaluation on three datasets: DementiaBank Cookie Theft (English picture descriptions), Cinderella (Portuguese narratives; both raw and manually “cleaned” versions), and ABCD (Portuguese immediate and delayed retellings with automatic sentence segmentation). Reported best accuracies: 65% (Cookie Theft), 75% (Cinderella), 75% (Cinderella cleaned), and 80% (ABCD) with multi-view combinations.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1) An application of embedding-enriched word co-occurrence networks to short, noisy clinical narratives for MCI detection across two languages, showing that these features can be competitive with BoW and hand-crafted linguistic features.
2) A multi-view ensemble (CNE, LM, BoW) yielding modest accuracy gains over single-view models on two datasets.
3) An empirical claim that this approach reduces reliance on resource-intensive NLP preprocessing in Portuguese clinical transcripts.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual and multi-dataset evaluation: The paper tests on English and Brazilian Portuguese, across elicitation paradigms (picture description, story retelling), including both automatically and manually processed transcripts. This breadth is valuable in a clinical NLP context where robustness across settings matters.
- Simplicity and resource-light pipeline: The approach avoids dependency parsing or heavy manual normalization, which is pragmatic for languages without robust clinical NLP tools and aligns with noisy, disfluent transcripts.
- Addressing short-text sparsity: Enriching co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based edges is a reasonable and computationally cheap way to densify networks for short texts, consistent with prior observations that bare co-occurrence networks for very short documents are nearly linear and uninformative.
- Competitive results with BoW and hand-crafted features: On ABCD, BoW linear SVM is strong, but the multi-view combination improves further. On the Portuguese Cinderella (manual version), CNE+BoW reaches 75%, indicating complementarity rather than redundancy.
- Framing and related work: The paper connects to prior complex network literature (e.g., Perozzi et al., Amancio et al.) and clinical MCI/AD detection studies, and clearly motivates the choice of topological descriptors.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject-level leakage in cross-validation: ABCD includes two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed). The paper uses 5-fold CV but does not state that folds are grouped by subject. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test folds, results can be significantly inflated. This is a critical methodological flaw that must be addressed for all datasets with multiple samples per subject.
- Ambiguous and inconsistent dataset descriptions: For Cookie Theft, the text says “We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD,” which is contradictory (AD vs MCI) and does not clearly specify control counts. The sampling strategy, inclusion/exclusion, and final class distributions need clarification. Without this, accuracy is hard to interpret and class imbalance cannot be assessed.
- Insufficient baselines and missing modern methods: The comparison omits simple and strong representation-learning baselines such as averaged word embeddings, doc2vec, and especially transformer-based encoders (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa/XLM-R) fine-tuned on sentence/document classification, which are standard in current NLP. Given that the main novelty is an alternative representation, stronger baselines are necessary to establish competitiveness.
- Preprocessing choices may remove key clinical signal: The pipeline removes stopwords and some disfluencies. In dementia detection, function word usage, pronouns, fillers, and disfluencies are known to be informative. An ablation evaluating the impact of stopword removal and disfluency removal is essential. Likewise, not lemmatizing in a morphologically rich language like Portuguese could confound topological features via type sparsity; this choice should be empirically justified.
- Hyperparameter tuning and reporting: The cosine similarity threshold is “optimized” with best values reported per dataset, but the procedure (grid, validation protocol, nested CV) is unspecified. If tuned on the same CV, results may be biased. Similarly, classifier hyperparameters (e.g., C and gamma for SVM) appear to be defaults; there is no nested CV or validation set. No confidence intervals or statistical tests are reported, despite small data sizes and many comparisons.
- Limited analysis/interpretability: The paper reports accuracies but lacks analysis of what topological features drive performance, how graph density/size relate to document length, or whether CNE gains persist after controlling for length. No error analysis or per-class sensitivity/specificity is provided, which is crucial in clinical applications.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you perform subject-wise CV splits (i.e., no subject appears in both training and test) for ABCD and any other dataset with multiple samples per subject? If not, please re-run with grouped CV and report updated results.
- Embeddings training data: On what corpora were the fastText embeddings trained for English and Portuguese? Were they trained only on the small clinical transcripts or on large external corpora? Please specify sizes, domains, and preprocessing. If trained on the task data, how did you prevent train-test leakage?
- Threshold tuning: How was the cosine similarity threshold selected (grid, range, step)? Was nested CV used, or a held-out validation set? Please report sensitivity to threshold and the resulting average graph density per document before/after enrichment.
- Preprocessing: Which disfluencies were removed automatically and how (regex? ASR metadata)? Please quantify removals. Can you report an ablation retaining stopwords and disfluencies to assess their contribution?
- Controls for length: Since topological measures can correlate with network size/density, did you normalize or regress out text length (tokens/sentences) or number of nodes/edges? Please report correlations and, if possible, results matched on length.
- Class imbalance and metrics: What are the exact class counts per dataset? Please report macro-F1, ROC-AUC, and per-class recall (especially MCI), along with confidence intervals over multiple random CV splits.
- Baselines: Can you add baselines with averaged embeddings, doc2vec, and a simple transformer fine-tuned classifier (monolingual BERT for English; BERTimbau or XLM-R for Portuguese) to contextualize performance?

Additional comments
- The multi-view “majority vote” is generic; learning weights (e.g., stacking with calibrated probabilities) could be stronger and still simple.
- Clarity: There are figure/cross-reference inconsistencies and some typos; please revise. Providing code and fold definitions would greatly aid reproducibility.

Overall, the idea of embedding-enriched networks for short clinical transcripts is interesting and practically motivated, but methodological clarity (especially subject-wise CV), stronger baselines, and deeper analysis are needed before the claims are compelling.
