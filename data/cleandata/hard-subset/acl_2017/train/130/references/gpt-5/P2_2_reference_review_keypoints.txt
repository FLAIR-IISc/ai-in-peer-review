1. Paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with embedding-similarity edges and uses topological metrics to classify MCI vs controls across three datasets.

2. Main claimed contribution: embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks (CNE) are a competitive, often strongest single-view feature set and complement LM/BoW in multi-view fusion.

3. Secondary contribution: cross-lingual, multi-dataset pipeline that requires minimal preprocessing and no deep parsing.

4. Tertiary contribution: simple practical enrichment mechanism (add edges for high-cosine pairs) to make network features informative for short texts.

5. Strength: consistent cross-lingual, multi-task results showing CNE typically outperforms plain CN and multi-view fusion (CNE+LM+BoW) gives best scores on two datasets.

6. Strength: language- and tool-agnostic approach using subword embeddings to handle OOVs and noisy clinical transcripts without parsers.

7. Strength: comparisons to multiple baselines and classifiers suggest effects are not tied to a single learning algorithm.

8. Strength: practical motivation—method is simple, robust to minimal preprocessing, and relevant for early MCI detection from language.

9. Weakness: potential subject-level CV leakage (document-level CV used), e.g., ABCD has two retells per subject which may inflate accuracy if not folded by subject.

10. Weakness: major demographic confounds (notably age differences in ABCD) risk classifiers learning proxies rather than impairment; no matching or covariate control reported.

11. Weakness: hyperparameter selection (cosine thresholds) appears to have been done within CV without separate dev set, risking biased results.

12. Weakness: baselines are underpowered — BoW uses raw TF only, no tf-idf/n-grams/tuned models; no simple embedding or sentence-embedding baselines included.

13. Weakness: SVM and other classifier tuning and feature-scaling details are missing, possibly disadvantaging baselines.

14. Weakness: limited statistical analysis — only accuracy reported, no precision/recall/F1, confidence intervals, or significance tests across many models.

15. Weakness: reproducibility gaps — embedding training data and settings unspecified, enrichment thresholding unjustified, no sensitivity analysis for threshold/density, preprocessing details insufficient.

16. Weakness: clarity and presentation issues — confusing dataset descriptions, missing/poor tables and formatting errors hampering assessment.

17. Question: Was CV performed subject-wise for all datasets (e.g., keeping both retells in same fold for ABCD)?

18. Question: Can authors control for demographics (match on age/education, include covariates, or stratify) to show robustness?

19. Question: What corpora/settings were used for embeddings; were public pre-trained fastText vectors tried and how sensitive are results to embedding choices?

20. Question: How were cosine thresholds selected; was density normalized (e.g., k-NN) and is there a sensitivity analysis and reported average network sizes/densities?

21. Question: Will authors compare to stronger baselines (tf-idf unigrams+bigrams with tuned classifiers, averaged word vectors, sentence embeddings) and report precision/recall/F1, CIs, and significance tests?

22. Question: Will authors release code, feature extraction scripts, exact preprocessing (stopword lists, tokenization), and network weighting/component handling for reproducibility?

23. Overall assessment: idea is interesting and promising with encouraging cross-lingual evidence, but substantial validity, baseline, statistical, and reproducibility gaps limit confidence and need addressing before acceptance.
