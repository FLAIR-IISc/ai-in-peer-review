Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) complex networks and enriches them with semantic edges derived from word embeddings (cosine similarity above a threshold). It then extracts standard topological metrics (with mean/std/skewness) to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs controls. Experiments on three datasets (English Cookie Theft picture descriptions; Portuguese Cinderella narratives; Portuguese ABCD immediate/delayed recall) compare complex networks (CN), enriched networks (CNE), Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic features (LM), Bag-of-Words (BoW), and simple multi-view majority voting. CNE is generally competitive or superior among single-view features; multi-view combinations sometimes improve accuracy.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical application of enriched complex network features to clinical MCI detection from speech transcripts across two languages, showing competitive performance to linguistic feature baselines while requiring less fragile NLP processing.
2) An operational trick for short, noisy transcripts: densifying word co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges yields more informative topological features.
3) An empirical comparison across three datasets (including one with automatic sentence segmentation) demonstrating that multi-view combinations can improve over single feature spaces.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual, low-dependency pipeline: The approach avoids heavy syntactic parsing/annotation, which is brittle on spontaneous clinical speech. This is important for Portuguese and for realistic ASR-like transcripts. The method’s reliance on simple preprocessing plus embeddings is attractive for deployment.

- Embedding-enriched networks improve short-text modeling: The paper targets a known limitation of complex networks on short texts and shows that adding semantic similarity edges lifts performance (CNE > CN in most settings). The idea is simple and supported by results.

- Multi-dataset evaluation, including different elicitation protocols and segmentation regimes: Evaluations on Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese, immediate/delayed recall, automatic sentence segmentation) improve external validity. Reported accuracies (up to ~80% in ABCD with multi-view) are in the range of prior literature for related tasks.

- Transparent feature construction with interpretable graph metrics: Using standard centrality/structure measures and their distributional summaries is reproducible (once graphs are defined) and interpretable, which is valuable for clinical stakeholders compared to opaque end-to-end models.

- Sensible baselines: Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) and BoW linear SVM baselines are competitive; indeed, BoW with a linear SVM is strong on ABCD, underscoring that the proposed features are not beating weak baselines.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject-level leakage in cross-validation: In the ABCD dataset, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate and delayed). The paper uses 5-fold CV but does not state that folds are stratified by subject. If narratives from the same subject appear in both train and test, accuracy can be inflated. This is a critical methodological issue; similar concerns may apply to other datasets if multiple samples per subject exist.

- Insufficient detail and controls for embedding training: The paper states it trained Bojanowski et al. embeddings with default parameters but does not specify the training corpus, its size, language(s), or whether English and Portuguese models differed. Training on the (small) task data would be inappropriate; using external corpora without detail harms reproducibility and may confound results. Robustness to embedding choice, OOV rate, and the impact of subword modeling are not analyzed.

- Threshold tuning and lack of statistical rigor: The cosine threshold is “optimized” (0.7 for Cookie Theft; 0.4 for Portuguese), but the tuning protocol is unclear (nested CV vs. using test folds). No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported, and only accuracy is presented (no F1, AUC, or per-class metrics). Given small sample sizes, variance can be large; some reported differences are small.

- Dataset and preprocessing clarity: The Cookie Theft cohort description is inconsistent (MCI counts vs AD sampling) and control counts are unclear. Preprocessing choices (e.g., stopword removal) may discard clinically relevant function-word usage, which is known to be informative in dementia detection. The paper partially mitigates this via LM features, but the rationale and impact on CN/CNE features are not analyzed.

- Limited analysis/ablation: The paper does not report how many edges are added by similarity enrichment, how graph density and specific metrics change, or sensitivity to the similarity threshold. Feature importance, per-metric contributions, and failure modes are not discussed. Multi-view “majority vote” is simplistic; learned weighting or stacking could be stronger and more informative.

Questions to authors
- How were cross-validation folds constructed? For ABCD (and any dataset with multiple narratives per subject), did you ensure subject-disjoint splits to avoid leakage?

- What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese, respectively? How large were they, and were embeddings trained separately per language? What was the OOV rate per dataset?

- How was the similarity threshold tuned? Was tuning performed within the training folds (nested CV)? Please report sensitivity to threshold and the average percentage of added edges.

- Are the classes balanced in each dataset? Please provide counts per class and report additional metrics (macro-F1, AUC) and confidence intervals.

- Did you try keeping function words when building CN/CNE? Given their clinical relevance, how does including stopwords affect performance and network topology?

- For ABCD, do results differ between immediate and delayed recalls? If so, please report breakdown.

Overall, this is a promising, lightweight approach applied to an important clinical problem, but the evaluation methodology and missing details (especially subject-level CV and embedding training) need clarification and likely additional experiments to ensure validity and reproducibility.
