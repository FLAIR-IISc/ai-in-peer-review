Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes representing clinical speech transcripts as word co-occurrence complex networks, enriched with semantic edges derived from word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The authors extract topological graph features and compare them to traditional linguistic metrics and bag-of-words (BoW), using multiple classifiers and multi-view ensembles across three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Brazilian Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD).

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A novel application and instantiation of embedding-enriched word co-occurrence networks for clinical MCI detection from transcripts, aimed at minimizing reliance on heavy linguistic preprocessing and making the approach more language-agnostic and robust to disfluencies/short texts.
2) An empirical study across three datasets in two languages, showing that enriched network features are competitive with, and often superior to, linguistic metrics, and are complementary in multi-view ensembles.
3) A practical pipeline mimicking automated deployment (automatic sentence segmentation in parts, minimal manual cleaning), suggesting portability to low-resource clinical settings.

Strengths
1) Conceptual novelty and boldness: Enriching co-occurrence networks with distributional semantics for MCI detection is, to my knowledge, new for this task. The approach directly addresses a known limitation of network models on short/disfluent texts (near-linearity) by adding semantically motivated edges. This is a clear, interesting direction that can inspire follow-up work in clinical NLP and beyond.
2) Language- and resource-agnostic orientation: The method avoids dependence on robust constituency/dependency parsing and heavy manual correction, which is valuable for clinical languages/genres (e.g., Brazilian Portuguese) where high-accuracy tools are scarce. The cross-lingual evaluation strengthens this claim.
3) Empirical signal for the central idea: Across datasets, enriched CN (CNE) generally outperforms plain CN and is often competitive with or better than linguistic metrics. The best results often arise from multi-view combinations where CNE contributes meaningfully, including a notable 0.80 accuracy on ABCD with CNE-LM-BoW.
4) Pragmatic modeling choices: Using subword embeddings (fastText) to mitigate OOV/disfluencies is apt for noisy transcripts. The feature set of graph topological measures is standard, interpretable, and reproducible.
5) Clinically relevant problem: Early MCI detection is high-impact. Showing accuracies in the 60–80% range aligns with prior literature, suggesting the approach is viable and worthy of further maturation.

Weaknesses
1) Potential data leakage and confounds: It is unclear whether cross-validation was performed at the subject level. ABCD includes two retells per subject; if folds split by narrative rather than by subject, leakage likely inflates scores. Additionally, ABCD shows large age/education imbalances (e.g., mean age 61 vs 72), and no attempt is made to control for these. Without subject-grouped CV and demographic controls/matching, reported gains may be confounded.
2) Under-specified embedding training and model selection: The corpora used to train fastText embeddings (size, domain, tokenization) are unspecified. If trained only on small in-domain transcripts, semantic quality may be weak; if trained on large external corpora, domain mismatch could matter. Thresholds for similarity edges (0.7/0.4) appear tuned on the same data used for evaluation; the selection protocol (inner CV vs outer CV) is not described, risking optimistic bias.
3) Limited baselines and analysis: There is no comparison to strong, simple neural baselines (e.g., averaged word vectors, doc2vec, SBERT/transformer sentence embeddings with linear classifiers) that directly test whether graph topology adds value beyond distributional features. No ablations on the density/number of added semantic edges or on which topological measures matter. No robustness checks to sentence segmentation or ASR errors.
4) Evaluation reporting: Only accuracy is reported; class distributions per dataset are unclear in parts, and no precision/recall/F1, confidence intervals, or statistical significance are provided. Given small sample sizes, this limits interpretability.
5) Clarity/data inconsistencies: The Cookie Theft section suggests MCI samples were taken from participants with probable/possible AD, which is confusing; the count of controls and exact labeling criteria are unclear in the provided text. Some figure references and typographical issues hinder readability.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds constructed at the subject level for all datasets, especially ABCD with immediate/delayed retells? If not, please re-run with subject-grouped CV. Also report exact class counts per split.
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train fastText for English and Portuguese (size/domain/preprocessing)? Or were embeddings trained solely on the transcripts? Please clarify.
- Hyperparameter selection: How were similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) chosen? Was there nested CV or a held-out dev set? Please detail to ensure no test leakage.
- Demographic confounds: Can you report results controlling for age/education (matching, covariate-adjusted models, or adding demographics as features) and show whether conclusions hold?
- Cookie Theft labeling: Please clarify the composition of MCI vs control groups (numbers, selection, whether any probable/possible AD are included) and the final sample sizes used for classification.
- Edge enrichment analysis: How many semantic edges are added on average (and variance) per document? Provide performance as a function of threshold and/or fixed edge budgets. Is the gain from increased connectivity or semantic coherence?
- Stronger baselines: How does CNE compare to simple document embedding baselines (mean word vectors, doc2vec, SBERT) with linear/RBF SVM? This will help isolate the value of graph topology.
- Metrics and uncertainty: Please report precision/recall/F1 (macro/micro), class balance, and confidence intervals or significance tests across folds.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessing scripts, and (where allowed) train/val/test splits?

Overall, I find the core idea novel and promising for clinical NLP and am inclined to be positive if the authors can address leakage/confound concerns and strengthen baselines/analysis.
