Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them by adding semantic edges between words whose embedding cosine similarity exceeds a threshold. It then characterizes each transcript via topological graph metrics (means/stds/skewness of 10 measures) and evaluates these features for detecting mild cognitive impairment (MCI) across three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD). The approach is compared to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix variants) and bag-of-words, with additional ensemble and multi-view (feature-space voting) setups.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) Empirical finding that enriching word co-occurrence networks with embedding-based edges yields competitive or superior performance to traditional linguistic features for MCI detection, especially on short/noisy transcripts.
2) Cross-lingual, multi-dataset evaluation (English and Brazilian Portuguese; picture description and narrative retell tasks) showing the approach is robust to different elicitation protocols and transcript lengths.
3) A simple, low-resource pipeline (no lemmatization/parsing; modest feature dimensionality) that can work when high-accuracy NLP tools are unavailable or transcripts include disfluencies.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset, cross-lingual evaluation: The method is tested on three datasets spanning two languages and different elicitation paradigms. This strengthens claims of robustness and generality beyond a single corpus or language.
- Practicality for noisy, short texts: The approach avoids heavy preprocessing (no dependency parsing/lemmatization) and explicitly addresses the “linear network” problem in short transcripts by adding semantic edges. Results show consistent gains of enriched networks (CNE) over plain co-occurrence networks (CN).
- Competitive performance with simple features: With limited, interpretable graph features, the method matches or exceeds linguistic metrics and sometimes BoW baselines. Notably, CNE features are often the strongest single-view features, and multiview voting further improves accuracy (e.g., 0.80 accuracy on ABCD with CNE-LM-BoW).
- Clear ablation of enrichment: The comparison between CN and CNE isolates the value of embedding-based augmentation, which is an informative result for the community working on complex networks for text.
- Multi-view and ensemble exploration: Demonstrates that combining complementary views (CNE, LM, BoW) via simple majority voting can yield further improvements, suggesting these features capture nonredundant signals.

Weaknesses
- Potential subject and demographic confounds: The ABCD dataset shows large age and education imbalances (controls substantially younger and more educated than MCI). Without controlling for or matching demographics, the classifier might learn age/education correlates rather than MCI. Moreover, it is unclear whether cross-validation is subject-independent (especially in ABCD where each subject contributes two narratives). Any leakage of a subject across train/test folds can inflate accuracy.
- Incomplete baselines and evaluation metrics: BoW uses term frequency only (no tf-idf, regularized linear models, or simple document embedding baselines such as averaged word vectors). No neural or pre-trained sentence encoders are compared, despite the embedding theme. Only accuracy is reported; no precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, confidence intervals, or statistical significance tests, which limits conclusions about robustness.
- Embeddings training details missing: The paper states it trains the Bojanowski et al. (fastText) model with default parameters but does not specify the training corpora, their size, or whether the embeddings were trained solely on the tiny clinical transcripts (likely inadequate) or on large external corpora. This is crucial for reproducibility and for interpreting the gains from enrichment.
- Hyperparameter tuning and model selection unclear: The similarity threshold is “optimized” to 0.7/0.4, but the procedure (nested CV versus tuning on all data) is not described. Similarly, it is unclear whether classifier hyperparameters were tuned within folds. Without proper nested validation, reported accuracies may be optimistic.
- Clarity and dataset description issues: The Cookie Theft section ambiguously describes sample selection (“We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD”), and the control cohort size is not clearly stated. Some preprocessing choices (which disfluencies are removed/kept per dataset) differ across datasets, potentially affecting comparability. The definition of “multi-view learning” and the distinction from “ensemble” methods are not consistently described.

Questions to authors
- Splitting protocol: Did you ensure subject-wise splits (no subject appears in both train and test) in all datasets, especially ABCD with two narratives per subject and DementiaBank where multiple recordings per participant may exist? If not, can you re-run with subject-level CV?
- Demographics: Given the age/education imbalance in ABCD, did you control for these variables (e.g., stratified/matched splits, regression control, or inclusion as features)? How do results change on age/education-matched subsets?
- Embedding training: On what corpora were the fastText embeddings trained (size, domain, language)? If trained only on your transcripts, how do results compare to using large pre-trained fastText vectors? Please report sensitivity to embedding choices.
- Hyperparameters: How was the similarity threshold selected (grid, inner CV)? Were classifier hyperparameters tuned with nested CV? Please clarify to rule out overfitting.
- Baselines: Can you add stronger baselines such as tf-idf with linear SVM/logistic regression, averaged word embeddings with a linear classifier, and perhaps a lightweight sentence encoder? Also report F1/ROC-AUC and statistical tests across folds.
- Leakage via preprocessing: For Cookie Theft and Cinderella, are multiple sessions per participant present? If so, how did you prevent leakage when removing disfluencies and segmenting sentences?

Additional comments
- An error analysis (e.g., typical false positives/negatives, dependence on transcript length) and a sensitivity study of the similarity threshold would help interpretability.
- Reporting feature importance (e.g., which network metrics drive decisions) would strengthen the case for interpretability in clinical settings.
