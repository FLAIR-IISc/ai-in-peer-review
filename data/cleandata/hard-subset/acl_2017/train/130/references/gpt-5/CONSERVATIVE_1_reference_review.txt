Summary and main contributions
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with word-embedding similarity edges, then uses topological network features to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. It evaluates on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD retellings), and compares against Coh-Metrix-style linguistic features and a simple BoW baseline, with several classifiers and simple multi-view majority voting.
- Claimed contributions as I see them:
  1) An application study: using embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features for MCI detection from transcripts in English and Portuguese, with some evidence they can be competitive and complementary to established features.
  2) A multi-view majority-vote combination of CNE, linguistic, and BoW feature spaces that yields modest accuracy improvements on some datasets.
  3) A cross-dataset, cross-language evaluation without heavy reliance on parsing or manual annotation.

Strengths
- Cross-language, multi-dataset evaluation. The study includes both English and Brazilian Portuguese, and multiple elicitation tasks (picture description, story retellings), which increases potential generality beyond a single dataset.
- Focus on features less dependent on deep parsing. For noisy transcripts with disfluencies and limited NLP tools (Portuguese), this is a pragmatic direction. The complex network features are relatively parser-agnostic and may be robust when high-quality syntactic tools are unavailable.
- Complementarity across feature views. The empirical results suggest that simple majority-vote combinations across CNE, linguistic metrics, and BoW can improve performance in some cases, indicating the network view captures information different from standard features.
- Competitive results relative to simple baselines. In several settings, the enriched complex network features are on par with or better than Coh-Metrix features, and not far from BoW SVM performance.

Weaknesses
- Methodological rigor and potential leakage/confounds:
  - Subject-level leakage risk: In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives (immediate/delayed). The paper does not state that cross-validation was stratified by subject. If narratives from the same subject appear in train/test folds, accuracy can be inflated. This must be addressed.
  - Demographic confounds: ABCD shows large age differences between groups (mean 61 vs. 72) and different education distributions; Cinderella and Cookie Theft demographics are not clearly controlled. Without matching or including demographics as covariates, classifiers may exploit these confounds rather than cognitive status.
  - Threshold and hyperparameter selection: The embedding-similarity threshold was “optimized” per dataset, but no nested CV is reported. This risks tuning on test folds and optimistic estimates. Classifiers appear to use defaults, but threshold is tuned—this asymmetry undermines claims of superiority.
- Weak baselines and limited metrics:
  - BoW uses term frequency only; no tf-idf, no bigrams/trigrams, no tuned regularization, no logistic regression baseline, no simple neural baselines. In ABCD, linear SVM with BoW already yields strong results. Without stronger standard baselines, it’s hard to argue the added value of CNE convincingly.
  - Only accuracy is reported. No F1, AUC, sensitivity/specificity, confidence intervals, or statistical significance testing across folds. Given small sample sizes, variance and significance are essential.
- Inconsistency and unclear preprocessing:
  - Preprocessing differs across datasets (manual vs. automatic segmentation; which disfluencies are removed). Cinderella has two versions (raw vs. manually revised). These inconsistencies complicate comparisons and the claim of a fully automated pipeline.
  - The sentence “we removed disfluencies” is not consistently defined across languages/datasets; the extent of removal can materially affect results.
- Limited novelty relative to established methods:
  - Word adjacency networks for text classification and enrichment with embeddings follow known lines (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main novelty appears to be the application to MCI detection. Several results show BoW linear SVM matches or exceeds CNE in single-view settings (e.g., ABCD), weakening the case for the proposed features.
- Missing details and reproducibility:
  - Embedding training data and scope are unspecified (corpus size, domains, per-language handling). Training fastText “with default parameters” on small transcript corpora could yield poor embeddings; if external corpora were used, that should be detailed.
  - Graph feature set dimensionality, normalization, and implementation specifics are not fully described. No code/data release; no per-fold metrics or variance.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Was CV stratified by subject (especially for ABCD with two narratives per subject)? If not, please re-evaluate with subject-level splits and report updated results.
- Demographic control: Did you match groups by age/education or include these as covariates? Can you report results controlling for demographics, or show that performance persists after matching?
- Embeddings: On what corpora were the fastText embeddings trained for English and Portuguese? How large were they, and did you use pre-trained models? How were OOVs handled in practice?
- Threshold optimization: How was the similarity threshold selected (grid, range)? Was threshold tuning nested within CV folds? Please report the search space and whether the choice generalizes across datasets.
- Data composition: For the Cookie Theft dataset, the description “43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD” is confusing. How many controls were used? Were multiple sessions per subject included? How was balance ensured?
- Evaluation metrics: Please report per-fold variance, confidence intervals, and additional metrics (F1, AUC, sensitivity/specificity). Are improvements over baselines statistically significant?
- Baselines: Can you include stronger, established baselines (e.g., tf-idf with tuned linear SVM/logistic regression; n-grams; possibly simple neural text classifiers) and conduct basic hyperparameter tuning for all classifiers?
- Preprocessing consistency: Precisely which disfluencies were removed in each dataset/language? How was sentence segmentation quality verified for the automatic pipeline? Can you quantify the impact of these steps via ablations?
- Feature ablations: How sensitive are results to the set of network measures used, and to the embedding similarity threshold? Can you provide ablations showing which components drive gains?

Overall, the direction is interesting and potentially useful in low-resource, noisy settings. However, given the limited novelty and methodological concerns (potential leakage, weak baselines, lack of statistical rigor), I am not yet convinced the proposed approach offers clear, validated benefits over established methods. Strengthening the evaluation as suggested would substantially improve the paper.
