Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence (adjacency) networks and enriches them with edges derived from word-embedding cosine similarity to compensate for short, near-linear networks. It then uses topological features of these networks to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from English and Brazilian Portuguese transcripts.
- It compares complex-network features (with and without embedding enrichment) against linguistic features (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag-of-Words (BoW), across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD), and explores multi-view learning and simple ensembles.
- Main empirical finding: embedding-enriched networks are competitive with, and sometimes outperform, linguistic metrics; multi-view combinations yield the best accuracies (up to ~80% on ABCD), without heavy manual preprocessing.

Primary contributions (in order of strength)
1) Empirical demonstration that embedding-enriched word adjacency networks provide useful, language-agnostic features for MCI detection from noisy, short speech transcripts, sometimes outperforming traditional linguistic metrics and complementing BoW.
2) Cross-dataset, cross-language evaluation showing that multi-view combinations of enriched networks, linguistic metrics, and BoW can improve accuracy under limited resources and imperfect preprocessing.
3) A practical pipeline that avoids heavy manual annotation or robust parsing, potentially increasing applicability in low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Practical relevance and cross-language scope: The work targets a clinically important task (early MCI detection) and evaluates on both English (DementiaBank Cookie Theft) and Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD), increasing external validity. Evidence: consistent experiments across three datasets with different elicitation protocols.
- Tackling noisy, short transcripts: The core idea—enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based edges—is well motivated for short, disfluent speech transcripts where plain network topology becomes near-linear and uninformative. Evidence: CNE improves over CN in most settings (Tables 4–7).
- Reduced dependence on fragile NLP preprocessing: By avoiding parsing/lemmatization and using graph features + embeddings, the approach is more robust to disfluencies than parser-dependent Coh-Metrix features. Evidence: The manually “cleaned” Portuguese Cinderella set shows LM improves when grammar is corrected; CNE remains competitive without such edits.
- Simple, transparent features and classifiers: The topological measures are interpretable, and the use of standard classifiers (SVM, k-NN, RF, NB) and multi-view voting is easy to reproduce in principle.
- Multi-view fusion benefits: Combining feature spaces yields the best accuracies on Cookie Theft (65%), Cinderella (~75%), and ABCD (~80%). Evidence: CNE-LM-BoW with SVM-linear reaches 0.8018 on ABCD (Table 7), improving over the best single-view baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Embedding-enriched text networks have been explored (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main novelty here is application to MCI detection; the modeling and fusion methods themselves are largely known. This weakens the contribution from an ACL standpoint unless accompanied by deeper analysis or new theory.
- Evaluation rigor and potential leakage: The paper uses 5-fold CV with accuracy only, but lacks details essential for validity.
  - Subject-level splits are not specified. In ABCD, each subject contributes two narratives; if folds are at the narrative level, speaker leakage can inflate accuracy.
  - Thresholds (0.7/0.4) appear tuned on the data; without nested CV, this risks overfitting.
  - No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals; no F1/ROC-AUC; class distributions per fold are unknown.
- Confounds and dataset clarity: The Cookie Theft description is inconsistent (MCI sampled from AD cohorts; unclear control composition and counts). ABCD has a large age mismatch (controls avg 61 vs MCI 72), a likely confound unaddressed by modeling or matching. Without controlling for demographics, it is hard to attribute gains to the linguistic method rather than age-related differences.
- Baselines and ablations are insufficient:
  - BoW uses raw term frequency, not tf-idf or stronger lexical baselines (e.g., averaged word embeddings, doc2vec, simple neural encoders, or PLMs).
  - No acoustic features, despite prior work showing their value in MCI/AD detection.
  - No feature importance or ablation across network measures and the enrichment threshold; no sensitivity to stopword removal/lemmatization.
- Reproducibility and missing details: It is unclear on which corpora the embeddings were trained (only the algorithm and hyperparameters are given). If trained on the small task datasets, embeddings may be poor; if trained on external corpora, pretraining data/sizes should be disclosed. Code, preprocessing scripts, and train/test splits are not provided; some tables/figures are referenced but incomplete.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds created at the subject level (so that all narratives from a given speaker are confined to one fold)? Specifically for ABCD where subjects provide two narratives, how did you prevent speaker leakage across folds?
- Embedding training data: What corpora (per language) were used to train the fastText embeddings? Sizes, domains, and whether transcripts themselves were included? Did you use pre-trained vectors?
- Threshold selection: How were cosine similarity thresholds (0.7/0.4) chosen? Was nested CV employed to avoid tuning on test folds?
- Dataset composition: For Cookie Theft, please clarify the exact numbers of MCI and control transcripts used, the source of controls, and demographic matching. For ABCD, how did you mitigate age/education confounds?
- Metrics and significance: Can you report precision/recall/F1 and ROC-AUC, with confidence intervals or statistical tests across folds? Confusion matrices would help interpret errors.
- Baselines: Did you try tf-idf BoW, averaged word embeddings with linear SVM, or doc2vec? How do network features compare to these stronger lexical baselines?
- Feature analysis: Which topological measures drive performance? Any robustness analysis to the removal of stopwords, to lemmatization, or to varying the enrichment threshold?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate (application-focused; limited novelty in methodology).
- Soundness/rigor: Mixed; promising idea and positive results, but evaluation has potential leakage/confounds and lacks statistical rigor.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but important dataset and training details are missing or inconsistent.
- Significance: Moderate for clinical NLP due to cross-language practicality; stronger with more rigorous evaluation and stronger baselines.
- Reproducibility: Currently limited; key details/code not provided.

With addressed evaluation concerns (subject-level splits, proper tuning, stronger baselines, and confound control), this work could make a useful empirical contribution.
