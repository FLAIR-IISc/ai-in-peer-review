Summary and claimed contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them by adding edges between semantically similar words (via word embeddings), then uses topological network metrics for classifying Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs. controls. It evaluates on three datasets (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella; Portuguese ABCD), and explores multi-view and ensemble combinations with linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and BoW.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Introduction of embedding-enriched word co-occurrence networks (CNE) as language/resource-light features for detecting MCI from short, noisy clinical transcripts, across two languages and multiple tasks.
2) Empirical finding that CNE features are competitive with or better than traditional linguistic metrics and (sometimes) BoW in this domain, and that multi-view fusion further improves accuracy.
3) Practical framing toward a fully automated pipeline (minimal manual preprocessing), with evidence that network features degrade less than parser-dependent metrics on disfluent/unedited transcripts.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty for this task: Using complex network topology enriched by word embeddings to mitigate the well-known limitation of network features on short texts is a clever, language-agnostic idea. While both components exist, their combination targeted at clinical, noisy transcripts is a fresh direction with potential beyond the datasets used.
- Cross-lingual, cross-task validation: Results on English and Brazilian Portuguese, across three elicitation paradigms (picture description, story retell, narrative) show the approach is not tied to one dataset or heavy language-specific tools—important for clinical deployment where robust parsers are lacking.
- Minimal manual resources: The pipeline avoids dependency parsing and extensive hand curation; the comparison between raw vs. manually revised Portuguese shows CNE is less brittle than parser-dependent metrics. This is valuable for realistic clinical settings and low-resource languages.
- Multi-view gains: Demonstrates that combining CNE with LM and/or BoW gives consistent boosts (e.g., 0.65 on Cookie Theft; 0.75 on Cinderella; 0.80 on ABCD), suggesting complementarity and a practical recipe for practitioners.
- Transparent, interpretable features: Using standard graph measures (PageRank, betweenness, clustering, assortativity, etc.) makes the approach more interpretable than black-box deep models and potentially more acceptable in clinical contexts.

Weaknesses
- Possible data leakage and unclear splits: The ABCD set includes two narratives per subject (immediate and delayed recall). The paper does not state whether cross-validation is at the subject level. If folds are created at the narrative level, the same subject can appear in train and test, inflating accuracy. Similarly, Cookie Theft participant counts/controls are unclear (numbers appear inconsistent). This is potentially a critical threat to validity.
- Baselines and metrics are limited: BoW uses tf only; tf–idf is a standard, often stronger baseline. No comparison to modern neural text baselines (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/ClinicalBERT/multilingual BERT) or to prior published DementiaBank SOTA on the same splits. Only accuracy is reported; no F1/precision/recall, no confidence intervals/significance tests. Given small sample sizes, variance matters.
- Embedding training details are underspecified: It’s unclear which corpora were used to train fastText-like embeddings for English and Portuguese (in-domain transcripts? large external corpora?). Using embeddings trained on tiny clinical transcripts would be weak; using large corpora raises domain mismatch questions. Also, OOV and subword behavior need quantification in this setting.
- Hyperparameter tuning and thresholding: The cosine similarity thresholds (0.7, 0.4) are “optimized,” but the selection protocol is unclear. If tuned on the same CV folds without nested CV or a held-out dev set, results may be optimistic. No ablation of threshold sensitivity or edge-density effects.
- Limited analysis/interpretability: The paper doesn’t analyze which network metrics mattered, how enrichment changes graph topology (beyond a figure), or why CNE helps in specific datasets. No error analysis by discourse phenomena, length, or disfluency type; no robustness study to ASR noise.

Questions to authors
- Splits/leakage: For ABCD and any dataset with multiple samples per subject, were folds created at the subject level to avoid the same subject in train and test? Please clarify for all datasets. If not, can you re-run with subject-level CV?
- Cookie Theft composition: How many MCI vs. control subjects and narratives were used? The text mentions “43 transcripts of MCI” sampled from AD cohorts, which is confusing. Please provide exact counts and balance.
- Embeddings: What corpora were used to train embeddings for EN and PT (size, domain)? Were they trained only on your transcripts, or on large external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, Gigaword)? Any domain adaptation?
- Threshold tuning: How were similarity thresholds selected (nested CV, held-out dev, or tuned on test folds)? How sensitive are results to this threshold and to the resulting network density?
- Baselines: Can you report tf–idf BoW, and a simple pretrained transformer baseline (e.g., mBERT/Portuguese BERT/English BERT with linear head), to contextualize CNE gains?
- Metrics and significance: Please report F1 (macro/micro), per-class performance, and confidence intervals or significance tests across CV folds.
- Robustness to ASR: Since clinical pipelines often rely on ASR, can you add a simulation with injected ASR errors or results on actual ASR outputs to support claims about automation?

Overall assessment
I find the core idea—embedding-enriched co-occurrence networks for clinical transcript classification—novel for this task and promising, especially for low-resource, disfluent speech where heavy NLP tools underperform. However, methodological clarity (subject-level CV, embedding sources, threshold tuning) and stronger baselines are necessary to fully trust the reported gains. If the authors can confirm no leakage and strengthen baselines/metrics, I would lean toward acceptance because the conceptual direction is interesting and potentially impactful beyond this application.
