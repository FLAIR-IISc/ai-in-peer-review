Summary and contributions
This paper models speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges, extracts topological features, and uses these to classify Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) vs controls. It evaluates on three datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella with two preprocessing variants, and Portuguese ABCD), and explores combining feature “views” (complex networks, Coh-Metrix(-Dementia) linguistic metrics, and Bag-of-Words) via majority voting, along with standard classifiers.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical study showing that embedding-enriched co-occurrence network features can be competitive for MCI detection from transcripts, across English and Portuguese datasets.
2) Multi-view ensemble combining network, linguistic, and BoW features, with some gains over single-view baselines on certain datasets.
3) Practical focus on minimizing manual preprocessing for noisy clinical transcripts, including use of automatic sentence segmentation and removal of selected disfluencies.

Strengths
- Cross-lingual and multi-dataset evaluation: The work tests on three datasets spanning two languages and different elicitation paradigms (picture description, storytelling, immediate/delayed recall), which is valuable in a domain where dataset comparability is limited.
- Low-resource, linguistically light features: Complex network features are computationally cheap and avoid reliance on brittle parsers for noisy speech transcripts; this is practically relevant for languages lacking robust parsers (e.g., Portuguese).
- Embedding-enrichment for short texts: Augmenting sparse co-occurrence graphs with embedding-based edges is a reasonable idea to mitigate near-linear structures in short transcripts. The reported improvements of CNE over CN in multiple settings suggest the approach can help.
- Multi-view gains in some settings: The best results often arise from combining views (e.g., 0.80 accuracy on ABCD with CNE-LM-BoW), indicating complementary information across representations.
- Baseline breadth: Multiple classifier families are evaluated. Reporting of both “raw” network features and conventional LM/BoW baselines is appreciated.

Weaknesses
- Methodological ambiguities and inconsistencies:
  - Multi-view definition/reporting is unclear. The text says multi-view uses majority voting across feature spaces, yet Tables report SVM/KNN/etc. accuracies for CNE-LM(-BoW), which would not be a single SVM trained on a concatenated feature vector if majority voting across views is used. This needs precise definition; as presented it appears inconsistent.
  - Embeddings: It is not specified on what corpora the fastText-style embeddings were trained (size, domain, language-specific corpora, train on target transcripts vs external data), nor whether separate models were used per language. This critically affects both performance and reproducibility.
  - Edge creation: Are similarity edges binary (thresholded) or weighted? Are co-occurrence edges across sentence boundaries allowed? Are stopwords removed before network construction (stated yes), and what is the impact on edges across sentences? Network density after enrichment is not reported.
  - Threshold tuning (0.7 for Cookie Theft, 0.4 for others): How was it tuned (held-out validation vs cross-validation folds)? If tuned on full data, this risks leakage and optimistic bias.
  - ABCD has multiple narratives per subject (immediate and delayed recall). Were folds split by subject to prevent speaker leakage? This is essential; otherwise accuracies are inflated.
- Evaluation shortcomings:
  - Accuracy only, no precision/recall/F1, no confidence intervals, and no statistical significance testing. Given small Ns, variance can be high; reporting 4-decimal precision (e.g., 0.5750) is misleading.
  - Baselines are weak: BoW uses term frequency only, no tf-idf, no character or word n-grams beyond unigrams, no regularized logistic regression baseline. SVM-linear with tf-idf n-grams is a standard strong baseline and should be included.
  - Hyperparameters appear at defaults (SVM C/gamma, K in KNN, RF trees). Without tuning (ideally nested CV), comparisons are hard to interpret.
- Data description and potential leakage:
  - Cookie Theft details are incomplete/contradictory. The text states “We used 43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD.” This is confusing (mixing MCI and AD sampling) and does not report the number of controls. Table 1 is referenced but not provided.
  - For all datasets, it is not stated whether cross-validation is at narrative- or subject-level; for ABCD this is critical.
- Claims not consistently supported:
  - The conclusion “CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics” is not consistent across datasets. In the manually processed Cinderella set, LM (SVM-RBF) outperforms CNE; in ABCD, BoW (SVM-linear) surpasses CNE alone. Gains are dataset- and configuration-dependent.
  - The benefit of embedding enrichment is not quantified via ablations beyond CN vs CNE; reporting network densities/degree distributions pre/post enrichment would strengthen the argument.
- Presentation and reproducibility issues:
  - Numerous typos and formatting problems (“vertice,” “trainning,” inconsistent decimal separators 72,0 vs 74.8, hyphenation artifacts, figure numbering mismatch: Figure 1/2; references with “?” and “\Q2012\E”; missing Table 1; inconsistent citation formatting). This reduces clarity and confidence.
  - No code, models, or exact preprocessing rules are released; many crucial details are unspecified (feature normalization, scaling, sentence boundary handling, disfluency removal rules).

Questions to Authors
1) Embeddings: What corpora were used to train embeddings for English and Portuguese (size, domain, token counts)? Were embeddings trained exclusively on the target datasets or on large external corpora (e.g., Wikipedia)? Are models separate per language?
2) Cross-validation: For datasets with multiple narratives per subject (ABCD), were folds split by subject to avoid speaker leakage? If not, please re-run with subject-level CV.
3) Threshold selection: How was the cosine threshold selected (data splits, grid, criterion)? Was tuning nested within CV to avoid leakage?
4) Multi-view: Precisely define how CNE-LM(-BoW) results are obtained per classifier in Tables. Are you concatenating features and training a single SVM, or voting across separate classifiers per view? If the latter, why report per-classifier numbers for multi-view?
5) Networks: Do edges cross sentence boundaries? Are similarity edges weighted or binary? Please report average node count, edge count, density, and degree distribution before/after enrichment per dataset.
6) Baselines: Why not include tf-idf with linear SVM and word/char n-grams? Given 0.75 with BoW-SVM-linear on ABCD, stronger baselines could change conclusions.
7) Cookie Theft: Please clarify the control group size, inclusion criteria, and whether any AD cases were included. Provide the missing Table 1.
8) Preprocessing: Specify exact rules for disfluency removal and whether this differs across datasets. Did you normalize/scale features before SVM/KNN/RF?

Overall, while the application is interesting, the methodological ambiguities, evaluation weaknesses, and presentation issues are substantial and should be addressed before the work is ready.
