Summary
The paper investigates detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling texts as word co-occurrence networks enriched with word-embedding similarity edges. From these graphs, the authors extract topological features and compare them to linguistic metrics (Coh-Metrix/Coh-Metrix-Dementia) and Bag-of-Words (BoW) features, using several classifiers and multi-view/ensemble combinations. Experiments span three datasets and two languages (English Cookie Theft; Portuguese Cinderella and ABCD), including both manually cleaned and automatically segmented transcripts. Reported best accuracies are ~65% (Cookie Theft), 75% (Cinderella), 75% (Cinderella processed), and 80% (ABCD), with multi-view combinations generally outperforming single feature sets.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that complex network features enriched with word embeddings are competitive for MCI detection from noisy, short transcripts, sometimes outperforming traditional linguistic metrics and BoW, across English and Portuguese datasets.
2) A multi-view/ensemble setup showing complementary gains when combining enriched network features with linguistic and BoW features, including scenarios without costly manual preprocessing.
3) Cross-dataset evaluation on narrative and description tasks with both clean and noisy (ASR-like) transcripts, supporting the robustness of the approach.

Strengths
- Robustness to noisy, short texts: The approach avoids dependency on high-precision parsing and manual normalization, which is valuable in clinical transcripts with disfluencies. The enriched network features maintain competitive performance even when linguistic metrics degrade on unprocessed Cinderella data.
- Cross-lingual and cross-task evaluation: Results on English descriptions and Portuguese narratives/retellings strengthen external validity and suggest generality beyond a single dataset or language.
- Complementarity via multi-view learning: Consistent improvements from combining CNE, LM, and BoW (e.g., up to 80% on ABCD) indicate the proposed features contribute information beyond standard baselines.
- Simplicity and computational practicality: The proposed features are straightforward to compute and potentially attractive for low-resource or clinical settings with limited tooling.
- Clear motivation to reduce manual preprocessing: The paper addresses a well-motivated pain point in clinical NLP pipelines, especially for languages lacking robust parsers.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and evaluation design: The ABCD dataset includes two narratives per speaker. The paper does not state that the 5-fold CV is subject-independent. If narratives from the same speaker appear in both train and test folds, accuracies may be inflated. Similarly, the cosine similarity threshold (0.7/0.4) appears tuned globally rather than via nested CV, risking leakage. No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals are reported.
- Limited novelty and insufficient analysis: Enriching co-occurrence networks with embedding-based similarity edges is adapted from prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014). The main novelty is in application to MCI detection. There is little ablation/sensitivity analysis (e.g., effects of thresholds, which topological measures drive performance) and limited interpretability analysis.
- Baseline strength and reporting: BoW uses raw term frequency only; stronger and standard baselines (tf-idf with linear SVM, character/word n-grams, logistic regression) are missing. Modern pre-trained representations (e.g., transformer-based embeddings used as features, not necessarily fine-tuned) are absent, even as non-invasive baselines. Only accuracy is reported; no per-class metrics (precision/recall/F1), calibration, or ROC-AUC, which is important for imbalanced clinical settings.
- Dataset description inconsistencies and confounders: The Cookie Theft section is confusing: the MCI subset is said to come from AD-labeled participants (probable/possible AD), which raises concerns about diagnostic labels. Numbers of controls in Cookie Theft are unclear; demographic matching and potential confounders (age/education differences, especially in ABCD) are not controlled for or included as covariates/features.
- Reproducibility gaps: The source corpora for training embeddings (EN/PT) are unspecified; preprocessing steps (disfluency removal, sentence segmentation) are only broadly described; no code or feature specifications are released; classifier hyperparameters (beyond defaults) are not detailed.

Questions to Authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: Was CV strictly speaker-independent in all datasets, especially ABCD where each subject has two narratives? If not, please rerun with leave-one-subject-out or group-stratified CV and report updated metrics with CIs.
2) Threshold tuning: How was the similarity threshold chosen (0.7/0.4)? Was tuning nested within CV, or on a held-out dev set? Please report sensitivity analyses over thresholds and any other key hyperparameters.
3) Embeddings: Which corpora were used to train fastText embeddings for English and Portuguese? Were the same embeddings used across datasets? Please report OOV rates and whether embeddings were trained only on external text (no test leakage).
4) Cookie Theft labels: Please clarify how MCI cases were selected and matched to controls, and the apparent reference to AD diagnoses (probable/possible) in the same paragraph. What are the final class counts and demographics for controls vs MCI?
5) Confounding variables: Were age and education balanced across folds and classes? If not, did you include demographics as features or adjust for them? Please report performance with demographic covariates.
6) Baselines and reporting: Could you include tf-idf SVM (word and character n-grams) and logistic regression baselines, and report precision/recall/F1 and ROC-AUC with CIs? Also, compare against a simple sentence-level transformer embedding average + linear classifier.
7) Feature importance and ablations: Which network features contribute most to performance? Please provide feature importance/rankings and ablations (CN vs CNE, per-metric removals).
8) Preprocessing consistency: Are disfluency removal and sentence segmentation applied uniformly across classes and datasets? Please detail rules and error rates (e.g., DeepBond segmentation accuracy).

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and provides a practical, cross-lingual evaluation. Addressing the evaluation design (speaker-independent CV, nested tuning), clarifying datasets, strengthening baselines and reporting, and adding ablations/interpretability would substantially improve the rigor and impact.
