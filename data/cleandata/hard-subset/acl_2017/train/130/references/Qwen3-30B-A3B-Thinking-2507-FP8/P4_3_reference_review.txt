

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word co-occurrence networks, then enrich these networks by adding edges between words with high cosine similarity from word embeddings. They evaluate this approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) using various machine learning classifiers and feature combinations, comparing against linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches.

**Main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):**

1. **Demonstration of complex networks enriched with word embeddings as effective features for MCI detection:** The core technical contribution is showing that this specific feature representation outperforms traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets and classifiers, particularly for handling disfluencies common in speech transcripts.

2. **Practical contribution of a fully automated system for MCI detection:** The authors successfully demonstrate a system that requires minimal manual preprocessing (no manual sentence segmentation or disfluency removal), addressing a significant limitation in previous work that required extensive manual revision.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and feature combinations:** The paper provides thorough empirical validation of the approach, including comparisons with linguistic features, bag-of-words, and various classifier types, with detailed analysis of performance improvements.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation of the core methodology:** The paper provides comprehensive results across three different datasets (English and Portuguese), multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, GNB), and feature combinations (single feature types, multi-view learning, ensemble methods). The results consistently show that enriched complex network features (CNE) outperform other approaches, with significant improvements (up to 10% accuracy gain in the Cinderella dataset). The use of 5-fold cross-validation and careful hyperparameter tuning (threshold optimization) demonstrates methodological rigor.

2. **Practical significance for automated MCI detection:** The authors successfully address a major practical limitation in existing approaches by demonstrating a fully automated system that doesn't require manual preprocessing (sentence segmentation, disfluency removal, or grammatical correction). This is particularly important for clinical applications where manual processing would be prohibitively expensive. The paper clearly articulates how their approach handles disfluencies naturally, which is a significant practical advantage over previous work (e.g., Aluísio et al., 2016).

3. **Technical soundness of network enrichment approach:** The methodology for enriching word co-occurrence networks with semantic information through word embeddings is well-justified and technically sound. The authors properly address challenges like out-of-vocabulary words (using character-level embeddings from Bojanowski et al., 2016) and provide a clear explanation of how the enriched networks differ from standard co-occurrence networks (evident in Figure 2). The selection of ten network topology metrics covers both local and global properties, providing a comprehensive representation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technical approach:** While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technique of enriching complex networks with word embeddings is not new (Perozzi et al., 2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from this prior work, which weakens the novelty claim. For ACL, which values novel technical contributions, this is a significant limitation. The authors acknowledge Perozzi et al. (2014) but don't clearly articulate how their specific adaptation for MCI detection from speech transcripts constitutes a meaningful advancement beyond the existing methodology.

2. **Lack of analysis explaining why the approach works:** The paper demonstrates that enriched network features outperform other approaches but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of *why* they work better. For example, the paper doesn't identify which specific network metrics (PageRank, clustering coefficient, etc.) are most informative for MCI detection, nor does it analyze how these metrics relate to cognitive decline patterns. Without this analysis, it's difficult to understand the underlying cognitive mechanisms the approach is capturing, limiting the theoretical contribution.

3. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art NLP approaches:** The paper compares against linguistic features and bag-of-words but doesn't compare with recent deep learning approaches that have shown strong performance in related NLP tasks (e.g., BERT, transformer-based models). For a paper targeting ACL, this is a significant omission, as it fails to position the work within the current landscape of NLP methods. The claim that "the accuracies found here are comparable to the values reported by other authors" is vague without specific comparison to recent work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the authors used the method proposed by Bojanowski et al. (2016) to generate word embeddings." Could the authors clarify whether they used pre-trained embeddings or trained their own embeddings from the available data? If they trained their own embeddings, what was the size of the training corpus, and how did they handle the limited size of the datasets?

2. The authors claim their approach is "fully automated" without manual preprocessing. However, they still perform some preprocessing (removing stopwords, punctuation, and certain disfluencies like "uh," "um"). How does this compare to the manual preprocessing required by Aluísio et al. (2016), and which specific disfluencies are automatically handled by the authors' approach?

3. The paper states that "linguistic features depend on grammatical texts to present good results, as can be seen in the results of Cinderella dataset manually processed (Table 6)." Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of why linguistic features perform poorly on the manually processed dataset (where the text is more grammatical), and how the network features remain robust? What specific linguistic features are most affected by grammatical corrections?

4. The authors mention that "the accuracies found here are comparable to the values reported by other authors, ranging from 60% to 85%." Could the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods in MCI detection from speech, particularly those using deep learning approaches, to better establish the significance of their contribution?
