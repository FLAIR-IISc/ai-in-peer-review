

# Peer Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors create word adjacency networks from transcribed narratives, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings using cosine similarity. They extract topological features from these enriched networks and compare their performance against linguistic features and bag-of-words representations. The approach is evaluated on three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese). The results show that enriched complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features in some cases, and combining different feature types improves classification accuracy.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts** - The authors propose a unique approach to model speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with semantic information, which addresses the challenge of handling disfluencies in neuropsychological assessments without requiring manual preprocessing.

2. **Automated preprocessing pipeline for speech transcripts** - The authors present a fully automated method to handle disfluencies (repetitions, revisions) in transcripts, eliminating the need for manual revision that was required in previous Portuguese language studies.

3. **Cross-lingual evaluation on multiple datasets** - The approach is validated on three distinct datasets with different languages (English and Portuguese) and different narrative tasks, providing a more comprehensive evaluation than single-dataset studies.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Methodology with Clear Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a technically sound approach to address a significant challenge in MCI detection: handling disfluencies in speech transcripts. The enrichment of word adjacency networks with semantic information via word embeddings is a well-motivated technical contribution that directly addresses the problem of short text classification using network methods. The authors provide a detailed explanation of how the enrichment process works and why it's beneficial for capturing semantic relationships beyond simple co-occurrence.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets**: The evaluation on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different linguistic characteristics and narrative tasks provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach. This multi-dataset validation is significantly stronger than previous single-dataset studies in this domain and demonstrates the robustness of the method across different linguistic contexts.

3. **Practical Automation of Neuropsychological Assessment**: The paper's focus on creating a fully automated system (without requiring human annotators for transcript preprocessing) is highly relevant to real-world clinical applications. The authors demonstrate how their approach avoids the expensive manual revision steps required in previous work, making it more practical for clinical settings where resources are limited.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Methods**: The paper lacks a thorough comparison to recent deep learning approaches for MCI detection that have been applied to speech data. For instance, there's no comparison to transformer-based models or other neural network architectures that have been successfully applied to similar tasks in the literature. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed method represents a meaningful improvement over current approaches.

2. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: The paper presents quantitative results but lacks qualitative analysis to explain *why* the enriched network approach works better than traditional linguistic features. Without visualizations of the enriched networks or examples of how the semantic edges change the network structure, it's difficult to understand the underlying mechanisms that make this approach effective.

3. **Overstated Novelty Claims**: The claim that "to the best of our knowledge, these metrics have never been used before to detect MCI in speech transcripts" is not sufficiently supported. A more thorough literature review would be needed to confirm this claim, especially given that complex networks have been used in related NLP tasks for text classification.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent transformer-based models for MCI detection from speech transcripts (e.g., those using BERT or other contextual embeddings)? Have you considered incorporating these more advanced language models in your framework?

2. Could you provide qualitative examples of the enriched networks (e.g., visualizations of the network structures with and without semantic enrichment) to help readers understand how the semantic edges change the network topology and potentially capture meaningful linguistic patterns related to MCI?

3. The paper mentions that "short texts have almost linear networks" which impairs classification. How does your enriched network approach specifically address this limitation, and what is the minimum text length needed for your approach to be effective? This is particularly important given that the ABCD dataset has very short narratives (average 5.23 sentences per narrative).

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to MCI detection from speech transcripts with clear practical advantages over previous methods. The comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets is a significant strength. However, the lack of comparison to recent deep learning approaches and the absence of qualitative analysis limit the paper's impact. With revisions addressing these weaknesses, particularly by adding a comparison to state-of-the-art methods and providing qualitative insights, this paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field of computational linguistics applied to healthcare.

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** after addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly the comparison to state-of-the-art methods and the qualitative analysis. The method is promising and the evaluation is robust, but these additions would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.
