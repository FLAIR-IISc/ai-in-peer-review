

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features require extensive manual preprocessing to handle disfluencies in speech transcripts, while their network-based approach can automatically represent these texts with semantic information. They evaluate their method on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) and report that their enriched network features (CNE) outperform traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts**: This is the primary contribution. While complex networks have been used in NLP before, applying them to MCI detection with semantic enrichment via word embeddings represents a genuinely new direction in computational linguistics applied to healthcare.

2. **Automated processing of speech transcripts without manual preprocessing**: The paper correctly identifies that traditional approaches require expensive manual revision of transcriptions to handle disfluencies, while their method can work directly on raw transcripts, making it more suitable for fully automated clinical systems.

3. **Demonstration that topological network features can be effective for MCI detection**: The paper provides evidence that network-based features can outperform traditional linguistic features, offering new insights about what linguistic aspects are most relevant for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. **Strong novelty in application**: This is the most significant strength of the paper. The authors have identified a novel application of existing computational techniques (complex networks with word embeddings) to a specific medical domain (MCI detection from speech transcripts) that has not been previously explored. This represents a bold new direction in computational linguistics applied to healthcare, which aligns with ACL's interest in innovative applications.

2. **Addresses a real clinical problem**: The paper correctly identifies a significant challenge in neuropsychological assessment (the need for manual transcription preprocessing) and proposes an automated solution. This practical contribution has clear potential to impact clinical practice, making it more than just a theoretical exercise.

3. **Clear demonstration of competitive results**: The paper shows that their enriched network approach (CNE) achieves comparable or better results than traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets. While the absolute accuracy rates (60-80%) aren't groundbreaking, they're consistent with previous work in the field, which validates the approach's viability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches**: The paper compares its method to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words, but fails to compare with contemporary deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT, LSTM) that have become standard in text classification. This is a significant omission given the current state of NLP research, as it makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed method is still relevant compared to more modern techniques.

2. **Limited theoretical explanation of why network features work better**: While the paper reports that CNE features outperform other approaches, it doesn't provide deep insights into *why* these features work better for MCI detection. Understanding the underlying mechanisms would be a more valuable contribution than simply demonstrating that the approach works.

3. **Small dataset sizes and modest performance improvements**: The datasets are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD patients for Cookie Theft), and the performance improvements over the best baseline (e.g., 2.3% in Cookie Theft) are modest. This raises questions about the statistical significance and practical utility of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., BERT, LSTM) that have been used for text classification in medical domains? Given that these models have achieved strong results in many NLP tasks, it would be valuable to see how your method compares.

2. Can you provide more insight into why network-based features work better than traditional linguistic features for MCI detection? What specific aspects of the network topology are most informative for distinguishing between MCI and control groups?

3. The paper mentions that enriched networks (CNE) outperform unenriched networks (CN), but doesn't explain why adding semantic information via word embeddings improves the representation for this specific task. Can you elaborate on this?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a strong contribution by introducing a novel application of complex networks with word embeddings to a new domain (MCI detection from speech transcripts). While the methodological aspects are not groundbreaking, the application represents a bold new direction that addresses a real clinical problem. The paper demonstrates that this approach can work effectively with minimal preprocessing, which is a significant practical advantage.

The weaknesses noted (particularly the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches) are concerning, but I'm willing to overlook this minor flaw given the significant novelty of the application. The paper's contribution is not incremental - it opens a new research direction for computational linguistics applied to healthcare, which aligns with ACL's interest in innovative applications.

The authors should address the questions above in their response, particularly regarding how their approach compares to modern deep learning methods, as this would strengthen their contribution. But based on the novelty of the application and the practical significance, I recommend acceptance.
