

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to model speech transcripts for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from neuropsychological assessments. The authors model narratives as word adjacency networks, enrich them with semantic information via word embeddings, and extract topological features for classification. They evaluate their approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) using various machine learning classifiers and compare against linguistic features and Bag-of-Words representations.

## Main Contributions
1. Application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to model speech transcripts for MCI detection
2. Demonstration that network-based features can outperform linguistic features in MCI detection tasks
3. Development of a fully automated pipeline for MCI detection that avoids manual preprocessing

## Strengths
1. **Novel Application of Network Theory**: The application of complex network analysis to MCI detection from speech transcripts represents a novel approach in the field. The use of word embeddings to enrich the networks addresses a key limitation of traditional network models for short texts.

2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation**: The evaluation across three different datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with varying language (English/Portuguese) and text length provides a robust assessment of the approach's generalizability.

3. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of three feature types (network topology, linguistic features, and BoW) and demonstrates that network features can be competitive with linguistic features, which is significant for the field.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Methodological Contradictions (Major Issue)
The paper claims to avoid manual preprocessing by using complex networks, stating: "This process is obviously expensive, which has motivated us to use complex networks in the present study to model transcriptions and avoid a manual preprocessing step." However, in Section 4.1.1, they explicitly describe "We automatically removed filled pauses such as uh, um, er, and ah" and "short false starts" and "repetition." This is a form of preprocessing that contradicts their main claim. The authors are not avoiding preprocessing, but rather replacing manual preprocessing with automatic preprocessing, which undermines their core contribution. This is a significant methodological inconsistency that weakens the paper's central claim.

### 2. Inconsistent Terminology and Presentation (Major Issue)
The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism:
- In Table 2 and Table 3, demographic data uses commas instead of decimal points (e.g., "72,0 (7.4)" instead of "72.0 (7.4)"), which is a critical error in scientific reporting.
- References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "? \Q2016\E" instead of standard year notation, inconsistent punctuation).
- Figure 2 caption states "the same co-occurrence network enriched with semantic information" but refers to Figure 2(b), while Figure 2(a) is labeled as "co-occurrence network" without enrichment. This creates confusion about which figure represents the enriched network.

### 3. Statistical Reporting Flaws (Major Issue)
The paper claims improvements like "a 10% of improvement compared to the best individual classifier" without providing statistical significance tests. In Table 5, the ensemble accuracy (0.6000) for CNE is lower than the SVM-RBF accuracy (0.6500) for CNE, which contradicts the expected behavior of ensemble methods. The paper fails to explain this inconsistency, raising questions about the validity of their experimental design and results.

### 4. Technical Inconsistency in Feature Description (Moderate Issue)
The paper claims: "The metrics affected by constituency and dependency parsing were not used because they are not robust to deal with disfluencies." However, in Supplementary Material A.2, they list "Syntactical Complexity: only cross entropy" as a metric used in Coh-Metrix-Dementia. Cross entropy is a statistical measure that typically requires parsing, which contradicts their claim. This inconsistency suggests either a misunderstanding of the metrics or a misrepresentation of their methodology.

### 5. Inadequate Explanation of Key Parameters (Moderate Issue)
The paper states: "the threshold parameter was optimized with best values being 0.7 in Cookie Theft dataset and 0.4 in both Cinderella and ABCD dataset." However, it never explains what threshold parameter they are referring to or how it was optimized. In a classification context, this is a critical omission that prevents reproducibility and understanding of the experimental setup.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "avoid a manual preprocessing step" but describes automatic removal of disfluencies. How is this automatic preprocessing fundamentally different from manual preprocessing, and why is it considered "fully automated" as claimed in the paper?

2. In Table 4, the ensemble accuracy for CNE (0.6042) is lower than the SVM-RBF accuracy for CNE (0.6264). How can an ensemble of classifiers have lower accuracy than the best individual classifier, and what does this imply about the ensemble approach?

3. The paper claims that "short texts have almost linear networks" and "the classification of small texts using networks can be impaired." However, the Cookie Theft dataset consists of short English descriptions, yet they report good accuracy (65% for multi-view learning). How did they address the issue of short text classification with networks as described in their methodology?

4. In Supplementary Material A.2, you list "Syntactical Complexity: only cross entropy" as a metric used. How is cross entropy related to syntactic complexity, and why is it not affected by parsing as claimed in Section 4.2.2?

5. The paper mentions "the threshold parameter" but never explains what it is or how it was optimized. Please clarify this critical parameter for reproducibility.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting application of complex networks to MCI detection, the methodological contradictions, formatting inconsistencies, statistical reporting flaws, and technical inaccuracies are too substantial to overlook. The core claim of avoiding manual preprocessing is contradicted by the described automatic preprocessing, and the inconsistent presentation of results and methodology undermines the paper's credibility. These are not minor issues but rather fundamental flaws that prevent the paper from meeting the standards expected for ACL publication. The authors should thoroughly revise their manuscript to address these issues before resubmission.
