

# Peer Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors demonstrate that this approach can effectively distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls without requiring extensive manual preprocessing of transcripts, which is a significant practical advantage over traditional linguistic feature extraction methods.

The main contributions, in order of strength as I see them:

1. **A novel application of complex network modeling with semantic enrichment for MCI detection from speech transcripts**, which avoids the need for manual preprocessing steps required by traditional linguistic approaches.

2. **A systematic evaluation across multiple datasets and languages** (English and Portuguese) that demonstrates the robustness of their approach across different narrative types and linguistic contexts.

3. **Demonstration that enriched complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features**, especially when applied to raw transcripts without manual correction for disfluencies.

## Strengths

1. **Significant practical contribution to clinical applications**: The paper addresses an important healthcare problem (early MCI detection) with a method that could be implemented in real-world clinical settings without requiring linguistic expertise or manual transcription correction. This is particularly valuable given the growing aging population and the need for scalable diagnostic tools.

2. **Strong evaluation across multiple datasets and languages**: The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) in both English and Portuguese, which demonstrates the robustness of their method across different linguistic contexts and narrative structures. This comprehensive evaluation strengthens the validity of their claims.

3. **Competitive results with clinical relevance**: The reported accuracies (65-80%) are competitive with state-of-the-art methods in the field (60-85% reported in literature). The paper correctly contextualizes these results within the broader literature, acknowledging that MCI detection is inherently challenging due to the subtle nature of cognitive changes.

4. **Clear methodological innovation**: The enrichment of complex networks with word embeddings to capture semantic relationships is a well-justified and technically sound approach to overcoming the limitations of traditional co-occurrence networks for short texts with disfluencies.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technical approach**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core methodology (complex networks enriched with word embeddings) builds directly on existing work by Amancio (2015) and Perozzi et al. (2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these prior works or demonstrate how this specific application advances the state-of-the-art in complex network modeling for NLP.

2. **Inadequate comparison with recent deep learning approaches**: The paper compares against traditional linguistic features and BoW, but doesn't address how their method compares to more recent deep learning approaches (e.g., transformer-based models) that have been successfully applied to similar clinical NLP tasks. This is a significant omission given the rapid advancement in this area.

3. **Small dataset sizes and limited statistical analysis**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD in Cookie Theft), which limits the statistical power of the results. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why these small samples are sufficient, nor does it address potential overfitting concerns with such limited data.

4. **Lack of discussion on clinical deployment challenges**: The paper focuses on the technical approach but doesn't address key practical challenges for clinical deployment, such as handling real-world speech transcripts with severe disfluencies, background noise, or multi-speaker conversations. The reliance on high-quality transcriptions (even if automated) is a significant limitation for real-world implementation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent deep learning methods (e.g., transformer-based models) that have been applied to similar MCI detection tasks? Have you considered incorporating these approaches in your framework?

2. Given the small dataset sizes, could you provide more details on your cross-validation strategy and how you addressed potential overfitting concerns?

3. How would your method handle transcripts with more severe disfluencies, background noise, or multiple speakers—common challenges in real clinical speech data?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the application of NLP techniques in clinical diagnostics by demonstrating that complex networks enriched with word embeddings can effectively detect MCI from speech transcripts without requiring manual linguistic preprocessing. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are clinically relevant. However, the limited novelty of the core technical approach and lack of comparison with recent deep learning methods are significant weaknesses. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written and makes a compelling case for the practical utility of this approach in clinical settings. With some clarifications regarding the novelty of the method and better comparison with state-of-the-art techniques, this paper would be a strong contribution to ACL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
