

# ACL Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks (specifically, word adjacency networks) enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors claim their approach outperforms traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches. They evaluate on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), reporting up to 80% accuracy in the ABCD dataset.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Minimal methodological novelty**: The core approach (complex networks with word embeddings) has been established in prior work for text classification (Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c; de Arruda et al., 2016; Perozzi et al., 2014). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from previous work.

2. **Marginal performance improvements**: The reported improvements over baselines are small (2.3-10%) across datasets, which may not be clinically meaningful for MCI detection.

3. **Lack of rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper doesn't compare with recent deep learning approaches that have been applied to similar medical NLP tasks.

## Strengths

1. The paper is well-structured and clearly written with a good introduction to the problem and related work.

2. The authors used multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) to demonstrate the approach's applicability across languages.

3. The paper provides a clear description of the proposed methodology and the features used.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The core approach is not novel. The use of word adjacency networks for text analysis has been extensively explored in prior work (e.g., Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c, 2013; de Arruda et al., 2016), and the enrichment with word embeddings is based on Perozzi et al. (2014). The authors cite these papers but don't convincingly demonstrate how their application to MCI detection constitutes a meaningful contribution.

2. **Marginal performance improvements**: The paper claims their method outperforms traditional approaches, but the improvements are marginal (2.3-10% across datasets). In medical diagnosis contexts, such small improvements may not be clinically significant. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for these improvements, making it impossible to determine if the results are meaningful or due to chance.

3. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper cites previous work but doesn't compare with the most recent and best-performing methods in the field. For example, they don't compare with recent deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT, LSTM) that have been successfully applied to MCI detection from speech. The comparison is limited to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words, which are not the current state-of-the-art for this task.

4. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD transcripts in Cookie Theft), which makes the evaluation less robust. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for these small sample sizes or address potential data leakage issues. Small datasets increase the risk of overfitting and make it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the method's effectiveness.

5. **Lack of ablation studies and feature contribution analysis**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component (network features, linguistic features, and BoW features). The authors claim that combining features improves performance, but they don't show how much each component contributes to the final accuracy.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent deep learning methods (e.g., BERT, LSTM) that have been applied to MCI detection from speech in the literature? Have you considered using these state-of-the-art methods for comparison?

2. The paper reports marginal improvements over baselines (2.3-10%). Can you provide statistical significance testing for these improvements? Without this, it's impossible to determine if the reported results are meaningful or due to random variation.

3. The Cookie Theft dataset contains only 43 MCI transcripts. How do you address concerns about the statistical power of your evaluation given these small sample sizes? Would you consider using techniques like bootstrapping or cross-validation to better assess the robustness of your results?

4. Why did you choose to use the specific topological metrics you did (PageRank, Betweenness, etc.)? Were there any metrics you considered but rejected, and if so, why?

5. The paper mentions that the method can be used for a fully automated system, but doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to traditional methods. How does the computational cost of your method compare to existing methods for MCI detection from speech?

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper presents an incremental extension of existing methods rather than a significant contribution to the field. The marginal performance improvements over baselines are not sufficient to justify the novelty claim, especially without rigorous statistical validation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods. The small dataset sizes further limit the reliability of the results. While the paper is well-written, it fails to meet the high standards required for publication at ACL, particularly regarding methodological novelty and rigorous validation.
