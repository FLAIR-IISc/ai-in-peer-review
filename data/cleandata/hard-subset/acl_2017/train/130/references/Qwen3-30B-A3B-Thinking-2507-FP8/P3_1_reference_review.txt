

# Peer Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling text as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features require manual preprocessing and are sensitive to disfluencies, while their approach avoids these limitations. They model narratives as word adjacency networks, enrich them with semantic information via word embeddings, extract topological features, and use these features with machine learning classifiers to distinguish between MCI and control participants. The method is evaluated across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with varying language (English/Portuguese) and text length.

## Main Contributions
1. **Network enrichment with word embeddings** for representing short speech transcripts without manual preprocessing
2. **Demonstration that network-based features outperform linguistic features** for MCI detection
3. **A fully automated pipeline** for processing speech transcripts, eliminating the need for manual revision

## Strengths

1. **Novel methodological contribution**: The enrichment of complex networks with word embeddings to address the small text size problem is genuinely novel in the context of MCI detection. The authors correctly identify that short texts produce linear networks with limited topological information, and their solution (adding semantic edges via cosine similarity) is well-motivated and directly addresses this limitation. This approach effectively leverages the semantic information captured by word embeddings to create more informative networks.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates that the enriched complex networks (CNE) outperform traditional linguistic features (LM) in most cases, with the highest accuracy (80%) achieved on the ABCD dataset using the CNE-LM-BoW multi-view approach. The results are comparable to the state-of-the-art (60-85% accuracy), which is significant given the challenging nature of MCI detection from speech.

3. **Practical automation advantage**: The authors successfully demonstrate a fully automated pipeline that avoids manual preprocessing steps (sentence segmentation, disfluency removal, grammatical correction) that have been major bottlenecks in prior work. This is particularly important for real-world clinical applications where manual annotation is costly and time-consuming.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: While the paper mentions accuracy ranges of 60-85% for prior work, it fails to provide a meaningful comparison with specific SOTA methods on the same datasets. For instance, Vincze et al. (2016) reported 78.7% accuracy on the same ABCD dataset using linguistic and acoustic features, yet the authors don't compare their 80% result to this. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of their method.

2. **Small dataset sizes and limited statistical analysis**: The datasets are quite small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD for Cookie Theft), raising concerns about statistical significance and generalizability. The paper uses 5-fold cross-validation but doesn't report standard deviations or p-values to assess the reliability of the results. Given the small sample sizes, the reported accuracy improvements (e.g., 4.71% on ABCD) may not be statistically significant.

3. **Superficial treatment of network enrichment**: The paper lacks detail on how the cosine similarity threshold was determined and how sensitive results are to this parameter. The authors mention using "a given threshold" but don't explain how they chose it or whether they conducted ablation studies to demonstrate its importance. This is a critical omission for reproducibility.

4. **Lack of analysis on feature importance**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of which specific network features contribute most to the classification performance. Without this, it's unclear whether the improvement is due to the overall network structure or specific topological measures.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you determine the optimal cosine similarity threshold for creating new edges? What ablation studies did you perform to assess the sensitivity of results to this threshold?

2. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with the state-of-the-art methods on the same datasets? Specifically, how does your 80% accuracy on ABCD compare to Vincze et al.'s 78.7% on the same dataset?

3. Given the small sample sizes (e.g., 43 MCI cases in Cookie Theft), what statistical tests did you perform to ensure the reported accuracy improvements are significant?

4. The paper states that "the results for the three datasets show that characterizing transcriptions into complexity networks is competitive with other traditional methods." However, the accuracy improvements over linguistic features are modest (e.g., 2.3% on Cookie Theft). Can you provide a more nuanced analysis of when the network approach is particularly beneficial compared to linguistic features?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to MCI detection from speech transcripts using enriched complex networks. The method addresses a genuine limitation in the field (manual preprocessing requirements) and demonstrates competitive performance. However, the paper's claims about the method's superiority are weakened by insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, small dataset sizes without proper statistical validation, and insufficient detail on the network enrichment process. With stronger comparisons to existing work and more rigorous statistical analysis, this could be a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes a modest but interesting contribution that would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions required to address the weaknesses, particularly the comparison with SOTA methods and statistical analysis of results)
