

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling narratives as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic feature extraction for MCI detection requires extensive manual preprocessing (e.g., lemmatization, disfluency removal) and is limited by the availability of robust NLP tools, especially for languages like Portuguese. They introduce a method that constructs word co-occurrence networks from transcripts and enriches them with semantic information using word embeddings, then extracts topological features from these networks for classification.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection**: The core contribution is applying complex network modeling with word embedding enrichment to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts, particularly addressing the challenges of disfluencies and the lack of robust NLP tools for Portuguese.

2. **Demonstration of topological features outperforming linguistic features**: The paper shows that network-based features can outperform traditional linguistic features, especially when dealing with disfluent speech transcripts that would require manual preprocessing for linguistic feature extraction.

3. **Fully automated approach avoiding manual preprocessing**: The method avoids the need for manual sentence segmentation, lemmatization, and disfluency removal that is typically required for linguistic feature extraction, making it more practical for real-world implementation.

## Strengths
1. **Meaningful problem with real-world impact**: The paper addresses an important healthcare problem - early detection of MCI, which can lead to earlier intervention and better patient outcomes. The potential for a practical, automated screening tool has significant clinical relevance.

2. **Practical advantage for resource-constrained languages**: The authors effectively demonstrate how their approach avoids the need for manual preprocessing that is particularly problematic for Portuguese (and other languages with limited NLP resources), which is a strong practical contribution.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates the approach on three different datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) in both English and Portuguese, with multiple machine learning algorithms and feature combinations. This provides a robust comparison of the method's performance across different contexts.

4. **Clear theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for why the network-based approach might work for this problem, citing relevant literature on complex networks in NLP and text representation.

5. **Well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear descriptions of the methodology, datasets, and evaluation procedures, making it easy to follow the authors' approach.

## Weaknesses
1. **Questionable novelty of the core methodology**: The use of complex networks for text representation is not new (Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c; Mihalcea and Radev, 2011), and the enrichment with word embeddings is directly based on Perozzi et al. (2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their specific application to MCI detection from speech transcripts differs from prior work in text representation, making it difficult to assess the novelty.

2. **Marginal performance improvements**: The best accuracy reported is 80% (ABCD dataset), which is on the lower end of what's reported in the field (60-85%). The improvements over baseline methods are marginal (e.g., 2.3% improvement for Cookie Theft, 10% for Cinderella), which is not particularly compelling for a medical application where higher accuracy is crucial.

3. **Small dataset sizes limit statistical significance**: The datasets used are quite small (e.g., Cookie Theft has only 64 samples total), making it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the method's effectiveness. Small sample sizes also increase the risk of overfitting, especially with the large number of features extracted (106 for English, 58 for Portuguese).

4. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper doesn't compare against recent deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT-based models) that have shown strong performance in related NLP tasks. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess where the proposed method stands in the current landscape of MCI detection.

5. **Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges**: The paper doesn't address how the method would perform in real-world clinical settings, including factors like varying speech quality, accents, background noise, or differences in narrative length that would be present in actual patient transcripts.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your method compare to recent deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT-based models) that have been applied to MCI detection from speech transcripts? Have you attempted to integrate your network features with these more recent models?

2. The authors claim their approach "can be applied to multiple languages" but the experiments are limited to English and Portuguese. How would you expect the method to perform on languages with different grammatical structures or with less available word embeddings?

3. Given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 64 total samples in Cookie Theft), how did you address the risk of overfitting, especially with the large number of features extracted (106 for English, 58 for Portuguese)? Would you recommend cross-validation strategies beyond 5-fold for these small datasets?

4. The paper states that the method avoids manual preprocessing, but still performs automated preprocessing (e.g., removing filled pauses, short false starts). How would you expect the method to handle transcripts with more severe disfluencies or different types of disfluencies that might not be captured by your current preprocessing steps?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend **rejection** of this paper. While the paper addresses an important healthcare problem and provides a practical approach that avoids manual preprocessing for Portuguese, the core methodology lacks sufficient novelty, the performance improvements are marginal, and the small dataset sizes limit the significance of the results. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, stronger evidence of the method's practical value, and a more compelling demonstration of the novelty of their approach.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Conduct a thorough comparison with recent deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT-based models) applied to MCI detection from speech transcripts. This would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution by showing where it stands relative to current best practices.

2. Address the small dataset issue by either collecting additional data or using more sophisticated techniques to handle small sample sizes (e.g., transfer learning, data augmentation, or Bayesian methods).

3. Provide a more detailed discussion of how the method would perform in real-world clinical settings, including potential limitations and challenges.

4. Clarify the novelty of the approach by more precisely distinguishing it from prior work on complex networks in NLP and text representation.

5. Include a more detailed analysis of which network features are most predictive of MCI, which would help clinicians understand which aspects of language are most informative for diagnosis.

While the paper has some merit, the lack of sufficient novelty, marginal performance improvements, and small dataset sizes prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field. With substantial revisions addressing these issues, the paper could be strengthened for potential acceptance at a future venue.
