- Novel application: Models speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings for MCI detection, avoiding extensive manual preprocessing.  
- Systematic evaluation: Tested across multiple datasets and languages (English, Portuguese) showing robustness across narrative types.  
- Performance: Enriched complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features, especially on raw transcripts.  

- Practical significance: Method addresses an important clinical need and could be implemented without linguistic expertise.  
- Robust evaluation: Uses three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) to demonstrate cross-linguistic and cross-narrative robustness.  
- Competitive results: Reported accuracies (65–80%) are in line with state-of-the-art ranges for MCI detection.  
- Methodological clarity: Semantic enrichment of networks is a well-justified approach to handle short, disfluent texts.  

- Limited novelty: Core technique builds on prior work (Amancio 2015; Perozzi et al. 2014) and lacks clear differentiation from those methods.  
- Missing deep learning comparison: No evaluation against recent transformer-based or other deep learning approaches.  
- Small sample sizes: Datasets are small (e.g., 43 MCI, 21 AD), raising concerns about statistical power and overfitting.  
- Deployment gaps: Paper lacks discussion of real-world clinical challenges (severe disfluencies, noise, multi-speaker scenarios).  

- Question: How does the method compare to recent deep learning/transformer approaches, and have those been considered?  
- Question: Provide more details on cross-validation strategy and measures taken to prevent overfitting with small datasets.  
- Question: How would the method handle transcripts with severe disfluencies, background noise, or multiple speakers?  

- Overall assessment: Sound, clinically relevant approach with comprehensive evaluation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify novelty and include comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
