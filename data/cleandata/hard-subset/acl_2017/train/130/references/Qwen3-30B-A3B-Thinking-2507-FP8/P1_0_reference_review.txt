

# Peer Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic analysis methods require extensive manual preprocessing to handle disfluencies (repetitions, revisions, etc.), which is time-consuming and expensive. Their approach instead models transcripts as word co-occurrence networks and enriches them with semantic information via word embeddings (using cosine similarity between word vectors), avoiding the need for manual preprocessing. 

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. The demonstration that complex networks enriched with word embeddings can effectively model speech transcripts for MCI detection without requiring manual preprocessing of disfluencies
2. The empirical validation that network-based features outperform traditional linguistic features for MCI detection on multiple datasets
3. The successful application of multi-view learning to combine network, linguistic, and bag-of-words features, achieving improved accuracy over individual feature spaces

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful healthcare problem with practical potential**: The paper tackles an important problem in early detection of cognitive decline, which has significant clinical implications. The proposed approach could potentially lead to more accessible diagnostic tools that don't require extensive manual annotation, making it valuable for real-world clinical applications where resources are limited.

2. **Good theoretical foundation and well-motivated methodology**: The authors provide a solid theoretical basis for using complex networks in NLP tasks and clearly explain why their enrichment approach with word embeddings is suitable for handling short, disfluent texts. The adaptation of Perozzi et al.'s approach to enrich networks with semantic information is well-justified and addresses a key limitation of standard co-occurrence networks for short texts.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates the approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) in both English and Portuguese, providing robust validation of the method's generalizability. This multi-language and multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the paper's claims significantly.

4. **Practical contribution of avoiding manual preprocessing**: The claim that their approach avoids the expensive manual preprocessing required by traditional linguistic analysis methods is particularly valuable, as this is a significant bottleneck in the field. The paper demonstrates this advantage through their experimental setup where they automatically removed disfluencies rather than manually revising texts.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core methodology of using complex networks for text representation is not new (Mihalcea & Radev, 2011; Amancio, 2015). The enrichment with word embeddings is a straightforward extension of existing work (Perozzi et al., 2014), and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work in the literature. The novelty claim is overstated.

2. **Marginal performance gains**: The improvements over baseline methods are modest (max 80% accuracy, with only 4.71% improvement over the best individual classifier). Given the clinical importance of this task, such marginal gains may not be sufficient for practical adoption, especially compared to more recent deep learning approaches that have shown higher accuracy in similar tasks.

3. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares only with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words, but fails to compare with more recent deep learning approaches (e.g., LSTM, Transformer-based models) that have shown superior performance in speech analysis tasks. This omission weakens the paper's claim that their approach is competitive.

4. **Insufficient clinical evaluation metrics**: The paper reports only accuracy, without providing precision, recall, F1-score, or ROC curves, which are critical for medical applications where class imbalance and different error types have different clinical consequences. The lack of these metrics makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the approach.

5. **Unclear disfluency handling**: The paper claims their approach avoids manual preprocessing of disfluencies, but the details of how they automatically handled disfluencies (beyond removing "uh" and "um") are unclear. The methodology section states they "kept other disfluencies (revisions, elaboration, paraphasias and comments about the task)" but doesn't explain how these might affect network construction or how they compare to other disfluency handling approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent deep learning methods for MCI detection from speech (e.g., using LSTM or Transformer models)? Have you considered comparing with these state-of-the-art methods?

2. The paper mentions that their approach avoids manual preprocessing, but it's unclear how the automatic disfluency removal compares to manual correction in terms of preserving clinically relevant information. Could you provide more details on the specific disfluency handling strategy and how it compares to manual annotation?

3. The paper reports accuracy as the sole metric, but for clinical applications, other metrics like precision, recall, and F1-score are critical. Could you provide these additional metrics, particularly for the most promising model (CNE-LM-BoW on ABCD)?

4. The paper claims that complex networks enriched with embeddings outperform linguistic features, but linguistic features are heavily dependent on grammatical structure. How do you explain the performance gap when the Cinderella dataset was manually processed (Table 6) versus the automatically processed version (Table 5), where linguistic features performed much better?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses an important problem with a potentially valuable approach, but the novelty is limited, the performance gains are modest, and the evaluation is not comprehensive enough. With significant revisions to strengthen the novelty claims, include comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and provide more clinically relevant evaluation metrics, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, the paper is borderline for acceptance at ACL.
