

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings. They extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification between healthy controls and MCI patients. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), with results showing that the enriched network features outperform traditional linguistic features in some cases, particularly when combined through multi-view learning.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel application of complex networks with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts** - This represents a significant new application of an existing NLP technique to a critical healthcare problem.
2. **Practical advantage of avoiding manual preprocessing** - The method eliminates the need for manual revision of transcripts (e.g., removing disfluencies, inserting omitted subjects), which is a major practical advantage over existing approaches.
3. **Demonstration of competitive accuracy with traditional linguistic features** - The approach achieves comparable or better accuracy than traditional linguistic metrics, with improvements when combining feature spaces through multi-view learning.

## Strengths
1. **Novel application in a critical healthcare domain**: The paper makes a significant contribution by applying complex network analysis (with word embeddings) to MCI detection from speech transcripts. This is a novel application of network-based text representation in healthcare NLP, addressing an important real-world problem where early detection of MCI could improve patient outcomes.

2. **Practical advantage of automated preprocessing**: The paper correctly identifies a major pain point in existing approaches (the need for manual revision of transcripts to remove disfluencies and grammatical errors). Their method avoids this step entirely, which is a significant practical contribution for real-world implementation of MCI detection systems.

3. **Competitive results with modest improvements**: The method achieves competitive accuracy compared to traditional linguistic features (e.g., 75% accuracy on the Cinderella dataset), with meaningful improvements when combining features through multi-view learning (10% improvement over best individual classifier on Cinderella). These results are comparable to the state-of-the-art in this domain.

4. **Cross-lingual evaluation**: The paper evaluates the approach on both English (Cookie Theft) and Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD) datasets, demonstrating the method's applicability across languages, which is valuable for broader adoption.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty in core methodology**: The core technique of using word embeddings to enrich networks is not novel (Perozzi et al., 2014 already proposed this). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from this prior work, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Modest performance gains**: While the results are competitive, the improvements over existing methods are modest (2.3-10% in most cases). This limits the significance of the contribution, especially when compared to recent deep learning approaches in NLP.

3. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning methods**: The paper doesn't compare with recent transformer-based approaches (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have achieved strong results in similar classification tasks. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true significance of their approach.

4. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI patients in Cookie Theft dataset), which limits the statistical power of the results and raises questions about generalizability.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your approach compare to recent transformer-based methods for MCI detection (e.g., using BERT or other pre-trained language models)? Have you considered incorporating these more recent NLP techniques for comparison?

2. The paper states that "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" - can you provide more insight into why this might be the case, beyond just the empirical results? What specific aspects of the enriched networks capture MCI-related language patterns?

3. The paper mentions that "the classification of small texts using networks can be impaired" but then uses short transcripts as input. How does the network enrichment with word embeddings specifically address this limitation for short texts?

## Overall Recommendation
**Accept**

While the core methodology isn't entirely novel, the paper makes a significant contribution by applying this technique to a novel healthcare domain (MCI detection from speech transcripts) with a clear practical advantage (avoiding manual preprocessing). The results are competitive with state-of-the-art in the domain, and the approach addresses a real-world problem with a practical solution. Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field that should be accepted. The modest improvements are justified by the novel application and practical advantages, and the paper would benefit from more thorough comparison to recent deep learning approaches in future work.
