

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes using complex networks (word adjacency networks) enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts as networks, enrich them with semantic information via word embeddings, and extract topological features to distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls. They evaluate their approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) using multiple classifiers and feature combinations, reporting improved performance over traditional linguistic features.

## Primary Contributions
1. **Novel application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection**: The paper demonstrates a fresh approach to analyzing speech transcripts for MCI detection, combining graph theory with semantic word representations.
2. **Fully automated processing pipeline**: The authors successfully avoid manual preprocessing steps typically required for linguistic analysis, making their approach more practical for real-world clinical settings.
3. **Empirical demonstration of network features' efficacy**: The paper provides evidence that topological network features can be as effective as (or better than) traditional linguistic features for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. **Practical contribution to clinical applications**: The work addresses a significant real-world problem (early MCI detection) with potential for clinical impact. The authors successfully demonstrate a fully automated pipeline that doesn't require manual annotation or preprocessing, which is a major practical advantage over previous approaches that relied on manual revisions of transcripts.

2. **Compelling empirical results**: The paper shows that enriched complex networks (CNE) consistently outperform traditional linguistic features across multiple datasets. The 65-80% accuracy rates are comparable to state-of-the-art methods (as the authors note), and the 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset with multi-view learning is significant for this challenging task.

3. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The authors provide a solid foundation for their approach by explaining how complex network properties relate to linguistic analysis, and they thoughtfully justify their preprocessing decisions (e.g., skipping lemmatization to avoid introducing errors in disfluent speech).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing NLP work**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technique of enriching word adjacency networks with embeddings is not new (Perozzi et al., 2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their specific application to MCI differs from previous uses of similar techniques in other NLP domains (e.g., text classification, authorship attribution). This makes the methodological contribution less impactful than claimed.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with traditional linguistic features but doesn't benchmark against recent deep learning approaches for MCI detection (e.g., transformer-based models). Given the rapid advances in NLP, this omission is significant. The paper states "accuracies found here are comparable to values reported by other authors," but doesn't provide a direct comparison with the best recent methods.

3. **Lack of clear explanation for why network features work**: The paper demonstrates that network features are effective but doesn't provide a compelling explanation for *why* they work better than traditional linguistic features. For instance, the paper doesn't analyze which specific network properties correlate with MCI (e.g., does reduced clustering coefficient indicate cognitive impairment?). This limits the theoretical contribution.

4. **Questionable significance of the 10% improvement**: The 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset (from 65% to 75%) is impressive, but the paper doesn't address whether this improvement is statistically significant or if it's due to dataset characteristics rather than the method itself. The small dataset sizes (e.g., 20 MCI subjects in the Cinderella dataset) make such improvements potentially unstable.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" but doesn't provide evidence of why these properties are more relevant for MCI detection. Could the authors provide specific examples of network properties that correlate with cognitive impairment?

2. The paper states that their approach "avoids a manual preprocessing step" but doesn't quantify the time or resource savings compared to previous approaches. Could the authors provide a comparison of processing times or human effort required?

3. The accuracy rates (65-80%) are comparable to existing methods, but the paper doesn't explain why these rates are considered good for MCI detection. Could the authors clarify the clinical significance of these accuracy levels in the context of real-world diagnostic applications?

## Overall Recommendation
This is a solid paper with a clear practical contribution to a clinically relevant problem. The approach is well-executed, and the results are promising. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to better contextualize the novelty and provide deeper analysis of why the network features work well for this specific task.

The paper's main contribution lies in demonstrating the practical viability of a fully automated pipeline for MCI detection from speech transcripts, which has clear clinical utility. While the methodological innovation isn't groundbreaking, the application to a high-impact medical problem makes this work valuable for the ACL community interested in practical NLP applications.
