

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling narratives as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors create word adjacency networks from speech transcripts and enrich them with semantic information from word embeddings using cosine similarity. They extract topological features from these networks and evaluate their performance in distinguishing between MCI and control participants across three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese). The paper compares their approach with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, and explores combinations of these feature types using multi-view learning.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Application of enriched complex network modeling to MCI detection**: The primary contribution is the adaptation of complex network analysis with word embeddings to the specific domain of MCI detection from speech transcripts, which appears to be a novel application of these techniques.
2. **Demonstration of competitive performance of network-based features**: The paper shows that topological features from enriched networks can achieve comparable or better performance than traditional linguistic features for MCI detection.
3. **Exploration of multi-view learning for feature combination**: The authors investigate combining different feature types (network, linguistic, BoW) through multi-view learning, which provides modest performance improvements.

## Strengths
1. **Important clinical application**: The paper addresses a significant clinical problem with real-world impact. Early detection of MCI is crucial for timely intervention, and automated analysis of speech transcripts could provide a cost-effective, non-invasive screening tool. The authors correctly emphasize the importance of early diagnosis for potential non-pharmacological interventions.

2. **Thorough evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates the approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different languages (English and Portuguese) and different narrative lengths. This demonstrates the robustness of the approach across different speech samples and linguistic contexts. The results are consistent across datasets, which strengthens the validity of the findings.

3. **Clear methodology and reproducibility**: The paper provides a detailed description of the network construction process, enrichment with word embeddings, and feature extraction. The preprocessing steps (including handling of disfluencies) are well-documented, making the approach reproducible. The use of standard evaluation protocols (5-fold cross-validation) with clear metrics (accuracy) adds to the methodological rigor.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core techniques (complex networks and word embeddings) are well-established in NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate between the novelty of the application versus the novelty of the methods themselves. The enrichment method (adding edges based on cosine similarity) is a straightforward adaptation of existing techniques (Perozzi et al., 2014), and the topological features used are standard in complex network analysis. This limits the paper's contribution to the broader NLP research community.

2. **Modest performance gains with unclear clinical significance**: The performance improvements over baseline methods are relatively modest (2-10% accuracy gains), and the paper doesn't discuss whether these improvements are clinically meaningful. For instance, the best result on the ABCD dataset is 80% accuracy, which is within the range reported in previous work (60-85%) but not substantially better. Without context on what constitutes a clinically useful improvement, it's difficult to assess the practical value of the work.

3. **Insufficient justification for the approach**: The authors claim that disfluencies in speech transcripts make traditional linguistic features less effective, but they don't provide strong evidence for this claim. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the complex network approach specifically handles disfluencies better than traditional methods, nor does it provide a clear theoretical rationale for why topological features would be more robust to disfluencies than linguistic features. The comparison between manually revised data (Table 6) and automatically processed data (Table 5) suggests that linguistic features perform better on clean text, but the paper doesn't explain why this is the case or how the network approach overcomes this limitation.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that disfluencies make linguistic features less effective, yet the results show that linguistic features perform worse on the manually revised data (Table 6) where disfluencies were removed. Could the authors clarify why their network-based approach would be particularly advantageous for handling disfluencies, given that linguistic features also improve with disfluency removal?

2. The paper states that "the classification of small texts using networks can be impaired" (Amancio, 2015c), yet they apply this approach to short narratives (e.g., Cookie Theft dataset has ~9-10 words per sentence). Could the authors provide more justification for why their approach works well despite the small text size, especially since they acknowledge this as a potential limitation?

3. The paper doesn't compare with recent state-of-the-art methods in MCI detection from speech, such as those using deep learning architectures. How does the proposed approach compare with these more recent methods in terms of both performance and computational requirements?

## Additional Comments
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the clinical implications of the results. What would an improvement from 75% to 80% accuracy mean for clinical practice? Is this a meaningful improvement in terms of reducing false positives/negatives?
- The paper mentions that "the transcriptions had to be manually revised" in prior work (Aluísio et al., 2016), but doesn't provide a clear quantitative comparison of the time/cost savings of their automated approach versus manual revision.
- The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of which specific topological features are most informative for MCI detection, rather than reporting all 10 features collectively.

## Overall Recommendation
This paper addresses an important clinical problem with a potentially useful approach. However, the core contribution is limited by the fact that the authors are applying established NLP techniques to a new domain rather than developing novel methods. The performance gains are modest and the clinical significance is not clearly established. While the work is technically sound and well-executed, it does not represent a significant advance in NLP methodology or substantially improve the state of the art in MCI detection.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the modest performance improvements do not justify acceptance for ACL, which has high standards for novelty and impact. The authors should consider addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly by demonstrating a more substantial novelty in their approach and providing stronger justification for why their method is superior to existing techniques. A more compelling version of this work would need to either:
1) Demonstrate significantly better performance than existing state-of-the-art methods, or
2) Provide a novel theoretical insight that explains why complex network features are particularly well-suited for MCI detection from speech, beyond the empirical results.
