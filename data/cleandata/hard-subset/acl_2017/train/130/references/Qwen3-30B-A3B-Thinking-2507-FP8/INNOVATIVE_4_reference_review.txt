

# ACL Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling transcripts as complex networks and enriching them with word embeddings. The authors claim that this approach can better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments and that the topological properties of these enriched networks can distinguish between control participants and MCI participants.

## Primary Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel application of complex networks to MCI detection**: The paper's most significant contribution is applying complex networks (specifically word adjacency networks) to detect MCI from speech transcripts, which has not been explored before in this domain.

2. **Enriching networks with word embeddings**: The paper introduces a novel method for enriching word adjacency networks with semantic information from word embeddings, addressing the short text problem common in neuropsychological assessments.

3. **Automated preprocessing approach**: The paper demonstrates how this network-based approach can avoid the need for manual revision of transcripts, making the system more automated and scalable compared to traditional linguistic feature extraction methods.

## Strengths (arguments supporting acceptance)

1. **High novelty in methodology**: The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to MCI detection by combining complex network theory with word embeddings. This is a bold and creative direction that significantly differs from existing methods in the field. The authors successfully apply a theoretical framework from network science to a new practical problem in clinical linguistics, which demonstrates strong conceptual innovation.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper builds on solid theoretical foundations from complex networks and NLP literature. The authors provide a compelling justification for why complex networks might be suitable for this task (based on prior work in text classification, author detection, etc.) and why enriching them with word embeddings would help with the short text problem. The explanation of the network enrichment process (Figure 2) is particularly clear and convincing.

3. **Competitive results with practical relevance**: The approach achieves competitive accuracy (up to 80% on the ABCD dataset) compared to the 60-85% range reported by other authors in the field. More importantly, the authors demonstrate that their enriched network features (CNE) outperform traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations in most cases, which is a significant practical contribution. The paper's focus on automated systems that avoid manual transcript revision addresses a real pain point in the field.

4. **Clear presentation of methodology**: The paper clearly explains the methodology of creating and enriching the complex networks, making it easy to understand and replicate. The supplementary material provides additional details about the datasets and features, which is helpful for reproducibility.

5. **Addresses a significant real-world problem**: MCI detection is an important problem with high real-world impact, as early detection can lead to better treatment outcomes. The paper's focus on this application demonstrates the potential for NLP research to make meaningful contributions to clinical practice.

## Weaknesses (arguments against acceptance)

1. **Limited novelty comparison**: While the paper claims novelty, it doesn't provide a sufficiently thorough comparison with related work to establish the uniqueness of their specific approach. The paper cites prior work on complex networks in NLP (Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c), but it's not clear whether this specific combination of complex networks and word embeddings for MCI detection has been done before. A more detailed comparison with related work would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares their approach to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words, but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods in MCI detection. For instance, they don't compare to deep learning approaches that have been successful in other NLP tasks. This weakens the claim that their approach is competitive with the current state-of-the-art.

3. **Limited interpretability**: While the paper reports that their approach works, it doesn't provide a deep analysis of why it works. The paper doesn't explain which topological features are most important for classification or how the enriched networks differ from traditional networks in ways that make them more informative for MCI detection. This lack of interpretability limits the theoretical contribution of the paper.

4. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets used in the paper are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in the Cookie Theft dataset), which is common in medical NLP research but still limits the generalizability of the results. The authors could have provided more discussion about the implications of these small sample sizes for the validity of their findings.

5. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of their approach. For example, they don't show how much the enrichment with word embeddings contributes to the performance compared to the basic network approach. This makes it difficult to determine which part of their method is most valuable.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods in MCI detection, particularly those using deep learning approaches?

2. Could you explain why the enriched networks (CNE) work better than the basic networks (CN) for MCI detection? What specific topological features are most important?

3. How would your approach scale to larger datasets, and what are the computational costs compared to traditional methods?

4. Could you elaborate on the choice of the Bojanowski et al. (2016) embedding model and why it was better suited for this task than other embedding models?

5. How generalizable is your approach to other languages and populations beyond English and Brazilian Portuguese?

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to MCI detection that combines complex network theory with word embeddings, which is a bold and creative direction that significantly differs from existing methods in the field. While there are some methodological weaknesses (small datasets, lack of comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning methods), the novelty of the approach is significant enough to warrant acceptance. The results are competitive with existing methods, and the approach addresses a practical problem in automated MCI detection. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by applying a theoretical framework from network science to a new practical problem in clinical linguistics, which aligns well with ACL's emphasis on novel and bold approaches. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
