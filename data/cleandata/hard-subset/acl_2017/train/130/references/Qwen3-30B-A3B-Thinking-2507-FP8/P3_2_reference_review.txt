

# Peer Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features require extensive manual preprocessing to handle disfluencies, which is expensive. Their approach creates word adjacency networks from transcripts and enriches them with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short texts. They evaluate their method on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) and compare against linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel application of complex network analysis to speech transcripts for MCI detection, specifically adapting the network model to handle short texts through semantic enrichment with word embeddings.
2. Comprehensive evaluation across three distinct datasets with different languages (English/Portuguese), tasks (picture description vs. story retelling), and transcript lengths.
3. Demonstration that multi-view learning combining network features, linguistic features, and bag-of-words can significantly improve classification accuracy (up to 10% improvement over individual classifiers).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Methodological Approach**: The adaptation of complex networks with word embeddings to handle short, disfluent transcripts represents a meaningful contribution to the field. Previous work has primarily relied on linguistic features that require substantial manual preprocessing to handle disfluencies. The authors' approach of using semantic similarity to enrich the network structure (Figure 2) provides a more robust representation of the text's semantic structure without requiring manual correction of disfluencies.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Strategy**: The paper evaluates the approach across three distinct datasets with different characteristics (language, task type, transcript length), providing strong evidence for the approach's generalizability. The comparison between automatically processed transcripts and manually revised versions (Table 6) is particularly valuable for understanding the trade-offs between automation and accuracy.

3. **Practical Significance of Multi-View Learning**: The paper demonstrates that combining different feature spaces through multi-view learning can significantly improve performance (e.g., 10% improvement for Cinderella dataset), which is an important methodological contribution. This approach avoids the pitfalls of simply concatenating heterogeneous features while allowing different classifiers to be optimized for each feature space.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Justification for Network Features' Superiority**: While the paper claims network features outperform linguistic features in some cases, it fails to provide a clear explanation for *why* this is the case. The authors should explain which specific network properties (e.g., clustering coefficient, average shortest path) correlate with cognitive impairment and how these relate to known linguistic deficits in MCI. Without this, the method's interpretability and clinical relevance remain unclear.

2. **Questionable Comparison to Prior Work**: The paper claims "accuracies found here are comparable to values reported by other authors, ranging from 60% to 85%," but fails to provide a fair comparison on the same datasets. Many prior works used different datasets, preprocessing methods, and evaluation protocols. The authors should have at least compared their results to the best previous results on the same datasets to establish a meaningful baseline.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports accuracy improvements (e.g., 2.3% for Cookie Theft dataset) without providing statistical significance testing. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the reported improvements are meaningful or simply due to random variation. This is particularly important given the relatively small dataset sizes (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft dataset).

4. **Overstated Claims About Automation**: The paper claims to have created a "fully automated system that does not require the input of a human annotator," but the preprocessing still involves automatic removal of disfluencies (e.g., "uh," "um") and sentence segmentation. The authors should clarify exactly what aspects of the processing are truly automated versus requiring some level of intervention.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more insight into which specific topological features (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness, Clustering Coefficient) are most predictive of MCI, and why these features might correlate with cognitive impairment? This would greatly enhance the clinical relevance of your approach.

2. How do your results compare to the best previous results on the same datasets? If you don't have this comparison, could you provide it in the revision?

3. Could you provide statistical significance testing for the improvements you report (e.g., 2.3% improvement in Cookie Theft dataset)?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel approach to MCI detection using complex networks enriched with word embeddings, with promising results across multiple datasets. The methodology is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in current approaches (the need for manual preprocessing of disfluencies). However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by providing deeper analysis of *why* the network features work, a fairer comparison to prior work on the same datasets, and statistical validation of the reported improvements. The current presentation of the results, particularly the lack of justification for the network features' superiority, limits the paper's impact.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of NLP, cognitive science, and healthcare applications, and with the suggested revisions, it has the potential to become an important reference in the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above.
