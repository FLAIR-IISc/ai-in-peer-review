

# Peer Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech narratives as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings (using cosine similarity to add edges between semantically similar words). They claim this approach outperforms traditional linguistic features and enables a fully automated system that avoids manual preprocessing of disfluencies.

## Main Contributions
1. Application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts (novel for this domain)
2. Demonstration that this approach can be fully automated without manual preprocessing of disfluencies
3. Multi-view learning framework to combine network features, linguistic features, and BoW features

## Strengths

1. **Clinically Significant Problem**: The paper addresses an important clinical challenge - early detection of MCI, which is crucial for interventions. The authors provide a strong justification for why language analysis is valuable for cognitive assessment, citing the frequent recognition of language changes by patients and family members.

2. **Methodological Justification**: The authors correctly identify limitations of traditional NLP approaches for disfluent speech (e.g., dependence on manual preprocessing, vulnerability to disfluencies), and their decision to avoid lemmatization (which can introduce noise) is well-motivated. The adaptation of Perozzi et al.'s (2014) word embedding enrichment approach is reasonable for addressing the small-text problem.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Design**: The use of three distinct datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD) across different languages and narrative lengths demonstrates robustness. The multi-view learning approach combining different feature types is a significant strength, with the best results (80% accuracy on ABCD) achieved through this method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Novelty**: The core methodology (complex networks + word embeddings) builds on well-established techniques (Amancio et al., 2012; Perozzi et al., 2014). The paper fails to convincingly argue why these techniques are particularly novel for MCI detection, rather than a straightforward application to a new domain. The authors cite the lack of prior work on complex networks for MCI detection, but don't sufficiently explain the specific challenges of this domain that make their approach necessary.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares to traditional linguistic features and BoW, but omits comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for text classification (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have shown strong performance in related clinical NLP tasks. The claim that "CNE is better than using only complex networks" is supported only by accuracy improvements, but without demonstrating that these improvements are statistically significant or meaningful in the context of clinical applications.

3. **Small Dataset Sizes and Limited Generalizability**: The datasets are quite small (e.g., 43 MCI transcripts in Cookie Theft), limiting statistical power. More critically, the paper doesn't address potential biases in the datasets (e.g., age, education, linguistic background of participants) that could affect generalizability. The ABCD dataset results (80% accuracy) are promising but require validation on larger, more diverse datasets.

4. **Lack of Explanation for Why Enrichment Works**: While Figure 2 shows enriched networks are more connected, the paper doesn't explain *why* this enrichment leads to better MCI detection. The authors don't analyze which specific network features or semantic relationships are most informative for distinguishing MCI from controls, which would strengthen their claims.

5. **Limited Discussion of Clinical Implementation**: The paper mentions using ASR for automatic segmentation but doesn't address how well ASR performs on disfluent speech from elderly patients with cognitive impairment - a critical issue for real-world clinical deployment.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your enriched network approach compare to state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., BERT) on the same datasets? Have you considered using contextual embeddings instead of static word embeddings?

2. The paper claims "CNE outperforms linguistic features," but linguistic features depend on grammatical texts. How do you explain the performance drop of linguistic features on the manually processed Cinderella dataset (Table 6) compared to the automatically processed one (Table 5)? Does this suggest your approach is particularly suited for disfluent speech?

3. Could you provide more details about the MCI diagnosis criteria used across the three datasets? How do they compare to standard diagnostic criteria (e.g., Petersen criteria)?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-motivated study addressing an important clinical problem with a methodologically sound approach. The results are promising (up to 80% accuracy), and the multi-view learning framework is particularly strong. However, the novelty claim is questionable, the comparison to state-of-the-art methods is incomplete, and the small dataset sizes limit the strength of the conclusions. With additional comparisons to modern deep learning approaches, larger datasets, and more detailed analysis of why the enrichment works, this work could make a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper presents a reasonable application of existing techniques to a new domain but lacks sufficient novelty to be considered groundbreaking.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions addressing the weaknesses above)
