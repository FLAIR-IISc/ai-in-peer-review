

# ACL Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings. They extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches. The paper demonstrates the approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) across multiple machine learning classifiers.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of complex networks with word embedding enrichment to MCI detection from speech transcripts
2. Demonstration that network-based features can be competitive with linguistic features for this task
3. Exploration of multi-view learning to combine different feature types

## Strengths

1. **Addressing an important clinical problem**: The paper tackles a meaningful healthcare challenge with potential real-world impact for early detection of MCI, which is crucial for effective intervention.

2. **Multiple datasets with different languages**: The use of both English (Cookie Theft) and Portuguese (Cinderella, ABCD) datasets demonstrates some level of cross-lingual applicability, which is valuable for a clinical application.

3. **Detailed experimental evaluation**: The paper provides comprehensive results across multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, GaussianNB), feature combinations, and datasets, which is helpful for understanding the approach's performance characteristics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient justification for the approach**: The core method (complex networks with word embedding enrichment) is not novel - it builds directly on Perozzi et al. (2014) and has been used in NLP for text classification (Mihalcea and Radev, 2011; de Arruda et al., 2016). The paper fails to convincingly argue why this specific combination is better suited for MCI detection than established NLP approaches. The authors claim complex networks "suffer less from the problems mentioned above [disfluencies]," but they still had to manually preprocess the transcripts (remove disfluencies) in several cases, contradicting their own claim.

2. **Marginal performance gains**: The reported improvements over individual classifiers are minimal (2.3% for Cookie Theft, 10% for Cinderella, 4.71% for ABCD). As the authors themselves note, the accuracy range (65-80%) is comparable to previous work (60-85%). These marginal gains do not justify the complexity of the proposed approach, especially when compared to established NLP methods.

3. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art NLP methods**: The paper compares to linguistic features and bag-of-words, but fails to compare with contemporary NLP approaches (e.g., transformer-based models, which have become standard in NLP). Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if the complex network approach offers any meaningful advantage over current best practices in NLP for this specific task.

4. **Insufficient methodological justification for the network approach**: The paper doesn't adequately explain why complex networks would be better suited for MCI detection than standard text classification approaches. The authors claim their approach avoids the need for manual preprocessing, but they still had to manually segment sentences in some cases and remove disfluencies, undermining this claim.

5. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 control for Cookie Theft; 20 MCI and 20 controls for Cinderella). Small datasets make it difficult to draw statistically significant conclusions about the method's effectiveness, especially given the marginal performance gains reported.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art NLP methods for MCI detection, such as transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)? Why is the complex network approach better than these established methods?

2. The paper claims to have a "fully automated system," but the authors still had to manually segment sentences in some cases. Can you clarify this contradiction and explain how your system is truly fully automated?

3. The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of results to the cosine similarity threshold used for adding edges in the enriched networks. How did you choose this threshold, and how sensitive are your results to this parameter?

4. The paper states that "the classification of small texts using networks can be impaired, since short texts have almost linear networks" (Amancio, 2015c), yet the ABCD dataset has only 5.23 sentences per narrative on average. How does your approach handle such short texts, which the authors themselves identify as problematic for network approaches?

5. The authors claim that "linguistic features depend on grammatical texts to present good results," but the best results for the Cinderella dataset were achieved with manually processed transcripts (Table 6, 75% accuracy for LM-BoW). This contradicts their own claim - can you explain this discrepancy?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the application area is important, the paper fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or meaningful improvement over established methods. The marginal performance gains (2-10% improvements) do not justify the complexity of the proposed approach, especially when compared to standard NLP methods. The paper's claims about avoiding manual preprocessing are undermined by the need for manual sentence segmentation and disfluency removal in several cases. Without a compelling comparison to state-of-the-art NLP approaches and without demonstrating significant advantages over existing methods, this work does not meet ACL's standards for novelty and impact.
