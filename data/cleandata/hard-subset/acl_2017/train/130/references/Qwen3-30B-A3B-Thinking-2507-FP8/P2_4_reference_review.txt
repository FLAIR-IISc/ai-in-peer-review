

# Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network modeling enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings, and extract topological features for classification. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. **Application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection**: This is the most significant contribution - applying complex network analysis with word embedding enrichment to a specific clinical problem (MCI detection) from speech transcripts.
2. **Demonstration of effectiveness for short text classification**: The paper addresses the challenge of analyzing short texts (common in neuropsychological assessments) by showing how network enrichment with embeddings overcomes the linear network problem.
3. **Comprehensive comparison of feature types**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of topological network features, linguistic features, and bag-of-words features for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful clinical problem**: Early detection of MCI is crucial for dementia management, and the paper tackles this important application with a computational approach. The authors correctly identify the potential of speech analysis as a non-invasive diagnostic method, and their work contributes to the growing literature on computational linguistics for healthcare applications.

2. **Sound methodology and thorough evaluation**: The paper uses multiple datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) with different languages (English and Portuguese) and different text lengths, providing a robust evaluation. The use of 5-fold cross-validation and multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, etc.) strengthens the evaluation. The comparison between manually processed and automatically processed data (for Cinderella dataset) is particularly valuable for demonstrating the feasibility of a fully automated system.

3. **Clear demonstration of the short text challenge**: The authors effectively explain why short texts pose a problem for complex network analysis (linear networks with little informative topology) and how their enrichment approach addresses this issue. The example in Figure 2 compellingly shows how the enriched network becomes more informative than the basic co-occurrence network.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty and insufficient comparison with prior work**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technique (complex networks with word embedding enrichment) isn't particularly innovative. The paper doesn't clearly differentiate their enrichment method from prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al., 2014), and the comparison with state-of-the-art methods is superficial. The claim that their approach "outperforms" linguistic features is not convincingly demonstrated, as the gains are marginal (e.g., 65% vs 64% in Cookie Theft dataset). The paper should directly compare with the most relevant prior work in MCI detection from speech transcripts, not just mention accuracy ranges.

2. **Insufficient methodological details**: The paper lacks critical details about the enrichment process. How was the cosine similarity threshold determined? What specific word embedding model was used (and why)? The paper mentions "Bojanowski et al. (2016)" but doesn't explain how their approach differs from the standard skip-gram model. Without these details, it's difficult to assess the contribution or replicate the work.

3. **Marginal performance improvements**: The reported accuracy improvements (e.g., 2.3% in Cookie Theft, 10% in Cinderella) are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the approach. For instance, in Table 7 for the ABCD dataset, the best individual classifier (SVM-RBF on BoW) achieves 75.47% accuracy, while the best combined approach (CNE-LM-BoW) achieves 80.18% - a 4.71% improvement. While this is positive, it's not transformative, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this marginal improvement is worth the added complexity.

4. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper reports accuracy values but doesn't provide statistical tests to confirm that the observed differences between methods are significant. This is particularly important given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD for Cookie Theft).

## Questions to Authors

1. Can the authors provide more details about how the cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment was determined? Was it optimized or set to a standard value?
2. How does the performance of their approach compare to the state-of-the-art specifically for MCI detection from speech transcripts, rather than just mentioning general accuracy ranges?
3. Why did the authors choose to use the Bojanowski et al. (2016) method for word embeddings when it's mentioned to be better for syntactic than semantic evaluation? What would be the impact of using a more semantically rich embedding method?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonable approach to an important clinical problem with a sound methodology and thorough evaluation across multiple datasets. However, the novelty is limited, the performance gains are marginal, and the methodological details are insufficient. The paper makes a modest contribution to the field, but it doesn't offer a transformative advance that would justify acceptance at ACL. The work is more appropriate for a clinical or healthcare-focused venue rather than a computational linguistics venue like ACL.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) a more detailed comparison with the most relevant prior work in MCI detection from speech transcripts, (2) more thorough explanation of the enrichment process, (3) statistical significance testing for the results, and (4) a clearer justification for why the marginal accuracy improvements are meaningful in a clinical context.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**
