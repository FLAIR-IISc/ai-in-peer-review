

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings. They then extract topological features from these enriched networks and combine them with linguistic and bag-of-words features for classification. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) in English and Portuguese.

The authors claim three main contributions:
1. Modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments.
2. Demonstrating that topological properties of enriched networks can distinguish between control participants and MCI participants.
3. Showing that combining complex network features with linguistic and traditional statistical features improves classification performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application to MCI Detection**: The paper makes a significant contribution by applying complex network modeling to MCI detection from speech transcripts, a novel application of this technique to this specific clinical problem. While complex networks have been used in other NLP tasks, this is one of the first applications to MCI detection, which has important clinical implications.

2. **Effective Network Enrichment with Word Embeddings**: The authors' approach of enriching word adjacency networks with semantic information via word embeddings addresses a critical limitation of using simple co-occurrence networks for short texts. As noted by Amancio (2015c), short texts produce nearly linear networks with limited topological information. The enrichment with word embeddings significantly improves network informativeness (Figure 2), providing a practical solution to this problem.

3. **Comprehensive Multi-Language Evaluation**: The paper evaluates the approach across three different datasets in both English and Portuguese, demonstrating the robustness of their method across languages and different data collection protocols. This is particularly valuable given the need for language-specific approaches in clinical settings.

4. **Practical Contribution to Automated Assessment**: The authors emphasize creating a fully automated system that doesn't require human annotators, which is a significant practical contribution to the field. This addresses a major limitation in current approaches where manual preprocessing is required.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology**: While the application is novel, the core methodology of enriching networks with word embeddings is directly based on Perozzi et al. (2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently explain how their implementation differs from or improves upon this prior work. The enrichment method appears to be a straightforward application of existing techniques without significant innovation, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis with Modern NLP Approaches**: The paper compares with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches but fails to compare with recent state-of-the-art NLP models such as BERT, RoBERTa, or other transformer-based architectures. Given the significant advances in NLP, this omission is substantial and limits the paper's contribution to the current state of the art.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Feature Importance**: The paper reports good classification results but lacks detailed analysis of which topological features are most informative for MCI detection. Without understanding which specific network properties correlate with cognitive impairment, it's difficult to interpret the results or understand the clinical relevance of the approach.

4. **Small Dataset Sizes and Limited Generalizability**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 control transcripts in the Cookie Theft dataset), which may limit the generalizability of the results. The paper doesn't discuss the potential impact of small dataset sizes on the performance metrics or provide evidence of robustness across different population samples.

5. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any error analysis to understand when the method fails or why certain cases are misclassified. This is particularly important for clinical applications, where understanding the limitations of the approach is crucial for practical implementation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your specific implementation of enriching networks with word embeddings differ from Perozzi et al. (2014)? What improvements do you claim over their method, and why is your specific implementation better suited for MCI detection?

2. The paper states that linguistic features depend on grammatical texts for good results (as seen in Table 6 where manually revised texts improve linguistic feature performance). How does this affect your claim that your approach doesn't require manual preprocessing? Would the enriched network approach also benefit from similar manual revision?

3. Why didn't you compare your approach with recent deep learning models like BERT or RoBERTa, which have shown remarkable success in other text classification tasks? How would you expect your method to perform against these approaches?

4. The paper reports an 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset with multi-view learning. Given that many recent clinical studies report similar or higher accuracy with different methods, how do you interpret these results in the context of the current state of MCI detection?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents an interesting application of complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts, with the network enrichment approach addressing a key limitation in modeling short texts. The multi-language evaluation across three datasets is a strength. However, the limited novelty in the core methodology, lack of comparison with modern NLP approaches, and insufficient analysis of feature importance significantly limit the paper's contribution. 

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the expectation that the authors would address the weaknesses in a revised version, particularly by comparing with modern NLP models and providing more detailed analysis of the features and their clinical relevance. The paper has potential but needs to better establish its novelty and significance in the current NLP landscape.
