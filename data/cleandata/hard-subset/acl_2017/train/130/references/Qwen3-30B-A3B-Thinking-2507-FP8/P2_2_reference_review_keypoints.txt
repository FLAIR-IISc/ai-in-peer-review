- Proposes detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings.  
- Models transcripts as word adjacency networks and injects semantic info from embeddings to better represent short, disfluent texts.  
- Claims contributions: (1) applying enriched complex networks to MCI detection, (2) outperforming traditional linguistic features in some cases, (3) improving accuracy by combining network, linguistic, and BoW features.  
- Novel application to an important clinical problem with strong motivation and demonstration that network features can be competitive with linguistic features.  
- Robust evaluation across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) in English and Portuguese, showing cross-linguistic applicability.  
- Practical contribution by avoiding manual preprocessing (sentence segmentation, disfluency correction), making a more automated clinical assessment pipeline.  
- Core technique’s novelty is limited, largely adapted from Perozzi et al. (2014) with little methodological innovation.  
- Reported accuracy gains are modest (e.g., 2.3% Cookie Theft, 10% Cinderella), raising questions about clinical significance and comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning.  
- Small datasets and use of 5-fold CV raise concerns about overfitting and the statistical reliability of results.  
- Lacks ablation studies to determine whether gains come from network structure, embeddings, or their combination.  
- Question: How does the network enrichment differ from Perozzi et al. (2014), and why is it better for short, disfluent transcripts?  
- Question: How are modest accuracy improvements justified as clinically meaningful, and what improvement threshold would be practically useful?  
- Question: Can you provide statistical significance analysis given small dataset sizes to rule out chance results?  
- Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on addressing methodological novelty, statistical significance, comparison to deep learning baselines, and ablation studies.
