- Proposed method: model speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI, evaluated on three datasets (English/Portuguese).

- Primary contributions: (1) novel use of embedding-enriched complex networks for MCI, (2) demonstrated superior performance over some traditional linguistic features, (3) cross-dataset evaluation across languages and narrative lengths.

- Strength: novel methodology tailored to short, disfluent transcripts, arguing advantages over traditional features.

- Strength: robust multi-dataset evaluation showing generalizability, with 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset.

- Strength: clear technical implementation and justified choice of topological measures for network analysis.

- Strength: strong clinical relevance for early MCI detection.

- Weakness: no comparison with contemporary transformer-based NLP models (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa), undermining ACL relevance.

- Weakness: insufficient theoretical justification or analysis of which topological features drive performance.

- Weakness: questionable preprocessing choice to omit lemmatization without empirical evidence it harms results.

- Weakness: small and unclear dataset/control sizes increase risk of overfitting and limit reliability.

- Weakness: absence of an ablation study to identify the most informative network features.

- Question: Why were transformer-based models not used for comparison?

- Question: Can you empirically show lemmatization would introduce noise in these disfluent transcripts?

- Question: Which specific topological features benefit most from embedding enrichment?

- Question: How does enrichment concretely address the “almost linear networks” problem in short texts?

- Question: Why does SVM-RBF outperform other kernels and what justifies that kernel choice?

- Overall assessment: promising, well-motivated approach with good results but weakened by missing SOTA comparisons, preprocessing concerns, small datasets, and lack of deeper analysis; substantial revisions needed.
