

# Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich them with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short texts with disfluencies. They evaluate the approach on three different datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) across English and Portuguese languages, demonstrating that the enriched network features outperform traditional linguistic features and can be combined with them for improved accuracy.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts** - This is the most significant contribution as it represents a fresh direction in a clinical domain where NLP approaches have been limited.
2. **Practical advantage of avoiding manual preprocessing** - The approach eliminates the need for expensive manual revision of speech transcripts, which is a substantial practical contribution for clinical applications.
3. **Demonstration of network features combined with linguistic features** - The paper shows how to effectively integrate different feature types through multi-view learning, providing a useful methodological contribution.

## Strengths
1. **Novel core concept**: The paper introduces a genuinely novel application of complex networks (specifically enriched with word embeddings) to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts. While complex networks have been used for text classification before, this is the first application to MCI detection from speech transcripts. This novelty aligns with the ACL reviewer's preference for bold new directions over incremental improvements.

2. **Practical clinical relevance**: The authors clearly identify a significant pain point in the field - the need for expensive manual preprocessing of speech transcripts to handle disfluencies. By avoiding this step, the approach has clear clinical applicability, which is a strong practical contribution.

3. **Thorough evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates the approach on three different datasets (English and Portuguese, different narrative styles, different lengths), demonstrating robustness across contexts. This comprehensive evaluation is a strength that compensates for the relatively modest accuracy improvements.

4. **Clear theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical explanation of why complex networks enriched with embeddings might work well for short, disfluent texts, citing relevant prior work in network science and NLP.

5. **Well-structured methodology**: The paper clearly describes how the networks are constructed and enriched, making the approach reproducible and transparent.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art NLP methods**: The paper does not compare with modern NLP approaches like BERT, RoBERTa, or other transformer-based models, which have become standard in NLP. This is a significant omission given that these methods have achieved substantial improvements in many NLP tasks. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if the proposed approach is actually competitive with current state-of-the-art.

2. **Modest accuracy improvements**: The accuracy improvements over the best individual classifier are relatively small (2.3% for Cookie Theft, 10% for Cinderella, 4.71% for ABCD), which, while statistically significant, may not be practically significant in a clinical context. The paper doesn't sufficiently address whether these improvements are meaningful for clinical applications.

3. **Overstated novelty claim**: While the specific application to MCI detection is novel, the underlying methodology (using complex networks for text representation) has been explored in prior work by the authors (Amancio et al., 2012-2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their approach differs from previous work on complex networks for text.

4. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper provides quantitative results but no qualitative analysis to show *why* the enriched network features are better at capturing MCI-related patterns. A few case studies would significantly strengthen the argument.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of the approach, such as when it might fail or what types of speech transcripts it works best with, which would be important for clinical adoption.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why didn't you compare your approach with state-of-the-art NLP methods like BERT or other transformer-based models? How would your approach compare to these methods in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, and data requirements?

2. Can you provide qualitative examples showing how the enriched network features capture MCI-related linguistic patterns that traditional features miss? For instance, can you show a specific sentence where the network features identified MCI-related patterns that other features did not?

3. What are the specific limitations of this approach, and in what clinical scenarios might it not be applicable?

4. How would you expect this approach to scale to larger datasets or different languages, especially those with less developed NLP resources?

## Recommendation
**Accept with minor revisions**

This paper introduces a novel and bold approach to a clinically important problem that aligns well with the ACL reviewer's preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection. While the accuracy improvements are modest and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art NLP methods is a significant weakness, the core idea of applying complex networks enriched with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts is genuinely novel and has clear clinical relevance. The paper makes a compelling case for a new direction in the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the lack of comparison with modern NLP methods and to provide more qualitative evidence supporting the approach. The practical advantages of avoiding manual preprocessing of speech transcripts and the thorough multi-dataset evaluation make this a valuable contribution to the field, even if the absolute accuracy rates are not groundbreaking. The novelty of the approach outweighs the minor methodological weaknesses for a reviewer who prioritizes new directions over incremental improvements.
