

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel application of complex network theory to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short texts. They extract topological features from these enriched networks and use them for classification, comparing against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches. The results demonstrate competitive accuracy (up to 80%) across three datasets, with the best performance coming from combining network features with linguistic features.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel application of complex networks with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts** (a new direction in computational linguistics for healthcare applications)
2. **Demonstration that topological features of enriched networks outperform traditional linguistic features** for MCI detection
3. **Development of a fully automated system avoiding manual transcript preprocessing** (a significant practical advantage over previous work)

## Strengths

1. **Bold novel application to a new domain**: The core strength of this paper is the application of complex network theory (specifically word adjacency networks enriched with word embeddings) to the domain of MCI detection from speech transcripts. While complex networks have been used for text analysis before, applying them specifically to MCI detection from speech transcripts represents a novel and significant direction. This is exactly the kind of bold application that ACL reviewers value - taking a technique from one domain and applying it to a new, meaningful problem in a way that opens up new research possibilities.

2. **Practical automation advantage**: The authors convincingly argue that their approach avoids the need for manual preprocessing of transcripts (removing disfluencies, etc.), which is a major limitation in previous work. As they note, previous studies required "manual revision; they were segmented in sentences, following a semantic-structural criterion and capitalization was added afterwards" - a process they explicitly state is "expensive." This automation aspect makes their approach more scalable and practical for real-world clinical applications.

3. **Competitive results with meaningful improvements**: The paper reports results that are competitive with state-of-the-art (60-85% accuracy), with the best approach (CNE-LM-BoW) achieving 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset. More importantly, the improvements over baselines are not trivial - the paper shows that the network-based approach (CNE) consistently outperforms traditional linguistic features (LM) and BoW, with up to 10% improvement in the Cinderella dataset when using multi-view learning. This demonstrates that the approach adds meaningful value beyond existing methods.

4. **Addressing a significant clinical problem**: The paper identifies the importance of early MCI detection for clinical intervention, noting that "dementias are chronic and progressive diseases, their early diagnosis ensures a greater chance of success to engage patients in non-pharmacological treatment strategies." This application has clear clinical relevance, which enhances the significance of the computational approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core method**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core method (word adjacency networks enriched with word embeddings) is directly based on Perozzi et al. (2014). The paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally new technique, which is a limitation given the reviewer's preference for bold methodological innovations. The novelty is primarily in the application domain rather than the method itself.

2. **Small datasets with modest improvements**: The datasets are relatively small (43 MCI + 21 AD for Cookie Theft; 20 MCI + 20 control for Cinderella; 23 MCI + 20 control for ABCD), and the improvements over baselines are modest (e.g., 2.3% improvement in Cookie Theft dataset). Given the small dataset sizes, the statistical significance of these improvements is questionable, which limits the impact of the findings.

3. **Lack of comparison to recent deep learning approaches**: The paper doesn't compare to state-of-the-art deep learning methods that have been used for similar tasks (e.g., using RNNs or Transformers for MCI detection from speech). This omission is significant for a paper in ACL, as it leaves unclear whether the approach is truly competitive with the current state of the art in natural language processing.

4. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why topological properties of these networks would be particularly relevant for detecting MCI. There's no strong theoretical basis connecting network topology to cognitive impairment, which makes it hard to understand why this approach works. The connection between the features extracted and the cognitive impairment is not well justified.

5. **Missing methodological details**: The paper lacks important details about handling out-of-vocabulary words and determining the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges. These are critical for reproducibility and would help readers understand how to implement the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent deep learning methods (e.g., using RNNs or Transformers) for MCI detection from speech transcripts? Have you considered these approaches for comparison?

2. Can you provide more details on how you handled out-of-vocabulary words, and how you determined the threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity? These details are critical for reproducibility.

3. Why do you think the topological features of these networks are particularly relevant for MCI detection? Is there a theoretical basis for this connection, or is it primarily empirical?

4. Given the small dataset sizes, how confident are you that the reported improvements (e.g., 10% improvement in Cinderella dataset) are statistically significant?

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a bold and novel application of complex network theory to a significant clinical problem (MCI detection from speech transcripts), which aligns with the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection. While the core method is based on existing work, the application to this new domain represents a meaningful contribution that opens up a new research direction. The practical advantage of automation and the competitive results (up to 80% accuracy) further strengthen the paper's contribution.

The weaknesses noted are important, but they don't outweigh the novelty and potential impact of the work. The modest improvements and small datasets are acceptable trade-offs for a paper that introduces a novel approach to a meaningful problem. The authors could address some of the weaknesses in a revised version, but the core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance.

The paper demonstrates that network-based features can outperform traditional linguistic features for MCI detection, which is a significant finding that could inspire future work at the intersection of computational linguistics, network theory, and healthcare applications.
