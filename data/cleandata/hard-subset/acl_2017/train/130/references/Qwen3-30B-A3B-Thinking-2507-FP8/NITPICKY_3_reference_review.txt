

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling text as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim that this approach outperforms traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words methods for MCI detection from speech transcripts. The paper evaluates their method on three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese).

## Primary Contributions
1. Modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings for MCI detection
2. Demonstrating that topological network features can outperform linguistic features in MCI classification
3. Proposing a fully automated approach that avoids manual preprocessing steps required by traditional linguistic features

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks**: The application of complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts is a fresh approach that leverages network theory to capture semantic relationships in short texts. The enrichment with word embeddings addresses the challenge of short text classification (as noted by Amancio, 2015c), which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates their approach across three different datasets with varying languages (English and Portuguese) and text lengths (short descriptions vs. longer narratives), providing a more robust evaluation than single-dataset studies.

3. **Practical Automation**: The paper's focus on creating a fully automated system that avoids manual preprocessing (unlike previous work requiring manual revision of transcripts) is valuable for practical applications in clinical settings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and Poorly Formatted Citations**: The paper contains numerous citation formatting inconsistencies throughout the text and reference list. For example:
   - "i Cancho and Solé, 2001" (should be "Iñaki Cancho and Solé, 2001" or "Cancho and Solé, 2001")
   - "Aluı́sio et al\.,? \Q2016\E" (should be "Aluísio et al., 2016")
   - "Amancio.,? 2015a" (should be "Amancio, 2015a")
   - "72,0 (7.4)" in Table 3 (should be "72.0 (7.4)" with a decimal point instead of comma)
   - These inconsistencies make the paper appear unprofessional and undermine the credibility of the work.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper claims "CNE is better than using only complex networks" but provides no statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to support this claim. Without such tests, it's impossible to determine if the observed accuracy improvements are statistically meaningful or simply due to random chance.

3. **Vague Methodological Details**: The paper lacks crucial details about the implementation of the network enrichment process. Specifically:
   - The threshold for cosine similarity used to create new edges is not specified.
   - The exact word embedding model used (e.g., GloVe, Word2Vec) is not clearly stated.
   - The paper states they used "Bojanowski et al. (2016)" but doesn't explain how they implemented it (e.g., dimensionality, window size).
   - The description of "enriching networks with semantic information" is too vague to reproduce.

4. **Inconsistent Results Presentation**: The results in Tables 4-7 contain several inconsistencies:
   - Table 4 shows "CNE-LM-BoW" as a column but the text refers to "CNE-LM" as the best multi-view approach.
   - Table 5 claims "CNE-LM" achieved 75% accuracy, but the "CNE-LM" column shows 0.7500, which would be 75% accuracy, yet the text states "75% of accuracy for narrative texts, a 10% of improvement."
   - Table 7 refers to "CNE-LM-BoW" but the columns only include "CNE-LM-BoW" (a likely typo).
   - The paper states "CNE-LM-BoW achieved the highest accuracy (80% of accuracy for narrative texts, a 4.71% of improvement compared to the best individual classifier)" but Table 7 shows 0.8018 for SVM-Linear, which is 80.18%, not 80%.

5. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims "the accuracies found here are comparable to the values reported by other authors, ranging from 60% to 85%," but doesn't provide a direct comparison to the most recent and relevant state-of-the-art methods. This makes it impossible to determine if the proposed method is truly competitive or just at the baseline level of performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you please provide the exact threshold value used for the cosine similarity when creating new edges in the network enrichment process? Without this detail, the method cannot be reproduced.

2. The paper claims "CNE is better than using only complex networks" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests. Could you add these tests to the revised manuscript?

3. In Table 3, the average age for MCI is listed as "72,0 (7.4)" - this should be "72.0 (7.4)" (using a decimal point instead of a comma). Could you please correct this typo?

4. The paper mentions "transcripts containing disfluencies" but then states they "automatically removed filled pauses such as uh, um, er, and ah" while "keeping revisions, elaboration, paraphasias and comments about the task." This seems contradictory. Could you clarify which disfluencies were removed versus kept?

5. Could you provide a detailed comparison of your method's performance with the most recent state-of-the-art approaches for MCI detection from speech transcripts?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting application of complex networks to MCI detection, the numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies in the paper (particularly the citation formatting, inconsistent results presentation, lack of statistical significance testing, and vague methodological details) are substantial enough to warrant rejection. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with these types of issues, even if the core idea has potential. The paper would need significant revision to address these problems before it could be considered for publication at ACL.

The paper's core idea has merit, but the execution is poor, and the presentation of results is confusing and inconsistent. The lack of statistical significance testing and the vague methodological details undermine the paper's claims. The authors should focus on addressing these fundamental issues before resubmitting.
