

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex network modeling enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich these networks with semantic information using word embeddings to better represent short texts. They then extract topological features from these networks and combine them with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words features to train classifiers that distinguish between MCI patients and healthy controls.

Main contributions as I assess them (ordered by strength):

1. **Novel enrichment of complex networks with word embeddings for short text representation**: The paper addresses the limitation of linear networks for short texts by proposing a method to enrich word adjacency networks with semantic information from word embeddings, which is a significant technical contribution to the field.

2. **Demonstration that network-based features can outperform traditional linguistic features**: The authors show that topological features from enriched networks achieve comparable or better performance than traditional linguistic features, without requiring manual preprocessing of transcripts.

3. **Fully automated pipeline for MCI detection**: The approach works directly on raw transcripts without requiring human annotation or manual preprocessing steps, addressing a key practical limitation in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Novel technical contribution**: The paper makes a clear technical contribution by adapting complex network modeling to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts. The enrichment of networks with word embeddings is a novel way to address the challenge of modeling short texts, which is a significant limitation in previous work. The authors provide a clear explanation of how this approach avoids the linear network problem for short texts (Figure 2 demonstrates the improvement).

2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The paper evaluates the approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) with different languages (English and Portuguese) and text lengths. The evaluation uses multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, Gaussian NB) and explores ensemble and multi-view learning approaches, providing a thorough assessment of the method's robustness. The results consistently show that enriched networks (CNE) perform well, with multi-view learning achieving the highest accuracy on all datasets.

3. **Addresses a significant practical limitation**: The paper directly tackles a major bottleneck in the field - the need for manual preprocessing of transcripts (removing disfluencies, correcting grammatical errors), which is time-consuming and expensive. The authors demonstrate that their approach works on raw transcripts without human annotation, which is a valuable practical contribution to the field of clinical language analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent deep learning approaches**: The paper compares its method to traditional linguistic features but does not compare to more recent state-of-the-art deep learning approaches that have been applied to similar clinical language analysis tasks. For example, there's no comparison to transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) that have shown strong performance in clinical NLP. This limits the assessment of the method's relative performance in the current landscape.

2. **Limited theoretical justification for network features**: While the paper uses a set of topological features from complex networks, it doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for why these particular features should be informative for MCI detection. The connection between cognitive decline and specific network properties isn't well established, which weakens the contribution.

3. **Insufficient discussion of clinical relevance**: The paper reports accuracy rates of 60-80%, but doesn't provide enough context to assess whether these results are clinically meaningful. It's unclear how these results compare to the current standard of care for MCI detection, and what the practical implications of the approach would be in a clinical setting. The paper mentions that "accuracies found here are comparable to the values reported by other authors," but doesn't provide sufficient context for this comparison.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could the authors provide a comparison with recent deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT-based models) that have been applied to similar MCI detection tasks? This would help contextualize the relative performance of their method.

2. The paper states that enriched networks (CNE) outperform linguistic features. Could the authors elaborate on why this is the case, and whether the improvement is due to the network modeling approach or the specific features used?

3. The paper mentions that the approach avoids manual preprocessing steps. Could the authors provide more detail on how the automatic disfluency removal works, and how it compares to the manual processing used in previous work (e.g., Aluísio et al., 2016)?
