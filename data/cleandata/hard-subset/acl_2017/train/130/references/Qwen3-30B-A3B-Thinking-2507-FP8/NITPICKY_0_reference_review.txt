

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features are hampered by disfluencies in speech transcripts, and that complex network approaches with semantic enrichment can better represent these short texts. They evaluate their approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) and compare their method against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations.

## Primary Contributions

1. A method for enriching complex network representations of speech transcripts with word embeddings to better capture semantic relationships, addressing the limitations of traditional co-occurrence networks for short texts.
2. An evaluation of the approach across three different datasets (English and Portuguese) showing competitive performance compared to linguistic features.
3. A demonstration that combining multiple feature spaces through multi-view learning can improve classification accuracy for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation**: The paper addresses an important healthcare problem with clear clinical relevance. Early detection of MCI is crucial for intervention, and the use of speech analysis as a non-invasive method is well-motivated.

2. **Novel application of complex networks**: While complex networks have been used in NLP before, the specific application to MCI detection from speech transcripts is novel and well-justified. The authors provide a good theoretical foundation for why complex networks might be better suited for handling disfluencies than traditional linguistic features.

3. **Multi-dataset evaluation**: The evaluation across three different datasets (English and Portuguese, different narrative lengths) provides a more robust assessment of the method's generalizability than a single dataset would.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical methodological inconsistencies in the network enrichment approach**: The authors claim to have "adapted the approach of modeling networks with word embeddings proposed by Perozzi et al. (2014)" but fail to clearly explain how their method differs. Perozzi et al. (2014) generate networks directly from word embeddings, while the authors claim to add edges to an existing co-occurrence network. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of the existing method. The paper states "for all pairs of words in the text that were unconnected, an edge was created if their vectors (from word embedding) had a cosine similarity higher than a given threshold," which is not what Perozzi et al. did. This inconsistency undermines the novelty claim and suggests a lack of understanding of the foundational work.

2. **Significant errors in result presentation**: In Table 7, the authors claim "multi-view CNE-LM-BoW achieved the highest accuracy (80% of accuracy for narrative texts, a 4.71% of improvement compared to the best individual classifier)." However, the best individual classifier (SVM-RBF for CNE) has an accuracy of 0.6750, while the CNE-LM-BoW combination has 0.8018, representing a 12.68% improvement, not 4.71%. This is a serious error that undermines the credibility of the paper. The discrepancy is particularly concerning because the authors explicitly state the improvement percentage in their abstract and conclusion.

3. **Inconsistent dataset descriptions and terminology**: The paper describes multiple versions of the Cinderella dataset with different statistics but fails to clearly explain why these versions exist or how they were created. For example, the unprocessed version has 30.80 sentences per narrative (Control) vs. 29.90 (MCI), while the manually revised version has 45.10 (Control) vs. 31.40 (MCI), yet the authors don't explain how the revision process affected the statistics. Additionally, inconsistent terminology (e.g., "complex networks" vs. "CN," "complex network enriched" vs. "CNE") creates confusion throughout the paper.

4. **Misrepresentation of the "fully automated" claim**: The authors claim to have created a "fully automated system" that "does not require the input of a human annotator," but they also describe a manually revised version of the Cinderella dataset (where "agrammatical sentences and all the patients' comments not related to the story were removed, and omitted subjects were inserted"). This contradicts their claim of full automation and undermines the practical significance of their approach.

5. **Inconsistent table formatting and description**: The paper uses "–" in tables to indicate "not possible to apply majority voting," but this is inconsistent with the actual content. For example, in Table 5, "CNE-LM" is listed as "–" but the value 0.7500 is present. In Table 4, "CNE-LM-BoW" is listed as 0.6500 but marked as "–" in the table. This inconsistency makes it difficult to interpret the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. Please clarify the exact method by which you enriched the co-occurrence networks with word embeddings. How does this method differ from Perozzi et al.'s (2014) approach? How did you determine the threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity?

2. The paper claims a 4.71% improvement for the ABCD dataset with multi-view CNE-LM-BoW over the best individual classifier, but the actual improvement is 12.68%. Please correct this discrepancy in your revision.

3. Can you explain why the manually revised version of the Cinderella dataset has such different sentence and word counts compared to the unprocessed version? How does this affect the validity of the comparison between the two versions?

4. The paper claims to have developed a "fully automated system" that does not require human annotators, yet you describe a manually revised version of the Cinderella dataset. How does this manual revision align with your fully automated claim, and why did you include this version in your evaluation?

5. In the abstract, you state "the complex network features were able to distinguish between control participants and mild cognitive impairment participants." However, the accuracy rates reported in the paper (around 60-80%) are not particularly impressive compared to the baseline of 50% for a binary classification problem. Please provide a more nuanced discussion of the practical significance of these accuracy rates.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the topic is important and the general approach has potential, the paper contains multiple critical methodological inconsistencies, significant errors in result presentation, and inconsistencies in terminology and dataset descriptions. The fundamental misunderstanding of Perozzi et al.'s method (which the authors claim to adapt) and the serious error in calculating the improvement percentage (4.71% vs. actual 12.68%) are particularly concerning. These issues suggest a lack of rigor in the methodology and evaluation that undermines the paper's credibility. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to address these fundamental issues before considering resubmission.
