

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich them with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short texts with disfluencies. The main contributions are: (1) applying complex networks enriched with word embeddings to MCI detection, (2) demonstrating that this approach outperforms traditional linguistic features in some cases, and (3) showing that combining network features with linguistic and BoW features improves classification accuracy.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application to a Significant Clinical Problem**: The paper addresses an important clinical problem - early detection of MCI - which has substantial implications for dementia prevention. The application of complex network approaches to this specific domain is novel, and the authors provide a strong clinical motivation for their work. The paper successfully demonstrates that complex network features can be competitive with linguistic features for this task, which is a significant contribution to the intersection of NLP and clinical diagnostics.

2. **Robust Multi-Dataset Evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach on three different datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) from different languages (English and Portuguese) and different narrative structures. This multi-dataset evaluation demonstrates the robustness of their approach across different linguistic and clinical contexts. The use of both English and Portuguese datasets is particularly valuable given the limited resources for Portuguese language processing.

3. **Practical Contribution to Automated Assessment**: The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of their approach by addressing the problem of manual preprocessing in neuropsychological assessments. By avoiding the need for manual sentence segmentation and disfluency correction, the authors present a more automated solution that could be integrated into clinical workflows. This is a significant practical contribution beyond the theoretical novelty.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Technique**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technique of enriching networks with word embeddings is directly adapted from Perozzi et al. (2014), with minimal innovation. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain how their specific implementation differs from existing approaches or why their method is particularly suited for short, disfluent speech transcripts. This lack of methodological novelty weakens the paper's contribution.

2. **Modest Performance Gains**: The accuracy improvements over baseline methods are relatively modest (e.g., 2.3% for Cookie Theft, 10% for Cinderella with manual processing). The paper fails to adequately justify why these small improvements are meaningful in a clinical context where diagnostic accuracy needs to be substantially higher than chance. The paper also doesn't compare against state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for text classification that might achieve higher accuracy.

3. **Insufficient Statistical Analysis**: The datasets used are quite small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 possible AD for Cookie Theft), and the paper doesn't adequately address potential overfitting or statistical significance of the results. The use of 5-fold cross-validation on such small datasets raises concerns about the reliability of the reported accuracy improvements.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to identify which specific network features or embedding techniques are most important for the performance gains. For instance, it's unclear whether the improvement comes from the network structure itself, the word embeddings, or the combination of both. This limits the interpretability of the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your network enrichment method differ from the approach in Perozzi et al. (2014), and why is your specific implementation better suited for short, disfluent speech transcripts than the original method?

2. Given the modest accuracy improvements reported (e.g., 2.3% on Cookie Theft), how do you justify that these results are clinically meaningful for MCI detection? What would be the minimum accuracy improvement needed to be practically useful in a clinical setting?

3. Could you provide more details on the statistical significance of your results, particularly given the small dataset sizes? How confident can we be that the observed improvements aren't due to chance?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel application of complex networks to a clinically important problem with promising results. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and the performance gains are modest. The paper would be significantly strengthened by (1) more rigorous statistical analysis of the results, (2) a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for text classification, and (3) more thorough ablation studies to identify the key components of the proposed method.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about methodological novelty, statistical significance, and clinical relevance in the camera-ready version. The clinical importance of the problem and the multi-dataset evaluation are strong points, but the paper's contribution would be more compelling with a clearer demonstration of why the specific network enrichment approach is superior to alternatives.
