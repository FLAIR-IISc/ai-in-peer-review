

# Review for ACL Conference

## Summary and Main Contributions

**Summary:** The paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches have limitations when dealing with disfluencies and short texts common in neuropsychological assessments. They model transcripts as co-occurrence networks and enrich them with semantic information using word embeddings, then characterize the networks using topological features. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) across English and Portuguese languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**
1. **Novel application of complex networks with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts:** The authors apply a network-based approach to model speech transcripts for MCI detection, which is a new application domain for this methodology. The enrichment of networks with semantic information is specifically tailored for the short, disfluent nature of neuropsychological assessment transcripts.
2. **Demonstration of network-based features outperforming traditional linguistic features in MCI detection:** The authors show that their complex network features, especially when enriched with word embeddings, provide better classification performance than traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches in the context of MCI detection.
3. **Systematic comparison of different feature types and fusion strategies for MCI detection:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different feature types (network-based, linguistic, bag-of-words) and fusion strategies (ensemble learning, multi-view learning), offering practical insights for MCI detection systems.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application to a clinically important problem:** The paper addresses a significant clinical challenge (early detection of MCI) with a potentially useful approach that could aid clinicians. The focus on fully automated systems that don't require manual annotation is particularly valuable for practical implementation. This application of complex networks to a specific clinical domain is a meaningful contribution that bridges NLP and healthcare.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets:** The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets with different characteristics (English vs. Portuguese, short descriptions vs. longer narratives, different levels of disfluency). This multi-dataset evaluation provides stronger evidence for the generalizability of their approach compared to single-dataset studies. The use of both English and Portuguese datasets also demonstrates the potential for cross-lingual applicability.

3. **Clear methodology and thorough feature characterization:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of how text is modeled as complex networks, how they are enriched with word embeddings, and which topological features are used. The description of the ten topological measurements (PageRank, Betweenness, Eccentricity, etc.) is thorough and well-justified. The authors also thoughtfully address preprocessing challenges specific to speech transcripts (e.g., not using lemmatization due to disfluencies).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty justification compared to prior work:** While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core methodology (using complex networks for text representation) has been explored in several prior NLP works (e.g., de Arruda et al., 2016; Amancio et al., 2012a). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these prior works. The claim that "these metrics have never been used before to detect MCI" needs stronger justification with a more thorough literature review and clearer differentiation from existing network-based text classification approaches.

2. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods:** The paper compares against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches, but doesn't compare against more recent deep learning methods that have been used for similar classification tasks (e.g., BERT-based approaches for MCI detection). This limits the significance of their claimed improvement, as the comparison is against older baselines rather than current state-of-the-art. The paper would be significantly strengthened by showing how their method compares to recent neural approaches.

3. **Methodological concerns with network enrichment:** The paper enriches co-occurrence networks using a cosine similarity threshold to add semantic edges. However, the paper doesn't explain how this threshold was determined, nor does it provide a sensitivity analysis showing how results change with different threshold values. This is a significant methodological gap as the threshold choice could substantially impact the results. The authors should provide a thorough analysis of this parameter.

4. **Lack of ablation studies to isolate contribution of network features:** The paper shows that combining network features with other features improves performance, but doesn't provide ablation studies to show how much of the improvement comes from the network features themselves versus the combination. This makes it difficult to assess the specific contribution of their novel network-based approach. The authors should present results showing the performance of network features alone, without combining with other features.

5. **Small dataset sizes and potential overfitting concerns:** The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD transcripts in Cookie Theft, 20 MCI and 20 controls in Cinderella), which increases the risk of overfitting, especially given the number of features used. The paper doesn't discuss how they mitigated overfitting concerns or performed additional validation. For a clinical application, this is a significant limitation that needs addressing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their complex network approach is "the first" to be used for MCI detection from speech transcripts. However, there are prior works (e.g., Vincze et al., 2016; Tóth et al., 2015) that have used linguistic features for MCI detection from speech. Could the authors clarify how their network-based approach differs from these prior works and why their specific methodology is novel for this application?

2. The authors enrich the networks using a cosine similarity threshold. How was this threshold determined (e.g., through hyperparameter optimization), and how sensitive are the results to changes in this threshold? Have they performed a sensitivity analysis to show that the threshold choice doesn't significantly impact the results?

3. The paper shows that combining network features with linguistic features improves performance, but doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the network features. Could the authors provide results showing the performance of network features alone (without combining with other features) and how much of the improvement comes specifically from the network features?

4. Given the small size of the datasets (e.g., 40-50 samples per class in some cases), how did the authors ensure that their results aren't due to overfitting? Have they performed additional validation steps (e.g., regularization, more folds of cross-validation, or using a separate test set) to address this concern?

## Overall Recommendation

**Decision: Reject**

**Justification:** While the paper addresses an important clinical problem with a potentially useful approach, the paper lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work on complex networks for text representation. The methodological gaps (particularly the lack of threshold sensitivity analysis and ablation studies) significantly undermine the claims of superiority of their network-based approach. The limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods and the small dataset sizes further limit the significance of the results. 

The paper could be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing a more thorough literature review that clearly differentiates their work from prior network-based text classification approaches
2. Adding a comparison with recent deep learning methods for MCI detection
3. Performing a sensitivity analysis of the cosine similarity threshold parameter
4. Including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of network features
5. Addressing overfitting concerns with additional validation steps

With these substantial revisions, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field, but as currently presented, it doesn't meet the high standards expected for ACL.
