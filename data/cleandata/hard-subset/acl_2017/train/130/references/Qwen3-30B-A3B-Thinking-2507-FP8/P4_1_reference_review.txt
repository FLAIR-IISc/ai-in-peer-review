

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings to better represent short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. They extract topological features from these enriched networks and evaluate their performance for classifying MCI versus control participants across three datasets: Cookie Theft (English), Cinderella (Portuguese), and ABCD (Portuguese). The results show that network-based features, particularly enriched networks, outperform traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations in most cases.

## Main Contributions (as assessed)
1. **A novel method for enriching complex networks with word embeddings to represent speech transcripts:** The authors introduce a technical approach to enrich word adjacency networks with semantic information from word embeddings, specifically addressing the problem of short texts having linear network structures with minimal topological information.

2. **Demonstration of network-based features outperforming linguistic features for MCI detection:** The paper provides empirical evidence that topological features from enriched networks achieve better classification accuracy than traditional linguistic features, particularly on raw (non-manually processed) transcripts.

3. **A fully automated system for MCI detection that avoids manual preprocessing:** The authors' approach eliminates the need for manual annotation or sentence segmentation, which is a significant practical advantage for clinical implementation.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of complex networks to MCI detection:** The paper presents a genuinely novel application of complex networks to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts. While complex networks have been used in other NLP tasks, their application to this clinical problem with a focus on speech transcripts is not well established in the literature, as the authors properly cite in their related work section.

2. **Technical soundness of network enrichment methodology:** The method for enriching networks with semantic information using word embeddings is well-motivated and technically sound. The authors clearly explain how they calculate cosine similarity between word vectors to add new edges, addressing the key challenge of short texts having linear network structures. The use of Bojanowski et al.'s method for word embeddings is appropriate for handling out-of-vocabulary words in the context of disfluent speech.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness across multiple datasets:** The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different characteristics, showing consistent performance improvements when using enriched networks. This multi-dataset validation strengthens the claim that their method is not dataset-specific and has general applicability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods:** The paper does not compare to recent deep learning approaches for MCI detection from speech, such as those using LSTMs, Transformers, or other neural network architectures. The authors focus on traditional machine learning methods and linguistic features, but the field has seen significant advances with neural approaches that should be addressed for a complete assessment of the method's competitiveness.

2. **Insufficient explanation for the observed performance differences:** While the paper reports that network features outperform linguistic features, particularly on raw transcripts, it lacks a thorough analysis explaining *why* this occurs. The claim that "linguistic features depend on grammatical texts to present good results" is made but not well-supported with evidence or detailed analysis of specific features affected by disfluencies.

3. **Small dataset sizes and limited statistical validation:** The datasets are relatively small (e.g., 64 samples in the Cookie Theft dataset), which limits the statistical power of the results. The paper does not perform statistical significance testing for the observed differences in accuracy between methods, which is a standard practice in machine learning papers and would strengthen the claims about performance improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that linguistic features depend on grammatical texts to present good results, as seen in the results of the manually processed Cinderella dataset (Table 6). Can the authors provide more detailed analysis of which specific linguistic features are most affected by disfluencies, and how the network approach better handles these challenges?

2. How does the performance of the proposed method compare to recent deep learning approaches for MCI detection from speech, such as those using RNNs, LSTMs, or Transformers, which have been shown to be effective in similar tasks?

3. Given the small dataset sizes (e.g., 64 samples for Cookie Theft), have the authors performed statistical significance testing to confirm that the observed performance differences between methods are meaningful?
