

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information using word embeddings. They claim that these network features outperform traditional linguistic features in distinguishing MCI patients from healthy controls, and that combining network features with linguistic features provides the best results. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with accuracy rates ranging from 60-80%.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Application of complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts** - This is the most prominent contribution, though it's not clear how novel this specific application is.
2. **Enrichment of word adjacency networks with semantic information** - The authors add edges based on word vector similarity, which is a minor extension of existing methods.
3. **Empirical validation across multiple datasets** - The paper tests the approach on three different datasets with different languages and narrative tasks.

## Strengths
1. **Practical healthcare application**: The work addresses a meaningful clinical problem with potential for real-world impact in early detection of cognitive decline.
2. **Multiple dataset evaluation**: The authors demonstrate their approach on three distinct datasets (English and Portuguese, different narrative tasks), which provides some evidence of generalizability.
3. **Combination of feature types**: The exploration of combining network features with linguistic and BoW features is a reasonable approach to improve performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty in core methodology**: The use of word adjacency networks for text analysis is not new (Mihalcea and Radev, 2011; Amancio et al., 2012a; etc.). The enrichment with word embeddings is a minor extension of Perozzi et al. (2014). The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how this specific implementation advances the state of the art beyond existing network-based approaches to text analysis.

2. **Insufficient comparison with established baselines**: The paper compares only against linguistic features and BoW, but doesn't compare with recent state-of-the-art NLP methods. In 2023, transformer-based models (BERT, RoBERTa, etc.) have become standard for text classification tasks. The absence of comparison with these methods is a critical weakness given the current state of NLP.

3. **Modest performance gains**: The reported improvements are marginal (2.3% in Cookie Theft, 10% in Cinderella, 4.71% in ABCD). Given the clinical importance of this task, these gains don't seem substantial enough to justify the new methodology, especially when compared to established methods that could potentially achieve better results with less complexity.

4. **Inadequate justification for methodological choices**: The paper states they removed lemmatization because it "may introduce undesirable noises/errors" but provides no evidence or experiments to support this claim. This is a significant preprocessing decision that could impact results, but they don't justify it adequately.

5. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper reports accuracy improvements but doesn't provide statistical significance testing to confirm that the improvements are meaningful. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the reported improvements are truly meaningful or just random fluctuations.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why didn't you compare your method with recent transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have become standard in NLP? How would your method compare to these state-of-the-art approaches?

2. Can you provide more detailed ablation studies to show which components of your method (network structure, word embeddings, etc.) are most responsible for the performance gains?

3. How do you justify the removal of lemmatization? Did you perform any experiments to compare the performance with and without lemmatization?

4. Were the reported improvements statistically significant? Can you provide p-values for the comparisons?

## Recommendation
Reject

## Justification
As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find this paper's contribution to be insufficiently novel and rigorously validated. The core methodology builds on existing work with minimal innovation, and the reported performance gains are marginal compared to the complexity of the proposed approach. Most critically, the paper fails to compare with recent state-of-the-art NLP methods, which is essential for a paper submitted to ACL. The lack of statistical significance testing and inadequate justification for key methodological choices further undermine the paper's credibility.

The healthcare application is meaningful, but the NLP contribution doesn't meet the standards expected for ACL. The paper would need to demonstrate substantially better results compared to established baselines, provide more convincing evidence of novelty, and address the methodological concerns before being acceptable for publication.
