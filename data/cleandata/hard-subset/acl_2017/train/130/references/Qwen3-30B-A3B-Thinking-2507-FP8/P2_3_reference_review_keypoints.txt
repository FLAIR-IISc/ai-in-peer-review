- Proposes modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI, addressing disfluencies and short text length.  
- Shows network topological features can distinguish MCI from controls and improve when combined with linguistic features.  
- Validates approach across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) in English and Portuguese.

- Tackles an important clinical problem (early MCI detection) with appropriate motivation and literature grounding.  
- Clearly states the problem and provides a well-explained, mathematically-defined solution.  
- Performs a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets, feature types, and classifiers.  
- Demonstrates potential for automated MCI detection without manual annotation.

- Core method has limited novelty, closely following prior work (e.g., Perozzi et al. 2014) without clearly distinguishing contributions.  
- Datasets are small, limiting statistical power and generalizability for clinical use.  
- Lacks comparison with recent state-of-the-art deep learning text classifiers (e.g., BERT, LSTM).  
- Claims about avoiding manual preprocessing are overstated given described disfluency handling.  
- Reported accuracies (max ~80%) are below clinically acceptable thresholds (>90%).

- How does the approach compare to modern deep learning methods in accuracy, compute cost, and clinical suitability?  
- Please justify dataset sizes, rationale for sampling, and plans for dataset expansion to improve power.  
- Clarify what preprocessing is automated versus requiring human input and how this differs from prior work.

- Overall: technically sound and well-evaluated but limited novelty, small datasets, missing SOTA comparisons, and subclinical accuracy; recommendation: reject unless these issues (data size, comparative baselines, clearer novelty, improved accuracy) are addressed.
