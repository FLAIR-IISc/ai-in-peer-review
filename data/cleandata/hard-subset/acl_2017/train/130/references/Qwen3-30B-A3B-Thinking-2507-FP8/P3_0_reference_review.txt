

# Peer Review: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling narratives as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim their method outperforms traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches for MCI detection. They evaluate their approach across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with varying languages (English and Portuguese) and narrative lengths.

## Primary Contributions
1. A novel application of enriched complex networks (with word embeddings) to model speech transcripts for MCI detection
2. Demonstration of superior performance over traditional linguistic features in certain datasets
3. Cross-dataset evaluation across English and Portuguese narratives with different lengths

## Strengths

1. **Novel methodology for MCI detection**: The use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings represents a significant departure from traditional linguistic feature-based approaches. The authors provide a compelling rationale for why this approach may be better suited for short, disfluent transcripts (Section 3), addressing a key challenge in this domain.

2. **Robust multi-dataset evaluation**: The evaluation across three distinct datasets with different languages and narrative structures demonstrates the approach's generalizability. The 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset is particularly impressive for a clinical detection task.

3. **Clear technical implementation**: The paper provides sufficient detail on network construction, embedding enrichment, and topological feature extraction. The specific choice of 10 topological measures (PageRank, Betweenness, etc.) is well-justified and standard in complex network analysis.

4. **Clinical relevance**: Early detection of MCI is a critical clinical need, and the paper directly addresses this problem with a promising computational approach that could potentially support clinical decision-making.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art NLP methods**: As an ACL submission, the paper's most significant weakness is the absence of comparison with contemporary deep learning approaches. The authors don't compare their method with transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have become the standard in NLP and have shown success in clinical text analysis. This omission significantly undermines the paper's contribution in the context of ACL, which prioritizes novel NLP approaches.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: While the paper claims enriched networks outperform traditional methods, it doesn't provide sufficient explanation for *why* this is the case. A deeper analysis of which topological features correlate most strongly with MCI would greatly strengthen the paper. The authors could better explain how the enriched network structure captures cognitive decline patterns that linguistic features miss.

3. **Questionable preprocessing choices**: The paper states they omitted lemmatization because it "may introduce undesirable noises/errors" in disfluent transcriptions. This is counterintuitive, as lemmatization typically reduces dimensionality and handles different word forms. The authors provide no evidence that lemmatization would actually harm their approach for these specific transcriptions, which weakens their methodology.

4. **Small dataset sizes**: The Cinderella dataset has only 20 MCI and 20 control participants, and the Cookie Theft dataset has limited controls (the paper states "43 transcripts of patients with MCI, sampled from 326 people diagnosed with probable AD; 21 with possible AD" - it's unclear how many controls they have). Small sample sizes increase the risk of overfitting and unreliable results, which the paper doesn't adequately address.

5. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper doesn't perform an ablation study to identify which topological features contribute most to classification performance. Understanding which network properties are most informative for MCI detection would provide valuable insights for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why didn't you compare your approach with transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) that have become standard in NLP for clinical text analysis, especially given this is an ACL submission?

2. Can you provide empirical evidence that lemmatization would actually introduce noise in these specific disfluent transcriptions? Given that the authors previously used manual revision to improve parsing (Section 4.1.2), wouldn't lemmatization help reduce the need for this manual processing?

3. The paper claims enriched networks (CNE) outperform standard networks (CN), but doesn't explain which specific aspects of the embedding enrichment are most beneficial. Could you elaborate on which topological features show the greatest improvement with enrichment?

4. The paper mentions that "short texts have almost linear networks" (Section 3), but doesn't explain how enrichment specifically addresses this limitation. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of this mechanism?

5. The results show SVM-RBF performs best in most cases, but the paper doesn't discuss why this kernel is particularly suitable for this task compared to linear or other kernels.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel approach to MCI detection that shows promising results, particularly on the ABCD dataset (80% accuracy). The method is well-motivated and the multi-dataset evaluation is a strength. However, the absence of comparison with state-of-the-art NLP methods (a critical omission for an ACL submission), the questionable preprocessing choices, and the small dataset sizes represent significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with contemporary NLP methods, a deeper theoretical analysis of why their approach works, and addressing the dataset limitations.

While the clinical relevance is strong, the contribution to NLP methodology is limited by the lack of comparison with modern techniques. The authors should reconsider the scope of their contribution in light of recent advances in NLP for clinical applications. With substantial revisions addressing these concerns, particularly the comparison with state-of-the-art methods, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.
