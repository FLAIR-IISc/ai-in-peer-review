

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling narratives as complex networks and enriching them with word embeddings. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features require extensive manual preprocessing due to disfluencies in speech transcripts, and they claim that their network-based approach can overcome this limitation. They evaluate their method on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) and report modest improvements over traditional linguistic features.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Methodological adaptation**: The paper adapts complex network modeling to MCI detection with word embedding enrichment, though the novelty is limited.
2. **Empirical evaluation**: The paper provides an evaluation across three different datasets, which is a positive contribution to the field.
3. **Automated processing claim**: The authors claim to provide a fully automated system that avoids manual preprocessing, which has practical clinical relevance.

## Strengths

1. **Empirical evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates on three distinct datasets (English Cookie Theft, Portuguese Cinderella, and Portuguese ABCD), which provides a more comprehensive assessment than many similar studies. This demonstrates the approach's potential across different languages and clinical contexts.

2. **Clear presentation of methodology**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the complex network modeling approach and how word embeddings are used to enrich the networks. The methodology is well-structured and easy to follow, which is valuable for reproducibility.

3. **Practical clinical relevance**: The paper makes a compelling argument for the need for automated systems in clinical settings where manual processing is time-consuming and expensive. This practical motivation is a strong point for the work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper fails to compare its approach to recent, established methods in MCI detection. The comparison is limited to traditional linguistic features (which are relatively simple and outdated) and bag-of-words, but not to modern deep learning approaches that have been successfully applied to similar tasks. This is a major weakness for an ACL paper, as it fails to establish the novelty or superiority of the proposed method.

2. **Questionable claims about network features outperforming linguistic features**: The paper claims that "topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics," but the evidence is weak. The linguistic features used are not the most advanced available (e.g., they don't use modern NLP tools for Portuguese, which the authors acknowledge are limited), and the improvements are marginal (e.g., 2.3% improvement for Cookie Theft, 10% for Cinderella). Without comparison to more sophisticated linguistic features or modern NLP approaches, this claim is unsubstantiated.

3. **Lack of rigorous statistical validation**: The paper reports accuracy numbers but provides no statistical significance tests to confirm that the observed improvements are meaningful. Without such validation, it's impossible to determine if the improvements are due to the proposed method or simply random variation.

4. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: The use of complex networks for text classification is not new (as cited in the paper, it has been used for text classification, author detection, etc.). The enrichment with word embeddings is a relatively simple extension that doesn't fundamentally advance the field. The paper fails to clearly articulate how this specific combination addresses limitations of previous network-based approaches.

5. **Small effect sizes**: The improvements claimed are relatively small (2.3% to 10% across datasets), which may not be clinically significant in the context of MCI diagnosis where high accuracy is crucial. The paper doesn't discuss the clinical implications of these small improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why didn't you compare your approach to recent deep learning methods (e.g., LSTM, Transformer-based models) that have been successfully applied to MCI detection from speech? How does your network-based approach compare to these state-of-the-art methods?

2. The paper claims that linguistic features "depend on grammatical texts to present good results," but the paper doesn't provide evidence that the linguistic features used here are the most advanced available. Why weren't more sophisticated linguistic features (e.g., those using modern NLP tools) used for comparison?

3. The paper states that "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics," but the linguistic features used appear to be relatively basic (e.g., Coh-Metrix metrics). Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why these specific network features are superior, and how they address limitations of traditional linguistic approaches?

4. The paper mentions that "short texts have almost linear networks, and the topological measures of these networks have little or no information relevant to classification." However, the paper doesn't provide evidence that the enrichment with word embeddings actually solves this problem. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of how the enrichment improves the network structure and what specific topological properties become more informative?

5. The paper claims to provide a "fully automated system that does not require the input of a human annotator," but the preprocessing steps (removing disfluencies) still require rule-based processing. How does this compare to other fully automated systems in the literature, and what specific advantages does it offer?

## Overall Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. While the paper presents a clear methodology and provides some empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, it lacks the methodological rigor and novelty required for an ACL publication. The claims about the superiority of the proposed approach are unsubstantiated due to inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and the improvements reported are marginal. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its evaluation by comparing against more recent and relevant approaches in the field, provide statistical validation of results, and better articulate the novelty of their approach before it would be suitable for publication at ACL.
