

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling narratives as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors represent speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, then enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings (using cosine similarity) to better capture the meaning of short texts produced in neuropsychological assessments. They extract topological features from these enriched networks and combine them with linguistic features and Bag-of-Words (BoW) features to train classifiers that distinguish between control participants and MCI participants. The approach is evaluated on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different languages (English and Portuguese) and text lengths.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel application of complex network modeling with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts**: The primary contribution is applying complex network modeling (with enrichment via word embeddings) to the specific clinical NLP problem of MCI detection, addressing challenges like disfluencies and short text lengths.

2. **Demonstration that enriched complex network features outperform traditional linguistic features in MCI detection**: The authors show that their enriched network features (CNE) consistently achieve better performance than standard complex network features (CN) and often outperform linguistic features, especially for shorter texts.

3. **Systematic evaluation of feature combination strategies**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of different feature sets (topological, linguistic, BoW) and combination methods (ensemble and multi-view learning), demonstrating that combining these approaches leads to improved accuracy.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and compelling motivation for the problem**: The paper provides a strong clinical motivation for detecting MCI early, explaining the importance of language as a cognitive marker and the limitations of current approaches. The problem statement is well-motivated, especially regarding the challenges of working with disfluent speech transcripts.

2. **Effective demonstration of the enriched network approach**: The paper provides clear evidence that enriching co-occurrence networks with word embeddings significantly improves performance, particularly for shorter texts. The comparison between CN and CNE in Tables 4-7 shows consistent improvements across datasets, which is a strong point for the methodology.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and languages**: The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets with different languages (English and Portuguese), text lengths, and clinical contexts. This demonstrates the robustness of their approach across different scenarios, which is valuable for a clinical application.

4. **Practical consideration of automated processing**: The authors explicitly address the need for a fully automated system that doesn't require human annotators, which is a significant practical consideration for clinical applications. Their preprocessing pipeline (removing disfluencies automatically) is well-justified.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to existing work**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technique of using complex networks for text classification has been extensively explored in prior work (Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c, 2013; de Arruda et al., 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these prior works. The authors claim their method is "never used before to detect MCI in speech transcripts," but don't adequately establish this by comparing to the relevant literature.

2. **Limited theoretical analysis of why the approach works**: The paper presents good empirical results but lacks a detailed analysis of *why* the enriched networks work better for MCI detection. For example, which specific topological features are most informative? How do the enriched networks capture MCI-related linguistic patterns that traditional features miss? Without this analysis, the theoretical contribution of the work is limited.

3. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art NLP methods**: The paper compares their approach to traditional linguistic features, BoW, and standard complex networks, but doesn't compare to recent deep learning methods that might be more suitable for this task (e.g., RNNs, LSTMs, transformers). The results are compared to other MCI detection studies, but not to the current state-of-the-art in NLP for similar clinical classification tasks.

4. **Small dataset sizes with limited statistical analysis**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD for Cookie Theft; 20 MCI and 20 control for Cinderella), which limits the statistical power of the results. The paper doesn't perform statistical significance tests to confirm that the performance differences between methods are significant, which is a concern for a conference like ACL.

5. **Lack of clinical significance discussion**: While the paper reports accuracy rates (up to 80%), it doesn't discuss the clinical significance of these results. What is the practical value of a 75-80% accuracy rate for MCI detection in a clinical setting? How does this compare to current clinical diagnostic methods? This is important for the paper's impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the enriched network approach is novel for MCI detection, but prior work has used complex networks for text classification (Amancio et al., 2012a, 2012b, 2012c, 2013; de Arruda et al., 2016). Can you clarify exactly how your application to MCI detection differs from these prior works in terms of methodology and clinical context?

2. The paper shows that CNE outperforms CN, but doesn't identify which specific topological features are most informative for MCI detection. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which features contribute most to the performance gains, and why they might be relevant for MCI detection?

3. Given the small dataset sizes, have you performed statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to confirm that the performance differences between methods are statistically significant? If not, how confident are you that the observed improvements are not due to chance?

4. The paper mentions that the ABCD dataset achieved 80% accuracy with CNE-LM-BoW. How does this compare to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for similar clinical text classification tasks? Have you considered comparing your approach to these methods?

5. The paper identifies a "clear need of publicly available datasets" for MCI detection. Will the authors make their datasets publicly available to facilitate future research in this area, as this would significantly benefit the field?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. The paper presents a reasonable application of complex networks to a clinically important problem with solid empirical results. However, the lack of clear novelty compared to existing work, the limited theoretical analysis of why the approach works, and the insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods prevent it from meeting ACL's standards for publication. The small dataset sizes and lack of statistical significance testing also weaken the contribution.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Strengthen the novelty claim**: Conduct a more thorough literature review to clearly establish how this application differs from prior work on complex networks in text classification. Focus on the specific challenges of MCI detection from disfluent speech transcripts.

2. **Add theoretical analysis**: Provide a more detailed analysis of which topological features are most informative for MCI detection and why they might capture relevant linguistic patterns associated with cognitive impairment.

3. **Include comparisons with deep learning methods**: Compare your approach to recent deep learning methods (e.g., BERT, LSTM-based classifiers) that have shown strong performance on similar text classification tasks.

4. **Perform statistical significance testing**: Add statistical significance tests to confirm that the observed performance differences between methods are meaningful.

5. **Discuss clinical significance**: Provide a discussion of the clinical significance of the reported accuracy rates and how they compare to current clinical diagnostic methods.

With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of clinical NLP and computational linguistics.
