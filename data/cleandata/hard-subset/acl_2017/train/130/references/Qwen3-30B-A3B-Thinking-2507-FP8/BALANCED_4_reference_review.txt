

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich these networks with semantic information by adding edges between words with high cosine similarity in their word embedding space. They evaluate this approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), comparing it to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches. The results show that the enriched complex network features achieve competitive performance, with multi-view learning combining network features, linguistic features, and bag-of-words providing the best results.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel application of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for MCI detection from speech transcripts, which avoids the need for manual preprocessing of disfluencies and grammar correction.
2. A comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) demonstrating that network-based features are competitive with linguistic features for MCI detection.
3. A demonstration that combining different feature spaces through multi-view learning can improve classification accuracy for MCI detection.

## Strengths

1. **Meaningful Application to Healthcare Problem**: The paper addresses an important healthcare problem - early detection of MCI, which can lead to better treatment outcomes. MCI detection is a significant challenge in clinical practice, and the paper's focus on a fully automated system that doesn't require manual annotation is a practical contribution to the field. The connection between language impairment and cognitive decline is well-established, making this a relevant application of NLP techniques to healthcare.

2. **Novel Representation Approach**: The authors' approach of modeling speech transcripts as complex networks and enriching them with word embeddings is creative and well-motivated. The paper clearly explains why traditional linguistic features have limitations with disfluent speech transcripts (requiring manual preprocessing), and how their network-based approach circumvents this problem. The supplementary figures (Figure 1 and 2) effectively demonstrate how the enrichment process creates more informative networks.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different languages (English and Portuguese) and different text lengths. This multi-dataset evaluation strengthens the claim that their approach is robust across different types of speech transcripts. The comparison with linguistic features and bag-of-words across these datasets provides valuable insights about the relative strengths of different feature types.

4. **Practical Considerations for Real-World Implementation**: The authors explicitly address the limitations of traditional approaches (requiring manual preprocessing for disfluencies) and position their method as a step toward a fully automated system. This focus on practical implementation challenges is valuable for both NLP and healthcare applications, where real-world deployment considerations are critical.

5. **Clear Methodological Presentation**: The paper provides a well-structured description of their methodology, including detailed explanations of the network enrichment process, the features extracted, and the evaluation framework. The supplementary material (A.1 and A.2) provides additional useful details about the datasets and metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methodology**: The core contribution - enriching complex networks with word embeddings - is largely based on Perozzi et al. (2014), which has been applied to text classification tasks before. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain how their application to MCI detection from speech transcripts differs substantially from prior work in text classification. The novelty of the approach is overstated compared to existing literature, particularly in NLP, where network-based text representations have been explored extensively.

2. **Modest Performance Gains**: The reported accuracy improvements are relatively modest (5-10% over baseline methods), and the absolute accuracy rates (60-80%) are not particularly high compared to other work in the field. The paper doesn't adequately address whether these accuracy levels are clinically meaningful or how they compare to the state-of-the-art in MCI detection from speech. Without strong performance gains, the practical impact of the method is questionable.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The related work section mentions other approaches to MCI detection but doesn't provide a direct comparison of results. The paper claims their method is competitive, but without a clear comparison to the current state-of-the-art methods in the same datasets, it's difficult to assess the true contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing results directly with recent approaches in the field.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Feature Importance**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of which specific network features are most important for distinguishing MCI from control participants. Understanding which topological properties are most informative could provide valuable insights into the cognitive processes underlying language production in MCI, which would be particularly valuable for clinical applications.

5. **Insufficient Discussion of Practical Limitations**: While the paper mentions avoiding manual preprocessing, it doesn't discuss other practical limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform with more diverse datasets? What are the computational costs compared to other approaches? How would the system handle noisy speech transcripts from different recording conditions? These considerations are critical for real-world clinical deployment.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper cites Perozzi et al. (2014) as the basis for their network enrichment approach. Can you clarify how your application to MCI detection from speech transcripts differs from their work, and how it specifically addresses the unique challenges of speech transcripts (vs. more formal text)?

2. The paper reports modest accuracy improvements (5-10%) over baseline methods. How do these results compare to the state-of-the-art in MCI detection from speech transcripts? What are the practical implications of these small improvements for clinical diagnosis?

3. The paper mentions that for the Cinderella dataset, they used a manually revised version of the transcripts to improve parsing results. How would your approach perform if you applied the same manual preprocessing to all datasets (including Cookie Theft), and how would this affect your comparison between network features and linguistic features?

4. The paper doesn't discuss the computational requirements of your method compared to traditional linguistic feature extraction. How does the computational cost of your approach compare to other methods for MCI detection, and how would this impact deployment in clinical settings?

5. The paper claims that "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" in some cases. Could you provide more details about which specific topological features were most informative for MCI detection, and how these features might relate to cognitive processes affected by MCI?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a reasonable contribution to the intersection of NLP and healthcare. However, the claimed novelty is somewhat overstated, and the performance gains are modest. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a more precise comparison with state-of-the-art methods in MCI detection from speech
2. More clearly articulate the novelty of their approach compared to existing network-based text representation methods
3. Discuss the practical limitations of their method and how they might be addressed
4. Provide more detailed analysis of which network features are most informative for MCI detection

The paper has potential for a strong contribution to the field, but would benefit from a more critical assessment of its novelty and practical impact. With these revisions, it could make a meaningful contribution to both NLP and healthcare applications.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents an interesting application of complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts, but the novelty of the core approach is limited and the performance gains are modest. While the work is well-executed and addresses an important healthcare problem, the paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of its contribution relative to existing literature. I recommend **weak reject** - the paper is of sufficient quality for publication in a specialized healthcare/NLP journal, but does not meet the high standards of ACL for novelty and impact in the broader NLP community. With significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the novelty assessment and performance comparison, this could become a stronger submission.
