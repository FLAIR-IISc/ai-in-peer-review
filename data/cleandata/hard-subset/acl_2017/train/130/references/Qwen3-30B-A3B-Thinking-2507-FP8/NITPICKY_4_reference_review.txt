

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks, enrich these networks with semantic information from word embeddings, and extract topological features for classification. They compare their method against linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches across three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD). The paper claims that their enriched network features (CNE) outperform other approaches, with multi-view learning combining features achieving the highest accuracy.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Application of complex networks to MCI detection**: The authors apply complex network modeling to speech transcripts for MCI detection, which is a novel application in this domain.
2. **Enrichment of networks with word embeddings**: The authors propose enriching word adjacency networks with semantic information using word embeddings to better represent short texts.
3. **Demonstration of reduced need for manual preprocessing**: The approach avoids the need for manual revision of transcripts, which is a practical advantage over previous methods.

## Strengths
1. **Novel application of complex networks**: The application of complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts represents a novel approach in the field, as the authors correctly note that this specific application hasn't been previously explored.

2. **Practical approach to preprocessing**: The authors correctly identify the problem with manual preprocessing of speech transcripts (which is expensive and error-prone), and their approach to avoid this is a significant practical contribution to the field.

3. **Multiple dataset evaluation**: The use of three different datasets (English and Portuguese, different narrative styles) adds credibility to the results and demonstrates the robustness of the approach across different languages and contexts.

## Weaknesses
1. **Critical discrepancy in reported results**: The paper claims a "4.71% improvement" for the ABCD dataset in the text (Section 5), but the actual improvement from the best individual classifier (SVM-RBF on CNE at 0.6436) to the best multi-view classifier (CNE-LM-BoW at 0.8018) is 15.64% (0.8018 - 0.6436 = 0.1582). This is a significant error that undermines the credibility of the paper. The text also claims a "2.3% improvement" for Cookie Theft, but the actual improvement is 2.36% (0.6500 - 0.6264), which is close enough to be considered correct, but the ABCD discrepancy is too large to ignore.

2. **Lack of justification for topological metrics**: The authors list 10 topological metrics for network characterization but provide no justification for why these specific metrics were chosen over others. This is a major methodological flaw as it's unclear why these metrics are relevant for MCI detection, and whether they're the optimal choice.

3. **Inconsistent and poorly defined terminology**: The paper uses "CNE" without proper definition (it's used in tables but not clearly defined in the text). The paper also inconsistently uses "complexity networks" and "complex networks" interchangeably, which is confusing. Additionally, "CNE" is used in the tables but not defined in the text.

4. **Poor formatting and grammatical issues**: The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent formatting, and unclear phrasing (e.g., "the Control group has an average of 9.58 sentences per narrative, with each sentence having an average of 9.18 words; as for the MCI group, it has an average of 10.97 sentences per narrative, with 10.33 words per sentence in average" should be rephrased for clarity).

5. **Inconsistent citation and reference formatting**: The references have inconsistent formatting with odd characters like "\Q" and "\E" in the year (e.g., "\Q2016\E" instead of "2016"), and some references are truncated (e.g., "https://doi.org/10.1088/1742-").

## Questions to Authors
1. The text claims a 4.71% improvement for the ABCD dataset, but the actual improvement from the best individual classifier (SVM-RBF on CNE at 0.6436) to the best multi-view classifier (CNE-LM-BoW at 0.8018) is 15.64%. Can you clarify this discrepancy? Is there a typo in the text or in the table values?

2. The paper lists 10 topological metrics for network characterization but provides no justification for why these specific metrics were chosen. Can you explain your selection criteria for these metrics and provide evidence that they're particularly relevant for MCI detection?

3. The paper uses "CNE" throughout the tables without defining it in the text. What does "CNE" stand for, and why is it used as a feature name?

4. The paper mentions using "word embeddings" but doesn't specify which ones or their parameters. Can you provide details on the specific word embeddings used (including dimensionality, training corpus, etc.)?

5. For the Bag-of-Words approach, the paper states "term frequency was used" but doesn't clarify if this is binary, TF, or TF-IDF. Can you specify which representation was used?

## Additional Comments
The paper has several minor formatting inconsistencies that need to be addressed before publication, including inconsistent citation styles, missing units in tables (e.g., "Avg. Years of Education (SD)" should specify "years"), and grammatical errors that affect readability. These are not critical for the main contribution but are important for a professional publication.

The most significant issue is the discrepancy in the reported improvement for the ABCD dataset, which is a major methodological error. This would be difficult to justify to the ACL community and would seriously undermine the credibility of the paper.

The authors make a good case for the need for automated approaches to MCI detection, but the paper would benefit from a stronger justification for the specific network metrics used and a clearer definition of the key terms.

Given the significant discrepancy in reported results and the lack of justification for key methodological choices, I recommend rejection of this paper. The technical flaws and inconsistencies are too severe to overlook, even though the overall approach is interesting.
