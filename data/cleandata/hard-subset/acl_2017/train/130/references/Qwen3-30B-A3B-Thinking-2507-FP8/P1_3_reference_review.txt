

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes using enriched complex networks (with word embeddings) to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts, avoiding the need for manual preprocessing required by traditional linguistic approaches. The authors model transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich them with semantic information from word embeddings. They then extract topological features from these networks and combine them with linguistic features and bag-of-words representations for classification.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1. A novel application of enriched complex networks for MCI detection from speech transcripts, avoiding manual preprocessing steps required by previous approaches.
2. Comprehensive evaluation across three different datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) in both English and Portuguese, demonstrating consistent improvements with the network-based approach.
3. The demonstration that multi-view learning (combining network features, linguistic features, and BoW) significantly improves classification performance over individual feature types.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Value**: The paper addresses a meaningful clinical problem (early MCI detection) with a method that could potentially be implemented in real-world clinical settings. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach avoids the labor-intensive manual preprocessing required by previous work, which is a major practical advantage for clinical adoption.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments across three distinct datasets with different linguistic characteristics (English vs. Portuguese, short vs. long narratives). The results consistently show that their enriched network features outperform traditional linguistic features (with up to 10% improvement in accuracy), and multi-view learning provides significant gains (up to 80% accuracy on ABCD dataset).

3. **Clear Technical Innovation**: While complex networks have been used for text analysis before, their specific application to MCI detection with enrichment via word embeddings is novel. The paper provides a compelling technical rationale for why this approach works better than traditional methods, particularly for handling disfluencies and short texts common in neuropsychological assessments.

4. **Well-Structured Comparison**: The paper provides a clear comparison of different feature types (network, linguistic, BoW) and classification algorithms, with detailed ablation studies showing the relative contributions of each component. This makes it easy to understand what aspects of the approach are most valuable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty Claim**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core methodology (using complex networks for text analysis) builds directly on existing work by Amancio et al. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their contribution from prior work in NLP. For instance, they cite Perozzi et al. (2014) for the word embedding enrichment approach, but don't clearly articulate how their specific application differs from or improves upon this prior method.

2. **Small Dataset Sizes**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., only 43 MCI and 21 control transcripts in the Cookie Theft dataset), which limits the statistical power of the results. With such small sample sizes, the reported accuracy improvements (e.g., 2.3-10% over baselines) might not be statistically significant, and the results may not generalize well to larger populations.

3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent deep learning approaches for text classification that might be more effective for this task. For example, they don't compare against transformer-based models or other contemporary neural network architectures that have shown strong performance in text classification tasks.

4. **Superficial Explanation of Enrichment Process**: The paper describes the network enrichment process but doesn't provide sufficient detail about how the threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity was determined, or how this affects the resulting network structure. This makes it difficult to replicate or understand the full methodological contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these improvements. Can the authors provide p-values or confidence intervals for the accuracy differences reported?

2. The authors state that "the enrichment with word embeddings helps with short texts" but don't provide evidence of how the network structure changes with enrichment (e.g., how the clustering coefficient or diameter changes). Could the authors provide additional analysis of how the enrichment affects the network properties?

3. The paper mentions using the Bojanowski et al. (2016) method for word embeddings, but doesn't explain why this specific method was chosen over others (e.g., GloVe or FastText). What was the rationale for this choice, and how did it compare to other embedding methods?

4. The paper reports 80% accuracy on the ABCD dataset with multi-view learning, which is comparable to the state-of-the-art. However, the paper doesn't discuss the clinical significance of this accuracy rate. What would be the clinical utility of a 80% accurate MCI detection system in real-world settings?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a promising approach for MCI detection from speech transcripts that avoids manual preprocessing and achieves competitive results. The main strengths are the practical clinical relevance, thorough evaluation across multiple datasets, and clear demonstration of the benefits of the network-based approach. However, the novelty claim is somewhat overstated, and the small dataset sizes limit the confidence in the results. With appropriate revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics for clinical applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
