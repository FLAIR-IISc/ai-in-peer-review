

# Review for ACL Conference

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors address the challenge of analyzing clinical speech transcripts, which contain disfluencies and are often short, making traditional linguistic feature extraction difficult. They model transcripts as word adjacency networks and enrich them with semantic information using word embeddings. The approach is evaluated across three datasets (Cookie Theft in English, Cinderella in Portuguese, and ABCD in Portuguese) using four machine learning classifiers, with results showing that enriched complex networks outperform traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words approaches.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel method for modeling speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with word embeddings specifically designed to handle the challenges of short texts with disfluencies common in clinical speech assessments.
2. Empirical demonstration that this network-based approach provides competitive or superior performance to traditional linguistic features for MCI detection, with improvements of 2-10% over baselines across multiple datasets.
3. Detailed analysis of topological properties of these networks and their relevance to cognitive impairment detection, providing new insights into how language structure relates to cognitive decline.

## Strengths
1. **Significant clinical relevance with a technically sound approach**: The paper addresses a meaningful clinical problem (early detection of MCI) with a novel technical approach that combines complex network theory with word embeddings. This is particularly valuable for clinical NLP applications where disfluencies and short text length are common challenges. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional linguistic approaches when dealing with clinical speech transcripts containing disfluencies.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across three different datasets in both English and Portuguese, demonstrating the generalizability of the approach. This is a strong point as it shows the method works across languages and different types of speech transcripts (short descriptions vs. longer narratives), which enhances the paper's contribution.

3. **Effective solution to the short text classification problem**: The authors correctly identify that short texts have almost linear networks, making topological measures less informative, and their solution of enriching networks with word embeddings is well-motivated and effective. The results (Figure 2) clearly demonstrate that enriched networks capture more meaningful structure than unenriched networks.

4. **Strong methodological foundation**: The paper builds on solid theoretical foundations in complex network theory and word embeddings, with appropriate citations to prior work. The preprocessing pipeline is well-justified (removing lemmatization to avoid introducing errors from parsing disfluent texts), and the evaluation methodology (5-fold cross-validation, multiple classifiers) is standard and appropriate for the task.

## Weaknesses
1. **Overstated novelty**: While the specific application to MCI detection is novel, the core approach of using complex networks for text analysis builds directly on prior work by Amancio et al. (2012, 2015), and the enrichment with word embeddings is similar to Perozzi et al. (2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their specific contribution from these prior works, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Modest performance gains**: The performance improvements over baselines are typically small (2-10% across datasets), which may not be clinically significant. For example, the best accuracy on the Cookie Theft dataset is 65%, which is on the lower end for clinical diagnostic tools. The paper doesn't adequately discuss the clinical relevance of these modest gains or how they might translate to real-world diagnostic utility.

3. **Lack of deeper analysis of why network features work better**: The authors claim that linguistic features depend on grammatical texts, but they don't provide a detailed analysis of what specific linguistic properties are being captured by the network features. A deeper exploration of the relationship between network topology and cognitive impairment would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets used are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD for Cookie Theft), which limits the statistical power of the results. While this is common in clinical NLP research due to data scarcity, the paper should acknowledge this limitation more explicitly and discuss its potential impact on the reliability of the findings.

5. **Limited discussion of computational complexity**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed approach, which could be a significant concern for real-world clinical deployment where efficiency is important.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do the authors plan to address the modest performance gains (typically 2-10%) in the context of clinical applications where higher accuracy is often required? Could the authors discuss potential clinical significance of these improvements?

2. The paper mentions that linguistic features depend on grammatical texts, but the authors also manually processed the Cinderella dataset to improve parsing results. Can the authors clarify the exact relationship between text grammaticality and the performance of linguistic features versus network features?

3. The paper states that lemmatization was removed from preprocessing because it might introduce errors, but doesn't compare performance with and without lemmatization. Could the authors provide this comparison to better justify their preprocessing choices?

4. The paper uses a small number of subjects in the datasets (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD for Cookie Theft). How do the authors address the potential limitations of small sample sizes on the statistical reliability of their results?
