- Proposes modeling speech transcripts as word adjacency networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI).
- Claims enrichment overcomes sparse/linear topology issues in short-text network analysis.
- Compares topological network features, linguistic features, and bag-of-words for MCI classification.

- Tackles an important clinical problem (early MCI detection) using non-invasive speech analysis.
- Uses multiple datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD), languages, and classifiers with 5-fold CV for robust evaluation.
- Demonstrates clearly how enrichment makes short-text networks more informative (Figure 2).

- Core technique (network + embedding enrichment) lacks clear novelty and insufficiently differentiated from prior work.
- Paper omits critical methodological details (cosine threshold selection, exact embedding model choices and rationale).
- Reported performance gains are marginal and may not justify added complexity (e.g., small absolute accuracy improvements).
- No statistical significance testing reported, problematic given small dataset sizes.

- How was the cosine similarity threshold for enrichment determined or optimized?
- How does this approach compare directly to state-of-the-art MCI-from-speech methods?
- Why choose Bojanowski et al. (2016) embeddings despite noted syntactic bias, and what about more semantic embeddings?

- Overall: sound methodology and evaluation but limited novelty, insufficient methodological detail, marginal gains—more suitable for a clinical venue than ACL.
- Suggested improvements: stronger comparison to prior MCI work, fuller enrichment methodology, statistical tests, and clinical justification for marginal improvements.
- Recommendation: Weak Reject.
