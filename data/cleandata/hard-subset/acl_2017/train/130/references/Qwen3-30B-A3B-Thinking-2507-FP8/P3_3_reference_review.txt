

# Peer Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim that their method outperforms traditional linguistic features for MCI detection while avoiding manual preprocessing steps. The main contributions I identify are: (1) application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts, (2) demonstration of cross-lingual applicability (English and Portuguese datasets), and (3) showing that topological features can effectively represent short texts with disfluencies.

## Strengths

1. **Meaningful Application to Clinical Problem**: The paper addresses an important clinical problem - early detection of MCI, which is critical for timely intervention. The authors convincingly argue why language analysis is valuable for cognitive assessment, citing the prevalence of language changes in early dementia stages.

2. **Thorough Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different linguistic characteristics and languages (English and Portuguese). This multi-dataset evaluation provides stronger evidence of their method's robustness than single-dataset studies.

3. **Thoughtful Preprocessing Considerations**: The authors make a well-justified decision to omit lemmatization to avoid introducing errors from disfluencies in speech transcripts. This is an important consideration that many prior NLP approaches for clinical text overlook.

4. **Demonstrated Practical Advantage**: The paper successfully shows that their approach can work without extensive manual preprocessing (unlike previous Portuguese language studies requiring manual sentence segmentation), which is a significant practical advantage for clinical deployment.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty Justification**: While the paper claims novelty in applying complex networks to MCI detection, the core technique (word adjacency networks) has been widely used in NLP for text classification (Amancio et al., 2012, 2016). The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its specific contribution from previous work. The key novelty appears to be the application to MCI detection, but the paper doesn't adequately establish how this differs from prior applications of similar techniques to text classification.

2. **Unimpressive Accuracy Results**: The highest accuracy achieved (80% on ABCD dataset) is only marginally better than the state of the art cited (60-85%). For a clinical diagnostic tool, this level of accuracy would likely be insufficient. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why these results are clinically meaningful, especially given the high stakes of MCI diagnosis.

3. **Insufficient Explanation of Enrichment Mechanism**: The paper claims that enriching networks with word embeddings "adds semantic information," but doesn't provide evidence of how this semantic information is being captured or why it's relevant for MCI detection. The results show CNE (enriched networks) outperforms CN (plain networks), but the paper doesn't explain why this enrichment specifically benefits MCI detection.

4. **Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent approaches to MCI detection, such as those using deep learning on raw speech audio or more sophisticated NLP techniques. For example, the paper cites Vincze et al. (2016) as achieving similar accuracy, but doesn't explain why their approach would be preferable to state-of-the-art deep learning methods for speech analysis.

5. **Misleading Claims in Results**: The paper claims "the topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics," but Table 4 shows that for the Cookie Theft dataset, linguistic metrics (LM) actually outperform complex networks (CN) in several cases (e.g., SVM-linear: LM 0.5694 vs CN 0.5250). This contradicts their main claim and undermines the paper's central contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your enriched network approach specifically capture the linguistic features that correlate with MCI, and how does this differ from traditional linguistic features?

2. Given the modest accuracy results (max 80%), what would be the clinical threshold for adoption of this method, and how would you address the limitations of your approach for clinical use?

3. Why did you choose the Bojanowski et al. (2016) word embedding method over more recent approaches like FastText or contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT), and how might these alternatives impact your results?

4. The paper states that "the best scenario for application of network features" was identified, but doesn't clearly explain what this scenario is or why it's optimal. Could you clarify this in the revision?

5. The paper mentions "a variety of features are required for this analysis," but doesn't sufficiently justify why the specific topological features were chosen. How were these ten measurements selected, and how do they relate to cognitive impairment markers?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound approach to MCI detection using complex networks with word embeddings. The evaluation across multiple datasets is a strength, and the practical advantage of reduced preprocessing is notable. However, the paper falls short in demonstrating clear novelty compared to existing NLP techniques, provides only marginally better results than prior work, and makes misleading claims about the superiority of their method. The authors need to substantially revise their claims to accurately reflect the contribution of this work, provide a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art approaches, and better justify their methodological choices. The clinical relevance of their results also requires stronger justification given the modest accuracy levels achieved.
