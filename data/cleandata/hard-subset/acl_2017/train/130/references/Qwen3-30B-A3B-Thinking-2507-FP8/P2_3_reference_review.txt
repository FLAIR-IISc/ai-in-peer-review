

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling them as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors address the challenge of analyzing speech transcripts with disfluencies and short text length, which hinders traditional linguistic feature extraction. They demonstrate that topological properties of these enriched networks can effectively distinguish between MCI and control participants, with improvements when combined with linguistic features.

The most significant contribution of this work is the application of complex network modeling to MCI detection from speech transcripts, with a specific focus on handling disfluencies and short text length. The second most significant contribution is the demonstration that network features enriched with word embeddings outperform traditional linguistic metrics, particularly in the context of disfluent speech. The third contribution is the comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) to validate the approach's robustness.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful clinical problem**: The paper tackles an important healthcare challenge - early detection of MCI, which can lead to timely interventions. The authors provide strong motivation for why language analysis is valuable for cognitive assessment, citing relevant clinical literature.

2. **Clear problem statement and solution**: The paper effectively identifies limitations in existing approaches (reliance on manual preprocessing, challenges with disfluencies, short text length) and proposes a well-motivated solution using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The technical approach is clearly explained with appropriate mathematical definitions and examples.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach across three distinct datasets (English and Portuguese), using multiple feature types (network, linguistic, BoW) and classifiers (SVM, RF, KNN, etc.). This thorough evaluation provides strong empirical evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

4. **Practical implications**: The paper demonstrates a path toward fully automated MCI detection without requiring human annotators, which aligns with the goal of developing clinically useful tools.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technical approach (enriching word adjacency networks with word embeddings) is directly based on Perozzi et al. (2014), which the authors cite but don't sufficiently differentiate from. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their specific implementation or application differs from prior work in a meaningful way.

2. **Small dataset sizes**: The datasets are quite small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 possible AD for Cookie Theft), which limits the statistical power of the results. The paper doesn't adequately justify these small sample sizes or discuss the implications for generalizability. For clinical applications, such small datasets are insufficient to establish reliable diagnostic tools.

3. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The authors use traditional machine learning classifiers (SVM, RF) but don't compare their approach to recent deep learning methods for text classification that have achieved superior results in other domains. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether their approach is competitive with current best practices in NLP.

4. **Overstated claims about automation**: The paper claims to "avoid a manual preprocessing step," but the description of their disfluency handling (removing filled pauses, short false starts) suggests significant automated preprocessing that isn't truly "automatic" in the sense of eliminating human input. This misrepresents the degree of automation achieved.

5. **Clinically insufficient accuracy**: The highest reported accuracy is 80% for the ABCD dataset, which is below the threshold typically required for clinical diagnosis (usually >90%). The paper doesn't adequately address this limitation or discuss how the approach might be improved to reach clinically useful accuracy levels.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent deep learning methods for text classification (e.g., BERT, LSTM) in terms of accuracy, computational requirements, and suitability for clinical deployment?

2. Could you provide more details about the dataset sizes and the rationale for using these specific sample sizes? Are there plans to expand the datasets to improve statistical power?

3. The paper claims to "avoid a manual preprocessing step," but the description of disfluency handling suggests significant automated preprocessing. Could you clarify how this differs from the manual preprocessing used in prior work and whether any human input is still required?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated approach to MCI detection from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The technical approach is sound, and the evaluation across multiple datasets is thorough. However, the limited novelty of the core technical approach, small dataset sizes, and lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods are significant weaknesses that prevent this paper from making a truly substantial contribution to the field. The accuracy levels reported are also below what would be clinically useful, which is a major limitation for the proposed application.

I recommend rejection, as the paper doesn't sufficiently advance the state of the art in either NLP or clinical MCI detection. The authors would need to address the limitations of small datasets, improve accuracy to clinically relevant levels, and provide stronger differentiation from prior work in complex network approaches to text analysis to warrant acceptance.
