

# ACL Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts by modeling narratives as complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a method to model speech transcripts as complex networks enriched with semantic information via word embeddings, (2) a demonstration that network features outperform traditional linguistic features for MCI detection, and (3) a multi-view learning approach that combines network features with linguistic and bag-of-words features to improve classification accuracy.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks**: The application of complex network modeling to MCI detection from speech transcripts is a novel contribution to the field. The authors provide a clear explanation of how to model speech transcripts as complex networks and enrich them with semantic information, which is a technically sound approach that could be valuable for other NLP tasks in healthcare.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Multiple Datasets**: The paper evaluates the approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) with different languages (English and Portuguese), which demonstrates the robustness of the method across different linguistic contexts and text types.

3. **Practical Contribution to Automated Assessment**: The authors successfully demonstrate a fully automated approach that doesn't require human annotation for preprocessing, which addresses a significant practical challenge in clinical assessment of MCI. The elimination of lemmatization (a common preprocessing step) is particularly relevant for handling disfluencies in speech transcripts.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Details Missing**: The paper lacks essential methodological details that are crucial for reproducibility. The paper states they "used the method proposed by Bojanowski et al. (2016) to generate word embeddings" but provides no details about the training data, vocabulary size, embedding dimensionality, or how out-of-vocabulary words were handled. The threshold for adding edges based on cosine similarity is mentioned but never specified. This omission makes the method impossible to replicate and undermines the paper's technical contribution.

2. **Incomplete Statistical Reporting**: The paper reports only mean classification accuracy without standard deviations or statistical significance tests. For example, Table 7 reports "80.18% accuracy" for CNE-LM-BoW on ABCD dataset but provides no indication of the variance or whether this result is statistically significant compared to other methods. The lack of statistical rigor makes it impossible to assess whether the reported improvements (e.g., "4.71% improvement") are meaningful.

3. **Inconsistent and Incomplete Results Reporting**: The paper makes several claims about results that cannot be verified due to incomplete reporting. For instance, it states "SVM with linear kernel in BoW feature space (75% of accuracy)" but doesn't specify which dataset this refers to, the exact accuracy value, or whether this is the mean or best result. The paper also claims "CNE-LM-BoW achieved the highest accuracy (80% of accuracy for narrative texts, a 4.71% of improvement compared to the best individual classifier)" but doesn't provide the accuracy of the "best individual classifier" for comparison.

4. **Lack of Justification for Key Methodological Choices**: The paper states they "eliminated lemmatization" based on "recent work" but fails to cite or explain this work. The paper also doesn't justify why they chose Bojanowski et al. (2016) over other word embedding methods, especially since the paper mentions that their method "provides some improvement in comparison with the traditional skip-gram model in syntactic evaluation (Mikolov et al., 2013b) but not for the semantic evaluation." This is a critical omission as it affects the validity of their approach.

5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies and typos that undermine its credibility. These include inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., "Aluı́sio et al.,? \Q2016\E" instead of "Aluísio et al., 2016"), inconsistent spacing (e.g., "15% a year" instead of "15% a year"), and missing words in figures (e.g., "the gain in information by enriching a co-occurrence network with seman- (a) (b)"). These errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading, which is particularly problematic for a high-quality venue like ACL.

## Questions to Authors

1. What specific threshold value did you use for adding edges based on cosine similarity between word vectors? How was this threshold optimized?

2. The paper claims SVM with linear kernel in BoW achieved "75% of accuracy" on the ABCD dataset, but Table 7 shows 75.47% for BoW. Please clarify which result is accurate and provide the standard deviation for this result.

3. The paper states "the threshold parameter was optimized with best values being 0.7 in Cookie Theft dataset and 0.4 in both Cinderella and ABCD dataset." Please clarify what this threshold parameter refers to, as it's not mentioned in the methodology section.

4. Why did you choose Bojanowski et al. (2016) for word embeddings when the authors state "it provides some improvement in comparison with the traditional skip-gram model in syntactic evaluation but not for the semantic evaluation"? How did this choice specifically benefit your task?

5. The paper mentions "the Control group has an average of 9.58 sentences per narrative" for Cookie Theft dataset. Is this the average number of sentences per narrative, or the total number of sentences across all narratives? This is a critical point for understanding the text size.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a potentially interesting application of complex networks to MCI detection, the significant methodological gaps, incomplete reporting of results, and numerous formatting errors make it impossible to evaluate the technical merits of the work. The lack of essential details for reproducibility and the absence of statistical significance testing for the claimed improvements are particularly concerning. In a venue like ACL, where methodological rigor and reproducibility are paramount, these issues are fundamental flaws that cannot be overlooked.
