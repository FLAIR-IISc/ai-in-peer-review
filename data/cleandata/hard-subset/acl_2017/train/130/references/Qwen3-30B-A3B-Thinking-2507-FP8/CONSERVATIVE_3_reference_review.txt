

# ACL Review: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

## Summary
This paper proposes applying complex networks (specifically, word adjacency networks enriched with word embeddings) to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors claim this approach outperforms traditional linguistic features for classifying MCI patients from healthy controls. They evaluate their method on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) using four classifiers and various feature combinations.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of complex network modeling to MCI detection from speech transcripts (modest contribution, as this is a specific application of a known technique)
2. Enriching complex networks with word embeddings for this specific domain (modest contribution, as this is a known technique applied to a new domain)
3. Demonstrating that combining features through multi-view learning improves accuracy (minor contribution, as this is a common technique)

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important clinical problem (early detection of MCI) with a potentially useful NLP approach. Early detection of MCI is clinically significant, and the paper correctly identifies the importance of this research area.

2. The authors provide three different datasets from different languages (English and Portuguese), which demonstrates some cross-lingual applicability of their approach, though this is not thoroughly evaluated.

3. The paper clearly describes the methodology and provides supplementary materials with examples of transcripts and feature definitions, which aids reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient evidence for superiority**: The core technique (complex networks enriched with word embeddings) is not novel - it builds directly on Perozzi et al. (2014) and Amancio et al. (2012). The paper fails to provide strong evidence that this approach is fundamentally better than established methods for MCI detection. The highest accuracy reported (80% on ABCD dataset) is only marginally better than traditional linguistic features (72.5% with SVM-RBF), and the paper doesn't compare against modern deep learning approaches that could handle disfluencies more effectively. The claim that "topological properties of CN outperform traditionally linguistic metrics" is not convincingly supported, as the improvements are small (typically 1-5% at most).

2. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper cites "accuracies ranging from 60% to 85%" but doesn't provide a meaningful comparison to recent SOTA methods in MCI detection. For example, Vincze et al. (2016) reported 78.5% accuracy using linguistic features, which is comparable to the authors' best result (80%). The paper doesn't explain why their approach would be preferred over existing methods with similar or better performance.

3. **Insufficient justification for handling disfluencies**: The authors claim traditional linguistic features "depend on grammatical texts" and that their approach "solves the problem of disfluencies," but they don't provide evidence to support this. They don't compare their method to one that uses lemmatization or other standard techniques for handling disfluencies. The paper states that "lemmatization introduces undesirable noises," but doesn't validate this claim with experimental results. The preprocessing steps they use (removing filled pauses, short false starts, and repetition) are similar to what others have done, yet they claim their method handles disfluencies better without evidence.

4. **Small datasets and lack of statistical significance**: The datasets are relatively small (e.g., 43 MCI and 21 AD transcripts in Cookie Theft), which limits the statistical power of the results. The paper doesn't report statistical significance testing for the improvements they claim, making it difficult to assess whether the observed differences are meaningful. For example, the 10% improvement on the Cinderella dataset (from 65% to 75%) is presented as significant without proper statistical validation.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't perform thorough ablation studies to understand which components of their approach contribute most to the performance. They don't explore how different thresholds for word embedding similarity affect results, how the network size affects classification, or why certain topological metrics are more informative than others.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that complex networks enriched with embeddings "outperform traditionally linguistic metrics," but the accuracy improvements are marginal (typically 1-5%). Can the authors provide a statistical analysis showing these improvements are significant? Without this, it's difficult to justify the complexity of their approach over simpler alternatives.

2. The authors state that lemmatization "introduces undesirable noises," but they don't provide evidence that their approach handles disfluencies better than methods that use lemmatization. Can they provide a comparison between their method and a baseline that uses lemmatization?

3. The paper mentions that "the classification of small texts using networks can be impaired," yet they apply this approach to transcripts that are relatively short (e.g., Cookie Theft has an average of 9.58 sentences per narrative). How did they address this specific challenge, and what evidence do they have that their enriched network approach overcomes this limitation?

## Additional Comments
The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. A more thorough comparison to recent SOTA methods in MCI detection
2. Statistical significance testing for all reported improvements
3. Ablation studies to identify which components of their approach contribute most to performance
4. A clearer explanation of why their approach is better suited for disfluent speech than existing methods
5. More discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly given the small dataset sizes

While the clinical problem addressed is important, the paper fails to provide compelling evidence that their specific methodological approach offers a meaningful advancement over existing techniques for MCI detection. The improvements claimed are marginal, and the paper doesn't adequately justify why their approach should be preferred over simpler, well-established methods.
