

# Review of "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts using complex networks enriched with word embeddings. The authors model speech transcripts as word adjacency networks and enhance them with semantic information from word embeddings by adding edges between words with high cosine similarity. The paper evaluates this approach on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) and compares the performance against linguistic features and bag-of-words representations.

**Main Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **Novel application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection from speech transcripts:** The paper applies a technique (complex networks enriched with word embeddings) that has been used in other NLP domains to the specific medical domain of MCI detection, demonstrating its viability for this application.

2. **Demonstration that enriched network features outperform traditional linguistic features:** The authors empirically show that their enriched network features (CNE) achieve better or comparable accuracy to linguistic features across multiple datasets, with improvements of up to 10% in some cases.

3. **Fully automated pipeline without manual preprocessing:** The approach avoids the need for manual revision of transcripts (which was required in previous work), making it more practical for real-world implementation.

## Strengths

1. **Clear technical contribution to a new application domain:** The paper successfully applies complex network analysis to a medical domain (MCI detection) where it has not been previously explored. The authors provide a compelling rationale for why this approach is suitable for handling the disfluencies and short text nature of neuropsychological assessments, which is a significant strength.

2. **Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets:** The paper evaluates the approach on three different datasets (English and Portuguese) with different characteristics, demonstrating consistent performance improvements over linguistic features. The results show that enriched complex networks (CNE) outperform standard complex networks (CN) and traditional linguistic features (LM) across all datasets, with the most significant improvements on the Cinderella dataset (65% vs 47.5% for CNE vs LM).

3. **Practical contribution to fully automated MCI detection:** The paper successfully demonstrates that their approach can be implemented without manual preprocessing steps (unlike prior work requiring manual revision of transcripts to improve parsing). This addresses a key practical limitation in the field and makes the method more scalable for clinical use.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technical approach:** While the application to MCI detection is novel, the core technique (complex networks enriched with word embeddings) is not new. The paper builds directly on Perozzi et al. (2014), which introduced this method for word networks. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from this prior work in terms of technical innovation, making the novelty somewhat incremental.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods:** The paper compares against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words, but fails to compare against more recent deep learning approaches that have been applied to MCI detection (e.g., transformer-based models, which have shown promise in other NLP tasks). The paper mentions that "the accuracies found here are comparable to the values reported by other authors," but doesn't demonstrate how the proposed method compares to the current state of the art in MCI detection.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of enrichment:** The paper claims that "CNE is better than using only complex networks" but doesn't provide ablation studies to quantify the specific contribution of the word embedding enrichment step. Without these, it's unclear whether the enrichment is truly necessary or if the standard co-occurrence network would have performed similarly.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that CNE outperforms CN across all datasets, but doesn't provide ablation results showing the specific contribution of the word embedding enrichment. Could the authors provide these results to demonstrate the value of the enrichment step?

2. The paper states that "the accuracy in classification is given in Tables 4, 5, 6, and 7" but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why performance varies across datasets. Could the authors explain the performance differences between the English Cookie Theft dataset and the Portuguese Cinderella/ABCD datasets?

3. The paper claims that their approach "does not require the input of a human annotator" but relies on automated disfluency removal and sentence segmentation. How does the quality of these automated preprocessing steps compare to manual annotation, and what is the error rate of these automated steps?
