## Review for ACL Submission: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex network representations, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare the performance of network-based features against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, across three datasets (English and Portuguese). They demonstrate that enriched complex networks perform competitively, and often outperform, other approaches, and that combining features leads to the best results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to a clinically relevant NLP task:** The novel application of complex network analysis, specifically enriched with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech is a valuable contribution. This explores a different representational approach than typical feature engineering in this domain.
2. **Cross-Lingual Evaluation:** The evaluation on both English and Portuguese datasets is a strength, demonstrating a degree of generalizability (although further investigation into language-specific performance is warranted).
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The thorough evaluation of multiple feature types, classifiers, and combination strategies provides a robust assessment of the proposed method's efficacy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clinical Relevance:** The introduction clearly articulates the importance of early MCI detection and the potential of language analysis for this purpose. This anchors the work in a significant clinical problem.
2. **Sound Methodology:**  The methodology is well-defined, with clear explanations of network construction, enrichment with embeddings, feature extraction, and classification procedures. The choice of topological measures is justified, and the preprocessing steps are described (though see weaknesses).
3. **Competitive Performance:** The results consistently show that the proposed method achieves competitive performance, often outperforming traditional approaches, especially when combined with other feature sets. The accuracy improvements, while not dramatic, are encouraging.
4. **Detailed Dataset Descriptions:** The paper provides detailed descriptions of the datasets used, including demographic information and data collection procedures.  This is essential for reproducibility and allows for a clear understanding of the context of the results.
5. **Exploration of Feature Combinations:** The systematic exploration of different feature combinations using multi-view learning is a positive aspect, highlighting the benefits of integrating different types of linguistic information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Network Approach:** While applying complex networks to this *specific* problem is novel, the underlying network construction and embedding enrichment techniques are well-established in NLP. The paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new network representation or embedding method, relying on existing approaches.  The contribution lies more in the application than in a methodological breakthrough.
2. **Preprocessing Concerns and Justification:** The decision to *not* lemmatize is justified, but the removal of only certain disfluencies (e.g., "uh", "um") while leaving others ("revisions, elaborations, paraphasias, comments") is questionable. These remaining disfluencies likely introduce noise, potentially impacting the network structure and analysis, especially in clinical speech.  The impact of this choice should be discussed more thoroughly. The justification for keeping these disfluencies ("to avoid a manual preprocessing step") feels weak given the potential impact on accuracy.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Reporting:** While the cross-validation approach is sound, the paper lacks detailed information about hyperparameter tuning.  The use of a fixed threshold (0.7 or 0.4) across datasets feels arbitrary. A more systematic hyperparameter search and reporting would strengthen the results.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on overall accuracy. A more detailed error analysis – identifying common misclassifications and potential reasons for these errors – would provide valuable insights and highlight areas for future improvement. What types of errors is the network-based approach *specifically* making?
5. **Dataset Imbalance:** Some of the datasets exhibit relatively small sample sizes and potential imbalances between control and MCI groups. This could bias the results, and the authors should discuss the potential impact of these imbalances and consider techniques to mitigate them (e.g., stratified cross-validation, oversampling).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind keeping certain types of disfluencies in the transcripts while removing others?  Have you explored the impact of removing these remaining disfluencies on the performance of your method?
2.  What was the process for selecting the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings?  How sensitive are the results to this threshold?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of misclassifications made by your method?  Are there any patterns in the errors that suggest areas for improvement?
4.  Given the varying dataset sizes and potential imbalances, how robust are your findings across different datasets?  Would you expect the same performance gains with larger, more balanced datasets?
5.  Can you discuss the computational cost of constructing and analyzing the complex networks, particularly for longer transcripts? Is this approach scalable to larger datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, although some references are missing full details.
*   The supplementary material is helpful for providing additional details on the datasets and features.
*   The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but could benefit from discussing more recent advances in network-based NLP techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of MCI detection using NLP. The application of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, is a promising approach. While the novelty of the network approach itself is limited, the comprehensive evaluation, cross-lingual analysis, and demonstration of competitive performance are significant strengths. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding preprocessing and hyperparameter tuning, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
