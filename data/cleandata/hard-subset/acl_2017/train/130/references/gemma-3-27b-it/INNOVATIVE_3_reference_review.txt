This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent language not just as a sequence of words, but as a network of interconnected concepts, capturing semantic relationships that might be indicative of cognitive decline. The authors combine this network-based approach with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, evaluating performance on English and Portuguese datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of a network-based approach, *specifically enriching word co-occurrence networks with word embeddings*, for MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been used in NLP, and word embeddings are commonplace, the combination in the context of neuropsychological assessment—and the attempt to bypass extensive pre-processing—is a noteworthy direction. A secondary contribution is the application of this method to both English and Portuguese datasets, providing some cross-linguistic validation. Finally, the fairly comprehensive comparison of different feature sets and classifiers is useful, offering insights into what combinations perform best.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea of enriching word adjacency networks with embeddings to capture semantic information in a relatively raw transcript is promising. The goal of reducing the need for extensive manual pre-processing of often noisy neuropsychological transcripts is a practical and valuable one. This avoids the pitfalls of reliance on high-quality parsers for languages like Portuguese where such tools are less mature.
2. **Cross-Lingual Evaluation:** The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets strengthens the findings. While the datasets are different in size and elicitation method, demonstrating some transferability of the approach is positive.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering and Classification:** The authors don't rely solely on the network-based features but thoughtfully combine them with established linguistic features and bag-of-words, testing multiple classifiers. This allows for a nuanced understanding of feature importance and classifier performance. The multi-view learning approach is also a strong point.
4. **Reasonable Results:** The reported accuracies, while not state-of-the-art, are competitive with existing literature and demonstrate that the proposed method is viable. Improvements through ensemble methods further solidify this.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the authors compare different feature combinations, a more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of *only* the word embedding enrichment within the network is lacking. How much of the performance gain is due to the network structure itself versus the added semantic information? This is crucial for understanding the core contribution.
2. **Dataset Heterogeneity & Size:** The datasets vary considerably in size, elicitation method (picture description vs. narrative retelling), and data quality (manual vs. automatic segmentation). This makes direct comparison challenging and raises concerns about generalization. The relatively small sample sizes (especially in the Portuguese datasets) limit statistical power.
3. **Lack of Clinical Significance Discussion:** The paper focuses primarily on accuracy. It doesn’t discuss the *clinical significance* of the achieved accuracy gains. What error types are being made? What are the implications of false positives and false negatives in a real-world clinical setting? This is a vital consideration for a study aiming to aid in early diagnosis.
4. **Limited Discussion of Network Parameters:** The paper lacks a detailed justification for the chosen network construction parameters (e.g., cosine similarity threshold for adding edges). A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of these parameters on performance would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study focusing specifically on the impact of the word embedding enrichment step on the performance of the complex network? For example, what is the accuracy using only the co-occurrence network *without* the embeddings?
2. Given the differences in datasets (size, elicitation method, segmentation quality), how confident are you that the observed improvements generalize beyond the specific datasets used in this study?
3. Can you provide a confusion matrix analysis for each dataset, outlining the types of errors the model is making? How might these errors impact clinical decision-making?
4. What sensitivity analysis was performed for the cosine similarity threshold used when enriching the network with word embeddings? How was this threshold determined?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are extensive. The supplementary material is a good addition. The paper is generally well-written, though some sentences are a bit convoluted. The formatting of tables could be improved for readability. Overall, while not perfect, the novelty of the approach and potential for reducing pre-processing requirements make this a worthwhile contribution. I lean towards acceptance, recognizing the potential of this work, and am willing to overlook the limitations given the exploratory nature of the study.
