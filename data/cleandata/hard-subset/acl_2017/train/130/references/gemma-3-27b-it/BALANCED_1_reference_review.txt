This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent narratives as networks where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, enhanced by semantic similarity based on word embeddings. The authors compare the performance of this network-based approach with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, across datasets in both English and Portuguese.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Complex Networks with Word Embeddings to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application of complex network analysis, specifically enriched with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their use in this specific clinical context, and combined with word embeddings for enhanced semantic representation, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Cross-Lingual Evaluation:** The study’s evaluation across both English and Portuguese datasets strengthens its findings and demonstrates the potential generalizability of the approach. The use of different datasets with varying characteristics (e.g., manually transcribed vs. ASR-segmented) provides a more robust assessment of the method.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:** The thorough comparison against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words models provides a clear understanding of the relative strengths of the proposed method. The exploration of feature combinations (multi-view learning) further enhances the analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The methodology is well-defined, starting with clear explanations of network construction, word embedding integration, and feature extraction. The use of established network metrics and machine learning algorithms adds to the rigor of the work.
2. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, including multiple datasets, cross-validation, and a variety of classifiers.  The inclusion of both manually processed and automatically segmented transcripts is a notable strength.  The clear presentation of results in tables facilitates comparison.
3. **Practical Relevance:**  The problem addressed – early detection of MCI – has significant clinical relevance.  The aim to develop a fully automated system is a practical and valuable goal.
4. **Addressing Challenges in Speech Transcripts:** The authors explicitly acknowledge and attempt to address the challenges of working with speech transcripts, such as disfluencies and noise, by carefully choosing preprocessing steps (or omitting them, as in the case of lemmatization).
5. **Good Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of related work in MCI detection, language analysis, and complex networks. The discussion of existing approaches helps to contextualize the authors’ contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Approach:** While the *application* to MCI is novel, the core complex network approach itself (word co-occurrence networks) is relatively established in NLP. The authors don’t sufficiently highlight what is truly *new* about their network construction beyond the word embedding enrichment. A more detailed discussion of why this particular network structure is well-suited for capturing MCI-related language changes would be beneficial.
2. **Word Embedding Details:** The paper mentions using word embeddings generated by Bojanowski et al. (2016), but lacks details about the specific training corpus and parameters used. Providing this information would improve reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of potential biases introduced by the embeddings.
3. **Statistical Significance:** While accuracy results are presented, the statistical significance of the improvements achieved by the proposed method (particularly in the multi-view learning scenarios) is not explicitly reported. Performing statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Interpretability:** While the method achieves promising accuracy, it lacks interpretability. It would be valuable to investigate *which* network metrics or linguistic features are most predictive of MCI, providing insights into the language characteristics associated with cognitive decline.  This could involve feature importance analysis or qualitative analysis of representative cases.
5. **Dataset Imbalance:** The datasets exhibit some imbalance in the number of control and MCI participants. While not a major flaw, it's important to acknowledge this limitation and potentially explore techniques for addressing it (e.g., oversampling, weighted loss functions).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing word co-occurrence networks specifically for this task? What linguistic properties related to MCI are you hoping to capture with this network structure?
2. Please provide more details about the training corpus and parameters used to generate the word embeddings.
3. Have you performed any statistical significance tests to assess the improvements achieved by your method compared to the baseline approaches? If so, please report the results.
4. Can you provide any insights into which network metrics or linguistic features are most predictive of MCI?
5. Did you consider alternative approaches to handle dataset imbalance, and if so, why were they not implemented?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 could benefit from a clearer explanation of the dotted edges representing semantic similarity links.
*   The Supplementary Material section is helpful and provides valuable details about the datasets and feature extraction process.
*   Consider discussing potential limitations of the study, such as the generalizability of the findings to different populations or languages.



Overall, this is a well-executed study that presents a promising approach to MCI detection using complex networks and word embeddings. While the core network methodology is not entirely novel, the application to this specific clinical problem, the cross-lingual evaluation, and the thorough feature comparison make it a valuable contribution. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
