This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent transcripts as networks where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, augmented with edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors compare performance to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, using several machine learning classifiers.

The primary contribution is the exploration of complex network-based features, enhanced with word embeddings, as a viable approach for MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been applied in NLP, this is a novel application to neuropsychological assessment and specifically, MCI detection. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese, varying narrative lengths and automatic vs. manual segmentation) and feature combinations. Finally, the investigation and finding that enriched networks outperform traditional complex networks, and offer competitive performance compared to linguistic features, is valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Applying complex network analysis with word embeddings to the problem of MCI detection from speech is a novel contribution. Traditional approaches rely heavily on hand-crafted linguistic features or statistical methods, and this work provides an alternative representation potentially less susceptible to issues with disfluencies and the need for extensive manual pre-processing.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, varying in language (English and Portuguese) and data acquisition methods (manual vs. automatic segmentation). This rigorous evaluation strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The comparison against a baseline of linguistic features and BoW is also well-motivated.
3. **Clear Demonstration of Improvement with Embeddings:** The paper clearly demonstrates that enriching the complex network with word embeddings leads to better performance than using the raw co-occurrence network alone. This indicates the value of incorporating semantic information into the network representation.
4. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Comparing the proposed method with existing linguistic features provides a solid baseline and allows the authors to highlight the potential advantages of their approach. The comparable performance suggests this is a worthwhile direction for future research.
5. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The authors provide sufficient details about the datasets, features, and classification algorithms used, making the study reproducible. The use of cross-validation is also appropriate.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Topology Choice:** While the paper lists ten topological metrics, it lacks a strong justification for *why* these specific metrics were chosen and what aspects of cognitive impairment they are expected to capture. A more theoretical grounding for the network features would strengthen the work.
2. **Overly Detailed Dataset Descriptions:** The extensive detail in the dataset descriptions (e.g., specific test administration procedures) feels somewhat tangential to the core contribution of the paper. While providing context is important, this level of detail could be streamlined.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents accuracy scores but does not delve into *what* types of errors the classifiers are making. Error analysis could reveal patterns and suggest improvements to the feature set or classification algorithms. For example, are certain types of disfluencies causing problems? Are specific linguistic features more predictive than others?
4. **Sensitivity to Parameter Tuning:** The threshold parameter optimization (0.7 for Cookie Theft, 0.4 for others) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification. While parameter tuning is necessary, a more systematic approach or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
5. **Limited Discussion of Clinical Significance:** The reported accuracy gains, while statistically significant, may not be clinically meaningful. A discussion of the practical implications of these improvements for early MCI detection would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale for selecting the specific network topological metrics used? What linguistic or cognitive impairments are these metrics intended to capture?
2. The threshold parameter for the classifiers differs across datasets. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how these thresholds were determined and whether a more unified approach is possible?
3. Did you perform any error analysis to understand the types of misclassifications made by the models? If so, what were the key findings?
4. Could you discuss the clinical significance of the observed accuracy improvements? What would be the practical impact of using this approach in a real-world clinical setting?
5. You mention the difficulty of obtaining robust dependency parsers for Portuguese. Has the performance of your method been compared to methods relying on dependency parsing *after* manual correction of parsing errors? Would the results be substantially different?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful for providing detailed information about the datasets and features.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections could be streamlined for brevity.
* The reference list is comprehensive and well-formatted.
