This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose modeling narratives as networks and evaluating their utility for classification, both independently and in combination with traditional linguistic features. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of complex network analysis – a technique reasonably well-established in other NLP areas – to this specific clinical task. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of feature combination strategies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Application Area:** The detection of MCI is a crucial medical challenge, and automated tools have the potential to significantly impact early diagnosis and intervention. The focus on analyzing speech, a readily available and relatively non-invasive data source, is a sensible approach.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Exploration:** The authors investigate a reasonable range of features, including network-based measures, traditional linguistic metrics, and bag-of-words representations. The attempt to combine these different feature types is commendable.
3. **Multiple Datasets:** The use of multiple datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella story, and ABCD battery) representing different languages (English and Portuguese) and elicitation methods strengthens the generalizability of the findings, although the dataset sizes remain a concern (see weaknesses).
4. **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup, employing 5-fold cross-validation and established machine learning algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF), is methodologically sound and provides a reasonable basis for comparison. The use of ensemble methods is also a good practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While applying complex networks to this domain isn’t *incorrect*, the core idea lacks substantial novelty. Complex networks have been applied to text analysis for years, and the paper doesn't present a fundamentally new network construction or analysis technique. The enrichment with word embeddings, while a common practice, is also not a breakthrough innovation.
2. **Small Dataset Sizes:** The datasets used are relatively small, especially for the complexity of the models being evaluated. This raises concerns about the statistical power of the results and the potential for overfitting. The reported accuracy improvements, while present, might not be robust given the sample sizes. The differing sizes across datasets exacerbate this issue.
3. **Baseline Comparison Insufficiency:** The comparisons to baseline methods, while present, aren't sufficiently rigorous. The paper doesn’t thoroughly justify why *these* specific linguistic features were chosen, nor does it perform a comprehensive ablation study to fully understand the contribution of each feature set. A comparison against simpler baselines (e.g., logistic regression with basic word counts) is also missing, making it difficult to assess the true added value of the proposed approach.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails and *why* would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Are there specific linguistic patterns or types of errors that the model struggles with?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale for selecting the specific linguistic features used from Coh-Metrix and Coh-Metrix-Dementia? Were any feature selection techniques employed?
2. Given the small dataset sizes, have you performed any regularization techniques or considered Bayesian methods to mitigate the risk of overfitting?
3. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of misclassifications and potential reasons for these errors?
4. Have you considered performing statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the observed accuracy improvements?



**Additional Comments:**

The description of the datasets is somewhat verbose and could be more concise. The inclusion of sample transcripts in the supplementary material is helpful. The formatting of the tables is slightly inconsistent. The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the study.
