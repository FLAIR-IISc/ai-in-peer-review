This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors claim novelty in applying this network-based approach to neuropsychological assessment data, and demonstrate improvements in classification accuracy when combining network features with traditional linguistic and statistical features. I identify one primary contribution: the exploration of a network-based approach, incorporating word embeddings, for analyzing speech data related to MCI detection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Exploration of a Novel Feature Representation:** The application of complex networks to speech transcript analysis for MCI detection is a relatively unexplored area. The authors’ attempt to capture semantic relationships through word embeddings within this network framework is potentially valuable.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD), encompassing both English and Portuguese, increasing the generalizability of their findings. This is a strong point, especially given the challenges of language-specific NLP tools.
3. **Combination of Features:** The demonstrated improvement in performance through the combination of network features with traditional linguistic and statistical features suggests a complementary relationship between these approaches. This highlights the potential for hybrid systems in MCI detection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Baseline Comparison Rigor:** While the authors compare their method against linguistic features and BoW, the baseline SVM implementations lack crucial details. Specifically, the kernel and hyperparameters are not consistently reported across datasets. A more systematic comparison against state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for MCI detection from speech, including those utilizing deep learning approaches (e.g., recurrent neural networks, transformers), is missing. The comparison is superficial and doesn’t establish a clear advantage of the proposed method.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Parameter Choices:** The choice of a cosine similarity threshold for enriching the networks is not sufficiently justified. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of different threshold values on performance is crucial. Similarly, the decision to *not* use lemmatization, while explained, lacks empirical validation. Showing the effect of lemmatization on network performance would strengthen the argument.
3. **Discrepancies in Data Preprocessing & Reporting:** The preprocessing steps applied to each dataset vary. The Cinderella dataset has two versions – one with manual revision and one without. The impact of manual revision on performance is not rigorously analyzed. The varying sentence lengths across datasets, and the implications for network construction, are not adequately addressed. There are inconsistencies in the reporting of demographic information (e.g., sample sizes changing between tables).
4. **Overstated Claims of Improvement:** The improvements in accuracy, while reported, are often marginal. Claims of significant improvements (e.g., “a 10% of improvement compared to the best individual classifier” in the Cinderella dataset) need to be carefully contextualized, considering the relatively small dataset sizes and the limited scope of baseline comparisons. The statistical significance of the reported improvements is not discussed.
5. **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing is often verbose and repetitive. The figures are not clearly explained within the text. The supplementary material, while helpful, could be better organized. Some of the tables are difficult to read due to formatting issues.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your method against state-of-the-art techniques for MCI detection from speech, including deep learning-based approaches?
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing the specific cosine similarity threshold used for network enrichment, and have you performed a sensitivity analysis to evaluate its impact on performance?
3.  Can you provide a detailed explanation for the varying preprocessing steps applied to each dataset and justify their consistency (or inconsistency)?
4.  Please clarify the statistical significance of the reported improvements in classification accuracy.
5.  Could you provide the specific hyperparameters used for the SVM baselines across all datasets, and justify their selection?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the study, particularly concerning the generalizability of the findings, is warranted.
*   Consider presenting a visual representation of the network structures generated for both healthy controls and MCI patients to illustrate any observable differences.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting exploration of a novel approach, the lack of rigorous baseline comparisons, insufficient justification for parameter choices, inconsistencies in data handling, and overstated claims of improvement raise significant concerns. The contributions, while present, are incremental rather than groundbreaking. Therefore, I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above and conduct a more thorough evaluation of their method before resubmission.
