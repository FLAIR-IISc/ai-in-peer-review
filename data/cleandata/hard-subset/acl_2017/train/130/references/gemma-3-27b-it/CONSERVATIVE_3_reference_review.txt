This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors investigate combining these network-based features with traditional linguistic and statistical features. The primary contribution is an attempt to apply a network-based approach, previously successful in other NLP tasks, to the specific problem of MCI detection. A secondary contribution is the exploration of feature combination strategies, aiming to improve classification accuracy. Finally, the application of this method to both English and Portuguese datasets provides a moderate contribution in terms of cross-lingual validation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Exploration of a Novel Approach:** The use of complex networks to represent speech transcripts for MCI detection is a reasonable avenue to explore, particularly given the challenges of traditional linguistic feature engineering with noisy, disfluent speech. This offers a potentially robust alternative to methods heavily reliant on parsing or manually cleaned data.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation on three datasets – two in English and one in Portuguese – strengthens the findings, demonstrating some degree of generalizability across languages and data collection methods. This is a positive aspect, particularly given the scarcity of publicly available datasets in this domain.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors thoughtfully incorporate a variety of features—network topology, linguistic metrics, and bag-of-words—and systematically evaluate their combinations. This thoroughness is commendable and provides a clear picture of the relative contributions of each feature type.
4. **Comparable Performance:** The reported accuracies are competitive with existing literature, suggesting that the proposed method is a viable approach for MCI detection. While not state-of-the-art, achieving comparable results with a relatively unexplored technique is a positive indicator.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While applying complex networks to this *specific* problem is a new application, the underlying methodology – word co-occurrence networks enriched with word embeddings – is not novel. The authors cite prior work demonstrating this technique’s efficacy in other NLP domains, suggesting a lack of significant methodological innovation. The core idea feels more like an adaptation than a breakthrough.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison against strong, established baselines. While some linguistic features are included, a more direct comparison with simpler machine learning models trained *solely* on these features (e.g., a well-tuned logistic regression or random forest) is crucial to demonstrate the added value of the complex network approach. Simply achieving comparable accuracy isn’t sufficient; the network-based approach should demonstrably outperform simpler methods to justify its complexity.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The authors combine multiple feature sets, but there’s no clear ablation study to determine the *specific* contribution of the network features. How much does performance degrade when the network features are removed? This is essential for understanding whether the added complexity is truly beneficial. The observed improvements in the combined models could simply be driven by the linguistic features.
4. **Data Quality Concerns & Limited Statistical Power:** The dataset sizes are relatively small, particularly for the Portuguese datasets, raising concerns about statistical power. The varying degrees of automated vs. manual transcription also introduce potential biases. The impact of these data quality issues on the results isn't thoroughly discussed.
5. **Overly Complex Experimental Setup:** The sheer number of classifiers and feature combinations makes it difficult to draw clear conclusions. A more focused experimental design with fewer, well-justified choices would improve clarity and interpretability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the complex network approach versus simpler baseline models (e.g., logistic regression with linguistic features)?
2.  Please present the results of an ablation study, specifically showing the performance of models trained *without* the complex network features.
3.  Given the small dataset sizes, have you considered using techniques like bootstrapping or cross-validation to estimate the statistical significance of your findings?
4.  Can you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when applying the method to the Portuguese datasets, and how these challenges might have affected the results?
5. How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding model and cosine similarity threshold used for enriching the network?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations of the technical concepts. The inclusion of supplementary material is helpful. The reference list is adequate.
