## Review for ACL

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex network features, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts as networks, leveraging both word co-occurrence and semantic similarity (via word embeddings) to capture linguistic information. They experiment with multiple datasets (English and Portuguese), various machine learning algorithms, and feature combinations (network features, linguistic features, bag-of-words) to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is exploring the use of complex network analysis – a method less common in this domain – for identifying MCI from spoken language. This provides a potentially complementary approach to existing methods relying on traditional linguistic features.
2. **Demonstration of Benefit from Embedding Enrichment:** The paper shows that enriching the co-occurrence networks with word embeddings improves classification performance, suggesting the semantic information captured by embeddings is valuable in this task.
3. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:**  Evaluating the method on both English and Portuguese datasets, including publicly available and custom datasets, provides a more robust assessment of its generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  While complex networks have been used in NLP, applying them specifically to MCI detection with the addition of word embeddings is a relatively novel idea. The authors successfully adapt a network-based approach to a challenging clinical problem.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors demonstrate a thorough experimental design, using several datasets, machine learning algorithms, and feature combinations. This allows for a robust evaluation of their method’s performance and provides valuable insights into the impact of different features.
3. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The tables presenting the results are clear and well-organized, making it easy to compare the performance of different configurations. The inclusion of results across diverse datasets strengthens the findings.
4. **Practical Focus:** The work explicitly aims for a fully automated system, which is a valuable goal in clinical applications. Removing the need for manual preprocessing, as they highlight, is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, the individual components (complex networks, word embeddings, machine learning classifiers) are well-established.  The paper doesn't present fundamentally new algorithms for any of these components. The key innovation is the application to this specific task and the combination.
2. **Performance Gains are Moderate:** The performance improvements achieved by the proposed method, while statistically significant in some cases, are not dramatically higher than those obtained with traditional linguistic features. This raises the question of whether the added complexity of the network approach is justified by the performance gain.  The accuracy range is within those reported in existing literature, which doesn't position this as a significant leap forward.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the errors made by the classifier. Understanding *which* types of errors are being made (e.g., misclassifying specific linguistic patterns) could provide valuable insights for improving the method.
4. **Word Embedding Details:** The paper mentions using a specific word embedding model (Bojanowski et al., 2016) but doesn’t discuss the rationale for choosing this particular model over others, nor how the embedding dimensions were determined. A brief justification would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis? Specifically, what types of transcripts are frequently misclassified, and what linguistic characteristics seem to be associated with these errors?
2.  What is the computational cost associated with building and analyzing these complex networks, especially for larger transcripts? Is this a practical consideration for real-time deployment?
3.  You mention the lack of robust dependency parsers for Portuguese as a motivation for using complex networks. Could you quantify the improvement in performance gained by avoiding manual preprocessing with dependency parsing? How does performance compare to using less sophisticated parsing methods?
4.  What is the impact of the cosine similarity threshold used for adding edges based on word embeddings? Did you explore a range of thresholds, and how did different values affect performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful for understanding the datasets and linguistic features used.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed study. The application of complex networks to MCI detection is a worthwhile exploration, and the results are promising. However, the limited novelty of the individual components and the moderate performance gains prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance. I believe the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of its limitations and a more thorough error analysis. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** contingent on addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
