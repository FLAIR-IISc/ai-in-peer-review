This paper investigates the application of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to model language as a network where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, augmented with semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors compare this network-based approach to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese).

The primary contribution is the exploration of complex networks as a feature representation for MCI detection from speech, particularly the enrichment with word embeddings to address limitations of short transcripts. A secondary contribution lies in the comparative analysis of this approach against established linguistic and statistical features. Finally, the application to both English and Portuguese datasets provides some degree of cross-lingual validation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Network Approach:** While complex networks have been applied in NLP, their application to MCI detection from speech transcripts, coupled with word embedding enrichment, is a relatively unexplored avenue. The premise of capturing semantic relationships within the network structure is potentially insightful for understanding language degradation in cognitive impairment.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets – two in English and one in Portuguese – which adds some robustness to the findings. This is particularly valuable given the language-specific challenges in NLP and the need for cross-lingual applicability.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:** The paper systematically compares the performance of complex networks (with and without embeddings) against traditional features. This allows for a fair assessment of the relative benefits and drawbacks of the proposed method. The inclusion of both linguistic and bag-of-words features provides a solid baseline.
4. **Attention to Data Preprocessing:** The authors acknowledge the challenges of working with spontaneous speech transcripts (disfluencies, errors) and attempt to address these through appropriate preprocessing strategies. This demonstrates an understanding of the practical difficulties of applying NLP techniques to real-world clinical data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** While the paper presents accuracy scores, it lacks rigorous statistical testing to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. Simply reporting accuracy numbers without confidence intervals or p-values makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the results. This is a critical flaw.
2. **Limited Justification for Parameters:** The choice of parameters (e.g., cosine similarity threshold for embedding enrichment, network parameters) is not well-justified. The authors state they used default parameters for the word embedding model, but provide no rationale for this choice. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different parameter settings would strengthen the findings.
3. **Superficial Embedding Integration:** The method of enriching the co-occurrence network with embeddings based on a fixed cosine similarity threshold feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks deeper integration. Exploring alternative methods for incorporating embedding information, such as weighting edges based on similarity or using embeddings directly in network construction, might yield better results.
4. **Inconsistent Data Handling & Reporting:** The description of data preprocessing varies across datasets. Some datasets undergo manual revision, while others rely on automatic segmentation. This inconsistency affects comparability and raises questions about the potential impact of preprocessing choices on performance. Additionally, reporting results in multiple tables, some with "--" indicating inapplicable comparisons, makes it difficult to quickly grasp the key findings.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any discussion of the types of errors made by the classifier. Understanding *which* cases are misclassified would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate that the reported accuracy improvements are statistically significant?
2.  What is the rationale behind the chosen cosine similarity threshold for embedding enrichment? Have you explored different threshold values, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the data preprocessing steps applied to each dataset, including the specific tools and parameters used?
4.  Could you provide an error analysis, detailing the types of misclassifications observed by the classifier?
5.  Why was only the word co-occurrence model used? Were other network models explored and if so, why were they rejected?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but the paper could benefit from more concise and focused phrasing.
*   The references are fairly comprehensive, but some are listed without full details (e.g., Aluı́sio et al.,? 2016).
*   The supplementary material is helpful but could be better organized.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a novel approach and multi-dataset evaluation, the paper suffers from significant methodological shortcomings, most notably the lack of statistical rigor and inadequate justification for key parameters. While the idea of using enriched complex networks for MCI detection is promising, the presented results are not sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version with stronger statistical validation and a more thorough analysis of the results.
