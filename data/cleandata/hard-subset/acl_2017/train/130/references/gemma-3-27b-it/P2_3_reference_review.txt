## Review for ACL

**Summary:**

This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, as a feature representation for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare this approach to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, testing their performance on English and Portuguese datasets. They demonstrate that network-based features can achieve competitive results, and that combining them with other features can further improve accuracy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The paper successfully demonstrates the feasibility of using complex network representations of speech transcripts for the task of MCI detection, a relatively novel approach in this domain.
2. **Cross-Lingual Evaluation:**  The evaluation across both English and Portuguese datasets provides a broader assessment of the method's generalizability.
3. **Comparative Analysis:** The thorough comparison against baseline features (linguistic and BoW) provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the network-based approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to the specific task of MCI detection from speech transcripts, *especially* coupled with word embeddings, is a significant contribution. This introduces a new avenue for feature engineering in this area.
2. **Strong Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a rigorous experimental setup with multiple datasets, different machine learning algorithms, and cross-validation, which strengthens the validity of their findings. The inclusion of both automatically segmented and manually processed datasets is valuable.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, features, and experimental results. The inclusion of supplementary material (datasets details, metrics) enhances reproducibility.
4. **Practical Relevance:** Early detection of MCI is crucial for intervention. The potential for a fully automated system (as indicated by the focus on ASR transcriptions) is a significant practical benefit.
5. **Good Performance:**  The results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly when combining network features with other types of features.  The reported accuracy improvements, while modest, are meaningful in a challenging classification task.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Methodology:** The core network construction (word co-occurrence) and enrichment with embeddings is well-established in NLP. The novelty lies more in the *application* to this specific domain rather than a groundbreaking network-based technique.  The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related network-based approaches *within the medical domain* (if any exist) to better position their work.
2. **Feature Engineering Detail:** While the feature extraction process is outlined, there's a lack of detail on *why* specific topological measures were chosen. A more principled justification for the selected network metrics would strengthen the paper. Were any feature selection techniques employed?
3. **Data Limitations:** The dataset sizes are relatively small, especially for the Portuguese datasets. This limits the generalizability of the results and the statistical power of the analysis.  A discussion of the potential impact of dataset size on the findings is warranted.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper states that threshold parameters were optimized but does not explain *how* this optimization was performed.  More detail on hyperparameter tuning would be beneficial.
5. **Interpretability:** The paper does not delve into *why* the network features are effective in distinguishing between MCI and control subjects.  An attempt to interpret the network structure (e.g., identifying key nodes or patterns) could provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter optimization process, specifically how the threshold parameters were determined for each dataset?
2.  What specific considerations guided the selection of the ten topological network metrics? Were any feature selection techniques explored to identify the most informative metrics?
3.  Given the relatively small dataset sizes, especially for the Portuguese datasets, how robust do you believe your findings are, and what steps might be taken to increase the statistical power of the analysis?
4.  Can you elaborate on any attempts made to interpret the network structures and identify patterns that differentiate between MCI and control subjects?
5.  Are there any potential biases in the datasets (e.g., in terms of demographic characteristics or the specific narratives used) that could affect the generalizability of the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the approach, including potential challenges related to data quality (e.g., ASR errors) and the inherent complexity of language processing in individuals with cognitive impairment.
*   Consider exploring alternative word embedding techniques and network construction methods in future work.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a promising approach to MCI detection using complex networks. While the core network methodology isn’t groundbreaking, the application to this domain, combined with the thorough experimentation and cross-lingual evaluation, makes a valuable contribution.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
