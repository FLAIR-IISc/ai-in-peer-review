## Review for ACL Submission: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, as a feature representation for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare this approach against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese), exploring the benefits of network-based features and various combinations thereof with different machine learning classifiers.  The core idea is to leverage the structural information captured in complex networks, enhanced by semantic information from word embeddings, to improve MCI detection.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the application and exploration of complex network representations for the challenging task of MCI detection from speech. This provides a novel perspective compared to more common linguistic feature-based approaches.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:**  The evaluation across three datasets – two in English and one in Portuguese – is a strength.  This demonstrates a degree of generalizability, albeit limited, and allows for comparison across different linguistic contexts.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Combination Analysis:**  The systematic comparison of network features, linguistic features, and BoW, alongside various combinations, provides valuable insights into the complementary nature of these approaches for this specific task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to the problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts, especially in combination with word embeddings, is relatively novel. This provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors employ 5-fold cross-validation and explore a range of machine learning classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes), providing a comprehensive assessment of the proposed approach. The use of both linear and RBF kernels for SVM is commendable.
3. **Addressing Preprocessing Challenges:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to mitigate the challenges posed by noisy speech transcripts (disfluencies) by minimizing manual preprocessing, which is crucial for real-world applicability.  Their decision to avoid lemmatization is justified given the transcript quality.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:**  The results are presented in a clear and organized manner in tables, facilitating comparison between different feature sets and classifiers. The identification of the best performing combinations for each dataset is valuable.
5. **Portuguese Language Inclusion:**  Including a Portuguese dataset is a significant strength, broadening the scope and potential impact of the research beyond English-speaking populations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Statistical Significance Testing:** While 5-fold cross-validation is used, the paper lacks statistical significance testing to confirm the observed improvements. Reporting p-values would strengthen the claims regarding the superiority of the proposed method. Are the observed differences truly statistically significant, or could they be due to chance?
2. **Embedding Method Justification:** The choice of using character-level word embeddings (Bojanowski et al., 2016) is briefly mentioned but lacks a more detailed justification. Why was this approach favored over more common word embedding models like Word2Vec or GloVe?  A comparative analysis could be beneficial.
3. **Network Construction Parameters:** The paper does not adequately explore the sensitivity of the results to key network construction parameters, such as the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings. A sensitivity analysis would reveal the robustness of the approach.
4. **Limited Explainability:**  While the method shows promising results, it lacks explainability.  Which network features are *most* indicative of MCI?  Providing insights into the underlying reasons for the observed performance would enhance the practical value of the research.  Feature importance analysis would be helpful.
5. **Dataset Imbalance:** The datasets appear to have imbalanced class distributions (Control vs. MCI). While not explicitly addressed, this could influence the performance metrics. Reporting precision, recall, and F1-score, in addition to accuracy, would provide a more complete picture of the model's performance.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to support the claims of improved performance?
2.  What was the rationale behind choosing the character-level word embedding model over more traditional word embedding approaches (Word2Vec, GloVe)? Did you experiment with these alternatives?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the cosine similarity threshold used for enriching the network with semantic information?  Have you performed a sensitivity analysis?
4.  Can you provide insights into which specific network topological features (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness) are most strongly correlated with MCI?
5.  Given potential class imbalance in the datasets, could you provide precision, recall, and F1-score alongside accuracy to better assess the model's performance?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-formatted and comprehensive.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing research in the field.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the study and potential directions for future research.  Specifically, exploring the incorporation of temporal information from the speech signals could be valuable.
