## Peer Review for "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings as a feature representation for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare these network-based features against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, utilizing several machine learning classifiers and exploring combinations of these feature sets. The work is evaluated on three datasets: two English (Cookie Theft) and one Portuguese (Cinderella & ABCD Battery), with varying data characteristics (narrative length, transcription quality).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Exploration of Complex Networks for MCI Detection:** The paper presents a novel application of complex network analysis to the problem of MCI detection from speech, a task typically tackled with more traditional NLP methods. This provides a different perspective and potentially captures subtle linguistic differences related to cognitive decline.
2. **Comparative Evaluation across Datasets:** The evaluation on three datasets, with varying characteristics, offers a more robust assessment of the proposed approach and highlights its adaptability to different types of speech data (short descriptions vs. narratives, manually transcribed vs. automatically segmented).
3. **Demonstration of Feature Combination Benefits:**  The results show that combining network-based features with traditional linguistic features leads to improved performance, suggesting a complementary relationship between these representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Utilizing complex network analysis, particularly when combined with word embeddings, is a relatively unexplored approach for MCI detection from speech.  This differentiates the work from a large body of literature relying on more conventional feature engineering.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The use of three datasets with different characteristics strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets is also a positive aspect, potentially expanding the applicability of the proposed methods.
3. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Comparing the network-based features against established methods like linguistic features and bag-of-words provides a clear benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The systematic exploration of feature combinations is also valuable.
4. **Practical Focus:** The stated goal of developing a fully automated system for MCI detection is commendable and the efforts to minimize manual preprocessing are aligned with this goal. The discussion of challenges related to transcript quality (disfluencies) is relevant and well-addressed.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear description of the methods and experiments. The inclusion of supplementary material with example transcriptions is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Construction:** While the application to MCI detection is novel, the method of constructing the complex network (word co-occurrence) is relatively standard. The paper doesn't explore more sophisticated network construction techniques or consider different edge weighting schemes beyond cosine similarity. 
2. **Word Embedding Implementation Details:** The implementation details regarding the word embeddings (e.g., pre-trained model, training parameters) are somewhat limited. It’s unclear how much the performance relies on the specific embedding model used and whether alternative embeddings would yield different results.
3. **Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper presents classification accuracy results, but lacks statistical significance testing to determine if the observed improvements are statistically significant. This makes it difficult to confidently assess the true benefit of the proposed approach.
4. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The dataset sizes, particularly for the English datasets (43 MCI vs 21 control in Cookie Theft), are relatively small, potentially limiting the statistical power of the results and increasing the risk of overfitting.
5. **Ablation Study:** The paper could benefit from a more focused ablation study to better understand the contribution of individual network features and embedding enhancements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Network Construction:** Did you consider alternative network construction methods beyond simple word co-occurrence, such as incorporating syntactic dependencies or semantic relationships? If so, why were these alternatives not pursued?
2. **Word Embeddings:**  What specific pre-trained word embedding model was used (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText)? How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding model? Did you experiment with different embedding dimensions or training parameters?
3. **Statistical Significance:** Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the improvements reported in the tables?
4. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide a brief error analysis to identify common misclassification patterns and gain insights into the limitations of the proposed approach?
5. **Disfluency Handling:** While you aim to minimize preprocessing, could you elaborate on the impact of different disfluency handling strategies on the performance of the network-based features?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references several datasets but provides limited details on data access. Providing links or information on how to obtain the datasets would be beneficial for reproducibility.
*   The use of multiple datasets and algorithms leads to a large number of results.  A more concise presentation of the key findings would improve clarity.



Overall, this paper presents a promising exploration of complex networks for MCI detection. While the approach is not entirely novel in its core components, the application to this specific problem domain and the comprehensive evaluation across datasets are valuable contributions. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding statistical significance and a more in-depth analysis of the network construction process, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
