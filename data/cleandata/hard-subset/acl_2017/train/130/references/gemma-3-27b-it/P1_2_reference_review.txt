## Peer Review for ACL

**Paper:** Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

**Overall Assessment:** This paper explores the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings as a feature engineering technique for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. While the application domain is valuable and the proposed method shows promise, the paper suffers from a lack of significant novelty in the methodology itself and a somewhat fragmented presentation. The experimental setup, particularly the diverse datasets and evaluation metrics, complicates a clear assessment of the method's effectiveness.

**1. Summary & Contributions:**

*   **Application to a Relevant Problem:** The primary contribution lies in applying complex network analysis, augmented with word embeddings, to the challenging problem of automated MCI detection from speech data. This is a clinically relevant area with potential for early diagnosis and intervention.
*   **Exploration of Feature Combinations:** The authors systematically explore combinations of network-based features with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, providing some insight into optimal feature sets for this task.
*   **Dataset Diversity:** The use of multiple datasets (English & Portuguese, varying narrative lengths and elicitation methods) demonstrates a degree of generalizability testing, even if the results are not uniformly strong across datasets.

**2. Strengths:**

*   **Well-Motivated Problem:** The introduction clearly articulates the importance of early MCI detection and the potential of linguistic analysis for this purpose.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The core idea of enriching complex networks with word embeddings to capture semantic relationships is a reasonable approach, building upon existing work in both network science and NLP.
*   **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The inclusion of topological network measures, linguistic features, and bag-of-words provides a diverse set of potentially informative features for the classifier.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The use of 5-fold cross-validation is a standard and appropriate evaluation technique.
*   **Clear Presentation of Results:** The tables presenting classification accuracies are well-organized and allow for easy comparison of different feature sets and classifiers.

**3. Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The use of complex networks and word embeddings is not novel *per se*. The primary novelty lies in the *application* to this specific problem, but the methodological contributions are incremental.  The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate a significant advancement over existing approaches in network-based text analysis.
*   **Inconsistent Results & Dataset Heterogeneity:** Performance varies significantly across datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD). This makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the generalizability of the proposed method.  The differences in data collection, transcription quality, and narrative length likely contribute to these inconsistencies, but the paper doesn’t adequately address these limitations.  The manual revisions done on the Cinderella dataset are a particular concern and introduce a potential bias.
*   **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** While the authors compare different feature combinations, they lack a strong comparison against established state-of-the-art methods for MCI detection from speech, particularly methods using more conventional machine learning techniques with carefully engineered linguistic features.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** It’s unclear how much of the performance gain comes specifically from the word embeddings versus the underlying complex network structure. A more thorough ablation study removing the embeddings or altering the similarity threshold would be beneficial.
*   **Overly Dense Presentation:** The paper is densely written and could benefit from a more streamlined presentation. The detailed descriptions of datasets and features, while thorough, sometimes obscure the core contributions.

**4. Questions to Authors:**

*   **Dataset Specificity:** How do you explain the substantial performance variations across the three datasets? What steps could be taken to harmonize the datasets or mitigate the impact of their inherent differences?
*   **Ablation Study:** Can you provide an ablation study quantifying the contribution of word embeddings to the overall performance? What happens when you remove the embeddings or vary the cosine similarity threshold?
*   **Baseline Comparison:**  How do your results compare to established methods for MCI detection from speech transcripts (e.g., those cited in the introduction)? Please include a comparison to a strong baseline in your revision.
*   **Feature Importance:**  Can you identify which network-based topological measures are most predictive of MCI? A feature importance analysis would provide valuable insight into the underlying mechanisms driving the classification performance.
*   **Manual Revisions:** You mention manual revisions to the Cinderella dataset. How does this influence the results and generalizability of the study?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful but could be more organized.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the study and potential directions for future research.
*   Clarify the specific implementation details of the word embedding model used (e.g., training data, dimensionality).



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** in its current form. While the work addresses a valuable problem and demonstrates a reasonable approach, the lack of significant novelty, inconsistent results, and absence of a strong baseline comparison prevent it from meeting the standards for acceptance at a top-tier NLP conference. However, the authors have potential to address these weaknesses through a thorough revision, including a stronger baseline comparison, a more in-depth analysis of the results across datasets, and a clearer articulation of the contributions.
