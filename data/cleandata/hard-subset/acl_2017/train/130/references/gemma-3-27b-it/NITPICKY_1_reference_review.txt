This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The primary contribution, as presented, is the demonstration that this network-based approach, combined with traditional features, can discriminate between control participants and those with MCI. A secondary contribution lies in identifying the optimal feature combinations for this task. Finally, the authors present a comparative analysis across different datasets (English and Portuguese).

However, while the idea of applying network analysis to speech transcripts is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws. The novelty of enriching complex networks with word embeddings feels incremental, as this technique has been previously employed in other NLP contexts. The core methodological details are often insufficiently detailed, and the presentation lacks the rigor expected of a high-quality ACL submission.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across both English and Portuguese datasets is a positive aspect, demonstrating some generalizability of the approach. This is valuable, as the availability of neuropsychological datasets is limited.
2.  **Feature Combination Exploration:** The investigation of combining network-based features with linguistic and traditional statistical features is sensible and reveals some useful insights into feature complementarity. The attempt to determine the "best scenario" is commendable, though the findings need more robust justification (see weaknesses).
3.  **Practical Relevance:** The application domain – early detection of MCI – is undeniably important. Developing automated tools for this purpose has significant potential clinical impact.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** The description of the network construction is vague. While the paper mentions using a co-occurrence network, the window size for determining adjacency is not explicitly stated. The threshold for cosine similarity when adding edges based on word embeddings is mentioned only in the figure caption and not in the main text. The specific word embedding model used (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) isn't specified, nor are the parameters used for its training. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility.
2.  **Data Preprocessing Concerns:** The differing levels of preprocessing across datasets (manual revision in one Cinderella dataset vs. automatic segmentation in the ABCD dataset) introduce confounding factors. The impact of these differing preprocessing steps on the results is not adequately discussed. Removing *all* disfluencies from some datasets while retaining them in others creates inconsistencies that influence the network structure and ultimately, the classification accuracy.
3.  **Statistical Rigor and Justification of Results:** The statistical significance of the reported accuracy improvements is not consistently addressed. Simply stating accuracy percentages without providing confidence intervals or p-values weakens the conclusions. The "best scenario" claims are not sufficiently substantiated. A 2.3% improvement (Cookie Theft) or 4.71% improvement (ABCD) might not be statistically significant, especially with relatively small dataset sizes.
4.  **Overly Broad Claims and Lack of Comparison:** The introduction states the approach "suffers less from the problems mentioned above" (disfluencies, reliance on manual resources). This is a strong claim that requires thorough justification, ideally through a direct comparison with existing methods handling disfluencies. No such comparison is provided.
5.  **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, poor table formatting). These detract from the paper's professionalism and readability. Tables 4, 5, 6 and 7 all use different formatting and are difficult to interpret.
6.  **Limited Discussion of Network Properties:** The paper mentions calculating several network properties (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness). However, there’s a lack of discussion regarding *which* properties are most informative for MCI detection and *why*. Simply listing the metrics without interpretation is insufficient.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed description of the word embedding model used, including its training data, parameters (e.g., dimension, window size, negative sampling rate), and the specific library used for its implementation.
2.  Justify the choice of the cosine similarity threshold used for adding edges based on word embeddings. How sensitive are the results to changes in this threshold?
3.  Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate the significance of the reported accuracy improvements. Include confidence intervals in the results.
4.  Discuss the impact of the differing data preprocessing strategies on the results. How might these differences have influenced the observed performance?
5.  Provide a more detailed explanation of *why* certain network properties are more informative for MCI detection than others. Include visualizations of the network structure to illustrate the differences between control and MCI groups.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially promising premise, the paper's numerous flaws – ranging from insufficient methodological detail to a lack of statistical rigor and presentation issues – outweigh its strengths. While the multi-dataset evaluation and exploration of feature combinations are positive aspects, they are not sufficient to warrant acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit a significantly revised version.
