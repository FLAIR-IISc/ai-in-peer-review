## Review for ACL: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, as a method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors construct word co-occurrence networks, enhance them with semantic information from word embeddings, and then leverage topological features of these networks alongside traditional linguistic features for classification. They evaluate their approach on multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) using various machine learning algorithms, finding that network-based features can be competitive with, and sometimes outperform, traditional methods, particularly when combined.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Exploration of Complex Networks for MCI Detection:**  The paper presents a novel application of complex network analysis to the problem of MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to neuropsychological assessment – specifically, leveraging the structure of language in impaired individuals – is a worthwhile contribution.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets (two English, one Portuguese) with differing characteristics (elicitation methods, transcription quality) provides a more robust assessment of the proposed method's generalizability than single-dataset studies. 
3. **Empirical Demonstration of Feature Combination:** The results consistently show that combining complex network features with traditional linguistic and statistical features yields improved performance, suggesting a complementary relationship between these approaches.



**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The combination of complex network theory with word embeddings is a logical and well-motivated approach. The use of word co-occurrence networks captures relationships between words, while embeddings provide semantic context, potentially mitigating some of the issues with sparse data and disfluencies in spontaneous speech.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:**  The inclusion of topological network features, alongside linguistic and bag-of-words features, allows for a holistic representation of the transcripts. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of different aspects of language that might be affected by cognitive impairment.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The use of 5-fold cross-validation and comparison against several established machine learning algorithms provides a solid empirical foundation for the findings. The inclusion of both supervised learning methods and ensemble learning demonstrates a thorough approach to evaluation.
4. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** The application of NLP techniques to the early detection of MCI is a clinically relevant and potentially impactful area of research.  The work directly addresses the need for automated tools to assist in neuropsychological assessment.
5. **Handling of Disfluencies (Partial):** While not a complete solution, the authors' acknowledgement of disfluencies and decision *not* to lemmatize due to potential noise introduction is a pragmatic and reasonable approach, especially considering the challenges of noisy transcripts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Approach:** The core idea of using word co-occurrence networks isn’t entirely new in NLP. The key novelty lies in its application to *this specific domain* (MCI detection) and the combination with embeddings. However, a clearer articulation of how this approach *differs* from and improves upon existing network-based NLP methods is needed.  A stronger connection to related work in network-based language analysis beyond the citations would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Word Embedding Details:** The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the chosen word embedding model (Bojanowski et al., 2016).  Why was this specific model chosen over alternatives (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe)? Discussing the impact of different embedding choices could be valuable.  Furthermore, the parameters used for training the embeddings are only briefly mentioned, potentially impacting reproducibility.
3. **Network Construction Parameters:**  The cosine similarity threshold used for adding edges in the enriched network is a crucial parameter. The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the chosen value, nor does it explore the sensitivity of the results to different threshold settings.  An ablation study varying this parameter would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Imbalance & Demographic Differences:**  The datasets exhibit varying degrees of imbalance between control and MCI groups. Additionally, there are demographic differences between the datasets (age, education).  While acknowledged, the impact of these factors on the results isn’t fully explored.  Stratified cross-validation and potentially weighting schemes could mitigate the effect of imbalance.  The potential confounding effects of demographic differences need to be discussed.
5. **Interpretability:**  While the model achieves reasonable accuracy, it lacks interpretability.  Identifying which network features are *most* indicative of MCI would provide valuable insights into the linguistic markers of cognitive impairment and could potentially inform clinical practice.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific motivations for choosing the FastText word embedding model (Bojanowski et al., 2016) over other popular alternatives?
2.  What is the rationale behind the chosen cosine similarity threshold for enriching the network with semantic links? Have you explored the sensitivity of your results to different threshold values?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the demographic differences between the datasets and discuss how these differences might influence your findings?
4.  Which specific topological network features proved to be most discriminatory between MCI and control groups?  Can you provide examples of how these features manifest in the actual speech transcripts?
5.  How would the proposed approach perform on a larger, more diverse dataset of speech transcripts, if available?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but some sections are a bit dense and could benefit from clearer explanations.
* Consider including a visualization of the enriched network to illustrate the added semantic links.
*  The reference list is extensive, which is positive, but ensure consistent formatting.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to MCI detection using complex networks and word embeddings. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are encouraging. While the novelty of the network approach itself is limited, the application to this specific domain and the demonstration of effective feature combination constitute a valuable contribution. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
