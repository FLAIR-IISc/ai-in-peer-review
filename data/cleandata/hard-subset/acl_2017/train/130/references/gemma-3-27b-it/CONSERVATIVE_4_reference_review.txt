This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent transcripts as networks where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, with additional edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors evaluate their approach on several datasets, combining it with traditional linguistic features and statistical baselines.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of complex network analysis to the MCI detection problem, particularly the attempt to leverage word embeddings to enhance the network representation of short, potentially noisy, transcripts. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation across multiple datasets – English and Portuguese – and the investigation of feature combinations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Multiple Datasets:** The use of datasets in both English and Portuguese is a positive aspect, demonstrating some degree of generalizability and addressing a potential language-specific bias. Though the datasets are relatively small, the variety is appreciated.
2. **Exploration of Feature Combinations:** The authors rightly investigate the benefits of combining network-based features with established linguistic and statistical features. The multi-view learning approach is reasonable and, in some cases, yields improved performance.
3. **Addressing Transcript Noise:** Recognizing the challenges posed by disfluencies in spontaneous speech transcripts is commendable. The decision to avoid extensive preprocessing, aiming to retain potentially informative elements, is a pragmatic one.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methodology:** While applying complex networks to this problem isn't *incorrect*, the methodology itself doesn’t represent a significant methodological advance. Complex networks and word embeddings are both well-established techniques, and their combination, while sensible, isn’t groundbreaking. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific network-based approach is expected to outperform existing, more conventional methods *a priori*.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparisons to existing methods feel inadequate. While linguistic features are included, the baseline performance isn’t always clearly established as state-of-the-art. For instance, the paper mentions several prior works in the introduction but doesn’t directly compare against them in the results section, making it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. Specifically, the performance of simpler machine learning models *without* the complex network features, but *with* the same linguistic features, should be presented for a fair comparison.
3. **Limited Justification for Network Parameters:** The choice of network construction parameters (e.g., the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges) and word embedding model is not thoroughly justified. A sensitivity analysis to these parameters would strengthen the findings and demonstrate robustness. The impact of different embedding sizes and training data on the embedding quality isn’t discussed.
4. **Small Dataset Sizes and Demographic Imbalance:** The relatively small dataset sizes limit the statistical power of the results. Additionally, there are demographic imbalances in the datasets (e.g., the ABCD dataset) that could potentially influence the findings. This needs to be addressed with caution when interpreting the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Why was this specific complex network approach chosen over other graph-based methods commonly used in NLP (e.g., dependency graphs, semantic role labeling graphs)? What are the theoretical advantages of the word co-occurrence network for this specific task?
2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison to relevant baseline methods from the literature, including specific performance metrics for those methods on the same datasets?
3. What is the sensitivity of your results to the cosine similarity threshold used for adding edges based on word embeddings? Have you explored a range of values and reported the impact on performance?
4. Could you address the potential impact of the demographic imbalances in the datasets on your findings? Have you considered any methods for mitigating this bias?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, but could benefit from more concise explanations of the technical details. Providing more context on the specific challenges in analyzing speech transcripts from individuals with MCI would strengthen the motivation for this work. The supplementary material is helpful for understanding the datasets and linguistic features used.
