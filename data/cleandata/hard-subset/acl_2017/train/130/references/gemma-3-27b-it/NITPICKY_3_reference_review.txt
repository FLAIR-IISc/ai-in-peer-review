This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose modeling narratives as networks and combining network-based features with traditional linguistic and statistical features for improved classification. The primary contribution is the application of complex network analysis, augmented with word embeddings, to the task of MCI detection from speech, a relatively unexplored approach within the field. A secondary contribution is the comparison of different feature sets and classifiers on multiple datasets (English and Portuguese). A tertiary contribution is the demonstration that enriching networks with word embeddings offers an improvement over using only network topology.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of complex network analysis with word embeddings to MCI detection from speech transcripts is a novel direction. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their use within the specific context of neuropsychological assessment and MCI detection is a valuable exploration.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, encompassing both English and Portuguese languages, enhancing the generalizability and robustness of their findings. This is a significant strength as it moves beyond a single, potentially biased dataset.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The combination of complex network features with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations allows for a holistic assessment of the transcript data. This provides a solid baseline for comparison and highlights the complementary nature of different feature types.
4. **Clear Methodology:** The paper provides a relatively detailed description of the methods employed, including network construction, word embedding integration, feature extraction, and classification algorithms. This transparency facilitates reproducibility and allows for further investigation by other researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Justification for Parameter Choices:** Several critical parameters, such as the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings (mentioned only in the methodology, no experimentation), are not adequately justified. The authors should provide rationale and/or conduct sensitivity analysis to demonstrate the robustness of their results to different parameter settings.
2. **Insufficient Discussion of Network Topology Metrics:** While the paper lists ten topological measures, there is limited discussion about *why* these specific measures were chosen and their relevance to cognitive impairment. A deeper theoretical justification connecting these network properties to linguistic changes associated with MCI would strengthen the work.
3. **Inconsistent Preprocessing:** The preprocessing steps differ between the datasets. Specifically, the Cinderella dataset has both a manually revised version and an automatically segmented version. This makes direct comparison of results difficult and potentially introduces confounding factors. The impact of manual revision on performance should be thoroughly analyzed and discussed.
4. **Minor Formatting and Grammatical Errors:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, hyphenation) and grammatical errors (e.g., awkward phrasing, missing articles). These issues detract from the overall professionalism of the manuscript. While not critical, they indicate a lack of careful attention to detail.
5. **Overstated Improvements:** The reported performance gains are, in some cases, marginal, and the authors’ claims of significant improvement should be tempered. For instance, a 2.3% improvement in the Cookie Theft dataset, while positive, does not necessarily constitute a breakthrough.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the rationale behind the chosen cosine similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings? Have you experimented with different threshold values, and if so, how did they impact the results?
2. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the connection between the selected network topology metrics and the linguistic characteristics of MCI?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the differing preprocessing approaches across the datasets? Specifically, can you compare the performance on the Cinderella dataset using only the automatically segmented version to address the inconsistency?
4. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the challenges of applying Coh-Metrix to Portuguese and the rationale behind discarding specific metrics?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive but could benefit from improved formatting and consistency.
*   Figures 1 and 2 are crucial for understanding the network enrichment process, but their resolution is low. Higher-resolution versions should be provided.
*   The Supplementary Material is helpful but lacks clear labeling and organization.



Overall, while the paper presents a novel and potentially promising approach to MCI detection, it suffers from several weaknesses related to methodological justification, experimental rigor, and attention to detail. Addressing these concerns is crucial for strengthening the manuscript and demonstrating the true value of the proposed work. I am inclined to **reject** the paper in its current form. Significant revisions addressing the aforementioned weaknesses are necessary for reconsideration.
