This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea—modeling language as a network and leveraging semantic information via embeddings—is the primary contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the application of this approach to neuropsychological assessment data, a domain where robust, automated tools are highly needed. Finally, the exploration of feature combinations (network features with traditional linguistic and statistical features) provides some insights into optimal configurations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea of representing speech transcripts as enriched complex networks is genuinely novel within the context of MCI detection. While complex networks have been used in NLP, applying them to *speech transcripts* specifically for cognitive impairment assessment, and *integrating* word embeddings in this manner, represents a fresh perspective. This is the strongest aspect of the paper.
2. **Potential for Reduced Preprocessing:** The authors rightly point out the challenges of extensive preprocessing required for traditional NLP methods applied to spontaneous speech. The claim that network-based methods might circumvent the need for meticulous manual cleaning (e.g., disfluency removal) is significant, potentially offering a pathway to more scalable and practical applications.
3. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Testing the approach on three datasets (English and Portuguese, different elicitation tasks) strengthens the findings and demonstrates a degree of generalizability, even if the performance variations are considerable.
4. **Comprehensive Feature Exploration:** The investigation of combining network features with established linguistic features and bag-of-words representations is commendable. The attempt to identify optimal feature combinations is valuable, even if the conclusions are dataset-dependent.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** While the approach shows competitive performance, the accuracy improvements over baseline methods are often marginal, particularly on the English dataset. The gains aren't substantial enough to definitively demonstrate the superiority of this network-based approach. The best results are achieved through ensemble methods, suggesting the network features aren’t particularly strong *on their own*.
2. **Lack of Deep Analysis of Network Properties:** The paper describes *what* topological features are used, but lacks a compelling explanation of *why* these features are expected to be informative for MCI detection. A more theoretically grounded justification for the choice of network metrics would strengthen the work. What specific network properties are hypothesized to be altered in MCI patients, and how do these alterations manifest in the chosen metrics?
3. **Dataset Imbalance and Size:** The datasets are relatively small and exhibit imbalances in participant numbers across groups. This limits the statistical power of the analyses and the generalizability of the results. More robust conclusions would require larger, more balanced datasets.
4. **Embedding Choice and Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not sufficiently discuss the choice of word embeddings (specifically, fastText) and the impact of different embedding dimensions or training parameters. A sensitivity analysis to these parameters is crucial to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the specific linguistic or cognitive mechanisms that you hypothesize are captured by the chosen network topological features in the context of MCI?
2. Have you investigated the impact of different word embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) on the performance of your system?
3. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the misclassified samples? Are there any common characteristics of the patients who are consistently misclassified?
4. What are the computational costs associated with building and analyzing these complex networks compared to traditional feature extraction methods?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of supplementary materials is helpful. While the methodological rigor could be improved (e.g., with a more thorough parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis), the novelty of the approach and the potential for reducing reliance on manual preprocessing warrant consideration. The paper's focus on a real-world problem with significant clinical implications is commendable. I believe the novelty outweighs the methodological shortcomings and recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer's concerns in a future iteration of the work.
