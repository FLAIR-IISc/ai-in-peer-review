This paper explores the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea – representing linguistic data as networks and leveraging semantic information from embeddings – is the primary contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the application of this approach to neuropsychological assessment data, a domain where automated analysis can be highly valuable. Finally, the authors provide a comparative analysis of different feature sets (network features, linguistic features, bag-of-words) and classification algorithms, offering insights into the best performing combinations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of complex networks *with* word embeddings for this specific task is genuinely interesting. While complex networks have been used in NLP, and word embeddings are commonplace, their synergistic application to MCI detection in speech transcripts feels fresh and potentially impactful. This isn't merely another incremental improvement in feature engineering.
2. **Relevance to a Significant Problem:** Early detection of MCI is crucial for timely intervention and improving patient outcomes. Automated tools for analyzing speech patterns can play a vital role in this process, making the research highly relevant.
3. **Exploration of Multiple Datasets:** The use of three datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, and ABCD) in both English and Portuguese strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The inclusion of automatically segmented data (ABCD) demonstrates a move towards a fully automated system, which is a practical goal.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate a variety of classifiers and feature combinations, providing a nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches. The multi-view learning approach is a commendable attempt to leverage the complementary nature of different feature spaces.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Statistical Significance/Effect Size Reporting:** While the paper reports accuracy scores, it lacks detailed statistical testing (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to demonstrate the significance of the improvements achieved by the proposed method. It’s difficult to assess whether the observed gains are statistically meaningful or due to chance.
2. **Lack of Deep Dive into Network Properties:** The paper doesn’t explore *why* certain network topological properties are more predictive of MCI than others. A deeper analysis of the network structures themselves (e.g., identifying specific subgraphs or patterns that differentiate MCI patients) would add significant value.
3. **Inconsistent Dataset Characteristics & Preprocessing:** The datasets vary considerably in size, elicitation method, and the degree of preprocessing. This makes direct comparisons between results challenging. For example, the ABCD dataset is automatically segmented, while others are manually transcribed and potentially cleaned. This variability isn’t sufficiently addressed in the analysis.
4. **Word Embedding Details:** The choice of using character-level embeddings (FastText) is mentioned, but further justification for this choice—especially in the context of the relatively short transcripts—would be beneficial. A comparison with traditional word embeddings might be informative.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) to support the reported accuracy improvements? Specifically, what is the p-value for the improvement observed with the enriched networks compared to baseline methods?
2.  Did you perform any analysis to identify specific network topological properties (e.g., high clustering coefficient, specific centrality measures) that are strongly correlated with MCI? Can you provide examples of networks from healthy controls versus MCI patients to illustrate any observed differences?
3.  Given the differences in datasets and preprocessing, how confident are you that the observed performance gains are transferable across different clinical settings and data collection protocols? Would you expect similar results if the datasets were more closely aligned in terms of their characteristics?
4.  Why did you choose character-level word embeddings over traditional word embeddings, considering the relatively short length of the transcripts? Did you experiment with both, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material is helpful for understanding the datasets and linguistic features used. It would be beneficial to include a visual example of the network enrichment process with a small sample transcript in the main body of the paper to enhance clarity. The paper's writing is generally clear, but some sections could benefit from more concise explanations. Despite the weaknesses, the novelty of the approach and the potential impact of the work outweigh its flaws. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the statistical significance and providing a more nuanced analysis of the network properties.
