## Review for "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, as a feature representation for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare the performance of these network-based features with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations across three datasets (English and Portuguese). They demonstrate that enriched complex networks are competitive, and sometimes superior, to traditional approaches, especially when combined in a multi-view learning framework.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is applying the complex network approach, specifically enriched with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech, a task traditionally dominated by linguistic feature engineering.
2. **Cross-lingual Evaluation:** The evaluation across both English and Portuguese datasets is valuable, highlighting the potential generalizability of the approach and providing a broader perspective than studies focusing on a single language.
3. **Demonstration of Feature Combination Benefits:** The results clearly show that combining network features with traditional linguistic features through multi-view learning yields improved performance, suggesting a complementary relationship between these representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to the specific problem of MCI detection from speech, and *especially* the enrichment with word embeddings, is a relatively novel approach. This opens up a potentially interesting new avenue for research.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform extensive experiments with multiple datasets, various machine learning algorithms, and different feature combinations. The use of 5-fold cross-validation strengthens the reliability of the results. The inclusion of both manually processed and automatically segmented data provides a realistic assessment of the method's robustness.
3. **Clear and Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The methodology is clearly described, and the results are presented in a concise and easy-to-understand manner.
4. **Practical Relevance:** Early detection of MCI is clinically significant, and an automated system reducing the need for extensive manual feature engineering could be a valuable tool.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The individual components – complex networks, word embeddings, and standard machine learning classifiers – are well-established techniques. The core novelty lies in the *combination* of these. While this is a valid contribution, the paper doesn’t fully articulate *why* this specific combination is particularly well-suited for the nuances of MCI detection in speech.  A deeper discussion of why the network structure and word embeddings capture relevant linguistic features for identifying cognitive decline is needed.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper reports accuracy scores, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *which* instances are misclassified and *why* could provide valuable insights for improving the model.  For example, are there specific linguistic patterns that the network features struggle to capture?
3. **Dataset Characteristics & Imbalance:** The dataset sizes are relatively small, particularly for the English dataset.  The demographic information provided suggests potential imbalances in the groups (e.g., more males in the MCI group), which could influence the results. A more thorough discussion of these limitations and their potential impact is necessary.  The authors should explore data augmentation techniques or address the imbalance directly.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the network is a hyperparameter. The paper doesn’t provide a strong justification for the chosen threshold or explore the impact of varying it. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the specific linguistic features or patterns you hypothesize are being captured by the complex network representation that contribute to improved MCI detection?
2.  Could you provide a detailed error analysis, outlining the types of misclassifications and potential reasons for these errors?
3.  Given the relatively small dataset sizes and potential imbalances, how confident are you in the generalizability of your findings? Have you considered data augmentation techniques or strategies to address the imbalance?
4.  What is the rationale behind the chosen cosine similarity threshold for network enrichment, and have you explored the sensitivity of your results to different threshold values?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are extensive and well-formatted.
*   The supplementary material (example transcript and details on Coh-Metrix metrics) is helpful.
*   Consider adding a discussion section to explicitly compare the proposed approach with other state-of-the-art methods for MCI detection.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel application of complex networks to a clinically relevant problem. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination and evaluation demonstrate promising results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly regarding the justification for the approach, error analysis, and dataset limitations – will significantly strengthen the contribution of this work.
