- Summary: Proposes enriching word co-occurrence networks with word embeddings to detect MCI from speech transcripts and evaluates combined network and linguistic features across English and Portuguese datasets.

- Contribution 1: Applies complex network analysis to MCI detection, leveraging language structure in impaired speech.

- Contribution 2: Evaluates the method on three diverse datasets, improving assessment of generalizability.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates that combining network-based features with traditional linguistic/statistical features improves performance.

- Strength 1: Methodology is sound and well-motivated, combining co-occurrence networks with semantic embeddings.

- Strength 2: Uses a comprehensive feature set including network topology, linguistic, and bag-of-words features.

- Strength 3: Empirical evaluation is rigorous, using 5-fold cross-validation and multiple ML algorithms, including ensembles.

- Strength 4: Addresses a clinically relevant problem—automated early detection of MCI from speech.

- Strength 5: Pragmatic handling of disfluencies (avoiding lemmatization) given noisy spontaneous speech transcripts.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty in using co-occurrence networks; novelty mainly in domain application and combination with embeddings.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient justification and detail on the choice and training parameters of the FastText embeddings.

- Weakness 3: Lack of analysis and justification for the cosine similarity threshold used to add semantic edges and no sensitivity/ablation study.

- Weakness 4: Dataset imbalance and demographic differences (age, education) are acknowledged but not fully explored or controlled for.

- Weakness 5: Limited interpretability—unclear which network features are most indicative of MCI and how they map to transcript patterns.

- Question 1: Why was FastText (Bojanowski et al., 2016) chosen over alternatives like Word2Vec or GloVe?

- Question 2: What rationale and sensitivity analysis support the chosen cosine similarity threshold for semantic links?

- Question 3: Can you provide a detailed analysis of demographic differences across datasets and their impact on results?

- Question 4: Which specific topological network features are most discriminatory between MCI and controls, with examples?

- Question 5: How would the approach scale and perform on a larger, more diverse speech dataset?

- Additional comment 1: Some sections are dense and would benefit from clearer explanations.

- Additional comment 2: Include a visualization of the enriched network to show added semantic links.

- Additional comment 3: Reference list is extensive but should have consistent formatting.

- Overall assessment: Promising, well-evaluated approach with practical relevance; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing novelty clarification, embedding and threshold details, demographic effects, and interpretability.
