This paper explores the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent text as a network where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, enhanced with semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors compare this approach to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, demonstrating comparable or slightly improved performance on several datasets.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of a complex network approach, coupled with word embeddings, to the specific task of MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been used in NLP previously, their application to this clinical domain, especially with the attempt to mitigate the issues of transcript noise (disfluencies), is a reasonable exploration. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive comparison across different feature sets (network-based, linguistic, and BoW) and datasets (English and Portuguese).

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem:** Early detection of MCI is a critical area of research with significant clinical implications. Automated methods using readily available speech data are highly desirable.
2. **Exploration of Network Approach:** The attempt to leverage complex networks to capture subtle linguistic changes associated with MCI is a potentially fruitful direction. The rationale for using networks to overcome the limitations of traditional methods in dealing with noisy transcripts is plausible.
3. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the approach on multiple datasets, including both English and Portuguese, strengthens the findings and suggests some degree of generalizability.
4. **Comparison to Baselines:** Including comparisons to established methods (linguistic features, BoW) provides a necessary benchmark for assessing the value of the proposed approach. While not overwhelmingly superior, the performance is competitive.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The core methodology – using complex networks and word embeddings – is not particularly novel. Both techniques have been extensively used in NLP. The specific application to MCI and the attempt to handle transcript noise are valuable, but do not constitute a significant methodological breakthrough.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Strength:** The baselines, while present, are not state-of-the-art. The linguistic features used (Coh-Metrix) are appropriate, but the lack of comparison to more sophisticated deep learning-based approaches for speech or text analysis weakens the claims of improvement. A comparison to, for example, a simple BERT or other transformer-based model trained on the same data would be crucial.
3. **Limited Statistical Significance:** The reported accuracy gains are often marginal and without statistical significance tests, it’s difficult to determine if the improvements are truly meaningful or due to chance. The variations across datasets also suggest sensitivity to data characteristics.
4. **Data Limitations & Reporting:** The demographic information provided in the tables seems incomplete. A clearer description of the cognitive assessment methods used to diagnose MCI is needed. Furthermore, the handling of disfluencies appears inconsistent across datasets (manual revision vs. automatic removal), potentially affecting comparability. The manual revision of the Cinderella dataset raises questions about the 'fully automated' goal stated in the introduction.
5. **Overly Complex Feature Engineering:** The number of topological network features (30, averaging, standard deviation and skewness for 10 measures) seems excessive and could lead to overfitting. A more principled feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique should be considered.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for all reported performance improvements.
2.  Why were more modern deep learning baselines (e.g., BERT, LSTM) not included in the comparison?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the criteria used for diagnosing MCI in each dataset?
4.  Given the claim of a 'fully automated' system, please justify the manual revision of transcripts in the Cinderella dataset. If manual revision is essential for achieving good performance, this should be acknowledged as a limitation.
5.  What methods were used to optimize the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, but the paper could benefit from a more concise presentation of the related work. Some of the background information on complex networks feels unnecessarily verbose. The Supplementary Material is helpful for providing details about the datasets and feature extraction.
