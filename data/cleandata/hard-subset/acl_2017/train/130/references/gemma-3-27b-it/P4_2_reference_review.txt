This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent transcripts as networks where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, augmented with edges based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The authors evaluate their approach on multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) and compare its performance to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations.

The primary contribution of this work is the application and evaluation of a complex network-based approach, incorporating word embeddings, for MCI detection from speech data. While complex networks have been used in NLP previously, their application to the specific task of MCI detection, combined with the word embedding enrichment strategy, represents a novel exploration. A secondary contribution lies in the comparative analysis of network-based features against established linguistic features and simpler statistical methods (BoW). This provides valuable insight into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach in this specific domain. Finally, the work provides a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and languages (English and Portuguese), offering a reasonably comprehensive assessment of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of enriched complex networks to the problem of MCI detection from speech transcripts is a novel idea. Leveraging graph theory to model language and incorporating semantic information via word embeddings offers a potentially powerful approach to capturing subtle linguistic changes associated with cognitive decline.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct experiments on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD), encompassing both English and Portuguese, demonstrating the generalizability of their approach. The inclusion of multiple datasets is a significant strength, as it increases the robustness of the findings.
3. **Comparative Analysis:** The detailed comparison against baseline methods, including traditional linguistic features and BoW, is well executed. This allows for a clear understanding of the added value of the proposed network-based approach. The results consistently show competitive or superior performance of the enriched network features.
4. **Thorough Feature Engineering:** The use of both topological properties of the networks and established linguistic features, combined with the exploration of different machine learning classifiers, indicates a thorough investigation of potential feature representations.
5. **Addressing Data Challenges:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to address the challenges posed by speech transcripts, such as disfluencies and the need for automated processing. While manual revision was performed on some datasets, the overall goal of a fully automated system is clearly stated and pursued.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Enrichment:** The methodology for enriching the network with word embeddings (using cosine similarity above a threshold) lacks sufficient detail. The rationale for choosing a specific threshold and its impact on the results are not thoroughly discussed.  A more detailed exploration of different similarity thresholds and their effect on performance would be beneficial.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The specific pre-trained embeddings used (Bojanowski et al., 2016) are mentioned, but a discussion of their suitability for the task, potential biases, and alternatives would strengthen the work.
3. **Interpretability of Network Features:** While the network features demonstrate good predictive performance, their interpretability is limited. It is unclear *why* certain network properties are more indicative of MCI than others. Providing some insights into the linguistic or cognitive processes captured by these features would be valuable.
4. **Dataset Imbalance:** Some of the datasets exhibit imbalances in the number of participants in each group (MCI vs. control). While the authors use cross-validation, the impact of this imbalance on the results is not explicitly addressed or mitigated.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The reported accuracy improvements are not always accompanied by statistical significance testing. Establishing statistical significance would provide stronger evidence supporting the superiority of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale for choosing the cosine similarity threshold used to enrich the network, and how different thresholds impact the results?
2.  What considerations were given to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings, and how might the performance be affected by using different embedding models?
3.  Can you offer any insights into the linguistic or cognitive processes that might explain the predictive power of the specific network features (e.g., PageRank, Betweenness) used in your model?
4. How did you account for the potential impact of dataset imbalance on the reported accuracy results? Have you considered using techniques like stratified cross-validation or cost-sensitive learning?
5.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to support the reported performance differences between the proposed method and the baseline approaches?



**Additional Comments:**

* The supplementary material is helpful for providing additional details about the datasets and features used.
* The writing quality is generally good, but could be improved with more concise explanations and clearer organization of the results.
* Consider exploring the use of attention mechanisms or other techniques to identify the most important words or phrases within the network that contribute to MCI detection.
