This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, to detect Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The primary contribution is the application of this network-based approach – a relatively unexplored avenue – to the challenging problem of early dementia detection using linguistic data. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration, across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese), that network features can contribute to classification accuracy, especially when combined with more traditional linguistic features. Finally, the work provides a comparative analysis of feature types and classification algorithms in this context.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of representing speech transcripts as complex networks and enriching them with word embeddings is genuinely novel in the context of MCI detection. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to neuropsychological assessment, particularly with this embedding enrichment, represents a fresh perspective. This is the paper’s strongest asset.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluating the approach on three different datasets – two in English and one in Portuguese – strengthens the findings and suggests some degree of generalizability. The inclusion of a Portuguese dataset is particularly valuable, as much research in this area is English-centric.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:** The authors thoughtfully compare the performance of network-based features against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations. This provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach and highlights the benefits of combining them.
4. **Practical Focus:** The stated goal of creating a fully automated system is commendable. The authors proactively address challenges related to transcript quality and preprocessing, recognizing the need for robust methods applicable to real-world clinical data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Validation & Statistical Rigor:** While the results are encouraging, the improvements achieved are often modest, and the statistical significance isn’t always convincingly demonstrated. The paper reports accuracy scores but lacks detailed statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) to confirm whether the observed differences are statistically significant.
2. **Embedding Choice & Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper uses a specific pre-trained word embedding model (Bojanowski et al., 2016) without a strong justification for this choice or a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of using different embeddings. The performance could be highly sensitive to the quality and characteristics of the embeddings.
3. **Network Construction Details:** The paper describes the network construction process at a high level. Key parameters, such as the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges, are mentioned but not thoroughly explored or justified. A more systematic investigation of these parameters would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Imbalance:** Some datasets exhibit a noticeable imbalance in the number of control and MCI participants. This imbalance could potentially bias the results, and the authors do not explicitly address this issue in their analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) to support the reported accuracy improvements?
2. Did you experiment with different word embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe, fastText)? If so, how did the performance vary? If not, why was the chosen model preferred?
3. How sensitive are the results to the cosine similarity threshold used when enriching the networks with word embeddings? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis?
4. Did you explore techniques to address the potential impact of dataset imbalance (e.g., oversampling, undersampling, cost-sensitive learning)?
5. Could you provide more details about the preprocessing steps applied to the Portuguese datasets, particularly regarding disfluency removal and sentence segmentation?




**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material is helpful for understanding the datasets and linguistic features used. The reference list is comprehensive. While the writing quality is generally good, some sections could benefit from more concise and focused explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some methodological limitations, I believe this paper presents a promising and novel approach to MCI detection. The application of complex networks with word embeddings is a valuable contribution, and the empirical results suggest potential for further development. I am inclined to accept this paper, prioritizing the novelty of the approach over the need for more rigorous statistical validation. The authors' willingness to address the raised concerns during the rebuttal phase could further strengthen the work.
