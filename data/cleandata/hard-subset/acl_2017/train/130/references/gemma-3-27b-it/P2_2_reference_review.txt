## Review for ACL

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, as a means of detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors propose modeling transcripts as networks, leveraging both word co-occurrence and semantic similarity via word embeddings, and then utilizing topological features extracted from these networks as input to machine learning classifiers. They evaluate their approach on datasets of English and Portuguese speech, comparing performance to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The primary contribution is the exploration of complex network analysis as a potentially effective method for analyzing speech transcripts in the context of MCI diagnosis. This is a relatively novel approach within this domain.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The paper presents a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation of their approach across three datasets – two English and one Portuguese – providing insights into the generalizability of the method.
3. **Demonstration of Feature Combination Benefits:** The study demonstrates that combining network features with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words can lead to improved performance, highlighting the potential for synergistic use of different feature types.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  Applying complex network analysis to the problem of MCI detection from speech is a genuinely interesting direction. The use of word embeddings to enrich the networks is a logical and potentially powerful extension to standard network-based text analysis.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors utilize a standard machine learning evaluation methodology (5-fold cross-validation) and a range of algorithms, allowing for a fair comparison of different feature sets. The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets adds to the robustness of the findings.
3. **Clear Presentation of Methods:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the methodology, including the network construction process, feature extraction, and classification pipeline. This allows for relatively straightforward reproducibility.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed approach achieves competitive accuracy compared to baseline methods, and in some cases outperforms them, especially when features are combined. The highest accuracy in the Cinderella dataset (75%) is a promising result.
5. **Practical Relevance:**  The focus on automating the analysis of speech transcripts is highly relevant to clinical practice, as it has the potential to reduce the burden on clinicians and facilitate early diagnosis of MCI.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of complex networks and word embeddings is interesting, neither component is entirely novel in itself.  The paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new network construction technique or embedding method. The novelty lies in applying these existing tools to this specific medical domain.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* the model makes specific errors.  Understanding which types of errors are most common could provide valuable insights for improving the model and identifying its limitations. For instance, are there specific linguistic patterns that are consistently misclassified?
3. **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The paper mentions optimizing the threshold parameter but doesn't provide detailed information on how this optimization was performed. The sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings (e.g., the cosine similarity threshold for adding edges) should be discussed.  A more extensive hyperparameter search might be warranted.
4. **Dataset Characteristics:** The datasets used have varying characteristics (narrative length, elicitation method). While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of how these differences might influence the results would be beneficial. The significant differences in accuracy across datasets suggest dataset-specific factors are playing a role.
5. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to baseline methods are provided, the paper doesn’t deeply compare against the current state-of-the-art in MCI detection from speech. A more comprehensive comparison to other recent approaches would strengthen the claim of contribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the parameter optimization process, particularly for the cosine similarity threshold used when enriching the network with word embeddings?  Did you explore a range of values, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
2.  Can you provide a more in-depth error analysis?  Specifically, what types of misclassifications are most frequent, and what linguistic characteristics are associated with these errors?
3.  How robust are your findings to variations in the size of the training data?  Did you investigate the performance of the model with smaller or larger training sets?
4.  You mention manual revision of transcripts in some datasets. How much impact do you think this has on your results, and how would you attempt to mitigate the effect of noisy, unrevised transcripts in a real-world clinical setting?
5. Can you clarify the choice of specific linguistic features used from Coh-Metrix and the rationale behind excluding certain features?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are a bit convoluted.
* The supplementary material is helpful for providing details on the datasets and features.
* It would be helpful to provide a link to the code and data, if possible, to facilitate reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a promising new approach to MCI detection from speech. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination of complex networks, word embeddings, and machine learning, along with the thorough empirical evaluation, makes this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
