This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors claim to demonstrate that this approach can distinguish between control participants and those with MCI, and that combining network features with traditional linguistic features improves performance. I see the primary contribution as the application of complex network analysis, incorporating word embeddings, to the specific task of MCI detection from spoken language – a relatively unexplored area. A secondary contribution is the exploration of feature combinations for improved performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Application Area:** The detection of early-stage MCI is a critical research area with significant clinical implications. Applying NLP techniques to this problem is valuable.
2. **Sound Methodology (in principle):** The core idea of representing speech transcripts as complex networks and enriching them with semantic information from word embeddings is a reasonable approach. Complex network analysis *can* reveal underlying patterns in language use that might be indicative of cognitive decline.
3. **Exploration of Feature Combination:** The attempt to combine network-based features with traditional linguistic and statistical features is a positive aspect. This recognizes that a multi-faceted approach is likely needed for accurate diagnosis.
4. **Multiple Datasets:** The use of datasets in both English and Portuguese adds a degree of generalizability, although the datasets themselves are somewhat limited in size (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the inadequate comparison to well-established baselines. While linguistic features are included, the paper doesn’t provide a thorough comparison against simpler, widely used machine learning models *without* the complex network component. For example, a basic logistic regression model trained solely on the linguistic features would provide a crucial point of reference. The claim of improvement with network features feels unsubstantiated without this.
2. **Limited Dataset Size & Heterogeneity:** The datasets are relatively small, particularly the English “Cookie Theft” dataset (43 MCI cases). This raises concerns about the statistical power and generalizability of the results. Furthermore, the datasets differ significantly in elicitation method (picture description vs. narrative retelling), potentially confounding the results and limiting cross-dataset comparisons.
3. **Insufficient Justification for Network Parameters:** The paper provides minimal justification for the choice of network construction parameters (e.g., cosine similarity threshold for adding edges). The impact of these parameters on performance is not explored, and the chosen values appear somewhat arbitrary.
4. **Overly Complex Methodology:** While not inherently a weakness, the complexity of the network-based approach requires strong justification through superior performance. Given the limited dataset size and weak baseline comparison, it’s unclear whether the added complexity is warranted. Simpler models might achieve comparable or even better results with less risk of overfitting.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any discussion of the types of errors made by the classifiers. Understanding *where* the model fails would provide valuable insights and guide future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Baseline Performance:** Please provide a detailed comparison against simpler baseline models (e.g., logistic regression, SVM) trained *solely* on the linguistic and statistical features, without any network-based features. Include performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score) for these baselines.
2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the performance to the cosine similarity threshold used for adding edges to the network? Please present results with different threshold values.
3. **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to demonstrate that the improvements achieved by including network features are statistically significant, and not simply due to chance.
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide an analysis of the types of errors made by the best-performing model? Are there specific types of linguistic errors that the network features help to identify?
5. **Data Heterogeneity:** How might the different elicitation methods used for the English and Portuguese datasets influence the results?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper’s formatting and presentation are generally clear. While the description of the complex network methodology is adequate, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this approach is expected to be effective for MCI detection would be beneficial. The supplementary material is helpful for providing details on the datasets and features.
