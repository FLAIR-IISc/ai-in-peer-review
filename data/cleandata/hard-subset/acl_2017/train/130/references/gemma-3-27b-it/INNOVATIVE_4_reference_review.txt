This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent language not just as sequences of words, but as a network of interconnected concepts, potentially capturing subtle cognitive changes that simpler methods might miss.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of a novel representation for language data in the context of MCI detection – specifically, enriching word co-occurrence networks with word embeddings. While complex networks have been used in NLP, applying this in conjunction with embeddings to neuropsychological assessment transcripts, and demonstrating its effectiveness, is a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimentation across multiple datasets (English and Portuguese, different elicitation tasks) and a comprehensive comparison of feature types (network topology, linguistic features, bag-of-words).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of complex networks and word embeddings to model speech transcripts for MCI detection is a genuinely interesting and novel idea. This goes beyond simply applying standard NLP techniques and attempts to capture a more nuanced representation of linguistic data. The intuition behind representing semantic relationships through network enrichment is sound.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors performed experiments on three distinct datasets, varying in language and task type. This is a significant strength, demonstrating the robustness (or lack thereof) of their approach across different scenarios.  The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets is especially valuable, acknowledging the language-specific challenges in NLP.
3. **Comparison to Established Methods:**  The inclusion of baseline features (linguistic metrics and bag-of-words) and their comparison to the network-based features provides a valuable benchmark.  The finding that enriched networks often outperform traditional methods is noteworthy.
4. **Practical Focus:** The authors explicitly aim for a fully automated system, acknowledging the challenges of manual preprocessing. This pragmatic approach is commendable and increases the potential for real-world application.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Statistical Significance/Effect Size:** While the results are promising, the reported accuracy improvements are often modest. The paper lacks a thorough statistical analysis of the significance of these improvements – simply reporting accuracies without p-values or confidence intervals makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the findings.  
2. **Embedding Choice and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper uses pre-trained word embeddings without a detailed justification for the chosen model. Further exploration of different embedding models and careful tuning of the cosine similarity threshold used for network enrichment could potentially improve performance. The lack of sensitivity analysis is a concern.
3. **Data Imbalance & Generalizability:** The dataset sizes are relatively small, and some datasets exhibit imbalances between control and MCI groups. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings and the potential for overfitting. A more detailed discussion of these limitations is needed.
4. **Interpretability:** While the method demonstrates predictive power, it remains somewhat of a “black box”. Exploring which network features are most informative for distinguishing between MCI and control groups could provide valuable insights into the underlying cognitive mechanisms.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) to support the claimed improvements in accuracy?
2. What impact does the choice of word embedding model have on the performance of your system? Have you experimented with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, fastText)?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your system to the cosine similarity threshold used for enriching the networks? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis on this parameter?
4.  Can you provide some insight into *which* network features (e.g., degree, betweenness centrality) are most strongly correlated with MCI status? This would help us understand *why* the method works.



I recommend acceptance, despite the noted weaknesses. The novelty of the approach and the comprehensive evaluation outweigh the methodological shortcomings. The paper introduces a potentially valuable new direction for research in automated MCI detection, and the exploration of network-based representations is a worthwhile contribution. The authors should address the questions raised in the review during the rebuttal phase to strengthen the paper.
