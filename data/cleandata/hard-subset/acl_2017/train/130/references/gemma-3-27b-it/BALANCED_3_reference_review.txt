This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea – representing transcripts as networks and leveraging semantic information via embeddings – is a promising approach to tackle challenges posed by disfluencies and the need for extensive manual preprocessing in traditional NLP pipelines for this task. The authors evaluate their method on multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) and demonstrate competitive performance, especially when combining network features with other linguistic and statistical features.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Network Enrichment:** The primary contribution lies in applying word embedding enrichment to complex network representations of speech transcripts for MCI detection. While complex networks have been used in NLP, the combination with embeddings specifically tailored to the noisy nature of neuropsychological assessments represents a significant step.
2. **Multi-Dataset Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets – two English (Cookie Theft, Cinderella) and one Portuguese (ABCD) – strengthens the findings and suggests generalizability. The inclusion of a Portuguese dataset is particularly valuable given the relative scarcity of resources for languages other than English in this domain.
3. **Feature Combination Analysis:** The systematic exploration of feature combinations (network features, linguistic features, BoW) and classification algorithms provides valuable insights into the most effective approaches for MCI detection from speech. This nuanced analysis identifies the strengths and limitations of different feature sets and algorithms.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** MCI detection is a crucial task with significant clinical implications. The automation of this process through NLP techniques offers the potential for early diagnosis and intervention.
2. **Robustness to Noise:** The authors explicitly acknowledge and attempt to address the challenges of noisy speech transcripts (disfluencies) through the network-based approach and avoidance of extensive manual preprocessing. This is a critical consideration for real-world applicability.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The use of 5-fold cross-validation and multiple classification algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes) provides a robust evaluation framework. The comparisons across datasets further enhance the credibility of the results.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, experiments, and results. The inclusion of supplementary material (example transcript, Coh-Metrix details) enhances reproducibility.
5. **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison with baseline methods and different feature combinations provides a valuable benchmark for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Construction:** The basic word co-occurrence network is a relatively standard approach. While the *enrichment* with embeddings is novel, the underlying network construction itself lacks significant innovation. More sophisticated network modeling techniques (e.g., incorporating syntactic dependencies) could potentially yield further improvements.
2. **Embedding Details:** The description of the word embedding training process is somewhat brief. More details about the corpus used for training, the embedding dimensions, and the training parameters would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding the impact of embedding quality. Specifically, stating the size of the corpus used to train the word embeddings would be helpful.
3. **Dataset Imbalance:** The datasets exhibit varying degrees of class imbalance (MCI vs. control). While this is common in medical datasets, the paper does not explicitly address the potential impact of this imbalance on the results or employ techniques to mitigate it (e.g., weighted classifiers, resampling).
4. **Statistical Significance:** The results are presented as classification accuracy, but statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) are not reported to demonstrate whether the observed improvements are statistically significant.
5. **Interpretability:** While the paper identifies the best-performing feature combinations, it provides limited insight into *why* these features are effective. Exploring the most informative network properties or linguistic features could shed light on the underlying cognitive mechanisms associated with MCI.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the corpus used to train the word embeddings, including its size and characteristics?
2.  How did you address the potential impact of class imbalance in the datasets on the reported results? Did you consider using techniques like weighted classifiers or resampling methods?
3.  Did you perform any statistical significance tests to confirm whether the observed improvements in classification accuracy are statistically significant?
4.  Could you elaborate on the specific network properties (e.g., centrality measures) or linguistic features that were most strongly associated with MCI detection?
5.  Were any error analyses conducted to identify specific types of errors made by the model, and to understand the limitations of the approach?

**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be beneficial to compare the performance of the proposed method with state-of-the-art approaches specifically designed for MCI detection from speech, beyond the baselines presented in the paper.
*   Exploring different thresholds for the cosine similarity used to create edges in the enriched network could further optimize performance.
*   Consider exploring the use of dynamic networks, which capture changes in network structure over time, as a way to model the progression of MCI.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a novel and promising approach to MCI detection from speech transcripts. The strengths of the work – its real-world relevance, robustness to noise, thorough evaluation, and clear presentation – outweigh the weaknesses. While there are areas for improvement, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
