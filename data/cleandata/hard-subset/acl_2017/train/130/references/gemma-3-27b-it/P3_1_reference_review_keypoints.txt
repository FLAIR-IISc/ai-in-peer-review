- Paper proposes using complex networks enriched with word embeddings to detect MCI from speech transcripts and compares this to linguistic features and BoW across datasets.  
- Core idea: leverage structural information from complex networks plus semantic info from embeddings to improve MCI detection.  

Main contributions:
- Applies complex network representations to MCI detection from speech transcripts (novel perspective).  
- Evaluates method across three datasets (two English, one Portuguese) to test generalizability.  
- Systematically compares network features, linguistic features, and BoW, and their combinations with multiple classifiers.  

Strengths:
- Novel application of network+embedding approach to MCI detection.  
- Rigorous experimental setup: 5-fold CV and multiple classifiers (SVM, KNN, RF, NB) with linear and RBF SVM kernels.  
- Minimizes manual preprocessing to handle noisy, disfluent transcripts (real-world applicability).  
- Clear, well-organized presentation of results and identification of best-performing combinations.  
- Inclusion of a Portuguese dataset broadens linguistic scope and impact.  

Weaknesses:
- No statistical significance testing reported to confirm observed improvements (p-values missing).  
- Insufficient justification or comparison for using character-level embeddings over Word2Vec/GloVe.  
- No sensitivity analysis for key network construction parameters (e.g., cosine similarity threshold).  
- Limited explainability: no analysis of which network features drive MCI detection (feature importance).  
- Potential class imbalance not addressed; only accuracy reported without precision/recall/F1.  

Questions for authors:
- Please provide statistical significance tests to support performance claims.  
- Why choose character-level embeddings (Bojanowski et al., 2016); were other embeddings tried?  
- How sensitive are results to the cosine similarity threshold used to add edges?  
- Which network topological features correlate most strongly with MCI?  
- Can you report precision, recall, and F1 to address possible class imbalance?  

Additional comments:
- References and related work are comprehensive and well-formatted.  
- Recommend discussing study limitations and future directions, e.g., incorporating temporal speech information.
