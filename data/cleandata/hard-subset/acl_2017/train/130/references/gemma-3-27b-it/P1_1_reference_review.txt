## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of complex network models, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare the performance of this approach against traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, utilizing several machine learning classifiers on three datasets (English and Portuguese). They demonstrate that network-based features, particularly when enriched with embeddings, can achieve competitive and in some cases superior performance in distinguishing between control participants and those with MCI, and that combining features yields further improvements.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The paper pioneers the application of complex network analysis, specifically word adjacency networks enhanced with word embeddings, to the problem of MCI detection from speech. This is a relatively unexplored avenue in this field, offering a novel approach beyond traditional linguistic feature engineering.
2. **Cross-lingual Evaluation:** The study evaluates the approach on both English and Portuguese datasets, demonstrating the potential generalizability of the method beyond language-specific linguistic resources.
3. **Empirical Comparison & Feature Combination:** The comprehensive comparison against baseline methods (linguistic features, BoW) and the exploration of feature combinations provide valuable insights into the relative strengths of different approaches for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Utilizing complex network models for MCI detection is a fresh perspective.  The combination with word embeddings addresses a key limitation of standard network analysis – the lack of semantic information – and the resulting improvement in performance validates this approach.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors employ multiple datasets, various classifiers, and robust cross-validation techniques.  The inclusion of both automatically segmented (ABCD) and manually transcribed data is a strength, allowing for assessment of robustness.
3. **Clear Presentation of Results:** Tables clearly present the performance of different feature sets and classifiers, enabling easy comparison and interpretation of the findings. The inclusion of ablation studies (comparing CN vs. CNE) is also helpful.
4. **Practical Relevance:**  The goal of automated MCI detection is clinically significant, as early diagnosis allows for intervention. The work towards a fully automated system (as highlighted in the dataset descriptions) is commendable.
5. **Detailed Dataset Descriptions:** The detailed description of the datasets, including participant demographics and data collection procedures, enhances the reproducibility of the study and provides valuable context for interpreting the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Network Properties:** While the paper reports performance gains using network features, it lacks a deeper analysis of *why* these features are effective. Investigating which network properties (e.g., centrality measures, clustering coefficient) are most strongly correlated with MCI status could provide valuable insights and guide future research.  The results mostly present accuracy numbers, without a discussion on which features are the most predictive.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:**  The use of pre-trained word embeddings (Bojanowski et al., 2016) is a common practice, but the impact of the embedding quality and potential biases on the results is not thoroughly discussed. Exploring training embeddings specifically on the target domain (speech transcripts from neuropsychological assessments) could potentially improve performance.
3. **Disfluency Handling:** While the authors acknowledge the presence of disfluencies, the preprocessing strategy is relatively simple (removing filled pauses, short false starts, repetitions). More sophisticated disfluency detection and removal/handling techniques could potentially improve the quality of the input data and enhance the performance of the models.  The different approaches between datasets (manual revision vs. automatic segmentation) impacts the results, and it would be useful to have more discussion about these differences.
4. **Statistical Significance:** The reported results are primarily based on accuracy. Reporting statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests) would strengthen the conclusions and provide a more rigorous assessment of the observed performance differences.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the network features that are most predictive of MCI status? Are certain centrality measures or other network properties consistently associated with differences between control and MCI groups?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with training embeddings specifically on your target dataset, and if so, what impact did this have on performance?
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind your disfluency removal strategy? Have you considered more sophisticated disfluency detection and handling techniques, and if so, why were they not implemented in this study?
4.  Could you perform statistical significance testing on the accuracy differences reported in the tables? This would provide a more robust assessment of the observed performance gains.
5.  Dataset sizes are relatively small. How do you anticipate performance scaling with larger datasets, and what steps would you take to mitigate potential overfitting?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but some references (e.g., Aluı́sio et al., 2016) are displayed incorrectly.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential ethical implications related to automated MCI diagnosis, such as the need for careful validation and responsible implementation of the technology.



Overall, this is a solid and promising contribution to the field of automated MCI detection. The novelty of the approach, rigorous experimental setup, and clear presentation of results make it a valuable addition to the literature. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the reviewer’s concerns.
