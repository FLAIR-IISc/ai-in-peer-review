## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts"

**Summary:**

This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts as networks, leveraging both word co-occurrence and semantic similarity via word embeddings, and then use these network-derived features in conjunction with traditional linguistic and statistical features to classify individuals with and without MCI. They evaluate their approach on three datasets – English (Cookie Theft), Brazilian Portuguese (Cinderella), and another Portuguese dataset (ABCD) – using various machine learning algorithms.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The core contribution lies in applying complex network analysis, a relatively unexplored method in this domain, to the problem of MCI detection from speech. This offers a potentially novel perspective compared to more conventional linguistic feature engineering approaches.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets (English and two Portuguese datasets) which increases the robustness of the findings and demonstrates some generalizability.  The inclusion of both manually transcribed and automatically segmented data is valuable.
3. **Demonstration of Feature Combination Benefits:** The work demonstrates that combining network features with traditional features improves classification accuracy, suggesting a complementary relationship between these approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The use of complex networks for representing speech transcripts and leveraging network properties for MCI detection represents a genuinely novel application within the field.  It moves beyond direct feature extraction and offers a more holistic representation of language patterns.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The inclusion of topological network features *alongside* linguistic features and bag-of-words representations provides a rich feature space, enabling the classifier to potentially capture diverse aspects of language impairment.
3. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of 5-fold cross-validation provides a robust evaluation methodology. The comparison to baseline methods (traditional features, and network features alone) appropriately contextualizes the contribution of the proposed approach.
4. **Multi-Lingual Evaluation:** Evaluating the approach on both English and Portuguese datasets is a significant strength, acknowledging the importance of language-specific nuances in cognitive assessment.
5. **Practical Focus:** The authors’ explicit goal of creating a fully automated system is commendable, and the attempt to reduce reliance on manual preprocessing steps is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Enrichment:** While applying complex networks to MCI detection *is* novel, the specific method of enriching the network with word embeddings using cosine similarity is a fairly standard technique in network-based NLP.  The authors don't present any novelties in *how* the embeddings are integrated.
2. **Lack of Deep Linguistic Analysis:**  The paper relies heavily on pre-existing tools like Coh-Metrix. While practical, this limits the ability to directly link specific network properties to underlying linguistic impairments associated with MCI. A deeper dive into *why* certain network features are predictive would strengthen the analysis.
3. **Dataset Imbalance and Size:** The datasets are relatively small, and the number of participants with MCI is often lower than the control group. This imbalance could impact the reliability of the results, and the limited sample size restricts the generalizability of the findings. The authors acknowledge this implicitly but do not perform a robust analysis of its impact.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning & Reporting:** The statement that the threshold parameter was optimized with "best values being 0.7 in Cookie Theft dataset and 0.4 in both Cinderella and ABCD dataset" feels ad-hoc and lacks justification. A more systematic approach to hyperparameter tuning and a clear explanation of the criteria used to select these values are needed.
5. **Interpretability of Network Features:** While the results show that network features *improve* performance, the paper doesn’t offer much insight into *which* specific network properties are most indicative of MCI.  More analysis is needed to understand the biological or cognitive relevance of these features.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Justification for Threshold Values:**  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the threshold values (0.7 and 0.4) were determined for the different datasets? Was this based on a validation set, a grid search, or another method?
2. **Ablation Study of Embedding Method:**  Did you experiment with different methods for incorporating word embeddings into the network (e.g., using different similarity metrics, or different embedding models)? If so, how did these alternatives compare to the cosine similarity approach?
3. **Feature Importance Analysis:** What feature importance analysis did you perform to identify the most influential network properties for MCI detection?  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of which network metrics are most predictive?
4. **Statistical Significance:** While accuracy scores are reported, were statistical significance tests performed to determine whether the improvements achieved by combining features were statistically significant?
5. **Impact of Disfluency Handling:** How sensitive are the results to the handling (or lack thereof) of disfluencies in the different datasets? Did you investigate the effect of explicitly removing disfluencies on network feature extraction?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful.
*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized, although some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   The literature review is adequate but could be more focused on recent advances in complex network analysis for biomedical applications.
