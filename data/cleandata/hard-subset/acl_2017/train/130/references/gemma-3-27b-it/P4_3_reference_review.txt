This paper investigates the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model transcripts as networks, enhance them with semantic information from word embeddings, and then employ machine learning classifiers to distinguish between control participants and those with MCI.

The primary contribution is the exploration of complex network representations, *enhanced with word embeddings*, as a feature set for MCI detection from speech. While complex networks have been applied in NLP, applying them to neuropsychological assessments and specifically enriching them with word embeddings to address the challenges of short, potentially disfluent transcripts is a novel combination. A second contribution lies in the comparative evaluation of this network-based approach alongside traditional linguistic and statistical features. This provides a useful benchmark and highlights the potential benefits of the proposed method. Finally, the authors evaluate their method on both English and Portuguese datasets, which broadens the applicability of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of complex network analysis with word embeddings for the specific task of MCI detection from speech transcripts represents a novel approach. The authors effectively leverage graph theory to model linguistic data in a way that potentially captures semantic relationships missed by traditional methods.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:**  The systematic comparison of complex network features with linguistic features and bag-of-words representations is a strong point. This allows for a clear assessment of the value added by the proposed network-based approach, demonstrating its competitive performance and, in some cases, superiority.
3. **Multi-Lingual Evaluation:** The inclusion of both English and Portuguese datasets is a valuable strength. This demonstrates the potential generalizability of the method beyond a single language and highlights the challenges of applying NLP techniques to different linguistic contexts.
4. **Addressing Data Challenges:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to address the specific challenges of using speech transcripts from neuropsychological evaluations, such as the presence of disfluencies. The decision to forego lemmatization, motivated by the potential for introducing noise in these types of transcripts, is a sensible one.
5. **Rigorous Experimental Design:** The use of 5-fold cross-validation and the evaluation of multiple machine learning algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes) provide a robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement over Baseline:** While the network-based features perform competitively, the improvements over traditional methods, particularly BoW and linguistic features, are often marginal. The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate *why* the network approach offers limited gain in this context, or under what specific conditions it might be more effective.  Further analysis is needed to explain these results.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not detail the tuning of parameters within the word embedding model (e.g., embedding dimension, window size) or the network construction (e.g., similarity threshold). The performance of the method could be significantly impacted by these choices, and a more detailed discussion of the parameter selection process is warranted.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A thorough error analysis is missing. Identifying the types of errors the network-based approach makes (e.g., misclassifying specific types of linguistic patterns) would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.
4. **Dataset Imbalance:**  The datasets exhibit imbalances in the number of participants in each diagnostic group. While this is common in medical datasets, the paper does not explicitly address the potential impact of this imbalance on the results or employ strategies to mitigate it (e.g., weighted classification).
5. **Limited Discussion of Network Interpretability:** While the paper demonstrates the predictive power of the network features, it does not explore the *interpretability* of these features. Understanding which network properties (e.g., centrality measures, clustering coefficient) are most strongly associated with MCI could provide valuable insights into the underlying cognitive processes.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed discussion of the parameter tuning process for the word embedding model and the network construction? Specifically, how was the similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings determined?
2.  What types of errors does the network-based approach make most frequently?  Are there specific linguistic patterns or characteristics of the speech that lead to misclassifications?
3.  Given the marginal improvements over baseline methods, under what specific conditions (e.g., transcript length, severity of MCI, language characteristics) do you anticipate the network-based approach to be most effective?
4.  How did you address the potential impact of dataset imbalance on the classification performance?
5.  Could you elaborate on the potential interpretability of the network features?  Are there any network properties that correlate with specific cognitive deficits associated with MCI?
