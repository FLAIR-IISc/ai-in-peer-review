This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea – modeling language data as networks to capture relationships beyond simple word counts – is promising, and the authors explore this approach in both English and Portuguese datasets. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the feasibility and effectiveness of this network-based approach for MCI detection, achieving competitive results compared to traditional linguistic features. A secondary contribution is the exploration of feature combinations (networks, linguistic features, bag-of-words) and classification algorithms, identifying optimal configurations for the datasets studied. Finally, the paper provides a valuable comparison across datasets and languages, highlighting the challenges and potential of this method in a real-world context.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Utilizing complex networks with word embeddings to represent speech transcripts for MCI detection is a relatively novel approach within this field. Most existing work relies on more traditional feature engineering and machine learning techniques. This exploration offers a different perspective on capturing linguistic nuances indicative of cognitive decline.
2. **Multi-Dataset and Cross-Lingual Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on three distinct datasets (Cookie Theft, Cinderella, ABCD) in both English and Portuguese. This broad evaluation provides stronger evidence for the generalizability of the method and highlights potential language-specific differences. The inclusion of a manually processed Portuguese dataset offers valuable insight into the importance of data cleaning and preprocessing.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Exploration:** The authors don’t simply present their network-based approach in isolation. They systematically compare it against established linguistic features and a bag-of-words representation, both individually and in combination. This thorough exploration allows for a nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each feature set. The investigation of ensemble and multi-view learning is also commendable.
4. **Clear Presentation of Methodology:** The paper clearly explains the construction of the complex networks, the use of word embeddings, and the chosen network metrics. The descriptions of the datasets and preprocessing steps are also well-defined, enabling reproducibility of the work.
5. **Competitive Results:** The reported classification accuracies are competitive with existing studies in the field, particularly when combining network features with other modalities. The improvement achieved with multi-view learning further strengthens the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Network Topology Insights:** While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the network-based features, they provide limited qualitative interpretation of *why* certain network properties are indicative of MCI. Exploring specific topological differences between networks built from healthy controls and MCI patients could yield valuable insights into the underlying cognitive mechanisms. For example, are networks from MCI patients less dense, more clustered, or have different centrality distributions?
2. **Dependency on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings (Bojanowski et al., 2016) introduces a potential bias, as these embeddings were not specifically trained on speech transcripts from individuals with cognitive impairment. Investigating the impact of training task-specific embeddings could further improve performance.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Reporting:** While accuracy scores are presented, the paper lacks statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values) to determine whether the observed differences between models are statistically meaningful. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the findings.
4. **Dataset Imbalance:** The datasets exhibit varying degrees of imbalance between control and MCI groups. The impact of this imbalance on the results is not explicitly addressed, and techniques like stratified cross-validation or cost-sensitive learning could have been employed.
5. **Limited Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study could help pinpoint the specific contributions of different components of the pipeline. For instance, how much does the cosine similarity threshold for enriching the network impact performance? A systematic exploration of these parameters would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the network topology differences between MCI and control groups? Are there specific network metrics that consistently differentiate the two groups?
2. Did you experiment with training word embeddings specifically on speech transcripts from individuals with and without cognitive impairment? If so, what was the impact on performance?
3. Can you provide p-values or other statistical measures to assess the significance of the differences in accuracy between the different models and feature combinations?
4. How did you address the potential impact of dataset imbalance on the results?
5. What is the computational cost of constructing and analyzing the complex networks, and how does it compare to traditional feature engineering approaches?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   Consider providing a visualization of example networks constructed from healthy control and MCI transcripts to illustrate the potential topological differences.
*   The supplementary material is helpful for providing details on the datasets and features.



Overall, this is a well-executed study with a promising approach to MCI detection. While there are areas for improvement, the work makes a valuable contribution to the field and lays the foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
