This paper investigates the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors model narratives and descriptions as networks and explore the utility of topological features extracted from these networks, combined with traditional linguistic features, for classification.

The main contributions of this work are: 1) Exploration of complex network-based features for MCI detection from speech transcripts, a novel approach in this domain; 2) Demonstration that enriching the networks with word embeddings improves performance compared to using solely co-occurrence networks; 3) Comprehensive evaluation on multiple datasets (English and Portuguese) using several machine learning algorithms, showing competitive results and identifying effective feature combinations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Utilizing complex networks to represent speech transcripts for MCI detection is a significant departure from traditional linguistic feature engineering. This offers a potentially more robust and flexible approach, less reliant on handcrafted rules or perfectly parsed text, which is particularly beneficial given the challenges of transcribing and processing spontaneous speech from individuals with cognitive impairment.

2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation across three datasets, encompassing both English and Portuguese languages. This diversity strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The use of multiple machine learning algorithms and feature combinations provides a nuanced understanding of the method’s performance.

3. **Performance Competitiveness:** The reported results are competitive with existing methods in the field, particularly when combining network-based features with traditional linguistic metrics. Achieving comparable or better accuracy demonstrates the potential of the proposed approach. The exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques further improves performance.

4. **Addressing a Practical Challenge:** The authors explicitly address the difficulty of working with speech transcripts containing disfluencies and their decision to avoid extensive preprocessing is sensible, making the approach more practical for real-world application.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Enrichment:** While the paper mentions enriching the networks with word embeddings based on cosine similarity, it lacks a detailed explanation of *why* this enrichment is beneficial in the context of MCI detection. The intuition behind this step isn’t clearly articulated. A more detailed analysis of how the added edges capture semantic relationships relevant to cognitive impairment would strengthen the paper.

2. **Feature Selection Justification:** The selection of topological features (PageRank, Betweenness, etc.) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more principled feature selection process, potentially using feature importance analysis from the machine learning models, could identify the most discriminative features and streamline the model.

3. **Dataset Characteristics & Imbalance:** The datasets used have varying sizes and potential imbalances between control and MCI groups. While acknowledged, a more detailed discussion of the impact of these factors on the results is needed. It's unclear if the observed performance differences are due to the method itself or variations in the data.  The datasets also represent specific elicitation protocols (Cookie Theft, Cinderella story, ABCD battery), raising questions about generalizability to other types of spontaneous speech.

4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions optimizing the threshold parameter but lacks details on the specific method used. Providing more specifics about the hyperparameter tuning process would enhance reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* enriching the co-occurrence networks with word embeddings is expected to be beneficial for MCI detection? What specific semantic relationships are captured by the added edges that are relevant to cognitive impairment?

2.  What methodology was used to determine the optimal threshold parameter for each dataset?  Please provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process.

3.  Given the different elicitation protocols used for each dataset, how confident are you in the generalizability of the findings across different types of spontaneous speech? Have you considered any methods to mitigate potential biases introduced by the specific tasks?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful, providing details on the datasets and features used.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the enriched network compared to a standard co-occurrence network to illustrate the impact of the embedding process.
*   A brief discussion of the limitations of the study and potential avenues for future research would be valuable.
