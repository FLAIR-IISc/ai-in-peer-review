This paper explores the application of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to represent text as a network where nodes are words and edges represent co-occurrence, subsequently enhancing this network with semantic relationships captured by word embeddings. The authors evaluate this approach on English and Portuguese datasets, comparing its performance to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of a network-based approach, augmented with word embeddings, as a feature extraction method for MCI detection from spoken language. While network representations of text aren't entirely novel, applying them specifically to this clinical task, and combining them with embeddings, constitutes a reasonable investigation. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the comparison across both English and Portuguese datasets. The inclusion of a Portuguese dataset is a positive aspect, given the limited resources available for NLP in that language.

**Strengths:**

1. **Exploration of a potentially promising feature representation:** The use of complex networks provides an alternative to traditional feature engineering methods. The inclusion of word embeddings intuitively enhances the network with semantic information, addressing a potential limitation of purely co-occurrence based approaches.
2. **Cross-lingual Evaluation:** The evaluation on both English and Portuguese datasets strengthens the findings, suggesting the approach may be generalizable beyond a single language. This is particularly significant for the Portuguese dataset, which represents a lower-resource language.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:** The authors provide a reasonably comprehensive comparison with established baseline features (linguistic features and bag-of-words), offering a benchmark for the proposed method's performance.
4. **Clear Presentation of Methodology:** The description of the network construction and enrichment process is clear and well-defined, allowing for potential replication.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Method:** The use of complex networks for text analysis is well-established. The addition of word embeddings, while sensible, doesn't represent a fundamentally new breakthrough. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight *why* this approach would be particularly well-suited for the challenges of MCI detection compared to existing methods.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Word Embedding Implementation:** While the authors mention using a character-level embedding method (Bojanowski et al., 2016), there’s a lack of detail regarding hyperparameter settings, pre-training data, and the specific embedding dimension. This makes it difficult to assess the impact of this particular choice.
3. **Inconsistent Dataset Preprocessing:** The differences in preprocessing between the English and Portuguese datasets (manual revision vs. automatic segmentation and disfluency removal) introduce potential biases and complicate cross-lingual comparisons. The justification for these differences is not sufficiently strong. The handling of disfluencies *specifically* is problematic, as the authors acknowledge their prevalence in neuropsychological assessments but remove them in some datasets.
4. **Overly Optimistic Results and Limited Statistical Rigor:** The accuracy gains reported, especially with the ensemble methods, appear somewhat inflated. The statistical significance of these improvements is not adequately addressed. While 5-fold cross-validation is used, reporting confidence intervals or p-values would strengthen the conclusions. The choice of threshold (0.7 and 0.4) seems arbitrary and lacks justification.
5. **Minor Formatting and Writing Issues:** Several instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors detract from the overall clarity of the paper. The reference list also contains inconsistencies in formatting. For example, some citations include "\Q...\E" tags, which seem extraneous.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of 0.7 and 0.4 as thresholds for classification? How sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?
2.  Why was manual revision of transcripts only applied to the Cinderella dataset, and not to the Cookie Theft or ABCD datasets? How might this difference in preprocessing affect the comparative results?
3.  Please provide specific details about the implementation of the word embeddings, including the size of the embedding dimension, training data, and hyperparameters used.
4.  Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to demonstrate that the performance improvements achieved by the proposed method are statistically significant?
5.  Why was the character n-gram embedding approach chosen over more traditional pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The Supplementary Material is helpful, but could be more clearly organized.
*   The sentence "This approach has been successful to several areas of NLP…" in the Introduction feels awkwardly worded.
*   Please carefully review the manuscript for grammatical errors and inconsistencies in formatting.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable, but not exceptionally strong, contribution. The weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of significant novelty, the inconsistencies in preprocessing, and the limited statistical rigor, prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing these concerns.
