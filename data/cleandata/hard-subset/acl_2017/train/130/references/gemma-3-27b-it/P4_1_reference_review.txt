This paper investigates the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea is to model narratives and descriptions as complex networks where nodes represent words and edges represent co-occurrence, enhanced with semantic similarity based on word embeddings. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets—two English and one Portuguese—comparing the performance of network-based features to traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations, both individually and in combination.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the application and evaluation of complex network representations, enriched with word embeddings, to the specific task of MCI detection from spoken language. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their adaptation to this specific clinical application and the demonstration of their competitiveness with established linguistic methods represent a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution is the comparison across different datasets (English and Portuguese) and feature sets, providing insights into the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed approach. A third, less prominent, contribution is the publicly available datasets utilized, supporting reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Applying complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to the task of MCI detection from speech transcripts is a novel approach. The authors effectively leverage graph theory to capture relationships between words in a way that traditional feature engineering might miss, especially when dealing with the disfluencies inherent in clinical speech. This differentiates the work from many existing studies focused on purely linguistic or acoustic features.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a fairly comprehensive evaluation across three datasets, encompassing both English and Portuguese language samples. This demonstrates the potential for cross-lingual application and offers a more robust assessment of the proposed method's performance.  The use of 5-fold cross-validation further strengthens the validity of the results.
3. **Comparison with Established Methods:** The authors thoughtfully compare the performance of their complex network features against established baseline features, including traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations. This allows for a clear understanding of the added value of the proposed approach.  The exploration of feature combinations (multi-view learning) provides further insight into the complementary nature of different feature types.
4. **Addressing Data Challenges:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to address the challenges posed by the nature of speech transcripts used in neuropsychological evaluations, specifically the presence of disfluencies. Their decision to avoid lemmatization and to retain some disfluencies is reasonable, given the potential for introducing noise with preprocessing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Parameter Choices:** The paper lacks a detailed justification for the specific parameters used in the construction of the complex networks and the word embedding model (e.g., embedding dimension, similarity threshold). The chosen parameters are mentioned but not rigorously explored or justified.  A sensitivity analysis, even limited, could strengthen the work.
2. **Modest Performance Gains:** While the proposed method demonstrates competitive performance, the improvements over baseline methods are often modest.  The authors should discuss the practical significance of these improvements in a clinical context.  Are these gains large enough to be meaningful for diagnostic accuracy?
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:**  The results presented in the tables show accuracy scores, but there is no indication of statistical significance testing to determine whether the observed differences between methods are statistically reliable. This makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the superiority of one approach over another.
4. **Insufficient Detail on Preprocessing:** While the authors mention removing some disfluencies, the specific criteria and methods used for this process are not fully detailed. This lack of transparency could hinder reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the rationale behind the chosen parameters for constructing the complex networks (e.g., similarity threshold for adding edges based on word embeddings)? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of these parameters on performance?
2.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to support the claims of performance differences between the proposed method and the baselines?
3.  Can you elaborate on the specific rules and methods used for removing disfluencies from the transcripts?
4.  Given the modest performance gains, how do you envision this method being integrated into a clinical workflow? What are the potential benefits and limitations in a real-world diagnostic setting?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material is helpful in providing details about the datasets and features used.
*   The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but could benefit from more recent references on the application of graph neural networks to NLP tasks.
*   Consider discussing the computational cost of constructing and analyzing the complex networks, especially for large datasets.
