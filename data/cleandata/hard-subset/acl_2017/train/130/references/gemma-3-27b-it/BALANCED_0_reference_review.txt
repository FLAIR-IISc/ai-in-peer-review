This paper investigates the use of complex networks enriched with word embeddings for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The core idea – leveraging network analysis to capture subtle linguistic changes indicative of cognitive decline – is promising, given the challenges of traditional feature engineering in this domain. The authors compare performance across datasets (English and Portuguese) and explore combinations of network-based features with linguistic and bag-of-words representations.

**Contributions:**

1. **Application of enriched complex networks to MCI detection:** The primary contribution is the novel application of complex network analysis, enriched with word embeddings, to the problem of differentiating individuals with MCI from healthy controls based on their speech transcripts. While complex networks have been used in NLP, their specific adaptation and evaluation in the context of MCI detection, particularly with the embedding enrichment, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Cross-lingual evaluation:** The evaluation across both English and Portuguese datasets strengthens the generalizability of the approach.  The inclusion of a Portuguese dataset is particularly noteworthy, as resources for NLP in Portuguese are often scarcer than for English.
3. **Comprehensive feature comparison and combination:** The systematic comparison of network-based features with traditional linguistic and bag-of-words features, and the exploration of combined approaches, provide useful insights into the complementary strengths of different feature representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of early MCI detection and the potential of language analysis as a non-invasive diagnostic tool. The motivation for exploring network-based approaches, addressing limitations of traditional methods, is well-established.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:**  The use of multiple datasets, cross-validation, and a range of classification algorithms (SVM, KNN, RF, Naive Bayes) demonstrates a commitment to robust evaluation. The exploration of ensemble and multi-view learning techniques further strengthens the experimental design.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering & Description:** The paper provides a thorough explanation of the feature extraction process, including the network construction, word embedding integration, and linguistic feature selection. The inclusion of the specific Coh-Metrix features used enhances reproducibility.
4. **Positive Results & Insights:** The results show that enriched complex networks perform competitively with, and in some cases outperform, traditional features, particularly when combined.  The observation that network features can be effective even without extensive manual pre-processing is a significant finding.
5. **Supplementary Material:** The inclusion of an example transcription and a detailed list of Coh-Metrix metrics in the supplementary material enhances the transparency and reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Network Approach:** While the application to MCI is novel, the core network construction (word co-occurrence) and the use of pre-trained word embeddings are established techniques. The paper doesn’t demonstrate significant innovation in the *network modeling itself*. A stronger justification for this specific network approach compared to alternatives (e.g., dependency networks, syntactic networks) is needed.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** While the paper presents accuracy scores, it lacks statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) to determine whether the observed differences in performance are statistically meaningful. This makes it difficult to confidently assess the robustness of the findings.
3. **Dataset Imbalance & Size:**  The datasets are relatively small, and some exhibit imbalance between the MCI and control groups. This can bias the results and limit generalizability. A discussion of the potential impact of dataset size and imbalance on the findings is missing.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to understand *why* the model makes mistakes. Identifying the types of linguistic patterns that are difficult to classify could provide valuable insights for future research.  Are there specific disfluencies or semantic features that consistently lead to misclassification?
5. **Word Embedding Details:** While the authors mention using word embeddings, more detail on the specific embeddings used (e.g., pre-trained model, training data, dimensionality) would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Network Model Justification:**  Why was the word co-occurrence network chosen over other potential network representations (e.g., syntactic dependency networks)? What are the specific advantages of this approach for capturing linguistic markers of MCI?
2. **Statistical Significance:** Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to support the reported performance differences between the different feature sets and classifiers?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Have you conducted any error analysis to identify the types of errors the model is making? Are there specific linguistic patterns or features that are consistently misclassified?
4. **Embedding Details:**  Please provide more detail about the word embeddings used, including the pre-trained model, training data, and dimensionality.
5. **Impact of Disfluencies:**  You mention that the model handles disfluencies relatively well. Can you quantify this – for example, by comparing performance on transcripts with and without disfluencies removed?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The inclusion of related work is comprehensive.
*   Consider providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the study and potential avenues for future research.




Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to MCI detection using complex networks. While the novelty of the network model itself is limited, the application to this specific problem, the cross-lingual evaluation, and the comprehensive feature comparison are valuable contributions. Addressing the weaknesses regarding statistical significance, dataset considerations, error analysis, and network model justification would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
