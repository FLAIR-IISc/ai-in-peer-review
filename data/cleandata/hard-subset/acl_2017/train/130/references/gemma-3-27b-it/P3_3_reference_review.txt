## Review for ACL: Enriching Complex Networks with Word Embeddings for Detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment from Speech Transcripts

**Summary:**

This paper explores the use of complex networks, enriched with word embeddings, as a feature extraction method for detecting Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) from speech transcripts. The authors compare the performance of these network-based features with traditional linguistic features and bag-of-words representations across three datasets (English and two Portuguese datasets). They demonstrate that enriched complex networks can achieve competitive performance, and that combining them with other feature types can further improve classification accuracy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of Complex Networks to MCI Detection:** The core contribution is the application of complex network analysis, a relatively unexplored technique in the context of cognitive impairment detection from speech, to the challenging problem of differentiating between cognitively healthy individuals and those with MCI. 
2. **Cross-Lingual Evaluation:** The evaluation of the approach on both English and Portuguese datasets provides a degree of generalizability assessment, crucial for real-world applicability.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:** The thorough comparison of network-based features against well-established NLP and statistical features (linguistic features and BoW) provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** While complex networks have been used in NLP, their application to MCI detection using speech transcripts, particularly in combination with word embeddings, is a novel and promising direction. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional methods (reliance on hand-crafted rules, difficulty handling disfluencies) and offer a potential solution.
2. **Strong Experimental Setup:** The use of three distinct datasets, each with its own characteristics (different languages, elicitation tasks, data quality), strengthens the validity of the findings. The 5-fold cross-validation approach is appropriate for evaluating model performance.
3. **Promising Results:** The results demonstrate that enriched complex networks can achieve competitive accuracy, and in some cases outperform traditional methods. The improvement gained from combining different feature types highlights the potential for building more robust diagnostic tools.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, datasets, and results. The inclusion of supplementary material (dataset details, metrics) enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Explanation of Network Interpretability:** While the paper demonstrates the *effectiveness* of complex networks, it offers limited insight into *why* these networks are effective for MCI detection.  What specific network properties (e.g., clustering coefficient, betweenness centrality) are most indicative of cognitive impairment?  Exploring these aspects would significantly strengthen the contribution.  It feels like a "black box" approach – we know *it works*, but not *how* it works.
2. **Word Embedding Choice & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper uses a specific word embedding method (FastText) with default parameters. The choice and parameter tuning of word embeddings can significantly impact performance.  A brief investigation into the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or hyperparameter settings would be beneficial.  Were other embeddings tested?
3. **Data Imbalance & Dataset Size:** The datasets are relatively small and exhibit some degree of class imbalance (e.g., Cookie Theft dataset). While the 5-fold cross-validation mitigates this to some extent, it’s crucial to acknowledge these limitations and discuss potential biases in the results. A larger, more balanced dataset would provide greater confidence in the findings.
4. **Preprocessing Consistency:** The differing levels of preprocessing across the datasets (manual revision in one Cinderella dataset vs. automatic segmentation in others) introduce potential confounding factors. It's difficult to directly compare results when the input data are processed differently.  More consistent preprocessing or a sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different preprocessing steps would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Interpretability:** Could you provide examples of specific network features (e.g., highly central nodes, densely connected subgraphs) that differentiate between MCI and control participants?  Are there any common linguistic patterns associated with these network characteristics?
2. **Embedding Sensitivity:**  Did you experiment with different word embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) or hyperparameter settings?  If so, what impact did these have on the results?
3. **Data Augmentation:** Have you considered data augmentation techniques to address the class imbalance and limited dataset size?
4. **Preprocessing Impact:** Could you discuss the potential impact of the differing preprocessing steps on the results, particularly when comparing performance across the different datasets? What steps were taken to mitigate these differences?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work in the area of network-based approaches to language analysis for clinical applications (beyond just general NLP).
*   The notation could be made more consistent throughout the paper.
*   The supplementary material is helpful, but could be further organized for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to MCI detection using complex networks. While there are some weaknesses regarding interpretability, sensitivity analysis, and data limitations, the strengths of the work – particularly the novelty of the application, robust experimental setup, and promising results – outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. The work has the potential to contribute significantly to the field of automated cognitive assessment.
