This paper presents SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach for time expression recognition. The authors analyze four datasets, observe patterns in time expressions (short length, small vocabulary, syntactic behavior), and leverage these observations to define syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple heuristic rules. The system is evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The analysis of the four datasets, highlighting the characteristics of time expressions (length, vocabulary size, syntactic patterns) is a valuable contribution. This provides a strong justification for the design choices in SynTime and corroborates Zipf’s principle of least effort in the context of temporal language.
2. **Type-Based Rule Design:** The idea of defining syntactic types for time expression components (rather than semantic types, as in SUTime) and designing rules based on these types and their relative positions is a reasonable approach. This offers a structured way to build and potentially expand a rule-based system.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The significant performance improvements on the Tweets dataset, which is known to be challenging due to its informal nature, is a noteworthy result. It suggests the approach is robust and adaptable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Justification:** The work is well-motivated by the observed characteristics of time expressions. The connection to Zipf's principle is insightful and reinforces the rationale for a lightweight, rule-based approach.
2. **Simplicity and Explainability:** SynTime’s design is relatively simple and transparent compared to complex machine learning models. This makes it easier to understand, debug, and potentially extend. The heuristic rules, while simple, seem effective.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on the benchmark datasets are competitive, and the performance on Tweets is particularly impressive, exceeding the performance of the baselines. The inclusion of both strict and relaxed match evaluations provides a comprehensive assessment.
4. **Efficient Implementation:** The claim that SynTime runs in "real time" is significant. This makes it suitable for applications requiring immediate processing, such as information extraction from live streams.
5. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of the method and experiments are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Rule-Based Approach:** While the *type-based* aspect is a reasonable refinement, the core approach remains a rule-based system. Rule-based systems have inherent limitations in handling unseen patterns or variations in language. The paper doesn't fully articulate how this approach fundamentally addresses the common challenges of rule-based methods (e.g., brittleness, difficulty in generalization).
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The reliance on accurate POS tagging is a potential bottleneck. Errors in POS tagging can propagate and negatively affect the performance of SynTime. This is acknowledged in the limitations section, but the impact is not quantified. A more thorough error analysis, demonstrating the system’s robustness to POS tagging errors, would strengthen the paper.
3. **Expansion Strategy:** While the expansion strategy is presented as a benefit, the small number of added rules (Table 5) raises questions about its practical impact. More details on the criteria for selecting rules to add and the effort required for manual annotation would be helpful. It's unclear how scalable this expansion process is.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes (e.g., false positives, false negatives, specific patterns it struggles with) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
5. **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** Considering the recent advances in neural sequence labeling, a comparison with state-of-the-art neural models for NER, adapted for time expression recognition, would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a quantitative assessment of the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime's performance? (e.g., running SynTime on text with artificially introduced POS errors).
2. Can you elaborate on the criteria used for selecting keywords to add during the expansion phase? What is the typical effort (in terms of time or annotations) required to expand SynTime for a new domain?
3. What types of errors does SynTime commonly make? Could you provide examples of time expressions that SynTime fails to recognize, and explain why?
4. How does the performance of SynTime compare to more recent neural sequence labeling models adapted for time expression recognition?
5. The paper mentions that Gigaword was not used due to potentially inaccurate labels. Could you briefly justify this decision given that it is a widely used dataset in the field?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive.
* The GitHub link for SUTime is helpful.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the regular expressions used for each syntactic type. This would improve transparency and facilitate reproducibility.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of time expression recognition. While the approach is not radically novel, the insightful analysis, type-based rule design, and strong performance on the challenging Tweets dataset make it a worthwhile contribution. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
