This paper presents SynTime, a type-based approach to time expression recognition, motivated by observations about the simplicity of time expressions in four datasets. The core idea is to define syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and then create simple heuristic rules for recognition based on these types. The authors claim contributions in (1) analysis of time expressions revealing adherence to the principle of least effort, (2) the SynTime approach itself, and (3) empirical validation showing outperformance over state-of-the-art methods on benchmark and Twitter datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of time expressions across different datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) is a strong point. The observations—short length, small vocabulary, high token overlap, and consistent syntactic behavior—are well-supported by data and provide a reasonable motivation for the proposed approach. Connecting these observations to Zipf’s principle of least effort provides a compelling rationale.
2. **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The SynTime approach is undeniably simple and lightweight. The reliance on syntactic types and heuristic rules, rather than complex machine learning models, is appealing. The reported real-time performance is a significant advantage, especially for applications requiring immediate processing.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime demonstrably outperforms several established baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The substantial recall improvements, particularly on Tweets, are noteworthy. The ablation study (SynTime-I vs. SynTime-E) provides some insight into the benefits of expansion.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The overview of SynTime (Figure 2) is particularly helpful for understanding the process flow. The explanations of the type definitions and rule design are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the approach is effective, the core idea of using syntactic information for time expression recognition isn't entirely novel. Existing rule-based systems (e.g., SUTime) already leverage syntactic patterns. The main contribution appears to be the specific *way* the types are defined (syntactically rather than semantically) and the use of heuristic boundary expansion, but this distinction isn’t substantial enough to represent a major breakthrough.
2. **Reliance on Existing Tools & Data:** The initialization of SynTime relies heavily on regular expressions extracted from SUTime, potentially inheriting its limitations. The performance gain may stem from the simplification and refinement of SUTime’s rules rather than a fundamentally new approach. Similarly, the use of Stanford POS tagger introduces a dependency and potential for error propagation.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance metrics, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* SynTime fails (types of errors, specific datasets) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, the WikiWars performance is comparable to the baselines; a closer look at the errors on this dataset could reveal weaknesses in handling more complex or domain-specific time expressions.
4. **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper highlights the ease of expansion, the manual annotation required for adding new keywords could become a bottleneck when applying SynTime to new domains or languages with significantly different time expression patterns. The Twitter cluster analysis attempts to mitigate this but needs more in-depth justification of its applicability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the type definitions in SynTime and SUTime, showcasing specifically how the syntactic-based approach differs and leads to performance gains?  Illustrative examples would be particularly helpful.
2. What is the runtime performance (e.g., processing speed in sentences/second) of SynTime compared to the baselines on the different datasets?  Providing more quantitative data on efficiency would strengthen the claims about real-time performance.
3. Can you elaborate on the error analysis? What are the most common types of errors SynTime makes, and why?  Are there specific syntactic patterns or time expressions that consistently cause problems?
4. Regarding scalability, how does the effort required for expansion scale with the size and complexity of the target domain? Have you experimented with semi-supervised or distant supervision techniques to reduce the annotation burden?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive but contains some inconsistencies in formatting.
* The Gigaword dataset was mentioned in the introduction but not used in the experiments. Clarify the reason for this omission.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the datasets themselves. For example, the Twitter dataset is relatively small compared to other available Twitter corpora.
