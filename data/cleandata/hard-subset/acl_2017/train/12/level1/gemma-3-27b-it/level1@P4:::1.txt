This paper presents SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The core idea is to leverage observations about the nature of time expressions – their short length, limited vocabulary, frequent presence of "time tokens," and consistent syntactic behavior – to simplify rule creation. The authors define three syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and employ heuristic rules for recognition. The system is evaluated on three datasets, demonstrating competitive performance and, in some cases, outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The analysis of time expression characteristics across four datasets provides a strong foundation for the proposed approach. The observations regarding length, vocabulary size, and syntactic behavior are well-supported and logically motivate the type-based methodology.
2. **Type-Based Rule Definition:** The introduction of a syntactic type system for time expressions, inspired by part-of-speech tagging, is a key contribution. This approach simplifies rule design by focusing on relationships between types rather than semantic meanings, potentially leading to greater flexibility and maintainability.
3. **Effective and Lightweight System:** SynTime achieves competitive performance on benchmark datasets, even outperforming existing methods on some, while being described as lightweight and running in real-time. This combination of accuracy and efficiency is valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Rationale:** The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the rationale behind the design choices. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort provides a compelling justification for the observed characteristics of time expressions.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons with established baselines on multiple datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets). The inclusion of a Tweet dataset is particularly valuable, as informal text presents unique challenges. The ablation study with SynTime-I and SynTime-E further clarifies the impact of expansion.
3. **Clear Explanation of Methodology:** The description of SynTime's construction and recognition process is clear and easy to follow. The use of Figure 2 to illustrate the overview of the system is effective. The explanation of the heuristic rules and the boundary expansion concept is well-presented.
4. **Emphasis on Practicality:** The authors highlight the ease of expansion of SynTime, making it adaptable to different text types and domains. This practical consideration is a significant strength. The real-time performance further emphasizes its utility in real-world applications.
5. **Competitive Results:** Achieving comparable or superior performance to state-of-the-art methods, especially on the challenging Tweet dataset, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Rule-Based Approach:** While the type-based rule definition is a valuable refinement, the core approach remains rule-based. Rule-based systems generally struggle with unseen variations and require substantial manual effort. The paper doesn't fully address how SynTime overcomes the inherent limitations of rule-based methods beyond the type-based simplification.
2. **Reliance on External Tools:** The dependence on Stanford POS Tagger introduces a potential source of error and limits the system’s portability. Errors in POS tagging can negatively impact the accuracy of SynTime's recognition process.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Specifically, it would be helpful to know *where* SynTime struggles (e.g., complex time phrases, ambiguous cases).
4. **Dataset Bias:** The Tweet dataset is relatively small compared to the other datasets, potentially limiting the generalizability of the results observed on that dataset.
5. **Comparison with more recent methods:** The baseline comparison lacks recent deep-learning-based methods for time expression recognition. Including these would give a more complete picture of the performance of SynTime.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically identifying common types of errors that SynTime makes and the reasons for those errors?
2. How sensitive is SynTime to errors in the POS tagger? Have you experimented with different POS taggers, or considered incorporating POS tagging directly into the SynTime pipeline?
3. Could you elaborate on the process of selecting keywords for SynTime expansion? What criteria are used to ensure that added keywords do not introduce ambiguity?
4. How does the performance of SynTime compare to more recent deep learning-based time expression recognition models?
5. How does the system perform on languages other than English? Does the type-based approach generalize well across different languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive and well-formatted.
*   The description of the Twitter word clusters and their relevance to the work is interesting and strengthens the motivation for the approach.
