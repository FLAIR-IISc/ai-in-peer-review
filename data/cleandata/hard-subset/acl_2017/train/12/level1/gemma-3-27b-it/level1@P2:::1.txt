This paper presents SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The authors motivate their work by analyzing four datasets and observing that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. They define three syntactic types – time token, modifier, and numeral – and develop simple heuristic rules based on these types. The paper reports competitive results on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets), outperforming state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets.

**Contributions:**

1. **Analysis of Time Expression Characteristics:** The dataset analysis revealing the short length, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior of time expressions is a valuable contribution. This analysis provides a strong justification for the proposed type-based approach and highlights the potential for simplification in time expression recognition.
2. **Type-Based Rule Design:** The introduction of a type-based approach, distinguishing it from semantic-based rule design used in prior work (e.g., SUTime), is a significant contribution. This reframing offers a potentially more flexible and extensible framework for rule creation.
3. **Competitive Performance:** SynTime achieves competitive and, in some cases, superior performance compared to strong baselines, particularly on the Tweets dataset. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its applicability to real-world data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated by the detailed analysis of time expression characteristics. The observations convincingly support the design choices made in SynTime.
2. **Clear and Concise Explanation:** The methodology and implementation of SynTime are clearly explained, making it easy to understand the key concepts and design decisions. The distinction between type-based vs. semantic-based approaches is particularly well articulated.
3. **Effective Heuristic Rules:** The use of simple, heuristic rules, coupled with the type-based approach, allows for real-time performance and easy extensibility. This is a practical advantage over more complex machine learning methods.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on three datasets, including the challenging Tweets dataset, provides a good assessment of the generalizability of SynTime. The comparison against strong baselines strengthens the validity of the results.
5. **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of SynTime’s expansion, and the acknowledgement of the limited role of complex machine learning features for time expressions are both insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Rule-Based Approach:** While the *type-based* aspect is novel, the underlying methodology remains a rule-based approach, a well-explored area. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of how SynTime differs fundamentally from existing rule-based systems beyond the type definition, perhaps detailing how the boundary expansion heuristic contributes uniquely.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging:** The reliance on accurate POS tagging is a potential limitation. While acknowledged by the authors, the sensitivity of SynTime to POS tagging errors could be investigated further. Error analysis on cases where POS tagging failures lead to incorrect time expression recognition would be helpful.
3. **Manual Expansion Effort:** The expansion of SynTime relies on manual keyword addition, which, while presented as manageable due to the limited vocabulary, could become a bottleneck when applying the system to new domains. Exploring techniques for automated keyword discovery would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed error analysis. Identifying common error types and providing examples would give further insights into the strengths and weaknesses of SynTime.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of the boundary expansion heuristic compared to other rule expansion techniques?  Are there any cases where this heuristic fails or introduces errors?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime to errors in the POS tagger? Have you explored techniques to mitigate the impact of inaccurate POS tags, such as using more robust taggers or incorporating error correction mechanisms?
3.  For the SynTime-E version, how were the keywords selected for expansion, and what criteria were used to ensure they were “generic”? Would you characterize the process as being prone to bias?
4. Can you provide some example errors made by SynTime on the test datasets and discuss the reasons for these errors?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* Table 4 is a little difficult to read due to the formatting. Consider using bolding or highlighting to more clearly indicate the best results.



Overall, this is a solid and well-presented paper. The analysis of time expression characteristics, the introduction of the type-based approach, and the competitive performance of SynTime make a valuable contribution to the field of temporal information extraction. Despite the limitations, I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance.
