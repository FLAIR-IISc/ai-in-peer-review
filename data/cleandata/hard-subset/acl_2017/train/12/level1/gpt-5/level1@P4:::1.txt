Summary
The paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight rule-based time expression recognizer that groups token-level elements into syntactic types—time tokens, modifiers, and numerals—and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble full expressions. The authors motivate this design via an analysis of four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and a tweets set), observing that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, mostly include an explicit time token, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes and optionally expands it with a small amount of manual additions. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, reporting higher F1 on TimeBank and Tweets and comparable results on WikiWars, with consistently strong recall.

Main contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1) A pragmatic, type-based rule framework with simple, position-aware heuristics (boundary expansion and segment merging) that achieves strong recall and competitive F1 across datasets while remaining lightweight and easily extensible.
2) An empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple corpora supporting design choices (short length, small lexicon, prevalence of time tokens, consistent POS/syntactic behavior).
3) An evaluation demonstrating improvements over established systems on standard datasets (notably in recall and on tweets), and a demonstration that small manual lexicon expansions can adapt the system to domain-specific data.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: Across all datasets and both strict/relaxed metrics, SynTime achieves the best or second-best results in most settings, and consistently the best recall. The large gains on tweets (e.g., strict F1 ~91.7 vs best baseline ~80.4) are particularly compelling and align with the method’s focus on short, token-centric patterns.
- Simple, interpretable rules with low engineering overhead: The boundary-expansion heuristics and segment-merging logic are straightforward and generalize across token types without requiring long, brittle pattern cascades. This provides practical benefits (maintainability, debuggability) over complex fixed-form rule sets.
- Sensible type system aligned with syntactic behavior: Grouping tokens as time tokens, modifiers (prefix/suffix/linkage), and numerals, and using positional constraints, reduces combinatorial rule design. The approach leverages observed syntactic regularities (e.g., POS distributions within TEs) rather than relying solely on semantic categories.
- Evidence of easy extensibility: The SynTime-I vs SynTime-E comparison shows that adding a small number of tokens/modifiers—especially in WikiWars—improves recall notably, supporting the claim that minor, targeted lexicon growth yields measurable gains.
- Broad analysis motivating design: The cross-corpus analysis (length distributions, token vocabulary size, fraction containing time tokens) provides empirical grounding for the approach and helps explain why it excels on tweets and performs robustly elsewhere.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems: While the syntactic type emphasis and boundary-expansion heuristic are cleanly formulated, the overall paradigm (token regexes + local expansion + merging) overlaps with SUTime/HeidelTime’s staged recognition. The primary contribution appears to be a streamlined design rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic idea.
- Dependence on SUTime lexicon and potential evaluation confounds: SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes. It is unclear whether improvements stem mainly from different rule logic or from differences in token coverage after manual expansion. An ablation controlling for identical token resources across systems is missing, complicating claims of methodological superiority.
- Incomplete rigor in empirical analysis and reporting: The corpus observations (e.g., “>93% contain a time token”) are informative but lack statistical testing or error bars. Runtime claims (“real time,” “extremely light-weight”) are not supported by measurements. Some baseline results are taken from prior papers; comparability may be affected by differing tool versions, configurations, or evaluation scripts.
- Limited error analysis and qualitative diagnostics: The paper lacks a thorough breakdown of common error types (e.g., prepositional phrase boundaries in WikiWars, ambiguous tokens like “May,” overlapping/linked ranges), making it hard to assess failure modes or guide future improvements.
- Reliance on POS tagging and tokenization quality: The method gates on POS to disambiguate tokens (e.g., “May”), but the robustness to tagging errors is not quantified. Given the known noisiness in tweets and some news text, the sensitivity to POS errors could impact generalization.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide runtime measurements (e.g., tokens/second) and memory usage, and compare them to SUTime and HeidelTime on the same hardware?
- To isolate the effect of rules vs lexicon, can you report results where SUTime and SynTime use exactly the same token regex inventory (no manual expansion), and each runs its own rule set?
- How are ambiguous capitalized tokens (e.g., sentence-initial “May”) handled beyond POS? Do you use capitalization or local context features?
- For WikiWars, do the gold annotations include prepositions as part of TEs more often than TimeBank? If so, can you quantify the proportion of strict-match errors attributable to prepositions that SynTime intentionally excludes?
- Did you run statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap) on F1 differences with baselines, especially for TimeBank and WikiWars?
- Will you release code, lexicons, and the tweets annotations to facilitate reproducibility and further comparisons?

Additional Comments
- Clarify the exact rule set: a concise, formal specification of the boundary-expansion and segment-merging rules (including precedence and tie-breaking) would improve reproducibility.
- Provide a small, categorized error analysis table (e.g., missed holidays, date ranges with linkages, comma-handling errors) to illuminate weaknesses.
- Some terminology could be tightened: “IN ARTICLE” for “a/an” is potentially confusing; consider “INDEFINITE_ARTICLE.” Ensure consistency in type names and counts.
- The connection to Zipf’s principle is suggestive but reads as speculative; consider framing as an intuition rather than evidence of a causal mechanism.
- Minor typos/grammar throughout; a proofreading pass would improve clarity.
