Summary and contributions
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition grounded in a syntactic type inventory (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The work is motivated by an analysis across four corpora showing that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, almost always include a time token, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior. SynTime is initialized with token regexes (largely from SUTime), adds minimal domain-specific keywords, and is evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A pragmatic, interpretable rule-based recognizer that attains strong recall and competitive-to-SOTA F1, especially on tweets, with real-time speed and minimal engineering.
2) A cross-corpus empirical analysis that distills design principles (shortness, small lexicon, token-centricity, syntactic regularity) and links them to a simple, generalizable typing scheme.
3) A flexible boundary-expansion procedure over syntactic types that unifies many fixed-pattern rules while remaining extensible with small lexicon updates.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and high recall: SynTime achieves best recall on all datasets and top F1 on TimeBank (strict F1 92.09; relaxed 94.96) and Tweets (strict 91.74, +11.37 over best baseline; relaxed 95.87, +6.33). This supports the claim that token-first typing captures most time mentions.
- Simplicity and efficiency: No training, rules are compact and interpretable, and expansion requires few additions (e.g., only 3–16 new tokens across corpora), suggesting low maintenance cost and easy portability.
- Clear methodology and robustness: The three-step pipeline (token identification, segment formation, merging) is transparent, and the system remains competitive on heterogeneous domains (news, Wikipedia, social media).
- Insightful analysis: The corpus study concretely motivates design choices (e.g., prioritizing time tokens; using POS only to disambiguate), increasing credibility of the approach.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: The type inventory and expansion strategy are close to prior rule-based systems; initialization reuses SUTime’s token regexes, blurring what is novel versus inherited.
- Potential evaluation bias: The Tweets corpus was prefiltered by SUTime, which may skew distribution and benefit a token-centric rule set; fairness of comparisons is unclear.
- Limited error analysis/ablations: No ablation on POS usage, boundary rules, or expansion to quantify which components drive gains; no statistical significance testing despite “significant” claims.
- Coverage gaps on descriptive expressions: Strict F1 on WikiWars lags UWTime/HeidelTime, and excluding many prepositions per TimeML reduces recognition of narrative temporal phrases.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations (e.g., without POS filters; without overlap-merging rules; without expansion) and significance tests?
- How sensitive is SynTime to POS tagging errors; does a character-level or casing heuristic recover “May” vs “may” ambiguity better?
- How was fairness ensured given SUTime-derived initialization and SUTime-based tweet prefiltering?
- What are measured runtimes and rule set sizes; will code/resources be released?
- How would the approach extend to normalization and to languages with richer morphology?
