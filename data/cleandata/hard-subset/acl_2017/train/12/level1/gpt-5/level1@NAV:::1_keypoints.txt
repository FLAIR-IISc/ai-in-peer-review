Paper proposes SynTime: a lightweight rule-based time recognizer using a syntactic type inventory (time token, modifier, numeral) and boundary-expansion heuristics, initialized with SUTime regexes and evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

An interpretable, pragmatic rule-based recognizer achieving high recall and competitive-to-SOTA F1, especially on tweets, with real-time speed and minimal engineering.

A cross-corpus empirical analysis identifying shortness, small lexicon, token-centricity, and syntactic regularity as design principles that motivate a generalizable typing scheme.

A flexible boundary-expansion procedure over syntactic types that unifies many fixed-pattern rules and is extensible with small lexicon updates.

Strong empirical performance and best recall on all datasets; top strict/relaxed F1 on TimeBank and Tweets.

Simple and efficient: no training, compact interpretable rules, and only a few lexicon additions required for portability.

Clear, transparent three-step pipeline (token identification, segment formation, merging) and robustness across heterogeneous domains (news, Wikipedia, social media).

Insightful corpus analysis that concretely motivates design choices (e.g., prioritize time tokens; use POS mainly for disambiguation).

Incremental novelty: type inventory and expansion strategy closely resemble prior rule-based systems and reuse SUTime’s token regexes.

Potential evaluation bias due to SUTime-prefiltered Tweets and SUTime-derived initialization, which may favor token-centric methods.

Limited error analysis and ablations; no component-wise ablations or statistical significance testing to support “significant” claims.

Coverage gaps for descriptive/narrative temporal expressions: WikiWars strict F1 lags competitors and many prepositions are excluded per TimeML.

Request for ablations and statistical tests (e.g., without POS filters, without overlap-merging, without expansion).

Question about sensitivity to POS tagging errors and whether character-level or casing heuristics can better disambiguate tokens like “May” vs “may.”

Clarification on fairness given SUTime-derived initialization and SUTime-based tweet prefiltering.

Request for measured runtimes, rule set sizes, and whether code/resources will be released.

Question on how the approach would extend to normalization and to languages with richer morphology.
