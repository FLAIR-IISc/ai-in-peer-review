Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition built around three syntactic “types” (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) to motivate design choices (short spans, small vocabulary, frequent “time tokens,” similar POS behavior). SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes, filters ambiguity with POS tags, forms segments by expanding around time tokens, and merges adjacent/overlapping segments with a few heuristics. Experiments compare against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets.

Contributions (ordered by strength):
1) A simple, high-recall rule-based recognizer (SynTime) that achieves strong performance (particularly recall) across three datasets, with especially large gains on tweets.
2) A cross-corpus descriptive analysis of time expressions (length distributions, small vocabulary, prevalence of “time tokens,” POS tendencies) that informs a practical design.
3) A pragmatic “type-based” rule design (position-sensitive modifiers and numerals around time tokens) that emphasizes expandability and runtime simplicity.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and recall, with simple rules:
  SynTime-I/E achieve the best or second-best F1 in most settings (12 best and 13 second-best of 18 reported metrics). Recalls in relaxed match exceed 92% on all datasets, aligning with the observation that ≥93% of time expressions contain a time token. On Tweets, SynTime improves strict F1 to ~91.7% and relaxed F1 to ~95.9%, substantially higher than baselines (e.g., +11.37 strict F1 over the best baseline reported). This indicates the heuristics effectively capture many surface forms with minimal complexity.

- Clear, reproducible design with intuitive heuristics:
  The segmentation/merging steps (expand left/right for specific modifier types, then merge adjacent/overlapping segments unless blocked by LINKAGE/COMMA conditions) are easy to understand and reimplement. The type taxonomy is explicit, with counts of token types and modifier categories, and implementation detail (Stanford POS tagger) is provided.

- Practical expandability and low engineering cost:
  The authors show SynTime-E requires few additions to reach near-ceiling performance on TimeBank and Tweets (4–5 added items for TimeBank; 5 for Tweets) and a moderate number for WikiWars (35 total). This supports the claim that time vocabulary is small and that adapting to domain specifics is feasible.

- Sensible, data-driven motivation:
  The descriptive analyses (length distributions clustered under 3–4 tokens; small set of distinct time tokens; POS distributions) justify the “find time token first, then expand” pipeline and explain why high recall is achievable with a small lexicon plus boundary heuristics.

Weaknesses
- Novelty over existing rule-based systems is modest and partially conflated with SUTime resources:
  SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes and discards only SUTime’s chunking rules. Gains may stem from reusing SUTime’s high-coverage lexicon and replacing its finite-state rules with simpler expansion strategies. An ablation disentangling (a) SUTime lexicon vs (b) SynTime heuristics is missing. Without this, it is hard to assess true methodological novelty beyond “simpler rules with similar resources.”

- Tweet dataset construction introduces selection bias:
  Tweets were preselected using SUTime to contain at least one time expression (942/4000), then those 942 were fully annotated and used for training/testing. This likely biases the dataset toward expressions SUTime can already detect and excludes negative documents, affecting precision/false-positive dynamics and limiting ecological validity. The “3% missed” estimate is based on a small sample (500 of 3,058 negatives) and weakly justified. A cleaner construction (fully annotate a random sample without prefiltering) is needed.

- Limited evaluation rigor: no significance testing, speed claims without measurements, borrowed baseline numbers:
  The paper claims “significant outperforms” but reports no significance tests. Runtime claims (“real time”) are not supported by benchmarks. Some baseline results are borrowed from prior papers, risking incomparable setups (tool versions, preprocessing, splits). A uniform re-run under the same pipeline would strengthen conclusions.

- Schema mismatches and scope limitations:
  SynTime excludes prepositions to align with TimeML/TimeBank annotation, yet WikiWars includes many prepositional/descriptive expressions; performance drops there (strict F1 ~80–81), and the method is not adapted to that schema. The work addresses recognition only; given TempEval emphasizes normalization as well, the end-to-end utility is constrained.

- Missing error analysis and deeper ablations:
  Beyond SynTime-I vs SynTime-E, the paper lacks analyses on error types, the impact of POS filtering, the contribution of each modifier type, or comparisons against simpler baselines (e.g., “time tokens only” or “tokens + fixed window”). This would clarify where the heuristics help and where they fail (e.g., compound ranges, nested mentions, noisy POS on tweets).

Questions to authors
- Can you provide an ablation disentangling the contributions of (i) SUTime token lexicon vs (ii) SynTime’s boundary-expansion rules? For example, use a reduced lexicon (e.g., only months/days) and show incremental gains from added types; or apply SynTime heuristics with a lexicon not derived from SUTime.

- How comparable are the borrowed baseline numbers to your runs (same tokenization, POS tagger, versions)? Can you re-run all baselines under your pipeline to ensure fair comparison?

- For Tweets, can you release or report results on a fully random, fully annotated sample (including negatives), and provide per-document and per-mention precision/recall? Also, how sensitive are results to the POS tagger used on tweets?

- Can you provide statistical significance tests for F1 improvements and concrete runtime measurements (e.g., tokens/sec) vs SUTime/HeidelTime?

- How does SynTime handle cross-sentence expressions and ranges with complex linkers (“between … and …”, “from … through …”)? Are there failure cases you can quantify?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The method is simple, effective, and well-motivated, with impressive recall and strong tweet results, but the novelty over existing rule-based systems is limited, the tweet dataset construction is biased, and evaluation lacks significance tests, runtime evidence, and thorough ablations. Addressing these concerns would make this a solid and practical contribution.
