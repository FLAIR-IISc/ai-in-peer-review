Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
The paper studies temporal expression recognition and proposes SynTime, a light-weight rule-based tagger built around “syntactic types” and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and report regularities (short spans, small vocabulary, near-ubiquity of “time tokens,” and intra-expression syntactic regularities). SynTime initializes a lexicon and token regexes (largely from SUTime), identifies “time tokens” filtered by POS, expands left/right to collect modifiers and numerals into “segments,” then merges adjacent/overlapping segments under simple rules to produce time expressions. On TimeBank/WikiWars/Tweets, SynTime reportedly achieves stronger recall and competitive-to-better F1 than HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, with very strong results on tweets.

Contributions (in order of significance):
1) A simple boundary-expansion rule system for temporal expression recognition that achieves strong recall and competitive state-of-the-art performance across three datasets, notably on tweets.
2) An empirical analysis of temporal expressions across four corpora that motivates the design choices (short spans, small lexicon, syntactic regularities).
3) A practical, extensible rule resource (built from SUTime tokens plus minor manual expansion) that runs in real-time.

Strengths
- High recall and strong overall performance: On all datasets the system attains the top or second-best F1 in most settings (Table 4), and consistently the highest recall, which is valuable for downstream temporal reasoning. Performance on tweets is particularly strong (strict F1 ~91.7, relaxed ~95.9), a challenging domain for many taggers.
- Elegant, transparent heuristics: The boundary-expansion procedure is easy to understand and implement, and the merging logic (including handling commas and linkages) captures many real cases (e.g., “January 13, 1951,” “8 to 20 days”). This contrasts with brittle, long fixed patterns and facilitates debugging/extensibility.
- Sensible type factoring that leverages syntactic behavior: Distinguishing time tokens, modifiers by relative position (PREFIX/SUFFIX/LINKAGE), and numerals provides a compact abstraction that generalizes across lexical variants and domains. The system exploits the observation that most mentions include at least one identifiable “time token.”
- Practicality and ease of adaptation: Manual expansion required very few additions on TimeBank and Tweets (Table 5), suggesting low maintenance cost. The claim that SynTime can be extended to new domains by adding a handful of tokens is well supported by the WikiWars improvements from SynTime-I to SynTime-E.
- Empirical analysis helps diagnose design choices: The length distribution, token coverage estimates, and POS observations (Observation 4) explain why a token-first approach plus local expansion is effective, and motivate rejecting purely POS-driven detection.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing rule-based systems is limited and not rigorously demonstrated: SUTime already operates over token regexes with cascading expansion, and HeidelTime also leverages POS and lexical resources. The distinction between “syntactic types” and SUTime’s categories is asserted but not quantified. Moreover, many of the 15 “time token” categories (e.g., MONTH, YEAR) are semantic classes. A clearer ablation showing what SynTime’s specific rules contribute over a SUTime-like baseline using the same lexicon is needed.
- Resource dependence and fairness of comparison: SynTime is initialized with SUTime’s token regexes, yet comparisons use off-the-shelf SUTime rather than a variant endowed with the same added keywords (for Tweets/WikiWars) or tuned rules. This risks overstating gains. Similarly, HeidelTime can be tuned to domains; it is unclear whether domain-specific resources were enabled.
- Tweet dataset construction introduces potential bias: Tweets were preselected by SUTime as containing a time expression; only a small sample of SUTime-negative tweets was manually checked. This selection likely under-represents expressions SUTime systematically misses (even if authors estimate ~3%), potentially biasing comparative recall. A more robust protocol would annotate a random sample without prefiltering or at least provide stratified evaluation.
- Claims about “real-time” and “light-weight” lack empirical evidence: There are no runtime measurements, memory footprints, or throughput statistics, nor comparisons to SUTime/HeidelTime speed. If “real-time” is a core selling point, provide concrete numbers.
- Limited analysis of errors and coverage gaps: The paper notes difficulties with prepositions and descriptive expressions (common in WikiWars) and POS tagging errors (Section 5.3), but lacks a systematic error analysis (e.g., distribution of boundary errors, missed tokens, overmerges) and an ablation (e.g., without POS filtering, without merging rules, without LINKAGE/COMMA logic). This would strengthen the methodological claims and guide future work (e.g., integrating a Twitter POS tagger).

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide an ablation study quantifying the contribution of (a) POS filtering for ambiguous tokens (e.g., May), (b) left/right expansion heuristics, and (c) merging rules (including special handling of commas and linkages) to recall/precision?
- How does SynTime compare to SUTime when both use exactly the same lexicon (including your manual additions) and when SUTime’s rules are minimally adapted to tweets? This would isolate the benefit of your expansion/merging strategy.
- For tweets, did you use a Twitter-specific tokenizer/POS tagger (e.g., CMU TweeterTagger)? If not, what is the measured POS tagging accuracy on your tweet set and how sensitive is SynTime to tagging errors?
- Can you report runtime (docs/sec) and memory usage vs. SUTime/HeidelTime on the three datasets?
- What is the inter-annotator agreement and exact annotation guideline alignment for tweets? Did you follow TimeML’s inclusion/exclusion of prepositions strictly, and how did you handle hashtags, user mentions, and tokens like “2day/tmrw”?
- Do your rules handle multi-token constructions like “last Monday morning,” “the end of next month,” and coordinated/prepositional variants (“on or about June 5,” “from 1998 through 2001”)? If not, what fraction of errors are of these types?

Additional comments
- The “least effort” framing is interesting, but the evidence is descriptive; consider tempering the claim or adding statistical tests (e.g., distributional similarity across domains).
- Please release code, rules, and expanded lexicons to ensure reproducibility.
- Clarify whether TimeBank/WikiWars experiments use the standard splits from prior work and whether evaluation includes only recognition (not normalization), to make comparisons apples-to-apples.
