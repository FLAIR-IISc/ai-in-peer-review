Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based recognizer for temporal expressions. It introduces a syntactic type system over tokens (time token, modifier, numeral; with subtypes) and a simple boundary-expansion heuristic that starts from identified time tokens and grows segments by looking for nearby modifiers/numerals, then merges adjacent/overlapping segments into full time expressions.
- The authors conduct an empirical analysis of temporal expressions across four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), observing that temporal expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, almost always include an explicit time token, and have consistent POS patterns. These observations motivate SynTime’s design.
- Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, showing strong recalls and best or competitive F1, particularly on Tweets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, simple type-based rule system with boundary-expansion that achieves high recall and competitive F1 versus strong baselines across domains.
2) An empirical characterization of time expressions across datasets that motivates the design and highlights small-vocabulary and syntactic regularities.
3) A Twitter evaluation (with a manually labeled subset) showing strong performance in a noisy domain.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with consistently high recall: SynTime(-I/-E) achieves the best recalls on all three datasets (relaxed recall >92% everywhere; strict F1 >91% on TimeBank and Tweets). This directly aligns with the design choice of starting from time tokens, supported by the analysis that ≥93% of gold spans contain such tokens. The gains are largest on Tweets (strict F1 91.74/91.77 vs. best baseline ~80), indicating robustness to noisy input.
- Simplicity and practicality: The method is training-free, easy to port/extend by adding a small number of keywords (Table 5 shows very modest additions), and is designed to run in real time. The boundary-expansion heuristic is clear and easy to implement, reducing engineering overhead compared to large rule banks or learned parsers.
- Sensible use of linguistic signal: The syntactic type system (time tokens vs. modifiers vs. numerals with positional behavior) is a clean abstraction, supported by the POS distribution analysis. Using POS to disambiguate ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May”) is reasonable and contributes to precision without complex modeling.
- Fair breadth of baselines and metrics: The paper compares against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, covering both rule-based and learning-based SOTA; reports both strict and relaxed matches; and includes a domain where rule systems often struggle (Tweets).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to SUTime and reliance on SUTime resources: The token lexicon/regexes are taken from SUTime, and the main algorithmic novelty is a different organization of simple rules (syntactic typing, boundary expansion) versus SUTime’s cascaded automata. While the engineering is neat, the conceptual advance is modest. The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of which improvements come from the new boundary-expansion logic versus from shared resources and POS filters.
- Potential bias and fairness issues in the Tweets dataset: The tweets selected for annotation were prefiltered using SUTime (only tweets SUTime flagged as containing a temporal expression were fully annotated; the remaining pool was sparsely sampled). This likely biases the dataset toward expressions that SUTime (and hence SynTime, which reuses SUTime tokens) already recognizes, underestimating out-of-vocabulary/creative forms and inflating performance. In addition, SynTime was expanded on training tweets; it is unclear whether analogous domain adaptation was allowed for HeidelTime/SUTime (e.g., adding abbreviations), which would be necessary for a fair comparison in a domain with many idiosyncrasies.
- Missing details and reproducibility concerns: The paper does not release the code/resources or provide full token/type lists, POS gating rules, and exact boundary conditions (e.g., maximum span lengths, handling punctuation beyond comma/linkage). Without these, reimplementation is nontrivial despite the claimed simplicity. No runtime numbers are reported to substantiate “real-time.”
- Lack of statistical testing and error analysis: The performance gains are not accompanied by significance tests. Error analysis is minimal, with only a brief note on POS errors. For example, which error types are reduced by boundary expansion, and which are introduced (e.g., over-merging across appositives/attributions)? On WikiWars, where SynTime underperforms in strict match, more insight would help.
- Scope limitations and theory overreach: Recognition without normalization addresses only half of the temporal tagging pipeline, limiting end-to-end utility. The “principle of least effort” framing is interesting but not rigorously tested; the observations (shortness, small vocab) are intuitive and previously noted in practice. The paper would be stronger if it tied the analysis more directly to concrete design choices and ablation results.

Questions to Authors
- How did you configure HeidelTime and SUTime for Tweets? Were any domain-specific resources or abbreviation lists added for these baselines, analogous to SynTime-E’s expansion?
- Can you quantify how much of SynTime’s improvements over SUTime derive from boundary-expansion rules vs. from the token lists/POS gating? An ablation (no POS filter; no merging; no dependent segments) would help.
- What is the exact search window for boundary expansion? Is there a hard limit on tokens spanned on each side, and how are nested or multiple linkages handled?
- How do you handle ambiguous tokens systematically (e.g., “May,” “march,” “sat”)? Please list the POS constraints used for key token types.
- Will you release the Tweets corpus, code, and the final token/type inventories to enable replication?

Additional Comments
- Consider re-annotating a truly random sample of tweets without SUTime prefiltering (or release a separate unbiased test set) to assess generalization to novel forms.
- Report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or paired tests) and include error breakdowns (over-merge, under-merge, missed modifiers).
- For WikiWars, discuss the impact of TimeML’s exclusion of prepositions on strict scoring vs. the corpus’ annotation conventions; a variant that includes prepositions might be informative.
- Since POS errors are a limitation, try a Twitter-specific POS tagger for Tweets and quantify the effect.
- Minor: clarify the count of modifier types (you mention 5 types; later list PREFIX, SUFFIX, LINKAGE, COMMA, IN_ARTICLE) and ensure terminology is consistent.
