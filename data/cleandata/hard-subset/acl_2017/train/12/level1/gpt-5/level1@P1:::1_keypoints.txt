- Proposes SynTime: a lightweight, rule-based temporal expression recognizer using a syntactic token type system and boundary-expansion heuristic to form time spans.
- Empirically analyzes four corpora showing temporal expressions are short, use small vocabularies, almost always include explicit time tokens, and have consistent POS patterns that motivate SynTime’s design.
- Demonstrates strong recall and competitive F1 vs. HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, with especially large gains on Tweets.
- Contribution: a simple type-based rule system with boundary-expansion achieving high recall and competitive F1 across domains.
- Contribution: an empirical characterization of time expressions highlighting small-vocabulary and syntactic regularities that motivate design choices.
- Contribution: a Twitter evaluation with a manually labeled subset showing robust performance in a noisy domain.
- Strength: consistently high recall (relaxed recall >92% and strict F1 >91% on TimeBank and Tweets), aligning with the time-token-first design.
- Strength: simplicity and practicality—training-free, easy to extend with a few keywords, and clear boundary-expansion heuristic for low engineering overhead.
- Strength: sensible linguistic abstraction—time/modifier/numeral typing and POS gating help disambiguate tokens and improve precision.
- Strength: fair range of baselines and metrics, including both rule-based and learning-based SOTA and both strict/relaxed matches.
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to SUTime, with many lexicons/regexes reused and only modest conceptual advance in rule organization.
- Weakness: Twitter dataset selection biased by SUTime prefiltering, likely inflating performance and underrepresenting creative/out-of-vocabulary forms.
- Weakness: unclear whether baselines received equivalent domain adaptation (e.g., added abbreviations) for fair comparison on Tweets.
- Weakness: reproducibility gaps—code/resources, full token/type lists, POS rules, and exact boundary conditions are not released or fully specified.
- Weakness: no runtime numbers provided to substantiate real-time claims.
- Weakness: lack of statistical significance testing and limited error analysis (e.g., which errors are fixed or introduced by boundary expansion).
- Weakness: scope limitation—recognition only (no normalization) limits end-to-end utility, and the "least effort" framing is not rigorously validated.
- Question: How were HeidelTime and SUTime configured for Tweets, and were domain-specific resources added for them like for SynTime-E?
- Question: Can you quantify SynTime gains attributable to boundary expansion vs. token lists/POS gating (requesting ablations)?
- Question: What is the exact boundary-expansion search window and hard span limits, and how are nested/multiple linkages handled?
- Question: How are ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May,” “march,” “sat”) handled—please list POS constraints for key token types.
- Question: Will you release the Tweets corpus, code, and token/type inventories for replication?
- Suggestion: Re-annotate a random sample of tweets without SUTime prefiltering or release an unbiased test set to assess generalization.
- Suggestion: Report statistical significance and provide error breakdowns (over-merge, under-merge, missed modifiers).
- Suggestion: For WikiWars, discuss TimeML preposition exclusion effects on strict scoring and consider a variant including prepositions.
- Suggestion: Try a Twitter-specific POS tagger and quantify its effect given POS error limitations.
- Minor fix: Clarify and consistently report the count and names of modifier types (inconsistency between “5 types” and listed categories).
