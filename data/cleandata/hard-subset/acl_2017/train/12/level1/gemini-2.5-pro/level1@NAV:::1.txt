**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in text. The method is motivated by an analysis showing that temporal expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and follow predictable syntactic patterns. SynTime defines three syntactic types—time token, modifier, and numeral—and applies simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion to identify and merge them. The core contributions of this work are:

1.  An effective and remarkably simple rule-based system that achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results by framing the task around syntactic types rather than more complex semantic ones. The system is computationally efficient and easily extensible.
2.  A strong empirical evaluation on diverse datasets, including formal news text (TimeBank) and informal social media (Tweets), where SynTime shows significant performance gains over established baselines.
3.  A concise linguistic analysis across four datasets that provides a clear motivation for the simplified design, linking observations to the "principle of least effort."

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The method's main strength is its effectiveness despite its simplicity. By relying on syntactic types and simple expansion heuristics, SynTime outperforms more complex rule-based and machine learning systems on TimeBank and Tweets. For instance, it achieves a strict F1 of 92.47% on TimeBank, surpassing previous methods. This demonstrates that a carefully designed, lightweight approach can be powerful for this task.
2.  **Robustness on Informal Text:** The system’s performance on the Tweets dataset is a significant advantage. It achieves an F1 score of 91.77% (strict match), a substantial improvement of over 11 points compared to strong baselines. This highlights its robustness in handling the short, direct, and sometimes noisy language characteristic of social media.
3.  **High Recall and Practical Extensibility:** SynTime consistently achieves excellent recall across all datasets (e.g., over 93% strict recall on TimeBank and Tweets). This is a direct result of its core design, which focuses on identifying central "time tokens" and expanding from there. Furthermore, the authors show that performance can be improved simply by adding a few keywords (SynTime-E vs. SynTime-I), making it a practical and adaptable tool.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated Novelty of Components:** While the framing around syntactic types is novel, the system is initialized with token regular expressions directly from SUTime, an existing state-of-the-art system. This makes it difficult to assess how much of the performance is due to the novel rule logic versus the reuse of SUTime’s high-quality, curated resources. The contribution appears to be more of an effective simplification of existing ideas rather than a completely new paradigm.
2.  **Limited Handling of Complex Expressions:** The model's simple heuristics are a double-edged sword. While effective for common patterns, the paper acknowledges that it struggles with more descriptive or preposition-heavy expressions, reflected in its lower F1 score on the WikiWars dataset compared to baselines. The reliance on simple boundary expansion may not be sufficient for capturing the full linguistic diversity of temporal language.
3.  **Dependence on Upstream Tooling:** The method's success is highly dependent on the accuracy of an external POS tagger for disambiguation. This is mentioned as a limitation, but its impact is not quantified. An error analysis differentiating failures caused by incorrect POS tags from failures of the core heuristic rules would provide a clearer picture of the system's intrinsic limitations.
