### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition (TER). The work is motivated by an analysis of four datasets which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and almost always contain a core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday'). Based on these observations, the authors propose a system that operates in three stages: 1) identify core time tokens in a sentence using regex and POS tags, 2) heuristically expand the boundaries around these tokens to include modifiers and numerals, forming "time segments," and 3) merge adjacent or overlapping segments to form the final time expressions. The key design principle is the use of "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) which classify words by their position relative to time tokens, rather than by their semantic meaning. The system demonstrates very strong performance, outperforming several state-of-the-art systems on benchmark datasets.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective, simple, and transparent rule-based system for TER.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. Despite its methodological simplicity (identify seed, expand boundaries, merge), it achieves state-of-the-art or superior results on multiple datasets, including informal text from Twitter. Its high recall is particularly impressive. The system's simplicity and transparency are significant practical advantages over more complex learning-based or rule-based systems.
2.  **A useful empirical analysis of time expressions across different corpora.** The observations presented in Section 3, while intuitive, provide a solid, data-driven motivation for the system's design. Quantifying properties like expression length, the small size of the core vocabulary, and the near-ubiquity of "time tokens" effectively justifies the architectural choice to build the system around identifying these core tokens first.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance.** The strongest argument for this paper is its outstanding results. As shown in Table 4, SynTime-E achieves a strict F1 score of 92.47 on TimeBank and 91.77 on Tweets, outperforming strong baselines like SUTime and the learning-based UWTime. The system's ability to achieve over 93% recall in strict match on TimeBank is a standout result. These are not incremental gains; they represent a significant improvement, especially for a rule-based system.
2.  **Simplicity, Efficiency, and Extensibility.** The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to understand. The three-step process is intuitive, and the rules for boundary expansion and merging are straightforward. The authors claim the system is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time," which is highly credible given the design and valuable for practical applications. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates a clear and simple path for extending the system to new domains by adding a small number of keywords (as shown in the SynTime-I vs. SynTime-E comparison on WikiWars).
3.  **Strong Performance on Informal Text.** The system's performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly noteworthy. Many NLP systems trained on newswire text degrade significantly on noisy, informal social media data. SynTime's F1 score of over 91% on tweets demonstrates its robustness and makes it a valuable tool for applications involving social media analysis, a challenging and important domain.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty.** The core approach—identifying anchor keywords and applying rules to expand their boundaries—is a well-established pattern in rule-based information extraction. The authors frame their main novelty as defining "syntactic types" over "semantic types." However, this distinction feels somewhat superficial. For example, a modifier in the `PREFIX` type (defined syntactically by its position) like 'last' or 'next' inherently carries a strong temporal semantic meaning. SUTime also operates on regular expressions over typed tokens. It is not clear that SynTime's typing system is a fundamentally new paradigm as opposed to a different, perhaps simpler, organization of similar underlying concepts. The novelty of the heuristic rules themselves is also not well-established.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on an Existing SOTA System for Initialization.** The paper states that the initial token lists and regular expressions for SynTime-I were "collected from SUTime." This significantly muddies the comparison and the contribution. It suggests that SynTime is not a completely new system built from the ground up, but rather a re-implementation of SUTime's lexical resources with a different, simpler rule-application engine. While this is a valid engineering contribution (simplifying the rule engine while improving performance), the paper should be more transparent about the extent of this dependency. It is difficult to assess SynTime's standalone contribution without knowing precisely what was borrowed.
3.  **Lack of In-depth Error Analysis.** The "Limitations" section is brief and only discusses upstream errors from tokenization and POS tagging, which is a generic problem for nearly all NLP systems. The paper would be much stronger with an analysis of the types of time expressions that SynTime fails on. For instance, how does it handle complex, compositional expressions (e.g., "the second Tuesday of the month after next") compared to a grammar-based system like UWTime? The discussion of WikiWars mentions "descriptive time expressions" as a challenge, but without concrete examples and analysis, it is hard to understand the system's failure modes and the remaining challenges for the proposed approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please further elaborate on the claimed distinction between your "syntactic types" and the token types used in other systems like SUTime? For example, SUTime has types for duration units or modifiers. How does classifying a token as `PREFIX` (a syntactic role) instead of, for instance, a `RELATIVE_MODIFIER` (a semantic role) lead to a fundamentally different and more effective rule design?
2.  Regarding the initialization of SynTime from SUTime, could you please quantify the extent of the borrowing? Were the 15 time token types and 5 modifier types and their corresponding keyword/regex lists taken directly from SUTime's resources? A clear statement on this would help clarify whether the main contribution is the novel type system and lexicon, or the simplified rule engine that operates on an existing lexicon.
3.  For the newly created Tweets dataset, what was the annotation process? Were there multiple annotators, and if so, was inter-annotator agreement measured to validate the quality of the labels?
