- SynTime: a lightweight rule-based system recognizing time expressions using syntactic types and simple heuristics.
- Data analysis of TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets shows time expressions are short, use small vocabularies, and usually contain a core "time token."
- Recognition pipeline: identify core time tokens, expand left/right to include modifiers and numerals, then merge overlapping segments.
- Evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; achieves state-of-the-art on TimeBank and Tweets, comparable on WikiWars.
- Contribution: a simple heuristic method relying on three syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) instead of complex cascaded rules.
- Contribution: strong empirical results, notably on noisy social media data, demonstrating robustness and domain adaptability.
- Contribution: empirical analysis motivating the "find token, then expand" strategy (>93% expressions contain a time token).
- Strength: simplicity and efficiency — lightweight, real-time, and less brittle than complex rule-based systems.
- Strength: excellent empirical performance and high recall (e.g., ~93–94% strict recall on benchmarks).
- Strength: robustness to out-of-domain/noisy text, large F1 improvement on Tweets over baselines.
- Strength: clear exposition and reproducibility; pragmatic initialization of token lists from SUTime.
- Weakness: novelty overstated — token categorization is similar in spirit to prior systems like SUTime.
- Weakness: excludes normalization, limiting the scope and making comparisons to full temporal taggers incomplete.
- Weakness: SynTime-E depends on manual domain-specific keyword additions, reducing scalability and out-of-the-box use.
- Weakness: lacks detailed qualitative error analysis and examples of failure modes (e.g., reasons for WikiWars underperformance).
- Question: clarify how "syntactic types" differ concretely from token categories in prior rule-based systems and what they handle better.
- Question: how can normalization be integrated without losing simplicity, and does the syntactic-typing help with normalization?
