**Summary and Contributions**

The paper proposes a type-based approach, SynTime, for recognizing time expressions in free text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations: (1) time expressions are very short, (2) only a small group of time-related keywords are used, (3) more than 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and designs simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) an analysis of time expressions from four datasets, (2) a type-based time expression tagger, SynTime, and (3) experimental results showing that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: SynTime's type-based approach is a fresh perspective on time expression recognition, and its simplicity and flexibility make it an attractive alternative to existing methods.
2. **Thorough analysis**: The authors' analysis of time expressions from four datasets provides valuable insights into the characteristics of time expressions and informs the design of SynTime.
3. **Strong experimental results**: SynTime's performance on three datasets is impressive, especially on the Tweets dataset, where it achieves exceptionally good results.
4. **Efficient and scalable**: SynTime's lightweight and rule-based design makes it efficient and scalable, allowing it to run in real-time.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability**: SynTime's performance on the WikiWars dataset is not as strong as on the other two datasets, suggesting that it may not generalize well to all domains or datasets.
2. **Assumes perfect tokenization and POS tagging**: SynTime relies on accurate tokenization and POS tagging, which may not always be the case in real-world scenarios.
3. **Lack of comparison to machine learning-based methods**: While SynTime is compared to rule-based baselines, it would be interesting to see how it performs against machine learning-based methods, which are commonly used in NLP tasks.
4. **No discussion of error analysis**: The authors do not provide an error analysis of SynTime's mistakes, which would help to identify areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how SynTime's type-based approach can be adapted to other languages or domains?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of imperfect tokenization and POS tagging in real-world scenarios?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of SynTime's performance to machine learning-based methods?
4. What are the most common errors made by SynTime, and how do you plan to address them in future work?
