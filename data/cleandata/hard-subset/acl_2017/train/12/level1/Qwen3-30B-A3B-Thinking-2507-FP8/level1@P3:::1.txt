

# Peer Review: Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper presents a novel rule-based approach for time expression recognition called SynTime, grounded in linguistic analysis of time expressions across four datasets. The authors make four key observations: time expressions are short (80% ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary, contain at least one time token (93%+), and exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they define three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and design simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition. The main contributions are:
1. The linguistic analysis providing empirical evidence for Zipf's principle of least effort in time expression usage
2. The syntactic type system for time expression recognition (distinguishing from semantic-based approaches)
3. The SynTime approach that outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on multiple datasets

## Strengths

1. **Insightful Linguistic Analysis**: The analysis of time expressions across four diverse datasets is thorough and well-supported. The four observations provide a strong theoretical foundation for the approach and offer valuable insights into how humans construct time expressions. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is particularly compelling and well-motivated by the data.

2. **Novel Syntactic Type System**: The approach of defining types based on syntactic behavior (inspired by part-of-speech tagging) rather than semantic meaning represents a significant conceptual advance over existing rule-based systems. This distinction is clearly articulated and validated through the experiments.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: SynTime demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets (91.74% F1 on Tweets), with particularly impressive recall rates (95.87% on Tweets in relaxed match). The results on the Twitter dataset are especially noteworthy given the informal nature of the text.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper overstates the novelty of the syntactic type system. SUTime (Chang & Manning, 2012) already uses part-of-speech information to some extent, and the distinction between "syntactic" and "semantic" types isn't sufficiently clarified. The paper doesn't adequately address how SynTime's type system differs from SUTime's underlying use of POS tags.

2. **Lack of Runtime Data**: The claim that SynTime "runs in real time" is unsupported by any runtime measurements or comparisons to baseline systems. For a paper claiming to be a "light-weight" system, this omission is significant and undermines the practical value of the approach.

3. **Insufficient Expansion Details**: The paper mentions SynTime-E (the expanded version) but provides minimal detail on the expansion process. It states that only a few tokens need to be added (Table 5), but doesn't explain how these tokens are identified, validated, or integrated into the system. The effort required for expansion is not quantified.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't explore how errors in these components affect performance. Given that rule-based systems are particularly sensitive to preprocessing errors, this is a critical limitation that deserves more attention.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide runtime measurements for SynTime compared to the baselines to substantiate the "real-time" claim? How does it compare to SUTime's runtime?

2. The paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "can be easily expanded." Please clarify the exact process for expansion: how are new tokens identified, validated, and added to the system? What is the average effort required to expand for a new domain?

3. The paper mentions that "in tweets people tend to use formal words" (Section 4.3), but the Twitter dataset contains informal language. How does SynTime handle cases where users employ informal spellings (like "2day" for "today") that aren't in the standard vocabulary? What is the impact on performance when encountering such variations?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and empirically validated approach to time expression recognition that offers a compelling alternative to existing rule-based systems. The linguistic analysis is strong, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing runtime data, clarifying the novelty claim, and detailing the expansion process. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field of temporal information extraction. As it stands, it's a solid paper with some important omissions that need to be addressed.
