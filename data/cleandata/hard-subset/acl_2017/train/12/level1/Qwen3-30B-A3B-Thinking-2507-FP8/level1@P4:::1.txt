

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper analyzes time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain no more than three tokens), (2) a small vocabulary of time-related keywords is used, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that defines syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple heuristic rules for recognition. The system identifies time tokens, searches for modifiers/numerals to form time segments, and merges segments into time expressions.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. The comprehensive analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing patterns that support the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949) in language usage.
2. The proposal of SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system that uses syntactic types and heuristic rules for time expression recognition.
3. The empirical demonstration that SynTime significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and tweets data.

## Strengths

1. The analysis of time expressions is thorough and provides valuable insights that are well-supported by data across multiple domains. The four observations are consistently demonstrated across four different datasets, showing strong evidence that time expressions follow the principle of least effort. This analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed system.

2. SynTime is well-designed as a lightweight, rule-based system that can be easily expanded by adding keywords. The system's simplicity is a significant advantage over machine learning-based approaches that require training data and over more complex rule-based systems. The heuristic rules based on boundary expansion are elegant and effective, as evidenced by the strong experimental results.

3. The experimental results are compelling, with SynTime achieving the best F1 scores on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, and excellent recall rates (above 92% in relaxed match) across all datasets. The high recall aligns with the authors' observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing theoretical validation for the system's design.

4. The paper clearly articulates the relationship between the observed patterns and the proposed system. The authors demonstrate how their observations directly inform the design of SynTime's syntactic types and heuristic rules, creating a coherent and well-motivated approach.

## Weaknesses

1. The novelty of the syntactic type approach is limited. While the authors distinguish between syntactic and semantic types, this distinction is subtle and not clearly justified as a significant contribution. Many existing rule-based systems already use token classification based on syntactic properties, making this aspect of the work incremental rather than transformative.

2. The evaluation lacks sufficient analysis of SynTime's sensitivity to errors in POS tagging, which the authors acknowledge is a critical assumption. Without understanding how POS errors propagate to time expression recognition errors, the practical applicability of the system is questionable, especially for informal text like tweets where POS tagging is particularly challenging.

3. The results on WikiWars are not as strong as on the other datasets, with SynTime achieving only competitive results. The authors note that the system struggles with prepositions and descriptive time expressions in this dataset, but they don't provide a detailed analysis of these failures or how to address them, limiting the generalizability of their approach.

4. The paper does not provide concrete evidence of SynTime's real-time performance compared to baselines. While the authors claim the system runs in real time, they don't present timing measurements or comparisons that would substantiate this claim and allow readers to assess the practical advantages of their approach.

5. The evaluation is limited to English datasets, with no analysis of how the system would perform on time expressions in other languages or domains. The authors mention future work on other languages, but the current contribution is restricted to English, reducing its broader impact for the NLP community.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims SynTime runs in real time but provides no timing measurements. Can the authors provide specific execution times for SynTime compared to the baselines on the same hardware?

2. How does SynTime handle time expressions that contain ambiguous tokens (e.g., "May" as a month vs. a modal verb)? The paper mentions using POS information for disambiguation, but a more detailed explanation would be helpful.

3. The authors state that the small vocabulary of time-related keywords makes expansion of SynTime easy. Can they provide statistics on how many additional keywords were needed for each dataset (e.g., TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) to achieve the reported performance improvements?
