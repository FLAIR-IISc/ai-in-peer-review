

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic types rather than semantic types. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) only a small vocabulary is used for time expressions, (3) ≥93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they define three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, and numerals) and design simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.

## Main Contributions
1. The most significant contribution is the empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing consistent linguistic patterns that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage. This analysis establishes a solid foundation for the proposed approach.

2. The SynTime system itself, which uses syntactic types and simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion to recognize time expressions. The key innovation lies in the syntactic type definition (based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning) and the heuristic rules for segment merging.

3. The experimental validation demonstrating SynTime's effectiveness, particularly its strong performance on Twitter data (achieving 95.87% recall in relaxed match) and its lightweight, expandable nature.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis**: The paper provides a thorough and well-supported analysis of time expressions across four diverse datasets. The four observations are well-documented with statistics and provide compelling evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage. This analysis is valuable in itself and serves as a strong foundation for the proposed approach.

2. **Simple yet effective system**: SynTime's lightweight design and simplicity are major strengths. The system requires no training data, runs in real-time, and can be easily expanded by adding new keywords. The authors demonstrate that the approach works well on both formal (TimeBank, WikiWars) and informal (Tweets) text, with particularly impressive results on Twitter data.

3. **Strong experimental results**: SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on two out of three datasets (TimeBank and Tweets) and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. The high recall rates (95.65% on TimeBank, 98.31% on Tweets in relaxed match) directly align with the authors' observation that ≥93% of time expressions contain time tokens. The results on Twitter data are especially noteworthy given the challenges of informal language.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in the core approach**: While the paper presents SynTime as novel, the core methodology is not substantially different from existing rule-based approaches like SUTime. The distinction between syntactic and semantic types is not clearly justified as a significant improvement. The authors claim that "SynTime defines types for tokens according to their syntactic behaviors" while "other rule-based taggers define types for tokens based on their semantic meaning," but the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that this approach leads to better performance or is fundamentally different from prior work.

2. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't provide any analysis of how errors in these components affect performance. For instance, how much would the recall drop if the POS tagger made errors (as noted in Section 5.3)? Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess the real-world applicability of the system.

3. **Overstated theoretical connection**: The paper ties the observations to the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949) but doesn't provide sufficient theoretical grounding for this connection. The observations (short expressions, small vocabulary, etc.) are interesting linguistic patterns, but they don't necessarily provide evidence for the principle of least effort in language use. This connection feels tenuous and could be more carefully qualified.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that SynTime's syntactic type system is a significant improvement over semantic type systems. Can the authors provide specific examples where the syntactic approach outperforms semantic approaches in time expression recognition, particularly for time expressions that are challenging for existing systems?

2. How does SynTime handle complex time expressions with multiple time tokens (e.g., "June 15 to August 20")? The paper mentions "LINKAGE" modifiers but doesn't explain how the system processes expressions with multiple time tokens.

3. The paper states that SynTime runs in real-time but doesn't provide any runtime measurements. Can the authors provide comparison of SynTime's runtime with the baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the same hardware?

4. The authors note that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words" and cite Twitter word clusters showing informal variants at about 3% rate. How does SynTime handle these informal variants, and what would be the impact on performance if these variants were not included in the expansion?
