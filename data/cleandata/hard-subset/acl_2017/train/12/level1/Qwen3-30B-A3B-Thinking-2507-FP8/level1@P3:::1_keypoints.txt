- SynTime: a rule-based time-expression recognizer using three syntactic types and simple heuristics, evaluated on four datasets.
- Four core observations: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, almost always contain a time token, and show similar syntactic behavior.
- Contribution: linguistic analysis supporting Zipf’s principle of least effort in time expressions.
- Contribution: a syntactic type system for time recognition that emphasizes POS-like behavior over semantics.
- Contribution: SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on multiple datasets (e.g., 91.74% F1 on Tweets).
- Strength: Thorough, well-supported linguistic analysis across diverse datasets.
- Strength: Novel conceptual shift to syntactic-type-based rule design inspired by POS tagging.
- Strength: Strong experimental results with particularly high recall on Twitter data.
- Weakness: Novelty may be overstated since SUTime already leverages POS information; distinction between syntactic and semantic types is underexplained.
- Weakness: No runtime measurements provided to substantiate “real-time” and “light-weight” claims.
- Weakness: Insufficient detail on SynTime-E expansion—how tokens are identified, validated, and integrated is unclear and effort is unquantified.
- Weakness: Limited discussion of sensitivity to tokenization and POS-tagging errors and their effect on performance.
- Question: Provide runtime comparisons of SynTime versus baselines, especially SUTime.
- Question: Clarify the expansion process: how new tokens are found, validated, added, and typical effort needed per domain.
- Question: Explain handling of informal spellings/variations in tweets (e.g., “2day”) and their impact on performance.
- Overall: Well-motivated and empirically strong approach that needs clearer novelty claims, runtime data, and expansion/robustness details to be fully convincing.
