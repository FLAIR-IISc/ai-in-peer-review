

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic patterns observed across multiple datasets. The authors analyze time expressions from TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets, revealing four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically very short (80% ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small, consistent vocabulary, (3) 93%+ contain time tokens, and (4) words in time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these insights, SynTime defines three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and employs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions through boundary expansion.

The primary contribution is the empirical analysis demonstrating time expressions' consistent patterns across diverse text types, supporting the principle of least effort in language usage. This analysis provides strong justification for the approach. The second contribution is SynTime itself, a lightweight, real-time rule-based tagger that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets datasets (achieving 91.74% F1 on Tweets). The third contribution is the practical demonstration of how minimal manual expansion (adding only 3-16 keywords) significantly improves performance on domain-specific data.

Strengths include the compelling empirical analysis that reveals consistent patterns across datasets, the impressive performance on informal Twitter data (where SynTime achieves 95.87% recall), and the system's real-time efficiency and simplicity. The approach's effectiveness in handling short, common time expressions aligns perfectly with the observed patterns.

Weaknesses include insufficient discussion of POS tagging limitations: as noted in the paper, errors in Stanford POS tagging (e.g., "sat" tagged as VBD instead of NNP) significantly impact performance, but this critical dependency isn't adequately addressed. The comparison with baselines is limited to three methods, missing context against more recent approaches. Additionally, while the authors claim "simplicity," the system requires manual configuration of 21 syntactic types (15 time tokens + 5 modifiers), which may not be as straightforward as implied.

Questions: How would SynTime handle complex time expressions like "the day after tomorrow" that violate the observed patterns? What specific challenges would you anticipate when applying this approach to languages with different syntactic structures?
