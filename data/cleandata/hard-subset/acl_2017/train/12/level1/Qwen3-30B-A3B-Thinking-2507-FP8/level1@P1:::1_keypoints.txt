- Paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer using syntactic types and heuristic rules.  
- Observation: 80% of time expressions are very short (≤3 tokens).  
- Observation: Time expressions use a small, limited vocabulary.  
- Observation: ≥93% of time expressions contain at least one time token.  
- Observation: Words in time expressions show consistent syntactic behavior, motivating syntactic type categories.  
- Contribution: Empirical analysis across four datasets revealing consistent linguistic patterns and supporting the principle of least effort.  
- Contribution: SynTime system that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion rules.  
- Contribution: Experimental validation showing strong performance, especially on Twitter, and a lightweight, expandable system requiring no training.  
- Strength: Thorough, well-documented empirical analysis across diverse datasets.  
- Strength: Simple, real-time, training-free system that is easy to expand with keywords.  
- Strength: Strong experimental results, outperforming baselines on TimeBank and Tweets and comparable on WikiWars.  
- Weakness: Core approach has limited novelty relative to existing rule-based systems like SUTime, and syntactic vs. semantic distinction is not convincingly justified.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of robustness to tokenization and POS-tagging errors and their effect on performance.  
- Weakness: Theoretical link to the principle of least effort is overstated and not well grounded.  
- Question: Provide concrete examples where syntactic typing outperforms semantic approaches on challenging expressions.  
- Question: Clarify how SynTime handles complex expressions with multiple time tokens (e.g., "June 15 to August 20").  
- Question: Provide runtime measurements and comparisons with baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the same hardware.  
- Question: Explain handling of informal Twitter variants and the performance impact if such variants are not included.
