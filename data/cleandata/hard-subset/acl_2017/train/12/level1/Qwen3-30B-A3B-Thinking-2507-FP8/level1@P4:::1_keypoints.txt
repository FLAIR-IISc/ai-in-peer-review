- Paper analyzes time expressions in TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets and proposes SynTime, a rule-based recognizer using syntactic types and heuristic boundary expansion.

- Contributions: (1) broad empirical analysis revealing short, small-vocabulary, token-containing, syntactically similar time expressions; (2) SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system using syntactic types; (3) strong empirical gains over baselines on benchmarks and tweets.

- Strength: Thorough, multi-domain analysis supporting the principle of least effort with consistent observations across datasets.

- Strength: SynTime is simple, extensible by adding keywords, and avoids training-data dependence of ML methods.

- Strength: Strong experimental results — best F1 on TimeBank and Tweets and high recall (>92% relaxed) across datasets.

- Strength: Clear link between empirical observations and system design, producing a coherent, well-motivated approach.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — syntactic-type distinction is incremental given prior rule-based token classifications.

- Weakness: No analysis of sensitivity to POS-tagging errors, raising concerns for informal text like tweets.

- Weakness: Weaker, only-competitive performance on WikiWars with noted struggles on prepositions and descriptive expressions, lacking failure analysis.

- Weakness: Claim of real-time performance is unsubstantiated — no timing measurements or comparisons provided.

- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to English; no experiments or analysis for other languages or domains.

- Question: Provide concrete execution times for SynTime versus baselines on the same hardware to support the real-time claim.

- Question: Explain in detail how SynTime disambiguates ambiguous tokens (e.g., "May" month vs. modal) beyond POS usage.

- Question: Report how many additional time-related keywords were added per dataset (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) to achieve reported performance.
