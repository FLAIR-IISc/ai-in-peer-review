This paper presents a rule-based approach for time expression extraction, grounded in the observation that time expressions are typically short and contain at least one identifiable “time token.” The method first identifies these time tokens using dictionary lookup, regular expression matching, and POS tagging. It then expands these tokens into segments bidirectionally, guided by a set of heuristic rules, and finally merges segments into complete time expressions using further rules. Evaluation against state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning-based systems on three datasets demonstrates significant performance improvements.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a simplified, type-based rule-based approach to time expression recognition that leverages syntactic properties of time expressions. The authors identify key characteristics of time expressions in text – their brevity and reliance on a limited set of time tokens – and design a system, SynTime, that exploits these characteristics. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets, providing support for the design choices made in SynTime. Finally, the demonstrated performance improvements over existing systems, particularly on the Twitter dataset, are noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper is clearly written and logically structured. The design of SynTime is well-motivated by the observed characteristics of time expressions in the analyzed datasets. The authors persuasively argue that focusing on time tokens and utilizing a type-based approach simplifies the rule design process.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that SynTime consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets, including a challenging Twitter dataset. The reported gains in both precision and recall are significant.
3. **Lightweight and Efficient:** As a rule-based system, SynTime’s efficiency is a notable advantage. The authors highlight its ability to run in real-time, making it suitable for applications requiring fast processing speeds. The claim of ease of expansion by simply adding keywords is also compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reproducibility Concerns:** The paper lacks sufficient detail to enable straightforward reproduction of the results. Specifics regarding the implementation of the heuristic rules and the exact configurations of the baseline systems are not fully provided. This hinders independent verification of the findings.
2. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance metrics, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes (e.g., specific types of time expressions it struggles with) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
3. **Potential Ceiling on Performance:** The authors acknowledge that SynTime's performance is approaching a ceiling due to the reliance on time tokens. While this is a reasonable observation, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential strategies for overcoming this limitation and further improving performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Type System Details:** Could you provide more details about the specific regular expressions used for each of the 15 time token types defined in Section 4.1?
2. **Heuristic Rule Specification:** Can you elaborate on the specific criteria used in the heuristic rules for time segment identification (Section 4.2.2)? Providing examples of these rules would be helpful.
3. **Future Improvements:** Given that approximately 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, and SynTime already achieves high recall (over 92%), what specific strategies are being considered to address the remaining 7% and further improve overall performance?
4. **Resource Availability:** Do you have plans to release the code and the complete set of rules used in SynTime to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

* In Section 4.1, the definition of "season" as having five types seems unusual. Please clarify this choice. Consider the inclusion of culturally relevant time periods like Ramadan or Holiday Season.
* In Section 5.1, the phrase "two benchmark datasets" is inaccurate; the paper actually uses three datasets.
* In Section 5.2, providing an example of a time expression *without* a readily identifiable time token would illustrate the system’s handling of more complex cases.
