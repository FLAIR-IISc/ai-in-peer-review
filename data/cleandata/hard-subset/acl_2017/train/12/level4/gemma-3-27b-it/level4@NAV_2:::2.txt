The paper proposes a rule-based method for time expression recognition, which is a valuable approach as it provides a transparent and interpretable tool for a fundamental task in many NLP applications. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method, SynTime, outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning-based methods on benchmark datasets and Twitter data.

The primary contribution of this work is the novel type-based approach to rule design, grounded in observations about the characteristics of time expressions across multiple corpora. Specifically, the authors identify consistent patterns in length, vocabulary, and syntactic behavior, leading to a simplified and effective rule set. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation, which clearly demonstrates SynTime’s competitive performance, particularly its strong recall scores. Finally, the lightweight nature of SynTime, enabling real-time processing and easy expansion, is a noteworthy benefit.

However, a key concern is the potential for overfitting of the rules to the corpora used for both observation and evaluation. The rule design process was informed by the characteristics of the evaluation datasets, raising questions about generalizability. While the analysis identifies common patterns, the paper lacks a quantitative assessment of the overlap in time expressions across the four datasets. Demonstrating a high degree of overlap would strengthen the argument for the rules’ broader applicability. 

Furthermore, the evaluation would be more convincing with experiments on a corpus entirely independent of the rule design process. This would provide stronger evidence of SynTime’s effectiveness on unseen data and address concerns about its wide-ranging applicability.

To better assess the method’s generalization capability, could the authors provide a quantitative analysis of the overlap in time expressions across the datasets used for analysis and evaluation? Additionally, what would be the performance of SynTime on a dataset that was not used during the observation or rule-creation phases?
