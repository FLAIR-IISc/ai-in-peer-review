This paper describes a rule-based approach to time expression extraction. Its key insights are that time expressions typically are short and contain at least one time token. The approach first identifies time tokens through a combination of dictionary lookup, regular expression matching with POS tagging information. It then expands the time segment from either direction of the time token based on a set of heuristic rules. Finally, it merges time segments into a single time expression based on another set of rules. Evaluation of this approach against both rule-based and machine learning-based systems on three datasets demonstrates significant improvements.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The rules are motivated by empirical observations of the data, and this justification is well supported by the evaluation results. The analysis of time expressions across the four datasets provides a strong foundation for the proposed method. The reported performance gains on TimeBank and Tweets, particularly the high recall scores, are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the type-based approach and heuristic rules. The design choice to focus on syntactic types, rather than semantic meaning, appears to be a beneficial simplification. The lightweight nature of the approach, enabling real-time performance, is also a notable strength.

**Weaknesses:**

There are some areas of underspecification in the paper that make reproducing the results challenging. Specifically, the exact regular expressions and rule sets used within SynTime are not fully detailed, hindering reproducibility. While the paper mentions using Stanford POS Tagger, it does not specify the version or settings used, which could affect performance. The expansion process of time segments, although described, lacks concrete examples that would help understand its intricacies and potential edge cases.  The impact of incorrect POS tags on the system's performance is acknowledged but not quantified.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Section 4.1: Could you elaborate on the rationale for defining 15 season types? How are concepts such as Ramadan or “Holiday Season” handled within the current framework?
*   Section 5.1: The text refers to "two benchmark datasets," but the subsequent description and experiments involve three datasets. Please clarify this discrepancy.
*   Section 5.2: Providing an example of a time expression that does *not* contain a time token would be helpful to illustrate how the system handles such cases.
*   Section 5.2: Given that the system already achieves high recall (92%), approaching the theoretical limit suggested by the 93% time token coverage observation, what are the primary avenues for further performance improvement?
*   Is there a plan to release the source code and a complete specification of the rules used in SynTime to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

The analysis of informal language usage in tweets and the observation that formal words still dominate is an interesting and relevant point. The comparison with Twitter word clusters adds valuable context.
