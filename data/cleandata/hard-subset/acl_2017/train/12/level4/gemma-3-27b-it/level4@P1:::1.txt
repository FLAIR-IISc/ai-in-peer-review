This paper describes a rule-based approach to time expression extraction. Its key insights are that time expressions are typically short and contain at least one time token. The approach first identifies time tokens through a combination of dictionary lookup, regular expression matching, and POS tagging information. It then expands the time segment from either direction of the time token based on a set of heuristic rules. Finally, it merges time segments into a single time expression based on another set of rules. Evaluation of this approach against both rule-based and machine learning-based systems on three datasets demonstrates significant improvements.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The rules are motivated by empirical observations of the data and appear well-justified, as evidenced by the evaluation results. The analysis of time expressions across different datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) is insightful and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method. The fact that SynTime achieves high recall, especially on the challenging Tweets dataset, is a significant strength. The lightweight nature of the system and its real-time performance are also notable advantages. 

**Weaknesses:**

There are some areas where the paper lacks specific details, making it difficult to fully reproduce the results. 

*   The descriptions of the heuristic rules used for time segment identification and time expression extraction (Section 4.2) could be more detailed. Providing concrete examples of how these rules are applied in different scenarios would greatly improve clarity.
*   The expansion process and the criteria for adding keywords from training data (Section 4.3) could benefit from more explanation. It is unclear how the keywords are selected and integrated into the system.
*   The implementation details, particularly regarding the POS tagger and any preprocessing steps, are not fully described.



**Questions to Authors:**

*   Section 4.1 mentions 15 time token types, including "SEASON" with 5 distinct tokens. Could you clarify what these five seasons are? How does the system handle time expressions related to culturally specific events like Ramadan or Holiday Season, which may not fit neatly into these predefined categories?
*   Section 5.1 refers to "two benchmark datasets," but the text goes on to discuss three datasets. Please clarify this discrepancy.
*   Section 5.2 would benefit from an example of a time expression that does *not* contain a time token, illustrating how the system handles such cases.
*   Given that the system’s recall is already high (around 92%), and the analysis indicates that approximately 93% of time expressions contain a time token, what strategies are planned to address the remaining challenges and further improve performance?
*   Do you have any plans to release the complete set of rules and/or the software implementation used in this work to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

The analysis in Section 3, particularly the observations regarding the length of time expressions and the limited vocabulary, is compelling and effectively motivates the design of SynTime. The comparison to the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949) adds an interesting perspective.
