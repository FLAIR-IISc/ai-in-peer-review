This paper presents a rule-based approach to time expression extraction. The core idea is that time expressions are generally concise and include at least one identifiable time token. The method first identifies these time tokens using a combination of dictionary lookup, regular expression matching, and part-of-speech (POS) tagging. It then expands these initial tokens into full time expressions by applying a set of heuristic rules in both directions, and finally merges overlapping segments using another rule set. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in performance through evaluation on three datasets, comparing their approach against both rule-based and machine learning-based systems.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presents its methodology. The rules are strongly motivated by empirical observations from the data and appear well-justified, as supported by the reported evaluation results. 

**Weaknesses:**

The paper suffers from a degree of underspecification, which hinders reproducibility. Specific concerns are detailed below.

**General Discussion and Questions:**

* **Section 4.1:** The paper mentions five seasons. This seems limited; could the approach accommodate culturally specific or less common temporal divisions, such as Ramadan or the Holiday Season?

* **Section 5.1:** The text refers to "two benchmark datasets," but the paper actually evaluates on three datasets. This discrepancy should be corrected.

* **Section 5.2:** Providing an example of a time expression *without* a clear time token would be valuable for understanding the system’s behavior in more complex cases.

* **Section 5.2:** Given that the approach appears to be nearing its performance ceiling – with 93% of expressions containing a time token and the system achieving 92% recall – what are the authors’ plans for future improvements? What avenues for further gains are being considered?

* **Reproducibility:** Will the complete set of rules and/or the software implementation be made publicly available? Releasing these resources would greatly benefit the research community and facilitate replication of the results.
