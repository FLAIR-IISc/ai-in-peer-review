The paper proposes a method for recognizing time expressions from text. It presents a simple rule-based approach, which is a strong advantage as an analytical tool, given that time expression recognition should be a fundamental process in many applications. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning-based methods for time expression recognition.

While the results are promising, my primary concern is the generality of the method. The rules were designed based on observations from the corpora used for evaluation. Therefore, I worry that the rules may overfit to these specific corpora. Furthermore, the domains of these corpora may have influenced the rule design. The paper lacks statistical analysis or discussion regarding the overlap of time expressions across the observed corpora. Demonstrating a significant overlap in time expressions would have strengthened the argument for the rules’ generality. 

Ideally, the experiments would have been conducted using a corpus entirely distinct from the one used during the rule design process. This would provide more robust evidence of the proposed method’s broad effectiveness. 

**Contributions:** The main contribution of this work is a simplified type-based approach to rule-based time expression recognition, leveraging syntactic types and heuristic rules. A second, notable contribution is the analysis of time expressions across four datasets, revealing patterns such as short expression length, limited vocabulary, and reliance on time tokens, informing the design of the SynTime system. Finally, the empirical evaluation, demonstrating competitive performance against established methods, particularly on the challenging Twitter dataset, represents a worthwhile contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime consistently outperforms or achieves comparable results to state-of-the-art methods (HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime) on benchmark datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars) and a noisy dataset (Tweets). The particularly strong performance on Tweets is noteworthy, suggesting robustness to informal text.
2. **Lightweight and Efficient:** As a rule-based tagger, SynTime is described as running in real-time, offering a practical advantage for applications requiring fast processing. The ease of expansion by adding keywords is also a significant benefit.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The initial analysis of time expressions across four datasets provides valuable insights into their characteristics, motivating the design choices of SynTime. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is interesting and provides a theoretical underpinning for the observed patterns.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Potential for Overfitting:** As noted above, the rules were developed based on the datasets used for evaluation, raising concerns about overfitting and limited generalization to unseen data or different domains.
2. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents overall performance metrics, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes could reveal areas for improvement and further refine the rules.
3. **Reliance on Existing Tokenization/POS Tagging:** The method’s performance is contingent on the accuracy of the input tokenization and POS tags. Errors in these pre-processing steps could propagate to the time expression recognition task.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a quantitative analysis of the overlap in time expressions observed across the four datasets used for analysis and evaluation?
2. Have you considered evaluating SynTime on a corpus that was not used during the rule design process to assess its generalization ability?
3. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the common types of errors made by SynTime and discussing potential strategies to address them?
