This paper presents a rule-based approach to time expression extraction. The core idea leverages the observation that time expressions are typically short and contain at least one identifiable time token. The method first identifies these time tokens using a combination of dictionary lookup, regular expression matching, and POS tagging. It then expands these initial segments, iteratively growing outwards from the token based on a defined set of heuristic rules. Finally, the resulting segments are merged into complete time expressions using another set of rules. The authors demonstrate significant improvements through evaluation against both rule-based and machine learning-based systems across three datasets.

**Strengths:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The rules employed are demonstrably motivated by empirical observations of the data, and their design appears well-justified by the reported evaluation results. The approach is intuitively appealing and addresses a core challenge in temporal information extraction with a pragmatic, rule-based strategy.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper suffers from some degree of underspecification, which hinders reproducibility. Specific details regarding the implementation of the rules and data processing are lacking, as detailed in the questions below. While the overall approach is clearly described, a more complete specification would greatly benefit the research community.

**General Discussion & Questions:**

Several points require further clarification. 

* **Section 4.1:** The discussion of seasons mentions five seasons. This is unusual and warrants explanation. Specifically, what constitutes these five seasons? Furthermore, the approach’s ability to handle culturally-specific time expressions, such as “Ramadan month” or “Holiday Season,” should be addressed. Do the current rules adequately capture such expressions, or are further extensions planned?

* **Section 5.1:** The text refers to “two benchmark datasets,” but the evaluation section (Section 5.2) explicitly uses *three* datasets. This discrepancy should be corrected for clarity.

* **Section 5.2:** Providing an example of a time expression *without* a readily identifiable time token would be helpful in illustrating the system's handling of more complex cases. While the authors acknowledge that most expressions contain a token, understanding the behavior on those that do not is important.

* **Section 5.2:** Given that the approach achieves 92% recall on a task where 93% of expressions contain a time token, suggesting a performance close to the theoretical ceiling, the authors should discuss potential avenues for further improvement. What limitations are anticipated, and what novel strategies could overcome these? Exploring improvements beyond simply adding more rules or refining existing ones would be valuable.

* **Reproducibility:** The research community would greatly benefit from the release of the complete set of rules used in the system. Ideally, the software implementation itself should also be made available to facilitate further research and allow for direct comparison with other approaches. This is crucial for verifying the reported results and building upon this work.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated and effective rule-based system for time expression extraction. The clarity of presentation and solid evaluation are significant strengths. Addressing the concerns regarding reproducibility and providing further clarification on the points raised above would significantly enhance the value and impact of this work. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing these issues.
