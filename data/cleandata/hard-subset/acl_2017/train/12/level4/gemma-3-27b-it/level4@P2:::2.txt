This paper presents a rule-based method for time expression recognition, leveraging syntactic types and simple heuristics. A key strength of the approach is its simplicity, making it a valuable analysis tool for a fundamental NLP task. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method, SynTime, outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning-based techniques on multiple datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is a novel type-based approach to rule-based time expression recognition. Rather than relying on semantic definitions, SynTime defines types based on syntactic behavior, resulting in a more flexible and lightweight system. A second significant contribution lies in the insightful analysis of time expressions across four datasets, revealing consistent patterns – short length, small vocabulary, frequent presence of time tokens, and predictable syntactic structure – that motivate the design of SynTime. Finally, the empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods, particularly on the challenging Twitter dataset.

However, a potential limitation concerns the generalizability of the proposed rules. The rules were developed based on observations from the same corpora used for evaluation, raising the possibility of overfitting. The paper lacks a quantitative analysis of overlap in time expressions across the evaluated corpora; demonstrating a high degree of overlap would strengthen the argument for generality. Ideally, evaluation on a completely independent corpus, not used during rule design, would provide more conclusive evidence of the method’s widespread effectiveness.

Additionally, the paper mentions the potential impact of domain on rule design, but doesn’t thoroughly explore this aspect. More discussion of how domain-specific language might affect the method’s performance would be valuable. While the expansion strategy is mentioned, further details about the effort required for adaptation to new domains would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a quantitative analysis of the overlap in time expressions across the TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets datasets?
2.  What is the estimated effort (e.g., annotation time) required to adapt SynTime to a new domain?
3.  How does SynTime handle more complex or ambiguous time expressions that may deviate from the observed syntactic patterns?

**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive and well-formatted. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how SynTime compares to other recent work in time expression recognition, beyond the baselines used in the experiments.
