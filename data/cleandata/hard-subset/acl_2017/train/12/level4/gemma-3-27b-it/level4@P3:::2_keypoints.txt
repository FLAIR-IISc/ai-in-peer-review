- Empirical analysis across four datasets shows time expressions tend to be short, use a limited vocabulary, and follow consistent syntactic patterns, motivating the approach.  
- SynTime introduces a syntactic-type rule system for time expression recognition, shifting from semantic to syntactic typing for greater flexibility and extensibility.  
- Experiments show SynTime achieves competitive or superior performance with high recall, notably strong on the challenging Twitter dataset.  
- The paper provides clear motivation and theoretical grounding (e.g., Zipf’s principle) linking observed patterns to the method.  
- The syntactic-based rule definition is a novel shift from prior semantic typing approaches, enabling more generalizable rules.  
- Strong performance on noisy, informal Twitter data highlights SynTime’s robustness.  
- The lightweight, rule-based boundary-expansion design is computationally efficient and easy to adapt to new domains or languages.  
- The paper includes thorough comparisons against several established baselines, supporting SynTime’s effectiveness.  
- Potential overfitting to the corpora used for observation and rule design is a concern; the paper lacks analysis of expression overlap across datasets.  
- Reliance on datasets used during rule creation raises questions about generalizability absent evaluation on truly independent data.  
- The paper provides limited error analysis, missing a detailed breakdown of common failure modes and areas for improvement.  
- Dependence on POS-tagging accuracy is a vulnerability; the impact of tagging errors is not thoroughly examined.  
- Request for a quantitative analysis of overlap in time expressions/types across the four datasets.  
- Request to evaluate SynTime on a completely independent dataset not used during rule design, or plans/results if already done.  
- Request for a more detailed error analysis identifying common error types and potential solutions.  
- Question about SynTime’s sensitivity to POS tagger accuracy and whether different taggers or tagging-error impacts were tested.  
- Citation formatting is inconsistent and should be standardized.  
- Language is slightly repetitive (e.g., repeated use of “observations”); vary wording for readability.
