- Paper: rule-based time-expression extraction leveraging the insight that time expressions are short and typically contain at least one time token; pipeline: time-token ID (dictionary + regex + POS), heuristic segment expansion, rule-based merging; shows significant improvements vs rule- and ML-baselines on three datasets.

- Strength: well-written and clearly presented with rules motivated by empirical data and supported by evaluation.

- Strength: comprehensive cross-dataset analysis provides a strong foundation for the method.

- Strength: strong performance gains on TimeBank and Tweets, notably very high recall.

- Strength: focusing on syntactic types (not semantics) is an effective simplification.

- Strength: lightweight design enabling real-time performance.

- Weakness: key regexes and rule sets (SynTime) are underspecified, hindering reproducibility.

- Weakness: Stanford POS tagger version/settings not reported, which could affect replicability.

- Weakness: expansion procedure lacks concrete examples and edge-case discussion.

- Weakness: effect of incorrect POS tags on overall performance is noted but not quantified.

- Question: why define 15 season types and how are concepts like Ramadan or “Holiday Season” handled?

- Question: clarify the discrepancy between mentioning "two benchmark datasets" and actually using three datasets.

- Question: please provide an example of a time expression that does not contain a time token.

- Question: given ~92% recall near the 93% token-coverage limit, what are the main avenues to further improve performance?

- Question: is there a plan to release source code and a complete rule specification for SynTime?

- Additional comment: the analysis of informal language in tweets (formal words still dominant) and the comparison with Twitter word clusters is insightful and valuable.
