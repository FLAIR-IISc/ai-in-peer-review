**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based approach for time expression recognition. The method is motivated by an empirical analysis of four datasets, which reveals that most time expressions are short, use a limited vocabulary, and crucially, almost always contain a core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday'). The SynTime system operates in three stages: it first identifies these time tokens in text using regular expressions and POS information, then heuristically expands the boundaries around these tokens to form candidate "time segments," and finally applies a set of rules to merge these segments into complete time expressions. The authors demonstrate through experiments on three datasets that their method significantly outperforms several state-of-the-art rule-based and machine-learning systems.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The proposal of SynTime, an effective and efficient rule-based tagger for time expressions. The system's novelty lies in its use of syntactic types and simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules, which contrasts with more complex, fixed-grammar systems.
2.  An insightful empirical analysis of time expressions across news, encyclopedic, and social media text. This analysis provides a strong justification for the system's design and lends support to the principle of least effort in this specific NLP context.
3.  A strong set of experimental results showing that a simple, well-motivated heuristic approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance, especially on informal text from Twitter where it shows marked improvement over existing methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The proposed method is not presented in a vacuum; instead, it is directly and logically motivated by the preliminary data analysis in Section 3. The four observations provide a compelling rationale for the core design choices, such as anchoring the recognition process on "time tokens."
2.  **Effective Simplicity:** The SynTime approach is conceptually simple and elegant. It forgoes complex, hand-crafted grammars or feature-engineered machine learning models in favor of a straightforward identify-and-expand strategy. The strength of this approach is validated by the excellent empirical results. The system achieves state-of-the-art or superior F1 scores on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets, demonstrating that its core principles are highly effective in practice.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation against three strong baselines across three distinct datasets. The results, particularly the consistently high recall scores, convincingly support the paper's central hypothesis that identifying a core time token is sufficient for recognizing the vast majority of time expressions. The performance on the Tweets dataset is especially impressive and highlights the method's robustness to informal language.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of specific details that would be necessary to fully reproduce the results. While the high-level methodology is clearly described, the implementation of a rule-based system depends entirely on the specifics of the rules and lexicons. The paper does not provide the complete set of regular expressions for the 15 time token types, the full list of modifier keywords, or the precise logic for the heuristic merging rules. Without access to these resources, it is difficult for the research community to build upon this work or verify its findings.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the syntactic types defined in Section 4.1, the paper mentions five tokens for the "SEASON" type. Could you please specify what these five seasons are? Additionally, how does the system architecture handle culturally specific temporal expressions (e.g., "Ramadan") or more general ones (e.g., "the holiday season")?
2.  The analysis in Section 3 notes that a small but non-trivial percentage (3-7%) of time expressions do not contain a "time token." Could you provide an example of such an expression from your datasets and clarify whether the current SynTime system is able to recognize it?
3.  The system's recall is already very high and approaches the theoretical performance ceiling suggested by your data analysis (i.e., the percentage of expressions containing a time token). What are the primary sources of the remaining errors (both precision and recall), and what do you see as the most promising avenues for future work to close this final performance gap?
4.  Given that the specific rules and keywords are central to this contribution, do you have plans to release the source code and the complete set of rules/regular expressions for SynTime? This would greatly benefit the community by ensuring reproducibility and facilitating future research.
