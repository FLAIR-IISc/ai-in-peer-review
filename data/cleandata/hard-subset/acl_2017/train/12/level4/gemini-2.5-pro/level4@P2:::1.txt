### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition. The method is motivated by a data-driven analysis of four corpora, which reveals that most time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and crucially, almost always contain a core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday').

The proposed approach first defines a set of syntactic types for words, categorizing them as time tokens, modifiers (e.g., 'next', 'ago'), or numerals. The recognition process operates in three stages: 1) It identifies time tokens in a given text using dictionary lookups and regular expressions, disambiguated by POS tags. 2) It applies simple heuristic rules to expand outwards from each time token, gathering adjacent modifiers and numerals to form "time segments." 3) It merges adjacent or overlapping time segments to form the final time expressions.

The authors evaluate SynTime against several state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning systems on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets corpus). The results show that SynTime significantly outperforms these baselines, particularly on the noisy Tweets dataset, achieving very high recall across all benchmarks.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Simple and Highly Effective Recognition Method (SynTime):** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. By focusing on a simple "find-and-expand" strategy anchored on core time tokens, the method achieves state-of-the-art performance while remaining conceptually simple and computationally lightweight. Its high recall, which validates the paper's core hypothesis, is particularly noteworthy.

2.  **Insightful Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The data analysis presented in Section 3 is a valuable contribution in its own right. The observations that over 93% of time expressions contain an identifiable time token and that the vocabulary is very small provide a strong empirical justification for the proposed method and offer useful insights for future research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method's effectiveness is its greatest strength. SynTime achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming established systems like HeidelTime and SUTime, as well as a learning-based system (UWTime), on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The exceptionally high recall (e.g., >92% relaxed recall on all datasets) demonstrates that the core assumption—that time expressions can be reliably identified by anchoring on a time token—is largely correct and leads to a very robust system.

2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The approach is refreshingly simple compared to complex, deeply-nested rule systems or feature-heavy machine learning models. The methodology, based on a small number of syntactic types and straightforward expansion/merging heuristics, is easy to understand and implement. The authors claim the system is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time," which makes it highly suitable for practical applications where speed and low computational overhead are important.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting their data-driven observations (Section 3) and then systematically building a method designed to leverage those specific findings (Section 4). This clear link between analysis and methodology makes the design choices feel well-justified and compelling.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Detail for Reproducibility:** The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of specific details needed to reproduce the work. While the high-level methodology is clear, the complete lists of tokens for the 15 time token types and 5 modifier types are not provided. Furthermore, the "simple heuristic rules" for boundary expansion and segment merging are described conceptually with examples but are not formally specified. Without access to the full rule set and lexicons, it is impossible for the research community to verify the results or build directly upon this system.

2.  **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the overall performance is strong, the paper would benefit from a more thorough error analysis. The central premise is that >93% of expressions contain a time token. It would be very instructive to see examples and a discussion of the remaining cases that do not contain a time token and understand how and why SynTime fails on them. A systematic analysis of false positives and false negatives would provide a clearer picture of the method's limitations and offer avenues for future improvement.

3.  **Ambiguity in the Type System:** The definition of the syntactic types is occasionally underspecified. For example, Section 4.1 mentions "SEASON (5)" but does not list the five tokens this type contains, leaving the reader to guess. It is also unclear how the system handles more complex or culturally specific temporal nouns (e.g., "the holiday season," "Ramadan") and whether they fit into the proposed type system. This ambiguity slightly undermines the claims of the system's clarity and extensibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that reproducibility is a key concern, do you plan to release the source code for SynTime, along with the complete set of token lists, regular expressions, and heuristic rules used in your experiments? Releasing these resources would be a major service to the community and would significantly increase the impact of your work.

2.  Your analysis shows that your method's core assumption covers 93-98% of time expressions, and your system's recall is already very high. This suggests the approach may be nearing a natural performance ceiling. Could you elaborate on your strategy for identifying the remaining, more challenging time expressions that do *not* contain one of your predefined time tokens?

3.  Could you please provide more detail on the composition of your syntactic types? For instance, what are the five tokens in the `SEASON` type? How does your system categorize and handle more descriptive temporal phrases like "the holiday season," which seem to fall outside the defined types?

### Additional Comments

This is a well-executed and clearly presented piece of work with impressive results. The following is a minor correction:
