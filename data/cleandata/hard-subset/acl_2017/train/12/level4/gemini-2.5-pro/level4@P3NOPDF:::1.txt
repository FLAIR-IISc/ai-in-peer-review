### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a rule-based approach for the extraction of temporal expressions. The methodology is founded on two key empirical observations: that temporal expressions are typically short, and that they almost always contain at least one "time token" (e.g., "Monday", "year", "evening"). The system operates in three stages. First, it identifies these core time tokens using a hybrid method that combines dictionary lookups, regular expression matching, and part-of-speech (POS) tag information. Second, once a token is identified, the system expands the candidate time segment bi-directionally, governed by a set of heuristic boundary rules. Finally, a third set of rules merges adjacent or related time segments into complete temporal expressions. The proposed system is evaluated against both rule-based and machine learning-based baselines across three datasets, demonstrating significant performance improvements.

### Strengths

The paper is well-written and the proposed approach is presented with exceptional clarity. The methodology is simple, intuitive, and effective. The heuristic rules, which form the core of the system, are well-motivated by empirical observations from the data. The strong performance reported in the evaluation section, outperforming more complex systems, provides compelling evidence for the justification of this design choice and highlights the power of a well-engineered, data-driven rule-based system.

### Weaknesses

While the high-level approach is clear, the paper lacks some specific details that would be necessary for full reproducibility. The effectiveness of this system is heavily dependent on the quality and comprehensiveness of the dictionaries and heuristic rules, but these are not fully enumerated. I have outlined specific points of underspecification in the "Detailed Comments" section below.

### Detailed Comments for the Authors

**Clarity and Reproducibility:**

*   **Section 4.1:** The paper mentions a dictionary of five seasons. For clarity, could the authors specify what these five seasons are? Furthermore, have the authors considered culturally or religiously specific temporal periods, such as "Ramadan" or the "Holiday Season," which function similarly to seasons but might not be captured by a standard list?
*   The success of this method hinges on the heuristic rules for expansion and merging. While the paper provides a good overview, a more detailed description or appendix with the full set of rules would be invaluable for reproducibility and for understanding the system's precise behavior. Is there any plan to release the full set of rules and/or the software implementation? Doing so would be a significant contribution to the community.

**Analysis and Future Work:**

*   **Section 5.2:** The paper notes that 7% of temporal expressions do not contain a "time token." It would be highly beneficial for the reader's understanding if the authors could provide an example of such an expression from the datasets and discuss the challenges in identifying it with the proposed framework.
*   **Section 5.2:** The system achieves an impressive 92% recall, which is very close to the theoretical ceiling of 93% (the proportion of expressions containing a time token). This is a strong result, but it also highlights a key limitation of the core assumption. Could the authors elaborate on potential future directions to capture the remaining expressions? Is it feasible to extend the current rule-based framework, or would a hybrid approach incorporating machine learning be necessary to bridge this final gap?

**Minor Correction:**

*   **Section 5.1:** The text mentions evaluating on "two benchmark datasets," but the tables and description appear to cover three distinct datasets. Please clarify or correct this minor inconsistency.
