- Paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition based on anchoring on core "time tokens."
- Data-driven analysis shows most time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and >93% contain an identifiable time token.
- SynTime classifies words into syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses a three-stage find-and-expand-and-merge recognition process.
- Evaluation on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets corpus shows SynTime outperforms rule-based and learning baselines, especially on noisy tweets, with very high recall.
- Main contribution: a simple, effective SynTime method achieving state-of-the-art performance with high recall and low computational cost.
- Main contribution: an empirical analysis demonstrating a small vocabulary and high prevalence of time tokens, justifying the method and informing future work.
- Strength: strong empirical performance, outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime with >92% relaxed recall across datasets.
- Strength: simplicity and efficiency—few syntactic types and straightforward heuristics make SynTime easy to implement and real-time.
- Strength: clear motivation and presentation linking data analysis to method design.
- Weakness: insufficient detail for reproducibility—lexicons and formalized heuristic rules are not provided.
- Weakness: limited error analysis—insufficient discussion of cases lacking time tokens and systematic false positive/negative analysis.
- Weakness: ambiguity in the type system—specific token lists (e.g., SEASON) and handling of culturally specific or descriptive temporal nouns are underspecified.
- Question: will the authors release SynTime source code, token lists, regexes, and heuristics to ensure reproducibility?
- Question: how do the authors plan to identify the remaining challenging time expressions that lack predefined time tokens?
- Question: request for detailed composition of syntactic types (e.g., the five SEASON tokens) and handling of phrases like "the holiday season."
- Minor comment: Section 5.1 incorrectly refers to "two benchmark datasets" though experiments use three.
