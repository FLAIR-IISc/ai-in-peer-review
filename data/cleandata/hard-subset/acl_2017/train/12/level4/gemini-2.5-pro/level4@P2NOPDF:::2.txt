This paper presents a rule-based method for recognizing temporal expressions in text. The simplicity of the approach is positioned as a significant advantage, given that temporal expression recognition serves as a foundational component for many downstream NLP applications. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating that the proposed system outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods, including both rule-based and machine learning-based approaches.

While the reported performance is impressive, my primary concern is the generalizability of the proposed method. The rules at the core of the system were designed based on manual observation of the same corpora that were subsequently used for evaluation. This methodology introduces a significant risk of overfitting, where the rules may be overly tuned to the specific characteristics, domains, and idiosyncrasies of the evaluation datasets rather than capturing more universal linguistic patterns.

This concern about generalization is twofold. First, the rules may not perform as well on new, unseen data because they are tailored to the evaluation corpora. Second, the specific domains of the corpora used for development may have heavily influenced the rule design, potentially limiting the method's applicability to other domains.

The authors could have partially mitigated these concerns by providing a statistical analysis of the development corpora. For instance, a discussion of the overlap in temporal expression types across the datasets would be valuable. If the corpora were shown to contain a diverse yet overlapping set of expressions, it would lend more credence to the generality of the derived rules. The paper currently lacks such an analysis or discussion.

To more convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness and robustness, the evaluation should have included a held-out test set from a corpus that was not used in any part of the rule design process. Such an experiment would provide much stronger evidence that the proposed method is widely effective and not just well-fitted to the specific datasets on which it was developed and tested.
