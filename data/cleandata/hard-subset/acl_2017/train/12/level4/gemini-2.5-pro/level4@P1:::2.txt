**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based method for recognizing time expressions in text. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora, which revealed that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain at least one core "time token." Based on these observations, the authors define a system of syntactic types for words in time expressions (e.g., time token, modifier, numeral). The recognition process involves three steps: first, identifying core time tokens; second, expanding their boundaries by searching for adjacent modifiers and numerals to form "time segments"; and third, merging these segments into complete time expressions. The authors claim that this type-based approach with simple heuristic rules is more flexible and extensible than existing rule-based systems. Experiments on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets) show that SynTime outperforms several state-of-the-art systems.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is SynTime, a simple and efficient rule-based tagger for time expression recognition. Its design, based on identifying core "time tokens" and then expanding their boundaries with heuristic rules, is intuitive and appears effective.
2.  A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis of time expressions across four different datasets, which provides useful observations that motivate the design of the proposed system and lend support to the "principle of least effort" in this context.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and, as a rule-based tagger, does not require a complex training pipeline. This is a significant advantage for a foundational task like time expression recognition, as the tool can be easily integrated into larger applications as a fast and lightweight pre-processing step.
2.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper reports impressive performance, with SynTime outperforming established rule-based and machine learning-based methods on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The consistently high recall scores are particularly noteworthy, suggesting that the core strategy of anchoring the search on "time tokens" is highly effective at identifying the vast majority of time expressions.
3.  **Extensibility:** The design of SynTime appears easy to extend. The authors demonstrate that adding a small number of new keywords can improve performance on a new domain (WikiWars), highlighting the system's potential for adaptation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Generalization Evidence and Potential Overfitting:** My main concern is the method's generality, which is not convincingly demonstrated. The rules and syntactic types were designed based on observations from a set of corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) that includes the very same corpora used for evaluation. This methodology creates a significant risk that the rules are over-tuned to the specific characteristics of the test data. An evaluation on a completely new corpus, unseen during the analysis and rule design phase, would be necessary to truly validate the claim that the method is widely effective.
2.  **Missing Inter-Corpus Analysis:** The paper would be stronger if it included a statistical analysis of the overlap in time expression patterns and vocabulary *across* the four analyzed corpora. For instance, showing that the most frequent time tokens and syntactic patterns are largely consistent between news articles, Wikipedia, and tweets would provide much-needed evidence to support the generality of the derived rules. Without this, it is difficult to judge whether the system's success is due to a genuinely universal set of patterns or simply the effective combination of patterns from the specific domains under study.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the process of rule development? Were the heuristic rules designed after observing all four datasets collectively? How did you ensure that the rules were not implicitly overfitted to the evaluation sets during this manual design process?
2.  To address the concerns about generality, could you report the results of a cross-domain experiment? For example, what is the performance of the initial system (SynTime-I), developed using only the TimeBank corpus, when evaluated directly on the WikiWars and Tweets test sets without any further expansion? This would provide a clearer picture of the method's out-of-the-box performance on unseen domains.
