Summary:
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora showing that time expressions are typically short, drawn from a small vocabulary, and almost always contain at least one “time token.” SynTime defines three syntactic types—time tokens, modifiers, and numerals—and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics around identified time tokens to form segments, then merges segments into final spans. The system is initialized with token-level regex resources (largely from SUTime) and can be expanded with a small number of domain-specific keywords. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset show strong results, particularly high recall.

Contributions:
- A type-based rule framework for time expression recognition that groups lexical items by syntactic behavior (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses boundary-expansion heuristics to compose full spans.
- An empirical analysis across four datasets supporting design choices (short spans, small vocabulary, high prevalence of time tokens), and a simple, extensible tagger that runs in real time.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art performance on TimeBank and superior performance on Tweets, with especially high recall across datasets.

Strengths:
- Clear, pragmatic design with strong empirical grounding: The paper’s observations (short spans, small lexicon, ≥93% contain a time token) convincingly motivate identifying time tokens first and expanding boundaries. This design yields high recall (≥92% relaxed recall on all datasets; very strong on Tweets).
- Lightweight, fast, and extensible: The system is simple to implement, runs in real time, and can be incrementally improved by adding a small number of keywords. The expansion results on WikiWars (notably recall gains) support the practicality of the approach.
- Competitive evaluation: On TimeBank and Tweets, SynTime outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime in F1 (strict and relaxed). The paper also offers a reasonable error analysis explanation for WikiWars (mismatch with TimeML span conventions and more descriptive expressions).
- Sensible use of syntactic types: Grouping by positionally defined modifier types (prefix/suffix/linkage) and distinguishing numerals from time tokens simplifies rule design and reduces brittle fixed-pattern rules seen in prior work.

Weaknesses:
- Reproducibility and underspecification: Several details are missing that are necessary to replicate results, including the full lists of token regexes and type assignments (especially after expansion), precise POS tag constraints used to disambiguate ambiguous tokens (e.g., May), and the exact rule set for segment merging (e.g., all comma/linkage cases). Without code or rule release, the work is difficult to verify.
- Dataset construction bias for Tweets: Tweets were pre-filtered with SUTime, then manually annotated. Although the authors estimate SUTime’s miss rate at ~3%, this pipeline risks selection bias that could inflate relative gains and recall. A fully random tweet sample with exhaustive annotation would be more reliable. Releasing the Tweets dataset would help.
- Novelty relative to prior rule-based systems: SUTime and HeidelTime also employ token-level regexes and expansion strategies (e.g., cascaded finite-state rules). SynTime’s main novelty is the syntactic type grouping and simplified expansion/merge heuristics, but the initialization reuses SUTime’s token resources. The contribution reads more as a useful simplification than a substantially new modeling paradigm.
- Evaluation and claims: On WikiWars, strict F1 lags behind HeidelTime/UWTime. The paper characterizes results as “comparable,” but stronger analysis would clarify where SynTime underperforms (e.g., prepositions, descriptive spans) and quantify trade-offs. Statistical significance testing and per-category breakdowns (dates, times, durations, ranges) are missing.
- Scope limitations: Recognition only; normalization is deferred, yet practical applications require both. The reliance on POS tagging (error-prone in informal text) is acknowledged but not quantified; it would help to show robustness to tagging noise.

Questions to Authors:
- Type inventories and coverage: Why are there “5” SEASON types? How are culturally specific periods handled (e.g., Ramadan, Lunar New Year, “holiday season”)? Are such items treated as HOLIDAY, SEASON, or TIME TOKEN subclasses? Please provide the complete type inventories after expansion for each dataset.
- POS disambiguation: What exact POS tag sets and constraints are used to filter ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May,” “sat”)? How sensitive is performance to tagging errors, especially on Tweets? Any fallback strategies if POS tags are unreliable?
- Segment merging rules: Could you publish the full set of merge heuristics, including all comma and linkage cases, range expressions (e.g., “5–7 pm,” “from 8 to 20 days”), and multi-clausal structures?
- Dataset construction: For Tweets, can you provide statistics on the distribution of types (date/time/duration), average span lengths, and the proportion of one-token vs multi-token expressions? Will you release the annotated tweet corpus?
- Baseline configuration: Were HeidelTime and SUTime tuned with their recommended settings for each domain? Any domain adaptation for WikiWars/Tweets? Please clarify to ensure fairness.
- Ceiling and future improvements: Given that ~93% of spans contain a time token and SynTime achieves ~92–98% relaxed recall, where do remaining errors come from (e.g., descriptive phrases, tokenization/POS errors, prepositions)? What concrete steps (e.g., learned disambiguation, character patterns for noisy text, joint normalization) are planned to push beyond the observed ceiling?

Additional Comments:
- Clarity/consistency: Section 5.1 refers to “two benchmark datasets” but you evaluate on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). Please fix.
- Examples: Add at least one illustrative example of a time expression without an explicit time token and show how SynTime handles it (or fails).
- Claims: Where you say “significantly outperforms,” please include significance tests or temper the language.
- References: Several entries contain typos/incomplete metadata; please clean up citation formatting.
- Release: Consider releasing the rule files, token regex resources (including expansions), and code to improve reproducibility.
