1) Paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system that identifies time tokens via dictionary/regex+POS, expands boundaries heuristically, and merges segments to recognize time expressions.  
2) Design motivated by dataset analysis showing time expressions are short, use a small lexicon, and almost always contain a “time token.”  

Main contributions:
3) A simple type-driven rule system (time token, modifier, numeral) with boundary-expansion heuristics achieving high accuracy and recall in real time.  
4) Empirical analysis across four datasets that justifies the design (brevity, small lexicon, frequent time tokens, similar syntactic behavior).  
5) Comprehensive evaluation vs. HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, showing consistent improvements and easy extensibility via small lexicon additions.  

Strengths:
6) Strong empirical performance with especially high recall (≥92% relaxed) and strong F1, notably robust on noisy Twitter data.  
7) Simple, well-motivated, data-driven design where boundary expansion avoids brittle fixed-length patterns.  
8) Lightweight, fast, and easy to extend with modest lexicon additions to boost recall across domains.  
9) Clear articulation of how the syntactic/type framing differs from prior semantically organized rule sets.  

Weaknesses:
10) Reproducibility/specification gaps: some rule counts and ambiguity-filtering details are underspecified.  
11) Limited discussion of coverage boundaries and how adherence to TimeML conventions affects performance on more descriptive corpora like WikiWars.  
12) Novelty concerns: approach (regex over tokens + expansion) is close to prior systems; lacks sharper quantitative ablations of key design choices.  
13) Dependence on POS accuracy/tokenization: errors in noisy text may propagate and impact performance.  
14) Ceiling effects: with ~93% of spans containing a time token and SynTime near that recall, the path to improving the remaining cases is unclear.  

Questions for authors:
15) Clarify type definitions: why five SEASON entries and how culturally specific seasons/periods (e.g., “Ramadan,” “holiday season”) are typed.  
16) Clarify dataset wording: Section 5.1 refers to “two benchmark datasets” despite evaluating three—please reconcile.  
17) Provide a worked example of a recognized time expression that lacks a time token and show how dependent-segment logic handles it.  
18) Describe strategies to capture remaining cases beyond token-based coverage (purely descriptive times, pragmatic references, excluded prepositional phrases).  
19) Will you release the full rule set, lexicons, and code (including dataset-specific expanded variants) to aid reproducibility?  
20) Provide ablation/error analysis: quantify contributions of POS filtering, boundary-expansion heuristics, modifier types, and breakdown of miss types (token vs. boundary vs. merge).
