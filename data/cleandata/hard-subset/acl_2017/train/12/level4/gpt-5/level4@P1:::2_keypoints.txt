- Proposes SynTime: a rule-based time expression recognizer using syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and heuristic boundary-expansion/merge rules.
- Claims SynTime outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, with especially strong recall and real-time performance.
- Argues time expressions are short, drawn from a small shared vocabulary, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior, motivating type-based rules.

- Strong empirical results: higher F1 on TimeBank/Tweets, competitive on WikiWars, and consistently high recall useful for downstream temporal reasoning.
- Simple and interpretable: few syntactic types and lightweight heuristics make the approach transparent and easy to inspect.
- Real-time and easy to extend: lightweight runtime and coverage can be expanded by adding a small number of domain keywords.

- Risk of overfitting: rules were developed from the same corpora used in evaluation, raising concerns about generality and corpus-specific tuning.
- Missing quantitative cross-corpus overlap evidence: no statistics (e.g., Jaccard similarity) for overlap in time tokens, modifiers, or pattern schemas to support generalization claims.
- Evaluation design could better isolate generalization: no held-out corpus or domain to test out-of-domain robustness.

- Were rule design and expansion strictly separated from the test sets, or were tokens/patterns added after inspecting test portions?
- Please report overlap statistics across corpora for (a) time tokens, (b) modifier tokens, and (c) common pattern templates.
- Do you have results on an additional held-out corpus not used in rule development to demonstrate out-of-domain performance?
- How sensitive is SynTime to domain-specific expansions; what is the performance drop if corpus-derived expansions are removed?
- Can you provide an error analysis showing where SynTime fails relative to HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime, especially on infrequent or descriptive patterns?

- Overall: a clear, effective, and lightweight system with strong results, but limited evidence for cross-domain generality; held-out evaluation and overlap analyses would substantially strengthen the paper.
