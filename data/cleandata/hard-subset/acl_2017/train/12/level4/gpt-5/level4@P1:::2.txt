Summary and contributions
- This paper presents SynTime, a simple, rule-based method for recognizing time expressions in text. The approach defines syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses heuristic boundary-expansion rules to form time segments and merge them into full time expressions.
- Empirically, SynTime reportedly outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a learning-based system (UWTime) on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a Tweets dataset, with especially strong recall and real-time performance.
- The paper also provides an analysis of time expressions across several corpora and argues that time expressions are short, draw from a small vocabulary, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior—motivating a type-based rule design.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across benchmarks: The method achieves higher F1 than prominent baselines on TimeBank and Tweets and competitive results on WikiWars, with consistently high recall. High recall is particularly valuable for downstream temporal reasoning tasks, and the reported gains over both rule-based and learning-based baselines are noteworthy.
- Simplicity and interpretability: The approach is conceptually simple (few syntactic types, lightweight heuristics) and easy to inspect. Given that time expression recognition is a foundational step in many applications, a transparent, dependable tagger is practically attractive.
- Real-time performance and ease of extension: The system is lightweight and designed to run in real time. The paper emphasizes that extending coverage primarily requires adding a small number of keywords per domain, which is feasible in practice, especially if most time expressions use a compact vocabulary.

Weaknesses
- Potential overfitting to the evaluation corpora: The rules were designed based on observations taken from the same corpora used in evaluation. This raises concerns about generality and possible overfitting to corpus-specific patterns or domains. Without a clear separation between the analysis/design phase and the test data, it is difficult to assess true out-of-domain robustness.
- Lack of quantitative evidence on cross-corpus overlap: The paper argues that time expressions are short and drawn from a small, recurring vocabulary, but it does not provide statistics on overlap across corpora (e.g., overlap in time tokens, modifier types, or pattern schemas). Demonstrating high overlap (e.g., Jaccard similarity of token sets or pattern distributions) would substantively support the claim that the rules generalize.
- Evaluation design could better isolate generalization: A stronger test of generality would evaluate on a corpus not used in rule development or expansion. As it stands, domains represented in the observed corpora may have influenced the rules. An additional held-out domain (e.g., clinical, legal, or different social media genres) would bolster claims of broad effectiveness.

Questions to authors
- How strictly were the rule design and expansion processes separated from the test sets for each dataset? For example, were any tokens or patterns added based on inspection of the test portions of TimeBank, WikiWars, or Tweets?
- Can you report quantitative overlap across corpora for (a) time tokens, (b) modifier tokens, and (c) common pattern templates (e.g., MONTH NUMERAL YEAR)? Even simple set-overlap or distributional similarity measures would help substantiate generality.
- Do you have results on an additional corpus not used in your initial analysis or rule expansion, to demonstrate out-of-domain performance?
- How sensitive is SynTime to domain-specific expansions? For instance, if you remove expansions derived from a given corpus, how much does performance drop on that corpus, and how well does the base system transfer?
- Could you provide an error analysis highlighting where SynTime fails relative to HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime, especially on patterns that are less frequent or more descriptive?

Overall evaluation
The paper proposes a clear, effective, and lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition, with strong empirical results and appealing practical properties. My main reservation is the lack of compelling evidence for generality across domains due to the rule design process being intertwined with the evaluated corpora and the absence of cross-corpus overlap statistics. Demonstrating performance on a held-out corpus and providing quantitative overlap analyses would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims.
