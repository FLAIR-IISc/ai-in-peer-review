Summary and contributions (as I see them)
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four datasets showing that most time expressions are short, drawn from a small vocabulary, and almost always contain at least one “time token.” SynTime first identifies time tokens via dictionary/regex with POS filtering, then expands boundaries heuristically to include nearby modifiers and numerals, and finally merges segments into full expressions. The system is evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a Tweets dataset, showing strong gains, especially in recall and on social media.

Main contributions (ordered by significance)
1) A simple, type-driven rule system (time token, modifier, numeral) with boundary-expansion heuristics that achieves strong accuracy and very high recall across domains while running in real time.
2) An empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets, yielding actionable observations (brevity, small lexicon, frequent presence of time tokens, and similar syntactic behavior) that justify the system design.
3) Comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art rule-based and learning-based baselines on three datasets, including a Twitter corpus, demonstrating consistent improvements and easy extensibility via small lexicon additions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: The reported results show SynTime (both initial and expanded versions) outperforming or matching HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and Tweets, with especially high recall (≥92% in relaxed match) and strong F1 (often >91%). The Twitter results are notably strong despite domain noisiness, supporting the claim that the approach is robust and practical.
- Simple, well-motivated design: The rules are grounded in clear, data-driven observations (e.g., >93% of expressions include a time token). The boundary-expansion strategy avoids brittle fixed-length patterns and appears to explain the system’s flexibility versus more rigid rule sets.
- Lightweight and fast: The system is real-time and easy to extend by adding a small number of keywords or variants. The expansion experiments (few additions for TimeBank/Tweets, more for WikiWars) show that modest domain-specific lexicon growth can meaningfully boost recall without complex retraining.
- Clear articulation of differences from prior rule-based work: The syntactic type framing and positional heuristics offer a coherent alternative to semantically organized rule inventories (e.g., SUTime), and the contrast is explained well in the paper.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and specification gaps: Several details appear underspecified, making exact reproduction difficult. Examples include unclear coverage and counts of certain types (e.g., “SEASON” has 5 entries—what are they?) and how ambiguous tokens are filtered beyond high-level POS guidance. More precise rule inventories and thresholds would help.
- Limited discussion of coverage boundaries: The adherence to TimeML/TimeBank conventions (e.g., excluding many prepositions) is sensible for comparability but likely depresses performance on corpora like WikiWars that include more descriptive, prepositional, or discourse-driven time mentions. The paper could better justify these design choices and quantify their impact.
- Novelty relative to existing rule-based systems: While the syntactic type framing and boundary heuristics are clean, the overall approach (regex over tokens + expansion) is close in spirit to prior systems. The paper would benefit from a sharper, quantitative ablation illustrating which rule design choices (types vs. POS filters vs. expansion logic) drive the observed gains over SUTime/HeidelTime.
- Dependence on POS accuracy and tokenization: The method relies on correct POS tags to resolve ambiguities (e.g., “May”). Errors in noisy text (as acknowledged by the authors) may propagate. It would be useful to quantify performance drops under realistic tagging noise, especially on social media.
- Ceiling effects and future improvements: Given that ~93% of gold spans contain a time token and SynTime already achieves ~92% recall, the path to further gains is unclear. The paper does not lay out concrete strategies for the remaining ~7% of expressions without time tokens or those missed due to boundary/merging errors.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Type definitions: Why are there five seasons? How are culturally specific seasons or periods handled (e.g., “holiday season,” “Ramadan”)? Are these modeled as SEASON, HOLIDAY, PERIOD, or TIME TOKEN elsewhere? Please clarify type assignment guidelines and examples.
- Dataset wording: In Section 5.1, you refer to “two benchmark datasets” but evaluate on three datasets overall. Please clarify terminology and splits.
- Examples without a time token: Could you include at least one fully worked example of a recognized time expression that lacks a time token, and show how the dependent segment logic handles it?
- Beyond the token-based ceiling: Given the high prevalence of time tokens and current recall, what techniques do you envision to capture the remaining cases (e.g., purely descriptive times, pragmatic references like “the next morning,” or prepositional phrases excluded by TimeML conventions)?
- Release and reproducibility: Will you release the full rule set, lexicons, and code (including the expanded variants per dataset)? That would greatly aid replication and comparison.
- Error analysis and ablations: Can you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of POS filtering, boundary-expansion heuristics, and specific modifier types? An error breakdown by category (missed token vs. boundary vs. merge) would also clarify where improvements are most needed.
