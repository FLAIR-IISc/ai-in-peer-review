Summary
This paper presents a rule-based method for time expression extraction built on two key observations: (1) temporal mentions are typically short, and (2) most contain at least one salient “time token.” The system first identifies time tokens via dictionary lookup and regular expressions informed by part-of-speech (POS) tags. It then expands spans bidirectionally from each token using heuristic boundary rules and finally merges adjacent or overlapping spans into complete time expressions using an additional set of rules. Experiments on three datasets show substantial gains over both prior rule-based and machine learning (ML) baselines.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and pipeline: The design is simple, interpretable, and well motivated by empirical properties of temporal mentions.
- Strong empirical results: The approach achieves significant improvements across three datasets, suggesting robustness.
- Practicality: The emphasis on high-precision token detection followed by constrained expansion appears effective and computationally light.
- Writing and presentation: The method and results are generally well explained.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility: Several components (token lexicons, regex patterns, POS tagger choice, and exact expansion/merge heuristics) are under-specified, making replication difficult.
- Coverage and generality: The treatment of seasonal and culturally specific temporal expressions (e.g., “Ramadan,” “holiday season”) is not clearly defined, raising questions about coverage beyond standard calendars.
- Ceiling effects and error analysis: Given that ~93% of expressions contain a time token and recall is ~92%, the paper should discuss the remaining gap, failure modes, and how to improve beyond the current ceiling.
- Minor inconsistencies: There appears to be a discrepancy regarding the number of datasets used.

Detailed comments and questions
- Section 4.1 (Seasons): Why are five seasons listed? How are seasonal expressions defined and normalized in your scheme? Do you handle culturally specific or domain-specific seasons (e.g., “Ramadan,” “Monsoon,” “holiday season,” “back-to-school season”)? Please clarify how such expressions are recognized and whether they are included in your lexicon, regexes, or handled by dedicated rules.
- Section 5.1 (Datasets): The abstract and/or main text alternately refer to “two benchmark datasets” and “three datasets.” Please reconcile this and clearly list all datasets, their sizes, domains, and splits. Indicate whether any development set was used for rule tuning.
- Section 5.2 (Examples): Including one or two annotated examples of correctly identified expressions and at least one example of a gold temporal mention that lacks a canonical “time token” would help readers understand boundary rules and failure cases.
- Performance ceiling and future improvements: If ~93% of gold mentions contain a token and your system attains ~92% recall, what drives the residual errors? Are they mainly boundary errors, missed token detection, or merge errors? What strategies could address token-less mentions (e.g., “a fortnight later,” “the following spring,” “late last quarter”)? Consider:
  - Expanding the lexicon with domain- and culture-specific variants (e.g., holiday names, fiscal quarters).
  - Character-level or subword patterns to capture morphological variants and ordinals not present in the dictionary.
  - A lightweight learned boundary classifier constrained by the current token detection, to improve expansion without sacrificing precision.
  - Contextual cues (dependency or chunk patterns) for constructions like “the week after,” “two days earlier,” and deictic references.
- Rules and merging: The paper would benefit from a concise but precise description (or pseudocode) of:
  - The expansion stopping criteria (e.g., punctuation, conjunctions, prepositions).
  - How conflicting expansions from adjacent tokens are resolved.
  - Merge rules and priorities when spans overlap or are contiguous (e.g., “Friday and Saturday morning”).
  - Handling of nested or coordinated structures and prepositional phrases (“in early May,” “at around 3 pm,” “from 9 to 11”).
- Evaluation details:
  - Specify the evaluation metric(s): strict vs. relaxed span matching, micro/macro averaging, and whether partial credit is given.
  - Report precision/recall/F1 per dataset and, if possible, per category (dates, times, durations, sets), since some types (e.g., durations, sets) can be harder for rule-based methods.
  - Clarify whether normalization (TIMEX3 value assignment) is evaluated, or if the task is span detection only.
  - Include ablations: token detection only; token + expansion (no merge); full system. This would quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Provide statistical significance testing for the reported gains.
- Practical aspects:
  - Domain robustness: How well do the rules transfer across domains (news, clinical, social media)? Were the dictionaries tuned per domain?
  - Runtime: Reporting throughput or complexity would strengthen the engineering story.
  - Error analysis: A brief categorization (e.g., missed deictics, boundary overshoot, rare lexical items) would make the findings more actionable.

Reproducibility and release
- To enable replication, please list or release:
  - The full set of regexes, dictionaries (with sources and versioning), POS tagger and its version, preprocessing steps, and rule order/priority.
  - Dataset splits and any tuning procedure.
  - A small development config or seed rules if the full set cannot be released due to IP constraints.
- Do you plan to release the rules and/or software? If so, under what license and with what documentation?

Overall assessment
This is a clear, well-motivated rule-based approach that achieves strong results with a simple and interpretable pipeline. The work would be significantly strengthened by clarifying underspecified components, reconciling dataset counts, and adding error analysis and ablations. Addressing culturally specific temporal expressions and discussing paths beyond the current apparent ceiling would also improve the paper’s impact and generality. Pending these clarifications, the method appears practically useful and competitive.
