Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions using a small set of syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple, boundary-expansion heuristics. The approach is motivated by an empirical analysis of four corpora showing that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, almost always include a time token, and display consistent syntactic behavior.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A type-based, syntactic design for time expression recognition with simple, position-sensitive heuristics that enable flexibility and real-time performance.
2) An empirical analysis across multiple datasets that informs the design and supports a “least-effort” hypothesis for time expression use.
3) Strong empirical results: SynTime outperforms or matches HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and Tweets and is competitive on WikiWars, with consistently high recall.

Strengths
- Simple, linguistically grounded design with clear rationale. The definition of types based on syntactic behavior and the boundary-expansion rules are intuitive, transparent, and easy to inspect. This stands in contrast to large fixed rule sets or opaque learned models. The approach is well motivated by the authors’ analysis (short spans, small vocabularies, high prevalence of time tokens), and the design aligns with those observations.

- Strong and consistent performance, especially recall. SynTime achieves best or second-best F1 on most settings and the best recall across all datasets (e.g., >92% relaxed recall). The especially strong results on Tweets (strict F1 ~91.7, relaxed F1 ~95.9) suggest robustness in informal text, where many systems struggle. The high recall is practically important for downstream tasks requiring coverage.

- Lightweight, fast, and extensible. SynTime runs in real time and can be extended by adding token-level patterns or keywords, which is attractive for practitioners and low-resource settings. The comparison between SynTime-I (initial) and SynTime-E (expanded) shows that small, targeted expansions can improve recall, especially in a domain like WikiWars.

- Thorough comparisons and clear reporting. The paper compares against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on shared benchmarks with both strict and relaxed metrics, and includes an informative contrast between SynTime-I and SynTime-E. The authors discuss error sources such as POS tagging, giving a realistic sense of limitations.

Weaknesses
- Potential overfitting and limited evidence of generality. The rules, types, and expansions are derived from the same datasets used for evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). For WikiWars and TimeBank, it is unclear whether any development/test separation was enforced when adding tokens (Table 5 lists expansions by dataset). This risks tuning to corpus idiosyncrasies and inflating results. A held-out domain not used in observations or expansion (e.g., another newswire set, forum text, or clinical notes) would better support generality.

- Sampling bias in the Tweets dataset. Tweets were pre-selected using SUTime, which likely biases the dataset toward expressions SUTime can find. Although the authors estimate that SUTime misses ~3% of time-expressive tweets, the filtering may still skew distributions and underrepresent hard cases. This affects fairness when comparing to other systems.

- Limited error analysis and ablations. The paper offers few insights into failure modes (e.g., types of missed expressions, precision errors in relaxed match), the impact of each modifier type, or the contribution of individual heuristic choices (e.g., merge rules at commas/linkers). An ablation study would help validate design decisions and inform future extensions.

- Dependence on POS tagging with limited robustness analysis. While the paper notes POS errors (especially in tweets), it does not quantify their impact on performance or explore mitigation (e.g., alternate taggers or POS-agnostic fallbacks). Given that type assignment often relies on POS, this is a nontrivial source of error.

- Scope and practicality: normalization not addressed. Recognition is only one part of temporal tagging pipelines; normalization is often required for downstream tasks. While out-of-scope, the absence of even a basic normalization component or an interface to existing normalizers limits practical utility and comparability to full systems.

Questions to Authors
- Expansion protocol: For TimeBank and WikiWars, did you add new tokens by inspecting the same data on which you report final results? If so, can you provide results for strictly held-out splits or leave-one-document-out evaluation without expansion on the test portion?

- Cross-domain generalization: Can you report results on at least one additional corpus not used in your observations or expansions (e.g., another newswire set, clinical notes, or forum data)? Alternatively, a leave-one-corpus-out evaluation (design on three, test on the fourth) would help quantify generality.

- Overlap statistics: Please report vocabulary overlap (time tokens and modifiers) across datasets, and the proportion of test expressions covered by token sets learned elsewhere. This would substantiate claims about small vocabularies and cross-domain portability.

- Impact of POS accuracy: How sensitive is performance to POS tagging errors? Do results change significantly with a Twitter-specific tagger for Tweets, or with a POS-agnostic fallback for ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May”)?

- Ablation and rule sensitivity: What is the contribution of each modifier type (PREFIX/SUFFIX/LINKAGE/COMMA/IN_ARTICLE) and of the boundary-expansion rules (e.g., merging at commas)? An ablation would help validate the heuristic choices.

- Efficiency and reproducibility: Please provide runtime/memory numbers and clarify whether code, lexicons, and expansion lists will be released. Since you reuse SUTime token regexes, are there licensing constraints?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with a simple and effective approach that achieves strong results and is likely to be useful in practice. Addressing the generalization and evaluation-design concerns (held-out domains, reduced sampling bias, and deeper error/ablation analysis) would substantially strengthen the paper.
