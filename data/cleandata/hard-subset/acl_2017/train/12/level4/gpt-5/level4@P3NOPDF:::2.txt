This paper proposes a rule-based method for recognizing time expressions in text. The approach is deliberately simple and transparent, which is a strength for downstream analysis and as a foundational component in larger systems. The experimental results indicate that the method outperforms both state-of-the-art rule-based systems and competitive machine-learning baselines on the evaluated datasets.

Strengths:
- Clarity and simplicity: The rule-based design is easy to understand and audit, which is valuable for error analysis and integration into pipelines.
- Empirical performance: The reported gains over strong baselines suggest the rules capture important regularities in time expression patterns.
- Practical relevance: Time expression recognition is a core NLP capability, and a robust, interpretable solution has clear utility.

Main concerns:
- Generality and potential overfitting: The rules appear to have been designed based on the same corpora used for evaluation. This raises the risk that they are tuned to corpus-specific idiosyncrasies rather than encoding domain-independent regularities. Domain characteristics (e.g., newswire vs. clinical vs. informal text) may have influenced rule crafting, limiting portability.
- Lack of overlap analysis: The paper does not present quantitative evidence about the overlap or coverage of time expression types across the corpora used to guide rule design. Without statistics on shared vs. corpus-specific expressions, it is difficult to assess how broadly applicable the rules are.
- Experimental design: Because there is no evaluation on a corpus that was not involved—directly or indirectly—in the rule development process, the current results may overestimate real-world effectiveness.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- Provide a clear separation between development and evaluation. For example, conduct leave-one-corpus-out experiments: design or tune rules on all but one corpus and evaluate on the held-out corpus, rotating through all corpora. This would directly test generalization.
- Add an external test set that played no role in rule design (ideally from a different domain). Demonstrating strong performance on a truly unseen corpus would substantially support claims of generality.
- Report coverage and overlap statistics across corpora. For instance:
  - The proportion of time expression types shared across datasets versus unique to a single dataset.
  - Per-category precision/recall (e.g., dates, times, durations, sets) to reveal where the rules generalize or fail.
  - Distributional differences in lexical and formatting variants (e.g., numeric vs. written forms, timezone markers, relative expressions).
- Include an error analysis focused on out-of-domain failures and rare or ambiguous forms. Identifying systematic errors can guide minimal extensions that improve robustness without inflating rule complexity.
- Clarify the rule development protocol to minimize leakage (e.g., using a predefined rule template library, specifying which corpora informed which rules, and documenting any tuning iterations).
- If possible, report results under domain shift conditions (e.g., by injecting noise, different tokenization, or varying punctuation conventions) to test robustness.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses an important task and presents a well-motivated, interpretable approach with strong reported results. However, the evidence for generality is currently limited by the tight coupling between rule design and evaluation data. Adding a rigorously separated, cross-domain evaluation—along with coverage/overlap statistics and targeted error analyses—would substantially strengthen the contribution and bolster the claim that the method is broadly effective beyond the observed corpora.
