Summary and contributions:
- This paper presents a rule-based approach (SynTime) for time expression recognition. The core insight is that time expressions are typically short and almost always contain at least one “time token.” The system first detects time tokens via dictionary lookup and token-level regular expressions with POS constraints, then expands boundaries heuristically to include nearby modifiers/numerals, and finally merges segments into full time expressions.
- The main contribution, in my view, is a simple, type-based rule design that is guided by empirical observations and yields strong performance across datasets, outperforming both rule-based and learning-based baselines.
- A secondary contribution is the analysis of multiple corpora to motivate the type system and heuristic expansion strategy, together with evidence that the approach is lightweight, real-time, and easily extensible by adding keywords.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized presentation. The pipeline (token identification → boundary expansion → segment merging) is easy to follow, and the heuristic rules are motivated by corpus analysis rather than ad hoc design.
- Strong empirical results. The method achieves substantial improvements over both rule-based (HeidelTime, SUTime) and learning-based (UWTime) systems on three datasets, including noisy tweets, which validates the generality of the approach.
- Practicality and extensibility. The system is lightweight, runs in real time, and can be adapted to new domains by simply adding token-level keywords; this lowers deployment and maintenance costs compared to more complex rule systems or supervised models.

Weaknesses:
- Reproducibility gaps. Certain design choices and resources are underspecified, making it hard to fully reproduce results. In particular, the definition and coverage of specific types (e.g., the “SEASON” category) and examples for edge cases (e.g., expressions without explicit time tokens) are not detailed enough. See questions below for concrete points.
- Taxonomy clarity. The paper mentions five seasons and special cases such as “Holiday Season,” but it is unclear how these are enumerated or how religious/holiday-related expressions (e.g., Ramadan) are handled within the type system.
- Dataset description inconsistency. Section 5.1 refers to “two benchmark datasets,” but the experiments span three datasets. This should be clarified to avoid confusion when interpreting results and setup.
- Ceiling effects and future improvements. Given that at least 93% of expressions contain a time token and the system already attains 92% recall, the paper should discuss how to push beyond this ceiling, especially for the minority of expressions without an explicit time token.
- Release and replication. It is not stated whether the full set of rules and code will be released. Without this, it will be difficult for others to verify or build upon the work.

Questions to authors:
- Section 4.1: Why are there five seasons? Please specify the exact list and rationale. How does the system treat expressions like “Ramadan,” “holiday season,” or “the festive season”? Are these mapped to SEASON, HOLIDAY, or handled via another type?
- Section 5.1: The text says “two benchmark datasets,” but the experiments include three datasets. Did you mean “three datasets”? Please clarify the setup and which are considered benchmarks.
- Section 5.2: Please provide at least one concrete example of a recognized time expression that does not contain a time token, and explain how the dependent segment rules capture it.
- On headroom: Since ≥93% of expressions contain a time token and your recall is ~92%, what strategies do you envision to improve recall further? For instance, do you plan to expand coverage for expressions without explicit tokens, incorporate lightweight context features, or refine POS/lexical disambiguation?
- Will you release the complete rule set, token regex resources, and software used in the experiments? If so, under what license and with what documentation to enable reproduction?

Overall assessment:
The paper proposes a simple, well-motivated, and effective rule-based tagger that achieves strong, state-of-the-art results while remaining lightweight and extensible. Addressing the specification gaps (type definitions, dataset description, edge-case examples) and clarifying release plans would significantly improve reproducibility and impact. I lean positive, contingent on these clarifications.
