Summary
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in text. The approach defines a small set of syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple, position-sensitive heuristics to expand from detected time tokens to complete time expressions. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets indicate that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a learning-based system (UWTime), particularly in recall and on tweets.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical, type-based rule system for time expression recognition that is simple, interpretable, and fast.
- Strong empirical results showing improvements over both rule-based and learning-based baselines across multiple datasets, with especially high recall.
- An analysis of time expressions motivating a small vocabulary and consistent syntactic behavior, which informs the rule design.

Strengths
- Simplicity and interpretability: The type-based rules are easy to understand and modify, making SynTime suitable as a robust preprocessing tool in downstream applications.
- Empirical performance: The method outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on two datasets and is competitive on WikiWars, with particularly strong recall and excellent results on tweets.
- Practicality: The system is lightweight, runs in real time, and can be extended by adding keywords; this makes it attractive for deployment and domain adaptation.
- Clear methodology: The paper articulates the type system and the boundary-expansion heuristics clearly, facilitating reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Potential overfitting and domain leakage: The rules were designed based on the same corpora (or domains) used for evaluation. This risks overfitting and limits claims about generality. It is unclear which datasets influenced which rules and to what extent.
- Lack of cross-corpus overlap analysis: The paper does not quantify how much the time expressions (tokens, modifiers, and common patterns) overlap across the analyzed datasets. Demonstrating high overlap would support the generality of the rules; conversely, low overlap would argue for broader validation.
- Missing truly held-out evaluation: There is no evaluation on a corpus that was not consulted during rule design and expansion. A leave-one-corpus-out or out-of-domain test would better establish robustness and mitigate concerns of circularity.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Rule design provenance: Which datasets were used to craft the initial rules and which to guide expansion? Can you quantify how many rules or keywords were influenced by each corpus?
- Overlap statistics: What proportion of time tokens, modifiers, and syntactic patterns are shared across TimeBank, WikiWars, Gigaword, and Tweets? Please report overlap measures (e.g., Jaccard) at the token, type, and pattern levels.
- Held-out validation: Can you provide results for a leave-one-corpus-out setup (e.g., no exposure to WikiWars during rule design/expansion, then test on WikiWars), or test on an entirely new corpus? This would directly address generality.
- Ablations: Beyond SynTime-I vs. SynTime-E, can you show performance without POS constraints, or without specific modifier types, to assess sensitivity and generality of the heuristics?

Overall assessment
The approach is elegant and practical, with convincing performance gains. However, the lack of a clearly disjoint evaluation and of quantitative overlap analysis weakens the claim of generality. Demonstrating held-out, cross-domain robustness and providing overlap statistics would substantially strengthen the paper.
