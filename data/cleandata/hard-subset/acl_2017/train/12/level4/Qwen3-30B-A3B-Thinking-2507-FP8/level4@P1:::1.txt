

This paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors conduct a thorough analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), revealing four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) a small vocabulary of time-related keywords is used, (3) over 93% of expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words within time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, the authors propose a syntactic type system with three main types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion. SynTime identifies time tokens first, then expands to include modifiers and numerals, and finally merges time segments into complete expressions. The approach is lightweight, runs in real-time, and can be easily expanded by adding domain-specific keywords.

The main contributions are:
1. A data-driven analysis revealing time expressions' characteristics that provide empirical support for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949).
2. A novel type-based approach for time expression recognition that defines syntactic types rather than semantic ones, with simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion.
3. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art methods, particularly on Twitter data.

Strengths:
1. The paper's core insight—that time expressions follow predictable syntactic patterns—leads to a surprisingly effective and lightweight solution. The authors' data analysis convincingly demonstrates that time expressions are short, use minimal vocabulary, and follow consistent syntactic patterns, providing a solid foundation for their approach.
2. The method's simplicity and real-time performance represent a significant practical advantage over both rule-based and machine learning approaches. SynTime achieves superior recall (95.65% on TimeBank and 98.31% on Tweets) while maintaining high precision, demonstrating that a simple rule-based system can outperform more complex alternatives.
3. The authors' thoughtful discussion of the principle of least effort provides a compelling theoretical framework for their approach. Their observation that time expressions follow linguistic patterns similar to part-of-speech grouping (Manning and Schutze, 1999) is particularly insightful and well-supported by their data analysis.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the system, particularly regarding the syntactic type definitions. Section 4.1 mentions "5 seasons" but doesn't specify what these are (e.g., are they January-February-March, etc.? How are they defined?), and it doesn't address how the system handles domain-specific terms like "Ramadan" or "Holiday Season."
2. The experimental section contains inconsistencies. Section 5.1 states they use "two benchmark datasets" when they actually use three (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets), and Section 5.2 would benefit from an example of a time expression without a time token to clarify the approach's limitations.
3. The paper doesn't adequately address the system's limitations when encountering time expressions that don't contain time tokens. Given that 93% of expressions contain time tokens, the system achieves high recall, but the authors don't explain how they handle the remaining 7% or plan to improve beyond this ceiling.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the definition of the "5 seasons" mentioned in Section 4.1 and explain how the system handles domain-specific time expressions like "Ramadan" or "Holiday Season"?
2. The paper states they use "two benchmark datasets" in Section 5.1, but they actually evaluate on three datasets. Could you please correct this inconsistency?
3. Given the high recall (95.65% on TimeBank) achieved by SynTime, which aligns with the observation that 93% of expressions contain time tokens, how do you plan to further improve performance on expressions without time tokens?
4. Will you release the full set of rules and software implementation to facilitate reproduction and further development?
