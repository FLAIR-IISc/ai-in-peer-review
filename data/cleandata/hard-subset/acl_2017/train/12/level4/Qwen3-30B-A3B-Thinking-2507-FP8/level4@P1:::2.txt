

Summary: The paper proposes a rule-based approach called SynTime for time expression recognition, which leverages syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets and make four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) over 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words within time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they define three main syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, and numerals) and design simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition. The main contributions are: (1) an insightful analysis of time expressions that supports the principle of least effort in language usage, (2) a lightweight rule-based approach that outperforms state-of-the-art methods, and (3) a simple, expandable framework for time expression recognition.

Strengths: 
1. The paper makes a compelling analysis of time expressions that reveals fundamental patterns in language usage. The four observations provide strong evidence for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), which is a significant theoretical contribution beyond just the technical approach.
2. SynTime demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets datasets while achieving comparable results on WikiWars. The high recall rates (exceeding 92% on relaxed match across all datasets) are particularly noteworthy and align with the authors' observation that most time expressions contain time tokens.
3. The simplicity and real-time performance of SynTime make it highly practical for applications. The method requires no training, runs quickly, and can be easily expanded by adding new time-related keywords, which is a significant advantage over more complex machine learning approaches.

Weaknesses:
1. The most serious concern is that the rule design was based on the same corpora used for evaluation. The authors analyze time expressions from TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets, then develop rules from these observations and evaluate on the same datasets. This creates a risk of overfitting, as the rules may be tailored specifically to these corpora rather than generalizing to new domains.
2. The paper lacks statistical analysis of overlap between time expressions across the different datasets. Without showing that the time expressions in these corpora are sufficiently diverse (rather than largely overlapping), it's difficult to assess whether the method truly generalizes or simply works well on these specific datasets.
3. The domain-specific nature of the training corpora (news articles, Wikipedia wars, and tweets) may limit the method's applicability to other domains. The authors acknowledge this in Section 4.3 when discussing expansion for different domains, but they don't provide evidence that the core rules would generalize across different text types without significant adaptation.
4. The method assumes perfect tokenization and POS tagging, which may not hold in real-world applications. As noted in Section 5.3, errors in tokenization and POS tagging (e.g., "sat" tagged as VBD instead of NNP) can significantly impact performance.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide statistical evidence of the diversity of time expressions across the four datasets? Specifically, what percentage of time expressions are unique to each dataset versus shared across multiple datasets?
2. How would you address the concern that the rules may be overfitted to the evaluation datasets? Would you recommend conducting experiments on a new, distinct dataset for evaluation?
3. Could you elaborate on the conditions under which the manual keyword expansion (Section 4.3) would be necessary for different domains, and how this would impact the method's generalizability?

The paper presents an interesting, practical approach to time expression recognition with strong empirical results. However, the lack of evidence for generalizability across datasets and domains is a significant limitation that needs to be addressed. If the authors can demonstrate that the rules generalize beyond the specific corpora used in their analysis, this would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.
