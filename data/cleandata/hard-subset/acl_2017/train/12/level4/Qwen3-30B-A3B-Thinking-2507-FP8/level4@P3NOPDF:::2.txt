

The paper presents a rule-based approach for time expression recognition, offering a simple and interpretable solution that serves as a valuable foundation for downstream applications. The method demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming both state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning-based systems on standard evaluation corpora. This performance gain, coupled with the method’s transparency, represents a notable contribution to the field.

However, the paper’s claims about the method’s generality are undermined by a critical methodological limitation. The rules were explicitly designed based on observations of the very corpora used for evaluation, creating a high risk of overfitting to these specific datasets. The authors do not provide evidence that the time expressions in these corpora are sufficiently diverse or representative of broader domains. Crucially, there is no analysis of overlap between the corpora used for rule development and evaluation—such as statistical measures of shared time expression patterns or domain coverage. If the corpora exhibited significant overlap in time expression types, this would likely explain the strong results but would also severely limit the method’s applicability to new domains or datasets. Without such evidence, the generality of the rules remains unverified.

The lack of a distinct development and evaluation split is the core issue. To establish that the method generalizes beyond the specific contexts of the evaluation corpora, the authors should have derived rules from a separate corpus (or a subset of the training data) and tested them on an entirely new dataset. This standard practice would have provided concrete evidence of the method’s robustness across diverse linguistic contexts. The current evaluation, while technically sound for the given corpora, fails to address whether the rules capture universal linguistic patterns or merely reflect idiosyncrasies of the evaluation sets.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing this gap. A revised version should include: (1) a clear separation between rule development and evaluation datasets; (2) statistical analysis of time expression diversity and overlap across corpora; and (3) experiments on a new, domain-diverse test set. Without these, the method’s practical utility remains confined to the specific contexts studied, limiting its broader impact.

Despite this concern, the approach itself is well-motivated and the results are compelling within the given framework. The simplicity of the rule-based design is a genuine strength, and the performance gains over existing methods are meaningful. With the proposed revisions to address generalizability, this work could make a significant contribution to time expression recognition research. As currently presented, however, the evaluation does not sufficiently support the method’s claimed robustness.
