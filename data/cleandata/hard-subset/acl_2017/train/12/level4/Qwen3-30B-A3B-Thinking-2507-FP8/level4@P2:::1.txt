

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper presents a syntactic type-based approach for time expression recognition called SynTime. The authors make four key empirical observations about time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets): (1) time expressions are typically short (80% contain ≤3 tokens); (2) a small vocabulary of time-related keywords is used; (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token; and (4) words in time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based tagger that defines three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic boundary expansion rules to recognize time expressions. The main contributions are: (1) a thorough empirical analysis of time expressions that provides evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage; (2) a novel syntactic type system for time expression recognition that differs from existing semantic-based approaches; and (3) a lightweight, high-performing rule-based tagger that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and tweets.

**Strengths**  
1. The empirical analysis is exceptionally strong and well-motivated. The observations about time expression length, vocabulary size, time token prevalence, and syntactic behavior are consistently supported across four diverse datasets, providing robust evidence for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949). This analysis is the paper's strongest contribution and directly motivates the proposed approach.  
2. SynTime's design is elegantly simple and well-justified. By defining types based on syntactic behavior (inspired by part-of-speech tagging) rather than semantic meaning, the authors create a more principled framework that enables flexible, lightweight rule design. The heuristic boundary expansion rules are intuitive and directly address the observed patterns in time expressions.  
3. The evaluation demonstrates clear, significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. SynTime achieves the highest F1 scores on TimeBank (92.09% strict match) and Tweets (91.74% strict match), with exceptionally high recall (95.65% relaxed match on TimeBank). The performance on Tweets is particularly impressive given the informal nature of the text, which aligns with the paper's key insights about minimal time expression length in casual communication.  
4. The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making the methodology and results accessible to readers. The comparison with existing approaches is thorough and highlights the novelty of the syntactic type system.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the system, particularly regarding the syntactic types. Section 4.1 does not clarify how the 5 "seasons" are defined or how to handle culturally specific time expressions like "Ramadan month" or "Holiday Season." This ambiguity hinders reproducibility and limits the method's applicability to diverse domains.  
2. The paper contains a factual error in Section 5.1, stating "two benchmark datasets" when three are used (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets). This misstatement undermines the paper's credibility and requires correction.  
3. The paper does not provide an example of a time expression without a time token (e.g., "last week" where "last" is a modifier but not a time token). Such an example would clarify the system's handling of edge cases and strengthen the presentation.  
4. The discussion of limitations fails to address how the system might improve beyond its current ceiling. Given that 93% of time expressions contain time tokens and SynTime achieves 92% recall, the paper should discuss strategies for addressing the remaining 1% of expressions that lack time tokens.  
5. The authors do not indicate whether they plan to release the full set of rules or software, which would significantly benefit the community and facilitate reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Please clarify how the "5 seasons" in Section 4.1 are defined, and provide examples of how culturally specific time expressions (e.g., "Ramadan month") would be handled.  
2. Please correct the reference to "two benchmark datasets" in Section 5.1 to "three datasets" and clarify which dataset(s) constitute the benchmark.  
3. Please provide an example of a time expression without a time token to illustrate how SynTime processes such cases.  
4. How do you plan to address the 1% of time expressions that lack time tokens (given that 93% contain time tokens and SynTime achieves 92% recall)?  
5. Will the authors release the full set of rules and software for SynTime?
