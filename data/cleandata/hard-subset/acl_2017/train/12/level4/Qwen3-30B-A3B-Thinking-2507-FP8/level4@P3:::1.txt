

Summary and Contributions:
This paper presents an analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and proposes a rule-based time expression recognition system called SynTime. The main contributions are: (1) a comprehensive analysis demonstrating that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, contain at least one time token in over 93% of cases, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior, providing empirical evidence for the principle of least effort in language; (2) a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) that enables a lightweight, rule-based approach to time expression recognition; and (3) a practical implementation that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets, particularly excelling on the Tweets dataset.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant empirical contribution by analyzing time expressions across multiple datasets and demonstrating consistent patterns that support the principle of least effort. The authors' observations about the brevity of time expressions (80% ≤3 tokens), small vocabulary (only 64-80 distinct time tokens across datasets), and high prevalence of time tokens (93-98%) provide compelling evidence for a fundamental linguistic principle. This analysis is thorough, well-justified, and forms the strong foundation for the proposed approach.

2. The proposed SynTime system is elegantly designed and effectively leverages the authors' empirical observations. By defining syntactic types rather than semantic types (as previous rule-based systems do), SynTime creates a more flexible framework that can be easily expanded. The heuristic boundary expansion rules are simple yet effective, allowing the system to handle various time expression patterns without complex training data requirements. This design choice directly addresses a key limitation of previous rule-based systems that relied on fixed-length patterns.

3. The evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly on the Tweets dataset where SynTime achieves 95.87% relaxed match F1 (6.33% improvement over the best baseline). The high recall rates (92-95.87% across all datasets) are especially impressive given that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, validating the authors' core insight. The system's lightweight nature and real-time performance are practical advantages for deployment in resource-constrained environments.

4. The paper provides a convincing explanation for SynTime's success, particularly on informal text like tweets. The authors demonstrate that even in informal environments, people tend to use formal time expressions (e.g., "today" appears 1.2M times vs. "2day" appearing only 34K times), which aligns with their core observations about the small vocabulary and minimal length of time expressions.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the system. The heuristic rules for time segment identification (Section 4.2.2) are described generally but without specific examples or implementation details. For instance, it's unclear how the system handles cases like "last week" (where "last" is a modifier but might not be captured by the described rules) or "from June to August" (where the LINKAGE type would apply). The authors should provide more concrete examples of the rule application.

2. The paper does not adequately address cases where time expressions lack time tokens (which the authors claim are rare but may exist). Section 4.2.1 mentions filtering ambiguous words using POS information, but it's unclear how the system handles time expressions that don't contain any time tokens (e.g., "the day before yesterday" where "day" might be a common noun rather than a time token). The authors should clarify this limitation and provide examples of such cases.

3. The expansion process for SynTime (Section 4.3) is underspecified. The paper states that the system can be expanded by adding keywords from training text, but it doesn't explain the criteria for determining which words to add or how the manual addition process works in practice. The claim that "only a few tokens are added" (Table 5) needs to be supported with more detailed examples and justification.

4. The evaluation methodology requires clarification. The paper mentions that "SUTime misses about 3% time expressions in tweets," but doesn't explain how this was calculated (e.g., was this based on manual annotation of the 500 sampled tweets? How were the 15 time expressions in the 500 tweets identified?). The authors should provide more detail about their evaluation protocol to ensure reproducibility.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide specific examples of time expressions that contain no time tokens (as claimed to be rare in the paper), and explain how SynTime would handle them?

2. The paper mentions that "the most often used words are the formal words" in Twitter, but the examples provided for "today" (1.2M vs. 34K) don't account for the total number of tweets analyzed. Could you clarify the scale of your analysis and provide the exact numbers used to support this claim?

3. The paper states that SynTime outperforms all baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, but achieves comparable results on WikiWars. Given that WikiWars contains many descriptive time expressions (e.g., "the time Arnold reached Quebec City"), how does SynTime handle such cases, and what specific improvements would be needed to handle them better?

4. The paper claims that SynTime can be easily expanded by adding keywords, but Table 5 shows that 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers were added for WikiWars. Could you provide a few examples of these added tokens and explain why they were necessary for WikiWars but not for the other datasets?
