

This paper presents a rule-based approach for time expression extraction, leveraging the insight that such expressions are typically short and contain at least one time token. The method first identifies time tokens via dictionary lookup, regular expressions, and POS tagging. It then expands segments bidirectionally from the token using heuristic rules until boundary conditions are met, before merging segments into cohesive expressions via additional rules. Evaluation across three datasets demonstrates significant improvements over both rule-based and machine learning baselines (Table 3).

**Strengths:**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured, with clear motivation for each rule derived from empirical data analysis. The evaluation design effectively compares against relevant baselines, and the results robustly support the claimed improvements. The authors’ focus on practical, interpretable rules is particularly valuable for real-world NLP applications.

**Weaknesses:**  
The implementation details lack sufficient specificity, hindering reproducibility. Key ambiguities include:  
- The exact criteria for "time tokens" (e.g., whether "next Monday" qualifies as a single token or two) and the scope of the dictionary.  
- The precise boundary conditions for segment expansion (e.g., how punctuation or conjunctions trigger stopping).  
- Ambiguities in the merging rules (e.g., handling overlapping segments like "July 4th and 5th").  
Without these details, replicating the system or diagnosing results is challenging.

**Specific Comments:**  
1. **Section 4.1 (Time Token Definition):** The paper lists "five seasons" (e.g., "spring," "summer") but does not clarify how culturally specific periods (e.g., "Ramadan month," "Holiday Season") are treated. Please specify whether these are handled as time tokens or require additional rules.  
2. **Section 5.1 (Datasets):** The text references "two benchmark datasets," but Table 1 lists three. Please correct this discrepancy.  
3. **Section 5.2 (Tokenless Expressions):** The method assumes all expressions contain at least one time token (noted in Section 5.2), but the paper lacks an example of a *non-token-containing* expression (e.g., "the week after Christmas") to illustrate how the system handles such cases. A concrete example would strengthen the discussion.  
4. **Section 5.2 (Performance Ceiling):** The authors note that 93% of expressions contain a time token and achieve 92% recall. Given this near-ceiling performance, how do they plan to address the remaining 8% of expressions (e.g., via additional rules or hybrid approaches)?  
5. **Reproducibility:** Will the authors release the full rule set, dictionary, and implementation code? Given the rule-based nature of the work, sharing these resources would significantly benefit the community.

**Overall Recommendation:**  
This is a strong contribution with clear empirical validation and practical methodology. The core approach is sound, and the results are compelling. However, the lack of implementation details in the current draft limits its reproducibility and adoption. Addressing the specific points above—particularly the missing examples, boundary conditions, and reproducibility concerns—would substantially strengthen the paper. With these revisions, the work would make an excellent contribution to the ACL community.
