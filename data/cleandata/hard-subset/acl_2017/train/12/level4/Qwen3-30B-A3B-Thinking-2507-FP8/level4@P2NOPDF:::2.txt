

The paper introduces a straightforward rule-based approach for time expression recognition, presenting a significant advantage as a fundamental preprocessing tool for applications requiring temporal analysis. Experimental results demonstrate that the method achieves superior performance compared to both state-of-the-art rule-based systems and machine learning-based approaches. This represents a notable contribution to the field.

However, a critical limitation concerns the method’s generality. The rules are derived from the same corpora used for evaluation, creating a risk of overfitting to these specific datasets. Consequently, the observed performance may not generalize to new domains or texts beyond the evaluation corpora. The authors do not address whether the time expressions in these corpora exhibit significant overlap or redundancy. If such overlap were substantial, it would weaken the argument for the rules’ robustness, as the method might simply exploit patterns unique to the evaluation data rather than capturing universal temporal expression patterns. The absence of statistical analysis or discussion regarding corpus overlap further obscures the validity of the generality claim.

To substantiate the method’s broad applicability, the experiments should have employed a distinct corpus for evaluation—one not involved in rule design or training. This would rigorously test whether the rules generalize across diverse domains and text types, rather than merely fitting the evaluation benchmarks. Without this separation, the reported performance gains remain ambiguous regarding real-world utility. While the rule-based design is inherently interpretable and efficient, its practical value hinges on demonstrated cross-corpus robustness.

The authors are encouraged to address this limitation in revision by conducting experiments on a new, independent dataset. This would strengthen the paper’s core claim and provide clearer evidence of the method’s effectiveness beyond the specific evaluation contexts. Additionally, including a brief analysis of time expression diversity (e.g., lexical or structural variation) across corpora would further support the generalizability argument. For instance, metrics like Jaccard similarity or shared expression frequencies between training and test sets could quantify overlap and inform the validity of the evaluation framework.

In summary, the method is well-motivated and empirically competitive, but the evaluation strategy undermines claims of generality. Addressing the overfitting risk through independent testing would significantly elevate the paper’s contribution. The work has potential, but its impact is currently constrained by the lack of validation on unseen data. A revised version with this critical experiment would be a strong candidate for publication.
