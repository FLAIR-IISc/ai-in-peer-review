

Summary: The paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: time expressions are short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, at least 93% contain time tokens, and words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a type-based approach that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A thorough analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets that provides empirical evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage.
2. A novel type-based approach (SynTime) that uses syntactic types rather than semantic types for rule-based time expression recognition, making the system more flexible and easily expandable.
3. Empirical validation demonstrating that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and Twitter data, with particularly strong recall results.

Strengths:
1. The paper's systematic analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets is comprehensive and provides strong empirical support for the principle of least effort in language. The four observations are well-supported by data and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
2. SynTime represents a significant conceptual shift from traditional rule-based approaches by defining types based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning. This approach leads to a more flexible and extensible system that can be easily expanded for different domains by adding keywords.
3. The experimental results are compelling, with SynTime achieving the highest F1 scores on TimeBank and Tweets datasets and exceptionally high recall rates (above 92% on relaxed match across all datasets). The performance on Twitter data is particularly noteworthy given the informal language used in social media.
4. The paper clearly demonstrates SynTime's practical advantages, including its lightweight nature, real-time operation, and ease of expansion, making it suitable for real-world applications.

Weaknesses:
1. The most significant concern is that the rules for SynTime were developed using the same corpora used for evaluation, creating a risk of overfitting. The paper does not provide evidence that the time expressions in these datasets have low overlap, which is necessary to support the claim of generalizability. A fairer evaluation would require using a distinct corpus for rule design and another for evaluation.
2. The paper does not adequately address how SynTime handles time expressions containing prepositions or descriptive elements (e.g., "The time Arnold reached Quebec City"), which appear in the WikiWars dataset. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore it thoroughly or propose solutions.
3. While the paper claims that "not many words are used to express time information," this claim lacks sufficient empirical support across diverse domains. The expansion process requires manual keyword addition, which may not scale well to domains with highly diverse time expressions.
4. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of computational efficiency between SynTime and baselines, despite claiming SynTime "runs in real time." A quantitative analysis of processing speed would strengthen this claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide evidence of the overlap between time expressions in the four analyzed datasets? This would help assess whether the rules are genuinely generalizable or overfit to specific corpora.
2. How would SynTime handle time expressions that include prepositions or descriptive phrases, such as "the time Arnold reached Quebec City"? What modifications would be needed to address this limitation?
3. Could you provide a quantitative comparison of processing speed between SynTime and the baselines to substantiate the claim that SynTime runs in real time?
4. Given the manual keyword addition required for expansion, how would you address the scalability of SynTime to domains with highly diverse time expression patterns?
