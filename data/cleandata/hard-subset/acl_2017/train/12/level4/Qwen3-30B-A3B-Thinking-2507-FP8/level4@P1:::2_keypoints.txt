1. Proposal: SynTime is a lightweight, rule-based method for time expression recognition using syntactic types and simple heuristics.  
2. Observation 1: Time expressions are very short—about 80% contain three tokens or fewer.  
3. Observation 2: Time expressions use a small, recurring vocabulary of time-related keywords.  
4. Observation 3: Over 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token.  
5. Observation 4: Words within time expressions show similar syntactic behaviors, motivating three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals).  
6. Contribution: Provides an analysis supporting the principle of least effort in time expressions.  
7. Contribution: Presents a no-training, fast rule-based system that outperforms SOTA on some datasets.  
8. Contribution: Offers a simple, expandable framework for time expression recognition.  
9. Strength: The analysis reveals fundamental linguistic patterns and supports theoretical claims (Zipf/least effort).  
10. Strength: SynTime shows strong empirical performance (high recall, beats SOTA on TimeBank and Tweets, comparable on WikiWars).  
11. Strength: Method is simple, real-time, requires no training, and is easily extensible via keyword addition.  
12. Weakness: Rule design was derived from the same corpora used for evaluation, raising overfitting concerns.  
13. Weakness: No statistical analysis of overlap/diversity of time expressions across datasets to assess generalizability.  
14. Weakness: Corpora are domain-specific (news, Wikipedia, tweets), so applicability to other domains is uncertain.  
15. Weakness: Method assumes perfect tokenization and POS tagging; errors in preprocessing can degrade performance.  
16. Question: What percentage of time expressions are unique to each dataset versus shared across datasets?  
17. Question: How will you address potential overfitting of rules—can you evaluate on a distinct, unseen dataset?  
18. Question: Under what conditions is manual keyword expansion necessary for new domains, and how would it affect generalizability?  
19. Overall concern: The method is promising and practical, but lacks demonstrated generalization beyond the analyzed corpora.
