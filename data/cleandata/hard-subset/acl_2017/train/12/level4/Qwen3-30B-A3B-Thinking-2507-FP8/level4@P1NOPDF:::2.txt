

The paper proposes a simple rule-based method for time expression recognition, a straightforward approach that serves as a strong foundation for applications requiring this basic processing step. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms both state-of-the-art rule-based approaches and machine learning-based methods for time expression recognition. The approach is well-motivated and the technical execution appears sound.

However, a key concern regarding the method's generality requires clarification. The rules were developed using the same corpora evaluated in the experiments, raising concerns about overfitting to these specific datasets. The specific domains represented in these corpora may have also unduly influenced rule design. Crucially, the paper provides no statistical analysis or discussion addressing potential overlap in time expressions across these corpora. If significant overlap existed—meaning the corpora shared many time expressions—the current evaluation would not sufficiently demonstrate the rules' ability to generalize beyond the training data. Such overlap would have weakened the argument for rule generality, whereas demonstrating minimal overlap would have strengthened it. The absence of this analysis leaves uncertainty about whether the rules are truly robust or merely tailored to the evaluation sets.

To convincingly demonstrate the method's broader applicability, the experiments should have been conducted on a corpus distinct from the one used for rule development. This would have provided a more rigorous test of the rules' generalizability across unseen data and domains. Without this, the reported performance gains may reflect adaptation to the evaluation corpora rather than genuine methodological superiority. The current evaluation framework does not fully address whether the rules would perform well on new, domain-diverse text. While the rule-based design offers transparency and simplicity as advantages, the generality claim hinges on evidence that the rules are not implicitly constrained by the specific corpora used for both development and testing. The authors should explicitly address this limitation and consider re-evaluating on an independent test set in future work to validate the method's wider applicability.
