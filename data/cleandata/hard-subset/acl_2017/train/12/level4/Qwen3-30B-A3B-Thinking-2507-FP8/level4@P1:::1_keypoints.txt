- Introduces SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer leveraging syntactic types and simple heuristics.  
- Analyzes four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and finds time expressions are usually very short (80% ≤3 tokens).  
- Finds a small, recurring vocabulary of time-related keywords across datasets.  
- Observes over 93% of time expressions contain at least one explicit time token.  
- Notes words within time expressions show similar syntactic behavior, motivating a type-based approach.  
- Proposes a syntactic type system with three main types (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple boundary-expansion rules.  
- Processing pipeline: identify time tokens → expand to modifiers/numerals → merge segments into complete expressions.  
- Claims the method is lightweight, real-time, and easily extended with domain-specific keywords.  
- Contribution: data-driven evidence supporting the principle of least effort for time expressions.  
- Contribution: novel syntactic-type-based recognition approach distinct from semantic labeling.  
- Contribution: comprehensive evaluation showing state-of-the-art performance, notably on Twitter data.  
- Strength: convincing data analysis that yields a simple, effective model grounded in linguistic patterns.  
- Strength: high recall and precision with real-time performance, outperforming complex alternatives.  
- Strength: theoretical framing via the principle of least effort and POS-like grouping.  
- Weakness: insufficient reproducibility detail, especially exact syntactic type definitions (e.g., "5 seasons").  
- Weakness: inconsistencies in the experimental description (claims of "two" benchmarks vs. three used).  
- Weakness: inadequate discussion of handling expressions without time tokens (the remaining ~7%).
