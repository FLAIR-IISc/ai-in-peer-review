

Summary: The paper presents a thorough empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets, revealing key properties: time expressions are typically short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, contain at least one time token in over 93% of cases, and demonstrate similar syntactic behavior within expressions. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition approach that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types, and designs simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion. The primary contribution is the empirical evidence supporting the principle of least effort in language usage through time expression analysis. The second contribution is the syntactic type system that provides a novel perspective on rule-based time expression recognition. The third contribution is the experimental validation demonstrating SynTime's superior performance on benchmark datasets and tweets compared to state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths:
1. The empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets is exceptionally thorough and provides compelling evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage. The detailed examination of expression length, vocabulary size, and syntactic behavior across different text types (news, Wikipedia, tweets) is well-executed and forms a strong foundation for the proposed method.

2. The SynTime approach is well-motivated by the empirical findings and represents a thoughtful simplification of rule-based time expression recognition. The syntactic type system clearly distinguishes from existing semantic-based approaches, and the heuristic rules are elegantly designed to be lightweight, flexible, and expandable without requiring retraining.

3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating significant improvements over three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The particularly strong recall results (92-95%) across all datasets align perfectly with the authors' empirical observation that over 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing strong validation for the approach.

4. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured, making the methodology and contributions accessible. The explanation of the time token identification, segment identification, and expression extraction processes is particularly clear and well-illustrated with examples.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient implementation details for full reproducibility, particularly regarding the specific regular expressions used for time token recognition and the exact heuristic rules for segment expansion. This makes it challenging to independently verify the results or understand the precise implementation details.

2. Section 4.1's description of time token types is insufficiently detailed, particularly regarding the "5 seasons" mentioned and how specific time expressions like "Ramadan month" or "Holiday Season" are handled within the proposed type system. This creates ambiguity about the approach's coverage of domain-specific time expressions.

3. Section 5.1 incorrectly refers to "two benchmark datasets" when three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets) are actually used in the evaluation, creating confusion about the experimental setup.

4. The paper does not provide a clear example of a time expression that does not contain a time token, which would help readers understand the rationale behind the authors' observation that "at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens" and the potential limitations of the approach.

Questions to Authors:
1. Section 4.1 states "5 seasons" as a time token type. Could you clarify what these five seasons are, and how time expressions like "Ramadan month" or "Holiday Season" would be handled within your type system?

2. In Section 5.1, the paper refers to "two benchmark datasets" but evaluates on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets). Could you clarify this discrepancy in the dataset description?

3. Could you provide a concrete example of a time expression that does not contain a time token, as mentioned in Section 3.2 (Observation 3)? This would help clarify the boundary conditions of your approach.

4. Given that your approach achieves 92-95% recall (aligning with the 93% observation that time expressions contain time tokens), what specific strategies do you propose to improve performance beyond this ceiling?

5. Will the full set of rules and implementation code for SynTime be made publicly available?
