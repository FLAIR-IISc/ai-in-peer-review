

Summary: The paper proposes SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic types rather than semantic types. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four key observations: time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, contain at least one time token in over 93% of cases, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior within time expressions. Based on these observations, they define three syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) to group time-related regular expressions and design simple heuristic rules for recognition. The main contributions of this work are: (1) the comprehensive analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets that supports the principle of least effort; (2) the novel type-based approach that defines syntactic types for tokens based on their syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning; (3) the empirical demonstration of SynTime's superior performance over state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and tweets.

Strengths: The analysis of time expressions across diverse datasets provides compelling evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage, with clear data showing short expressions, small vocabulary, high time token occurrence, and similar syntactic behavior. SynTime's lightweight design and real-time performance are significant practical advantages, particularly for applications requiring immediate processing. The method's exceptional recall rates (over 95% on relaxed match for Tweets) and strong performance across datasets demonstrate its effectiveness, especially on the Tweets dataset where it achieves 95.87% F1.

Weaknesses: The rules for SynTime were developed based on the same corpora used for evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets), which risks overfitting to these specific datasets and limits the assessment of generalizability. The paper does not provide any analysis of overlap between time expressions in the analysis and evaluation datasets, making it unclear whether the rules are capturing domain-specific patterns rather than general linguistic principles. The absence of experiments on a new corpus distinct from the analysis phase prevents a proper assessment of the method's broader applicability beyond the studied domains.

Questions to Authors: Could you provide details on the overlap between time expressions in the datasets used for analysis versus evaluation to assess potential overfitting? How would SynTime perform on time expressions from domains not covered in your analysis (e.g., medical or legal texts) where time expressions might follow different patterns? Have you considered testing SynTime on a new corpus that was not used during the analysis or rule design phase to demonstrate wider applicability?
