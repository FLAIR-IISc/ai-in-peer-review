The paper proposes a type-based approach, named SynTime, to recognize time expressions from text. This approach is a significant improvement over existing methods as it is based on syntactic types and simple heuristic rules, making it a lightweight and flexible tool for time expression recognition.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short, (2) only a small group of time-related keywords are used to express time information, (3) more than 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, which defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of time expressions from four datasets, which sheds light on the characteristics of time expressions and provides insights for designing effective time expression recognition methods.
2. SynTime is a novel approach that leverages syntactic types and simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions, making it a lightweight and flexible tool for time expression recognition.
3. The authors conduct experiments on three datasets and demonstrate that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, including rule-based time taggers and machine learning-based time taggers.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of time expressions, which is essential for designing effective time expression recognition methods.
2. SynTime is a simple and lightweight approach that can be easily expanded by adding time-related token regular expressions from training text.
3. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime in recognizing time expressions from various datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The rules in SynTime were designed based on observations of corpora that are used for evaluation, which raises concerns about the generality of the method.
2. There is no discussion on the overlap of time expressions in the observed corpora, which could have provided insights into the generality of the rules.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by SynTime, which could have helped to identify areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on the overlap of time expressions in the observed corpora and how it affects the generality of the rules in SynTime?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting to specific corpora and domains?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by SynTime and how you plan to improve its performance?
