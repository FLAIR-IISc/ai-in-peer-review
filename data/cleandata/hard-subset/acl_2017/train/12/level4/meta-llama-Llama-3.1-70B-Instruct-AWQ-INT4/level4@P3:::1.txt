**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a type-based approach, named SynTime, for recognizing time expressions in free text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and observe that they are typically short, contain a small group of time-related keywords, and demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions. The approach is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, showing significant improvements in performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in time expression usage.
2. They propose a type-based time expression tagger, SynTime, which defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules based on the idea of boundary expansion.
3. The evaluation of SynTime on three datasets shows that it outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, including rule-based time taggers and machine learning-based time taggers.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
2. The rules are motivated by empirical observations of the data and seem to be well-justified as evidenced by the evaluation.
3. The approach is simple and efficient, running in real-time.
4. The evaluation shows significant improvements in performance over state-of-the-art baselines.

**Weaknesses**

1. There are some underspecifications in the paper that make it difficult to reproduce the results, such as the exact definition of the syntactic types and the heuristic rules.
2. The paper could benefit from more examples and illustrations to help understand the approach and its application.
3. The authors do not discuss the limitations of their approach and potential avenues for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the definition of the syntactic types and the heuristic rules used in SynTime?
2. How do you plan to improve the performance of SynTime further, given that it has already achieved high recall rates?
3. Are there any plans to release the full set of rules and software used in SynTime?
4. Can you provide more examples of time expressions that do not contain time tokens and how SynTime handles such cases?
5. How does SynTime perform on other datasets and domains, and are there any plans to evaluate its performance on more diverse datasets?
