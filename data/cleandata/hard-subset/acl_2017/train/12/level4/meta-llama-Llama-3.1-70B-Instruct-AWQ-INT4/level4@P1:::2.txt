The paper proposes a method to recognize time expressions from text, leveraging a simple rule-based approach. This simplicity is a significant advantage, as time expression recognition should be a fundamental process in various applications. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based methods and a machine learning-based method for time expression recognition.

However, my primary concern is the generality of the method. The rules in the method were designed based on observations from the corpora used for evaluation. Consequently, I am concerned that the rules may overfit to these specific corpora. Similarly, the domains of these corpora may have influenced the rule design, potentially limiting the method's applicability to other domains.

There is no statistical analysis or discussion to show the overlap in time expressions across the observed corpora. If it were demonstrated that time expressions in these corpora exhibit significant overlap, this would have supported the generality of the rules. Nevertheless, conducting experiments on a new corpus distinct from the rule design process is a positive step, as it helps to demonstrate the proposed method's broader effectiveness.

**Summary of the paper:**
The paper presents a type-based approach, named SynTime, to recognize time expressions. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically short, (2) a small group of keywords are used to express time information, (3) most time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and uses simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.

**Main contributions:**

1. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four key observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in time expression usage.
2. They propose a type-based time expression tagger, SynTime, which defines syntactic types and designs simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.
3. The authors conduct experiments on three datasets and demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime against state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to time expression recognition, leveraging syntactic types and simple heuristic rules.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime, outperforming state-of-the-art rule-based methods and a machine learning-based method.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of time expressions from four datasets, offering insights into the characteristics of time expressions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The generality of the method is a concern, as the rules were designed based on observations from the corpora used for evaluation.
2. There is limited discussion on the potential limitations of the method, such as its applicability to other domains or languages.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the overlap in time expressions across the observed corpora, to support the generality of the rules.

**Questions to authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the concern of overfitting to the specific corpora used for evaluation?
2. Can you provide more information on the potential limitations of the method, such as its applicability to other domains or languages?
3. How do you envision the proposed method being integrated into larger applications, such as information retrieval systems or natural language processing pipelines?
