This paper presents a novel rule-based approach to time expression extraction, leveraging key insights that time expressions are typically short and often contain at least one time token. The proposed method, SynTime, employs a combination of dictionary lookup, regular expression matching, and POS tagging information to recognize time tokens. Subsequently, it expands time segments from the time token in both directions, utilizing a set of heuristic rules, and merges the time segments into a single time expression based on another set of rules.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper's primary contributions include: (1) an in-depth analysis of time expressions from four datasets, revealing that time expressions are generally short and expressed using a small vocabulary, with words in time expressions demonstrating similar syntactic behavior; (2) the proposal of a type-based time expression tagger, SynTime, which defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules based on the idea of boundary expansion; and (3) experimental results demonstrating that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, including rule-based time taggers and machine learning-based time taggers.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with rules motivated by empirical observations of the data. The evaluation results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Additionally, the paper provides a thorough analysis of the time expressions in the datasets, which is valuable for understanding the characteristics of time expressions.

**Weaknesses**

There are some underspecifications in the paper that make it difficult to reproduce the results. For instance, the paper does not provide a comprehensive list of the 15 types of time tokens, 5 types of modifiers, and 1 type of numeral used in SynTime. Furthermore, the heuristic rules for expanding time segments and merging time expressions are not fully specified, which may lead to difficulties in replicating the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the 15 types of time tokens, 5 types of modifiers, and 1 type of numeral used in SynTime?
2. How do you plan to improve the performance of SynTime, given that it has already achieved a high recall of 92% on the datasets?
3. Are there any plans to release the full set of rules and software used in SynTime to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
4. Can you provide an example of a time expression without a time token, as mentioned in Section 5.2, to illustrate the handling of such cases in SynTime?
5. How do you address the issue of underspecification in the paper, particularly with regards to the heuristic rules used in SynTime?
