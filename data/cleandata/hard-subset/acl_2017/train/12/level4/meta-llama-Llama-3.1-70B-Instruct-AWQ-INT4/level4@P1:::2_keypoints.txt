- Proposes SynTime, a simple type-based, rule-driven method that defines three syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) to recognize time expressions.
- Leverages corpus analysis (four datasets) and four observations: time expressions are short, use a small keyword set, usually contain a time token, and show similar syntactic behavior.
- Emphasizes simplicity as an advantage, arguing time expression recognition should be a fundamental, lightweight process.
- Experimental results on three datasets show SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based methods and a machine-learning baseline.
- Main concern: rules were designed from the evaluation corpora and may overfit to those specific datasets.
- Domain bias: corpora domains may have influenced rule design, potentially limiting applicability to other domains.
- No statistical analysis provided to show overlap of time expressions across corpora to support rule generality.
- Positive note: testing on a new corpus separate from rule design is a useful step toward demonstrating broader effectiveness.
- Contribution: corpus-driven observations support a “least effort” principle for time expression usage.
- Contribution: introduces a syntactic-type tagging approach with simple heuristic rules (SynTime).
- Strength: detailed analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets provides useful insights.
- Weakness: limited discussion of method limitations, especially cross-domain and cross-language applicability.
- Weakness: lack of detailed overlap analysis across the observed corpora.
- Questions for authors: how will overfitting to specific corpora be addressed?
- Questions for authors: what are the method’s limitations for other domains or languages?
- Questions for authors: how can SynTime be integrated into larger applications like IR or NLP pipelines?
