### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in free text. The method is based on the key empirical observation that most time expressions are short and contain at least one core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday', '2017'). The recognition process is a simple and effective three-step pipeline: 1) Time tokens are identified using a combination of dictionary lookups, regular expressions, and POS tag filtering. 2) The boundaries of the time token are expanded left and right using heuristic rules to include adjacent modifiers and numerals, forming "time segments". 3) Adjacent or overlapping time segments are merged into complete time expressions based on another set of rules. The system is evaluated on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets corpus) and demonstrates significant performance gains over strong rule-based and machine-learning baselines, achieving particularly high recall.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A simple, effective, and data-driven heuristic method for time expression recognition.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself, which simplifies the rule-based paradigm. Instead of relying on a large set of complex, fixed patterns, it uses a flexible "identify-expand-merge" strategy anchored on a small set of syntactic types. The strong empirical results across diverse datasets validate that this simple, intuitive approach is highly effective and outperforms more complex systems.

2.  **A concise analysis of the statistical properties of time expressions.** The paper's methodology is directly motivated by a clear analysis of four datasets, which confirms that time expressions are typically short (1-3 tokens), use a small, recurring vocabulary, and are almost always anchored by a time token. This provides a strong justification for the system's design and offers valuable insights for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance.** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming three strong baselines on two of the three datasets and remaining competitive on the third. The system's recall is particularly impressive, exceeding 92% in relaxed matches across all datasets. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the core assumption—that identifying a "time token" is sufficient to find the vast majority of time expressions.

2.  **Clarity and Simplicity of the Method.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The SynTime method is conceptually elegant and intuitive. The three-stage process is clearly explained and visualized, making the system's logic transparent. This simplicity is a notable strength compared to opaque machine learning models or brittle, hand-crafted grammar-based systems.

3.  **Well-Motivated Approach.** The design of SynTime is not arbitrary; it is directly grounded in the empirical observations presented in Section 3. The authors clearly show *why* their method should work before they show that it *does* work. This connection between data analysis and system design is a significant strength and makes the paper's narrative compelling.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underspecified Details Hinder Reproducibility.** While the high-level approach is clear, the paper lacks sufficient detail about the implementation of the heuristic rules. The precise rules for boundary expansion (e.g., all stopping conditions) and merging (e.g., how all `LINKAGE` types are handled) are not fully specified. Without access to the source code or a detailed appendix with the complete rule set and type dictionaries, it would be very difficult for other researchers to reproduce this work and build upon it.

2.  **Ambiguity in Type Definitions and Scope.** The definitions of the syntactic types are not fully transparent. For instance, the `SEASON` type is said to contain "5" distinct tokens (Section 4.1), but these are not listed. This raises questions about what they are and, more broadly, how the system handles culturally specific temporal concepts like "Ramadan", "Lent", or other festival seasons that are not part of a standard four-season model. The system's robustness on a wider range of global texts is therefore unclear.

3.  **Minor Inconsistencies and Missing Analysis.** The paper contains minor textual issues, such as referring to "two benchmark datasets" in Section 5.1 when three are used throughout the experiments. More substantively, the analysis could be strengthened by discussing the ~7% of time expressions that *do not* contain a time token. Providing an example of such an expression would help readers understand the inherent limitations of the proposed approach and the nature of the remaining errors.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the reproducibility concerns mentioned in the weaknesses, do you plan to release the source code for SynTime, along with the full set of token lists and heuristic rules?

2.  In Section 4.1, you state that the `SEASON` type contains 5 tokens. Could you please clarify what these five seasons are? How does your framework handle culturally or religiously significant time periods that function like seasons (e.g., "Ramadan", "the holiday season")? Are these handled by the `HOLIDAY` type, and if so, what are the rules to distinguish them from single-day holidays?

3.  Your analysis shows that over 93% of time expressions contain a time token. Could you please provide an example from your datasets of a valid time expression that lacks a time token? This would be very helpful for understanding the remaining challenges for your system.

4.  SynTime's recall is already very high (e.g., 92.75% strict recall on TimeBank), approaching the ceiling imposed by your finding that 95.15% of expressions in that dataset have a time token. What do you believe are the most promising avenues for further improving performance? Is the remaining error primarily due to failures in the upstream POS tagger, incorrect boundary expansion rules, or the small percentage of expressions that lack a time token altogether?
