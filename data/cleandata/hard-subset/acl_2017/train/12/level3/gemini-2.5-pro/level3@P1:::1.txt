### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in free text. The method is motivated by an empirical analysis of four datasets, which reveals that most time expressions are short, are constructed from a small vocabulary, and critically, that over 93% contain at least one core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Tuesday').

Based on these observations, SynTime employs a simple three-step process:
1.  **Time Token Identification:** It first identifies potential time tokens in a sentence using predefined lists of keywords and regular expressions, using POS tags to disambiguate.
2.  **Time Segment Identification:** Starting from each identified time token, it applies simple heuristic rules to bidirectionally expand the boundaries to include adjacent modifiers and numerals, forming a "time segment."
3.  **Time Expression Extraction:** Finally, it scans the identified segments and applies another set of rules to merge adjacent or overlapping segments into complete time expressions.

The authors evaluate SynTime against several state-of-the-art systems on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets corpus), demonstrating significantly improved performance, especially in terms of recall.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  The proposal of a simple, effective, and efficient rule-based method (SynTime) that achieves state-of-the-art results. The novelty lies in its simplicity, using syntactic types and heuristic expansion rules rather than more complex, cascaded finite-state grammars.
2.  A compelling empirical analysis of time expressions across different text genres. This analysis provides a strong, data-driven justification for the system's architectural choices and offers insights into the linguistic properties of time expressions.

### Strengths

1.  **Effectiveness through Simplicity:** The paper's primary strength is its demonstration that a carefully designed but simple and lightweight system can outperform more complex rule-based and machine learning approaches. The "identify-then-expand" strategy, grounded in the observation that most time expressions have an explicit time token at their core, is intuitive and powerful. The excellent results, particularly the consistently high recall across all datasets and the state-of-the-art performance on the noisy Tweets dataset, provide strong evidence for the validity of this simplified approach.

2.  **Strong Empirical Grounding:** The design choices for SynTime are not arbitrary but are directly motivated by a clear and insightful analysis of four diverse corpora. The observations that time expressions are short and rely on a small, identifiable set of core tokens provide a very strong justification for the proposed architecture. This data-driven approach makes the paper's claims transparent and well-reasoned.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate their motivation, describe their methodology in a logical progression, and present their results effectively. The overview figure (Figure 2) and the examples of segment merging (Figure 3) are particularly helpful for understanding the system's mechanics, making the paper's contributions highly accessible.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Detail Hinders Reproducibility:** The most significant weakness is the lack of specific details about the rules and resources used, which makes the work difficult to reproduce. The paper describes the *types* of rules and tokens (e.g., 15 time token types, 5 modifier types, rules for expansion and merging) but does not provide the actual lists, regular expressions, or the precise logic of the heuristic rules. While the authors state that the initial token lists are derived from SUTime, the specific subset used and any modifications are not detailed. Without access to the complete rule set, the research community cannot verify the results, build upon the work, or accurately compare it against future systems.

2.  **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper presents strong aggregate performance metrics but provides very little analysis of the system's failure modes. The only limitation mentioned is the system's dependence on correct upstream tokenization and POS tagging. A more in-depth error analysis would be highly beneficial. For instance, what types of time expressions does the system fail to recognize (false negatives)? What kinds of non-temporal phrases does it incorrectly tag (false positives)? A discussion of these cases would provide a clearer understanding of the method's boundaries and limitations.

3.  **Novelty of "Syntactic Types" is Overstated:** The paper emphasizes its use of "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) as a key distinction from other systems that use "semantic types." However, this distinction feels somewhat superficial. Positional information is fundamental to virtually all rule-based systems, including SUTime's cascaded grammars. The types defined here seem to be a straightforward encoding of a token's position relative to a time token, which is a blend of syntactic position and semantic role. A more detailed comparison would be needed to substantiate the claim that this typing system is fundamentally different and superior to prior approaches, beyond just being implemented with simpler heuristics.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The lack of detail in the paper makes reproducibility a major concern. Do you plan to release the full set of rules, the lists of tokens for each syntactic type, and the source code for SynTime?

2.  In Section 4.1, you mention "SEASON (5)" as a time token type. Could you clarify what these five tokens are? More generally, how does your system handle culturally specific or non-standard seasonal expressions (e.g., "Ramadan", "Lent", "the holiday season") that may not be present in the initial lists derived from SUTime?

3.  Your analysis shows that >93% of time expressions contain a time token. Could you provide an example from your datasets of a valid time expression that *does not* contain any of the defined time tokens? How does your system currently handle such cases?

4.  Your system achieves very high recall (e.g., 93.5% strict recall on TimeBank for SynTime-E), which is approaching the theoretical ceiling suggested by your analysis (95.2% of expressions in TimeBank have a time token). Given this, where do you see the most promising avenues for further improving performance? Is the primary challenge now increasing precision, or are there still important classes of time expressions that are being missed?
