### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes SynTime, a simple and lightweight rule-based method for recognizing time expressions from text. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora, which suggests that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain a core "time token". Based on these observations, the method defines three main syntactic types—time token, modifier, and numeral—and uses a set of simple heuristic rules to first identify core time tokens and then expand their boundaries to capture the full expression. The authors evaluate their method on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets) and show that it outperforms several state-of-the-art rule-based and machine-learning systems.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and simple heuristic-based method for time expression recognition.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself, which demonstrates that a straightforward, transparent, and computationally inexpensive approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance. The method's simplicity is a significant advantage, making it easy to analyze, implement, and adapt for practical applications.
2.  **An insightful analysis of the structural properties of time expressions across different domains.** The paper's initial analysis (Section 3) provides strong empirical motivation for the system's design. The observations that >93% of time expressions contain a core "time token" and that most expressions are very short directly justify the "identify-then-expand" strategy, grounding the engineering choices in data.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive performance with a simple, transparent model.** The most compelling argument for this paper is that a simple, heuristic-based method significantly outperforms more complex rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a machine-learning-based system (UWTime) on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The high F1 scores (e.g., >91% strict F1 on TimeBank and Tweets) are remarkable for a method based on a handful of types and boundary expansion rules. This result challenges the trend towards increasingly complex models and highlights the power of well-motivated, data-driven heuristics.
2.  **Consistently high recall.** The method achieves the best recall on all three datasets for both strict and relaxed matching. This directly validates the core hypothesis of the paper: by focusing on identifying the core "time tokens" that are present in over 93% of time expressions, the system can ensure broad coverage. For many downstream applications, high recall in identifying potential time expressions is more critical than precision, making this a valuable practical property.
3.  **Excellent extensibility and efficiency.** The rule-based nature of SynTime makes it transparent and easy to debug. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate its extensibility by creating an expanded version (SynTime-E), which improves performance on the domain-specific WikiWars corpus by adding just a few dozen keywords. This shows that the system can be easily adapted to new domains with minimal manual effort. As a lightweight tagger without a training phase, it is also highly efficient ("runs in real time"), making it suitable for real-world pipelines.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Major concern regarding the evaluation methodology and potential overfitting.** The paper's primary weakness lies in its evaluation protocol. The rules and syntactic types were developed based on an analysis of the same corpora used for the final evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). Section 3 explicitly states these datasets were used to derive the core observations that motivated the system's design. Furthermore, the SynTime-E version is explicitly expanded using keywords from the training portions of these datasets. This methodology creates a significant risk that the system is overfitted to the specific characteristics and vocabularies of the test sets. The reported state-of-the-art results may not reflect the method's general effectiveness but rather its specific tuning to the evaluation data.
2.  **Claims of generality are not sufficiently supported.** The authors propose a "syntactic type system" and suggest the approach is broadly applicable. However, the rule development and evaluation are confined to three specific domains: news (TimeBank), historical articles about war (WikiWars), and social media (Tweets). The types of temporal expressions in these domains may not be representative of other domains like legal texts, scientific papers, or clinical notes. Without demonstrating effectiveness on a corpus from a truly distinct domain that was held out from the initial analysis, the claims of generality remain unsubstantiated.
3.  **Lack of cross-corpus analysis to validate the type system.** The paper does not provide any statistics or discussion on the overlap of time-related keywords (e.g., time tokens, modifiers) across the different corpora. Such an analysis would be crucial for justifying that the proposed types are indeed "syntactic" and general, rather than just collections of domain-specific indicators. For example, knowing what proportion of keywords added for the WikiWars corpus were unique to that domain would help assess the cross-domain robustness of the initial keyword set and the overall approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The most critical issue is the potential for overfitting due to the evaluation methodology. Could you test the frozen SynTime-I system on a completely new dataset (e.g., a different TempEval dataset or a corpus from another domain) that was not used in any part of your initial analysis or rule development? This would provide much stronger evidence for the method's claimed generality.
2.  Could you please clarify the development process for the initial SynTime-I system? The paper states its token lists were collected from SUTime. Was there any manual filtering, addition, or re-categorization of these tokens based on your own analysis of the TimeBank, WikiWars, or Tweets corpora *before* creating SynTime-I?
3.  To support your claim of a general "syntactic type system," could you provide an analysis of the lexical overlap for your defined types across the different datasets? Specifically, how many of the keywords for "time tokens" and "modifiers" are shared between pairs of corpora (e.g., TimeBank-WikiWars, TimeBank-Tweets), and how many are unique to each? This would help clarify whether your system captures general patterns or domain-specific ones.
