- SynTime is a lightweight, rule-based method for recognizing time expressions in text, motivated by dataset analysis.  
- Empirical analysis finds time expressions are short, use a small keyword vocabulary, and usually contain at least one explicit "time token."  
- The method has three stages: identify time-related word types, expand from core time tokens to form segments, and merge adjacent/overlapping segments.  
- Time token identification relies on dictionaries, regular expressions, and POS-based disambiguation.  
- SynTime outperforms several rule-based and ML baselines on TimeBank and Tweets and is competitive on WikiWars, with particularly high recall.  
- The approach is notably effective on noisy Twitter data.  
- Main contribution: a simple, efficient identify-and-expand heuristic system that achieves state-of-the-art performance.  
- Secondary contribution: an empirical analysis across four datasets that justifies the system design and offers community insights.  
- Strength: clear motivation and presentation, linking observations to Zipf's principle for justification.  
- Strength: strong empirical results validating the core hypothesis that locating time tokens captures most expressions.  
- Strength: simplicity and real-time efficiency make the system practical for deployment.  
- Weakness: insufficient implementation detail (regexes, token lists, exact heuristic logic) harms reproducibility.  
- Weakness: reliance on explicit time tokens may fail on complex or implicit expressions (though rare), with limited error analysis.  
- Weakness: underlying components (dictionaries, regexes, POS rules) are standard; novelty mainly in simplifying rule application.  
- The token lists are initialized from SUTime, further tying the approach to existing resources.  
- Question: clarify what the five "SEASON" types are and how culturally-specific/non-standard seasons are handled.  
- Question: provide an example of a time expression without a time token and explain sources of remaining recall errors.  
- Question: will the authors release source code and the complete rule/lexical resources to facilitate reproducibility and future work?
