### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based method for recognizing time expressions in free text. The approach is motivated by an empirical analysis of four datasets, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary of keywords, and overwhelmingly contain at least one explicit "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday').

The SynTime method operates in three stages. First, it defines a set of syntactic types for time-related words (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and identifies instances of these types in text using dictionary lookups, regular expressions, and POS tags to disambiguate. Second, it forms initial "time segments" by starting with an identified time token and heuristically expanding its boundaries left and right to include adjacent modifiers and numerals. Finally, it applies a set of merging rules to combine adjacent or overlapping segments into complete time expressions. The system is evaluated on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets datasets, where it is shown to outperform several state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning baselines, particularly in terms of recall.

### Contributions
1.  **An effective and simple heuristic-based system for time expression recognition.** The primary contribution is the SynTime tagger itself, which achieves state-of-the-art performance with a conceptually simple and efficient "identify-and-expand" methodology. This approach, which focuses on locating core time tokens and applying simple expansion rules, represents a practical and lightweight alternative to more complex finite-state or machine learning models.
2.  **An insightful empirical analysis of the structure of time expressions.** The paper provides a valuable analysis across four distinct datasets that grounds the system's design in data. The observations that time expressions are short and are anchored by a small set of recurring time tokens provide strong justification for the proposed method and offer useful insights for the NLP community at large.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The design of the SynTime system is clearly motivated by the preliminary data analysis, which is itself a strong component of the work. The authors logically connect their observations to Zipf's principle of least effort, providing a solid theoretical foundation for why such a simple approach should be effective.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves impressive results, significantly outperforming strong baselines on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets and remaining competitive on WikiWars. The high recall across all datasets validates the core hypothesis that identifying time tokens is sufficient to capture the vast majority of time expressions. The system's excellent performance on noisy Twitter data is particularly noteworthy.
3.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The system's design is elegant in its simplicity. By eschewing complex grammars or feature-heavy models in favor of a small set of types and heuristic rules, SynTime is extremely lightweight and fast ("runs in real time"). This makes it a highly practical tool that could be easily deployed in real-world applications where efficiency is critical.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Detail Hinders Reproducibility:** While the high-level approach is clear, the paper lacks sufficient detail for others to reproduce the work. The full set of token regular expressions for the 15 time token types and 5 modifier types is not provided. Furthermore, the "simple heuristic rules" for boundary expansion and segment merging are described at an abstract level, but the exact logic and implementation details are underspecified. Without these details, it is difficult to verify the results or build upon the method.
2.  **Handling of Complex and Implicit Time Expressions:** The system's reliance on an initial "time token" suggests it may be brittle for expressions that do not contain one. While the analysis shows these are rare (less than 7% of cases), this is still a notable limitation. The paper does not provide a clear error analysis of what types of expressions are missed or how the system handles more complex or descriptive temporal language (e.g., "the day after the election").
3.  **Novelty of Components:** Although the overall system design is effective, its core components (dictionaries of time-related words, regular expressions, POS-based disambiguation) are standard techniques for rule-based information extraction. The method initializes its token lists from SUTime, further anchoring it in existing work. The primary novelty lies in the *simplification* of the rule application strategy (the identify-and-expand heuristic), but the novelty of the underlying building blocks is limited.

### Questions to Authors
1.  In Section 4.1, you define 15 types for time tokens, including "SEASON (5)". Could you clarify what the five seasons are? More broadly, how does your system handle culturally-specific or non-standard seasons (e.g., "Ramadan", "the holiday season", "monsoon season") that are not part of a small, fixed set?
2.  Could you provide an example of a time expression from your test data that does not contain a time token and discuss how your system (or a future version) might handle it? Given that your system already achieves very high recall, what is the primary source of the remaining recall errors?
3.  The proposed system is lightweight, efficient, and highly effective. To maximize its impact and facilitate future research, do you plan to release the source code and the complete set of rules and lexical resources?
