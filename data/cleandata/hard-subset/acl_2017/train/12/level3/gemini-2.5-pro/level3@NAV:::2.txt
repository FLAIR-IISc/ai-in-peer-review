**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces SynTime, a simple and lightweight rule-based system for recognizing time expressions from text. The method is based on an analysis of four corpora, which suggests that time expressions are typically short and use a small vocabulary. SynTime defines three syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and applies simple heuristic rules to identify and merge time segments. The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that a straightforward, type-based heuristic system can achieve state-of-the-art performance on several benchmark datasets, outperforming more complex rule-based and machine learning systems. A secondary contribution is the analysis of time expression characteristics across different corpora, which provides a solid motivation for the system's design.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Performance:** The experimental results are a key strength. SynTime significantly outperforms established baselines, including SUTime and HeidelTime, on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The high recall scores, in particular, validate the authors' core observation that most time expressions contain identifiable "time tokens."
2.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The method's simplicity is a major advantage. Unlike opaque machine learning models or intricate, hand-crafted rule systems, SynTime's approach is easy to understand, analyze, and debug. This transparency, combined with its real-time performance, makes it highly suitable for practical applications where speed and predictability are critical.
3.  **Well-Motivated Design:** The proposed method is well-grounded in the preliminary analysis of four datasets. The authors' observations about the concise nature of time expressions directly inform the design of the syntactic types and boundary-expansion rules, creating a clear and logical link between analysis and implementation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Questionable Generalizability and Risk of Overfitting:** The paper's most significant weakness is its evaluation methodology, which raises serious concerns about the generalizability of the proposed method. The rules for SynTime appear to have been derived from the analysis of the same corpora that were subsequently used for evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). This circular process risks overfitting the rules to the specific characteristics and idiosyncrasies of the test sets, rather than demonstrating a generally applicable method.
2.  **Lack of Justification for Cross-Domain Effectiveness:** While the authors use multiple corpora, they do not provide any analysis to substantiate the claim that the derived rules are broadly applicable. For example, there is no discussion of the overlap in time expression vocabulary or patterns across the different domains. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the high performance is due to a robust method or simply due to the datasets sharing similar temporal patterns that the rules were designed to capture. To truly demonstrate the strength and generality of SynTime, the authors should evaluate its performance on a new, unseen corpus from a distinct domain that was not used in any part of the rule design or analysis process.
