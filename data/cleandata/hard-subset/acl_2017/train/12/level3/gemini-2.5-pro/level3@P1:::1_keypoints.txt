- Proposes SynTime: a lightweight, rule-based identify-then-expand system for recognizing time expressions centered on core time tokens.  
- Empirical analysis of four corpora finds most time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and >93% contain at least one core time token.  
- Method: identify candidate time tokens, heuristically expand bidirectionally to form segments, then merge adjacent/overlapping segments into expressions.  
- Evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a Tweets corpus, showing strong performance and notably high recall, especially on noisy tweets.  
- Contribution: a simple, efficient rule-based approach achieving state-of-the-art results without complex grammars or ML models.  
- Contribution: data-driven empirical analysis motivating design choices and revealing linguistic properties of time expressions.  
- Strength: demonstrates effectiveness through simplicity — high recall and strong overall results with an intuitive strategy.  
- Strength: solid empirical grounding — corpus analysis directly informs system architecture and token choices.  
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation with helpful figures and examples.  
- Weakness: insufficient detail for reproducibility — rules, token lists, regexes, and exact heuristics are not fully provided.  
- Weakness: limited error analysis — little discussion of false negatives, false positives, or failure modes beyond POS/tokenization dependence.  
- Weakness: claimed novelty of “syntactic types” is overstated; positional typing appears similar to prior rule-based systems like SUTime.  
- Question: Will the authors release full rules, token lists, and source code to enable reproducibility?  
- Question: What are the five SEASON tokens, and how are culturally specific or nonstandard seasons handled (e.g., Ramadan, Lent)?  
- Question: Can the authors give examples of valid time expressions lacking any defined time token and explain how SynTime handles them?  
- Question: With recall near the theoretical ceiling, should future work focus on improving precision or targeting remaining classes of missed expressions?
