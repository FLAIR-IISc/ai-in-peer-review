**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression extraction. The work is founded on the key empirical observation that most time expressions are short and contain at least one "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday'). The proposed method is a three-step process: it first identifies time tokens using dictionaries, regular expressions, and POS tags; it then bidirectionally expands these tokens into segments using heuristic rules; and finally, it merges adjacent or overlapping segments into complete time expressions. The system is evaluated on three datasets, demonstrating significant performance gains over both rule-based and machine learning baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A simple yet highly effective heuristic method for time expression recognition that anchors its search on identifiable time tokens and uses straightforward expansion and merging rules. This provides an elegant alternative to more complex rule-based or feature-heavy machine learning systems.
2.  A valuable data-driven analysis across four datasets that empirically validates the core assumptions underpinning the method's design, namely that time expressions are short and almost always contain a time token.
3.  A strong empirical evaluation showing that this simplified approach outperforms several state-of-the-art systems, particularly on less formal text like tweets, achieving very high recall.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and the proposed method is presented with great clarity. The step-by-step process of token identification, segment expansion, and expression extraction is easy to follow, and the figures effectively illustrate the merging logic.
2.  The methodology is strongly motivated by the authors' preliminary analysis. The design choices, such as centering the entire process on finding time tokens, are direct consequences of their observation that over 93% of time expressions contain such a token. This strong connection between empirical observation and system design is a significant strength.
3.  The experimental results are impressive. Achieving state-of-the-art performance, especially in terms of recall, with a lightweight, interpretable rule-based system is a notable accomplishment and highlights the power of the core insight.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness is a lack of sufficient detail, which hinders reproducibility. While the general principles of the heuristic rules are explained, the full set of rules for expansion and merging is not provided. This makes it difficult for other researchers to precisely replicate the system or build upon it.
2.  Certain definitions and claims are underspecified. For example, Section 4.1 mentions a time token type for "5 seasons" but does not enumerate them or discuss how culturally specific seasons (e.g., Ramadan) are handled. A minor inconsistency also appears in Section 5.1, which refers to "two benchmark datasets" when three are used in the experiments.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  In Section 4.1, could you please clarify the "5 seasons" and explain the system's strategy for handling culturally specific or non-standard time expressions like "Ramadan" or "the holiday season"?
2.  Could you provide an example of a time expression that does *not* contain a time token, which your system might miss?
3.  Given that your system's recall (e.g., 92-95%) is already approaching the ceiling suggested by your analysis (93-97% of expressions having a time token), what are the primary sources of the remaining errors, and what are the most promising avenues for further improvement?
4.  To address the reproducibility concerns, do you plan to release the full set of rules and/or the software implementation of SynTime?
