The paper proposes a rule-based method, SynTime, for time expression extraction from free text. SynTime is based on the key insight that time expressions are typically short and contain at least one time token. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that support this insight. They define a syntactic type system for time expressions, which includes 15 types for time tokens, 5 types for modifiers, and 1 type for numerals. SynTime uses simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions, which are designed based on the idea of boundary expansion.

The main contributions of this paper are the analysis of time expressions from four datasets, the proposal of a type-based time expression tagger, SynTime, and the evaluation of SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets. The authors demonstrate that SynTime outperforms the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets and achieves comparable results on WikiWars dataset.

The strengths of this paper are the clear presentation of the idea and the thorough evaluation of the proposed method. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the time expressions and justify the design of SynTime based on the observations. The evaluation results show that SynTime is effective in recognizing time expressions, especially in informal environments like tweets.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The authors do not provide enough details about the "5 seasons" mentioned in Section 4.1, and it is unclear how SynTime handles time expressions like Ramadan or holiday seasons. Additionally, the text mentions "two benchmark datasets" in Section 5.1, but the paper actually uses three datasets. The authors should clarify these points to improve the paper's clarity.

Furthermore, the authors claim that SynTime can recognize time expressions without time tokens, but they do not provide an example of such a time expression. It would be helpful to include an example to illustrate this point. Moreover, the authors report that SynTime achieves a recall of 92% on all three datasets, but they do not explain how the performance can be further improved, given that 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token.

Finally, the authors should consider releasing the full set of rules and software for SynTime to facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.

In terms of the contributions of this paper, I would rank them as follows:

1. The proposal of a type-based time expression tagger, SynTime, which defines syntactic types for tokens and uses simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.
2. The analysis of time expressions from four datasets, which provides insights into the characteristics of time expressions and supports the design of SynTime.
3. The evaluation of SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, which demonstrates the effectiveness of SynTime in recognizing time expressions.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The clear presentation of the idea and the thorough evaluation of the proposed method.
2. The detailed analysis of the time expressions and the justification of the design of SynTime based on the observations.
3. The effectiveness of SynTime in recognizing time expressions, especially in informal environments like tweets.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The lack of clarity on certain points, such as the "5 seasons" mentioned in Section 4.1 and the handling of time expressions like Ramadan or holiday seasons.
2. The inconsistency in the text, such as the mention of "two benchmark datasets" in Section 5.1.
3. The lack of an example of a time expression without a time token and the unclear explanation of how the performance of SynTime can be further improved.

Questions to the authors:

1. Can you provide more details about the "5 seasons" mentioned in Section 4.1 and how SynTime handles time expressions like Ramadan or holiday seasons?
2. Can you clarify the inconsistency in the text regarding the number of benchmark datasets used in the paper?
3. Can you provide an example of a time expression without a time token and explain how SynTime recognizes such time expressions?
4. Can you explain how the performance of SynTime can be further improved, given that 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token?
