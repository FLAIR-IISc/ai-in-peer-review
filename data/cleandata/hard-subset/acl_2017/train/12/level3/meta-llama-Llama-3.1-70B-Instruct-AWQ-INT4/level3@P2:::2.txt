This paper proposes a type-based approach, SynTime, to recognize time expressions from free text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and observe that only a small group of words are used to express time information, and the words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types, namely time token, modifier, and numeral, to group time-related regular expressions over tokens. On the types, SynTime designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A thorough analysis of time expressions from four datasets, providing insights into the characteristics of time expressions and the principle of least effort.
2. A type-based approach, SynTime, which defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.
3. Experimental results showing that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets.

The strengths of this submission are:

1. The simplicity of the proposed method is a strong advantage for analysis and basic application needs. The use of syntactic types and simple heuristic rules makes the method easy to understand and implement.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime against state-of-the-art baselines, including rule-based time taggers and machine learning-based time taggers.
3. The analysis of time expressions from four datasets provides valuable insights into the characteristics of time expressions and the principle of least effort.

The weaknesses of this submission are:

1. The major concern is about the generality of the proposed method. The rules were designed using the same corpora as evaluation, risking overfitting. The evaluation corpora domains may have biased the rule design, and there is no discussion provided on the overlap of time expressions across corpora to justify generality.
2. The method relies heavily on the quality of the POS tagger, and the authors acknowledge that the incorrect tokens and POS tags can affect the result.
3. The expansion of SynTime requires manual annotation of words, which can be time-consuming and may not be feasible for large-scale applications.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting and ensure the generality of the proposed method?
2. Can you provide more details on the expansion process of SynTime, including the criteria for selecting words to be added and the manual annotation process?
3. How do you plan to improve the robustness of the method to handle incorrect tokens and POS tags?

Additional Comments:

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* The use of figures and tables is helpful in illustrating the proposed method and experimental results.
* The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work, including rule-based time expression recognition and machine learning-based methods.
