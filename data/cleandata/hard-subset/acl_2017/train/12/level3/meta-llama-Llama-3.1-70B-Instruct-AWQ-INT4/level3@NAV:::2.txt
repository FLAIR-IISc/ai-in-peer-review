**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a simple rule-based method, SynTime, for recognizing time expressions from text. The method is based on a syntactic type system that defines three main types: time token, modifier, and numeral. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage. They then design simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions, which outperform state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning methods in experiments on three datasets.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Proposed Method**: The authors propose a type-based approach, SynTime, that defines syntactic types and designs simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition. This method provides an idea to simplify rule-based time taggers.
2. **Analysis of Time Expressions**: The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage.
3. **Experiments and Evaluation**: The authors conduct experiments on three datasets and compare SynTime with three state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1. **Simplicity**: SynTime is a light-weight rule-based tagger that runs in real time, making it suitable for basic application needs.
2. **Flexibility**: SynTime can be easily expanded by adding time-related token regular expressions from training text, enabling it to recognize time expressions in text from different types and domains.
3. **High Recall**: SynTime achieves high recall on all three datasets, indicating that it covers most of the time tokens.
4. **Good Performance on Informal Text**: SynTime performs exceptionally well on tweets, which are informal text, demonstrating its ability to handle noisy and diverse data.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Overfitting**: The rules were designed using the same corpora as evaluation, risking overfitting. This may limit the generalizability of SynTime to other datasets.
2. **Lack of Statistics**: No statistics or discussion is provided on the overlap of time expressions across corpora to justify the generality of SynTime.
3. **Dependence on POS Tagging**: SynTime assumes that words are tokenized and POS tagged correctly, which may not always be the case in reality.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting and ensure the generalizability of SynTime to other datasets?
2. Can you provide more details on the expansion of SynTime and how it can be adapted to different domains and languages?
3. How do you plan to handle the limitations of POS tagging and ensure that SynTime is robust to errors in tokenization and POS tagging?
