**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a type-based approach, named SynTime, to recognize time expressions from free text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and observe that time expressions are generally short, expressed using a small vocabulary, and demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions. The method is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, and the results show that SynTime outperforms the baselines in terms of precision, recall, and F1 score.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Analysis of time expressions**: The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage.
2. **Proposed method**: SynTime is a type-based approach that defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.
3. **Experimental evaluation**: The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1. **Simplicity**: SynTime is a simple and lightweight method that can be easily expanded by adding keywords for different types and domains.
2. **Effectiveness**: SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and machine-learning methods in terms of precision, recall, and F1 score.
3. **Flexibility**: SynTime can be applied to different types of text, including informal text such as tweets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Overfitting**: The rules were designed using the same corpora as evaluation, which may lead to overfitting and reduce the method's generality.
2. **Domain bias**: The evaluation corpora domains may have biased the rule design, which may not generalize well to other domains.
3. **Lack of statistics**: The authors do not provide statistics or discussion on the overlap of time expressions across corpora to justify the method's generality.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the potential overfitting issue and ensure that SynTime generalizes well to other corpora?
2. Can you provide more information on the overlap of time expressions across corpora and how SynTime performs on a new, distinct corpus?
3. How do you plan to extend SynTime to recognize more complex time expressions, such as those involving prepositions or descriptive phrases?
