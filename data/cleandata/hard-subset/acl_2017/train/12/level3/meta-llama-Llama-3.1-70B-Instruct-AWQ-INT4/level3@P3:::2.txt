**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a simple rule-based method, SynTime, for recognizing time expressions from text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and observe that most time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they define a syntactic type system for time expressions and design simple heuristic rules for recognition. The proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning methods on benchmark datasets and tweets data.

**Contributions**

1. **Analysis of time expressions**: The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of time expressions from four datasets, revealing insights into their structure and syntax.
2. **SynTime: A type-based time expression tagger**: The proposed method defines a syntactic type system for time expressions and uses simple heuristic rules for recognition, demonstrating a novel approach to time expression recognition.
3. **Experiments and evaluation**: The authors conduct experiments on three datasets, comparing SynTime with state-of-the-art baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1. **Simplicity and flexibility**: SynTime's simple heuristic rules and type-based approach make it an extremely light-weight rule-based tagger that can be easily expanded and adapted to different domains.
2. **High performance**: SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and tweets data, demonstrating its effectiveness in recognizing time expressions.
3. **Insights into time expression structure**: The authors' analysis provides valuable insights into the structure and syntax of time expressions, which can inform future research in this area.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Generality concerns**: The rules used in SynTime were designed using the same corpora as the evaluation datasets, which may lead to overfitting and reduced generality.
2. **Domain bias**: The evaluation corpora may have biased the rule design, and the authors do not provide statistics or discussion on the overlap of time expressions across corpora to justify generality.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate SynTime on three datasets, and it is unclear how well the method would perform on other datasets or in different domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the generality concerns and potential overfitting of SynTime's rules to the evaluation corpora?
2. Can you provide more information on the overlap of time expressions across corpora and how SynTime's performance might be affected by domain differences?
3. Have you considered evaluating SynTime on additional datasets or in different domains to further demonstrate its effectiveness and generality?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' analysis of time expressions provides valuable insights into their structure and syntax, and SynTime's simple heuristic rules and type-based approach make it an attractive solution for time expression recognition. However, the concerns regarding generality and domain bias need to be addressed to fully establish the method's effectiveness and robustness.
