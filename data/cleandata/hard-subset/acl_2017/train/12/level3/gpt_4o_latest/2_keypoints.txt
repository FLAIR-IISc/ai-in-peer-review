- Introduces SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer using syntactic types and heuristics.  
- Analyzes four datasets and finds time expressions are short, use small vocabularies, and share syntactic behavior.  
- Defines a syntactic type system for time tokens, modifiers, and numerals as the basis for SynTime.  
- SynTime is lightweight, expandable, requires no training, and runs in real time.  
- Outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning methods on benchmarks, especially on tweets.  
- Contribution 1: Detailed analysis of time expressions supporting the principle of least effort.  
- Contribution 2: Introduction of a type-based approach (SynTime) for time expression recognition.  
- Contribution 3: Experimental validation showing SynTime's effectiveness.  
- Strength: Strong empirical performance with notable gains in recall and F1 on informal text.  
- Strength: Simplicity and efficiency—no training needed and easy to extend with keywords.  
- Strength: Novel dataset-driven observations and theoretical framing via least effort.  
- Strength: General, syntactic-focus approach that is flexible and extensible.  
- Weakness: Possible overfitting to datasets used for rule design; limited testing on unseen corpora.  
- Weakness: Missing analysis/statistics on overlaps of time expressions across corpora.  
- Weakness: Reliance on accurate tokenization and POS tagging, which may fail in real-world settings.  
- Weakness: Manual addition of domain-specific keywords may limit scalability in specialized domains.  
- Question: Request for experiments on a new, unseen corpus to demonstrate generality.  
- Question: How SynTime handles ambiguous tokens like “May” (month vs. modal verb).  
- Question: Clarify computational efficiency vs. machine learning methods in runtime and resource usage.  
- Conclusion: Promising, practical approach with strong results but needs addressing of generality and preprocessing dependencies before acceptance.
