Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition. The method is motivated by an empirical analysis of four datasets showing that time expressions are short, drawn from a small lexicon, and almost always contain at least one “time token.” SynTime defines a small inventory of syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules to grow time segments around identified time tokens, then merges adjacent/overlapping segments into final mentions. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets set show strong results, with especially high recall and SOTA F1 on TimeBank and Tweets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical characterization of time expressions across multiple domains (news, Wikipedia, Twitter) that motivates a type-based, token-first tagging strategy.
2) A practical, type-based rule system (SynTime) using heuristic boundary expansion and simple merging rules that achieves strong recall and competitive-to-SOTA F1 while being real-time and easy to extend.
3) A thorough evaluation against established rule-based and learned baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime), including a new tweets dataset with careful annotation.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall and competitive or better F1: SynTime-I attains strict F1 of 92.09 on TimeBank and 91.74 on Tweets, outperforming HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime; relaxed F1 also leads on Tweets (95.87) and TimeBank (94.96). These results substantiate the paper’s core hypothesis that a token-first approach with simple expansion rules can recover most mentions.
- Simple, well-motivated design that is easy to extend and run in real time: The design follows clear observations (short spans, small lexicon, presence of time tokens), leading to a compact set of syntactic types and heuristic rules. The explicit expansion from SUTime’s token resources plus minimal domain-specific additions (Table 5) demonstrates practicality and low engineering cost, especially on Tweets where only five additions yield excellent performance.
- Clear articulation of the approach and rationale: The type inventory (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and the boundary-expansion strategy are easy to understand. The paper convincingly argues why fixed-length, fixed-position rules (common in prior taggers) can miss variants (e.g., “year 1” vs “1 year”) and how SynTime’s heuristics address this.
- Useful analysis and cross-domain perspective: The cross-corpus analysis (length distributions, token coverage, POS tendencies) provides insight that should be of interest beyond this specific system and may inform future learning-based taggers (e.g., type-level modeling).
- Competitive results on WikiWars with limited tuning: Despite not modeling many prepositions and descriptive expressions (as per TimeML conventions), SynTime achieves comparable F1, and expansion (SynTime-E) improves recall notably. This suggests a good path to domain adaptation through modest lexicon augmentation.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and underspecified implementation details: The exact token regexes carried over from SUTime, the full modifier/num types and POS constraints, disambiguation policies (e.g., “May,” “sat”), and the precise merging criteria (including stopping conditions and tie-breaking) are not fully enumerated. Without the full rule set or code, it will be hard to replicate results or apply SynTime to new domains/languages.
- Limited error analysis and ablations: The paper lacks qualitative error examples and a breakdown of failure modes (e.g., descriptive mentions, POS/tokenization errors, prepositional constructions). No ablations quantify the contribution of: (i) POS filtering; (ii) boundary expansion vs fixed rules; (iii) segment merging heuristics; (iv) SUTime initialization vs added tokens. Such analysis would strengthen the claims and guide future improvements.
- Novelty relative to existing rule-based systems is moderate: The approach is rule-based and builds on SUTime’s token resources; the main novelty is the syntactic-type orientation plus heuristic boundary expansion. A more explicit comparison of rule design philosophies and coverage (with examples SUTime/HeidelTime miss that SynTime captures, and vice versa) would better position the contribution.
- Evaluation clarity and fairness concerns: UWTime is trained on TimeBank+Tweets for Tweets evaluation; discuss fairness and whether similar supervision benefits (‘expansion’) are comparable across systems. For WikiWars, note where TimeML conventions vs corpus guidelines diverge (prepositions) and how this affects scores. Also clarify whether tweet annotation strictly followed TimeML and inter-annotator agreement.
- Missing concrete examples for key claims: The text states that ~7% of time expressions lack a time token; an explicit example set and how SynTime handles them (e.g., dependent segments) would help readers understand coverage gaps and design choices.

Questions to authors
- Types and coverage (Sec. 4.1): What are the “5” SEASON types? How are religious/cultural seasons (e.g., Ramadan, Lent), “holiday season,” or “back-to-school season” categorized (SEASON vs HOLIDAY vs PERIOD)? How are overlapping categories resolved?
- Non-token time expressions (Sec. 5.2): Please provide several concrete examples of time expressions that contain no time token and explain how SynTime recognizes or misses them (e.g., purely descriptive phrases).
- Recall upper bound and improvements: With ≥93% of mentions containing a time token and SynTime’s recall around 92–95%, what are the main residual recall errors? Are they due to POS/tokenization errors, missing tokens/modifiers, or merging failures? Which targeted changes would most improve recall?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the full rule inventory (token regexes, POS constraints, modifier/num lists, merging heuristics) and code? If not, can you release a complete rulespec to enable replication?
- Domain adaptation: How robust is SynTime to noisy POS (e.g., Twitter)? Did you try a POS-agnostic fallback? What is the impact on recall/precision?
- Ablations: Can you provide ablation results for POS filtering, boundary expansion, and segment merging to quantify their contributions?

Additional comments
- Editorial inconsistency (Sec. 5.1): The text says “two benchmark datasets,” but the paper evaluates on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). Please fix.
- Clarify initialization: Specify exactly which SUTime resources were imported and which were excluded; include counts per type for transparency.
- Tweets sampling and SUTime miss-rate: The 3% estimate is based on a 500 tweet sample; report confidence intervals and annotation agreement to contextualize this estimate.
- Limitations: Consider discussing generalization beyond English and the feasibility of porting the type system and heuristics to other languages with different time-expression conventions.

Overall, this is a well-written, practical paper with strong empirical results and a clear, insightful analysis. Addressing reproducibility, adding ablations/error analysis, and clarifying coverage details would significantly strengthen the submission.
