- Paper proposes SynTime: a lightweight rule-based system using syntactic types and boundary-expansion rules for time expression recognition.
- Key insight: time expressions are typically short and almost always contain at least one “time token,” enabling token-first extraction then boundary expansion/merging.
- SynTime pipeline: identify time tokens via dictionary/regex + POS filtering, expand left/right with heuristic rules, then merge segments into full expressions.
- Empirical analysis across four datasets shows short lengths, small vocabulary, and ≥93% of expressions contain a time token, motivating the design.
- Main contribution: a simple, interpretable type-based rule system (time token, modifier, numeral) with boundary-expansion and merging rules.
- Main contribution: thorough evaluation against strong rule-based and ML baselines on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new annotated Tweets dataset.
- Main contribution: demonstrated substantial gains, especially on tweets, with consistently high recall.
- Strength: strong empirical grounding linking observations to algorithm design and results.
- Strength: simple, effective, and maintainable syntactic-type framing with clear examples of merging logic.
- Strength: solid results—matches/exceeds state-of-the-art on TimeBank and tweets, competitive on WikiWars, notably high tweet strict F1 (~91.7).
- Strength: practical—lightweight, real-time, and easily extensible via small domain-specific token additions.
- Strength: well written and clearly presented with coherent narrative.
- Weakness: reproducibility gaps—missing exact token regexes, POS constraints, full rule set, and dataset lexical additions.
- Weakness: claims of significance lack statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap resampling on F1).
- Weakness: unclear baseline fairness and tuning parity, especially whether HeidelTime/SUTime were similarly customized.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on POS tags may hurt performance on noisy text; unclear whether Twitter-specific taggers or sensitivity analyses were used.
- Weakness: minor inconsistencies/ambiguities in paper wording and category definitions (e.g., “two benchmark datasets,” unclear “SEASON (5)”).
- Question: What are the five SEASON tokens and how are cultural/religious/regional seasons handled or mapped?
- Question: Will the full rule base, token regexes, POS constraints, lexical additions, and software be released for reproducibility?
- Question: Provide examples of time expressions that lack a time token and explain how SynTime handles them (e.g., adverbials like “then,” “recently”).
- Question: How will the remaining ~7% of token-less expressions be recovered—patterns for temporal adverbs, shallow dependency cues, or a small classifier?
- Question: What versions/configs of HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime were used and were domain adaptations enabled; was UWTime also trained only on Tweets for fair low-resource comparison?
- Question: Which POS taggers were used per domain; was a Twitter-focused tagger tried; how sensitive is SynTime to POS errors?
- Question: Provide statistical significance testing details for main F1 improvements (test used and number of bootstraps).
- Question: Did the Gigaword analysis influence rule design or expansions, and if so, clarify separation to avoid leakage.
- Additional comment: Fix wording inconsistencies (e.g., “two benchmark datasets” vs. three).
- Additional comment: Add a short error analysis categorizing common misses (token-less expressions, POS errors) and possible extensions.
- Additional comment: Note compatibility with existing normalizers (e.g., SUTime) and any corner cases from SynTime spans.
