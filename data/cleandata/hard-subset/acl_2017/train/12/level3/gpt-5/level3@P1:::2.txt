Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that groups time-related tokens into a small set of syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and applies simple heuristic “boundary expansion” rules to assemble full timex spans. The authors first analyze four datasets and observe that time expressions are short, draw on a small vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime is initialized from token-level regexes (largely from SUTime) and can be expanded by adding a small number of keywords. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset show strong performance, particularly high recall, surpassing HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on two datasets and remaining competitive on the third.

Main contributions (reviewer’s view)
1) A practical, simple, and fast rule-based recognizer that achieves state-of-the-art performance while being easy to extend and deploy (primary).
2) A syntactic type system and heuristic boundary-expansion strategy that simplifies rule design and improves flexibility over fixed-length, fixed-position rules (secondary).
3) An empirical analysis of timex properties across datasets (length, vocabulary size, syntactic behavior), informing the system design and offering interpretable insights (tertiary).

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime attains top F1 on TimeBank and Tweets and comparable results on WikiWars, with best recall across all datasets. For instance, on TimeBank (strict), SynTime-I achieves 92.09 F1 (higher than HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime), and on Tweets (strict) 91.74 F1, with relaxed recall ≥92% on all datasets. This supports the claim that the type-first strategy covers most timexes.
- Simplicity and extensibility: The system relies on a small number of syntactic types and very simple rules, runs in real time, and requires minimal expansion. Table 5 shows only a handful of added tokens/modifiers per corpus (e.g., 4/5 on TimeBank, 3/2 on Tweets), underscoring low maintenance cost.
- Robustness on noisy data: The method performs exceptionally well on tweets (strict F1 ~91.7, relaxed F1 ~95.9), a domain where machine-learning systems often degrade. The analysis connecting short expressions, small lexicon, and formal variants in social media provides a convincing explanation for this robustness.
- Clear methodology and link from analysis to design: The observations (short spans, small vocabulary, syntactic regularity) directly motivate the type system and boundary expansion. This makes the approach interpretable and likely easier to adapt.
- Competitive with both rule-based and learning baselines: Outperforming HeidelTime and SUTime while matching or exceeding UWTime demonstrates that a concise, linguistically motivated rule set can be highly competitive without training.

Weaknesses
- Generality and potential overfitting to analyzed corpora: The rules and expansions appear to be informed by the same datasets used in evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). Although SUTime regexes provide initialization, expansions and heuristic choices may implicitly encode corpus/domain characteristics, risking optimistic results. A clean separation between analysis/design data and evaluation data is not clearly enforced.
- Domain bias and limited breadth of evaluation: The three evaluation corpora (newswire, Wikipedia wars, English tweets) are related to the analysis and to common timex benchmarks. Absent is a truly unseen domain (e.g., clinical, legal, emails, forums) where vocabulary and style differ. This limits claims of broad applicability.
- Lack of overlap statistics and coverage analysis: The paper argues that the timex lexicon is small and shared, but does not quantify cross-corpus overlap (e.g., Jaccard of time tokens/modifiers across datasets) or show coverage before/after expansion. Such statistics would substantiate the generality claim.
- Dataset construction concerns for Tweets: Tweets were pre-filtered by SUTime to contain at least one timex, which could bias the sample toward expressions SUTime is already inclined to find (even if SynTime later outperforms it). The estimate that SUTime misses ~3% is based on a small manual subsample; a larger, unbiased sample would be preferable.
- Missing significance tests and deeper error analysis: The paper reports many “significant” improvements but does not present statistical tests. A breakdown by error type (boundary errors, missed tokens, POS-tagging failures) and by category (DATE/TIME/DURATION) would clarify where SynTime helps or struggles, particularly on WikiWars where strict F1 lags.

Questions to Authors
- Data usage and separation: Which datasets informed the rule and type design, and which informed expansion? Was any dataset reserved exclusively for final evaluation without prior inspection? Please clarify the exact train/dev/test protocol per corpus.
- Cross-domain generalization: Can you evaluate SynTime on a genuinely unseen corpus (e.g., ACE/Newswire not in TimeBank, clinical notes, email, or forum text) without any expansion? Reporting zero-shot and few-shot (minimal expansion) results would strengthen generality claims.
- Overlap and coverage: What fraction of time tokens/modifiers are shared across the three corpora? How does coverage change before vs. after expansion on each dataset? A small table of overlaps would be informative.
- Statistical significance: Are F1 improvements significant (e.g., paired bootstrap on document-level predictions)? Please report p-values or confidence intervals.
- Runtime and resource use: Can you provide wall-clock timings vs. HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime and memory footprint to substantiate the “real-time” claim?
- Robustness to tagging errors: Given reliance on POS to disambiguate (e.g., May), how sensitive is SynTime to POS errors, especially on tweets? Have you considered backoff strategies without POS?
- Scope and multilinguality: The analysis ties timex properties to the principle of least effort; do you have preliminary evidence that the same type system and rules transfer to other languages, or languages with richer morphology?
