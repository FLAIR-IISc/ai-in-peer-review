Summary and contributions
- A lightweight, rule-based framework (SynTime) for time expression extraction that defines syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses bidirectional boundary expansion and merging heuristics to form expressions.
- A thorough empirical evaluation on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime showing consistent recall gains and strong F1, especially on Tweets.
- A cross-corpus analysis of time expressions (four datasets) that motivates the design with observations about short lengths, small vocabularies, and type regularities.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and recall: SynTime-I/E achieve best or second-best results in most metrics, with >92% relaxed recall on all datasets and large F1 gains on Tweets (e.g., strict F1 ≈ 91.7, +11.3 over the best baseline). The reported best recall across all datasets substantiates the core design choice of anchoring on time tokens.
- Well-motivated, clearly presented rule design: The move from semantic categories to syntactic types plus boundary expansion is simple and compelling, aligning with the analysis (e.g., 93% expressions contain a time token; most are ≤3 tokens).
- Practicality and efficiency: The approach is real-time, language-resource light, and extensible by adding a small number of domain tokens (e.g., WikiWars gains with modest expansions), making it attractive for production settings.
- Clarity of writing and structure: The pipeline, types, and merging conditions are explained with concrete examples that make replication conceptually straightforward.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility is underspecified: Exact dictionaries/regexes taken from SUTime, POS tag filters, the full merging rule set (e.g., comma/linkage conditions), and the added tokens during expansion (Table 5 lists counts but not content) are not fully enumerated, impeding replication.
- Taxonomy ambiguities: Sec. 4.1 claims SEASON has “(5)” instances; it is unclear what the five seasons are and how religious/holiday “seasons” (e.g., Ramadan, Lent) or overlapping periods are classified, especially given a separate HOLIDAY type.
- Evaluation/reporting inconsistencies: Sec. 5.1 states “two benchmark datasets” while experiments use three datasets, which needs correction and clearer split/descriptive statistics (e.g., tweet sampling, train/test protocol for all systems).
- Domain and annotation assumptions: Adherence to TimeML (excluding many prepositions) appears to hurt WikiWars and may limit generality; reliance on POS tags introduces brittleness (e.g., “sat” error) without quantified sensitivity analysis.

Questions to authors
- Sec. 4.1: What are the “5 seasons”? How are periods like Ramadan, Lent, “holiday season,” monsoon, etc., handled (SEASON vs. HOLIDAY vs. PERIOD), and how are overlaps resolved?
- Sec. 5.1: Please reconcile “two benchmark datasets” with the use of three datasets and provide precise splits and preprocessing details for all systems.
- Sec. 5.2: Provide a concrete example and handling strategy for a time expression without a time token.
- With 93% of expressions containing a time token and system relaxed recall at 92%, where are the residual errors, and what are the most promising paths to further improve recall/precision (e.g., token-less cases, POS errors, prepositional spans)?
- Will you release the full rule sets, dictionaries/regexes, added tokens for SynTime-E, and code to ensure reproducibility?
