Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition built around a small set of syntactic types and simple heuristic “boundary expansion” rules. The key insight is that time expressions are typically short and almost always contain at least one “time token.” SynTime first identifies time tokens via dictionary/regex lookup refined by POS tags, then expands left/right to form time segments using heuristic rules over modifiers and numerals, and finally merges segments into full expressions. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset show strong gains over HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, particularly on tweets, with consistently high recall.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A clear empirical analysis across four datasets showing time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and are anchored by time tokens; this motivates the design and is borne out in results.
- SynTime: a simple, interpretable, type-based rule system (time token, modifier, numeral) with boundary expansion and segment-merging rules that run in real time and are easy to extend.
- A thorough evaluation against strong rule-based and ML baselines on three datasets, including a new manually annotated tweets set, demonstrating substantial gains (especially recall).

Strengths
- Strong empirical grounding: The four observations (short length, small vocabulary, ≥93% include a time token, syntactic regularities) are convincing and directly inform the algorithm. The subsequent high recalls (≥92% relaxed across datasets) align with the 93% observation.
- Simple and effective method: The syntactic-type framing and boundary-expansion rules are clear, intuitive, and easy to maintain. Examples in Fig. 3 make the merging logic understandable.
- Solid empirical results: SynTime matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on TimeBank and tweets and is competitive on WikiWars. The tweet results are especially notable (strict F1 ≈ 91.7 with large gains over baselines).
- Practicality: Lightweight, real-time execution, and low-cost extensibility via adding a small number of domain-specific tokens (Table 5), with clear evidence of improvements in WikiWars after expansion.
- Well written and clearly presented: The paper is easy to follow, with a coherent narrative from observation to design to evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility gaps: Key resources and procedural specifics are underspecified. The exact token regexes, POS constraints per type, full boundary-expansion and merging rules, and the added lexical items per dataset are not fully enumerated. Without code/rules, replication is difficult.
- “Significance” without statistics: The paper claims significant improvements but does not report statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap resampling on F1). Please add tests or temper the claim.
- Baseline fairness and tuning: SynTime-E benefits from manual expansion using training data. It is unclear whether comparable tuning was allowed for HeidelTime/SUTime (which can be customized), particularly for tweets. Clarify configurations and tuning parity.
- POS reliance in noisy text: Disambiguation hinges on POS tags (e.g., filtering “May”). For tweets, standard POS taggers can be brittle; the paper does not discuss whether a Twitter-specific tagger was used, nor sensitivity analyses to POS errors beyond a brief note in 5.3.
- Minor inconsistencies/ambiguities: Section 5.1 says “two benchmark datasets,” yet the experiments use three datasets. Also, “SEASON (5)” is unclear (what are the five?), and the handling of religious/cultural seasons is unspecified.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Sec 4.1: What are the “5” SEASON tokens? Are both “fall” and “autumn” counted separately? How are cultural/religious or regional seasons handled (e.g., Ramadan, Lent, monsoon, rainy/dry season, “holiday season”)? Are they mapped to HOLIDAY, PERIOD, or SEASON?
- Release plan: Will you release the full rule base (token regexes, POS constraints, boundary-expansion and merging rules), added lexical items per dataset, and software to ensure reproducibility?
- Sec 5.2: Please provide at least two concrete examples of time expressions that do not contain a time token (i.e., within your type inventory), and explain how SynTime handles them. Candidates might include adverbials like “then,” “recently,” or “afterwards,” if applicable.
- Beyond the 93% anchor: With 93% of expressions containing a time token and system recall ≈92%, how do you plan to recover the remaining cases? Would adding patterns for temporal adverbs/deictics, shallow dependency cues (e.g., “by then,” “until further notice”), or a small learned classifier for token-less spans help?
- Baselines: What versions/configurations of HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime were used? Were any domain adaptations enabled (e.g., SUTime custom rules for tweets)? For UWTime, you trained with TimeBank+Tweets; did you also try training only on Tweets for a fairer low-resource comparison?
- POS tagging: Which POS tagger(s) were used per domain (news vs. tweets)? Did you try a Twitter-focused POS tagger (e.g., ARK)? How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors (ablation or error rates by tagger)?
- Statistical testing: Please report significance testing for the main F1 improvements (strict and relaxed), including the test used and number of samples/bootstraps.
- Gigaword: You analyze four datasets but evaluate on three. Did the Gigaword analysis affect the rule design or expansions? If so, please clarify the separation to avoid leakage.

Additional comments
- Sec 5.1 wording issue: It says “two benchmark datasets” although three datasets are used; please correct for clarity.
- Consider adding a short error analysis categorizing common misses (especially token-less time expressions and POS errors) and how your rules could be extended to cover them.
- Normalization is out of scope, but a brief note on compatibility with existing normalizers (e.g., SUTime’s) and any corner cases created by SynTime’s spans would be helpful.
