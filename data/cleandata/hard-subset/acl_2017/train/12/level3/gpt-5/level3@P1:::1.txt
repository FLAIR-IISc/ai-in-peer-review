Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition. The approach is driven by an analysis across four corpora, which finds that time expressions are short, rely on a small set of keywords (“time tokens”), and display consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime defines syntactic types for time-related tokens (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics: detect time tokens via lexicon/regex and POS constraints; expand left/right to collect modifiers/numerals; then merge adjacent/overlapping segments into full time expressions. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset compare SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

Main contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A type-based, boundary-expansion method that achieves strong recall and competitive or superior F1 to state-of-the-art systems, with a compact, real-time rule set.
2) An empirical analysis of time expressions (length, vocabulary size, prevalence of time tokens, syntactic regularities) that motivates the design and helps explain the gains.
3) Practical demonstration of low-effort extensibility via small lexicon additions that adapt the system to new domains (Tweets, WikiWars).

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance, especially on Tweets and TimeBank:
  • TimeBank: strict F1 92.09 (SynTime-I) vs. 83.10 (UWTime), 81.34 (HeidelTime), 79.57 (SUTime); relaxed F1 94.96 (SynTime-I), highest.
  • Tweets: strict F1 91.74 and relaxed F1 95.87, with absolute gains of 11.37 (strict) and 6.33 (relaxed) over the best baseline; recalls >92% across datasets. These numbers substantiate the claim that the type+heuristic design is effective.
- Simple, well-motivated method with clear intuition and practical advantages:
  • Three-stage pipeline with minimal moving parts; runs in real time.
  • Easy extensibility: adding a handful of keywords improved WikiWars recall (e.g., +3.25 strict recall), while TimeBank/Tweets needed very few additions (Table 5), consistent with the “small vocabulary” observation.
- Data-driven motivation:
  • The analysis across four datasets (short spans, compact lexicon, ≥93% contain a time token, POS cues within time expressions) is convincing and aligns with the system’s design choices (POS filtering, token-first discovery).
- Good clarity of the core rules:
  • The boundary expansion and merge conditions (e.g., handling comma/linkage boundaries, dependent segments like “8 to 20 days”) are described concisely with illustrative examples (Figure 3).

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and specificity of resources:
  • The paper does not provide the complete token lists/regexes (beyond saying they were initialized from SUTime), the exact POS constraints per type, or the full set of expansion/merging rules. Without releasing code/rules, reproducing the reported gains may be difficult. The handling of ambiguity (e.g., “May”) and POS constraints are only described at a high level.
- Incremental novelty vs. prior rule-based systems:
  • While the “syntactic vs. semantic types” distinction is interesting, SUTime/HeidelTime also rely on token classes, POS, and rule-based expansion. The paper could strengthen the novelty case via ablations showing which aspects (syntactic typing, boundary heuristics, POS filters) drive the improvements, and by clarifying differences from SUTime’s cascaded finite automata in practice.
- Evaluation design and reporting:
  • Tweets dataset was constructed and annotated by the authors, with initial selection seeded by SUTime; this risks selection bias and lacks inter-annotator agreement statistics. The test set is relatively small (200 tweets). Gigaword is analyzed but not used in recognition experiments, which is slightly confusing.
  • The paper mentions “two benchmark datasets” but then evaluates on three datasets; minor but confusing inconsistency.
- Coverage limitations tied to annotation choices:
  • Following TimeML/TimeBank’s exclusion of many prepositions reduces coverage of more descriptive expressions, especially in WikiWars; the system underperforms there relative to other datasets. The approach currently tackles only recognition, not normalization, limiting direct applicability in downstream tasks.
- Limited error analysis and diagnostics:
  • The paper would benefit from concrete examples of time expressions without time tokens, breakdowns by length/type, and a systematic analysis of POS tagging errors (noted only anecdotally). Sensitivity to POS/tagger quality and to ambiguous tokens would help explain residual errors and guide improvement.

Questions to authors
- Sec 4.1 (types): You list SEASON (5). What are the five seasons? How are entities like Ramadan, Lent, “holiday season,” or domain-specific “seasons” (e.g., “hurricane season,” “football season”) categorized—SEASON vs. HOLIDAY vs. PERIOD? Are they included in the released lexicon?
- Datasets (Sec 5.1): You state “two benchmark datasets,” yet evaluate on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). Please clarify wording and confirm whether all baselines were run with comparable configurations (e.g., HeidelTime/SUTime versions and language settings, UWTime training with TimeBank+Tweets, any tuning).
- Examples: Please provide concrete examples of time expressions without a time token, and report their proportion per dataset; how are these handled by your dependent-segment mechanism?
- Improving recall beyond ~92%: Given that ≥93% of time expressions contain a time token but system recall is ~92%, what are the dominant failure modes (missed/ambiguous tokens, POS errors, boundary decisions)? Which enhancements (expanded lexicon, better POS filtering, inclusion of prepositions, contextual disambiguation) do you expect to help most?
- Resources: Will you release the full rule set, lexicons/regexes (including added keywords per dataset), and code? If not, please include an appendix detailing the rules, POS constraints, and added tokens to ensure reproducibility.
- Ablations and efficiency: Can you report ablations (without POS filtering, without merging heuristics, without expansion) and runtime/memory on the three datasets? This would solidify the claims of simplicity, speed, and the contribution of each component.
