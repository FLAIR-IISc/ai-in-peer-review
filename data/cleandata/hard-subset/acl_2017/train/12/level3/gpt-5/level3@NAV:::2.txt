Summary and contributions
The paper proposes SynTime, a light-weight, rule-based time expression recognizer that groups tokens into syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble mentions. The authors report strong performance on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a Tweets dataset, with especially high recall.

Contributions (as I see them), in order:
1) A simple, interpretable type-based heuristic framework (SynTime) that achieves competitive or superior accuracy to established rule-based and ML systems.
2) An efficiency and engineering contribution: a minimal rule set that runs in real time and is easy to extend via small keyword additions.
3) An empirical analysis highlighting short length, small vocabulary, and syntactic regularities of time expressions that motivate the approach.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime consistently matches or exceeds HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, notably on tweets where the method achieves the best F1 and recall. The results support the claim that the type-based heuristics capture common temporal patterns effectively.
2) Simplicity and interpretability: The approach avoids complex grammars and training, making it easy to analyze, debug, and adapt. This is valuable for practical deployments and for downstream error analysis.
3) Real-time efficiency and low-cost extensibility: The small number of added tokens per domain (e.g., few additions for TimeBank/Tweets) indicates the method is easy to port and maintain, and the rules are transparent.
4) Clear methodological framing: The syntactic-type idea and boundary-expansion strategy are well articulated and intuitive, and they offer a useful counterpoint to more brittle fixed-pattern rules.

Weaknesses
1) Generality and potential overfitting: The rule design, analysis, and expansions were derived from the same corpora used for evaluation (and in Tweets, expansions used training data from the same domain), risking corpus-specific tuning. The paper lacks a held-out, unseen domain to demonstrate robustness.
2) Possible domain bias in rule design: Analysis on the very datasets later used for testing can bias keyword and pattern selection, inflating measured gains.
3) Missing cross-corpus overlap statistics: The claim of a “small, shared vocabulary” is not substantiated by overlap/OOV rates across corpora; without this, generality remains unproven.
4) Insufficient ablations and error analysis: The contribution of POS filtering, boundary merging, and expansions is not isolated; reliance on POS introduces brittleness not quantified.

Questions to authors
- Did any keyword additions or rule refinements use documents that later appeared in evaluation for TimeBank/WikiWars? If so, can you report results on a corpus entirely unseen during rule design?
- Please report cross-corpus token overlap and OOV rates (e.g., proportion of time expressions in each test set covered by tokens learned elsewhere).
- Can you add an evaluation on a distinct domain (e.g., clinical, legal, or narrative fiction) with no prior expansion?
- Provide ablations: without POS filtering; without boundary expansion; without expansions; and timing benchmarks. Will you release rules and code?
