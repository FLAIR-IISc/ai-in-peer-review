Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer built around three syntactic “types” (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors first analyze four corpora to motivate the approach, observing that time expressions are short, rely on a small lexicon, nearly always contain a time token, and display regular syntactic behavior. SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes and optionally expands it with a small set of manually added keywords. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a Twitter dataset show high recall and strong F1, outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on two datasets and being competitive on the third.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, type-based formulation with heuristic boundary-expansion rules for time expression recognition that delivers consistently high recall and competitive F1 across domains.
2) An empirical analysis across multiple corpora that supports a design anchored on time tokens and a small vocabulary.
3) A lightweight, extensible implementation requiring minimal resources and running in real time.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and recall: SynTime attains the best or second-best F1 on most settings, with especially high recall (>92% relaxed across all datasets). On TimeBank (strict F1 92.09 with SynTime-I; 92.47 with SynTime-E) and Tweets (strict F1 ~91.7–91.8), it outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime. This matches the design goal of anchoring on time tokens and expanding boundaries.
- Simplicity and extensibility: The type system and boundary-expansion heuristics avoid brittle fixed-length patterns and make the rules easy to extend. The expansion step adds very few keywords per domain (e.g., 4–16 time tokens and 2–19 modifiers), suggesting good coverage at low maintenance cost.
- Clear motivation via corpus analysis: The observations (short expressions; small lexicon; ≥93% containing time tokens; syntactic regularity) provide a convincing, data-driven rationale for the design. The explicit use of POS mainly for disambiguation (e.g., “May”) is pragmatic.
- Real-time, lightweight system: No training is required; reliance on token regexes and POS makes the approach deployable and interpretable. This fills a practical niche for applications needing fast, transparent time tagging without heavy ML pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and overfitting: It is unclear how SynTime-E’s manual expansion was derived for TimeBank and WikiWars. If the same corpora used for evaluation were also used to select/add keywords, reported gains may be optimistic. The paper should define the training text for expansion and establish a clean separation (e.g., cross-validation or held-out corpora).
- Tweet dataset selection bias: The Twitter corpus was constructed by first running SUTime to detect candidate tweets, then manually annotating only those 942 tweets (plus a small audit of non-detected). This pre-filtering by a baseline risks distorting the distribution of time expressions and underrepresents cases that SUTime systematically misses. A truly random, fully annotated sample is needed for an unbiased evaluation and for substantiating the claimed generality.
- Baseline configuration comparability: Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior work; others may be run out-of-the-box. It is unclear whether HeidelTime/SUTime were tuned on the same domains (especially tweets), or whether UWTime was given comparable resources. Without clear, uniform tuning/evaluation protocols, differences may be partly due to configuration rather than method.
- Limited error analysis and ablations: The paper lacks analyses that would deepen understanding of the approach’s behavior, such as breakdown by expression types (dates, times, durations, ranges), failures on longer/descriptive expressions (noted for WikiWars), and the contribution of each component (POS filtering, left/right expansion, comma/linkage rules). Runtime/throughput numbers are claimed but not reported.
- Claims of generality not fully substantiated: While the corpus analysis is helpful, the paper does not quantify token inventory overlap across datasets or test on a genuinely new, unseen domain that did not influence rules/lexicon. The “principle of least effort” framing is interesting but not validated empirically beyond the four datasets.

Questions to Authors
- Expansion protocol: Precisely which texts were used to derive SynTime-E expansions for TimeBank and WikiWars? If they overlap with test material, can you re-run with expansions learned on disjoint training sets or on a separate corpus?
- Tweets dataset: Can you provide results on a randomly sampled, fully annotated tweet set without SUTime pre-filtering? Alternatively, release the full annotation protocol and inter-annotator agreement for the 4,000-tweet sample to assess selection effects.
- Baseline settings: How were HeidelTime and SUTime configured for each dataset? Were domain-specific resources (e.g., colloquial forms for tweets) enabled? For UWTime, what training data and features were used per dataset?
- Ablations: What is performance without POS-based disambiguation? What is the effect of disabling specific merging heuristics (comma/linkage rules), or using only time-token anchoring (no expansion)?
- Runtime: Please report throughput and latency (e.g., tokens/sec or docs/sec) and memory footprint to substantiate “real time.”
- Lexicon overlap: Can you report overlap statistics (e.g., Jaccard) of time token inventories across datasets and how much expansion improves coverage?

Additional Comments
- Consider an out-of-domain test (e.g., newswire outside TimeBank, clinical notes, forum text) to demonstrate generality and reduce overfitting concerns.
- Provide an error taxonomy and qualitative examples, especially for cases where WikiWars hurts precision/recall.
- Minor: there are some typos and formatting issues in references; clarifying the counts for types (e.g., DECADE “-”) would help reproducibility. If possible, release code and lexicons.
