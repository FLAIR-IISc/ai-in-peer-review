Summary
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based time expression recognizer built around a small set of syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors motivate the design with an analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), observing that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and share syntactic behavior. SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token-level regexes, uses POS tags to disambiguate ambiguous tokens, and then expands from detected time tokens to include nearby modifiers/numerals, merging segments via simple rules. On TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset, SynTime reports strong performance, frequently outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, with especially high recall and notable gains on Tweets. The system is simple, fast, and easy to extend by adding keywords.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical, type-based rule design with minimal, position-aware heuristics that achieves strong performance and high recall across multiple datasets.
- An empirical characterization of time expressions (short length, small vocabulary, syntactic regularity) that justifies the above design and enables a streamlined recognizer.
- A new Tweets dataset and evaluation showing excellent performance in informal text.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and high recall: SynTime-I achieves F1 of 92.09 (strict) on TimeBank and 91.74 on Tweets, outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime in most settings; relaxed recalls exceed 92% across all datasets. The recall aligns with the observation that >93% of time expressions contain time tokens.
- Simplicity and practicality: The system uses a small, interpretable type inventory and a handful of boundary-expansion rules, making it easy to analyze, debug, and adapt. This simplicity is a genuine advantage for many applications that need robust, transparent temporal tagging.
- Sensible error-robust design: Using POS tags only to filter ambiguous time tokens is a measured use of NLP signals that avoids overfitting to brittle features while still reducing false positives (e.g., “May”).
- Lightweight extensibility: The expansion mechanism (adding a small number of tokens/modifiers) improves recall with little annotation cost (e.g., only 3–5 additions for TimeBank/Tweets), which is attractive for new domains.
- Clear articulation of linguistic regularities: The corpus analysis (short spans, small vocabularies, syntactic regularities) is well argued and helps explain why the approach works, especially on informal text like tweets.

Weaknesses
- Generality and potential overfitting: The rules and type inventory are designed and tuned using the same corpora used for evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). This risks domain-specific overfitting, particularly because the type lists and merging heuristics may implicitly reflect corpus-specific conventions (e.g., TimeML treatment of prepositions).
- Domain bias in corpora and annotation policies: WikiWars includes more prepositions/descriptive phrases, while TimeBank excludes many prepositions from spans. SynTime explicitly adheres to TimeML’s narrower span conventions, which may disadvantage baselines on WikiWars or inflate performance where policy matches SynTime’s choices. A broader cross-domain test is needed.
- No analysis of overlap/coverage across corpora: The paper claims the vocabulary is small, but does not quantify token/type overlap between corpora or provide OOV rates. Without these statistics, claims of generality are not well supported.
- Limited ablations and significance testing: There is no ablation isolating the contributions of (i) SUTime-derived token regexes vs. newly added tokens, (ii) the POS filter, and (iii) the boundary-merging heuristics. Statistical significance of the reported gains is not shown.
- Fairness of initialization: SynTime inherits token regexes from SUTime. While reasonable, this could advantage SynTime in recall relative to SUTime if SUTime’s own longer-range rules are less flexible; discussing this design choice and its impact would clarify fairness.

Questions to Authors
- How were the core rules (boundary expansion, merging with commas/linkage) designed and validated? Were they tuned on any of the evaluation corpora? Please specify which corpora were used for developing vs. only testing rules.
- What is the token/type overlap across TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets? Please report OOV rates and performance specifically on OOV expressions to substantiate generality.
- Can you provide a leave-one-corpus-out evaluation (e.g., design/types from TimeBank only, evaluate on WikiWars; or design from TimeBank+WikiWars, evaluate on Tweets without expansion) to demonstrate domain transfer?
- How sensitive is performance to POS tagging errors? An ablation with/no POS filtering (or with a noisy POS tagger) would help quantify robustness.
- Could you report runtime (docs/sec) and memory usage to substantiate the “real time” claim?

Additional Comments
- Please add statistical significance testing (e.g., paired bootstrap) for F1 gains over HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime.
- Include an ablation table: (a) remove POS filtering; (b) remove comma/linkage-specific merge conditions; (c) use SUTime tokens only vs. with expansions; (d) vary search window boundaries.
- Provide an error analysis by type (DATE, TIME, DURATION, etc.) and by span-length to clarify where SynTime excels/fails.
- Consider adding a truly out-of-domain corpus (e.g., clinical, forum, historical) not used in any analysis or expansion to address generality concerns.
- Clarify whether the Tweets dataset and code will be released; this affects reproducibility.
