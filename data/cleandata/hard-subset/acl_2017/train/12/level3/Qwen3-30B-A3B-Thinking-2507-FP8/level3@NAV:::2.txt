

**Review**

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based method for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors make four key observations from analyzing time expressions across four datasets: (1) time expressions are typically very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) a small vocabulary of time-related keywords is used, (3) >93% of expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they define three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and design simple heuristic rules for recognition.

The main contributions are: (1) the syntactic type system for time expressions, which provides a linguistically grounded approach to time recognition; (2) the demonstration that simple rule-based methods can outperform both rule-based and machine learning baselines on benchmark datasets; and (3) the evidence supporting Zipf's principle of least effort in time expression usage.

Strengths include the method's simplicity and real-time performance, which make it suitable for basic applications requiring interpretability. The experimental results are compelling, with SynTime achieving superior F1 scores (92.09% strict match on TimeBank) compared to state-of-the-art baselines. The authors also provide a clear justification for their rule design approach.

The primary weakness is the evaluation methodology, which represents a significant concern. The rules were designed using the same corpora as the evaluation data, creating a high risk of overfitting and undermining claims of generalizability. The paper lacks any statistics on overlap between training and evaluation corpora, and the evaluation datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) may have biased rule design due to domain similarities. Without testing on a completely new, distinct corpus, the claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art methods" lacks robustness.

Additional weaknesses include insufficient discussion of why the method works better than previous rule-based approaches, and the lack of analysis regarding how the method handles time expressions containing words not in the predefined lexicon. The paper also does not address how well the method handles time expressions in non-English languages.

The authors should address these concerns by testing SynTime on a new, independent corpus not used in rule development, and providing statistics on corpus overlap to justify generalizability. Without this, the paper's contribution is limited to a specific domain and dataset.
