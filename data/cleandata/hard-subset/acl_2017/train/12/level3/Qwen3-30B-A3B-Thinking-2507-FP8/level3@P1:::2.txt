

Review: The paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic types and heuristic rules. The core contribution lies in the analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), revealing four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically very short (80% ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small, constrained vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) ≥93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, the authors propose a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion. The main contributions are: (1) the linguistic analysis demonstrating time expressions adhere to Zipf's principle of least effort, (2) the syntactic type system that redefines rule-based tagging through syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning, and (3) SynTime's empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods.  

Strengths:  
1. **Insightful linguistic analysis**: The analysis of time expressions across diverse domains (news, Wikipedia, tweets) provides compelling evidence for Zipf's principle, which is both theoretically grounded and practically relevant. The observation that 93%+ of expressions contain time tokens (Table 2) directly justifies the core recognition strategy.  
2. **Simplicity and efficiency**: SynTime’s lightweight design (no training, real-time execution) and expandability via keyword addition (Table 5) address critical pain points in production systems. The method’s simplicity is a significant strength for interpretability and deployment.  
3. **Strong empirical results**: SynTime outperforms all baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets (Table 4), achieving 91.74% F1 (strict) on Tweets—a 11.37% improvement over SUTime. The high recall (95.87% relaxed) aligns with the paper’s key observation about time token prevalence.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Evaluation methodology flaw**: The rules were derived from SUTime (used as a source for token regular expressions) and tested on the same corpora (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) used for analysis. This risks overfitting, as the "time tokens" in the rule set (e.g., from SUTime) likely overlap with the evaluation datasets. The paper does not justify why the rule design isn’t biased toward the evaluation corpora.  
2. **Lack of corpus diversity**: The evaluation uses three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) that are all news-related or social media, with no cross-domain testing on, e.g., medical or legal texts. The claim of "domain independence" is unsupported without testing on a distinct domain.  
3. **No corpus overlap analysis**: The paper fails to report overlap between training and test sets (e.g., time expressions shared between TimeBank and WikiWars) or provide statistics on how much the rules generalize beyond the analyzed corpora. Without this, the generality claim is unsubstantiated.  
4. **Domain-specific limitations**: SynTime struggles with descriptive time expressions (e.g., "the time Arnold reached Quebec City" in WikiWars), as it follows TimeML’s strict definition. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t quantify the impact or propose solutions.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. How was the rule set derived from SUTime to ensure it doesn’t implicitly incorporate evaluation data? Could you provide statistics on the overlap between SUTime’s token rules and the evaluation corpora?  
2. Have you tested SynTime on a corpus *unrelated* to the four analyzed datasets (e.g., biomedical texts)? If not, why not?  
3. The paper states "even in tweets people tend to use formal words" (Section 4.3), yet Tweets are used for evaluation. How do you reconcile this with the claim that SynTime handles informal language?  

Overall, SynTime is a well-motivated, efficient approach with strong empirical results. However, the evaluation methodology’s potential overfitting and lack of cross-domain validation significantly undermine the generality claims. Addressing these issues would strengthen the paper substantially. The core linguistic insights are valuable, but the method’s real-world applicability hinges on demonstrating robustness beyond the evaluation datasets.
