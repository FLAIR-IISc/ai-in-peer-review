- Paper proposes SynTime, a simple rule-based system using three syntactic types to recognize time expressions, claiming state-of-the-art rule-based performance.
- Analysis across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets shows time expressions are short, use a small keyword set, usually contain a time token, and have similar syntactic behavior.
- Contribution: a syntactic type–based, Zipf-inspired approach demonstrating that simple rules can be highly effective for time expression recognition.
- Contribution: cross-domain analysis revealing consistent linguistic patterns in time expressions that support the principle of least effort.
- Contribution: a lightweight, real-time, easily extensible rule-based system as a practical alternative to complex ML models.
- Strength: strong empirical results — highest F1 on TimeBank and Tweets with particularly high recall.
- Strength: method is simple, easy to implement and maintain, and can be expanded by adding keywords.
- Strength: provides insightful linguistic analysis that could inform future temporal recognition work.
- Weakness: rules were designed using the same corpora as evaluation, creating a high risk of overfitting and undermining generality claims.
- Weakness: evaluation datasets may share domain biases; paper lacks discussion of representativeness across broader language use.
- Weakness: no statistics on dataset overlap or discussion of how much rule modification is needed for new domains, so “wide effectiveness” is unsupported.
- Weakness: lacks ablation studies to show which syntactic types or rules drive performance improvements.
- Question: provide overlap statistics of time expressions across the four datasets to assess overfitting risk.
- Question: have you tested SynTime on an entirely new dataset not used in rule design, and if not, what performance do you expect?
- Question: how would SynTime be adapted for languages other than English given claims about cross-linguistic applicability?
- Question: what limitations do you foresee when applying SynTime to domain-specific texts (e.g., medical or legal) with specialized time expressions?
