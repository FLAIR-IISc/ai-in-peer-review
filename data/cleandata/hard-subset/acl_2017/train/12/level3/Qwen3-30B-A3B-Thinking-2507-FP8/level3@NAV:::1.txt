

**Review**

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression extraction that leverages four key empirical observations: time expressions are typically short (80% ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary, contain at least one time token (93%+), and exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. The method identifies time tokens via dictionary lookup, regex, and POS tagging, then expands time segments bidirectionally using heuristic rules before merging segments into complete expressions.

**Main Contributions:**  
1. The empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets provides compelling evidence for the principle of least effort in language, revealing fundamental properties that underpin the proposed method.  
2. SynTime's simple, rule-based approach with syntactic type definitions (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) achieves state-of-the-art results on three datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over both rule-based and ML baselines.  
3. The method's light-weight nature enables real-time performance and easy domain adaptation through simple rule expansion.

**Strengths:**  
1. The empirical observations are thoroughly validated across multiple datasets and provide strong justification for the method's design.  
2. Evaluation results are impressive, with SynTime achieving 95.87% relaxed match F1 on Tweets (6.33% improvement over best baseline) and consistently high recall (≥92%) across all datasets.  
3. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.  
4. The simplicity and real-time capability of SynTime offer practical advantages over heavier ML-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. Critical implementation details are underspecified, hindering reproducibility (e.g., exact rule sets, POS tagging thresholds).  
2. The paper claims "two benchmark datasets" but evaluates on three (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), creating confusion.  
3. The paper fails to address how holiday seasons (e.g., Ramadan) or "5 seasons" (Sec 4.1) are handled, limiting real-world applicability.  
4. No example is provided for time expressions without time tokens despite claiming 93%+ contain them.  
5. The authors don't explain how to improve performance further given the high recall (92%) and the fact that 93% of expressions contain time tokens.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. Will the full rule set and software be released?  
2. Please provide an example of a time expression without a time token.  
3. How are holiday-specific expressions like "Ramadan 2023" handled in your system?
