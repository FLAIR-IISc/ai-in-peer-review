- Paper analyzes time expressions across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets and identifies four core observations about their form and usage.
- Observation: 80% of time expressions are very short (≤3 tokens).
- Observation: Time expressions use a small, consistent vocabulary of time-related keywords.
- Observation: Over 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token.
- Observation: Words in time expressions show similar syntactic behaviors across domains.
- Proposal: SynTime, a rule-based recognizer defining syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and using heuristics to build and merge time segments.
- Contribution: Empirical support for the principle of least effort in time expression usage across multiple domains.
- Contribution: Introduction of a syntactic-type system for time expression recognition distinct from semantic rule sets.
- Contribution: Strong performance on benchmarks with high recall (over 92% on all datasets), notably excellent on Tweets.
- Strength: Comprehensive cross-domain empirical analysis revealing overlooked linguistic patterns.
- Strength: SynTime is simple, efficient, real-time, and easily extensible without retraining.
- Strength: High recall validates the observation that most time expressions contain time tokens, even in informal text.
- Weakness: Potential overfitting from developing rules on the same corpora used for evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars).
- Weakness: Lack of statistics on overlap between rule-development expressions and evaluation datasets undermines generalizability claims.
- Weakness: Insufficient evidence supporting the claim that tweets use formal time words and how this aids performance.
- Questions for authors: Provide overlap statistics between development and evaluation datasets.
- Questions for authors: Test SynTime on a distinct corpus (e.g., medical or legal) not used in rule development.
- Questions for authors: Elaborate evidence that tweets use formal time words and how this affects method effectiveness.
- Recommendation: Minor revision—address overfitting/generalizability concerns by testing on a new corpus and reporting dataset overlap.
