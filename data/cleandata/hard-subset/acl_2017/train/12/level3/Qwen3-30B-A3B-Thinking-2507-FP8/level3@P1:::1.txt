

This paper presents a rule-based approach to time expression recognition called SynTime, which is motivated by empirical observations about time expressions across four datasets. The authors analyze time expressions in TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets, revealing four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words within time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. These observations provide evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage. Based on these insights, the authors propose SynTime, which defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and employs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) a thorough empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets that provides evidence for linguistic principles, (2) the SynTime system that uses syntactic types and heuristic rules for time expression recognition, and (3) comprehensive evaluation showing SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and tweets.

The paper's strengths are significant. First, the empirical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by data across four diverse datasets, providing strong motivation for the proposed approach. The observations about time expressions being short, using limited vocabulary, containing time tokens, and exhibiting similar syntactic behaviors are compelling and directly inform the system design. Second, the SynTime system is elegantly designed with simple heuristic rules that expand time segments bidirectionally from time tokens, making it lightweight and real-time applicable. The evaluation results are impressive, with SynTime achieving 91.74% F1 in strict match and 95.87% in relaxed match on the Tweets dataset, showing 11.37% and 6.33% improvements over the best baseline, respectively. Third, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making the rationale and methodology accessible to readers.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the paper claims to use "two benchmark datasets" (TimeBank and WikiWars) but actually evaluates on three datasets including the manually annotated Tweets dataset. This discrepancy needs correction as it misrepresents the experimental setup. Second, the implementation details are underspecified, hindering reproducibility. For instance, Section 4.1 mentions "5 seasons" without clarifying what they are or how culturally-specific expressions like Ramadan are handled. Third, the paper claims 93% of expressions contain time tokens but reports 92% recall, raising questions about the remaining expressions. The authors should provide an example of a time expression without a time token to clarify this gap.

The evaluation is strong overall, with SynTime achieving the best results on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, and comparable results on WikiWars. The high recall across all datasets (above 92% in relaxed match) aligns with the observation that 93%+ of expressions contain time tokens. The expansion of SynTime (SynTime-E) shows modest improvements on WikiWars, confirming that adding domain-specific tokens is effective but not necessary for most domains.

I have several questions for the authors: What are the "5 seasons" referenced in Section 4.1, and how are culturally-specific time expressions handled? Please clarify the discrepancy between claiming "two benchmark datasets" and using three. Provide an example of a time expression that doesn't contain a time token, as claimed. Given the 92% recall with 93% of expressions containing time tokens, what are the main challenges in improving performance further? Finally, will the full set of rules and software be released?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to time expression recognition by providing a simple yet effective rule-based approach grounded in empirical analysis. The system's lightweight nature and real-time performance make it particularly appealing for practical applications. The empirical analysis itself is a significant contribution that could inform future work in temporal information extraction.

Overall, this paper presents a well-motivated, clearly articulated, and effective approach to time expression recognition. With minor clarifications to the methodology and experimental reporting, this work would be a strong candidate for acceptance. The empirical observations provide valuable insights into language usage, and the SynTime system demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The main issue is the lack of implementation details for reproducibility, which should be addressed in the camera-ready version.
