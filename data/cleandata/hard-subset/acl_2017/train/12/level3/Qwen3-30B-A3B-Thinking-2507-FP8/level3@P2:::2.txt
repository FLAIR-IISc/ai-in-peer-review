

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
The paper analyzes time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) over 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions. SynTime first identifies time tokens, then searches for modifiers/numerals to form time segments, and finally merges these segments into complete time expressions.

## Main Contributions
1. The paper provides empirical evidence supporting the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949) in time expression usage across multiple domains, demonstrating that time expressions are short, use minimal vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic patterns.
2. The authors introduce a novel syntactic type system for time expression recognition, which differs fundamentally from existing rule-based approaches by defining types based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning.
3. SynTime demonstrates strong performance on benchmark datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results with particularly high recall rates (over 92% on all datasets), including exceptional performance on the Tweets dataset.

## Strengths
1. The empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets is comprehensive and provides valuable insights into linguistic patterns that are often overlooked in time expression research. The observations about short length, small vocabulary, and similar syntactic behavior across diverse domains (news articles, Wikipedia, tweets) provide strong evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage.
2. The simplicity and efficiency of SynTime are significant strengths. The method is lightweight, runs in real time, and can be easily expanded by adding new keywords without retraining. This makes it particularly suitable for resource-constrained applications and domain adaptation.
3. The high recall rates achieved by SynTime (95.65% on TimeBank and 98.78% on Tweets in relaxed match) directly support the authors' key observation that most time expressions contain time tokens. This high recall is especially impressive given the informal nature of the Tweets dataset.

## Weaknesses
1. The most significant weakness is the potential for overfitting due to using the same corpora for both rule development and evaluation. The authors developed their rules based on TimeBank and WikiWars, which were also used for evaluation, making it difficult to assess the method's true generalizability to new domains.
2. The paper lacks statistics on the overlap between the time expressions used in rule development and those in the evaluation datasets. Without this information, it's unclear whether the high performance is due to the method's generalizability or simply because the rules were tuned to the evaluation data.
3. The paper's claim that "people tend to use formal words even in tweets" lacks sufficient evidence. While the authors reference a Twitter word cluster analysis, they don't provide enough detail to support how this finding translates to better performance on tweets, which would strengthen the argument for the method's robustness across informal text.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide statistics on the overlap between time expressions in the rule development datasets and the evaluation datasets? This would help determine if the high performance is due to generalization or overfitting.
2. Could you test SynTime on a new, distinct corpus (e.g., medical or legal documents) that was not used in rule development to better demonstrate the method's generalizability?
3. The paper mentions that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words" but does not provide sufficient evidence for this claim. Could you elaborate on the specific evidence supporting this assertion and how it contributes to the method's effectiveness on tweets?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting empirical analysis of time expressions and a novel rule-based approach that achieves strong results. However, the potential overfitting issue and lack of generalizability testing are significant concerns that need to be addressed. I recommend minor revision to include a new test on a distinct corpus and provide more detailed analysis of dataset overlap.
