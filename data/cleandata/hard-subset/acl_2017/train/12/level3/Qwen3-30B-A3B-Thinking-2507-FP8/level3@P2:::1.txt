

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically short (80% have ≤3 tokens), (2) the vocabulary used to express time is very small, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words within time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based approach that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.

The primary contributions as I see them are:
1. The empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets that reveals patterns consistent with Zipf's principle of least effort, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
2. A syntactic type system for time expressions that groups tokens based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning, offering a new perspective on time expression recognition.
3. A lightweight rule-based approach that achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets while being easily expandable through simple keyword additions.

## Strengths

1. The empirical analysis of time expressions is thorough, well-supported by data across multiple datasets, and provides valuable insights into language usage patterns. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is compelling and well-justified by the observed patterns (short expressions, small vocabulary, high time token occurrence, similar syntactic behavior).

2. The proposed method is simple yet effective, with clear evidence of its advantages over state-of-the-art approaches. SynTime achieves significant improvements over baselines on TimeBank (92.09% F1 vs. 83.10% for UWTime) and especially on Tweets (91.74% F1 vs. 79.44% for UWTime), with the authors correctly attributing this to the simplicity and formality of time expressions in informal contexts.

3. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and match types (strict/relaxed), and the authors provide appropriate analysis of the results in the context of their observations.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the system. While the authors claim "simple heuristic rules," they provide minimal detail about the actual rule set, the criteria for rule design, or the specific conditions under which rules are applied. For example, the paper states "SynTime can be easily expanded by simply adding keywords" but doesn't explain how the expansion process is implemented or what constitutes a valid keyword addition.

2. The paper presents an interesting discrepancy between their observation that 93% of time expressions contain a time token and the best recall achieved (94.49% on Tweets). This gap needs explanation - why do they achieve higher recall than the observed time token occurrence rate? The authors should clarify whether their analysis included time expressions without time tokens, or if the 93% figure is an underestimate.

3. The paper makes strong claims about the superiority of their approach but lacks sufficient detail to understand *why* their method outperforms others. For instance, they state that SUTime cannot recognize "year 1" but not "1 year," but they don't provide a systematic comparison of rule coverage between approaches. A more detailed analysis of the rule sets would strengthen their claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. Section 4.1 mentions "5 seasons" (Table 1) but doesn't specify which seasons. How are holidays like Ramadan or seasonal events like "the holiday season" handled in the system? Please clarify.

2. Section 5.1 states they use "two benchmark datasets" but evaluates on three (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets). Please clarify this discrepancy.

3. The paper claims 93% of time expressions contain a time token, yet achieves 94.49% recall on Tweets. Could you provide an example of a time expression without a time token to illustrate the remaining 7% of expressions?

4. Given that 93% of expressions contain a time token and the system achieves 92% recall, what are the main challenges preventing perfect recall (100%)?

5. Will the complete rule set and implementation be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and future work?
