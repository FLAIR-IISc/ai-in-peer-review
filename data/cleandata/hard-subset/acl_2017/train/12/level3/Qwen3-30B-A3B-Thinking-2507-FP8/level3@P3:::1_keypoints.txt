- Presents empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets, identifying four observations supporting the principle of least effort.  
- Proposes SynTime, a syntactic-type-based time expression recognizer using simple heuristic boundary-expansion rules.  
- Shows SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based and ML baselines, especially on Tweets (91.74% F1).  
- Empirical findings: time expressions are short (80% ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary, contain time tokens in 93%+ cases, and show consistent syntactic behavior.  
- SynTime’s design is simple, lightweight, real-time, and easily extended via domain keywords by leveraging syntactic types instead of semantic types.  
- Evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime using strict and relaxed matching across three datasets.  
- Lacks sufficient implementation details (regexes, exact heuristic rules, POS integration), hindering reproducibility.  
- Misleading dataset description: paper claims "two benchmark datasets" but evaluates on three, creating confusion.  
- Does not reconcile the 93% time-token statistic with achieved 92% recall or explain paths to exceed that ceiling.  
- Fails to provide an example of a time expression without a time token as requested in the paper.  
- Inconsistent terminology: mentions "5 seasons" without definition or handling details, raising cultural/coverage concerns (e.g., Ramadan, holidays).  
- Asks authors to clarify what the "5 seasons" are and how culturally specific time expressions are handled.  
- Requests clarification on the dataset count discrepancy (two vs. three datasets used).  
- Requests an example of a time expression lacking a time token.  
- Asks how performance can be improved beyond the apparent time-token coverage limit.  
- Requests release of full rules and software to enable reproduction and extension.  
- Overall: strong, elegant contribution with solid empirical and experimental support but requires missing methodological details and clarifications for reproducibility and acceptance.
