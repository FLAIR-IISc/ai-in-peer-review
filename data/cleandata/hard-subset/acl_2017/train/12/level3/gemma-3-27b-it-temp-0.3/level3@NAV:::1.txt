## Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based approach to time expression recognition motivated by observations about the characteristics of time expressions in natural language – namely their brevity, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. The core contribution lies in a type-based rule system that leverages syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and heuristic rules for boundary expansion, offering a lightweight and easily expandable alternative to existing methods. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over baseline systems (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets).

The strengths of this submission are considerable. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making the methodology easy to follow. The rules are convincingly motivated by empirical analysis of the datasets, and the experimental results strongly support the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly on the challenging Twitter dataset. The emphasis on a lightweight, expandable system is a practical advantage. The analysis of time expressions aligning with Zipf’s principle of least effort provides a compelling theoretical underpinning.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of detail hindering reproducibility. While the overall approach is clear, the specific rules themselves are not fully presented, making it difficult to assess their complexity and generalizability. The description of the expansion process could also benefit from more concrete examples. 

I have several questions for the authors:

1. **(Sec 4.1):** The paper mentions "5 seasons." Could you clarify what these seasons are and how the system handles culturally specific time periods like Ramadan or other holiday seasons that don't neatly fit into these categories?
2. **(Sec 5.1):** The text states evaluation was performed on "two benchmark datasets" but the paper utilizes three. Please clarify this discrepancy.
3. **(Sec 5.2):** Could you provide an example of a time expression that *doesn't* contain a time token, to better understand the system’s handling of such cases?
4. **(Sec 5.2):** Given that 93% of expressions contain a time token and the system achieves 92% recall, what avenues are being explored to further improve performance, particularly for the remaining 7% of expressions?
5. Finally, will the full set of rules and/or the software implementation of SynTime be released to the research community to facilitate further research and validation?
