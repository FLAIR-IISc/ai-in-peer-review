## Review of “Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules”

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The authors motivate their work by analyzing time expression characteristics across four datasets, observing short expression lengths, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. They leverage these observations to define a type-based system (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple heuristic rules for recognition, claiming improved performance over state-of-the-art methods. The primary contribution lies in the simplification of rule-based time expression tagging through a syntactic typing approach, offering a potentially more flexible and expandable system. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of SynTime’s effectiveness on benchmark datasets and tweets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates that SynTime outperforms existing rule-based and machine learning methods on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. The consistently high recall scores are particularly noteworthy.
*   **Lightweight and Expandable Design:** The simplicity of the rule-based approach and the type-based design make SynTime computationally efficient and easily adaptable to new domains by simply adding keywords. This is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The initial analysis of time expression characteristics provides a solid foundation for the proposed method and highlights interesting linguistic patterns. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is a compelling justification for the approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of illustrative examples aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overfitting Concerns:** The most significant concern is the potential for overfitting. The rules were designed and evaluated using the same corpora, raising doubts about the generalizability of the results. The authors acknowledge this, but the evaluation doesn't sufficiently address it.
*   **Limited Corpus Diversity:** While four datasets are used, their domains are relatively similar (news, Wikipedia, tweets). The lack of evaluation on a truly distinct corpus (e.g., legal documents, medical reports) limits confidence in the method’s robustness.
*   **Lack of Inter-Corpus Analysis:** The paper doesn’t provide statistics on the overlap of time expressions *across* the different corpora. Demonstrating that the observed patterns are consistent across diverse texts would strengthen the argument for generality.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* SynTime fails could reveal areas for improvement and provide insights into the limitations of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide statistics on the overlap of time expressions across the four datasets used in your analysis?
2.  Have you tested SynTime on a corpus significantly different in domain from TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets (e.g., legal, medical, or scientific text)? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the types of errors SynTime makes and potential reasons for those errors?
