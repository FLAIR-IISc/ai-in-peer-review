## Review for ACL 2017 - Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

This paper presents SynTime, a novel rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The core idea is to leverage observed characteristics of time expressions – their brevity, limited vocabulary, and reliance on “time tokens” – to create a simplified and efficient system. The authors motivate their approach with an analysis of four datasets and demonstrate strong performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, including a challenging Twitter dataset.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a carefully designed, type-based rule-based system can achieve competitive and, in some cases, superior performance to more complex methods in the task of time expression recognition. This is achieved through a combination of insightful empirical analysis of time expression characteristics and a clever simplification of rule design via syntactic typing and heuristic expansion. 

A secondary contribution lies in the empirical analysis of time expressions across four diverse datasets. The identified observations – short length, small vocabulary, high time token presence, and consistent syntactic behavior – provide a strong foundation for the proposed system and offer valuable insights into the nature of time expression in natural language.

Finally, the paper presents a practical and efficient implementation of time expression recognition that runs in real-time, making it suitable for applications demanding low latency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Motivation:** The paper is grounded in a thorough analysis of time expression data. The observations about length, vocabulary size, and the prevalence of time tokens convincingly motivate the design choices of SynTime. This grounding significantly strengthens the plausibility and appeal of the proposed approach.

2. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents the core ideas, methodology, and results. The system architecture is illustrated effectively with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The explanations of the rules and their design are straightforward and easy to follow.

3. **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that SynTime achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on three datasets, significantly outperforming baselines on the Twitter dataset. The consistent high recall scores across all datasets are particularly noteworthy, indicating robust identification of time expressions.

4. **Efficiency and Practicality:** The emphasis on real-time performance and ease of expansion are valuable practical considerations. The authors clearly articulate the advantages of their lightweight rule-based approach over more computationally expensive machine learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Reproducibility:** While the paper provides a high-level description of the system, important details are missing that would be required to reproduce the results. Specifically, the exact regular expressions used for the types, the full set of heuristic rules, and the precise details of the POS tagging configuration are not provided. The dependency on SUTime’s rules is mentioned, but the specific subset used is not fully detailed.

2. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance metrics, but lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes (e.g., false positives, missed expressions) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.

3. **Overgeneralization of Principle of Least Effort:** While the connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is interesting, the paper leans heavily on it as a justification for the observed characteristics of time expressions. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging other contributing factors would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Type Definitions:** In Section 4.1, the description of the syntactic types mentions “5 seasons.” Could you elaborate on what these five seasons are? How are cultural or regional variations in seasonal definitions (e.g., Ramadan, Chinese New Year) handled?

2. **Time Token Absence:** The paper states that >93% of time expressions contain at least one time token. Could you provide a concrete example of a time expression that *does not* contain a time token, as identified by SynTime?

3. **Recall Improvement:** Given the already high recall scores (often >92%), what potential avenues exist for further improving the system’s performance, particularly in addressing the remaining missed time expressions?

4. **Code Availability:** Will the code and the complete set of rules for SynTime be released publicly to facilitate reproducibility and further research?



**Additional Comments:**

*   In Section 5.1, the paper refers to "two benchmark datasets" but proceeds to evaluate on three (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets). This should be clarified.
*   The references are numerous and comprehensive, demonstrating a good understanding of the relevant literature.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to time expression recognition. The combination of insightful analysis, clear presentation, and competitive performance makes it a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses regarding reproducibility and error analysis would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and comments raised above.
