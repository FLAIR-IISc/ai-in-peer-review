- SynTime: a type-based, rule-based method for time expression recognition using three syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and heuristic boundary expansion.
- Key observation: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, often contain time tokens, and show consistent syntactic behavior.
- Primary contribution: simplified type-based rules that balance precision and recall, outperforming existing rule- and ML-based systems on TimeBank and Tweets.
- Empirical result: SynTime matches performance on WikiWars and notably improves recall on the challenging Twitter dataset.
- Practical advantage: real-time processing speed makes SynTime suitable for rapid time expression identification.
- Dataset analysis: cross-corpus study (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) showing limited vocabulary, short lengths, and prevalence of time tokens that motivate design choices.
- Strong evaluation: comparisons against established baselines across multiple datasets support SynTime’s effectiveness.
- Main weakness: risk of overfitting because rules were designed using the same datasets used for evaluation, threatening generalizability.
- Missing analysis: no quantitative discussion of overlap and differences in time expressions across the corpora to justify cross-dataset claims.
- Preprocessing reliance: dependence on external tokenization and Stanford POS Tagger without sensitivity analysis to tagging/tokenization errors.
- Lack of detail on how POS/tagging accuracy impacts SynTime’s results or exploration of alternative preprocessing tools.
- Runtime evaluation is insufficiently detailed—no per-sentence processing time or scalability analysis presented.
- Clarification needed: more concrete examples and details of the “boundary expansion” heuristic.
- Suggestion: discuss limitations of rule-based approaches versus ML methods, especially for adaptation to new domains or languages.
- Questions for authors: report results on an independent dataset not used in rule design, provide quantitative overlap analysis, evaluate sensitivity to preprocessing errors, and supply detailed runtime/scalability metrics.
