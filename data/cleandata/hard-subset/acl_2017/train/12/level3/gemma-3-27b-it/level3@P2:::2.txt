## Review for ACL 2017 - Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

This paper presents SynTime, a novel rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The authors motivate their work through an analysis of four datasets, observing that time expressions are generally short, utilize a limited vocabulary, frequently contain “time tokens,” and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three syntactic types – time token, modifier, and numeral – and employs simple heuristic rules for recognition. The paper reports strong empirical results, demonstrating that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and a Twitter corpus.

The primary contribution of this work is the *type-based simplification of rule-based time expression recognition*. While rule-based approaches are well-established, SynTime distinguishes itself by abstracting from semantic definitions of time expression components to a syntactic categorization. This, combined with the heuristic boundary expansion method, results in a surprisingly effective and lightweight system. A secondary contribution is the *analytical study of time expressions across multiple datasets*, providing empirical support for the claim that time expressions adhere to the principle of least effort, as evidenced by their brevity and reliance on a small set of common terms. Finally, the paper offers a *strong empirical evaluation* demonstrating the effectiveness of SynTime against competitive baselines, including both rule-based and machine learning approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. SynTime demonstrably outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable performance on WikiWars. The reported improvements in both precision and recall (particularly the high recall scores) are significant and indicative of a robust system.
2. **Lightweight and Efficient Design:** The authors emphasize the real-time performance of SynTime, a key benefit for practical applications. The simplicity of the rules and the type-based approach contribute to this efficiency, making the system easily expandable and adaptable to different domains.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The initial analysis of time expression characteristics is well-motivated and directly informs the design of SynTime. The observation that time expressions adhere to the principle of least effort provides a plausible explanation for the observed patterns and strengthens the rationale for the proposed approach.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The overview of the SynTime architecture (Figure 2) is particularly helpful in understanding the system’s operation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Potential for Overfitting and Limited Generalizability:** A critical concern is the potential for overfitting. The authors acknowledge using SUTime to initially collect token regular expressions and that the evaluation corpora may have influenced rule design. However, the paper lacks a discussion on the overlap of time expressions *across* the datasets used for analysis, rule creation, and evaluation. This makes it difficult to assess the true generalizability of the system. Simply stating that the rules are easily expandable doesn’t address the underlying risk of designing rules that are too specific to the training data.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance metrics, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement. For example, are errors concentrated in specific types of time expressions, or are they evenly distributed?
3. **Reliance on Pre-processing:** SynTime’s performance relies heavily on accurate POS tagging. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but don’t sufficiently discuss the impact of POS tagging errors on the overall results. A sensitivity analysis exploring the effect of POS tagging accuracy would be beneficial.
4. **Incremental Benefit of Expansion:** The experimental results (Table 5) show a relatively small number of additional tokens added during the expansion phase, and the corresponding performance gains appear modest. The paper could better justify the effort involved in manual expansion by demonstrating a more substantial performance improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could the authors provide statistics on the overlap of time expressions (or their components) across the TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets datasets? This would help to assess the extent to which the rules are tailored to specific corpora.
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the types of errors SynTime commonly makes?
3.  Have you investigated the sensitivity of SynTime’s performance to POS tagging errors? If so, what were the results?
4.  What is the expected time investment to expand SynTime to a new domain, and what level of performance improvement can be reasonably expected?



**Additional Comments:**

- The reference list appears incomplete. For example, the citation for Alonso et al. (2011) is missing the full reference details.
- It would be helpful to compare SynTime’s performance to other recent, state-of-the-art time expression recognition systems, beyond those explicitly mentioned in the paper.
- The use of "type" could be clarified, as it's used in both a syntactic (as defined in the paper) and a broader sense (e.g., "type of dataset").



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of temporal information extraction. The proposed approach is novel, effective, and well-motivated. Addressing the concerns regarding generalizability and providing a more detailed analysis of the system’s limitations would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
