- Paper proposes SynTime: a rule-based time-expression recognizer using syntactic types and simple heuristics, claiming strong empirical performance.
- Main contribution: surprisingly strong empirical results from a simple rule-based system (especially on Twitter); the analysis mainly serves as motivation rather than a standalone contribution.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results: outperforms existing rule-based and ML methods on several datasets, notably Twitter.
- Simplicity & explainability: clear type definitions and rules make the system understandable and easy to modify.
- Efficiency: demonstrated real-time performance suitable for low-latency applications.
- Effective type-based approach: syntactic-type design (inspired by POS tagging) offers a structured, intuitive framework for rules.

Weaknesses:
- Overfitting concerns: rules were designed and tested on the same corpora, raising generalization doubts.
- Limited corpus diversity: chosen benchmarks may bias results; lack of analysis on cross-corpus pattern differences.
- Lack of external validation: no evaluation on an independent corpus not used during rule development.
- Limited error analysis: insufficient in-depth examination of where SynTime fails and why.

Questions for authors:
- Provide a detailed breakdown of the rule design process and how biases from training corpora were mitigated.
- What steps and challenges are involved in adapting SynTime to another language?
- Can you provide statistics on overlap (or lack thereof) of time-expression patterns across TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets?
- What is the computational cost of expanding SynTime with new keywords/rules and how does that affect real-time performance?
