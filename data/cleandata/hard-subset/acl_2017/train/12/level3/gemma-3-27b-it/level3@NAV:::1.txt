## Review of “Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules”

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based approach to time expression recognition motivated by empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets. The core insight is that time expressions are typically short and contain at least one identifiable “time token.” SynTime leverages this observation by defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and applying heuristic rules for expansion and merging to identify complete time expressions. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over rule-based and machine learning baselines on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets).

The primary contribution of this work lies in the well-motivated and effective simplification of rule-based time expression recognition. By focusing on syntactic types and employing a boundary expansion heuristic, the authors present a light-weight and surprisingly accurate system. The clear presentation of the method and the positive empirical results supporting their observations are also strong points. A second contribution is the insightful analysis of time expression characteristics, highlighting the prevalence of short expressions containing identifiable time tokens, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.

However, several weaknesses hinder reproducibility. While the paper describes the methodology clearly in principle, the specific details of the rules and the implementation are underspecified. Providing access to the full rule set and/or the software would significantly strengthen the work. 

Specifically, I have the following questions for the authors:

* **(Sec 4.1):** The paper mentions “5 seasons.” Could you clarify which seasons are included, and how the system handles culturally specific time periods like Ramadan or holiday seasons that don’t neatly fit into a standard seasonal framework?
* **(Sec 5.1):** The text states evaluation on “two benchmark datasets” while the experiments utilize three datasets. Please clarify this discrepancy.
* **(Sec 5.2):** Could you provide an example of a time expression that *doesn’t* contain a time token, and explain how SynTime handles such cases?
* **(Sec 5.2):** Given that 93% of expressions contain a time token and SynTime achieves 92% recall, what avenues for further performance improvement have been considered or are planned? 
* Will the full set of rules and/or the software implementation of SynTime be made publicly available? This would greatly facilitate further research and validation of the findings.

Overall, this is a promising work with a clearly articulated approach and strong empirical results. Addressing the reproducibility concerns and clarifying the aforementioned points would substantially enhance the contribution of this paper.
