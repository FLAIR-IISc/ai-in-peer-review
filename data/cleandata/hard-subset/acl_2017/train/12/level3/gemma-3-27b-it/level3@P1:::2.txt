This paper presents SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach for time expression recognition. The authors observe that time expressions are often short, utilize a small vocabulary, frequently contain time tokens, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Building on these observations, SynTime defines three syntactic types – time token, modifier, and numeral – and employs simple heuristic rules for recognition, focusing on boundary expansion. The experimental results demonstrate that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art rule-based (HeidelTime, SUTime) and machine learning-based (UWTime) methods on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, achieving comparable performance on WikiWars.

The primary contribution of this work is the simplified, type-based approach to rule-based time expression recognition.  While rule-based systems are known for their high precision, they often suffer from limited recall due to difficulty in anticipating all possible expressions. By framing the problem in terms of syntactic types and utilizing heuristic boundary expansion, SynTime achieves a favorable balance between precision and recall, exceeding the performance of existing systems, particularly on the challenging Twitter dataset. This simplified approach, coupled with the real-time processing speed, makes SynTime a practical solution for applications requiring rapid time expression identification.

A second noteworthy contribution is the insightful analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The authors demonstrate a clear pattern – the limited vocabulary, short length, and prevalence of time tokens – which validates the design choices within SynTime. This analytical grounding provides a strong rationale for the approach and could prove valuable for future research in this area.

Finally, the experimental evaluation is a positive aspect of the submission. The use of multiple datasets and comparison against established baselines provides a solid foundation for assessing the effectiveness of SynTime. The consistent outperformance of existing methods, especially in recall, is a significant achievement.

However, there are several weaknesses that need to be addressed. The most critical concern is the potential for overfitting. The authors designed the rules using information extracted from the datasets used for evaluation (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets). This introduces a risk that the rules are specifically tuned to these datasets and may not generalize well to unseen data, especially from different domains. 

The paper lacks a discussion of the overlap of time expressions across the used corpora. A demonstration of the commonalities and differences in how time is expressed in these datasets would significantly strengthen the argument for the generalizability of the proposed approach.  Without such analysis, the reported performance gains could be attributed to the specific characteristics of the training and testing data rather than the inherent advantages of SynTime.

Another weakness lies in the reliance on pre-existing tools for tokenization and POS tagging (specifically, Stanford POS Tagger). The authors acknowledge that errors in these processes can affect performance, but do not explore the sensitivity of SynTime to these errors or consider incorporating more robust pre-processing techniques.  Furthermore, the paper does not detail the impact of the POS tagging accuracy on the results.

Finally, while the speed of SynTime is mentioned as an advantage, a more detailed performance analysis (e.g., processing time per sentence, scalability to larger datasets) would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  To address the overfitting concern, have you evaluated SynTime on a completely independent dataset that was *not* used during the rule design or evaluation process? If so, please present those results.
2.  Could you provide a quantitative analysis of the overlap in time expressions (and the constituent words/tokens) across the four datasets used in this study?
3.  How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to errors in tokenization and POS tagging? Have you investigated the impact of using alternative pre-processing tools or error correction techniques?
4. Could you provide more detailed information on the runtime performance of SynTime (e.g., average processing time per sentence, scalability analysis)?

**Additional Comments:**

- The description of the “boundary expansion” heuristic could be more detailed with concrete examples.
- Consider discussing the limitations of using a rule-based approach compared to machine learning approaches, particularly regarding the effort required to adapt the system to new domains or languages.
