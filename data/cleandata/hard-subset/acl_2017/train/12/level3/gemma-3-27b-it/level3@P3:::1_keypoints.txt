- SynTime: rule-based time expression recognizer using time-token identification and bidirectional heuristic expansion, showing strong gains on multiple datasets.
- Empirical analysis across four datasets reveals time expressions are short, use small vocabularies, and usually contain an identifiable “time token.”
- Type-based rule design: categorize tokens by syntactic behavior and build positional rules for flexible, efficient recognition.
- Strong empirical results: substantial improvements over baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, competitive on WikiWars, especially robust in noisy Twitter data.
- Clear, well-written presentation with helpful diagrams and a concise exposition of motivation, methodology, and results.
- Strong motivation supported by detailed data analysis that directly informs system design.
- Efficient, real-time performance attributed to a simplified, type-based rule set.
- Effective baseline comparisons including both rule-based and ML methods.
- Demonstrated adaptability: domain-specific keyword expansion (SynTime-E) further improves performance.
- Main weakness—lack of reproducibility due to missing implementation details (regexes, POS tagger config, specific rules).
- Limited qualitative error analysis; paper reports metrics but lacks detailed breakdown of error types.
- Ambiguity around “5 seasons” in Section 4.1—unclear definition or domain dependence.
- Dataset discrepancy: Section 5.1 incorrectly refers to “two benchmark datasets” though three are used.
- Question: please define the “5 seasons” and explain handling of seasonal/holiday expressions like Ramadan.
- Question: provide an example of a time expression without a time token and how SynTime processes it.
- Question: with 93% token-containing expressions and 92% recall, what strategies will target the remaining 7% (e.g., contextual methods)?
- Question on reproducibility: will the full rules, regexes, and software be released?
- Additional comment: reference list is extensive and the invocation of Zipf’s principle of least effort provides an interesting theoretical justification.
