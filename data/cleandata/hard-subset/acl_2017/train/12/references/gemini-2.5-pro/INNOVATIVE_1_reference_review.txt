### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes SynTime, a novel rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis of time expressions across four datasets, observing that they are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and are anchored by a core "time token". Motivated by these observations and the linguistic principle of Part-of-Speech (POS), they introduce a "syntactic type system" for time expressions. Unlike existing systems that use semantic types (e.g., duration, frequency), SynTime defines types based on their syntactic role and position relative to a time token (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX). The recognition process involves identifying core time tokens, then applying simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries to include surrounding modifiers and numerals. The final expressions are formed by merging these segments. The authors evaluate SynTime on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), showing that their lightweight system significantly outperforms strong rule-based and machine-learning baselines, especially on informal text.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel conceptual framework for rule-based time tagging based on "syntactic types".** The primary contribution is the bold idea to model time expressions not by their semantic meaning, but by the syntactic behavior of their components (e.g., words that appear before a time unit, words that appear after). This is a fresh and elegant simplification inspired by a fundamental concept in linguistics, offering a new direction for designing rule-based NLP systems.
2.  **A highly effective and lightweight tagger that achieves state-of-the-art performance.** The paper demonstrates that this conceptually simpler approach translates into a practical system that outperforms more complex, heavily engineered systems like SUTime and learning-based approaches like UWTime, particularly in challenging domains like Twitter. The exceptionally high recall is a strong validation of the system's design.

### Strengths
1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance:** The paper's core strength lies in its bold departure from conventional rule-based design. The idea of abstracting away from complex semantic categories to simpler, position-based syntactic types is a powerful one. It challenges the assumption that recognizing a concept requires explicitly modeling its semantics. Framing this simplification through the lens of Zipf's law and the principles behind POS tagging provides a strong theoretical and linguistic grounding for the work. This is exactly the kind of high-level conceptual contribution that can inspire new research directions.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Validation:** The proposed method is not just a theoretical exercise; it delivers outstanding results. Outperforming well-established systems like SUTime and HeidelTime, as well as a strong learning-based parser (UWTime), on standard benchmarks is a significant achievement. The performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly noteworthy, suggesting that the simplified, flexible rules of SynTime are more robust to the noise and variability of informal language than the more rigid rules or feature-based models of its competitors.
3.  **Simplicity and Extensibility:** The proposed system is described as being lightweight, fast, and simple. The "boundary expansion" heuristic is intuitive and contrasts sharply with the complex cascaded finite-state automata often used in this domain. This simplicity makes the system easier to understand, debug, and potentially extend to new languages or other similar extraction tasks (e.g., quantities, measurements) that follow a "core token + modifiers" structure.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on Existing Resources for Initialization:** The system's vocabulary of time tokens and modifiers is bootstrapped from SUTime. While the novelty of SynTime lies in its unique typing and rule system, this reliance on a prior state-of-the-art system somewhat obscures the true standalone capability of the framework. A discussion of how this initial lexicon could be constructed or learned from data would have strengthened the paper's claims of creating a new system from its core principles. As it stands, it's a novel method for *reorganizing and using* an existing lexicon.
2.  **The "Syntactic" vs. "Semantic" Distinction is Blurry:** The paper draws a strong distinction between its syntactic types and the semantic types of other systems. However, the 15 initial "Time Token" types (e.g., YEAR, MONTH, HOLIDAY, DURATION) are clearly semantic in nature. The syntactic rules for expansion seem to operate *after* this initial semantic categorization. This makes the core claim slightly less clean than presented. The true innovation seems to be in simplifying the types for modifiers, not for all components of the system.
3.  **Manual Expansion Process:** The improved performance of SynTime-E relies on a manual process of adding new terms from a training set. While the authors argue this is a low-effort task, it is still a manual bottleneck that limits the system's adaptability and scalability compared to end-to-end learning systems. This weakness is minor, given the strong performance of the initial SynTime-I version, but it's a practical limitation worth noting.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The distinction between semantic and syntactic types is central to your contribution. However, the 15 base "Time Token" types you define are inherently semantic. Could your system function with a single, unified `TIME_TOKEN` type, relying purely on the surrounding `PREFIX`, `SUFFIX`, and `NUMERAL` types for recognition? Or is this initial layer of semantic knowledge essential for the system's success, and if so, how does that affect your core claim of shifting from a semantic to a syntactic paradigm?
2.  Your system achieves remarkable performance on tweets, where standard NLP tools like POS taggers often falter. Given that your method uses POS tags for disambiguation (e.g., for "May"), did you analyze the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime's performance? How robust is your system to this potential source of upstream error?
