### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct a linguistic analysis on several datasets, observing that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and nearly always contain a core "time token". Grounded in Zipf's principle of least effort, they argue that this regularity allows for a much simpler recognition model than prior work. The proposed SynTime system operationalizes this by defining "syntactic types" for tokens (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and using simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules to identify expressions, rather than relying on complex, fixed patterns. The system is evaluated against strong baselines on news, Wikipedia, and social media text, demonstrating competitive and often superior performance, particularly on informal text from Twitter.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and simplified paradigm for rule-based temporal tagging.** The core contribution is the shift away from complex, cascaded, and semantically-defined rules (as in SUTime) towards a more flexible and lightweight model based on syntactic token types and a simple boundary-expansion heuristic. This represents a genuinely new and bold way of thinking about this well-established problem.
2.  **A principled, empirically-grounded motivation for the simplified approach.** The design of SynTime is not ad-hoc; it is directly justified by a clear analysis of the statistical properties of time expressions across different corpora. Linking these observations to a classic linguistic principle (Zipf's Law) provides a strong theoretical underpinning for why such a simple method should be effective.
3.  **An effective and extremely lightweight time expression tagger.** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that this simplified paradigm works, achieving state-of-the-art results on several benchmarks, especially showcasing remarkable robustness on noisy social media data.

### Strengths

This is an excellent paper that I believe makes a significant contribution to the field. Its primary strengths are:

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance:** The paper's main strength lies in its bold conceptual leap. In a field where rule-based systems have become increasingly complex (e.g., SUTime's cascaded finite-state automata) and learning-based systems require extensive features and training data, this work argues for radical simplification. The idea of defining simple types and using a dynamic expansion heuristic is both elegant and highly novel. It challenges the community's assumptions about the level of complexity needed for this task. I highly value such "less is more" approaches that introduce a fresh perspective.
2.  **Strong Principled Motivation:** The system's design is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors don't just present a new set of heuristics; they first present a compelling analysis of the phenomenon they are modeling. The four observations in Section 3 provide a clear and convincing rationale for every design choice made in SynTime. This grounding in empirical observation and linguistic theory elevates the work from a simple engineering contribution to a piece of insightful scientific research.
3.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The novel idea is backed by strong results. SynTime not only competes with but often outperforms established and more complex systems like SUTime and HeidelTime. The exceptionally high recall across all datasets is a powerful testament to the effectiveness of the core "find a time token, then expand" heuristic. Furthermore, the outstanding performance on the Tweets dataset demonstrates that this simplified, flexible approach is more robust to the linguistic variation and noise of informal text than prior methods.
4.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed system is extremely lightweight, requires no training, and runs in real-time. This makes it highly practical for real-world applications where computational resources or training data may be limited. The paper provides a clear blueprint for building a simple yet powerful component for downstream NLP pipelines.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few points that could be considered weaknesses, though in my view, they do not undermine the core contribution.

1.  **Dependence on SUTime's Lexicon:** The system's token lists (the foundation of its type system) are bootstrapped from SUTime. While the authors are transparent about this, it does mean the success of SynTime's novel rule engine is built upon the significant manual effort that went into creating SUTime's lexicon. This slightly lessens the self-contained nature of the contribution and raises questions about how the system would perform if built from scratch.
2.  **Performance on Complex Expressions:** The system's F1 score on the WikiWars dataset, while competitive, lags slightly behind the baselines. The authors correctly attribute this to the prevalence of more descriptive and preposition-heavy expressions in that domain, which their simplified model is not designed to capture. This highlights a potential limitation: the "least effort" principle may not hold as strongly for all genres of text, and the system's simplicity may come at the cost of capturing more complex, compositional temporal language. However, given the paper's premise, I see this as an acceptable trade-off rather than a critical flaw.
3.  **Manual Expansion Process:** The improvement from SynTime-I to SynTime-E relies on a manual process of identifying and adding missing keywords. While the authors convincingly argue that this effort is minimal due to the small vocabulary of time expressions, it is still a manual step that hinders full automation and adaptability to new, unseen domains.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more insight into the system's sensitivity to the initial lexicon? Your method's novelty lies in its rules, but its performance clearly also depends on the quality of the initial token lists borrowed from SUTime. How much performance do you think is attributable to the novel rule engine versus the comprehensiveness of the borrowed lexicon?
2.  The paper draws a clear line between its "syntactic" types and the "semantic" types of prior work. However, a modifier like `next` has both a syntactic role (appearing before a time unit) and a clear semantic function. Is the key innovation the positional, heuristic-based rules themselves, rather than the "syntactic" nature of the types? In other words, would the system perform similarly if the types were given more semantic labels but the expansion rules remained the same?
