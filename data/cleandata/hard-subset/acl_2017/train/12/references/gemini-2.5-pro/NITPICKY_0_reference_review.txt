### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis on four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and make four observations: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary of keywords, almost always contain a core "time token," and exhibit consistent syntactic patterns. Motivated by these observations and Zipf's principle of least effort, they propose a system that defines syntactic types for tokens (Time Token, Modifier, Numeral) rather than semantic ones. The recognition process first identifies core time tokens and then uses simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries of the expression by searching for adjacent modifiers and numerals. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, demonstrating superior or comparable performance, particularly in terms of recall.

### Contributions
1.  **A Simple and Effective Rule-Based Tagger:** The primary contribution is SynTime, a lightweight time expression tagger that uses a novel syntactic typing system and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The method is conceptually simple compared to more complex rule-based systems and achieves strong empirical results, outperforming established baselines on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The consistently high recall across all datasets is particularly noteworthy.
2.  **Analysis of Time Expression Characteristics:** A secondary contribution is the quantitative analysis of time expressions across four diverse corpora. The findings (e.g., >80% of expressions are <= 3 tokens, >93% contain a time token) provide valuable empirical grounding for the system's design and could inform future work in this area.

### Strengths
1.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The proposed system, in both its initial (SynTime-I) and expanded (SynTime-E) versions, achieves excellent results. It significantly outperforms strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the standard TimeBank dataset and a newly annotated Tweets dataset. For instance, on TimeBank, SynTime-E achieves a strict F1 of 92.47, a notable improvement over the best baseline's 83.10. The system's ability to achieve very high recall (e.g., >95% relaxed recall on TimeBank) is a significant strength.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Elegance:** The core idea of defining types based on syntactic roles (core unit, prefix, suffix, etc.) and using simple boundary expansion rules is elegant. It stands in contrast to systems that rely on large, complex, and potentially brittle sets of handcrafted patterns or cascaded finite-state transducers. This simplicity makes the system transparent and likely easy to maintain and extend.
3.  **Creation and Evaluation on a Tweets Dataset:** The authors have manually annotated a new dataset of 942 tweets, providing a valuable resource for evaluating time expression taggers on noisy, informal text. The exceptional performance of SynTime on this dataset (e.g., >91% strict F1) demonstrates its robustness and adaptability to different domains.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Reproducibility:** The description of key components of the method lacks the necessary detail for full understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The heuristic rules for time segment identification (Section 4.2.2) and merging (Section 4.2.3) are described only in high-level prose. A more formal description, such as pseudocode or an algorithmic outline, would be essential for clarity. For example, the precise conditions under which left/right searching stops are not fully specified.
    *   The "SynTime Expansion" process is ad-hoc. The criterion that "added words can not cause ambiguity and should be generic" is subjective and not reproducible. A systematic protocol for this expansion is needed. Without it, the SynTime-E results feel cherry-picked and less scientifically sound.
2.  **Unsupported Claims and Superficial Analysis:** The paper makes several claims without providing supporting evidence.
    *   It repeatedly claims that SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time." However, no quantitative data on runtime, memory usage, or computational complexity is provided. A comparison of processing speed against the baseline systems is necessary to substantiate this claim.
    *   The analysis of why SynTime outperforms baselines is speculative. The claim that in machine learning methods "some of the features they used actually hurt the modelling" is a strong assertion made without any diagnostic experiments or evidence.
3.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and severe formatting issues, which collectively detract from its professional quality.
    *   **Typos/Grammar:** "Time expression recognition main involves..." (p.1), "Particulaly" (p.2), "SynTime can recoginize..." (p.2), "overall perfermance" (p.5), "due to the limit of used tools" (p.6).
    *   **Footnotes:** Raw URLs are included in footnotes (e.g., footnotes 1, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9), which is improper formatting.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted and contains numerous errors, suggesting a lack of care. For example, many entries have extraneous characters (`Alonso et al\.,? 2011, Alonso et al\.,`), inconsistent author formatting, and one entry (`Verhagen et al., 2005`) is incorrectly attributed to `Pustejovsky`. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference publication.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide a more formal, algorithmic description (e.g., pseudocode) of your time segment identification and time expression extraction rules as described in Sections 4.2.2 and 4.2.3?
2.  Can you outline a more rigorous, systematic, and reproducible protocol for the "SynTime Expansion" process used to create SynTime-E from SynTime-I? What specific criteria were used to manually select the words listed in Table 5?
3.  Could you provide empirical evidence (e.g., processing speed in words/second, compared against baselines) to support the repeated claim that SynTime "runs in real time"?
