### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The approach is motivated by an analysis of time expressions across four corpora, which concludes that such expressions are typically short, use a limited vocabulary, and are anchored by core "time tokens". The proposed system defines syntactic types for tokens (Time Token, Modifier, Numeral) and uses simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion to identify full time expressions. The system is initialized using token lists from SUTime and can be manually expanded for new domains. The authors evaluate SynTime on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets datasets, showing that it achieves performance comparable or superior to state-of-the-art rule-based and machine learning systems.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An effective and lightweight rule-based method for time expression recognition that achieves strong empirical results, particularly high recall, against established baselines on multiple datasets, including the challenging social media domain.
2.  A linguistic analysis of time expressions across different genres (news, Wikipedia, tweets) that provides useful empirical observations to motivate and ground the system's design.
3.  The demonstration of a simplified rule design paradigm based on syntactic types and boundary expansion, which serves as a compelling alternative to more complex cascaded finite-state approaches while maintaining high performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its experimental results. SynTime significantly outperforms three strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets and achieves comparable results on WikiWars (Table 4). The consistently high recall across all datasets is particularly impressive, suggesting that the system's core assumption—that most time expressions contain an identifiable time token—is sound and effectively implemented. The performance on the Tweets dataset is a standout result, showing the method's robustness in handling informal text.
2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and well-explained. The core logic of identifying anchor tokens and then expanding their boundaries using a small set of heuristic rules is more transparent and potentially more maintainable than complex cascaded rule systems. The authors' claim that the system is "extremely light-weight" and runs in "real time" is plausible given the design, representing a significant practical advantage over computationally intensive machine learning models or intricate finite-state transducers.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Analysis:** The paper is well-motivated by a preliminary data analysis presented in Section 3. The four observations about the structural properties of time expressions (short length, small vocabulary, presence of a time token, and constituent-level POS patterns) provide a solid and intuitive foundation for the system's design. This grounding analysis strengthens the paper by justifying the design choices and moving beyond a purely engineering-driven approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the system is effective, its core components lack significant novelty. Rule-based systems built on dictionaries and expansion heuristics are a well-established paradigm in NLP. The key idea of defining token types and rules for their composition is central to systems like SUTime. The distinction between "syntactic types" (positional) and "semantic types" is a useful framing, but the resulting rules (e.g., search left for PREFIX, search right for SUFFIX) are functionally similar to what other systems implement. The heavy reliance on SUTime's token lists for initialization further positions this work as an incremental refinement or simplification of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2.  **Ambiguity in Comparison and Contribution:** The system's initialization with token lists from SUTime complicates the interpretation of the results and the paper's core contribution. It is unclear how much of SynTime's strong performance is attributable to its novel rule logic versus the high quality of the SUTime lexicon it is built upon. This makes the head-to-head comparison with SUTime difficult to interpret; SynTime is effectively a different rule engine running on SUTime's vocabulary. An ablation study would be necessary to disentangle the contribution of the rule engine from that of the lexicon.
3.  **Insufficient Detail for Reproducibility:** The description of the heuristic rules, particularly for merging time segments (Section 4.2.3), is too high-level to ensure reproducibility. The paper states general principles (e.g., merge adjacent segments, merge overlapping segments unless the boundary is a LINKAGE), but the implementation of this logic can be complex and requires handling many edge cases. For example, the three conditions for merging across a comma seem insufficient to handle common patterns like "Monday, October 10". Without a more formal description of the rule logic or pseudo-code, it would be difficult for another researcher to reimplement the system and verify the results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper claims that SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time". Could you substantiate this with quantitative metrics? For example, what is the processing speed (e.g., tokens/second) and memory footprint of SynTime compared to the baselines on the test sets?
2.  Given that the initial lexicon is borrowed directly from SUTime, can you clarify the precise contribution of your rule engine? Could you conduct an experiment where you provide SUTime's system with the few additional expansion tokens found for WikiWars (Table 5) to see if its performance improves commensurately? This would help isolate whether the performance gains come from the lexicon or the rule logic.
3.  Could you provide more detail on the rule for merging segments separated by a COMMA? The example in Figure 3(d) ("June 30, 1990") seems to be handled, but how does your logic address more complex cases like "On Friday, October 31, at 4 p.m."? A more detailed explanation or pseudo-code for the merging algorithm would strengthen the paper's technical clarity.
