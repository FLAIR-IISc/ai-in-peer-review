**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions (TIMEX) in text. The work is motivated by a corpus analysis across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), which concludes that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic patterns. The core contribution is a novel approach to rule-based TIMEX recognition that defines token types based on their syntactic behavior (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) rather than their semantic meaning. On top of this type system, SynTime applies simple, heuristic rules based on boundary expansion, first identifying core "time tokens" and then expanding left and right to include modifiers and numerals. The system is evaluated against strong rule-based and machine learning baselines on three datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on TimeBank and a newly annotated Tweets corpus, and competitive performance on WikiWars.

**Main Contributions**
1.  **A simple, high-recall, and effective rule-based method for TIMEX recognition.** The main contribution is the SynTime system itself. Its design, which combines syntactic token types with simple boundary-expansion heuristics, is elegant and proves highly effective. The system's strong performance, particularly its consistently high recall and its robustness on informal text (Tweets), makes it a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **A useful empirical analysis of time expression characteristics across different genres.** The analysis in Section 3, while confirming some common intuitions, provides a solid, data-driven motivation for the system's design. The creation and annotation of a new Tweets dataset for this task is also a welcome resource contribution.

**Strengths**
1.  **Impressive Empirical Performance and Robustness.** SynTime achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on three diverse datasets. Its performance on the new Tweets dataset is a standout achievement, where it significantly outperforms established systems like SUTime and UWTime (e.g., +14.78 F1 over SUTime in strict match). This demonstrates that the simplified, flexible rule system is highly effective for handling the noisy and informal language common in social media, a challenging domain for many NLP systems. The consistently high recall across all datasets is another major strength, indicating the method is very effective at identifying candidate time expressions.
2.  **Simplicity, Interpretability, and Efficiency.** The proposed method is remarkably simple and transparent. The idea of identifying a seed "time token" and expanding its boundaries is intuitive and easy to follow. This stands in contrast to the often complex and opaque cascaded finite-state automata used in other rule-based systems or the black-box nature of neural models. This simplicity makes the system easy to understand, debug, and likely very efficient (as claimed by the authors), which is a significant practical advantage.
3.  **Novel Conceptualization of a Rule-Based System.** While rule-based TIMEX taggers are well-established, the paper's central idea of abstracting away from semantic types (e.g., `frequency_modifier`) to syntactic types (e.g., `PREFIX`) is a novel and powerful simplification. This abstraction appears to capture general patterns of linguistic composition more flexibly than fine-grained semantic rules, allowing the system to generalize well without an explosion of specific rules. This presents a compelling alternative design principle for rule-based information extraction systems.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **The Central Claim about Syntactic Types is Not Empirically Validated.** The paper's primary conceptual claim is that using syntactic types is a key advantage over the semantic types used in systems like SUTime. However, this is only presented as an assertion. The experiments compare entire systems, which differ in many aspects (rule formalism, rule content, architecture) beyond just the type system. A more rigorous evaluation would require an ablation study to isolate the impact of the type system. For example, one could implement SynTime's boundary-expansion logic but with SUTime's semantic types to see if performance drops. Without such a direct comparison, it is difficult to conclude that the syntactic types, rather than the boundary-expansion heuristic or other factors, are the main reason for the system's success.
2.  **Significant Dependence on an Existing SOTA System for Initialization.** The paper states that SynTime is initialized with token regular expressions "collected from SUTime." This is a critical detail that potentially weakens the claim of a novel, self-contained system. SUTime's lexicons are comprehensive and meticulously curated. A substantial portion of SynTime's high recall may be directly inherited from this resource, rather than being a product of its novel rule engine. To properly assess the contribution of SynTime's architecture, it would be necessary to show how it performs when initialized with a more generic or independently created lexicon. The current framing makes it hard to disentangle the performance gained from SUTime's resources versus SynTime's rules.
3.  **Lack of In-depth Error Analysis.** The paper reports strong aggregate scores but provides little insight into the system's failure modes. The heuristic rules are presented as "simple," but the description of merging logic (e.g., the three conditions for handling commas) suggests that edge cases require increasingly specific logic. An error analysis would be highly beneficial. For instance, SynTime's performance on WikiWars is lower than on the other datasets; the paper speculates this is due to "descriptive time expressions," but providing concrete examples of errors and analyzing *why* the syntactic rules failed would offer a much deeper understanding of the method's limitations and areas for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the initialization from SUTime: Could you quantify how much of your initial lexicon (i.e., the regular expressions for time tokens and modifiers) is derived from SUTime? How does a version of SynTime-I perform if initialized with a more basic set of resources (e.g., only terms found in a standard library like Joda-Time)? This would help clarify the contribution of your rule system itself versus the inherited lexicon.
2.  The lower performance on WikiWars is an interesting result. Could you provide 1-2 examples of false negatives or false positives from this dataset and trace them back to a specific failure of your syntactic types or heuristic rules? This would help clarify the boundaries of where your approach excels and where it struggles.
