**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), concluding with four observations about their length, vocabulary, and syntactic patterns, which they link to Zipf's principle of least effort. Based on these observations, they propose a system that defines "syntactic types" for tokens (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion to identify time expressions. The system is initialized with token lists from SUTime and can be optionally expanded with new terms. The authors evaluate SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on three datasets, reporting state-of-the-art or comparable performance, with particularly strong results on the Tweets dataset.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that a lightweight, rule-based system (SynTime) built on simplified principles of anchor-word identification and boundary expansion can achieve very high recall and strong overall performance on time expression recognition, especially on informal text.
2.  A secondary contribution is the analysis of time expressions across several corpora and the creation of a new, manually annotated dataset of time expressions in tweets.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The system achieves impressive results, outperforming three strong baselines on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets in F1 score. Its recall is consistently the highest across all datasets and evaluation settings, which is a significant practical achievement. The performance on the noisy Tweets dataset is particularly noteworthy.
2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, consisting of token identification followed by heuristic boundary expansion. The authors claim it is extremely lightweight and runs in real time, which represents a valuable engineering contribution compared to more complex, computationally expensive machine learning models or intricately handcrafted rule systems.
3.  **Focus on a Challenging Domain:** The paper tackles the difficult domain of social media text. By creating and evaluating on a new Tweets dataset, the authors demonstrate the robustness of their approach on informal language, where time expressions can be abbreviated or non-standard.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Overstated Novelty and Questionable Framing:** The paper's central claim of novelty rests on its "syntactic types" as opposed to the "semantic types" of prior work. This distinction is tenuous at best. A type like `PREFIX` is defined by its position relative to a time token, but the lexicon for this type contains semantically coherent words like 'last', 'next', 'previous'. The system is still fundamentally reliant on semantic lexicons. More critically, the paper explicitly states that the initial token lists for SynTime are collected directly from SUTime. This severely undermines the claim of a novel approach; the system appears to be more of a structural simplification and re-engineering of SUTime's components rather than a fundamentally new system.
2.  **Methodological Flaws in Analysis and Evaluation:**
    *   The "observations" in Section 3 are not particularly novel. It is well-established that functional linguistic units are often short and draw from a limited vocabulary. Citing Zipf's law is a post-hoc rationalization rather than a deep insight that drives the method's design.
    *   The creation of the Tweets dataset is methodologically suspect. The initial corpus was filtered using SUTime to find tweets containing time expressions. This introduces a significant selection bias, as the resulting dataset is predisposed to contain expressions that SUTime can already recognize. The subsequent check for SUTime's recall on a small sample of 500 tweets is too rough to be reliable.
    *   The comparison against UWTime, a learning-based method, is potentially unfair on the new Tweets dataset. Training a model on a combination of TimeBank and a small, 742-tweet training set is unlikely to yield optimal performance for a learning-based system in a new domain, giving an inherent advantage to rule-based systems that do not require in-domain training data.
3.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Professional Polish:** The paper suffers from a substantial number of issues that suggest a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   There are numerous typographical and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., "Time expression recognition main involves", "Rulebased", "Particulaly", "recoginize", "perfermance").
    *   The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted and appears to be an unedited raw export from a reference manager. It contains extraneous characters, question marks, and inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Alonso et al\.,?", "Parker et al\.,? \Q2011\E"). This is unacceptable for a formal submission.
    *   Important methodological details, such as the reason for excluding the Gigaword dataset from experiments, are relegated to footnotes. Footnotes are also used inappropriately for embedding URLs (Footnote 1) and block quotes (Footnote 3).
    *   The description of the core algorithm is vague. The rules for merging segments in Section 4.2.3 are described at a high level, and it is unclear how the system handles more complex or ambiguous cases. Figure 3, which is meant to illustrate this process, is cryptic and inadequately explained.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Given that SynTime's lexicons are bootstrapped directly from SUTime, could you please clarify why the method is presented as a fundamentally different, "syntactic-type" approach rather than a simplification or optimization of SUTime's rule execution strategy?
2.  Can you provide more details on the annotation of the Tweets dataset? Specifically, what was the inter-annotator agreement, and how do you account for the potential selection bias introduced by using SUTime to pre-filter the tweets?
3.  The paper claims SynTime's rules are more "flexible" than the "fixed" rules of other taggers. Could you provide a concrete example of a complex time expression (e.g., with nesting or ambiguity) that SUTime fails on but SynTime correctly recognizes due to this claimed flexibility?
