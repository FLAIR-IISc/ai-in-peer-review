### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis of time expressions across four datasets, observing that they are typically short, use a small set of core "time tokens," and exhibit consistent syntactic patterns, which they connect to Zipf's principle of least effort. Based on these observations, they introduce a novel approach that defines abstract *syntactic types* for tokens (e.g., Time Token, PREFIX, SUFFIX) based on their function and position, rather than their semantic meaning. The recognition process uses simple heuristic rules, primarily a "boundary expansion" method that starts from identified time tokens and extends outwards to include adjacent modifiers and numerals. The authors evaluate their system against strong rule-based and machine-learning baselines on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), demonstrating that their conceptually simpler model achieves state-of-the-art or superior performance, especially in terms of recall.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel, simplified paradigm for rule-based information extraction.** The core contribution is the conceptual shift from complex, semantic-based rule systems to a much simpler framework based on abstract syntactic types and straightforward boundary-expansion heuristics. This "less is more" approach is a bold and refreshing alternative to the prevailing trends of either increasingly intricate hand-crafted grammars or feature-heavy machine learning models for this task.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the "simple is effective" principle.** The paper shows that this lightweight and conceptually clean model can outperform more complex and established state-of-the-art systems on standard benchmarks. The particularly impressive results on noisy social media data (Tweets) highlight the robustness and practical value of the proposed method.
3.  **An insightful analysis that grounds the work in linguistic principles.** The initial analysis in Section 3 provides a compelling motivation for the model's design. By connecting observable properties of time expressions (e.g., brevity, small vocabulary) to the principle of least effort, the authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for why their simplified approach should be effective.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's primary strength lies in its conceptual novelty. The idea of abstracting away from semantic types (e.g., SUTime's "frequency modifiers") to purely syntactic, position-based types (e.g., "PREFIX") is an elegant and powerful simplification. It challenges the assumption that rule-based systems must encode deep semantic knowledge to be effective. This work offers a promising new direction for building lightweight, efficient, and interpretable NLP systems, not just for time expressions but potentially for other extraction tasks as well.
2.  **Impressive Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed SynTime system is remarkably simple. The entire logic is based on identifying a core token and expanding its boundaries using a handful of intuitive rules. This stands in stark contrast to the complexity of cascaded finite-state automata, CCG parsers, or large statistical models often used for this task. The resulting system is extremely lightweight and fast, making it highly suitable for real-time applications where resource consumption is a concern.
3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. SynTime significantly outperforms three strong baselines on the standard TimeBank benchmark and on a challenging Tweets dataset. Achieving consistently high recall across all datasets lends strong support to the authors' central hypothesis: that accurately identifying the core "time token" is the most crucial step, and the rest of the expression can be captured with simple heuristics. Beating more complex systems with a much simpler model is a significant achievement.
4.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The work is very well-motivated. The analysis in Section 3 is not just a perfunctory data summary; it directly informs and justifies every aspect of the SynTime design. Framing the approach within the context of Zipf's principle of least effort elevates the paper from a simple engineering contribution to a piece of work with a clear scientific hypothesis and grounding.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on External Resources for Initialization:** The SynTime system is initialized with token regular expressions directly borrowed from SUTime. While a pragmatic choice for bootstrapping, this dependency somewhat undermines the self-contained nature of the work and makes its performance contingent on the quality of another system's manual effort. The contribution would be stronger if the paper explored methods for learning or deriving these initial type lists with minimal supervision, which would align better with the system's overall philosophy of simplicity.
2.  **Scope Limited to Recognition (Ignoring Normalization):** The paper focuses exclusively on identifying the spans of time expressions and omits the critical sub-task of normalization (i.e., mapping an expression to a canonical value). While many papers focus on one sub-task, the authors' claim that normalization can be handled by "methods similar to the existing rule systems" is a bit dismissive. The proposed syntactic abstraction discards semantic information that might be crucial for normalization (e.g., knowing that "every" is a frequency modifier). The lack of discussion on how the SynTime framework might aid or hinder normalization is a notable omission.
3.  **Manual and Ad-hoc System Expansion:** The process of improving the initial model (SynTime-I to SynTime-E) relies on manually adding missing keywords from a training set. This process is not scalable and feels ad-hoc, running counter to the elegance of the core framework. A more principled approach to expansion or adaptation would strengthen the method's practical applicability.
4.  **Lack of a Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit greatly from a more in-depth qualitative analysis of its failure cases. The simple, heuristic-based approach is bound to have limitations, particularly with complex or ambiguous constructions. For example, why does it perform worse on WikiWars? What specific "descriptive time expressions" cause it to fail? Presenting and analyzing these errors would provide a much clearer picture of the model's boundaries and offer valuable insights for future work in this direction.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the initialization from SUTime: Have you considered methods to construct the initial token-to-type mappings automatically or with minimal supervision (e.g., using distributional clustering or distant supervision from a knowledge base)? This would make the approach more self-sufficient and potentially more adaptable to new domains.
2.  The core conceptual shift is from semantic to syntactic types. While this beautifully simplifies recognition, does this abstraction pose challenges for the downstream task of normalization? For instance, SUTime's semantic types seem directly usable for writing normalization rules. Could you elaborate on how normalization would be approached within the SynTime framework, and whether you foresee any trade-offs?
3.  The performance on WikiWars is notably lower than on the other datasets. You attribute this to "descriptive time expressions." Could you provide 1-2 concrete examples of such expressions from WikiWars that SynTime fails on, and explain why the simple boundary expansion heuristic is insufficient in these cases? This would help clarify the specific limitations of the current model.
