### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition. The method's design is motivated by an analysis of four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), which reveals that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain at least one core "time token". Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main "syntactic types" for tokens: time tokens, modifiers, and numerals. The recognition process involves first identifying time tokens in a sentence, and then applying simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries of these tokens to include adjacent modifiers and numerals. The resulting segments are then merged to form the final time expressions. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets datasets, demonstrating strong performance, particularly in terms of recall and on the informal Tweets dataset.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A simple and effective rule-based method (SynTime) for time expression recognition.** The system is based on a small set of syntactic token types and simple boundary-expansion heuristics, making it conceptually clear and computationally efficient.
2.  **A strong empirical evaluation demonstrating the method's effectiveness across diverse domains.** The system achieves comparable or superior performance to more complex rule-based and machine learning systems, showing particular strength on informal social media text.
3.  **An empirical analysis of time expressions across four corpora.** This analysis provides valuable insights into the linguistic properties of time expressions and serves as a solid motivation for the proposed system's design.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The primary strength of this work is the demonstration that a simple, lightweight approach can be highly effective for time expression recognition. SynTime eschews the complex, hand-crafted grammars of systems like SUTime in favor of a straightforward three-step process: identify core time tokens, expand boundaries, and merge segments. Despite this simplicity, it achieves state-of-the-art or competitive F1 scores on all tested datasets. Its high recall across all three datasets is particularly noteworthy, suggesting the core principle of identifying and expanding from "time tokens" is a robust strategy.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation against three strong and well-established baselines. The choice of datasets is excellent, covering standard newswire text (TimeBank), a specific domain (WikiWars), and challenging informal text (Tweets). The outstanding performance on the Tweets dataset (e.g., a +14.78 F1 improvement over SUTime in strict match) is a significant result, highlighting the robustness and adaptability of the proposed method to noisy, non-standard language where other systems falter.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The design of SynTime is directly and convincingly motivated by the preliminary data analysis presented in Section 3. The observations that most time expressions contain a core "time token," are short, and use a limited vocabulary provide a clear rationale for the system's architecture. Grounding these observations in the broader linguistic context of Zipf's principle of least effort adds theoretical depth to the work. This clear link between analysis and system design makes the paper easy to follow and methodologically sound.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unclear Distinction from Prior Rule-Based Methods:** The paper's central claim is that SynTime differs from other rule-based taggers through its use of "syntactic types" instead of "semantic types" and heuristic expansion rules instead of fixed rules. This distinction is not sufficiently justified. Systems like SUTime also use typed regular expressions over tokens (e.g., `YEAR`, `MONTH`, `ORDINAL`), and their cascaded rules effectively perform a similar expansion from core tokens to larger chunks. The modifier types proposed (`PREFIX`, `SUFFIX`) are defined positionally, not by true syntactic function. The contribution appears to be more of a simplification and re-engineering of existing rule-based ideas rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. A more detailed comparison of the rule mechanisms is needed to substantiate the claims of novelty.
2.  **Dependence on Manual Effort and External Tools:** The system's effectiveness relies on two components that limit its autonomy. First, the initial lexicon of typed tokens is bootstrapped from SUTime, meaning it builds directly on prior manual engineering. Second, adapting the system to new domains requires further manual effort to expand the lexicon, as demonstrated by the performance gap between SynTime-I and SynTime-E on the WikiWars dataset. The paper claims this cost is low but does not quantify it, making it difficult to compare with the cost of annotating data for a learning-based model. Furthermore, the system is dependent on an upstream POS tagger, and the impact of tagging errors is mentioned but not analyzed.
3.  **Limited Scope (Recognition Only):** The work is explicitly scoped to time expression recognition, setting aside the task of normalization (i.e., mapping an expression like "last Tuesday" to a formal representation like `2023-10-17`). Normalization is a critical component of a complete temporal processing system, and it is handled by all three baseline systems. The paper's assertion that normalization "could be achieved by using methods similar to the existing rule systems" is speculative and overlooks significant challenges. The proposed abstraction to "syntactic types" might discard semantic information crucial for normalization, but this trade-off is not discussed. This makes the presented system an incomplete solution compared to the baselines.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the conceptual distinction between SynTime's "syntactic types" and the token-level types used in other rule-based systems like SUTime? For instance, types such as `HOLIDAY` or `MONTH` seem primarily semantic. What makes your type system fundamentally syntactic, and how does it differ from the typed regular expressions SUTime is built on?
2.  The expansion from SynTime-I to SynTime-E for the WikiWars dataset required adding 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers. Could you quantify the human effort (e.g., in hours) required to identify and add these new terms? How does this manual adaptation effort compare to the alternative of annotating a small amount of in-domain data to fine-tune a machine learning model?
3.  Given that your system does not perform normalization, could you discuss how your proposed type system would facilitate or potentially complicate this crucial downstream task? For example, does grouping modifiers into broad positional categories like `PREFIX` (which includes "last", "next", "of") make it harder to write normalization rules that depend on the specific semantics of these modifiers?
