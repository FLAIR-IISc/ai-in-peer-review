### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis of time expressions across four diverse corpora (news, Wikipedia, tweets), making four key observations: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, almost always contain a core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Tuesday'), and their components exhibit consistent syntactic behaviors. Motivated by these findings, particularly the "principle of least effort," they propose a system that operates in three stages: 1) identify core time tokens, 2) expand their boundaries using simple heuristic rules to find modifiers and numerals, forming "time segments," and 3) merge adjacent or overlapping segments to form the final time expressions. The system's novelty is claimed to lie in its use of "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) for tokens, as opposed to the semantic types used in other rule-based systems, and its simple, flexible boundary-expansion rules. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets dataset show that SynTime achieves state-of-the-art or comparable performance, with particularly strong results on the noisy Tweets dataset.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and simple rule-based framework for time expression recognition.** The proposed SynTime system, based on the clear and intuitive principle of "find a time anchor and expand," is demonstrated to be highly effective, outperforming strong baselines on two of three datasets. Its simplicity stands in contrast to more complex, hand-crafted grammars and makes it an attractive, lightweight approach.
2.  **A strong empirical analysis motivating the system's design.** The paper provides a valuable analysis of the properties of time expressions across different domains. The observations that >93% of time expressions contain a core time token and that most are very short provide a strong, data-driven justification for the proposed method's architecture.
3.  **Demonstrated robustness on informal text.** The system's state-of-the-art performance on the Tweets dataset is a significant result. It shows that the simple, flexible heuristic approach is more robust to the linguistic variation and noise of social media text than existing rule-based and learning-based systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Simplicity and Elegance:** The core idea behind SynTime is compellingly simple: identify an unambiguous time-related keyword and then expand outwards. This contrasts with systems that rely on complex, cascaded finite-state machines or large feature sets for machine learning models. The strength of the paper lies in showing how this simple heuristic, grounded in solid empirical observations, can lead to state-of-the-art performance.
2.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation against three strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on standard datasets. SynTime achieves the best F1 scores on TimeBank and Tweets, with a particularly large margin on the Tweets dataset (+11 F1 points in strict match over the next best). Achieving high recall across all datasets is also impressive and directly supports the hypothesis that anchoring on "time tokens" is a high-coverage strategy.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The analysis in Section 3 is a key strength. It provides a clear, data-driven rationale for every major design decision in SynTime. The observation that most time expressions contain a core time token directly motivates the "identify-then-expand" strategy. The observation about short lengths justifies the simple, local nature of the expansion rules. This grounding makes the work feel well-considered and principled.
4.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The method is described clearly, particularly with the aid of Figure 2 (overview) and Figure 3 (merging examples). The distinction between SynTime's "syntactic" types and other systems' "semantic" types is explained well and serves as a clear point of differentiation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Components and Reliance on SUTime:** While the overall framework is elegant, its components are not entirely novel. The core of the system relies on regular expressions for identifying time-related tokens, a standard technique. More importantly, the system is initialized using token lists and regular expressions from SUTime. This makes the direct comparison to SUTime somewhat problematic. It is unclear how much of SynTime's performance stems from its novel boundary-expansion rules versus its inheritance of SUTime's comprehensive, manually curated token lists. The contribution would be stronger if the authors had demonstrated the effectiveness of their framework by building the initial token lists from a resource like TimeBank from scratch.
2.  **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The "Limitations" section is very brief, only mentioning potential errors from upstream POS tagging. A more detailed error analysis is needed to understand the system's failure modes. For example, on WikiWars, SynTime's strict F1 is noticeably lower than UWTime's. Why? The paper mentions that WikiWars contains "descriptive time expressions" that SynTime cannot handle, but fails to provide examples or analyze this class of errors. Understanding what the simple heuristic rules fail to capture is crucial for assessing the method's limitations and potential for future work.
3.  **The "Syntactic vs. Semantic" Distinction May Be Overstated:** The authors frame their main contribution as using syntactic types (e.g., PREFIX) instead of semantic types. While position is a syntactic property, the *contents* of the PREFIX list (e.g., 'several', 'last', 'next') are chosen based on their semantic function of modifying a time unit. Therefore, the distinction is more nuanced than presented. The key innovation seems to be less about a purely syntactic typing system and more about a simplified rule system that operates on these types, regardless of their label.
4.  **Lack of Efficiency Analysis:** The paper claims that SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time." These are important practical claims, especially for a rule-based system. However, no quantitative evidence (e.g., processing speed in sentences/sec, memory footprint) is provided to substantiate these claims or compare them against the baselines.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the system's performance if it were initialized without SUTime's token lists? For instance, if you were to build the initial type lists (Time Token, Modifier, etc.) only from the TimeBank training data, how much does performance degrade? This would help disentangle the contribution of your rule framework from the contribution of the inherited SUTime resources.
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors on the WikiWars dataset? What are some examples of the "descriptive time expressions" that SynTime fails to recognize, and which of your heuristic rules are responsible for these failures?
3.  Can you provide quantitative results to support the claim that SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and runs in "real time"? A comparison of throughput (e.g., tokens processed per second) against SUTime and HeidelTime would be very valuable.
