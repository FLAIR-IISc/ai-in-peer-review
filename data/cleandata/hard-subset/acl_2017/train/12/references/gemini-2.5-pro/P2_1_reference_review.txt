### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in text. The authors first conduct a linguistic analysis of time expressions across four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), making four key observations: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, almost always contain a core "time token," and their constituent words exhibit consistent syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, which they link to Zipf's principle of least effort, they propose a system that operates in three stages: 1) identify core time tokens using a predefined lexicon, 2) expand the boundaries of these tokens using simple heuristic rules to include modifiers and numerals, and 3) merge adjacent or overlapping segments into final time expressions. The core lexicon and regular expressions are admittedly bootstrapped from the existing SUTime system. The authors claim their main novelty lies in defining token types based on syntactic roles (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) rather than semantic roles, and using a simple, flexible boundary-expansion mechanism instead of complex, fixed rules. The system is evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset, where it is shown to outperform state-of-the-art systems, including HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, particularly on TimeBank and Tweets.

### Contributions
1.  **An effective and simple rule-based system for time expression recognition that achieves state-of-the-art recall.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. Despite its conceptual simplicity (find a time token, then expand left and right), it achieves impressive empirical results, significantly outperforming well-established baselines on two of the three test sets. The design, which prioritizes identifying a core token first, leads to exceptionally high recall, which is a valuable asset for many downstream applications.

2.  **A concise and useful analysis of the properties of time expressions across different genres.** The observations presented in Section 3, while perhaps intuitive to domain experts, are well-supported by data from news, encyclopedic text, and social media. This analysis provides a strong and clear motivation for the system's design and serves as a useful reference for future work in this area.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The system's performance is its strongest selling point. Achieving an F1 of 92.09 on TimeBank (strict match) is a significant improvement over strong baselines like UWTime (83.10) and SUTime (79.57). The results on the Tweets dataset are even more impressive, with SynTime-I achieving 91.74 F1 compared to the next best baseline at 80.37. These are not marginal gains; they represent a substantial step forward in performance, especially for a rule-based system.

2.  **Simplicity, Efficiency, and Extensibility:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to understand. The core idea of identifying an anchor and expanding its boundaries with simple heuristics is more straightforward than the complex cascaded finite-state transducers used by systems like SUTime. This simplicity likely translates to high computational efficiency ("runs in real time") and makes the system easier to maintain and extend. The SynTime-E results demonstrate that performance can be improved on new domains with minimal manual annotation effort.

3.  **High Recall by Design:** The system is designed to maximize recall by anchoring its search on high-confidence "time tokens." As Observation 3 shows, over 93% of time expressions contain such a token. By building expressions around these anchors, the system is less likely to miss entities entirely. The results confirm this, with recall rates consistently in the mid-90s for relaxed match across all datasets, a significant advantage over other methods.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Overstated Novelty and Understated Reliance on SUTime:** The paper's primary weakness is its framing of novelty. It heavily emphasizes its new approach to defining "syntactic types" and "heuristic rules" as a departure from prior work. However, Section 4.1 critically states: "The token regular expressions for initializing SynTime are collected from SUTime...we collect from SUTime only the tokens and the regular expressions over tokens, and discard its other rules." This is a crucial point. The most labor-intensive part of any rule-based system is the creation of the core lexicon and its regex patterns. By bootstrapping this from a major competitor, the work is less a new system built from scratch and more a new, and admittedly more effective, *rule engine* that sits on top of SUTime's comprehensive lexicon. The contribution is still valid, but the paper should be more transparent about this dependency and frame its novelty more precisely as an improved method for composing lexical items, rather than a wholly new approach to rule-based tagging.

2.  **The "Syntactic vs. Semantic" Distinction is Unclear:** The paper draws a sharp distinction between its "syntactic" types and the "semantic" types of other taggers. This distinction feels artificial. While SynTime's modifier types (PREFIX, SUFFIX) are clearly syntactic, its 15 time token types (e.g., MONTH, HOLIDAY, SEASON) are fundamentally semantic. Conversely, other systems' rules inherently contain syntactic information (e.g., word order). The key difference appears to be the *simplicity* and *positional nature* of SynTime's rules, not a fundamental shift from semantics to syntax. This oversimplified dichotomy weakens the paper's central theoretical argument.

3.  **Lack of Technical Detail in Rule Description:** The description of the heuristic rules, particularly for merging time segments (Section 4.2.3), is provided only in high-level prose. This makes the system difficult to reproduce precisely. For instance, the conditions for merging segments across a comma are quite specific but lack formal definition. Providing pseudocode or a more formal rule specification would significantly strengthen the paper's technical contribution and aid reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you quantify your system's reliance on SUTime's lexicon? What percentage of your initial 15 time token types and 5 modifier types are populated with regular expressions taken directly from SUTime? How does a version of SynTime perform if initialized with a more basic, generic lexicon (e.g., only containing months, weekdays, and basic time units)? This would help disentangle the contribution of your rule engine from the contribution of SUTime's mature lexicon.

2.  The distinction between syntactic and semantic types is a cornerstone of your paper's motivation. Could you provide a concrete example of a time expression that SUTime fails on due to its "semantic" typing, which SynTime correctly recognizes due to its "syntactic" typing? This would help clarify the practical impact of this philosophical difference.
