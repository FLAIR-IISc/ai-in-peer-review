### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in text. The method is motivated by an analysis of four datasets, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small set of keywords ("time tokens"), and exhibit regular syntactic patterns. SynTime defines a set of "syntactic types" for tokens (Time Token, Modifier, Numeral) and uses a simple, three-step process: (1) identify core time tokens using regexes and POS tags, (2) expand their boundaries to form "time segments" by greedily including adjacent modifiers and numerals, and (3) merge adjacent or overlapping segments to form the final time expressions. The system is evaluated on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) and shows strong performance, outperforming state-of-the-art rule-based and learning-based systems on TimeBank and Tweets, particularly in terms of recall.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A simple and effective rule-based method for time expression recognition.** The core contribution is the SynTime system itself, which demonstrates that a straightforward approach centered on identifying key "time tokens" and applying simple boundary expansion rules can be highly effective. This contrasts with more complex, hand-crafted rule systems.
2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation.** The paper validates its approach against powerful baselines across three diverse domains, including news, Wikipedia articles, and noisy social media text. The impressive results, especially the high recall and state-of-the-art performance on the Tweets dataset, convincingly demonstrate the method's robustness and practical value.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and well-motivated. The core idea of identifying anchor "time tokens" and then heuristically expanding their boundaries is intuitive and easy to implement. This simplicity makes the system transparent and interpretable, which is a significant advantage over complex machine learning models or intricate rule-based systems. The fact that such a simple approach achieves or surpasses state-of-the-art performance is a very strong result.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Analysis:** The experiments are thorough, comparing SynTime against both strong rule-based (HeidelTime, SUTime) and learning-based (UWTime) baselines on standard benchmarks. The system's consistently high recall across all datasets is a standout feature, confirming the hypothesis that identifying a core time token is sufficient to capture most expressions. The performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly impressive, showing a >11% F1 improvement over the next best system and highlighting the method's robustness to informal language.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The introductory analysis of time expressions across different corpora (Section 3) provides a clear, data-driven rationale for the design choices made in SynTime. Linking these observations to the Principle of Least Effort provides a nice theoretical framing for the work. The overall structure and clarity of the paper are commendable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Novelty of "Syntactic Types":** The paper heavily emphasizes its "syntactic type" system as a key point of novelty, contrasting it with the "semantic" types of other systems. However, this distinction feels somewhat superficial. Types like `PREFIX` (modifiers before time tokens) and `SUFFIX` (modifiers after) are defined by their syntactic position and function, which is precisely how rules are constructed in other systems like SUTime via `TokensRegex`. The core components of SUTime are also cascaded regular expressions over tokens and their attributes. The paper needs to provide a more detailed comparison to articulate what is fundamentally different and novel about its type system and rule formalism, beyond just the final implementation being simpler. The primary innovation appears to be the simple boundary-expansion heuristic rather than a fundamentally new kind of type system.
2.  **Dependence on SUTime for Initialization:** SynTime-I is initialized using token regular expressions collected directly from SUTime. This makes the direct comparison against SUTime somewhat complicated, as SynTime is effectively acting as a new, simpler rule engine built on top of SUTime's expertly curated lexicon. While this demonstrates the superiority of the new engine, it also means the performance is not independent of the baseline system. This dependency should be more clearly acknowledged and discussed. An ablation study showing performance with a lexicon built from scratch or another source would have helped isolate the contribution of the SynTime rule engine itself.
3.  **Lack of Detail on Heuristic Rules:** The description of the rules for boundary expansion and segment merging (Sections 4.2.2 and 4.2.3) is high-level and lacks the specificity needed for full reproducibility. For instance, the conditions for merging two adjacent, non-overlapping time segments (as in Figure 3b) are not explicitly stated. While the examples are helpful, the procedural logic of the rules is not fully described. Providing pseudocode or a more formal definition of the heuristic merging rules would significantly strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the novelty of "syntactic types," could you elaborate on the fundamental difference between your approach and the `TokensRegex` formalism used by SUTime? SUTime also defines rules over sequences of tokens based on their attributes and relative positions. Is the key contribution the simplicity of the boundary expansion algorithm itself, rather than a novel type system?
2.  The initial lexicon for SynTime is bootstrapped from SUTime. How critical is this specific lexicon to your system's performance? Have you experimented with building the initial set of token regexes from scratch (e.g., from the TimeBank training data) to better isolate the performance gains attributable to your rule engine?
3.  You claim that SynTime "runs in real time." Could you provide quantitative runtime performance figures (e.g., documents/second) and compare them to the baselines? This would provide concrete evidence for the efficiency benefits of your lightweight approach.
