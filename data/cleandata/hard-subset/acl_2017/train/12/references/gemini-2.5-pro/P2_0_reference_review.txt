### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing temporal expressions in text. The system's design is motivated by an analysis of four datasets, which concludes that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary of keywords, and exhibit consistent syntactic patterns, aligning with Zipf's principle of least effort. SynTime operates in three stages: it first identifies core "time tokens" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday'), then applies simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries of these tokens to include surrounding modifiers and numerals, forming "time segments." Finally, it merges adjacent or overlapping segments to form the complete time expressions. The authors evaluate SynTime on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset, showing that it outperforms strong baselines, including SUTime and HeidelTime, particularly in terms of recall.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Simple and Highly Effective Rule-Based Method:** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. It demonstrates that a lightweight, non-learning approach based on a simple "seed-and-expand" heuristic can achieve state-of-the-art performance, especially on informal text like tweets. Its high recall and efficiency are significant practical advantages.
2.  **Corpus Analysis and Motivation:** The paper provides a useful quantitative analysis of time expressions across varied corpora (news, Wikipedia, tweets). While the observations themselves (e.g., expressions are short) are not entirely new, framing them explicitly under the principle of least effort provides a solid and compelling motivation for the simplicity of their proposed method.
3.  **An Annotated Dataset for Tweets:** The authors contribute a manually annotated dataset of 942 tweets containing time expressions. While modest in size, this is a valuable resource for evaluating temporal taggers on noisy, informal social media text, a domain where many existing systems falter.

### Strengths
1.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The standout strength of this work is its strong experimental results. As shown in Table 4, SynTime significantly outperforms established rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a competitive learning-based system (UWTime) on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. Achieving an F1 of over 92% (strict) on TimeBank and over 91% (strict) on Tweets is a notable achievement.
2.  **Exceptionally High Recall:** The system consistently achieves the best recall across all datasets. For instance, on TimeBank, SynTime-I reaches 92.75% strict recall, compared to ~80% for the baselines. This suggests that its core strategy of identifying "time tokens" and expanding from there is extremely effective at not missing potential time expressions, a crucial quality for downstream applications.
3.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, intuitive, and described as "extremely light-weight" and running in "real time." In an era of increasingly large and computationally expensive models, demonstrating that a simple, heuristic-based approach can be highly competitive is a valuable contribution. This makes the system practical for real-world applications where speed and low resource usage are critical.
4.  **Robustness on Informal Text:** The system's performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly compelling. Many NLP systems trained on well-formed newswire text struggle with the brevity, noise, and non-standard language of tweets. SynTime's high F1 score demonstrates the robustness of its token-centric approach in this challenging domain.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Overstated Novelty and Unclear Distinction from SUTime:** The paper's primary weakness is its claim of methodological novelty, particularly in its distinction from SUTime. The authors claim SynTime uses "syntactic types" while others use "semantic types," but this distinction is not convincingly justified. SUTime's types (e.g., modifiers for frequency, relative time) also have clear syntactic roles (e.g., they tend to appear before or after a time unit). More critically, SynTime's core approach of identifying token types and applying expansion rules is conceptually very similar to SUTime's use of TokensRegex in a cascaded finite-state machine. The paper fails to clearly articulate what makes its "simple heuristic rules" for boundary expansion fundamentally different from or superior to SUTime's cascaded rules, beyond just being a simpler implementation.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on Existing Resources for Lexicon:** The paper states in Section 4.1 that "The token regular expressions for initializing SynTime are collected from SUTime." This is a major limitation that significantly tempers the paper's contributions. The system's high performance is likely due in large part to this comprehensive, high-quality lexicon bootstrapped directly from a strong baseline. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of SynTime's rule mechanism alone. The contribution is less about a new system built from first principles and more about a simplified rule engine applied to an existing, powerful lexicon.
3.  **Lack of Substantive Error Analysis:** The paper is missing a detailed analysis of its system's errors. The "Limitations" section briefly mentions upstream tokenization and POS tagging errors, which is a generic issue for nearly all NLP systems. A more insightful analysis would examine the types of false positives that lead to its slightly lower precision compared to its recall (e.g., on WikiWars). What kinds of non-temporal expressions does the boundary expansion heuristic incorrectly capture? Similarly, what are the characteristics of the few time expressions it fails to recall? Without this, it is difficult to understand the inherent limitations of the proposed approach.
4.  **Ambiguity in the Evaluation of SynTime-E:** The SynTime-E version is created by manually adding tokens found in the training data. However, for the benchmark datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars), which do not have standard train/test splits, it is unclear what "training text" was used for this expansion. Was the expansion performed on the test set itself? If so, this would represent a form of indirect overfitting and would invalidate the results for SynTime-E on those datasets. This needs to be clarified.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please elaborate on the key algorithmic difference between SynTime's heuristic boundary expansion and SUTime's cascaded approach using TokensRegex? Both seem to identify seed tokens and expand based on rules over token types. What specific limitations of the SUTime rule mechanism does your approach overcome?
2.  Given that the initial lexicon is taken directly from SUTime, could you provide some insight into how much of the system's performance is attributable to this high-quality lexicon versus your novel rule application mechanism? For instance, have you tried building a lexicon from scratch or a more basic one to isolate the contribution of your method?
3.  Could you please clarify the protocol used for expanding the lexicon for SynTime-E, particularly for the TimeBank and WikiWars datasets? Was the expansion performed using the same data that was used for testing, or was a separate development set held out?
