### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions from text. The authors begin by analyzing time expressions in four diverse datasets, observing that they tend to be short, use a small vocabulary, and are almost always anchored by an explicit "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Friday'). Motivated by these findings, which they connect to Zipf's principle of least effort, they design SynTime. The system operates by first identifying core time tokens in a sentence. It then applies simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries of these tokens to include surrounding modifiers and numerals, thereby forming complete time expressions. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset. The results show that SynTime achieves state-of-the-art performance, with particularly strong results on the informal text from Twitter.

**Contributions**

1.  **A simple and highly effective rule-based system for time expression recognition.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. Its design, centered on identifying anchor tokens and applying simple boundary-expansion heuristics, is shown to be more effective than more complex rule-based systems and competitive machine learning models, especially on noisy social media text.
2.  **An insightful, data-driven analysis of the properties of time expressions.** A secondary contribution is the clear analysis of time expressions across different corpora. The paper provides quantitative evidence for their structural properties (e.g., short length, reliance on a core vocabulary), which provides a strong and intuitive motivation for the system's design.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The most compelling argument for acceptance is the strength of the empirical results. SynTime significantly outperforms strong and widely-used baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly impressive, with an F1 score improvement of over 11 points in the strict match setting. This demonstrates the method's robustness and utility for challenging, informal domains where many NLP systems struggle.
2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and elegant. The core idea of identifying an anchor "time token" and then expanding its boundaries is intuitive, easy to understand, and likely efficient to implement. The authors' claim that the system is "extremely light-weight" and runs in "real time" represents a significant practical advantage over computationally intensive machine learning models or complex, deeply-cascaded rule systems. This work is a strong example of how a well-designed, simple heuristic approach can outperform more complex alternatives.
3.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The design of SynTime is clearly motivated by the preliminary analysis of four datasets. The observations that most time expressions are short and contain an explicit "time token" provide a direct and convincing justification for the system's architecture. This grounding in data analysis makes the proposed approach feel principled and well-founded.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Novelty Compared to Prior Rule-Based Systems:** The paper's main weakness lies in its positioning relative to existing rule-based systems, particularly SUTime. The authors claim a key difference is SynTime's use of "syntactic types" versus "semantic types" and "heuristic boundary expansion" versus "fixed rules." However, this distinction is not clearly established. SUTime is also a cascaded system that identifies individual words and then expands them into chunks, which sounds conceptually very similar to SynTime's process. Furthermore, many of SynTime's "Time Token" types (e.g., HOLIDAY, SEASON, MONTH) are defined by their semantic meaning, not by unique syntactic behavior, which blurs the claimed distinction. The paper needs to more precisely articulate the fundamental novelty of its rule engine.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on SUTime's Lexical Resources:** SynTime is initialized using token regular expressions collected directly from SUTime. This makes SynTime appear less like a fundamentally new system and more like a different, albeit more effective, rule engine built upon SUTime's existing knowledge base. This reliance complicates the interpretation of the results; it is difficult to tell whether the performance gains stem from a superior architectural design or simply a better-tuned set of heuristics applied to the same high-quality lexical resources. The contribution would be clearer if the resources were built from scratch or if the new rule logic could be isolated and evaluated more directly.
3.  **Incomplete Task Formulation:** The work focuses exclusively on the *recognition* of time expressions and omits the crucial step of *normalization* (i.e., resolving an expression like "last Tuesday" to a concrete date like '2023-10-17'). Most state-of-the-art systems, including the HeidelTime and SUTime baselines, perform both tasks as part of the standard temporal tagging pipeline. While the authors correctly state that normalization could be added later, its absence makes SynTime an incomplete solution and renders the comparison with full systems slightly less direct.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you further elaborate on the core algorithmic difference between SynTime's "boundary expansion" and the "cascaded" expansion used in systems like SUTime? Could you provide a concrete example of a time expression that SynTime correctly identifies but a system with a more rigid, cascaded finite-state approach (like SUTime) would fail on, and explain why your flexible expansion handles it better?
2.  Your initial lexical resources are borrowed from SUTime. This raises the question of how to attribute the impressive performance gains. How can you disentangle the contribution of your new rule engine from the contribution of SUTime's high-quality token lists? Have you considered any ablation studies to isolate the impact of your proposed rule mechanism?
3.  The paper emphasizes a distinction between your "syntactic" types and prior systems' "semantic" types. However, your core "Time Token" types (e.g., `YEAR`, `HOLIDAY`) appear to be defined by semantic categories. Could you clarify what specific syntactic behaviors (beyond broad POS categories) distinguish these types from one another, and how this represents a fundamental departure from how other systems categorize time-related words?
