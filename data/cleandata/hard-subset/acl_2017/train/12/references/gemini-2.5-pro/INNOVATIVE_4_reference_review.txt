### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The work is motivated by an analysis of time expressions across several corpora, which reveals that they are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and are anchored by a core "time token." Drawing inspiration from Zipf's principle of least effort, the authors propose a system that eschews the complexity of existing rule-based taggers. Instead of complex, fixed rules based on semantic types, SynTime defines a simpler system of "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) and applies simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules to identify time expressions. The system is shown to achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on three datasets, notably outperforming existing methods on TimeBank and a challenging Tweets dataset.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and bold simplification of rule-based time expression tagging.** The core contribution is the shift away from complex, cascaded rule systems towards a simple, intuitive model of identifying seed tokens and expanding their boundaries based on a small set of syntactic types. This presents a conceptually new direction for designing rule-based systems in this domain.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence that this simplified approach is highly effective.** The proposed SynTime system achieves excellent results, particularly in terms of recall, and outperforms more complex state-of-the-art systems on standard benchmarks. The performance on informal text (Tweets) is especially impressive.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its conceptual novelty. Rather than presenting another incremental improvement using complex machinery, the authors take a step back and propose a fundamentally simpler approach. The motivation from linguistic principles (Zipf's Law, inspiration from POS tagging) provides a refreshing and compelling narrative. The resulting system—identifying a core time token and heuristically expanding left and right—is elegant, intuitive, and a welcome departure from the opaque, handcrafted complexity of many rule-based systems. This is precisely the kind of bold, first-principles thinking that can push a field forward.

2.  **A Strong "Less is More" Argument:** The paper makes a bold claim that a much simpler, more flexible heuristic system can outperform more rigid and complex ones like SUTime or learning-based ones like UWTime. The excellent experimental results provide powerful backing for this claim. The fact that such a lightweight system can achieve state-of-the-art F1 scores on TimeBank and Tweets is a significant finding. It challenges the assumption that improved performance in this area must come from more intricate rules or more complex learning models.

3.  **Excellent Empirical Performance, Especially Recall:** The system's performance is impressive across the board. The consistently high recall on all three datasets demonstrates that the core insight—that most time expressions contain an identifiable time token—is sound and powerful. High recall is often the harder metric to achieve for rule-based systems without sacrificing precision, yet SynTime does so effectively. The exceptional results on the Tweets dataset, a notoriously difficult domain, further highlight the robustness and flexibility of the simple heuristic approach compared to more brittle systems.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few points that could be strengthened. I see these as minor issues that do not detract from the core contribution, but they are worth noting.

1.  **The Novelty of the "Syntactic Types" May Be Overstated:** The paper frames its novelty around a new "syntactic type system" in contrast to the "semantic" types of other systems. While types like `PREFIX` and `SUFFIX` are clearly syntactic, other types like `HOLIDAY` or `SEASON` appear just as semantic as those in other systems. The truly innovative element seems to be less the type system itself and more the *simplicity of the rules* that operate on these types (i.e., the boundary expansion heuristic). The paper would be clearer if it focused more on the novelty of the simple, position-aware rule engine rather than claiming a wholly new paradigm of typing.

2.  **Dependence on SUTime for Initialization:** The SynTime system is initialized with token regular expressions collected from SUTime. While a pragmatic choice, this makes it difficult to fully disentangle the performance contribution of SynTime's novel rule engine from the contribution of SUTime's high-quality, manually curated lexicons. It leaves open the question of how much of the system's success is owed to its new architecture versus the borrowed vocabulary.

3.  **Weaker Performance on WikiWars:** The system's performance on the WikiWars dataset is less impressive than on the others, only becoming competitive after domain-specific expansion. The authors correctly note that this is likely due to more descriptive and preposition-heavy expressions not covered by TimeML standards. However, this also suggests a potential limitation of the simple heuristic model: it may struggle with more complex, compositional phenomena where a simple seed-and-expand approach is insufficient. While I appreciate the focus on a new direction, this does point to a potential ceiling on the approach's capabilities.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the principled distinction between your "syntactic" types and the "semantic" types in a system like SUTime? Is the key difference that your *rules* for combining types are purely syntactic (positional), even if some of the types themselves (e.g., `HOLIDAY`) are defined by their semantics?

2.  To help isolate the contribution of your novel rule engine, could you provide any ablation or analysis on the impact of bootstrapping from SUTime's lexicons? For instance, how would the system perform if initialized with a much smaller, independently created set of core time tokens? This would help clarify whether the power comes from the rules, the lexicon, or the combination.
