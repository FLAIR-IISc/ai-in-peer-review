### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition. The method is motivated by an analysis of four corpora, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain at least one core "time token". Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main "syntactic" types of tokens (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses a simple, two-stage heuristic process: first, it identifies core time tokens, and second, it expands their boundaries to include adjacent modifiers and numerals. The paper's primary contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  A simple and effective heuristic-based method for time expression recognition that achieves very strong empirical results, outperforming several state-of-the-art systems on standard benchmarks.
2.  An analysis of the structural properties of time expressions across different corpora, which provides the motivation for the proposed simplified approach.
3.  A new, manually annotated dataset of time expressions in tweets, which is a valuable resource for a challenging domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The most significant strength of this work is its performance. SynTime achieves state-of-the-art results on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets, significantly outperforming strong rule-based and learning-based baselines. The reported F1 scores, particularly the high recall across all datasets, demonstrate the effectiveness of the simple boundary-expansion approach. This is a compelling result for a system that is presented as being lightweight and simple.

2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and, according to the authors, extremely efficient ("runs in real time"). In an era of increasingly large and computationally expensive models, a simple, transparent, and high-performing rule-based system is a valuable contribution. The idea of simplifying complex rule sets into a general boundary-expansion heuristic is elegant and appears to work very well in practice.

3.  **New Community Resource:** The creation and release of a manually annotated dataset for time expressions on Twitter is a useful contribution. This domain is characterized by informal and abbreviated language, making it a challenging testbed for NLP systems. This dataset will be valuable for future research in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility:** The description of the core algorithm is insufficiently detailed. The boundary expansion and segment merging rules (Sections 4.2.2 and 4.2.3) are described only in high-level prose. Crucial details—such as the exact search-stopping conditions and the full logic for merging segments—are not specified with the precision required for reproduction. The absence of pseudocode or a formal grammar makes it impossible for other researchers to re-implement the method faithfully. This lack of rigor extends to the initial analysis; for instance, the process for "manually normalizing" tokens in Observation 2 is not described, and the POS analysis in Observation 4 is confusingly explained.

2.  **Biased Evaluation on the Tweets Dataset:** The methodology for creating the Tweets test set is seriously flawed. The authors first used SUTime to find tweets that likely contain time expressions and then manually annotated this pre-filtered set. This introduces a strong selection bias, as the test set is unnaturally enriched with expressions that a SUTime-like system can easily find. It is therefore unsurprising that SynTime—another rule-based system that borrows its initial lexicon from SUTime—achieves "exceptionally good performance." The claims of superiority on this dataset are significantly undermined by this methodological choice, as the evaluation was not conducted on a representative sample of Twitter data.

3.  **Overstated Novelty and Unclear Distinction from Prior Work:** The paper positions itself as novel by contrasting its "syntactic" types with the "semantic" types of systems like SUTime. However, this distinction is not clearly substantiated and feels somewhat superficial, especially given that SynTime's initial token lists and regular expressions are admittedly "collected from SUTime". The core novelty seems to be a simplification and re-application of SUTime's components into a different heuristic framework, rather than a fundamentally new approach based on truly syntactic principles. The contribution is more one of engineering and simplification than of conceptual innovation.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Formatting Errors:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and typos (e.g., "Time expression recognition main involves two", "Particulaly", "recoGinize"). More egregiously, the entire references section is malformed, filled with BibTeX artifacts like question marks, backslashes, and extraneous tags ("Alonso et al\.,?", "\Q2011\E"). This demonstrates a profound lack of attention to detail and care in the preparation of the manuscript, which falls far below the expected standards for a top-tier conference. Such sloppiness makes the paper difficult to read and erodes confidence in the rigor of the work itself.

### Questions to Authors

1.  To improve reproducibility, could you please provide pseudocode or a more formal specification for the time segment identification and time expression extraction algorithms described in Section 4.2?

2.  Regarding the Tweets dataset: Do you acknowledge that pre-filtering the data with SUTime likely biased the test set in favor of your approach? Have you analyzed SynTime's performance on the 15 expressions found in the 500 randomly sampled tweets that SUTime missed? While a small sample, this could provide a more realistic picture of your system's performance on the difficult cases that challenge existing systems.

3.  Could you provide a more concrete example to illustrate the practical difference between your "syntactic" modifier types (e.g., PREFIX) and SUTime's "semantic" ones (e.g., frequency modifiers)? Given that the underlying token lists are the same, does this recategorization lead to fundamentally different recognition behavior, or is it primarily a simplification that groups multiple semantic types into one positional type?
