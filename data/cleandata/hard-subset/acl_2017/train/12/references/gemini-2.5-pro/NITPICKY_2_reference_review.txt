**Summary of the paper**

The paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis on four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and observe that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and almost always contain a "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'year'). Based on these observations, they design a system that first identifies core time tokens and then applies simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries to include surrounding modifiers and numerals. The main claimed novelty is the use of "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) for tokens, which they contrast with the "semantic types" of previous rule-based systems. The system is evaluated on three datasets and is shown to outperform several state-of-the-art baselines, particularly on a newly annotated Twitter dataset.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is an extremely lightweight and efficient rule-based time expression tagger that achieves strong empirical results, especially on informal text (Tweets), where it shows a substantial improvement over existing systems.
2.  The paper provides a useful corpus analysis of time expressions across different genres, offering quantitative support for the intuition that such expressions are concise and follow predictable patterns.
3.  The authors introduce a conceptual framing of "syntactic types" and a boundary expansion mechanism, which, while not entirely novel, represents a simplification of the complex, cascaded rule systems often found in prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The system's performance, particularly the high recall across all datasets and the state-of-the-art F1 scores on TimeBank and Tweets, is impressive. The gains on the Tweets dataset are especially significant and suggest the method is robust to informal, short-form text, a challenging domain for NLP.
2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and, being rule-based without a complex cascade, is extremely fast ("runs in real time"). This is a valuable practical characteristic, making the system potentially more useful for real-world applications than heavier, learning-based models or more intricate rule-based systems.
3.  **Clear Motivation:** The initial data analysis in Section 3 provides a clear and compelling motivation for the system's design. The observations that most time expressions are short and anchored by a time token directly inform the proposed "identify-then-expand" strategy.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated Novelty of "Syntactic Types":** The paper's central claim to novelty—the distinction between its "syntactic types" and the "semantic types" of other systems like SUTime—is not convincingly substantiated. Defining a modifier type like `PREFIX` based on its position relative to a time token is fundamentally a syntactic definition, but systems like SUTime also implicitly or explicitly rely on such syntactic regularities. For example, SUTime's rules for frequency modifiers (e.g., 'every', 'daily') are inherently tied to their syntactic positions. The paper presents this as a paradigm shift, but it appears to be more of a re-labeling or a change in emphasis rather than a fundamentally new approach to rule design. The claim that "Other rule-based taggers define types for tokens based on their semantic meaning" is an oversimplification that weakens the core argument.
2.  **Lack of Methodological Precision and Reproducibility:** The description of the core algorithm is vague. The "simple heuristic rules" for time segment identification and merging are described only in prose, which leaves many ambiguities. For instance, the conditions for merging segments around a `COMMA` (Section 4.2.3) seem ad-hoc and are not justified. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more formal description of the rules, perhaps using pseudocode or a formal grammar. Furthermore, the process of initializing SynTime by "collecting" tokens from SUTime is opaque, as is the manual expansion process for SynTime-E, which is described with a subjective rule ("cannot cause ambiguity and should be generic"). This lack of detail hinders reproducibility.
3.  **Questionable Evaluation Practices:**
    *   **Baseline Performance:** The performance reported for baselines on the Tweets dataset seems suspiciously low. The F1 scores for SUTime (76.99) and UWTime (79.44) are drastically lower than SynTime's (91.74). This raises concerns about whether the baselines were properly configured or adapted for tweet-specific phenomena (e.g., tokenization). Without such adaptation, the comparison is not entirely fair, especially since SynTime-E was explicitly expanded using this data.
    *   **Dataset Bias:** The new Tweets dataset was constructed by first using SUTime to find tweets containing time expressions. This methodology introduces a selection bias, favoring expressions that SUTime can already identify. While the authors performed a small manual check on the remainder, this does not eliminate the possibility that the final dataset is skewed towards more canonical time expressions, potentially inflating the performance of a pattern-based system like SynTime.
    *   **Omission of Gigaword:** The paper analyzes the Gigaword dataset in Section 3 but then excludes it from the experiments, citing that the labels "may not be the ground truth." This is a weak justification, as this dataset was a standard part of the TempEval-3 shared task. Its exclusion, coupled with the introduction of a new, potentially biased dataset, could be perceived as cherry-picking evaluation venues.
4.  **Poor Presentation and Attention to Detail:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and formatting inconsistencies.
    *   Grammar: "Time expression recognition main involves two kinds of methods...", "SynTime recognizing time expression includes three main steps...".
    *   Formatting: Footnote 3 is just a quote that should be integrated into the main text. Figure 3 is critical to understanding the method, but its components (`s1`, `s2`, `e1`) are not defined.
    *   Bibliography: The reference list is messy, with stray question marks, inconsistent capitalization, and incorrect author formatting (e.g., "Strotgen" instead of "Strötgen"). This lack of polish detracts from the paper's scientific credibility and suggests a rushed submission.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the claimed distinction between your "syntactic types" and the types used in systems like SUTime? How is defining a `PREFIX` class fundamentally different from defining a class of, for example, `RelativeTimeModifier` (like 'next', 'last') which also has a clear syntactic role?
2.  The performance of baseline systems on your new Tweets dataset is substantially lower than that of SynTime. Can you provide details on the setup for these baselines? Specifically, did you use tweet-specific tokenizers or make any other adaptations that might be necessary for these systems to perform well on this domain?
3.  The creation of the Tweets dataset was seeded by SUTime. How can you be certain that this did not introduce a significant bias towards simpler, more regular time expressions that your system is inherently good at, thereby inflating the performance gap against the baselines?
