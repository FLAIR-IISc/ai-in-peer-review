### Summary
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors first conduct an analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), making four key observations: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary of keywords, almost always contain a core "time token", and their constituent words exhibit consistent syntactic patterns. Motivated by these findings, particularly the last one, they propose a system built on "syntactic types". The method defines three main categories of types—Time Token, Modifier, and Numeral—which are populated with regular expressions over tokens, largely sourced from the existing SUTime system. The recognition process involves three steps: 1) identifying Time Tokens using the predefined lists and POS tag filtering, 2) expanding these tokens into "time segments" by heuristically searching for adjacent Modifiers and Numerals, and 3) transforming and merging these segments into final time expressions. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets, demonstrating superior or competitive performance, especially in terms of recall and on informal text from Twitter.

### Contributions
1.  **A Simple and Effective Rule-Based Method:** The primary contribution is SynTime, a time expression recognition system that uses a small set of token types and simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules. Despite its architectural simplicity, the method achieves state-of-the-art performance, particularly in recall, suggesting that complex, cascaded rule systems may not be necessary for this task.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The paper provides a useful analysis of the properties of time expressions across different corpora (news, Wikipedia, social media). The observations that time expressions are short, lexically constrained, and anchored by specific time-related keywords provide strong motivation for the proposed approach and offer valuable insights for future work in this area.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on Informal Text:** The evaluation demonstrates the system's exceptional performance on a manually annotated dataset of Tweets. It significantly outperforms strong baselines on this challenging, informal domain, highlighting the robustness of its simple, high-recall approach.

### Strengths
1.  **High Empirical Performance:** The proposed method, in both its initial (SynTime-I) and expanded (SynTime-E) versions, achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on the evaluated datasets. It significantly outperforms all baselines on TimeBank and Tweets in F1 score. This strong empirical performance is a compelling argument for the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Excellent Recall:** A standout result is the system's consistently high recall across all datasets and evaluation settings (strict and relaxed). For example, it achieves 92.75% strict recall and 95.65% relaxed recall on TimeBank, and over 94% strict recall on Tweets. High recall is critical for many downstream applications where failing to identify a temporal expression is a significant error. The method's design, which seeds the search with high-coverage time tokens, directly contributes to this strength.
3.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The core logic of the system—identifying seed tokens and applying simple left/right expansion rules—is conceptually simple and transparent compared to complex cascaded finite-state automata or machine learning models. The authors claim it is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time," which, if true, makes it an attractive and practical tool. The simplicity also aids in error analysis and extensibility.
4.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The design of SynTime is directly and clearly motivated by the preliminary analysis of time expression characteristics. The four observations presented in Section 3 provide a logical foundation for a strategy that prioritizes identifying core time tokens and then expanding their boundaries, justifying the architectural choices made.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Unclear Novelty of "Syntactic Types":** The paper's central claim is that it introduces a novel approach based on "syntactic types" as opposed to the "semantic types" of other systems like SUTime. This distinction is not clearly substantiated. The paper defines modifier types like `PREFIX` and `SUFFIX` based on their position relative to a time token, but this is a syntactic property inherent in the rules of any system. SUTime also uses types like `MODIFIER` or `REL_MOD` which are applied with positional constraints. The actual token lists for SynTime's types appear to be grouped by a mix of semantic and functional criteria, much like other rule-based systems. The contribution seems to be a re-organization or simplification of type granularity rather than a fundamentally new "syntactic" paradigm.
2.  **Dependence on an Existing State-of-the-Art System:** SynTime is initialized using token regular expressions directly collected from SUTime. This heavy reliance makes it difficult to disentangle the contribution of SynTime's novel rule engine from the contribution of SUTime's comprehensive, manually curated token lists. The strong performance could be largely attributed to leveraging SUTime's knowledge base. The paper lacks an ablation study or a direct comparison that would isolate the impact of its heuristic expansion rules versus SUTime's rule mechanism when both start with the identical set of token definitions.
3.  **Ambiguity Handling and Rule Robustness:** The system's method for handling ambiguity appears simplistic. For example, disambiguating words like "May" relies solely on a POS tagger, and the paper notes that POS errors lead to system errors. It does not discuss more complex contexts where POS tags may be insufficient. Furthermore, the rules for merging time segments (Section 4.2.3), such as the three conditions for merging across a comma, seem specific and potentially brittle. It is unclear how this small set of heuristic rules would generalize to more complex and varied linguistic constructions not well-represented in the test sets.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you further elaborate on the conceptual distinction between SynTime's "syntactic types" and the types used in systems like SUTime? For example, SUTime's rules also implicitly encode syntactic behavior (e.g., a relative modifier must precede a time unit). What makes SynTime's type definition fundamentally syntactic in a way that others' are not?
2.  Since SynTime's initial token lists are derived from SUTime, can you provide a more direct comparison or analysis to demonstrate the superiority of your heuristic boundary-expansion rules over SUTime's cascaded finite automata? For instance, are there specific classes of time expressions that SynTime correctly identifies but SUTime fails on (like the "year 1" example mentioned), and if so, how significant are these cases in the evaluation datasets?
3.  The manual expansion process (for SynTime-E) seems crucial for adapting to new domains like WikiWars. Could you provide more detail on the effort and expertise required for this process? How would you expect the system to perform on a completely new domain without this manual tuning, and how scalable is this manual annotation step?
