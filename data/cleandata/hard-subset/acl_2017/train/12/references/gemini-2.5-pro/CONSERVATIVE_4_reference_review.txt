### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The method is motivated by an analysis of four datasets, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and nearly always contain a "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Tuesday'). Based on these observations, the authors define a system of "syntactic types" for tokens (time token, modifier, numeral) and apply simple, heuristic rules to identify time expressions. The core algorithm first identifies time tokens and then expands their boundaries to include adjacent modifiers and numerals. The system is evaluated on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new manually-annotated Tweets dataset, where it is shown to achieve performance that is competitive with or superior to established baselines like HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

### Contributions

1.  **A simplified, effective rule-based system for time expression recognition.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. Its main novelty lies in eschewing complex, fixed grammars in favor of a simpler, more flexible boundary-expansion heuristic. This approach proves to be highly effective, particularly in achieving high recall.
2.  **An empirical analysis of the structural properties of time expressions.** The paper provides a useful quantification of common intuitions about time expressions (e.g., they are short, use a limited vocabulary) across several domains. This analysis serves as a sound motivation for the design of their simplified system.
3.  **A new annotated dataset for time expressions in tweets.** The authors have created and used a manually labeled dataset of 942 tweets, which is a valuable resource for a domain where standard systems often struggle.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method demonstrates very strong results, particularly on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets, where it surpasses the F1 scores of all three established baselines. The system's ability to achieve state-of-the-art recall (over 92% relaxed recall on all datasets) is a significant achievement and validates its core design of identifying anchor "time tokens" and expanding from there.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The core mechanism of boundary expansion around identified time tokens is conceptually simple and elegant. Compared to the complex, cascaded finite-state grammars often used in other rule-based systems (e.g., SUTime), SynTime's approach appears easier to understand, implement, and potentially maintain. This simplicity, combined with its high performance, is a commendable aspect of the work.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation Setup:** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation against strong and relevant baselines, including both top-performing rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a well-regarded learning-based system (UWTime). The evaluation across three distinct domains (news, Wikipedia, social media) provides a comprehensive view of the system's capabilities and limitations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Novelty of "Syntactic Types":** The paper frames its approach as a paradigm shift from "semantic" to "syntactic" types. This distinction feels largely terminological rather than fundamental. Existing rule-based systems like SUTime heavily rely on syntactic patterns and relative positions (e.g., a rule for "last" applies before a time unit). The proposed `PREFIX` and `SUFFIX` types are essentially a formalization of this existing concept. The method is an incremental refinement and simplification of existing rule-based paradigms, not a fundamentally new approach.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on an Existing State-of-the-Art System:** A critical detail mentioned in Section 4.1 is that SynTime is initialized using token regular expressions collected directly from SUTime. This means a significant portion of the system's lexical knowledge and power is borrowed from one of its key baselines. The experiments do not provide an ablation study to isolate the performance gains from the novel heuristic rule engine versus the gains from using SUTime's mature and comprehensive token lists. This dependency makes it difficult to assess the true standalone contribution of the proposed rule mechanism.
3.  **Ad-Hoc and Unreproducible Expansion Process:** The creation of the expanded version, SynTime-E, relies on a manual process of adding new keywords based on subjective criteria like being "generic" and not causing "ambiguity." This process lacks scientific rigor and is not reproducible. For instance, the authors added 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers to improve performance on WikiWars, but the principles governing these specific additions are not clearly articulated. This makes the improved results from SynTime-E feel like dataset-specific tuning rather than a generalizable improvement.
4.  **Inconsistent Performance Generalization:** While the results on TimeBank and Tweets are excellent, the system's performance on the WikiWars dataset is notably weaker. In the strict match setting, both SynTime-I and SynTime-E achieve lower F1 scores than the HeidelTime and UWTime baselines. The authors attribute this to "descriptive time expressions," but this precisely highlights a limitation of their simple heuristic approach. It suggests the system may be less robust on domains that deviate from the most common temporal patterns, challenging the claim of general applicability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you conduct an ablation study to quantify the contribution of your heuristic rule system? Specifically, what is the performance of SynTime if its initial token lists are built from a more basic resource (e.g., a simple list of months and weekdays) rather than being imported from a sophisticated system like SUTime? This would help clarify how much of the performance is attributable to the rule engine versus the borrowed lexical resources.
2.  Can you provide a more formal set of principles or a clearer methodology for the manual expansion process that created SynTime-E? Without this, the results of SynTime-E are difficult to interpret and the process cannot be replicated. For example, what specific linguistic criteria were used to select the 35 new terms for the WikiWars dataset while excluding others?
