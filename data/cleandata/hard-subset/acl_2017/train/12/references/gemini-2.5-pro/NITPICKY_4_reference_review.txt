**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The work is motivated by a linguistic analysis of four corpora, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain at least one core "time token". Based on these observations, the authors propose a system that first identifies core time tokens and then applies simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules to include adjacent modifiers and numerals. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  An empirical analysis of time expression characteristics across news, Wikipedia, and social media text, which provides a solid, data-driven motivation for a simple, token-centric recognition approach.
2.  The SynTime system itself, a lightweight and efficient rule-based tagger that relies on a simple syntactic type system and boundary-expansion heuristics, rather than complex, fixed-length patterns.
3.  A strong set of experimental results on three datasets, showing that this simple approach achieves very high recall and outperforms several state-of-the-art systems, especially on informal text (Tweets).

**Strengths:**
1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its results. SynTime achieves state-of-the-art or superior F1 scores on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets, outperforming established rule-based systems like SUTime and HeidelTime, as well as the learning-based UWTime. The system's consistently high recall across all datasets is particularly impressive and validates the core design principle of anchoring recognition on identifiable "time tokens".
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is refreshingly simple and intuitive. The idea of defining a small set of token types (time token, modifier, numeral) and then using straightforward boundary-expansion rules is elegant. This simplicity leads to a system that is transparent, easily debuggable, and, as the authors claim, extremely fast, which is a significant practical advantage over more complex rule systems or computationally intensive machine learning models.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The design of SynTime is grounded in a thoughtful preliminary analysis of four datasets. The observations about the conciseness of time expressions and the near-ubiquity of a central time token provide a compelling justification for the system's architecture. This data-driven motivation is a welcome departure from ad-hoc rule engineering.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Unacceptable Level of Presentation Quality:** The paper is marred by a significant number of errors that severely undermine its credibility. Multiple figures (1, 2, 3) and a table (1) are referenced in the text but are not included in the paper. This is a critical omission, as these figures are essential for understanding the length distribution of time expressions, the system overview, and the crucial segment-merging logic. The bibliography is completely malformed, containing stray LaTeX-like commands, inconsistent entry formats, and repeated citations. Furthermore, the text contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "Time expression recognition main involves...", "are quite simple that it makes SynTime..."). This lack of attention to detail is inexcusable for a scientific publication.
2.  **Vague Methodological Details and Subjectivity:** While the high-level concept is clear, key details of the methodology are either opaque or subjective. The initialization of the system's lexicon from SUTime is a critical detail, but the extent of this borrowing is not quantified, making it difficult to disentangle the contribution of the new heuristic rules from the contribution of SUTime's comprehensive, manually-curated lexicon. The "SynTime Expansion" process is described as a manual annotation task governed by the vague principle that added words "can not cause ambiguity and should be generic." This lacks scientific rigor and reproducibility.
3.  **Oversimplification of Related Problems and Novelty:** The paper dismisses the non-trivial task of time expression normalization in a single sentence, claiming it can be "achieved by using methods similar to the existing rule systems." This is a significant hand-wave that ignores the complexity of the problem and the fact that recognition and normalization are often tightly coupled. Additionally, the claimed novelty of using "syntactic" types versus "semantic" types feels overstated; it can be viewed as simply a different level of abstraction in rule design rather than a fundamental paradigm shift.
4.  **Potential Experimental Bias:** The newly created Tweets dataset was curated by pre-filtering a large corpus using SUTime to find tweets containing time expressions. Although the authors performed a small-scale manual check to estimate SUTime's miss rate, this process inherently biases the dataset towards expressions that SUTime can already identify. This may artificially inflate the performance of their own system, which is bootstrapped from SUTime's lexicon, and may not reflect performance on a truly unbiased sample of tweets.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Can you please provide the missing Figures 1, 2, 3 and Table 1? It is impossible to properly evaluate your analysis of time expression length, your system's architecture, and your core merging logic without them.
2.  Could you provide a more formal, reproducible methodology for the manual "SynTime Expansion" process? What specific guidelines are used to determine if a word is "generic" and does not "cause ambiguity"?
3.  To clarify the contribution of your heuristic rules versus the inherited lexicon, could you quantify what percentage of the time expression tokens identified by SynTime-I in your experiments were matched using the specific lexicons borrowed from SUTime, versus being identified by your more general NUMERAL and modifier types?
4.  The paper argues that SynTime outperforms SUTime because SUTime's rules are too "fixed" (e.g., recognizing '1 year' but not 'year 1'). Given that SynTime's lexicon is derived from SUTime, does this imply that SUTime's *lexicon* is comprehensive but its *compositional rules* are flawed? Could you provide a more systematic error analysis beyond this single example?
