### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing temporal expressions (timex). The method is motivated by a quantitative analysis of timex across four datasets, which finds that they are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and are anchored by a core "time token." Based on these observations, SynTime operates on a simple heuristic: first, it identifies core time tokens (e.g., 'month', 'Monday') using lexicons; second, it expands the boundaries of these tokens to include surrounding modifiers and numerals based on their "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX). The authors contrast this with existing systems like SUTime, which they argue use more complex "semantic types." The system is evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets dataset, where it achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A simple and highly effective heuristic-based method for timex recognition.** The core idea of identifying an anchor "time token" and expanding its boundaries is intuitive and shown to be very powerful, achieving excellent performance (especially high recall) while remaining computationally efficient.
2.  **A useful analysis of timex characteristics across different genres.** The empirical observations in Section 3, while confirming some expert intuitions, are well-quantified and provide a solid motivation for the proposed approach.
3.  **Strong empirical results on informal text.** The system's outstanding performance on the new Tweets dataset demonstrates its robustness and highlights the effectiveness of the simple heuristic approach for noisy, short-form text where complex linguistic structures are less common.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Efficiency.** The method is elegant in its simplicity. The "identify anchor, expand boundary" heuristic is easy to understand and implement, a welcome contrast to increasingly complex models. The authors' claim that it is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time" is a significant practical advantage for many real-world applications.

2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance.** The experimental results are very strong. SynTime achieves state-of-the-art F1 scores on TimeBank and Tweets, significantly outperforming well-established systems. The consistently high recall across all datasets is particularly impressive and validates the core hypothesis that most timex are built around an identifiable time token.

3.  **Well-Motivated Approach.** The design of SynTime is clearly and logically derived from the data analysis presented in Section 3. The four observations provide a compelling rationale for why a simple, token-type-based expansionary approach is well-suited for this task. Linking these empirical findings to a broader linguistic principle (Zipf's law of least effort) adds a nice theoretical grounding to the work.

4.  **Clear Exposition.** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly with helpful examples (Figure 3), and the distinction the authors draw between their approach and prior work is well-articulated, making the paper's positioning and claimed novelty easy to grasp.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Novelty of "Syntactic Types" is Overstated.** The paper frames its key novelty as the distinction between its "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX) and the "semantic types" of other systems like SUTime. However, this distinction feels more rhetorical than fundamental. Any rule-based pattern matcher, including SUTime, inherently encodes syntactic position. A rule that matches `[every] [day]` implicitly treats 'every' as a prefix. The primary novelty of SynTime appears to be in the *simplification of the rule engine* to a generic boundary expansion heuristic, rather than a fundamentally new way of categorizing tokens. The system is less a new paradigm and more a very effective simplification and generalization of existing rule-based principles.

2.  **Heavy and Under-acknowledged Reliance on SUTime.** The paper states that the initial lexicons for SynTime are "collected from SUTime." This is a critical detail that significantly impacts the assessment of the work's contribution. The high recall of SynTime is likely due in large part to the comprehensive, manually curated lexicons developed over years for the SUTime project. The contribution is thus less about building a new timex system from analysis and more about designing a simpler, faster rule engine that operates on SUTime's existing knowledge base. This dependency should be more prominently discussed, and an ablation study would be valuable to disentangle the performance gains from the rule engine versus the borrowed lexicons.

3.  **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis.** While the aggregate performance metrics are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative error analysis. The simple heuristics are a strength, but they must have limitations. For instance, how does the system handle more complex, long-range dependencies or ambiguous conjunctions (e.g., "Monday and Tuesday of next week")? Discussing specific failure cases would provide a much clearer picture of the method's boundaries and offer better insights for future work. The only limitation mentioned—dependency on correct tokenization and POS tagging—is generic and applies to nearly all NLP systems.

4.  **The "Expansion" Protocol for SynTime-E is Unclear and Potentially Flawed.** The authors evaluate an "expanded" version (SynTime-E) where new tokens are manually added after observing them in "training text." For the TimeBank and WikiWars datasets, which are standard evaluation benchmarks without official training splits, this implies development on the test set. If tokens were added after observing them in the evaluation data, the reported SynTime-E results are not comparable to the baselines and would be considered an unfair evaluation. The authors must clarify the exact protocol used for this expansion to ensure the validity of their claims.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the experimental protocol for creating SynTime-E for the TimeBank and WikiWars datasets? Specifically, what data was used to identify the new tokens for expansion (listed in Table 5)? Using the test sets for this purpose would inflate performance, so a precise description of the data hygiene is needed.

2.  Given the heavy reliance on SUTime's lexicons, could you help contextualize the contribution of your new rule engine? For example, can you provide an intuition or, if possible, an experiment that isolates the impact of your simplified boundary-expansion rules from the impact of using SUTime's comprehensive, pre-existing knowledge base?

3.  Could you provide some examples of complex time expressions that SynTime fails to correctly identify, and explain how the simple boundary expansion heuristic leads to these errors? This would help in understanding the specific limitations of your proposed approach.
