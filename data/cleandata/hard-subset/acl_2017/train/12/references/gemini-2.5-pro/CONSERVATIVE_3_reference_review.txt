### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in text. The method is motivated by an analysis of four corpora, which concludes that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and nearly always contain a core "time token." Based on these observations, the authors design a system that first identifies these core time tokens and then applies simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries to include surrounding modifiers and numerals. The authors frame their main contribution as a novel approach using "syntactic types" (e.g., based on a token's position relative to a time token) as opposed to the "semantic types" used in prior work. The system is evaluated on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset, where it is shown to outperform or be competitive with established baselines like HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **An effective and lightweight rule-based tagger:** The paper presents a simple and efficient method for time expression recognition that achieves strong empirical results, particularly a high recall, across three different datasets, including challenging social media text.
2.  **A new annotated dataset for time expressions in tweets:** The authors have created and manually annotated a dataset of 942 tweets containing time expressions, which is a useful resource for evaluating systems on informal text.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its results. The proposed system, even in its initial un-expanded form (SynTime-I), reports a substantial improvement in F1-score over well-established systems on the TimeBank (+12.5 over SUTime) and Tweets (+14.7 over SUTime) datasets. Achieving over 90% F1 on these datasets with a simple, non-learning-based method is impressive and suggests the approach is effective.
2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, relying on a small set of token types and straightforward boundary expansion heuristics. This contrasts with more complex cascaded finite-state automata (SUTime) or CCG grammars (UWTime). This simplicity likely leads to high efficiency ("runs in real time"), which is a significant practical advantage for a component often used in larger NLP pipelines.
3.  **Effectiveness on Informal Text:** The system's performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly strong. Many NLP systems trained on newswire text suffer a significant performance drop when applied to social media. SynTime's high F1 score (91.74 strict) demonstrates its robustness and suitability for this domain, which is a valuable finding.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated Novelty and Misleading Framing:** The central claim of a novel system based on "syntactic types" is not well-supported and seems to be a matter of framing rather than a fundamental methodological shift. Firstly, the distinction between "syntactic" and "semantic" types is tenuous; many of the defined "time token" types like `MONTH`, `HOLIDAY`, and `SEASON` are clearly semantic in nature. Secondly, the system's strength appears to derive heavily from its lexicon, which the authors explicitly state is collected from SUTime. This makes the contribution less about a new paradigm and more about a different, arguably simpler, rule engine built upon the lexical resources of a prior state-of-the-art system. The work should be positioned more transparently as an effective simplification or re-engineering of existing rule-based approaches.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Ablation:** The paper lacks a deep analysis of *why* its simple heuristics outperform the more complex, manually engineered rules of systems like SUTime and HeidelTime. The performance gap on TimeBank is particularly large and warrants more than an anecdotal example (`1 year` vs. `year 1`). A thorough error analysis comparing SynTime and SUTime is needed to understand what categories of expressions each system fails on. Without this, it is difficult to determine if the proposed heuristics are genuinely superior or if SUTime's implementation has specific, addressable flaws that SynTime happens to avoid.
3.  **Ambiguity in the Expansion Methodology:** The creation of the expanded version, SynTime-E, lacks rigor. The process is described as a manual addition of keywords from "training text" based on subjective criteria like not causing ambiguity. For the benchmark datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars), it is critically important to know what data was used as this "training text." If the standard test partitions were consulted in any way to derive these new rules/keywords, the evaluation would be invalid. This procedure needs to be specified with much greater clarity to ensure reproducibility and soundness of the evaluation.
4.  **Limited Scope of the Task:** The paper focuses exclusively on time expression recognition, setting aside the equally challenging task of normalization. While this is a valid decision to limit scope, leading systems like HeidelTime and SUTime handle both. The claim that normalization could be easily added is an assertion without proof. The evaluation thus compares a component of a system against more complete systems, which should be acknowledged as a limitation.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact data-splitting protocol used for the SynTime-E experiments on the TimeBank and WikiWars datasets? Specifically, what data was designated as "training text" for identifying new keywords to add, and how did you ensure no information from the test set influenced this manual expansion process?
2.  The paper argues for a novel distinction between your "syntactic types" and the "semantic types" of prior work. However, many of your types (`HOLIDAY`, `TIME_UNIT`, etc.) are defined by their meaning. Could you provide a more rigorous definition of this distinction and explain why this re-categorization constitutes a fundamental shift in methodology?
3.  Given that SynTime is initialized with SUTime's lexicon, the dramatic performance improvement on TimeBank is surprising. Could you provide a more systematic error analysis? For example, what are the top 3-5 categories of time expressions that SynTime correctly identifies but SUTime misses on the TimeBank dataset?
