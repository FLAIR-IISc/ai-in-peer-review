### Summary

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions (TIMEX). The authors first conduct an analysis of TIMEX properties on four datasets, observing that they are typically short, use a small vocabulary of keywords, and almost always contain a core "time token". Motivated by these observations and Zipf's principle of least effort, they propose a system that operates in three stages: 1) identifying core time tokens from a predefined lexicon, 2) forming "time segments" by heuristically expanding the boundaries around these tokens to include modifiers and numerals, and 3) merging adjacent or overlapping segments to form the final time expressions. A key design choice is the use of "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) for modifiers, which define words by their position relative to time tokens, rather than by their semantic meaning. The system is initialized with a lexicon from SUTime and evaluated on the TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset. Results show that SynTime significantly outperforms strong baselines, including SUTime and HeidelTime, on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets, achieving state-of-the-art F1 scores, and is competitive on WikiWars.

### Contributions

1.  **A Simple and Effective Rule-Based Method (SynTime):** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. The approach of identifying a core token and then applying simple, position-based "boundary expansion" rules is an elegant and effective simplification of more complex, cascaded rule systems. Framing the rules around syntactic (positional) types for modifiers, rather than semantic ones, is a clear and well-articulated design principle that leads to a lightweight and high-performing system.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and a New Annotated Dataset:** The paper provides a robust evaluation against three state-of-the-art systems on three diverse datasets. The reported results are very strong, particularly the high recall across all datasets and the state-of-the-art F1 scores on TimeBank and the challenging Tweets domain. The creation and use of a manually annotated dataset of 942 tweets for this task is a valuable, albeit secondary, contribution to the community.
3.  **Corpus Analysis of Time Expressions:** The initial analysis in Section 3, which grounds the system's design in empirical observations about the properties of time expressions (length, vocabulary, structure), provides a strong motivation for the paper's approach. Connecting these observations to the principle of least effort adds a nice theoretical framing.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Performance and High Recall:** The system's performance is its most compelling strength. It achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three datasets, with particularly impressive gains on the noisy Tweets data (e.g., +14 F1 points over SUTime in strict match). The consistently high recall across all datasets suggests that its core strategy of anchoring on "time tokens" is fundamentally sound and provides excellent coverage.
2.  **Simplicity and Elegance of the Approach:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to understand. The three-stage process (identify token, expand boundaries, merge segments) is intuitive, and the heuristic rules for expansion are straightforward. This simplicity makes the system attractive from a practical standpoint, as it is likely to be fast, easy to maintain, and adaptable to new domains, as demonstrated by the SynTime-E variant.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Exposition:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to prior rule-based and machine learning systems. The distinction drawn between their "syntactic type" approach and the "semantic type" approach of systems like SUTime is clear and effectively highlights the novelty of their design. The initial corpus analysis provides a strong, data-driven rationale for the system's architecture.
4.  **Strong and Relevant Baselines:** The authors compare their method against well-established and high-performing systems: HeidelTime and SUTime (rule-based SOTA) and UWTime (a strong learning-based system). This makes their impressive results highly credible and demonstrates a significant advance over existing methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on an Existing System's Lexicon:** SynTime is initialized using the token regular expressions from SUTime. While the authors state they discard SUTime's other rules, this heavy reliance makes it difficult to fully disentangle the contributions. A significant part of a rule-based system's power comes from its lexicon. It is unclear how much of SynTime's high performance is attributable to its novel expansion/merging logic versus simply starting with a comprehensive and mature lexicon from an existing state-of-the-art system. An ablation study showing performance with a more basic, independently created lexicon would have strengthened the claims about the power of the proposed heuristic rules.
2.  **Ambiguity in the "Syntactic" vs. "Semantic" Distinction:** The paper's central claim is its use of syntactic types. This holds well for modifier types like PREFIX and SUFFIX. However, the 15 "Time Token" types, such as `HOLIDAY`, `SEASON`, and `DURATION`, are fundamentally semantic categories. The system is therefore a hybrid of semantic token types and syntactic modifier types. Overstating the system as purely "syntactic" is a slight mischaracterization; the key innovation appears to be the syntactic treatment of *modifiers* and the corresponding expansion logic, not a complete rethinking of the entire type system.
3.  **Opacity of the Manual Expansion Process:** The creation of the "expanded" version, SynTime-E, relies on a manual process of adding new terms from a training set based on subjective criteria like "cannot cause ambiguity and should be generic." While practical, this raises questions about reproducibility and effort. For the WikiWars dataset, 16 new time tokens and 19 new modifiers were added, which is a substantial number. The paper would benefit from more detail on this process, such as the time required or examples of the added rules, to allow for a better assessment of the system's adaptability.
4.  **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the aggregate results are excellent, the paper provides little insight into the system's failure modes. What types of time expressions does it still miss or incorrectly identify? A qualitative error analysis comparing SynTime's mistakes to those of the baselines would provide a more nuanced understanding of its advantages and limitations. For instance, on WikiWars, where its strict F1 is lower than the baselines, what are the specific constructions that cause it to fail?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the contribution of your heuristic rules in isolation from the SUTime lexicon? For instance, how does your system perform if you use a simpler, publicly available lexicon of time-related words (e.g., from word lists) as the initialization point, instead of SUTime's curated regular expressions?
2.  You define 15 "Time Token" types that appear to be semantic (e.g., HOLIDAY, DURATION). Could you clarify your rationale for describing the overall type system as "syntactic"? Would it be more accurate to say that the main innovation is the syntactic typing of *modifiers* and the simple boundary expansion rules that operate on them?
3.  The manual expansion for SynTime-E added 35 terms for the WikiWars dataset. Could you provide a few examples of the most important terms added? And can you give an estimate of the human effort (e.g., in hours) required to perform this expansion for one of the datasets? This would help in assessing the practical cost of adapting your system to a new domain.
