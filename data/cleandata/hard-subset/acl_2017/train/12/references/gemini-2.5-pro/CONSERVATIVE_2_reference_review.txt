**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The method is motivated by an analysis of four datasets, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain core "time tokens". SynTime defines "syntactic types" for tokens (e.g., time token, prefix, numeral) and uses simple heuristic rules to first identify core time tokens and then expand their boundaries to capture the full expression. The system is evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets dataset, where it is shown to outperform or be competitive with established rule-based and machine-learning baselines from the early 2010s.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An effective and lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition that achieves high recall and strong overall performance on standard benchmarks, particularly on noisy social media text.
2.  A useful, albeit not entirely novel, empirical analysis of the structural properties of time expressions across different text domains, which provides a clear motivation for the system's design.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The system achieves impressive results, outperforming strong baselines like HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The high F1 scores, especially the greater than 11-point improvement in strict F1 over the next-best system on Tweets, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Consistently High Recall:** Across all three datasets, both versions of SynTime achieve the highest recall scores, often by a significant margin. In many real-world applications (e.g., downstream event ordering), maximizing recall is critical, and the paper convincingly demonstrates that its architecture is well-suited for this goal.
3.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and, according to the authors, lightweight and fast. This is a significant practical advantage over complex machine learning models that may require substantial computational resources and training time. The clear, three-step process (token identification, segment expansion, expression extraction) is easy to understand and implement.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core methodology of identifying seed tokens and expanding their boundaries using curated lexicons and heuristic rules is a very established paradigm in rule-based information extraction. The authors' central claim to novelty rests on the distinction between their "syntactic types" and the "semantic types" of prior work. However, this distinction appears to be more a matter of terminology than a fundamental shift in approach. Systems like SUTime also use rules that implicitly capture the syntactic roles of tokens (e.g., a number preceding a time unit). The paper fails to sufficiently argue how its rule framework is fundamentally different from or superior to these established cascaded finite-state approaches.
2.  **Outdated Baselines and Evaluation Context:** The evaluation compares SynTime against systems that are now 8-10 years old. While these were state-of-the-art at the time, the field of NLP has since largely shifted towards neural, end-to-end models for sequence tagging tasks. A rigorous, modern evaluation would require comparison against a standard neural baseline, such as a BiLSTM-CRF or a fine-tuned transformer model. Without this, the paper's claims of being "state-of-the-art" are not well-supported in the context of current NLP research, which heavily favors such established ML methods.
3.  **Heavy Reliance on Existing Resources and Manual Effort:** SynTime-I is initialized using token lists directly from SUTime. This makes it difficult to disentangle the performance contribution of the novel heuristic rules from the high-quality, pre-existing resources. Furthermore, the performance gains of SynTime-E rely on manual, domain-specific vocabulary expansion. This highlights the classic scalability and portability limitations of rule-based systems, a key weakness that modern machine learning methods were developed to address.
4.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper would be strengthened by an ablation study to quantify the impact of its core components. For instance, what is the performance drop if POS-based disambiguation is removed? How sensitive is the model to different boundary expansion heuristics? This analysis is needed to validate the specific design choices and move the contribution beyond simply being a well-engineered system.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the key architectural differences between SynTime's "boundary expansion" and SUTime's "cascaded finite automata"? Both seem to build larger expressions from smaller components based on positional rules. What is the fundamental innovation that allows SynTime to outperform SUTime so significantly?
2.  To better isolate the contribution of your rule framework, have you considered an experiment where the initial token lists for SynTime are derived automatically from the training portion of TimeBank, rather than being taken from SUTime? This would provide a clearer picture of your method's capabilities versus the inherited value of SUTime's resources.
3.  The paper focuses on comparisons with other rule-based systems and an older statistical model. How do you see SynTime positioned relative to current, standard neural sequence labeling models (e.g., BERT-based token classification), which are often the preferred established method for such NER-like tasks today? Do you have any preliminary results or expectations on how SynTime would compare in terms of performance, efficiency, and domain adaptability?
