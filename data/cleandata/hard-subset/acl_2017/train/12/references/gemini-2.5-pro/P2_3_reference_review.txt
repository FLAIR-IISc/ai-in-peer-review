### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions (TIMEX). The authors first conduct an analysis of TIMEX properties on four datasets, observing that they are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and nearly always contain a core "time token". Based on these observations, they propose a system that operates in two main stages: first, it identifies core time tokens, and second, it applies simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries of these tokens to capture surrounding modifiers and numerals. The core novelty claimed is the use of "syntactic types" (e.g., modifiers defined by their position relative to a time token) and a simple boundary expansion mechanism, as opposed to the more complex, semantically-typed, cascaded rules of systems like SUTime. The system is evaluated on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) against strong rule-based and machine-learning baselines.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A simple, highly effective rule-based system for TIMEX recognition.** The primary contribution is the SynTime system itself. It demonstrates that a simple, elegant approach based on identifying core tokens and expanding their boundaries can significantly outperform more complex state-of-the-art systems, especially on news text and social media data. The strong empirical results are the most compelling aspect of this work.
2.  **A new annotated dataset for TIMEX on Twitter.** The authors created and manually annotated a dataset of 942 tweets containing time expressions. This is a valuable resource for the community, as it allows for the evaluation of TIMEX systems on informal, short-form text, a domain where they often struggle.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The most significant strength of this paper is the experimental results. SynTime achieves state-of-the-art performance, significantly outperforming three strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on the standard TimeBank dataset and the newly introduced Tweets dataset. For instance, on TimeBank, SynTime-I achieves a strict F1 of 92.09%, a notable improvement over the best baseline (UWTime at 83.10%). The performance on Tweets (91.74% F1) is also exceptionally strong and demonstrates the method's robustness on noisy, informal text.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed approach is refreshingly simple and intuitive. The core idea of identifying an anchor "time token" and then expanding left and right based on simple rules is easy to understand, implement, and debug. This stands in contrast to systems like SUTime, which rely on a complex cascade of finite-state automata. This simplicity is a scientific contribution in itself, showing that complexity is not always necessary for high performance on this task.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation. They compare against the right set of state-of-the-art baselines, including both top-performing rule-based systems and a strong learning-based system. The use of three datasets from different domains (news, Wikipedia, social media) effectively demonstrates the system's strengths and weaknesses. The analysis of results, including the discussion of why SynTime struggles on the descriptive expressions in WikiWars, is insightful and honest.
4.  **Clear Motivation and Justification:** The paper begins with a data-driven analysis that directly motivates the design of the SynTime system. The four observations (short expressions, small vocabulary, etc.) provide a clear and logical justification for the "find a time token, then expand" architecture. This grounds the work in empirical evidence and makes the authors' design choices easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Novelty of "Syntactic Types":** The paper heavily emphasizes the distinction between its "syntactic types" and the "semantic types" of other systems like SUTime. While the distinction is valid (e.g., PREFIX vs. a frequency modifier), the practical innovation seems less about the type system itself and more about the simple, flexible boundary expansion algorithm that uses it. Other rule-based systems also inherently rely on the syntactic positions of tokens. The key contribution appears to be the replacement of a complex, fixed rule cascade with a much simpler, flexible heuristic, but the paper frames this primarily as a difference in "typing," which may be slightly misleading.
2.  **Dependence on SUTime for Lexicon Initialization:** A critical detail is that SynTime is initialized with token regular expressions "collected from SUTime." This means the system heavily relies on the extensive, manually curated lexicon of a competing baseline. This is a pragmatic choice, but it significantly weakens the claim of a fully novel system. The contribution is more accurately a novel and superior rule *engine* that operates on an existing, high-quality lexicon. The paper should be more transparent about this dependency and discuss how much of the performance gain is attributable to the new rule engine versus the inherited lexicon.
3.  **Unsupported Claims of Efficiency:** The authors claim their system is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time." While these claims are highly plausible for a simple rule-based system, they are presented without any empirical evidence. A basic efficiency analysis (e.g., documents processed per second) comparing SynTime to the baselines would have substantiated these claims and strengthened the paper's practical contribution. Without this, the claims remain anecdotal.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the extent of the lexical resources borrowed from SUTime? Specifically, what proportion of your initial time tokens, modifiers, and numerals are directly taken from SUTime's rule files? Have you considered evaluating SynTime's rule engine when initialized with a more basic, independently created lexicon to better isolate the contribution of your proposed rule mechanism?
2.  You claim that SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and runs in "real time." Could you provide any quantitative data on its processing speed (e.g., words/second) and perhaps compare it to the runtime of the baselines, particularly SUTime, to empirically support this claim? This would be a very compelling practical advantage to highlight.
