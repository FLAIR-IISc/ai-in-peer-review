### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces SynTime, a simple, rule-based system for recognizing temporal expressions in text. The method is motivated by a corpus analysis across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), which concludes that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and are almost always anchored by a central "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Tuesday'). Based on this, SynTime operates in three stages: 1) It identifies these core time tokens using a predefined lexicon and POS tags. 2) It applies simple heuristic rules to expand the boundaries of these tokens left and right to include modifiers and numerals. 3) It merges adjacent or overlapping segments to form the final time expressions. The authors claim their main novelty lies in defining token types based on their *syntactic* behavior (e.g., PREFIX, SUFFIX) rather than their *semantic* meaning, which simplifies rule design. The system is evaluated on three datasets, showing strong performance against state-of-the-art baselines.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of significance:

1.  **Strong Empirical Results with a Simple Model:** The paper demonstrates that a surprisingly simple, lightweight, and fast rule-based method can outperform or match more complex rule-based and machine learning systems on standard benchmarks. The exceptional performance on the informal Tweets dataset is particularly noteworthy and demonstrates the robustness of the approach.
2.  **An Effective Simplification of Rule-Based Tagging:** While drawing heavily on existing resources, the paper presents a compelling and elegant reframing of the rule-based approach. The "identify seed, then expand" heuristic is intuitive, easy to implement, and demonstrably effective, providing a valuable alternative to more complex cascaded finite-state automata or learned models.
3.  **Corpus Analysis and a New Annotated Dataset:** The analysis in Section 3 provides useful quantitative backing for common intuitions about the structure of temporal expressions. Additionally, the creation and manual annotation of a new Tweets dataset for this task is a useful contribution to the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance and High Recall:** The strongest argument for this paper is its results. As shown in Table 4, SynTime significantly outperforms strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets in F1 score. Its consistently high recall across all datasets validates its core hypothesis: identifying a "time token" is sufficient to find the vast majority of time expressions. Achieving an F1 of over 92% (strict) on TimeBank and over 91% (strict) on Tweets with such a simple system is a very strong result.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. Compared to the complex, hand-crafted cascade of rules in systems like SUTime or the feature engineering and training required for ML models, SynTime's "identify-and-expand" logic is remarkably straightforward. This makes the system transparent, easy to debug, and likely very fast, as claimed by the authors ("runs in real time"). This work serves as a powerful reminder that simpler, well-motivated heuristics can sometimes be superior to more complex machinery.
3.  **Robustness Across Different Domains:** The system demonstrates strong performance on both formal newswire/encyclopedic text (TimeBank, WikiWars) and highly informal social media text (Tweets) without significant domain-specific adaptation (other than vocabulary expansion). This is a key advantage, and the stellar results on Tweets suggest the method's core principles are robust to noise and linguistic variation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty is Overstated due to Heavy Reliance on SUTime:** The paper's primary weakness is its understated dependency on SUTime for its initial lexicon. Section 4.1 states, "The token regular expressions for initializing SynTime are collected from SUTime...we collect from SUTime only the tokens and the regular expressions over tokens." This significantly reduces the novelty of the work. The system is less a new approach built from scratch and more a re-engineering of SUTime's lexical resources with a much simpler rule engine. The contribution is therefore not in the creation of the type inventories but in the simplified application of them. This should be made much clearer and the novelty claims should be moderated accordingly.
2.  **The "Syntactic vs. Semantic" Distinction is Unclear and its Impact is Not Proven:** The authors frame their main conceptual contribution as defining types based on syntactic behavior (e.g., `PREFIX`) rather than semantic meaning (e.g., SUTime's frequency modifiers). However, this distinction feels somewhat superficial. Both approaches ultimately result in lists of words that are used in rules. The paper fails to provide a compelling example of a time expression that SUTime's semantic approach inherently fails on, but SynTime's syntactic approach correctly captures. The real innovation seems to be the simple expansion algorithm, not the philosophy behind the type naming. The paper would be stronger if it focused on the algorithmic simplification rather than the slightly ambiguous typological distinction.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis or Ablation:** The evaluation is solid, but it lacks depth. An error analysis would be highly beneficial: what kinds of expressions does SynTime fail on? Given its high recall and slightly lower precision in some cases, what does it over-generate? More importantly, an ablation study is needed to test the core claims. What would performance be if the lexicons were built from scratch using only the training data, instead of being ported from SUTime? This would provide a true measure of the proposed framework's power, independent of prior work. The manual expansion process from SynTime-I to SynTime-E is also opaque; insight into the effort and principles guiding this process would be valuable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please quantify the reliance on SUTime's resources? What percentage of the initial token lists and regular expressions in SynTime-I are taken directly from SUTime? How does this impact your claims of novelty, which are centered on your type system? A crucial experiment would be to show how SynTime performs if you build its lexicons from scratch using only the training portion of the evaluation datasets.
2.  The distinction between your "syntactic" types and prior systems' "semantic" types is presented as a key contribution. Could you provide a concrete example of a time expression that is difficult to recognize with a semantic type system but is handled well by your syntactic approach? This would help clarify the practical advantage of your proposed type system versus the simplified rule engine itself.
3.  Regarding the expansion from SynTime-I to SynTime-E, what was the approximate human effort (e.g., in hours) required to identify and add the new tokens for each dataset? This is important for understanding the practicality and scalability of keeping SynTime updated for new domains.
