This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors motivate their work by analyzing four datasets and observing characteristics of time expressions (short length, small vocabulary, presence of time tokens, similar syntactic behavior). Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach defining time tokens, modifiers, and numerals, and building rules around these types. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, demonstrating competitive and in some cases superior performance, particularly on Tweets. 

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration and leveraging of syntactic typing for building a time expression recognition system. While rule-based methods are not novel, the explicit focus on syntactic types as opposed to semantic meanings, coupled with the heuristic boundary expansion approach, provides a relatively simple yet effective way to build a time expression tagger. A secondary contribution is the detailed analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, providing empirical support for the design choices made in SynTime. Finally, the strong performance, especially on the noisy Tweets dataset, is a noteworthy result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Empirical Support:** The paper begins with a strong motivation grounded in data analysis. The four observations about time expressions—short length, limited vocabulary, token presence, and syntactic consistency—are well-supported by the data presented and directly inform the design of SynTime. This makes the proposed approach feel principled and not arbitrary.
2. **Novelty in Type Definition:**  The distinction between defining types based on syntactic behavior (SynTime) versus semantic meaning (e.g., SUTime) is a valuable contribution. This shift in perspective leads to a simpler, more flexible rule-building process. The explanation of this difference is clear and well-justified.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The performance of SynTime on the Tweets dataset is particularly impressive. This suggests the system is robust to the inherent noise and informal language used in social media, which is a significant practical advantage. The authors correctly attribute this performance to the characteristics of time expressions in that domain.
4. **Simplicity and Efficiency:**  The authors emphasize the light-weight nature of SynTime and its ability to run in real-time. This is a desirable trait, especially for applications that require fast processing. The heuristic rule design contributes to this efficiency.
5. **Thorough Evaluation:** The comparison with three strong baselines across three datasets provides a comprehensive evaluation of SynTime’s effectiveness. The use of both strict and relaxed matching criteria allows for a nuanced understanding of the system’s strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Overall Approach:** While the *type definition* aspect is novel, the fundamental approach of using rules for time expression recognition is not. The paper doesn’t fully address how SynTime fundamentally differs from other sophisticated rule-based systems beyond the type definition and boundary expansion.  A deeper comparison in terms of rule complexity, maintainability, and scalability would strengthen the argument for SynTime’s advantages.
2. **Reliance on Existing Tokenization and POS Tagging:** The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't fully explore its potential impact. Errors in these upstream processes could significantly affect SynTime's performance.  Providing an analysis of the impact of POS tagging errors on the results would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:**  The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding *what* types of errors SynTime makes would provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific time expression patterns that the system consistently fails to recognize?
4. **Expansion Process Details:** While the authors mention expanding SynTime with new keywords, the process is described vaguely. More details about how keywords are selected and integrated would be helpful. The example of filtering for generic terms is good, but more concrete guidelines would improve reproducibility.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** It is not clear how much each component (syntactic typing, boundary expansion) contributes to the overall performance. An ablation study, where components are systematically removed, could clarify their individual contributions and justify the design choices.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the complexity (number of rules, length of rules, etc.) of SynTime versus SUTime?
2. How sensitive is SynTime to errors in the POS tagging? Can you provide some results with intentionally noisy POS tags?
3. Could you provide examples of time expressions that SynTime consistently fails to recognize, and explain why?
4. What specific criteria are used to decide whether to add a new keyword during the expansion process?
5.  How does the performance of SynTime degrade as the length of the input text increases? Does the real-time performance remain consistent for very long documents?



**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting of the table with experiment results is a bit cramped and difficult to read.
* Consider adding a brief discussion of related work in the area of syntactic parsing for time expressions. This might further contextualize the contribution of SynTime.
* The reference list appears incomplete.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of time expression recognition. While the overall approach is not entirely novel, the focus on syntactic types and the strong empirical results, particularly on the challenging Tweets dataset, make it a valuable addition to the literature. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on addressing the concerns raised in the questions.
