This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The core idea is to leverage syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple, heuristically designed rules based on boundary expansion. The authors first analyze four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) to observe characteristics of time expressions – short length, small vocabulary, frequent presence of time tokens, and consistent syntactic behavior. They argue these observations support the principle of least effort and motivate their type-based approach.  SynTime is evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on three datasets, demonstrating competitive and, in some cases, superior performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset. The primary contributions of the work are: (1) a detailed analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing inherent linguistic properties; (2) the SynTime system, a novel rule-based approach employing syntactic typing and heuristic rules; and (3) empirical validation of SynTime’s effectiveness, demonstrating strong performance, especially on noisy text like Tweets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Data Analysis:** The analysis of the four datasets provides a strong foundation for the proposed approach. The observed characteristics – short length, small vocabulary, and the prevalence of time tokens – are well-supported by the presented data and clearly motivate the design choices in SynTime. This analysis is a key strength of the paper, informing the development of a targeted and efficient system.
2. **Novelty in Rule Design:** The proposed type-based approach with heuristic boundary expansion offers a distinct alternative to existing rule-based systems.  The distinction between defining types based on syntactic behavior (SynTime) versus semantic meaning (e.g., SUTime) is clearly articulated and represents a potentially valuable direction for time expression recognition. The focus on simplicity and flexibility in rule design is also commendable.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The significantly better performance of SynTime on the Tweets dataset compared to the baselines is a notable achievement. This suggests the approach is more robust to the informal language and variations commonly found in social media text. This is a significant practical contribution given the importance of time expression recognition in social media analytics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of syntactic typing and heuristic rules is a reasonable approach, the individual components themselves aren’t fundamentally novel. Syntactic typing is a well-established concept in linguistics and NLP, and heuristic rule design is commonplace in rule-based systems. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how SynTime's specific implementation of these components differs significantly from existing methods beyond the type definition approach.
2. **Dependence on Pre-existing Resources:** SynTime’s initialization relies heavily on regular expressions extracted from SUTime. While this is acknowledged, it raises concerns about the originality of the system. The paper does not adequately address how much of SynTime’s performance is directly attributable to the pre-existing rules inherited from SUTime. A more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of the inherited rules versus the newly designed rules would strengthen the evaluation.
3. **Evaluation Limited by Dataset Choice & Metrics:** The exclusion of Gigaword due to potentially inaccurate labels is a valid concern, but it reduces the scope of the evaluation. Furthermore, the reliance solely on strict and relaxed match metrics doesn’t fully capture the nuances of time expression recognition. More granular evaluation metrics, potentially focusing on specific error types (e.g., incorrect normalization, boundary errors), could provide a more comprehensive assessment of SynTime's performance.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide an ablation study showing the performance of SynTime with and without the inherited regular expressions from SUTime? This would clarify the contribution of the novel components of your system.
2.  The paper mentions that UWTime requires training data. How does the performance of SynTime compare to UWTime when trained on a larger and more diverse corpus of time expression data?
3.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of POS tagging and provide specific examples of POS tagging errors that impacted SynTime’s performance? What strategies could be employed to mitigate the effects of noisy POS tags?
4.  Regarding the observation about informal language in Tweets: Is there an analysis of the types of syntactic variations (beyond length) that SynTime handles effectively in Tweets compared to other datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are verbose and could be more concise.
* The references are extensive, but a few appear to be incomplete (e.g., Alonso et al.,?).
* A brief discussion of the computational cost of SynTime (e.g., processing time) would be valuable, especially given the claim of real-time performance.
