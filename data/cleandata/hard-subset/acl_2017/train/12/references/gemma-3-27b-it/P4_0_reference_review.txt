This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets, observing that they are generally short, use a limited vocabulary, frequently contain time tokens, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime employs a type-based approach, defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and using simple heuristic rules for recognition. The system is evaluated against established baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), demonstrating competitive or superior performance, particularly on Tweets.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a simplified and effective rule-based approach to time expression recognition. While rule-based systems are common in this field, the authors highlight the benefits of focusing on *syntactic* types rather than semantic meanings, and employing boundary expansion heuristics, which leads to a lightweight and real-time capable system. The empirical results, especially the strong performance on the Tweet dataset, suggest this approach is particularly well-suited for informal text. A second, supporting contribution is the analysis of time expression characteristics across different datasets, which provides empirical grounding for the design choices made in SynTime. The observations regarding length, vocabulary size, and syntactic behavior are valuable and justify the focus on simplicity. A third contribution is the release of the system and potentially the expanded rule set, enabling reproducibility and future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime demonstrably outperforms or matches state-of-the-art systems on the evaluated datasets, specifically exhibiting significant improvements on the challenging Tweet dataset. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The detailed results presented in Table 4 are clear and convincing.
2. **Clear and Justified Design:** The design of SynTime is logically motivated by the observed characteristics of time expressions. The authors clearly articulate how their observations (short length, limited vocabulary, syntactic regularity) inform the type definitions and rule design. This provides a strong rationale for the chosen approach.
3. **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The emphasis on simplicity and heuristic rules is a key strength. The authors highlight that this results in a lightweight system capable of running in real-time, which is a practical advantage. This is particularly relevant in applications requiring fast processing of large volumes of text.
4. **Insightful Data Analysis:** The initial analysis of time expression characteristics is well-conducted and provides valuable insights. The observations made contribute to a better understanding of the problem and justify the specific design choices in SynTime. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is interesting and helps to frame the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the *combination* of syntactic typing and boundary expansion heuristics is effective, the individual components are not entirely novel. Rule-based systems and the use of syntactic information have been explored in previous work (as acknowledged in the related work section). The paper needs to more strongly emphasize *what* is novel about the combination and *why* it leads to improved performance compared to existing approaches.
2. **Dependence on Pre-processing:** The system relies on accurate POS tagging, and the authors acknowledge this as a limitation. The performance is thus sensitive to the quality of the POS tagger.  A more robust approach might incorporate methods to handle potential errors in POS tagging or integrate POS tagging directly into the system.
3. **Limited Generalization Discussion:** The performance gains on Tweets are highlighted, but the paper lacks a detailed discussion of *why* SynTime performs so well on this informal dataset. Is it due to the simpler sentence structures in tweets, the prevalence of specific time expressions, or something else? A more in-depth analysis of this would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Choice Justification:** The explanation for excluding the Gigaword dataset ("labels may not be ground truth") feels weak. A more thorough justification or a small experiment exploring the impact of using Gigaword would strengthen the evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors that SynTime makes? Are there specific types of time expressions that the system consistently misidentifies?
2. What is the computational complexity of the heuristic rules, and how does this scale with the length of the input text?
3. While the Tweet results are strong, could you provide more insight into *why* SynTime performs so well on this informal dataset compared to the others? Are there any specific characteristics of Tweet time expressions that make them easier to recognize?
4.  The paper mentions UWTime requires training data. Were any attempts made to train SynTime using a machine learning approach to improve performance, or is the completely rule-based approach a deliberate design choice? If the latter, please elaborate on the rationale.

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*  The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* Consider adding an ablation study to investigate the contribution of each component of SynTime (e.g., syntactic typing, boundary expansion). This would help to further validate the design choices.
