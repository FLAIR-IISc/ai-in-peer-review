This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system, motivated by observations about the structure of time expressions in four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The core idea is to define syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and design simple, heuristically-driven rules based on these types and their relative positions, rather than semantic categories or fixed patterns as found in previous work. The authors demonstrate that SynTime outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable performance on WikiWars.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The detailed analysis of time expressions across four datasets—highlighting their brevity, limited vocabulary, high reliance on time tokens, and consistent syntactic behavior—is a significant contribution. These observations provide a strong rationale for the design of SynTime and are presented convincingly.
2. **Type-Based Rule Definition:** The shift from semantic-based rule definition (as in SUTime) to a syntactic-based approach is a novel idea, and the authors successfully implement it in SynTime. This represents a potentially valuable simplification of rule-based time expression recognition.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** SynTime demonstrably outperforms established baselines on two of the three datasets (TimeBank and Tweets), and achieves competitive results on the remaining dataset (WikiWars). The substantial recall improvements on Tweets are particularly noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Justification:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind SynTime, grounding it in empirical observations about the nature of time expressions. This makes the proposed approach feel well-reasoned and practical.
2. **Well-Defined Type System:** The syntactic type system is clearly defined and explained, allowing for a straightforward understanding of how SynTime categorizes and processes time-related tokens.
3. **Effective Heuristic Rules:** The heuristic rules, based on boundary expansion, appear to be both simple and effective, enabling SynTime to achieve strong performance while maintaining a lightweight and efficient implementation.  The authors convincingly argue that this flexibility is a key advantage.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The experiments are well-designed and thorough, comparing SynTime to multiple state-of-the-art baselines across three different datasets. The inclusion of Tweets is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates the method’s applicability to more informal text.
5. **Focus on Recall:** The consistently high recall scores achieved by SynTime are a notable strength. This suggests the method is good at identifying a large proportion of time expressions, which is crucial in many applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Rule-Based Approach:** While the *way* rules are defined is novel, the fundamental approach of using rules for time expression recognition is well-established. The paper could benefit from more explicitly acknowledging this and focusing on *how* SynTime differs substantially from prior rule-based systems beyond the type system and boundary expansion heuristic.
2. **Reliance on Existing Tokenization and POS Tagging:** The performance of SynTime is dependent on the quality of the input tokenization and POS tags. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation, but it’s a significant one, particularly for noisy data like Tweets. A more robust evaluation might involve testing the system with different tokenizers and POS taggers or incorporating these steps into the SynTime pipeline.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying common types of errors made by SynTime would provide valuable insights into its limitations and suggest avenues for improvement. For example, understanding why performance is lower on WikiWars would be helpful.
4. **Limited Discussion of Scalability:** While described as lightweight, the paper doesn’t provide detailed discussion or empirical evidence regarding the scalability of SynTime to very large corpora.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detail on the specific types of errors that SynTime makes, particularly on the WikiWars dataset where performance is less impressive?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime to the quality of the input tokenization and POS tagging? Have you experimented with different tokenizers/taggers, and how do they impact performance?
3.  Regarding the boundary expansion heuristic: could you provide a more concrete example illustrating *how* this method handles complex time expressions compared to a fixed-length/position approach?
4.  How does the addition of new keywords to SynTime-E affect its runtime performance? Does the expansion process introduce any computational overhead?



**Additional Comments:**

* The references section appears to be incomplete and oddly formatted. Please ensure all cited works are included and formatted according to the conference guidelines.
* The discussion of Zipf’s Principle feels slightly forced. While the observations align with the principle, the connection could be made more explicit and less repetitive.
* The statement that "UWTime requires training" is somewhat misleading as SUTime and HeidelTime also require some level of configuration and resource loading. Clarify the specific difference you intend to highlight.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of time expression recognition. The empirical analysis is strong, the proposed method is well-motivated and implemented, and the experimental results are promising. While the novelty of the rule-based approach itself is limited, the specific way in which rules are defined and applied represents a significant improvement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
