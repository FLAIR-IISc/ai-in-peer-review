## Review for "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

**Summary:**

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors first analyze time expression characteristics across four datasets, observing their short length, limited vocabulary, high token overlap, and consistent syntactic behavior. Leveraging these observations and inspired by part-of-speech tagging, they propose a type-based approach defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and employing simple, expandable heuristic rules.  SynTime is evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, demonstrating competitive or superior performance, particularly on Tweets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The analysis of the four datasets provides a solid foundation for the design of the SynTime system and offers valuable insights into the inherent properties of time expressions.  This analysis, focusing on linguistic simplicity, is a key motivator for the proposed approach.
2. **Syntactic Typing for Time Expression Recognition:** The idea of defining time expression components based on *syntactic* behavior, rather than semantic meaning, is a worthwhile contribution, offering a different perspective compared to existing rule-based systems.
3. **Demonstrated Effectiveness, Particularly on Noisy Data:** SynTime achieves strong results, specifically outperforming baselines on the challenging Tweet dataset, suggesting its robustness to informal language.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Rationale:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind SynTime, tying it directly to observed characteristics of time expressions (short length, small vocabulary) and the principle of least effort. This connection strengthens the design choices.
2. **Competitive Performance:**  The experimental results demonstrate SynTime’s competitiveness, particularly the strong performance on the Tweet dataset. This is significant because recognizing time expressions in social media text is a notoriously difficult task. The high recall is a notable achievement.
3. **Simplicity and Expandability:** The heuristic rules are described as simple and the system itself is lightweight, leading to real-time performance.  The authors clearly outline the expansion strategy, making the system adaptable to different domains.
4. **Solid Baseline Comparison:**  The comparison to established systems (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) provides a good benchmark for evaluating SynTime's performance.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-structured, easy to follow, and clearly presents the methods, experiments, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Rule-Based Approach:** While the *application* of syntactic typing to time expression recognition is a good idea, the fundamental approach (rule-based) isn’t novel.  The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing rule-based systems beyond the type definition. More discussion on *why* this syntactic approach is inherently better, or more easily maintainable, than semantic approaches would be helpful.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper reports overall performance metrics, but lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common error types would provide valuable insight into the limitations of SynTime and suggest avenues for future improvement. Knowing *where* the system fails is critical.
3. **Dependency on POS Tagging Quality:**  The system relies on the accuracy of a POS tagger. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation, but don't quantify the impact of POS tagging errors on the system's performance.  A brief experiment exploring this sensitivity would strengthen the paper.
4. **Limited Scope of Datasets:** While the datasets used are standard, the lack of evaluation on a more diverse range of domains could limit the generalizability of the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Could you elaborate on the design choices that led you to prioritize syntactic types over semantic types, particularly in light of the existing successful semantic approaches (like SUTime)? What specific challenges did you anticipate with a semantic approach that syntactic typing addresses?**
2. **Could you provide a brief error analysis of SynTime’s performance, highlighting the most common types of errors it makes?**
3. **How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to the accuracy of the POS tagger? Have you explored the impact of different POS taggers, or POS tagging errors, on the system's results?**
4. **The paper states the Gigaword dataset was not used due to potential labeling issues. Could you provide more detail on why you believe the labels are unreliable?**



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive but contains some inconsistencies in formatting.
*   The figure showing the SynTime overview (Figure 2) is helpful for understanding the system architecture.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated and reasonably effective approach to time expression recognition. While the core technique (rule-based tagging) isn't groundbreaking, the syntactic typing approach and the strong performance, particularly on the Tweet dataset, are valuable contributions. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, especially providing a more in-depth error analysis and discussing the novelty of the approach in comparison to existing semantic methods, would significantly strengthen the paper.
