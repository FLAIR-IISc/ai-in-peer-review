The paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system leveraging syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets, identifying patterns like short length, small vocabulary, and frequent time tokens, motivating a type-based approach differentiating from semantic-based rule systems. They define three types—time token, modifier, and numeral—and employ boundary expansion for rule creation.  Evaluation against state-of-the-art systems on three datasets shows competitive, and in some cases superior, performance, particularly in recall. The primary claimed contribution is the type-based approach itself, simplifying rule design and achieving real-time performance. A secondary claim is the analysis of time expressions supporting Zipf’s principle of least effort.

A key strength is the comprehensive analysis of time expression characteristics. The observations about length, vocabulary size, and the prevalence of time tokens are well-supported by the data and provide a reasonable justification for the proposed approach. The meticulous comparison with existing systems – HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime – demonstrates a clear understanding of the state-of-the-art. The reported improvements in recall, particularly on the Tweets dataset, are noteworthy, and suggest the approach is robust to informal language.

However, the paper suffers from a concerning number of minor flaws that collectively detract from its overall quality. Firstly, the writing quality is uneven, with several instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors (e.g., "Time expression recognition main involves..."). Secondly, the novelty of the proposed approach is overstated. While the syntactic typing is a sensible distinction from semantic typing, the fundamental concept of using types to categorize and define rules is not novel in itself; part-of-speech tagging, for example, has long used this principle. The claim of simplification feels weak as the type definitions and rules, while presented clearly, are still complex and require manual construction. Thirdly, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The exact parameters used for the baseline systems (e.g., SUTime’s configuration) are not specified, hindering reproducibility. The rationale for excluding Gigaword, citing concerns about label quality, is weak; a discussion of the potential impact of noisy labels would be more appropriate. Fourthly, the analysis of the expansion phase (SynTime-E) is insufficient. Table 5 only shows the *number* of added tokens/modifiers; it does *not* demonstrate how these additions impacted performance on each dataset. The lack of ablation studies exploring the contribution of specific types or rules is a significant omission. Fifthly, the discussion of related work is superficial. While mentioning relevant systems, it fails to deeply engage with their limitations and how SynTime specifically addresses them. Finally, numerous minor formatting issues exist, including inconsistent citation styles and unclear figure captions.

Despite the reasonably good performance, the lack of significant novelty, coupled with the numerous shortcomings in presentation and experimental rigor, leads me to recommend rejection. While the analysis is useful, it does not justify a publication in a highly competitive venue like ACL. The authors need to substantially revise the paper, addressing the concerns raised above, and more convincingly demonstrate the benefits of their approach.

Questions to Authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the specific configuration and parameters used for each baseline system during the experiments?
2.  What is the computational complexity of the boundary expansion rules, and how does it scale with the length of the input text?
3.  Please provide a more detailed breakdown of the performance gains achieved by SynTime-E on each dataset, specifically showing the impact of adding each type of token/modifier.
4.  How does the performance of SynTime degrade when applied to a domain significantly different from the training data (e.g., scientific articles)?

Additional Comments:

*   The reference list is inconsistently formatted.
*   Figure captions are often unclear and lack sufficient detail.
*   Consider providing a qualitative error analysis to identify the types of time expressions that SynTime struggles to recognize.
*   The claim about running “in real time” requires quantification (e.g., processing time per sentence).
