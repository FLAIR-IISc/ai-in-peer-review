- Paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition using syntactic token types and heuristic rules.
- Empirical analysis across four datasets shows time expressions are short, use limited vocabulary, and follow consistent syntactic patterns.
- Introduces syntactic type-based grouping ("time token", "modifier", "numeral") as an alternative to semantic-based rule definitions.
- Designs simple heuristic rules informed by the syntactic typing and dataset analysis.
- Evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on three datasets, showing competitive performance.
- Achieves notably strong results on the noisy Tweets dataset, particularly improving recall.
- Motivation and justification for the type-based approach are clear and well-supported by analysis.
- SynTime offers simplicity, efficiency, and potential for real-time use compared to complex ML models.
- Thorough empirical comparison with established baselines strengthens the evaluation.
- Paper is generally well-written with helpful system overview figures.
- Core method is limited in novelty since it remains a rule-based approach and similar systems exist.
- Lacks detailed error analysis to show where SynTime fails and which expression types are missed.
- Heavy reliance on external tools (POS tagger, SUTime regexes) raises concerns about sensitivity to upstream errors.
- Limited discussion of why the system excels on Tweets and whether that success generalizes to other noisy domains.
- Requests for clarification on expansion process, keyword selection criteria, and impact on complexity.
- Asks about sensitivity to POS tagging errors and whether different taggers were tested.
- Seeks the total number of implemented rules and concerns about rule growth as the system expands.
- Notes that discussion of Zipf's principle feels tangential and could be better integrated.
- Suggests improving formatting of Table 4 and fixing inconsistent citation style.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, contingent on adding error analysis and deeper generalizability discussion.
