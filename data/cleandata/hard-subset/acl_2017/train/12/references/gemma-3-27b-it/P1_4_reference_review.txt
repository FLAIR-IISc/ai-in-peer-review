## Peer Review: Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

**Summary:**

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors motivate their approach through an analysis of four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), identifying characteristics like short expression length, small vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime leverages these observations by defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and using simple, heuristically-designed rules for recognition.  The system is evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, demonstrating competitive or superior performance, particularly in recall, especially on the Tweets dataset.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by this reviewer):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The detailed analysis of time expression characteristics across diverse datasets is a valuable contribution.  Confirming and quantifying observations like expression length and vocabulary size provide strong grounding for the proposed approach and offer insights for future research.
2. **Type-Based Rule Definition:**  Framing rule-based time expression recognition around *syntactic* types, rather than semantic ones (as often done in existing systems), is a novel and potentially beneficial approach. This simplifies rule creation and allows for more flexible expansion.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:**  Achieving notably good performance on the noisy and informal Tweets dataset suggests the system’s robustness and adaptability, highlighting a practical application.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the rationale behind SynTime, linking design choices directly to the observed characteristics of time expressions. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is a compelling justification.
2. **Clear and Concise Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The description of SynTime's architecture and rule design is clear and provides sufficient detail for understanding the approach. Figure 2 is particularly helpful.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation using three datasets and comparison against strong baselines provides a good assessment of SynTime’s effectiveness.  The inclusion of the Tweets dataset is particularly valuable, as it represents a challenging real-world scenario.
4. **Focus on Recall:**  The emphasis on achieving high recall is commendable. Missing time expressions can be particularly problematic in downstream applications, and SynTime’s strong recall scores are a significant strength.
5. **Lightweight and Fast:**  The authors highlight the speed of SynTime, which is a practical advantage for real-time applications.  This is a welcome attribute in a field where complex models can often be computationally expensive.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Rule-Based Approach:** While the *type-based* aspect is interesting, the fundamental approach remains rule-based. This is less innovative compared to learning-based methods. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully address how SynTime overcomes limitations inherent in rule-based systems beyond simplification and expandability.
2. **Dependence on Pre-processing:**  The system’s performance relies heavily on accurate POS tagging and tokenization. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation, but the impact of pre-processing errors is not quantified.  How robust is SynTime to noisy POS tags, common in real-world text?
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:**  While the paper presents overall performance metrics, a deeper analysis of the types of errors SynTime makes would be beneficial. Understanding the failure modes could guide future improvements.  Are there specific types of time expressions that consistently cause problems?
4. **Expansion Methodology Detail:** The description of the expansion process (Section 4.3) feels a bit underspecified.  More detail on the criteria for adding new tokens and modifiers would be useful. The mention of informal words in tweets, and comparison to the Twitter cluster analysis is interesting, but the actual additions made aren't shown.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** It would be helpful to see an ablation study to determine the contribution of each aspect of the system (e.g., the type system versus the heuristic rules). This would clarify which components are most responsible for the observed performance gains.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors SynTime makes on each dataset?  Specifically, are there certain classes of time expressions that are consistently misidentified?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime's performance to errors in the POS tagging and tokenization stages? Could you present results with intentionally noisy pre-processing to quantify this dependence?
3.  Can you elaborate on the criteria used to select new time tokens and modifiers during the expansion phase?  Provide examples of tokens added to SynTime-E and explain why they were chosen.
4.  How does the computational cost of SynTime scale with the size of the input text and the number of rules?  Provide some performance benchmarks (e.g., processing time per sentence).
5.  The paper mentions UWTime requires training. Is the performance of SynTime-I comparable to UWTime when UWTime is trained on only TimeBank, without Tweets? This would provide a more direct comparison between the rule-based and learning-based approaches on a common dataset.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are a bit lengthy, but appreciated.
*   The Gigaword dataset being excluded is understandable given the label quality concerns.
*   The link to the GitHub repository is a positive aspect of the submission.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a well-motivated and effectively evaluated time expression recognition system. While the core approach isn't radically new, the type-based rule definition and strong performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset, make a valuable contribution. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
