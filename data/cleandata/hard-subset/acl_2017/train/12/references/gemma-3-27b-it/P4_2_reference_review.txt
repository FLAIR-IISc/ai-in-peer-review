This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets, making observations about their brevity, limited vocabulary, and syntactic consistency. They then propose a type-based approach, defining types (time token, modifier, numeral) and designing heuristic rules based on these types to recognize time expressions. The paper reports competitive results against state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is the novel combination of syntactic typing and simple, boundary-expanding heuristics for time expression recognition. While rule-based approaches and type definitions are not new, the specific focus on *syntactic* types, as opposed to semantic ones, and the resulting simplification of rules offer a potentially valuable alternative to more complex systems. The empirical evaluation demonstrating competitive performance, especially on the Tweets dataset, is a supporting contribution. The analysis of time expressions in the datasets, while providing rationale for the approach, is a secondary contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Focused Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for SynTime, linking it directly to observations about the nature of time expressions. This grounded approach makes the design choices more justifiable and understandable. The focus on syntactic types provides a unique perspective within the field of time expression recognition.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that SynTime achieves comparable or superior performance to established baselines on multiple datasets. The strong performance on the Tweets dataset, where informal language is prevalent, is particularly noteworthy. This suggests the system's robustness and adaptability.
3. **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The authors highlight the simplicity of the rules and the lightweight nature of the system, leading to real-time performance. This is a significant advantage, especially for applications where speed is critical. The “boundary expansion” heuristic is a clever way to achieve flexibility without increasing complexity.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a comparison against three strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) and evaluation on three distinct datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets). This thorough evaluation provides a solid basis for assessing the effectiveness of SynTime.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination of syntactic typing and heuristic rules is interesting, the individual components are not fundamentally novel. Rule-based systems, type definitions, and boundary expansion techniques have all been used in previous work. The paper needs to better articulate what specifically distinguishes SynTime beyond this combination.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging:** The system’s reliance on accurate POS tagging is a potential weakness. Errors in POS tagging can propagate and negatively impact the performance of SynTime. The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not explore strategies for mitigating its effects (e.g., robust tagging, error correction).
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance metrics but lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors SynTime makes would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, identifying common failure cases or specific types of time expressions that are difficult to recognize.
4. **Dataset Choice Justification:** The stated reason for not using the Gigaword dataset (automatically generated labels) seems insufficient. A more detailed explanation or discussion of the potential impact of using automatically labeled data would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *how* the syntactic types were chosen and the rationale behind defining those specific types?
2. How does SynTime handle ambiguous cases where a word could be a time token or have another POS tag? What strategies are used to resolve such ambiguities?
3. Could you provide some examples of error cases made by SynTime? What are the most common types of errors?
4. Were any attempts made to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors? If so, what were the results?
5. What are the computational costs associated with the “boundary expansion” heuristic? Does it scale well to very large texts?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing style is generally clear and concise.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The code is publicly available, which is a positive aspect.
