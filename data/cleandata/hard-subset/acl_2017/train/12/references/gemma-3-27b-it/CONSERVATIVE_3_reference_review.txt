This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition, motivated by observations about the simplicity and regularity of time expressions in natural language. The authors analyze four datasets and claim that time expressions are typically short, use a limited vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple, heuristically designed rules for recognition.  The method is evaluated on three datasets, demonstrating competitive, and in some cases superior, performance to established baselines.

The primary contribution of this work is a demonstration of the effectiveness of a simplified, type-based rule-based approach to time expression recognition. While rule-based methods are well-established, the authors successfully streamline the rule definition process by focusing on syntactic types and boundary expansion, achieving good performance with a relatively lightweight system. A secondary, though less impactful, contribution is the empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, which provides some support for the motivating principles of the method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** SynTime demonstrably outperforms existing state-of-the-art systems (HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime) on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. This is a significant result, demonstrating that a simplified rule-based approach can be highly effective. The high recall scores are particularly noteworthy.
2. **Lightweight and Efficient:** The authors emphasize the real-time performance of SynTime, a desirable characteristic for many NLP applications. This efficiency is a direct result of the simplified rule set and type-based approach, making it a practical alternative to more complex methods.
3. **Sound Motivation:** The empirical analysis provides a reasonable justification for the design choices in SynTime. The observations about the length, vocabulary, and syntactic regularity of time expressions lend credibility to the approach. While not groundbreaking, this analysis helps to contextualize the proposed method.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, method, and experimental setup. The description of the type system and rule design process is understandable and logical.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of using rules for time expression recognition is not new. The contribution lies in the simplification and refinement of the rule definition process.  However, the degree of novelty here is questionable. The move from semantic to syntactic typing, while logical, isn't a fundamentally new paradigm.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Modern Approaches:** While comparisons to existing rule-based systems are thorough, the paper lacks a more detailed comparison to recent neural network-based approaches for sequence tagging. Demonstrating that SynTime can compete with, or even outperform, modern machine learning methods would significantly strengthen the paper. Simply stating they are complex and require training is not a sufficient justification for omitting a comparison.
3. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The method’s reliance on POS tagging introduces a potential source of error. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the impact of POS tagging errors on the overall performance is not adequately investigated. An error analysis quantifying the effect of incorrect POS tags would be valuable.
4. **Limited Generalizability:** The performance gains on the Tweets dataset are attributed to the informal nature of the text and the prevalence of formal time expressions. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the method to other domains with more complex or varied language use. More testing on diverse datasets is necessary.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison to recent neural network-based approaches for sequence tagging, even if it requires running additional experiments?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to errors in the POS tagging process? Could you provide an error analysis quantifying the impact of incorrect POS tags?
3.  Have you considered incorporating character-level information or other features that could improve the robustness of the method to variations in spelling or grammar?
4. The paper states that the Gigaword dataset was not used because of label quality. Was any investigation done to potentially clean or filter this dataset for use in evaluation?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of Zipf’s principle of least effort feels somewhat tacked on and could be more tightly integrated with the technical contributions of the paper.
*   The GitHub link for SUTime is a directory within a larger project; providing a direct link to the SUTime code would be helpful.
* Clarify if the expansion of SynTime was done on a separate development set and not merely a re-evaluation on the test sets.
