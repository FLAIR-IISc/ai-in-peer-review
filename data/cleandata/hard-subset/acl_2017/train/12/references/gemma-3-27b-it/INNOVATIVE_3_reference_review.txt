The paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The core idea is to leverage syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) rather than semantic types, coupled with simple, heuristically-defined rules based on boundary expansion. The authors motivate this approach through an analysis of time expressions across several datasets, arguing that brevity, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior support a simplified, type-based tagging system. They demonstrate SynTime’s effectiveness through experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, showing competitive or superior performance to established baselines.

The primary contribution of this work is the *concept* of framing time expression recognition as a syntactic typing problem. While rule-based approaches are not novel, the deliberate shift from semantic-based rule design (as seen in SUTime) to a syntactic one, and the demonstration that it *works* – and even outperforms more complex systems in certain scenarios – is a valuable and potentially fruitful direction. The analysis of time expression characteristics supporting this approach is a secondary, but useful, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The syntactic typing approach to time expression recognition is a genuinely fresh perspective. The field has seen diminishing returns from increasingly complex machine learning models, and a return to carefully designed rules, informed by linguistic observation, is refreshing. The authors successfully demonstrate that a simpler model, grounded in syntactic observation, can be competitive.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime consistently outperforms the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. The particularly strong performance on the challenging Tweets dataset is noteworthy. The reported recall scores are impressive.
3. **Clear Motivation & Analysis:** The paper presents a convincing argument for the syntactic approach, grounded in observations about the properties of time expressions – their brevity, limited vocabulary, and predictable syntactic structures. This analytical foundation adds weight to the proposed method.
4. **Simplicity & Efficiency:** The authors highlight the lightweight nature and real-time performance of SynTime, which is a significant advantage for practical applications. This simplicity is a direct consequence of the core design principle.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the results are promising, the evaluation is limited to three datasets. Expanding the evaluation to other domains and languages would strengthen the generalizability claims. The justification for excluding Gigaword is weak - automatic labels aren’t ideal, but that doesn’t invalidate the data entirely.
2. **Heuristic Nature of Rules:** The "simple heuristic rules" are not presented with sufficient detail. The reader is left with a general idea, but a more precise description of the rules, potentially with examples, would aid understanding and reproducibility. While the authors emphasize simplicity, a clearer articulation of the rules is crucial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* SynTime fails would provide valuable insights for future improvements. What types of time expressions does it consistently misidentify? Are there patterns in the errors?
4. **Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches:** The baseline comparison doesn't include state-of-the-art neural models for time expression recognition. While the authors focus on rule-based and simpler ML methods, acknowledging and contrasting SynTime against contemporary neural approaches is important for positioning the work within the current landscape.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detailed examples of the "simple heuristic rules" used in SynTime? A short table or illustrative examples would be helpful.
2. What specific error patterns did you observe during evaluation? Could you provide examples of time expressions that SynTime consistently fails to recognize?
3. Have you considered evaluating SynTime against more recent neural time expression recognition models? If not, why?
4. The paper mentions the potential for expansion with keywords. How does this expansion process scale, and what are the trade-offs between adding more keywords and maintaining the simplicity of the system?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is an interesting and insightful framing device. While the paper isn't perfect, the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. This work represents a promising direction for time expression recognition. I lean towards acceptance.
