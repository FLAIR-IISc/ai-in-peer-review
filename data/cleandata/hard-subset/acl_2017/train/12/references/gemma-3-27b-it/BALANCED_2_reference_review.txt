This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets, observe characteristics like short length and small vocabulary, and leverage these observations to design a type-based approach using syntactic types and heuristic rules. The paper claims contributions in the form of (1) an analysis of time expressions supporting Zipf’s principle of least effort, (2) the SynTime system itself, a type-based tagger simplifying rule design, and (3) experimental results demonstrating SynTime's effectiveness against state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Empirical Motivation:** The analysis of the four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) provides a strong and well-supported motivation for the design of SynTime. The observations regarding the short length of time expressions, the limited vocabulary, and the prevalence of time tokens are compelling and directly inform the system's architecture. This is a significant strength, as it grounds the work in observable linguistic properties rather than purely theoretical considerations.
2. **Novel Type-Based Approach:** The concept of defining types based on syntactic behavior, rather than semantic meaning (as in SUTime), is a potentially valuable contribution. This approach appears to offer greater flexibility in rule design, as highlighted by the “boundary expansion” heuristic.  The paper clearly articulates the difference between SynTime's approach and existing methods, which is crucial for establishing novelty.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** SynTime consistently outperforms the baseline methods (HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. The particularly strong performance on Tweets is noteworthy and suggests that the system effectively captures the characteristics of informal text. The high recall scores across all datasets are also a positive indication of the system’s coverage.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Rule-Based Approach:** While the type-based approach is a reasonable idea, the core methodology remains a rule-based system. In the context of NLP, rule-based systems, while practical, are often viewed as less innovative than learning-based methods. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate how SynTime addresses the inherent limitations of rule-based systems, such as difficulty in handling unseen expressions or adapting to different domains without extensive manual rule engineering.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Heuristic Rules:** The description of the “heuristic rules” based on boundary expansion is relatively high-level. The paper states the rules are “simple,” but doesn’t provide enough specific examples to fully understand their implementation and effectiveness. A more detailed explanation of these rules would strengthen the paper and allow for a better evaluation of their contribution.
3. **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** UWTime is a relatively dated baseline and the comparison doesn’t include more recent, potentially stronger, state-of-the-art models. Including comparisons to more contemporary systems would provide a more robust assessment of SynTime’s performance. The justification for not using Gigaword is also weak; while label quality concerns are valid, it's common to acknowledge these issues and still report results.
4. **Over-reliance on SUTime for Initialization:** The reliance on SUTime for initial regular expressions raises concerns about potential biases. If SUTime has limitations in recognizing certain types of time expressions, these limitations might be inherited by SynTime.  The paper doesn't adequately discuss this potential issue.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detailed examples of the "simple heuristic rules" used in SynTime, including specific patterns and their corresponding actions?
2. How does SynTime handle ambiguous time expressions (e.g., "May" as a month vs. a modal verb)?  What mechanisms are in place to resolve such ambiguities?
3.  What is the computational complexity of SynTime? How does it scale with larger text corpora?
4. Have you investigated incorporating machine learning techniques into SynTime, perhaps to refine the heuristic rules or address the limitations of the rule-based approach?
5.  What steps were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the manual annotation for the Tweets dataset?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly recent advancements in time expression recognition beyond the mentioned baselines.
*   The discussion of Zipf’s principle of least effort, while interesting, feels somewhat tangential to the core technical contribution. It could be more tightly integrated with the design of SynTime.
*   Consider providing an ablation study to evaluate the impact of different components of SynTime (e.g., the type system, the heuristic rules).



Overall, this is a well-written and reasonably sound paper. The analysis is insightful, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the limited novelty of the overall approach and the lack of detail regarding the heuristic rules prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. With revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more specifics of the rules and including more modern baseline comparisons, this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. **I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.**
