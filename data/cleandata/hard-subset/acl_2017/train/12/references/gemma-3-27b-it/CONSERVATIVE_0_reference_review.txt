This paper proposes a rule-based time expression recognition system, SynTime, motivated by observations regarding the simplicity of time expressions and leveraging syntactic types. The core contribution appears to be a re-framing of rule-based tagging, focusing on syntactic types rather than semantic ones, and employing a heuristic, boundary-expansion approach to rule creation. A secondary contribution lies in the analysis of time expressions across four datasets, confirming expectations about their brevity and reliance on a limited vocabulary. Finally, the empirical evaluation against established baselines demonstrates competitive performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** SynTime demonstrably outperforms existing systems (HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. The reported improvements, particularly the gains in recall, are significant and suggest a practical benefit for applications requiring comprehensive time expression detection.
2. **Principled Motivation:** The paper is grounded in a sensible observation – time expressions *are* often simple. Linking this to Zipf’s principle of least effort provides a reasonable rationale for the design choices, establishing a clear connection between linguistic theory and the proposed method.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The explanation of the type-based approach and the heuristic rules is relatively straightforward. The inclusion of Figure 2 is helpful in visualizing the system's architecture.
4. **Focus on Recall:** The consistently high recall scores across all datasets are a noteworthy strength. In many NLP tasks, recall is crucial, and SynTime's ability to identify a large proportion of time expressions is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the type-based approach and heuristic rules are presented as novel, the core idea – leveraging syntactic information for time expression recognition – isn’t entirely new. UWTime also utilizes linguistic information (CCG), and the reliance on regular expressions and rule-based systems is well-established. The paper needs to more clearly articulate what genuinely differentiates SynTime from existing methods beyond the specific syntactic typing scheme.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper presents results for SynTime-I and SynTime-E (expanded version), but lacks a detailed ablation study demonstrating the impact of each component (e.g., the specific syntactic types, the boundary expansion heuristic). Understanding which aspects of SynTime contribute most to its performance is crucial for assessing its true value.
3. **Dataset Bias & Limited Generalization:** The strong performance on Tweets is highlighted, but the paper doesn't adequately address potential biases in the dataset. Tweets are inherently noisy and often contain informal language. The success on this dataset doesn't necessarily guarantee similar performance on more formal text domains. Further evaluation on a more diverse set of datasets is needed to establish generalizability.
4. **Reliance on Existing Tools/Resources:** SynTime heavily relies on SUTime for its initial regular expressions and on Stanford POS Tagger. While this isn’t inherently negative, it raises questions about the system's independence and the extent to which its performance is attributable to these external resources.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. Identifying the types of errors SynTime makes (e.g., false positives, false negatives, mis-identified expression boundaries) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of SynTime's syntactic types with those used in other rule-based systems (e.g., SUTime, HeidelTime)? Specifically, how do the types map onto semantic categories, and what benefits does the syntactic focus offer?
2.  What is the computational cost of the boundary expansion heuristic? Is there a point at which the expansion becomes inefficient?
3.  What steps were taken to ensure the quality of the manually annotated Tweets dataset? Inter-annotator agreement scores would be helpful.
4.  How does the performance of SynTime degrade when using a different POS tagger?
5.  Can you provide some examples of time expressions that SynTime correctly identifies but SUTime misses, and vice-versa? This would help illustrate the strengths and weaknesses of each system.
