This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors analyze time expressions in four datasets, identifying patterns such as short length, small vocabulary, and reliance on time tokens. Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and using heuristic rules for recognition. SynTime is evaluated against three baselines on three datasets, demonstrating competitive and in some cases superior performance, particularly on Tweets. The primary claimed contributions are the analysis of time expression characteristics, the type-based SynTime approach, and the experimental validation showing its effectiveness. I view the contribution as primarily a competent engineering effort building a rule-based system leveraging syntactic insights, rather than a groundbreaking methodological advancement. The analysis, while insightful, largely confirms previously known properties of time expressions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation of SynTime against strong baselines on multiple datasets. The results demonstrate SynTime’s competitiveness, particularly its strong recall performance. The inclusion of Tweets as a test dataset is valuable, given the unique challenges of informal text.
2. **Systematic Rule Design:** The type-based approach and the heuristic rules, driven by the observed syntactic patterns, offer a clear and organized approach to rule-based time expression recognition. The boundary expansion strategy seems practical and allows for flexibility.
3. **Detailed Analysis:** The initial analysis of the datasets regarding time expression length, vocabulary size, and reliance on time tokens provides a reasonable justification for the design choices of SynTime. While not entirely novel, it's a well-documented and helpful starting point.
4. **Lightweight and Real-time Performance:** The authors highlight SynTime's efficiency and real-time capabilities. This is a significant advantage for applications requiring low latency.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the *combination* of syntactic typing and heuristic rule design is reasonable, the core ideas aren't particularly novel. Type-based approaches have been explored in other NLP tasks, and rule-based time expression recognition is a well-established field. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new algorithmic or representational paradigm.
2. **Overstated Significance of Observations:** The claim that the observed characteristics of time expressions *prove* the principle of least effort feels like a stretch. Correlation doesn’t equal causation, and alternative explanations are possible. The connection to Zipf’s principle feels somewhat tenuous and added unnecessarily.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Knowing *where* SynTime fails (specific error types, dataset-specific issues) would be immensely valuable for understanding its limitations and guiding future improvements. A qualitative examination of misidentified time expressions is missing.
4. **Minor Implementation Details Lacking Rigor:** The reliance on Stanford POS tagger without discussing potential errors or their impact is a concern. The decision to use SUTime’s regular expressions as a starting point is mentioned but not fully justified. How were the expressions adapted or modified? What quality control was performed on the initial set of regular expressions? The handling of abbreviations and informal variants is acknowledged but lacks specifics.
5. **Inconsistent Formatting & Style:** Several minor formatting issues detract from the professionalism of the paper. (e.g., inconsistent spacing, the use of “\Q” and "\E" around "2011" in a reference, awkward phrasing). These suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors SynTime makes on each dataset?
2. What specific criteria were used to select and adapt the regular expressions from SUTime? Was any manual validation or refinement performed on these expressions?
3. How sensitive is SynTime's performance to errors in the POS tagging step? Have you experimented with different POS taggers or error-correction techniques?
4. Could you elaborate on the process of "expansion" of SynTime, including how keywords are identified and added, and how you avoid overfitting to the training data?
5. Why was Gigaword not used in the experiments? The justification provided (labels might not be ground truth) seems weak.



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is poorly formatted and contains inconsistencies.
* The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the trade-offs between rule-based and machine learning approaches to time expression recognition.
* The figures are rather basic and could be improved with better visual clarity.
* The language is sometimes awkward and could be refined for better readability.



Overall, this is a reasonably competent piece of work, but it falls short of being a truly significant contribution due to its incremental novelty and lack of in-depth analysis. The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, collectively detract from the paper's overall quality. I lean towards **rejection** due to the high bar for acceptance at ACL, given the level of detail and rigor expected. The authors would need to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the error analysis and justification of implementation choices, to significantly improve the paper’s standing.
