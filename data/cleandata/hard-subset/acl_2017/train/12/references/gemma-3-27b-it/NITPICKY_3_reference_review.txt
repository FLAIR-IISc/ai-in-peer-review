This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer leveraging syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets, observing properties like short length, small vocabulary, and frequent presence of time tokens. Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach defining time tokens, modifiers, and numerals, and design rules based on their relative positions.  Evaluation against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets shows SynTime outperforms baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, achieving comparable results on WikiWars. The primary claimed contributions are: 1) the analysis of time expressions revealing properties relating to Zipf’s principle of least effort; 2) the proposed SynTime tagger with its type-based approach; and 3) empirical demonstration of SynTime’s effectiveness. 

I believe the main contribution lies in the *detailed* analysis of time expression characteristics across multiple datasets, identifying patterns that justify a simplified, type-based rule system. The empirical evaluation, while not groundbreaking in terms of absolute performance gains, is thorough and demonstrates the viability of the approach, particularly on the more informal Tweets dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The analysis of the four datasets is meticulous and provides strong justification for the design choices in SynTime. The observations regarding expression length, vocabulary size, and the prevalence of time tokens are well-supported by data and provide a solid foundation for a simplified approach.
2. **Clear and Logical Approach:** The motivation behind SynTime – leveraging syntactic types instead of semantic ones – is clearly explained and logically connected to the observed dataset properties. The separation of time tokens, modifiers, and numerals provides a structured framework for rule creation.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The notable performance improvement on the Tweets dataset suggests the approach is particularly well-suited for less formal text where the identified characteristics (short expressions, simple vocabulary) are more pronounced. This is a practical and valuable outcome.
4. **Efficiency:** The authors emphasize the real-time performance of SynTime due to its lightweight nature. This is a key advantage for applications requiring fast processing.
5. **Thorough Evaluation:** The comparison against three established baselines across three datasets provides a robust evaluation of SynTime's capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Rule-Based Approach:** While the *application* of syntactic types is a reasonable idea, the core approach remains rule-based, a well-explored area in time expression recognition. The novelty of this particular rule-based system is incremental, rather than revolutionary. The authors don't adequately address how SynTime advances beyond existing rule-based methods beyond the syntactic typing.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The system heavily relies on the accuracy of the POS tagger. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t quantify the impact of POS tagging errors on performance. A detailed error analysis, showing the types of POS tagging errors that most frequently lead to incorrect time expression recognition, is needed.
3. **Inconsistent Reporting of Dataset Details:**  The statement about Gigaword not being used because of potentially inaccurate labels is problematic. This should have been explicitly stated in the dataset description (Section 3.1) and a justification for excluding it provided *before* presenting the dataset table. It feels like an afterthought.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the results are presented, there's a lack of detailed error analysis. What *types* of time expressions does SynTime struggle with? Where do the errors originate? Addressing these questions would provide valuable insights into the system's limitations and potential areas for improvement. 
5. **Minor Formatting and Clarity Issues:** The paper contains some grammatical inconsistencies and awkward phrasing (e.g., "Time expression recognition main involves..."). While not major, these detract from the overall polish.  Also, some citations are not formatted correctly (e.g., "Alonso et al.,? 2011").

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors SynTime makes and identifying the primary causes (e.g., POS tagging errors, limitations in rule coverage)?
2. How does the performance of SynTime degrade as the accuracy of the POS tagger decreases? Have you conducted any experiments to evaluate this sensitivity?
3. You mention that SynTime is "extremely lightweight." Can you provide concrete metrics (e.g., memory usage, processing time per sentence) to quantify this claim?
4.  Can you elaborate on the specific improvements made to SUTime token regular expressions used for SynTime's initialization? Which token types were particularly impactful to add and why?
5. The statement on Gigaword seems dismissive. Were any attempts made to filter or correct the labels within the Gigaword dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

* The references section is poorly formatted, with many inconsistencies in style.
* The figure captions are brief and could provide more context.
* There are numerous instances of unnecessary repetition throughout the paper.  Conciseness would improve readability.

Overall, this paper presents a well-motivated and reasonably effective approach to time expression recognition. However, the limited novelty of the method and the lack of detailed error analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding error analysis, POS tagging sensitivity, and the justification for excluding the Gigaword dataset, to strengthen their case.
