The paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system leveraging syntactic types instead of semantic ones, combined with a heuristic boundary expansion approach. The authors motivate their work by analyzing time expression characteristics across several datasets, observing short lengths, limited vocabularies, and consistent syntactic behavior. The primary claimed contribution is a simplified rule-based approach that achieves competitive performance, particularly on the Twitter dataset.

I see the primary contribution as the insightful analysis of time expressions and the resulting *hypothesis* that syntactic typing offers a viable alternative to semantic typing in rule-based systems. While rule-based systems aren’t novel, the emphasis on syntactic structure, driven by the observed characteristics of time expressions, is a relatively fresh perspective.  A secondary contribution lies in the practical demonstration of this approach with SynTime, and the empirical results suggesting its effectiveness, especially in noisy environments like Twitter.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of prioritizing syntactic types over semantic ones in designing time expression recognition rules is a genuinely interesting and potentially impactful shift.  Most rule-based systems rely heavily on semantic definitions (e.g., frequency modifiers), and the authors convincingly argue for a different, simpler foundation.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on Twitter:** The reported performance on the Twitter dataset is particularly compelling. The significant improvement over baselines suggests the approach is well-suited to the informal, often abbreviated language found in social media. This hints at a robustness that might be lacking in systems relying on strict semantic parsing.
3. **Insightful Data Analysis:** The analysis of time expressions across the four datasets provides a solid empirical foundation for the proposed approach. The observed characteristics (short lengths, small vocabulary) directly inform the design of SynTime and bolster the argument for simplification.  The link to Zipf’s principle of least effort is also a nice touch.
4. **Lightweight and Real-time:** The authors highlight SynTime’s efficiency, running in real-time. This is a practical benefit, particularly for applications requiring fast processing of text streams. While not a core theoretical contribution, it adds to the paper’s value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the Twitter results are strong, performance on the more standard datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars) is only competitive, not groundbreaking. This raises questions about the generalizability of the approach. A more thorough evaluation on a wider range of datasets, including those with more complex time expressions, would be beneficial.
2. **Heuristic Nature of Rules:** The "simple heuristic rules" are vaguely described.  More detail about *how* these rules are generated and refined would be helpful. The lack of transparency makes it difficult to assess the system’s robustness and potential for improvement. While simplicity is a goal, it shouldn’t come at the cost of clarity.
3. **Reliance on Pre-existing Tools:** The system relies on Stanford POS tagging and leverages SUTime for initial token definitions. This introduces dependencies and potential error propagation. While acknowledging this is reasonable, the impact of these external components on performance isn’t sufficiently discussed.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:**  A detailed error analysis would be extremely valuable.  Identifying the types of time expressions that SynTime struggles with would provide crucial insights for future development. Are there specific syntactic constructions that consistently cause errors?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more specific examples of the heuristic rules used in SynTime? A table or pseudocode would be helpful.
2. How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to the accuracy of the POS tagger? Have you experimented with different taggers or investigated the impact of tagging errors?
3. What is the computational complexity of the boundary expansion algorithm? How does it scale with the length of the input text?
4. Can you elaborate on the challenges encountered when applying SynTime to languages other than English? Are the observed patterns in time expression structure universal?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference formatting is inconsistent.
* The discussion of related work is adequate but could be more focused. For example, the section on time expression normalization seems somewhat tangential.
* While the paper emphasizes simplicity, a more rigorous comparison with other lightweight time expression recognition systems (e.g., those based on finite-state transducers) would strengthen the evaluation.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel idea and encouraging results. The focus on syntactic structure is a refreshing perspective in the field of time expression recognition. Despite some weaknesses in the evaluation and description of the rules, the paper's potential impact justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above. The novelty of the approach outweighs the methodological imperfections.
