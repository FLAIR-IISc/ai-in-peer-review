## Review for ACL: Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

**Summary:**

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors begin with an analysis of time expressions across four datasets, observing characteristics like short length, small vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior.  Based on these observations (framed within Zipf's principle of least effort), they propose a “type-based” approach defining types based on syntactic roles (time token, modifier, numeral) rather than semantic meaning, and implementing simple, heuristically-designed rules. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, demonstrating competitive and in some cases superior performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The paper provides a concise and useful analysis of time expression characteristics across multiple datasets.  This strengthens the rationale behind the design choices of SynTime.
2. **Syntactic Typing Approach:** The shift in focus from semantic to syntactic typing for rule definition is a reasonable and potentially beneficial design decision, offering a different perspective compared to existing rule-based systems.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** Achieving competitive results, especially notably high recall, on the challenging Twitter dataset is a positive outcome. This suggests the system is reasonably robust to the noise and informality of social media text.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Justification:** The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the observed properties of time expressions and linking them to the system's design. The connection to Zipf’s principle adds a compelling theoretical grounding.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The analysis of the datasets is straightforward and well-documented. The experimental setup is reasonable, utilizing established baseline methods and evaluation metrics.
3. **Practicality & Efficiency:** The authors highlight SynTime’s simplicity and real-time performance, which are attractive qualities for a time expression tagger. This is a practical advantage over more complex machine learning approaches.
4. **Detailed Explanation of Rules:** The paper provides sufficient detail regarding the type definitions and heuristic rules used in SynTime, enabling reproducibility and future extension.  The explanation of the boundary expansion approach is clear.
5. **Focus on Recall:** The emphasis on achieving high recall is appropriate, as missing time expressions can be particularly detrimental in downstream applications like event extraction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:**  While the syntactic typing approach is a worthwhile exploration, the core concept of a rule-based time expression tagger is not novel. The key contribution lies in *how* the rules are defined, and the improvement over existing systems is incremental rather than revolutionary.  The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what fundamentally differentiates SynTime beyond the typing scheme.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the results demonstrate SynTime’s effectiveness, there is a lack of detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* SynTime struggles (types of errors, problematic cases) would provide valuable insights for future improvement and a more nuanced understanding of its limitations.  For example, the authors mention WikiWars poses challenges due to descriptive expressions; this needs to be elaborated with examples.
3. **Over-reliance on SUTime’s Resources:** The initialization of SynTime with rules extracted from SUTime raises concerns about originality. While acknowledging the source, the paper doesn’t adequately demonstrate how SynTime significantly builds *upon* these resources to achieve its performance gains.  A more detailed comparison of the rule sets would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper would be strengthened by an ablation study to assess the impact of different components of SynTime. For instance, how much does the syntactic typing contribute to performance compared to a rule-based system with simpler type definitions?
5. **POS Tagging Dependency:** The system’s reliance on POS tagging introduces a potential point of failure.  The authors acknowledge this, but a quantitative analysis of the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime’s performance would be helpful.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Rule Complexity:** Can you provide a quantitative comparison of the number of rules in SynTime versus SUTime (before and after initialization)? This would help assess the simplification achieved by the type-based approach.
2. **Error Examples:** Please provide a few concrete examples of time expressions that SynTime fails to recognize, and explain *why* the system struggles with these cases.
3. **Generalizability:** How well do you expect SynTime to generalize to other domains (e.g., legal documents, scientific papers) without further adaptation?
4. **Ablation Study:**  Have you considered conducting an ablation study to evaluate the individual contributions of the syntactic typing scheme and the heuristic rules?  If not, why?
5. **POS Tagging Robustness:** Have you investigated the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime's performance? If so, can you share the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The formatting is generally good, but some references seem incomplete (e.g., "Alonso et al., 2011").
*  The phrase "extremely light-weight" seems somewhat informal for an academic paper.
*  Consider expanding the discussion of related work to include more recent approaches, particularly those based on deep learning.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of time expression recognition. The authors’ analysis is insightful, the proposed system is well-designed and implemented, and the experimental results are promising, especially on the Twitter dataset. While the novelty of the approach is somewhat limited, the paper offers a valuable alternative to existing methods and provides a clear rationale for its design choices.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the need for more detailed error analysis and a clearer demonstration of the system's contributions beyond leveraging SUTime’s resources.
