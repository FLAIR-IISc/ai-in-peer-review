This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system, motivated by observations about the simplicity and regularity of time expressions across several datasets. The core contribution is a type-based approach to rule definition, focusing on syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and heuristic boundary expansion, rather than semantic categories or fixed-length patterns common in existing rule-based systems. The authors demonstrate improved performance over several baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, with comparable results on WikiWars.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Motivation:** The analysis of time expression characteristics (short length, limited vocabulary, reliance on time tokens, consistent syntactic behavior) provides a reasonable justification for the proposed approach. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is interesting, though not groundbreaking. This analysis, while not entirely novel, is a solid foundation for their system design.
2. **Competitive Performance:**  The reported results on TimeBank and, particularly, Tweets are encouraging. The significant improvements in recall on these datasets suggest that SynTime effectively captures time expressions missed by existing systems. This is a practical benefit and a noteworthy result.
3. **Lightweight and Efficient:** The authors correctly emphasize the simplicity and efficiency of SynTime, particularly its ability to run in real-time. This is valuable in scenarios where computational resources are limited or rapid processing is required.  While efficiency isn’t a primary evaluation criterion, it’s a positive attribute.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivations, methodology, and experimental setup. The figures illustrating the system overview and rule application are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of a type-based, rule-based system isn’t entirely new. Existing systems, such as SUTime and UWTime, already incorporate elements of rule-based processing and linguistic information. The novelty lies primarily in *how* types are defined (syntactically rather than semantically) and the heuristic rule expansion, but these contributions are incremental. The authors don’t sufficiently distinguish SynTime from existing work beyond these nuances.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While three baselines are used, the comparison feels somewhat superficial. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into *why* SynTime outperforms these baselines. Is it specifically the syntactic typing? The boundary expansion? A more detailed error analysis, comparing SynTime’s errors to those of the baselines, would be highly beneficial.
3. **Overreliance on Existing Resources:** The initialization of SynTime relies heavily on the regular expressions from SUTime. This raises concerns about how much of the performance gain is due to the new type system and heuristic rules versus simply inheriting the strengths of a well-established baseline. A more thorough ablation study, starting from a minimal rule set and incrementally adding components, would better isolate the impact of each innovation.
4. **Limited Generalizability:** The strong performance on Tweets is presented as a major advantage. However, the characteristics of tweets (short length, informal language) might make them particularly well-suited to SynTime’s approach. The performance on WikiWars, which is comparable to the baselines, suggests that the system’s advantages may not generalize to all text types.  Testing on more diverse datasets is crucial.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** While the F1 scores are reported, there is no indication of statistical significance testing. Given the potential for random variation, it’s important to demonstrate that the observed improvements are statistically reliable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, comparing the types of errors made by SynTime and the baseline systems?
2.  What is the impact of initializing SynTime with SUTime’s regular expressions?  Have you performed an ablation study to isolate the contribution of your type system and heuristic rules?
3.  How does the performance of SynTime vary across different domains or genres of text? Have you tested it on datasets beyond TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets?
4.  Have you conducted statistical significance tests to confirm that the observed improvements in F1 score are reliable?
5. What are the computational costs (memory usage, processing time) of SynTime compared to the baseline systems, particularly for large-scale text processing?
