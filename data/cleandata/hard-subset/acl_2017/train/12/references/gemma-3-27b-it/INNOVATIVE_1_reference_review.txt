## Review

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The core idea—leveraging syntactic types rather than semantic categories for rule definition, coupled with a heuristic boundary expansion approach—is genuinely interesting and potentially impactful. The authors convincingly argue that time expressions adhere to the principle of least effort, justifying their focus on simplicity and generality. The empirical results, particularly the strong recall on the Tweet dataset, are encouraging.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A novel type-based approach to time expression recognition:** Framing the problem through syntactic types—time token, modifier, and numeral—is a refreshing perspective, moving away from traditional semantic categorization. This allows for more flexible and extensible rule design.
2. **Demonstrated effectiveness, especially on noisy data:** SynTime outperforms strong baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, with particularly impressive results on the challenging Tweet dataset. This suggests the approach is robust and adaptable to real-world text.
3. **Insightful analysis of time expression characteristics:** The observations regarding length, vocabulary size, and syntactic behavior provide a solid foundation for the proposed method and highlight key properties of temporal language.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Idea:** The central premise of using syntactic types is a significant departure from existing rule-based and learning-based approaches. This demonstrates intellectual risk-taking and could potentially inspire new directions in the field.
2. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** Achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on the Tweet dataset is noteworthy. The inherent noise and informality of social media data makes this a particularly challenging test case.
3. **Clear Motivation and Justification:** The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is well-articulated and provides a compelling rationale for the design choices.
4. **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The authors emphasize the lightweight nature and real-time performance of SynTime, a valuable attribute for practical applications.
5. **Thorough Analysis:** The initial data analysis provides good support for the approach. The observations about the limited vocabulary and short length of time expressions are relevant and convincing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the results on Tweets are strong, the evaluation on WikiWars is less impressive. The paper does not fully explore *why* SynTime struggles with this dataset—a deeper analysis of error cases would be beneficial.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  The paper presents SynTime-I and SynTime-E, but doesn’t fully dissect the contribution of the added keywords. A more rigorous ablation study, evaluating the impact of each keyword or keyword category, would strengthen the claims.
3. **Dependence on POS Tagging:** The reliance on POS tags introduces a potential point of failure. The authors acknowledge this, but don't thoroughly explore the impact of POS tagging errors on performance. A sensitivity analysis examining the effect of different POS taggers or variations in tagging accuracy would be useful.
4. **Rule Definition Still Somewhat Ad-Hoc:** While the authors emphasize the heuristic nature of rule design, the process feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic method for generating or refining rules, perhaps informed by the initial data analysis, could improve the robustness and generalizability of the system.
5. **Limited Comparison to Modern Learning-Based Approaches:** While the comparisons to SUTime, HeidelTime, and UWTime are valuable, the field has seen advancements in deep learning-based approaches to temporal information extraction. Comparing SynTime to a more recent and powerful neural model would provide a more comprehensive assessment.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by SynTime on the WikiWars dataset? What types of time expressions are most frequently misidentified?
2.  Could you elaborate on the process for selecting the keywords added to SynTime-E? Were there any specific criteria or heuristics used?
3.  How sensitive is SynTime to errors in POS tagging? Have you experimented with different POS taggers or evaluated the impact of varying POS tagging accuracy?
4.  The paper mentions the possibility of applying SynTime to other languages. What adjustments would be necessary to adapt the system to a different language with different syntactic structures?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clear. The figures are helpful in illustrating the system architecture and rule application. The reference list is comprehensive. I would encourage the authors to release the code and the expanded keyword list to facilitate reproducibility and further research.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of temporal information extraction. The novel type-based approach, strong performance on challenging data, and insightful analysis outweigh the minor flaws. I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more in-depth analysis of the WikiWars results and exploring the sensitivity of the system to POS tagging errors. The potential impact of this work, and the boldness of the approach, make it a worthwhile addition to the ACL conference.
