- Proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer using syntactic types and simple heuristic rules; outperforms baselines on three datasets with strong recall.

- Empirical analysis shows time expressions are typically short, use limited vocabulary, contain “time tokens,” and have consistent syntactic behavior.

- Introduces syntactic type definitions (time token, modifier, numeral) and a heuristic rule-expansion approach for recognition.

- Demonstrates strong performance particularly on Tweets and TimeBank, highlighting effectiveness on informal text.

- Strong motivation and justification grounded in dataset analysis and linkage to Zipf’s Principle of Least Effort.

- Clear and concise methodology with an understandable type-based architecture and rule-design process.

- Competitive results with consistently high recall across datasets.

- Lightweight and efficient implementation suitable for real-time use.

- Solid experimental setup with multiple datasets and comparisons including UWTime.

- Limited novelty: core rule-based approach is not new and paper lacks depth on how the type system overcomes traditional rule-based limitations.

- Dataset limitations: reliance on only three datasets (Gigaword excluded) limits generalizability.

- Lacks detailed error analysis to show where SynTime fails or which error types baselines avoid.

- Dependence on POS tagging introduces potential errors; impact and robustness strategies are not explored.

- Question: Provide concrete examples where SynTime handles cases that systems like SUTime miss and explain why.

- Question: Quantify the impact of POS-tagging errors and any robustness measures tested.

- Question: Provide a detailed error analysis with examples of time expressions SynTime struggles with.

- Question: Any plans to incorporate machine learning for type expansion or rule refinement?

- References formatting could be more consistent.

- Inclusion of a code repository is a definite plus.

- Paper is well written and easy to understand.
