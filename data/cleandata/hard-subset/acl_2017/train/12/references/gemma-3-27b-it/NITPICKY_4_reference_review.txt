This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer, motivated by observations about the characteristics of time expressions in four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The core idea is to define syntactic types for time-related tokens (time token, modifier, numeral) and use simple, heuristically designed rules for recognition. The paper claims contributions in (1) the analysis of time expressions supporting the principle of least effort, (2) the proposal of a type-based time expression tagger, and (3) demonstrating SynTime’s effectiveness against state-of-the-art baselines.

While the analysis of time expression characteristics is somewhat interesting, and the idea of a type-based approach isn’t entirely novel, the paper suffers from a multitude of small but significant flaws that collectively lead me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Analysis:** The initial data analysis, while not groundbreaking, provides some support for the intuition that time expressions are relatively simple structures. The observation regarding short expression length and small vocabulary is reasonable.
2. **Lightweight Implementation:** The authors correctly highlight the potential for a lightweight and real-time implementation due to the simple heuristic rules. This could be an advantage in resource-constrained environments.
3. **Competitive Performance:** SynTime achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, results to existing baselines on the tested datasets, specifically showing better recall. This is a positive outcome.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of using types and rules for time expression recognition is not new. SUTime, explicitly mentioned and compared against, *also* uses regular expressions over tokens, albeit with a different rule-design philosophy. The claimed distinction between “syntactic” vs. “semantic” types feels superficial; both approaches ultimately categorize tokens for rule application. The "boundary expansion" heuristic is a minor variation, not a fundamental innovation.
2. **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup has several issues. Firstly, Gigaword was excluded because of label quality concerns, but no detail is given about *how* this determination was made. Secondly, the training data split for UWTime is not clearly justified. Why only 500 annotated tweets for training? A more thorough evaluation with larger datasets, and perhaps cross-validation, would be crucial.
3. **Overstated Claims & Simplification:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes the simplicity of SynTime and its rules. While simplicity is desirable, it often comes at the cost of expressiveness and robustness. The authors gloss over the intricacies of rule design and the potential for rule conflicts. The claim of “real-time” performance is made without providing any actual runtime measurements.
4. **Presentation and Writing Quality:** The paper contains numerous instances of awkward phrasing, grammatical errors, and inconsistencies in terminology. For example, the repeated use of “observations provide evidence *in terms of* time expression…” is clunky. The figures are poorly labeled and lack clarity. The citation style is also inconsistent.
5. **Minor Technical Issues:** The reliance on Stanford POS tagging introduces a dependency and a potential source of error. The performance could be affected by the POS tagger's inaccuracies. Furthermore, the treatment of commas and conjunctions within the rules appears simplistic and may lead to incorrect recognition. The manual expansion process is not well described, and its impact on performance is only partially analyzed.
6. **Limited Error Analysis:** There's no error analysis presented. Understanding *where* SynTime fails would provide valuable insight and guide future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison of SynTime’s rule-design process with that of SUTime, highlighting the specific limitations of SUTime that SynTime addresses?
2.  What were the specific criteria used to determine that the Gigaword dataset’s labels were unreliable? Can you provide evidence to support this claim?
3.  Please provide runtime measurements for SynTime on the different datasets to substantiate the claim of "real-time" performance.
4.  What is the specific strategy for handling ambiguous cases where a token can belong to multiple syntactic types?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of abbreviations (e.g., “POS”) without prior definition is unhelpful.
*   The references are formatted inconsistently and contain errors.
*   The writing style would benefit from a thorough revision by a native English speaker.



In conclusion, while SynTime shows some promise, the lack of significant novelty, the methodological shortcomings, and the numerous presentation issues preclude acceptance in its current form. The paper reads as a preliminary study rather than a mature research contribution.
