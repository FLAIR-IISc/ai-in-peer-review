- Paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition using syntactic types and simple heuristics.  
- Authors analyze four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and observe time expressions are short, use limited vocabulary, and show syntactic consistency.  
- Main contribution: empirical analysis of time expressions across datasets that motivates design choices.  
- Main contribution: shift from semantic to syntactic type definitions (time token, modifier, numeral) for rule creation.  
- Main contribution: SynTime achieves competitive or superior performance to baselines, especially on noisy Tweets data.  
- Strength: strong motivation grounded in data analysis and connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort.  
- Strength: clear, concise presentation with helpful figures explaining architecture and rules.  
- Strength: thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets and standard metrics.  
- Strength: lightweight, real-time, and efficient due to simple heuristic rules.  
- Strength: demonstrated improvement over strong baselines in reported F1 scores.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—core approach remains rule-based amid a trend toward deep learning; theoretical advantage over learned methods not well defended.  
- Weakness: no clear ablation study to quantify the impact of added keywords in SynTime-E.  
- Weakness: reliance on POS tagging not thoroughly analyzed; potential sensitivity to tagging errors unquantified.  
- Weakness: limited error analysis; lacks detailed breakdown of failure cases, particularly on WikiWars.  
- Questions for authors: provide ablation quantifying SynTime-E keyword impact.  
- Questions for authors: quantify SynTime’s sensitivity to POS tagging errors and performance degradation.  
- Questions for authors: specify which time expression types SynTime struggles with and provide examples and mitigation strategies.  
- Questions for authors: clarify advantages of rule-based SynTime versus neural NER approaches and how they could complement each other.  
- Additional comment: reference list is long with many self-citations; could be more concise.  
- Additional comment: Gigaword was mentioned but not used—explain its exclusion.  
- Overall assessment: well-motivated, empirically validated work recommended for accept with minor revisions to address noted weaknesses.
