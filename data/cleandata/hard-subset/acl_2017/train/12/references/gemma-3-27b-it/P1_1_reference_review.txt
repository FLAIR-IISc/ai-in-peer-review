## Peer Review for "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

**Summary:**

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors motivate their work through an analysis of four datasets, observing that time expressions are generally short, utilize a limited vocabulary, frequently contain time tokens, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime leverages syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.  The paper demonstrates that SynTime outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, achieving comparable results on WikiWars, and achieving high recall across all datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expression Characteristics:** The detailed analysis of time expression length, vocabulary size, and syntactic patterns across diverse datasets provides valuable insights into the nature of temporal language. This is a significant contribution, grounding the proposed system in observable linguistic properties.
2. **Type-Based Rule Design for Time Expression Recognition:** The authors propose a novel approach to rule-based time expression recognition by focusing on *syntactic types* rather than semantic categories. This is a potentially useful shift in perspective, as it allows for simpler, more flexible rules.
3. **Strong Experimental Results, Particularly on Tweets:** The experimental results, especially the performance on the challenging Tweet dataset, demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime and validate the principles behind its design.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Motivation:** The analysis in Section 3 convincingly demonstrates the characteristics of time expressions, justifying the design choices in SynTime. The principle of least effort is a compelling framework for understanding these patterns.
2. **Simplicity and Efficiency:**  SynTime's reliance on simple heuristic rules and syntactic types, coupled with its reported real-time performance, is a significant strength. This contrasts with more complex machine learning approaches or heavily engineered rule-based systems.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The substantial improvement over baselines on the Tweets dataset is particularly noteworthy, suggesting the system effectively handles the noise and informality of social media text.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its evaluation.
5. **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The inclusion of strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) provides a robust benchmark for evaluating SynTime's performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Rule-Based Approach:** While the *type-based* rule design is interesting, the underlying approach remains fundamentally rule-based. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing rule-based systems beyond the specific syntactic typing approach.  The authors acknowledge this but downplay it.
2. **Reliance on POS Tagging:** The system’s performance is dependent on the accuracy of the POS tagger.  The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but doesn’t quantify its impact or explore methods to mitigate it.  Errors in POS tagging could lead to significant errors in time expression recognition.
3. **Limited Scalability of Rule Expansion:** The manual rule expansion process, while acknowledged as potentially feasible due to the small vocabulary, might not scale well to significantly different domains or languages. More automated methods for rule adaptation would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* SynTime fails could provide insights for future improvement.  For example, are there specific types of time expressions that consistently pose challenges?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you quantify the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime’s performance?  Have you experimented with different POS taggers or methods to handle noisy POS tags?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for automated rule adaptation. Could you elaborate on potential strategies for this, beyond manual keyword addition?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis? What types of time expressions does SynTime struggle with, and why?  Are there common error patterns?
4.  You mention that the Tweet dataset's labels are based on SUTime with manual correction. What was the error rate of SUTime on the initial sample, and how many corrections were made during annotation? This would help understand the quality of the ground truth.
5.  How does the performance of SynTime-I compare to SUTime on datasets *other* than Tweets, where the benefits of the syntactic typing approach seem less pronounced? This will clarify the actual contribution of the new approach.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The Github link provided in the paper is for the Stanford CoreNLP project in general, not specifically for the code related to SUTime. A link to the specific SUTime implementation would be helpful.
*   Consider expanding the related work section to include more recent approaches to time expression recognition, particularly those based on deep learning.
