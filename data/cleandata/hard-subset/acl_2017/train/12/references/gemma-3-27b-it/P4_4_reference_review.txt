This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors analyze time expressions in four datasets, observing that they are typically short, use a limited vocabulary, frequently contain time tokens, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach defining three types – time token, modifier, and numeral – and developing simple heuristic rules for recognition. The system is evaluated on three datasets, demonstrating performance competitive with and often exceeding state-of-the-art baselines. The primary contributions of this work are: (1) an analysis of time expressions revealing characteristics aligned with the principle of least effort; (2) the SynTime system, a type-based rule-based tagger, and (3) empirical validation of SynTime’s effectiveness against established baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets is a significant strength. The observations regarding length, vocabulary size, and the presence of time tokens provide a strong rationale for the design choices in SynTime. Linking these observations to Zipf’s principle of least effort is a clever and compelling justification.
2. **Effective System Design:** The type-based approach, with its focus on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning (as in SUTime), appears to be a well-motivated and effective design. The heuristic rules, described as boundary expansion, provide flexibility and efficiency, resulting in real-time performance.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime consistently outperforms or matches the performance of strong baselines on the evaluated datasets, particularly showing improvements on the Tweets dataset. Achieving better recall across all datasets is a notable accomplishment, demonstrating robust recognition capabilities.
4. **Lightweight and Expandable:** The simplicity and lightweight nature of SynTime are advantageous. The authors clearly explain the straightforward expansion mechanism which enables adaptation to new domains and datasets. The focus on minimal manual effort required for expansion is also compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Rule-Based Approach:** While the type-based approach and heuristic rules are well-executed, the fundamental methodology remains rule-based. In a field increasingly dominated by machine learning, the novelty of a rule-based system requires stronger justification. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the advantages and disadvantages of the rule-based approach relative to machine learning, specifically addressing limitations in scenarios with limited training data.
2. **Reliance on External Tools:** The system's reliance on external tools for POS tagging introduces a potential source of error. While acknowledged, the impact of POS tagging inaccuracies on the overall performance of SynTime is not thoroughly investigated. A more robust evaluation could include experiments with different POS taggers or analysis of errors attributable to POS tagging.
3. **Dataset Selection and Justification:** The decision to exclude the Gigaword dataset due to potentially inaccurate labels is questionable. A more thorough investigation of the label quality, or even using the dataset with caution and reporting results separately, could have strengthened the evaluation.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results demonstrate strong performance, a more detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the system’s weaknesses. Identifying specific types of time expressions that SynTime struggles to recognize would inform future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper highlights the simplicity and low computational cost of SynTime. Could you provide more specific details on the runtime performance (e.g., processing time per sentence or per document) compared to the baseline methods?
2. Could you elaborate on the process of choosing the specific regular expressions for the initial SynTime initialization? How were potential ambiguities and edge cases handled during this process?
3. You mention that SynTime could be extended to perform time expression normalization. Could you provide a brief outline of how existing rule systems for normalization could be integrated with SynTime?
4. Considering the reliance on POS tagging, how sensitive is SynTime’s performance to variations in POS tagging accuracy? Have you explored the use of alternative POS taggers or methods for mitigating the impact of POS tagging errors?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The code availability (GitHub link) is a positive aspect.
*   A table summarizing the key differences between SynTime and the baselines (SUTime, HeidelTime, UWTime) would be beneficial.
