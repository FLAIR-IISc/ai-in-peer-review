This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets, observing characteristics like short length, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and crafting simple, heuristic rules for recognition. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets show SynTime outperforms baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars, with consistently high recall.

The primary contribution is the exploration of a simplified rule-based approach leveraging syntactic types rather than semantic meanings, coupled with a boundary expansion heuristic for rule design. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical analysis of time expression characteristics across diverse datasets, reinforcing the applicability of a lightweight approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime demonstrably outperforms existing state-of-the-art systems on two of the three datasets (TimeBank and Tweets), and achieves comparable performance on the third (WikiWars). The consistent high recall across datasets is particularly noteworthy. This indicates a robust and effective approach, especially for capturing a broad range of time expressions.
2. **Novelty in Rule Definition:**  The distinction between defining types based on syntactic behavior versus semantic meaning is a valid point of differentiation. While rule-based systems are common, the emphasis on syntactic typing and the boundary expansion heuristic offer a potentially valuable alternative to traditional approaches.
3. **Lightweight and Efficient:** The paper highlights the simplicity and efficiency of SynTime, emphasizing its ability to run in real time. This is a significant advantage, particularly for applications requiring rapid processing of textual data.
4. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The initial dataset analysis is thorough, providing a solid foundation for the design choices made in SynTime. The observations regarding expression length, vocabulary size, and syntactic consistency are well-supported by the presented data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** While the syntactic typing is a reasonable approach, the underlying concept of rule-based tagging is hardly novel. The paper doesn’t sufficiently demonstrate *how much* benefit derives specifically from the syntactic typing versus a well-designed semantic rule-based system.  A more compelling comparison against a similarly well-engineered, purely semantic rule-based system would be needed.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Heuristic Rules:** The description of the "boundary expansion" heuristic remains vague. The paper states it's simple, but doesn’t provide concrete examples illustrating how these rules are formed or applied in different scenarios.  A more detailed explanation, perhaps with example rule templates, is crucial for understanding the system's functionality.  A table summarizing the key rules would be extremely beneficial.
3. **Dataset Selection & Gigaword Omission:** The justification for excluding the Gigaword dataset (“labels…may not be ground truth”) is weak and unsatisfying. It’s a significant dataset and excluding it without further investigation or discussion is questionable.  Further, the Tweet dataset annotation process is not detailed enough. What are the inter-annotator agreement scores?  This is vital information for assessing the reliability of the evaluation results on that dataset.
4. **Minor Formatting and Style Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the professionalism of the paper. For example, inconsistent use of bolding, reference formatting inconsistencies (e.g., “Alonso et al\.,? 2011”), and occasional grammatical errors (e.g., "Time expression main involves"). While individually small, these accumulate and create a somewhat careless impression.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance metrics but lacks any discussion of the types of errors SynTime makes.  A detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the system's limitations and potential areas for improvement.  Are there specific types of time expressions that are consistently misidentified?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the "boundary expansion" heuristic, including example rule templates?
2. What was the inter-annotator agreement score for the Tweet dataset annotation?
3. Why was a full investigation into the quality of Gigaword labels not performed before its exclusion?
4. Could you provide a comparison of SynTime's performance against a well-engineered, purely semantic rule-based system on the same datasets?
5. What is the runtime of SynTime on a single sentence and on the entire datasets? Provide specific numbers.



**Additional Comments:**

* Please carefully proofread the paper for formatting inconsistencies and grammatical errors.
* A visualization of the type system (e.g., a diagram) would be helpful.
* The reference list is extremely long and contains many entries with unclear formatting. Please standardize the formatting.
* The use of "\Q" and "\E" around "2011" in the references is unnecessary and should be removed.
