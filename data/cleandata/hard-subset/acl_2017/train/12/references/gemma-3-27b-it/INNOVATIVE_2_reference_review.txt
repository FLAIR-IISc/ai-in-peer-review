The paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer leveraging syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expression characteristics across several datasets, observing short lengths, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. They argue this supports a type-based approach, defining types based on syntactic roles rather than semantic meaning, as typically done in other rule-based systems. The core contribution is a simplified, flexible, and lightweight rule system based on boundary expansion. Experiments demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art systems, particularly on the Tweets dataset.

The most significant contribution of this work is the **novel framing of time expression recognition as a syntactic problem**, rather than solely a semantic one. While rule-based systems exist, the explicit focus on syntactic types and the resulting simplification of rule design represent a genuinely different approach. The observation that time expressions adhere to principles of linguistic economy (Zipf’s principle of least effort) provides a strong motivation for this approach. 

A second noteworthy contribution is the **demonstration of strong performance, particularly on noisy data (Tweets)**. Achieving competitive or superior results to established systems, particularly in a challenging domain like Twitter, lends credibility to the proposed method. The emphasis on recall is also positive, indicating a system that captures a broad range of time expressions.

Finally, the **thorough analysis of time expression characteristics** provides valuable insights. While such analyses aren’t entirely novel, the clear presentation and connection to the proposed method strengthens the paper. The empirical validation of these observations in multiple datasets is a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The type-based, syntactic-focused approach is a clear departure from traditional rule-based or machine learning methods. This makes the paper stand out.
2. **Performance on Tweets:** The strong performance on the Tweets dataset is compelling. It suggests the method is robust and generalizable, and highlights its potential for real-world applications dealing with informal text.
3. **Clear Motivation:** The authors effectively connect their method to linguistic principles, grounding it in a theoretical framework.
4. **Simplicity & Efficiency:** The emphasis on simplicity and real-time performance is a practical advantage, and is clearly articulated.
5. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The dataset analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method and demonstrates a good understanding of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation:** While the results are positive, the evaluation is limited to three datasets. Expanding the evaluation to include more diverse datasets (e.g., biomedical text, historical texts) would strengthen the claims.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper doesn’t include an ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of SynTime (e.g., the impact of syntactic typing versus boundary expansion). It’s difficult to determine which aspects are most crucial for performance.
3. **Comparative Analysis Depth:** The comparison to baselines is somewhat superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the errors made by SynTime and the baselines could provide valuable insights. Why does SynTime perform well in certain cases but not others?
4. **Dependence on POS Tagging:** The method relies on accurate POS tagging. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but doesn’t thoroughly investigate its impact on performance. How robust is SynTime to POS tagging errors?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors SynTime makes, and how these errors differ from those made by the baseline systems?
2. How sensitive is SynTime to the quality of the POS tagging? Have you experimented with different POS taggers?
3.  An ablation study would be helpful to determine the individual contributions of the syntactic typing and the heuristic boundary expansion rules. Could you provide results for variants of SynTime that omit one or the other?
4.  What is the computational complexity of the boundary expansion heuristic? Can you provide a rough estimate of its scaling behavior with respect to sentence length?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The references are comprehensive. The github link is a valuable asset. While the method isn’t a dramatic breakthrough, the novelty of the approach and the solid experimental results make it a worthwhile contribution. Given the novelty, I lean towards acceptance, despite the weaknesses, and believe the authors' response to the questions could further strengthen the paper.
