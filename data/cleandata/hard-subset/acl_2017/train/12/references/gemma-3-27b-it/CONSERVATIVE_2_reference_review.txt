This paper proposes a rule-based time expression recognition system, SynTime, motivated by observations about the simplicity and regularity of time expressions in four datasets. The core contribution lies in framing time expression recognition through syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and applying simple, heuristically designed rules based on these types and their relative positions. The authors also present a thorough analysis of time expression characteristics across different corpora, supporting their design choices.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Motivation & Analysis:** The initial analysis of time expressions across diverse datasets – TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets – is well-executed and provides a strong rationale for the proposed approach. The observations regarding expression length, vocabulary size, and the prevalence of time tokens are compelling and justify a simpler, type-based approach. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is a reasonable, if somewhat tenuous, justification.
2. **Competitive Performance:** SynTime achieves strong results, significantly outperforming established baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieving comparable performance on WikiWars. The notably higher recall scores across all datasets demonstrate that the system effectively captures a broad range of time expressions.
3. **Lightweight and Efficient:** The authors correctly emphasize the simplicity and efficiency of SynTime. The heuristic rule design and avoidance of complex learning algorithms suggest a practical system suitable for real-time applications, a point strengthened by the claim of real-time performance (though not quantitatively demonstrated).
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, methodology, and experimental setup. The description of the type system and rule design process is particularly lucid.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the experimental results are positive, the underlying approach – a rule-based system – is not inherently novel.  The authors position SynTime as distinct due to its *type-based* approach and heuristic rules, but these are incremental improvements over existing rule-based systems like SUTime and HeidelTime, rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that the type-based approach offers significant advantages beyond organizational clarity.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison Detail:** The description of the baselines lacks depth. While the names are provided, details about their configurations and specific rule sets used are scarce.  A more thorough comparison to the inner workings of SUTime, for instance, would better illuminate the advantages (or lack thereof) of SynTime’s approach.  Simply stating it’s different isn't enough; quantifying *how* it’s different in terms of rule complexity or coverage is necessary.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance metrics but lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common error types and the reasons behind them (e.g., ambiguity, rule coverage gaps, tokenization errors) would provide valuable insights and guide future improvements.  Knowing *where* the system fails is as important as knowing its overall accuracy.
4. **Reliance on Existing Resources:** SynTime’s initialization relies heavily on regular expressions extracted from SUTime. This raises concerns about how much of the performance gain is attributable to the new type-based system versus inheriting strengths from a well-established resource. A more rigorous evaluation would involve starting with a completely blank slate rule set.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the number of rules and their complexity in SynTime versus SUTime? Specifically, can you quantify how the type-based approach simplifies the rule set?
2. What is the computational cost (e.g., processing time) of SynTime compared to SUTime on a representative corpus?  Please provide concrete numbers to support the claim of "real-time" performance.
3.  Could you present a detailed error analysis, highlighting common error types and their causes?  Provide examples of incorrect predictions and explain why SynTime failed to correctly identify the time expressions.
4. How sensitive is SynTime to the quality of the POS tagging?  Have you experimented with different POS taggers, and what impact did that have on performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The inclusion of Gigaword in the analysis but not the experiments is a minor point of confusion. The statement regarding automatically generated labels in Gigaword could be expanded upon, clarifying the potential issues with its ground truth. The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
