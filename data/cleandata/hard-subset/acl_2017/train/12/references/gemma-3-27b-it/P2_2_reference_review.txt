## Review for "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

**Summary:**

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based system for time expression recognition. The authors begin with an analysis of time expressions in four datasets, identifying characteristics like short length, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. They leverage these observations to define a type-based approach, grouping tokens into "time token," "modifier," and "numeral" types, and design heuristic rules for recognition.  SynTime is evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, demonstrating competitive and, in some cases, superior performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The detailed analysis of time expression characteristics across multiple datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) is a valuable contribution.  The observations regarding length, vocabulary size, and syntactic patterns provide a strong motivation for a simplified rule-based approach.
2. **Type-Based Rule Definition:** The shift from semantic-based type definitions (common in existing rule-based systems like SUTime) to *syntactic* type definitions is a novel and potentially beneficial design choice. This simplifies rule creation and allows for greater flexibility.
3. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The significantly improved performance on the Tweets dataset suggests that the system is well-suited to noisy, informal text, a common challenge in NLP.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Justification:** The paper is well-motivated. The initial analysis of time expressions strongly supports the design choices made in SynTime. The authors convincingly argue why a type-based, heuristic rule approach is suitable given the observed characteristics of the data.
2. **Competitive Results:** SynTime achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks (TimeBank, WikiWars) and significantly outperforms baselines on Tweets.  The improvements in recall, particularly, are noteworthy.
3. **Simplicity & Efficiency:** The paper highlights the simplicity and real-time performance of SynTime, a key advantage over more complex machine learning models. This is important for practical applications.
4. **Thorough Comparison:** The comparison with strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) provides a solid evaluation of SynTime’s effectiveness.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The figures illustrating the system overview and rule application are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the *application* of syntactic typing to rule-based time expression recognition is novel, the underlying technique remains a rule-based approach. The core idea of using rules to identify patterns isn’t groundbreaking. The paper doesn't fully acknowledge this in the context of existing rule-based systems.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. While performance numbers are reported, understanding *where* SynTime struggles is crucial. What types of time expressions does it miss? What are the common errors? This would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
3. **Dependence on Existing Tools:**  SynTime relies heavily on POS tagging (Stanford POS Tagger) and initial regular expressions from SUTime. The performance is, therefore, partially dependent on the quality of these external resources.  The impact of errors in POS tagging on SynTime's performance isn't sufficiently addressed.
4. **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** The strong performance on Tweets is highlighted, but the paper doesn’t deeply discuss the factors contributing to this success and whether these factors would generalize to other noisy or domain-specific datasets.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the common types of errors SynTime makes?  Specifically, are there any patterns in the errors that reveal limitations of the syntactic type system?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime's performance to errors in the POS tagging stage?  Have you experimented with different POS taggers or considered incorporating POS tagging directly into the rule-based system?
3.  The paper mentions expanding SynTime. Could you provide more detail on the expansion process?  What criteria are used to select new keywords to add, and how does the expansion impact the system’s complexity and performance?
4.  While the authors highlight the simplicity of SynTime, what is the total number of rules currently implemented in the system?  Is there a concern about rule complexity growing as the system is expanded?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of Zipf's principle of least effort feels somewhat tangential and could be more tightly integrated with the core arguments.
*   The formatting of Table 4 could be improved for readability.
*   The citation style appears inconsistent (e.g., "Alonso et al., 2011" vs. "Chang and Manning, 2012").



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of temporal information extraction. The empirical analysis is strong, the method is clearly explained, and the results are promising, particularly on the challenging Tweets dataset.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the error analysis and discussion of generalizability, would significantly strengthen the paper.
