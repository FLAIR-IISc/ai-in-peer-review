This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors motivate their work with an analysis of time expressions across four datasets, observing properties like short length, small vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. They argue this supports a type-based approach, defining types based on syntactic roles rather than semantic meaning, and employ simple, heuristically designed rules. The paper demonstrates SynTime outperforms several baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable performance on WikiWars.

The primary contribution of this work is a simplified rule-based approach to time expression recognition that leverages syntactic patterns. While the idea of focusing on syntactic types isn't entirely novel (UWTime employs CCG which inherently captures syntax), the authors’ specific type definitions and boundary expansion heuristic are a reasonable, if incremental, contribution. The thorough analysis of the four datasets provides some justification for this approach, though its generalizability remains questionable. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of strong performance on the Tweets dataset, suggesting the method may be particularly well-suited to informal text.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Motivation:** The initial analysis of the datasets, demonstrating the prevalence of short, vocabulary-constrained time expressions with predictable syntactic structure, provides a reasonable basis for exploring a simplified rule-based approach. While these observations aren't groundbreaking, they are well-presented and support the design choices made in SynTime.
2. **Strong Performance on Tweets:** The significant improvements over the baselines on the Twitter dataset are a clear strength. This suggests the method is effective in handling the noisy and informal nature of social media text where simpler, more robust approaches may outperform complex machine learning models.
3. **Computational Efficiency:** The authors highlight the real-time performance of SynTime, which is a practical advantage for applications requiring fast processing. Rule-based systems generally have this advantage, and it’s good to see it explicitly demonstrated.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology and experimental setup are clearly described.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of a rule-based time expression recognizer is well-established. The key innovation – syntactic typing – feels incremental. The distinction between syntactic vs. semantic typing isn’t particularly profound, and similar concepts exist in other parsing and NLP systems. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish *why* a purely syntactic approach is superior to approaches that combine syntactic and semantic information.
2. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the comparison to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime is adequate, it lacks more recent, state-of-the-art neural network-based models. A comparison against BERT-based or Transformer-based approaches would be crucial to understand the true competitive advantage of SynTime. The authors’ dismissal of the Gigaword dataset due to potentially noisy labels is insufficient justification.
3. **Over-Reliance on SUTime for Initialization:** The fact that SynTime is initialized with regular expressions from SUTime raises concerns about the true originality of the system. It appears SynTime largely re-packages existing knowledge rather than discovering new patterns.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* SynTime fails (e.g., specific types of time expressions, edge cases) is critical for identifying areas for improvement and assessing the limitations of the approach. The discussion of limitations is superficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the types of time expressions that SynTime struggles to recognize?
2. How does SynTime perform against more recent neural network-based time expression recognition models (e.g., those based on BERT or Transformers)?
3. How sensitive is SynTime's performance to the quality of the initial POS tagging? Have you explored the impact of different POS taggers?
4. The paper mentions the ease of expanding SynTime with new keywords. Could you provide quantitative data on the performance gains achieved through this expansion process on datasets beyond the ones already presented?



**Additional Comments:** The statement regarding Zipf’s principle feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't strongly connect to the practical aspects of the system. The discussion of UWTime's training setup could be clearer – specifying the exact datasets used for training is important for reproducibility.
