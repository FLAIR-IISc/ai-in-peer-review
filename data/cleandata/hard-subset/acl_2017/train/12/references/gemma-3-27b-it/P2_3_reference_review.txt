## Review for "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

**Overall Assessment:** I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. It presents a solid, well-motivated, and empirically sound approach to time expression recognition. While the core idea isn't groundbreakingly novel, the analysis of time expression characteristics and the resulting simplification of rule-based systems is valuable. The strong experimental results, particularly on the challenging Twitter dataset, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Summary of the Paper & Contributions:**

This paper focuses on time expression recognition, a crucial task in NLP. The authors begin with an analysis of time expressions across four datasets, identifying characteristics like short length, small vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior.  Leveraging these observations, they propose "SynTime," a type-based rule-based tagger that defines syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple, heuristically designed rules for recognition.  They demonstrate SynTime’s superior performance compared to established baselines on standard datasets.

I see the main contributions as:

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The detailed analysis of time expression characteristics (length, vocabulary, syntactic behavior) provides a strong rationale for the design of SynTime and highlights key properties often overlooked.
2. **Simplified Rule-Based Approach:** SynTime’s type-based approach offers a significant simplification compared to existing rule-based systems (like SUTime) by focusing on syntactic patterns rather than semantic complexity. This simplification translates to ease of implementation and efficiency.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results, particularly the performance on the noisy Twitter dataset, convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Justification:** The paper is well-motivated by the principle of least effort and a solid analysis of time expression data. The observations regarding expression length, vocabulary size, and syntactic behavior provide a strong rationale for the proposed method.
2. **Clear and Concise Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and easy to understand.  The explanation of SynTime, its construction, and the rule design is clear and well-structured.  The use of figures (especially Figure 2) effectively illustrates the process.
3. **Competitive Performance:** SynTime consistently outperforms or matches the performance of strong baselines on multiple datasets. The improvements, especially on the Twitter data, are noteworthy and demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
4. **Practicality & Efficiency:**  The authors emphasize the lightweight nature and real-time performance of SynTime, which is a practical advantage over more complex machine learning approaches. This is a valuable characteristic for many applications.
5. **Thorough Experimentation:**  The experiments are well-designed, using standard datasets and appropriate evaluation metrics. The inclusion of both strict and relaxed matching provides a comprehensive assessment of the system’s performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the implementation and results are strong, the fundamental concept of using rule-based systems and syntactic information for time expression recognition isn't entirely new. The paper would benefit from more explicitly acknowledging this and highlighting *how* SynTime differs substantially from existing methods *beyond* the type-based approach.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging:** The reliance on POS tagging introduces a potential point of failure. The performance of SynTime is directly tied to the accuracy of the POS tagger. While acknowledged by the authors, a more in-depth analysis of the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime’s performance would be valuable.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of errors SynTime makes (e.g., specific patterns it misses) would provide insights for future improvements and help understand the limitations of the approach. For instance, are there specific types of temporal expressions (e.g., relative time expressions) that consistently pose challenges?
4. **Dataset Choices:** While standard datasets are used, the Gigaword dataset was excluded due to concerns about label quality. It would be useful to briefly discuss the potential impact of using automatically generated labels in other related work and perhaps explore methods for mitigating these issues.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of SynTime’s rule complexity (e.g., number of rules, length of rules) compared to SUTime and HeidelTime? This would further quantify the simplification achieved by the type-based approach.
2. How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to the accuracy of the POS tagger?  Have you conducted any experiments to quantify this sensitivity?
3.  Could you provide some examples of the types of time expressions that SynTime frequently fails to recognize, and discuss potential strategies to address these limitations?
4.  Regarding the exclusion of Gigaword, what strategies might be employed to account for potentially noisy labels in datasets like Gigaword when evaluating or training time expression recognition systems?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The code availability (GitHub link) is a significant strength.
*   The paper is generally well-polished and requires only minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
