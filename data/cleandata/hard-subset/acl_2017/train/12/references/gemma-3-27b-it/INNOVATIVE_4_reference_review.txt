This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The core idea is to define syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and design simple, heuristic rules based on these types and their relative positions. The authors motivate this approach with an analysis of four datasets, observing that time expressions are generally short, use a limited vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior.  They demonstrate SynTime outperforms strong baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, achieving competitive results on WikiWars.

The primary contribution is a *shift in perspective* for rule-based time expression recognition.  Instead of focusing on semantic categories (as in SUTime) or complex linguistic structures (as in UWTime), SynTime prioritizes syntactic patterns. This simplification is a potentially valuable direction, especially given the observed characteristics of time expressions. A secondary contribution lies in the analysis of time expression datasets, providing empirical support for the principle of least effort applied to language use in this specific domain. Finally, the strong performance on Tweets, a notoriously challenging dataset, is noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The type-based approach, focusing on syntactic roles rather than semantic meaning, is a genuinely fresh take on a relatively mature problem.  It’s a bold simplification that challenges the conventional wisdom of needing detailed semantic knowledge.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  Outperforming established baselines on TimeBank and *especially* Tweets is significant. Tweets are noisy and informal, making accurate time expression recognition particularly difficult. This suggests SynTime’s simplicity and robustness are well-suited to real-world text.
3. **Clear Motivation and Justification:**  The paper clearly articulates the motivations behind SynTime, grounding it in observations from data analysis and linking it to Zipf’s principle of least effort. This provides a compelling rationale for the design choices.
4. **Lightweight and Efficient:** The authors emphasize the system’s real-time performance and ease of expansion. These practical advantages are valuable, especially for applications requiring rapid processing.
5. **Thorough Experimentation:** The comparison to three strong baselines on three datasets provides a reasonable evaluation of SynTime's performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* SynTime fails would provide valuable insights for future improvement. Are there specific types of time expressions it consistently misses? Are the errors related to POS tagging inaccuracies?
2. **Heuristic Nature of Rules:** The reliance on "simple heuristic rules" is a double-edged sword. While it contributes to the system’s efficiency, it also raises concerns about its generality and robustness.  The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs would be beneficial.  It’s not clear *how* the boundary expansion is optimized or whether it’s prone to over-expansion.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The use of Gigaword was abandoned due to concerns about label quality. This is understandable, but it limits the scope of the evaluation.  It would be helpful to briefly discuss how the results might generalize to other datasets.
4. **Dependence on Existing Tools:** The reliance on Stanford POS tagger introduces an external dependency and potential source of error. While acknowledged, its impact isn’t quantified.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Learning-Based Systems with Similar Feature Sets**: Comparing to machine learning systems utilizing similar feature sets (e.g. syntactic features) would strengthen the argument that the type-based approach is beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, illustrating the types of errors SynTime makes and potential reasons for those errors?
2.  How did you determine the optimal stopping criteria for the boundary expansion heuristic? Were any evaluation metrics used to fine-tune this aspect of the system?
3.  Could you elaborate on the process of selecting and adding keywords during the expansion phase (SynTime-E)? What criteria were used to prioritize which keywords to add?
4.  How sensitive is SynTime's performance to errors in the POS tagging stage? Have you investigated the impact of using different POS taggers?



**Additional Comments:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The reference to Zipf’s principle is a nice touch, providing a theoretical grounding for the observed patterns. The paper’s focus on simplicity and efficiency is refreshing in a field often dominated by complex models.



I recommend **acceptance**. While the paper has some weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the clear motivation outweigh these concerns. The authors have presented a promising direction for future research in time expression recognition. The contribution is significant enough that overlooking minor methodological imperfections is warranted.
