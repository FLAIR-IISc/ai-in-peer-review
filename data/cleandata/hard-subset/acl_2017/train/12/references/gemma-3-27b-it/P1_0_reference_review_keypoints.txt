- Paper presents SynTime: a rule-based time expression recognizer using three syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics, showing competitive F1 and strong recall on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets.

- Empirical analysis: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, often contain time tokens, and show consistent syntactic behavior—this motivates the method.

- Type-based rule design: SynTime defines time token, modifier, and numeral types and builds rules around syntactic types rather than semantic categories.

- Strong performance on Tweets: SynTime handles noisy, informal text well, achieving notably high recall on the Twitter dataset.

- Clear motivation & sound design: method is well-grounded in data-driven observations about syntactic patterns of time expressions.

- Simplicity & efficiency: heuristic rules are lightweight and suitable for real-time/low-resource use.

- Strong recall performance: consistently captures a large proportion of actual time expressions across datasets.

- Effective baseline comparison: paper compares comprehensively to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime and shows competitive results.

- Insightful framing: relates observed patterns to Zipf’s principle of least effort, offering a thoughtful theoretical perspective.

- Limited novelty: approach remains rule-based with inherent scalability and adaptability limitations not fully addressed.

- Lack of detailed error analysis: paper emphasizes overall F1 without analyzing specific error types or recurring failure modes.

- Dependence on POS tagging: system vulnerability to POS errors is acknowledged but not quantitatively studied.

- Limited scalability evidence: no empirical results or detailed discussion on performance with much larger or more complex corpora.

- Dataset bias concern: UWTime’s use of TimeBank for training may create an evaluation advantage that isn’t quantified.

- Request for error analysis: reviewers ask for identification of common errors and challenging expression types.

- Request for POS robustness analysis: estimate impact of POS tagging errors and mitigation strategies.

- Request for scalability data: provide discussion or experiments on larger datasets (e.g., Gigaword).

- Request for expansion strategy details: describe semi-supervised or automated approaches to find keywords for expansion.

- Request for deeper comparison to learning methods: explain why SynTime outperforms UWTime and trade-offs between rule-based and ML approaches.

- Additional notes: paper is well-written, GitHub link is useful, and the Zipf-related discussion is interesting.
