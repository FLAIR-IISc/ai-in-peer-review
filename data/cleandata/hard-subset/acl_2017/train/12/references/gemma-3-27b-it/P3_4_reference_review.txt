## Review for "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

**Summary:**

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system. The authors first analyze time expressions in four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identify several observations: time expressions are short, use a limited vocabulary, frequently contain time tokens, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach defining syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and designing simple, heuristic rules for recognition. They evaluate SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, reporting superior performance on TimeBank and Tweets, and comparable results on WikiWars, particularly in recall.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The analysis of time expression characteristics across multiple datasets is a valuable contribution. Quantifying the length, vocabulary size, and reliance on time tokens provides a solid foundation for a simplified approach. This analysis reinforces the idea that time expressions, while diverse in manifestation, possess underlying regularities.
2. **Syntactic Typing for Time Expressions:**  Framing time expression recognition through a syntactic lens, rather than purely semantic, is a noteworthy conceptual shift.  The proposed typing system (time token, modifier, numeral) offers a more streamlined and potentially extensible approach than existing methods.
3. **Demonstrated Effectiveness of a Lightweight Rule-Based System:**  SynTime demonstrates that a simple, type-based rule system can achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to more complex systems, especially when tailored to specific domains (like Tweets).  This highlights the potential for efficiency in time expression recognition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated by the careful analysis of time expressions across diverse datasets. The observations presented are clearly linked to the design choices in SynTime, bolstering the rationale behind the approach.
2. **Clear and Concise Methodology:**  The description of SynTime's type system and rule design is clear and easy to follow. The “boundary expansion” heuristic is a sensible approach given the identified characteristics of time expressions. The diagram (Figure 2) effectively illustrates the system's workflow.
3. **Competitive Performance:** SynTime achieves strong results, particularly on the challenging Tweets dataset.  The high recall scores across all datasets suggest a robust ability to identify time expressions, which is crucial in many downstream applications.
4. **Efficiency:** The authors highlight the real-time performance of SynTime, a significant advantage over more computationally intensive methods.  This is a crucial practical benefit.
5. **Good Comparison to Baselines:**  The selected baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) are all well-respected systems in the field, providing a solid benchmark for comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Rule-Based Approach:** While the *application* of syntactic typing to time expression recognition is interesting, the underlying approach remains fundamentally rule-based. The paper doesn’t significantly advance the state-of-the-art in rule-based systems themselves (e.g., no novel parsing techniques are introduced). The novelty lies more in the *way* the rules are defined, leveraging syntactic types.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of the errors SynTime makes.  Understanding *where* the system fails would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Are errors due to incorrect POS tagging, limitations in the type definitions, or the heuristics themselves?
3. **Dependence on External POS Tagger:** The system’s reliance on an external POS tagger (Stanford POS Tagger) introduces a potential point of failure. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t quantify the impact of POS tagging errors on SynTime’s performance. Exploring more robust or integrated POS tagging solutions could be beneficial.
4. **Limited Generalizability without Expansion:** While the results on Tweets are impressive, the need for expansion to achieve optimal performance on WikiWars suggests limited generalizability without domain-specific adaptation. This highlights the potential cost of adapting the system to new domains.
5. **Gigaword Dataset Exclusion:** The justification for excluding the Gigaword dataset ("labels may not be ground truth") feels weak.  While concerns about label quality are valid, it would be more informative to include the dataset and discuss the potential impact of noisy labels on the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed error analysis, highlighting the most common types of errors SynTime makes and their causes (e.g., POS tagging errors, insufficient rule coverage, ambiguous cases)?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to errors in the POS tagging?  Have you experimented with different POS taggers or explored ways to make the system more robust to POS tagging errors?
3.  What is the cost (in terms of time and effort) associated with expanding SynTime for a new domain? Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the manual annotation process?
4.  Why was the Gigaword dataset excluded?  Could you provide more information about the concerns regarding label quality? Were any attempts made to validate the labels?
5.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required to run SynTime (memory, CPU time) and compare it with the baseline systems?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are formatted correctly.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is a clever and insightful observation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of time expression recognition. The empirical analysis, syntactic typing approach, and demonstrated performance of SynTime are all valuable. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly through error analysis and a discussion of generalizability, would further strengthen the paper. While the core rule-based approach isn't groundbreaking, the novel application of syntactic typing and the resulting efficiency gains are sufficient to warrant acceptance.
