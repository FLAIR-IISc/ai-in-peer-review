- Proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer using syntactic types and heuristic boundary expansion.  
- Empirical analysis across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets finds time expressions are short, use small vocabularies, almost always contain a time token, and show similar syntactic behavior.  
- Defines a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and expands identified time tokens to include modifiers/numerals.  
- Evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; outperforms baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, comparable on WikiWars.  
- Contribution: thorough cross-dataset empirical analysis linking observations to Zipf’s principle of least effort.  
- Contribution: a syntactic-type system for organizing rules, positioned as an alternative to semantic-type approaches.  
- Contribution: a lightweight, efficient rule-based system suitable for real-time applications.  
- Strength: strong, data-supported empirical analysis with theoretical grounding and valuable tweet-domain insights.  
- Strength: practical, simple system achieving competitive results and high recall (e.g., >95% relaxed match on Tweets).  
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across three datasets and demonstration of easy domain adaptation (SynTime-E improvements with few added tokens/modifiers).  
- Strength: reveals that informal language (tweets) still largely uses formal time expressions, explaining strong performance.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—syntactic types and heuristic expansion insufficiently differentiated from prior rule-based systems like SUTime.  
- Weakness: weak justification for linking observed patterns to Zipf’s principle; the causal claim is not rigorously supported.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed failure-case analysis to show limitations on complex expressions or other domains.  
- Weakness: inadequate comparison with related work (e.g., SUTime, GUTime) on how syntactic types differ from existing approaches.  
- Weakness: questionable generalizability claims to other languages or linguistic phenomena without supporting evidence.  
- Question: clarify how SynTime’s “syntactic types” differ from syntactic features/ POS usage in SUTime and other systems.  
- Question: provide stronger evidence that patterns arise from minimizing cognitive effort rather than other linguistic factors.  
- Question: give concrete examples of time expressions SynTime fails to recognize (e.g., WikiWars prepositional/descriptive cases) and explain why.  
- Question: offer a more comprehensive analysis of informal time expressions in tweets beyond a single example.  
- Question: provide runtime measurements to substantiate the claim that SynTime runs in real time and compare to baselines.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify novelty and strengthen theoretical and comparative justification.
