- Summary: Proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer grounded in analysis of four datasets; defines a syntactic type system and heuristic boundary expansion; outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, notably on Tweets (95.87% relaxed F1).

- Contribution 1: Empirical analysis showing time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, nearly always contain time tokens, and share syntactic behavior—aligning with the principle of least effort.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) that classifies tokens by syntactic behavior rather than semantic roles.

- Contribution 3: Presents SynTime, a lightweight, training-free system using the syntactic type approach and simple rules that achieves high performance and real-time operation.

- Strength 1: Comprehensive, well-executed analysis across diverse datasets providing strong empirical support for the proposed observations.

- Strength 2: Practical and efficient system design that is training-free, real-time, and shows strong results on informal text (Tweets).

- Strength 3: Clear, well-structured presentation with illustrative figures and sufficient implementation detail.

- Strength 4: Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets and matching metrics, with SynTime showing especially high recall and adaptability via minimal keyword expansion.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty—core components overlap with existing systems (e.g., SUTime) and lack ablation evidence separating syntactic vs. semantic type benefits.

- Weakness 2: Limited evaluation scope—only three datasets used and Gigaword excluded, raising generalizability concerns across domains and languages.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient analysis of limitations—no quantification of how tokenization or POS tagging errors affect SynTime's performance.

- Weakness 4: No runtime measurements or baseline comparisons to substantiate the "real-time" efficiency claim.

- Weakness 5: Ambiguous keyword expansion process—unclear how many/which keywords were added, selection criteria, and dataset-specific differences.

- Question 1: Provide ablation studies comparing syntactic type definitions to semantic type definitions to quantify the syntactic approach's contribution.

- Question 2: Explain handling of preposition-including expressions (e.g., "on Friday") excluded by TimeML conventions and potential recall impact.

- Question 3: Provide runtime measurements for SynTime versus baselines for large-scale processing to support real-time claims.

- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify novelty, expand evaluations of limitations, and provide runtime/expansion details.
