

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a new approach for time expression recognition called SynTime, which is based on a detailed analysis of time expressions across four different datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The authors make four key observations about time expressions: 1) they are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), 2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, 3) at least 93% contain at least one time token, and 4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. These observations support the principle of least effort in language usage (Zipf, 1949).

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:

1. **Insightful analysis of time expressions**: The thorough analysis across multiple datasets provides strong empirical evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage, with the four key observations forming a solid foundation for the proposed approach.

2. **Syntactic type-based approach**: The paper introduces a novel way of defining types for time-related tokens based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning, which leads to a more flexible rule-based system.

3. **Simple heuristic rules with boundary expansion**: The design of simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion rather than fixed-length patterns provides a lightweight, real-time solution that outperforms existing methods on multiple datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis and well-supported observations**: The paper provides compelling evidence for its four key observations through detailed analysis across four different datasets. The data in Tables 2-3 and Figure 1 clearly demonstrate the short length, small vocabulary, high occurrence of time tokens, and similar syntactic behavior in time expressions. This analysis is particularly valuable as it provides concrete evidence for the principle of least effort in language, which is a significant contribution to understanding time expression patterns.

2. **Compelling experimental results**: The evaluation on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets) shows SynTime consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, particularly achieving exceptionally high recall rates (over 92% on relaxed match). The results are especially impressive on the Tweets dataset (F1 of 95.87% in relaxed match), which aligns perfectly with the authors' observation that informal communication uses time expressions in minimal length. The high recall rates directly support the authors' claim that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens.

3. **Practical efficiency and simplicity**: SynTime is presented as an extremely lightweight rule-based tagger that runs in real time without requiring training data. This is a significant practical advantage over machine learning-based approaches that require substantial training data and computational resources. The expansion mechanism (adding new time-related tokens) is simple and requires minimal manual effort, as evidenced by the small number of tokens added in the experiments (Table 5).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core methodology**: While the authors claim that SynTime defines types based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning, this distinction appears to be more of a semantic nuance than a substantive methodological contribution. Other rule-based systems (like SUTime) also implicitly use syntactic information (e.g., through POS tags) to recognize time expressions. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their syntactic type system fundamentally differs from or improves upon existing approaches beyond the naming convention.

2. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares with HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, but doesn't include comparisons with more recent approaches, particularly deep learning-based methods for time expression recognition that have emerged in the last few years. For example, recent work like "Temporal Expression Recognition with Transformer-based Models" (2021) or "BERT for Temporal Expression Recognition" (2022) might provide a more comprehensive comparison of the current state of the art.

3. **Insufficient explanation of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime struggles with time expressions containing prepositions or descriptive phrases (as in WikiWars), but doesn't provide a clear analysis of why this limitation exists or how it might be addressed. The paper states that "SynTime could not fully recognize such kinds of time expressions because it follows TimeML and TimeBank," but this explanation is vague and doesn't provide insights into how the approach could be extended to handle these cases.

4. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper shows that SynTime-E (the expanded version) performs better than SynTime-I on WikiWars, but doesn't provide an ablation study to demonstrate the impact of each component of the proposed approach. For instance, it would be valuable to know how much the boundary expansion heuristic contributes to performance versus the syntactic type system itself.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that SynTime defines types based on syntactic behavior, but other rule-based systems (like SUTime) also use POS information. Could you elaborate on how the syntactic type system in SynTime is fundamentally different from the implicit syntactic information used in other rule-based approaches?

2. The paper mentions that "in informal environment people tend to use time expressions in minimum length," which explains SynTime's strong performance on Tweets. However, the paper also acknowledges that Twitter uses some informal variants (like "2day"). Could you provide more details on how SynTime handles these variants and whether the small number of tokens added for expansion (Table 5) was sufficient to capture these variations?

3. The paper states that "POS could not provide enough information to distinguish time expressions from common words," but then uses POS information for time token identification. Could you clarify how SynTime overcomes this limitation in practice, especially since POS tags are used to filter ambiguous words like "May"?

4. The paper mentions that "Gigaword was not used in our experiments because the labels in the dataset were automatically generated." Could you provide more details about the quality of these automatically generated labels and whether you believe they would be suitable for a more comprehensive evaluation?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a solid paper with a strong empirical analysis and practical contributions. The insights about time expression patterns are valuable and well-supported by data. While the core methodology isn't radically novel, the simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results make it a useful contribution to the field. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a clearer distinction from existing approaches and comparing with more recent state-of-the-art methods. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
