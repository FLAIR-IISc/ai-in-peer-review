

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper proposes a rule-based approach called SynTime for time expression recognition, grounded in an analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The authors identify four key observations: time expressions are short (80% ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary, contain time tokens in at least 93% of cases, and demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. These observations align with Zipf's principle of least effort. Based on these insights, they define a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and develop SynTime, which uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion rather than fixed-length patterns. Evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets, SynTime achieves superior performance, especially on the Tweets dataset (95.87% relaxed match F1).

## Main Contributions
1. The insightful analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets revealing their alignment with the principle of least effort.
2. The novel syntactic type system for time expressions that defines types based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning.
3. The SynTime system that demonstrates the practical utility of the syntactic type approach with high performance and real-time capabilities.

## Strengths

**1. Comprehensive and Insightful Analysis:** The paper's analysis of time expressions is thorough and reveals meaningful patterns across diverse datasets. The four observations provide strong empirical evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage, which is a compelling theoretical contribution. The analysis of time expression length, vocabulary size, time token prevalence, and syntactic behavior is well-executed and well-motivated, setting a strong foundation for the proposed approach.

**2. Practical and Efficient System Design:** SynTime is remarkably lightweight and efficient, requiring no training data and running in real time. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the simple heuristic rules based on syntactic types outperform more complex systems. The excellent results on the Tweets dataset (95.87% relaxed match F1) are particularly impressive given the informal nature of Twitter text, where time expressions tend to be short and use formal words despite the informal context.

**3. Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with a logical flow from analysis to method to evaluation. The figures (especially Figure 2 and Figure 3) clearly illustrate the approach, and the explanation of the syntactic type system is precise. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand and potentially implement SynTime, which is valuable for the NLP community.

**4. Strong Experimental Validation:** The evaluation across three diverse datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) with two matching criteria (strict and relaxed) provides comprehensive evidence of SynTime's effectiveness. The results consistently show SynTime outperforming baselines, particularly in recall, which aligns with their observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens. The expansion of SynTime (SynTime-E) with minimal additional keywords further demonstrates the system's adaptability.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited Novelty of Core Approach:** While the syntactic type system is an interesting variation, the core components (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) have appeared in other systems like SUTime. The distinction between semantic vs. syntactic types is not sufficiently emphasized or validated. The authors claim this is a key difference, but they don't provide ablation studies showing how the syntactic approach specifically improves performance compared to a semantic approach. The novelty is somewhat overstated.

**2. Limited Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is restricted to three datasets, with Gigaword excluded due to "automatically generated" labels. This raises questions about generalizability to other domains. The paper doesn't provide evidence of how SynTime performs on other text types or in different languages. The exclusion of Gigaword, which is commonly used in TempEval, weakens the evaluation's comprehensiveness.

**3. Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't analyze how errors in these steps affect performance. For example, the authors mention Stanford POS Tagger's occasional mislabeling (e.g., 'sat' as VBD instead of NNP), but don't quantify the impact on SynTime's accuracy. This is a significant limitation for a rule-based system that relies heavily on syntactic information.

**4. Lack of Runtime Analysis:** While the authors claim SynTime runs in real time, they don't provide actual runtime measurements or comparisons with baselines. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the practical advantage of SynTime's efficiency claims. For a system targeting real-world applications, this is a critical omission.

**5. Ambiguous Expansion Process:** The paper mentions expanding SynTime with additional keywords but doesn't detail the expansion process. How many keywords were added for each dataset? What were the specific criteria for inclusion? Table 5 shows minimal additions for TimeBank and Tweets, but the paper doesn't explain why some words were included and others excluded (e.g., the WikiWars example of "The time Arnold reached Quebec City" was excluded). This affects the assessment of the system's practicality.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide ablation studies comparing the syntactic type approach with a semantic type approach (using the same rules but different type definitions) to better quantify the contribution of the syntactic type system?

2. How would you handle time expressions that include prepositions (e.g., "on Friday") that are excluded from your system based on TimeML conventions? Would this affect recall on datasets where prepositions are part of the time expression?

3. Could you provide runtime measurements for SynTime compared to the baselines, especially for large-scale processing tasks, to better support your claim of "real-time" performance?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed analysis of time expressions and a practical system that achieves strong results. While the novelty of the syntactic type approach is somewhat limited, the insights from the analysis and the effectiveness of the resulting system make this a valuable contribution to the field. With improved emphasis on the novelty of the syntactic approach and more thorough evaluation of limitations, this paper would be an excellent fit for ACL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.
