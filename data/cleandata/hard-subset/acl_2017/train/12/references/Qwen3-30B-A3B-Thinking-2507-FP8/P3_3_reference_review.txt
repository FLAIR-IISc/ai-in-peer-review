

# Peer Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper presents a rule-based time expression recognition system (SynTime) grounded in four key empirical observations about time expressions: (1) they are typically short (80% ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary, (3) they contain time tokens in at least 93% of cases, and (4) words within them exhibit similar syntactic behavior. The authors argue these observations reflect Zipf's principle of least effort. Based on these observations, they propose a syntactic type-based approach (as opposed to semantic types used by prior work) with simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical foundation**: The four observations are well-supported by comprehensive analysis across four diverse datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets). The statistics are convincing—particularly the consistent finding that time expressions are extremely short (average length ~2 tokens) and that time tokens are present in the vast majority of cases (93-98%). This provides a solid empirical basis for their approach.

2. **Compelling explanation for time expression patterns**: The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is well-motivated and well-justified by the data. The authors demonstrate how short expressions, small vocabulary, and syntactic consistency all reduce communicative effort—this is a theoretically rich contribution that advances our understanding of time expression patterns.

3. **Strong empirical results**: SynTime significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets (with F1 improvements of 11.37% in strict match on Tweets), and achieves comparable results on WikiWars. The high recall (95.65% on TimeBank, 98.31% on Tweets) aligns perfectly with their observation that time tokens are present in >93% of expressions, providing a clear explanation for the performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims their syntactic type system is fundamentally different from prior work (e.g., SUTime), but this distinction is not clearly established. SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) explicitly uses part-of-speech information to identify time expressions, which is syntactic. The authors don't convincingly demonstrate how their "syntactic types" differ from the POS-based approaches already in use. The paper's claim of novelty is therefore questionable.

2. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but fails to analyze how errors in these components would impact performance. For example, if the POS tagger misidentifies "sat" in "friday or sat" as VBD instead of NNP, the system would fail to recognize "sat" as a time token. This is a significant limitation for a rule-based system that relies heavily on syntactic information.

3. **Insufficient domain generalization analysis**: While SynTime-E performs better on WikiWars with domain-specific additions (16 time tokens, 19 modifiers), the paper doesn't demonstrate how to automatically identify or generalize these domain-specific patterns. The manual expansion process is labor-intensive, and the paper doesn't address how this would scale to new domains without significant human effort.

4. **Limited analysis of complex time expressions**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime struggles with time expressions containing descriptive phrases or prepositions (common in WikiWars), but doesn't explore how to address this limitation. For example, how would SynTime handle "the time Arnold reached Quebec City"? The system's inability to handle such cases isn't adequately addressed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their syntactic type system differs from prior work that uses POS information. Can you provide a concrete example where SUTime would fail but SynTime would succeed due to their syntactic type distinction?

2. The paper states that time expressions in tweets use "formal words" (with only ~3% informal variants), but the CMU Twitter clusters example only covers "today." Can you provide similar statistics for other common time expressions (e.g., "month," "year," "week") to strengthen this claim?

3. Given that SynTime relies on POS tagging, how would the system perform on non-English languages where POS tagging is less accurate? Would the system require language-specific modifications?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong empirical contribution by demonstrating that time expressions follow Zipf's principle of least effort, with clear evidence across multiple datasets. The analysis is thorough and well-supported. However, the proposed SynTime system doesn't represent a significant methodological advance over existing rule-based approaches, and the novelty claim is overstated. The empirical results are impressive, but the theoretical contribution (the principle of least effort connection) is more significant than the technical contribution.

The paper would be strengthened by a more rigorous comparison to prior work that uses syntactic information, a more detailed analysis of error cases (especially complex time expressions), and a clearer discussion of limitations. The current work is valuable for its empirical insights but doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in time expression recognition in a fundamentally novel way.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions (addressing the novelty claims and limitations more thoroughly). The empirical insights are valuable, but the paper needs to more clearly position its contribution relative to existing syntactic approaches.
