- Paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets and finds they are short, use a small vocabulary, usually contain time tokens, and show similar syntactic behavior; proposes SynTime, a syntactic-type rule-based recognizer that outperforms baselines on some datasets.

- Contribution: comprehensive empirical analysis linking time expression patterns to Zipf’s principle of least effort.

- Contribution: proposal of a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and development of SynTime, a training-free rule-based recognizer.

- Contribution: strong empirical results, especially on Tweets (95.87% relaxed F1), and competitive performance on TimeBank and WikiWars.

- Strength: rigorous empirical analysis with solid theoretical grounding and clear evidence for the principle of least effort.

- Strength: outstanding performance on noisy Tweets data, notably improving recall over baselines.

- Strength: practical advantages—lightweight, real-time, no training required, and easy to extend with domain-specific expressions.

- Strength: clear demonstration that time expressions are concise and linguistically patterned, aiding NLP applications.

- Weakness: limited novelty claim—paper doesn’t convincingly distinguish its “syntactic types” from prior rule-based systems that use syntactic features (e.g., SUTime).

- Weakness: exclusion of Gigaword from experiments is insufficiently justified and weakens generalizability.

- Weakness: insufficient analysis of robustness to tokenization/POS tagging errors and lack of error analysis for such failures.

- Weakness: comparisons omit recent neural/state-of-the-art methods post-2014, so “state-of-the-art” claims are weakened.

- Weakness: heuristic boundary-expansion rules are presented as novel/flexible but may be straightforward and not clearly superior to existing rule-based methods.

- Question: provide a concrete example showing how a token like “ago” is classified differently by SynTime vs. prior systems (e.g., SUTime) and why that matters.

- Question: give specific examples of mislabeled time expressions in Gigaword to justify its exclusion and assess impact on evaluation.

- Question: clarify how SynTime’s boundary expansion heuristics differ in practice from SUTime’s rule-based expansion.

- Question: explain why SynTime excels on Tweets despite informal language—provide evidence that time tokens remain formal in social media.

- Question: detail criteria for labeling “time tokens” versus regular words (e.g., how “month” is treated in different contexts).
