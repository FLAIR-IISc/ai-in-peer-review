

# Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that leverages syntactic types rather than semantic types for recognition. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four key observations: 1) time expressions are very short (80% ≤ 3 tokens), 2) a small vocabulary of time-related keywords is used, 3) at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, and 4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they propose SynTime, which defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion to recognize time expressions.

The primary contributions are: 1) a thorough analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets that provides evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage; 2) a novel type-based approach to time expression recognition that uses syntactic types rather than semantic types; and 3) an extremely lightweight rule-based system that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong, well-supported observations**: The four observations about time expressions are well-grounded in empirical analysis across multiple datasets. The paper provides comprehensive statistics (Tables 2-3) that clearly demonstrate the short length of time expressions, small vocabulary size, high percentage of expressions containing time tokens, and similar syntactic behaviors within time expressions. These observations provide a solid foundation for the proposed approach and offer valuable insights into how time expressions function as part of language.

2. **Clear novelty in the type-based approach**: The paper effectively differentiates SynTime from existing rule-based methods by emphasizing the use of syntactic types rather than semantic types. The distinction between how SynTime defines types (based on syntactic behavior) versus other rule-based methods (based on semantic meaning) is well-explained and well-motivated by the authors' observations. The paper also convincingly argues that this approach leads to a more flexible and lightweight system.

3. **Strong experimental results**: The results demonstrate that SynTime significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, achieving the highest recalls across all three datasets. The high recall rates (above 92% for relaxed match on all datasets) are particularly impressive and directly align with the authors' observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens. The paper's explanation of why SynTime performs exceptionally well on Tweets (due to the short length of time expressions and use of formal words) is compelling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty justification**: While the paper claims novelty in using syntactic types, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate this approach from existing work. For example, the paper states that "other rule-based taggers define types for tokens based on their semantic meaning," but doesn't clearly explain how this differs from existing methods. The paper doesn't provide enough evidence that this is truly novel, as similar approaches (like using POS tags) have been used in other time expression recognition systems.

2. **Inadequate evaluation on standard datasets**: The paper excludes Gigaword from their experiments because the labels were automatically generated, which means they aren't testing against the standard TempEval benchmarks. This limits the generalizability of their results. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison of how SynTime performs on the standard TempEval-3 dataset, which would strengthen their claims of outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't discuss how errors in these components might affect performance in real-world scenarios. For example, the paper mentions that Stanford POS Tagger assigns incorrect tags to some words (e.g., "sat" in "friday or sat"), but doesn't quantify how these errors impact SynTime's performance. This is a significant limitation for a rule-based system that relies on POS information.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent learning-based methods**: The paper compares with UWTime, but doesn't compare with more recent learning-based approaches like those using neural networks or transformer models. Given that the field has advanced significantly since the cited works (e.g., Lee et al., 2014), this omission weakens the paper's claim that SynTime is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that SynTime uses syntactic types rather than semantic types, but the distinction between these two approaches isn't sufficiently clear. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how your syntactic types differ from standard part-of-speech tags, and why these differences are significant for time expression recognition?

2. The paper excludes Gigaword from experiments due to automatic labels. Could you provide an estimate of how much performance might change if Gigaword were included, and how this would affect your conclusions?

3. The paper states that SynTime "runs in real time," but doesn't provide concrete timing measurements. How long does SynTime take to process a document of average length compared to the baselines?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and empirically grounded approach to time expression recognition. The observations about time expressions are valuable and well-supported, and the resulting SynTime system demonstrates strong performance on the datasets tested. While the novelty claim could be strengthened, and the evaluation could be more comprehensive, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field. The lightweight nature of SynTime and its strong performance on Tweets (where time expressions tend to be short and formal) are particularly noteworthy.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty justification and evaluation limitations. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to time expression recognition.
