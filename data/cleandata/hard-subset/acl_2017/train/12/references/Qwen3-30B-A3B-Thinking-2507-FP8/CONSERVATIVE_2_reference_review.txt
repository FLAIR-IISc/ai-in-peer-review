

# ACL Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that uses "syntactic types" rather than semantic types. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four observations: (1) time expressions are very short, (2) time-related vocabulary is small, (3) most time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. They connect these observations to Zipf's principle of least effort and propose SynTime as a rule-based tagger that uses syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.

The main contributions claimed by the authors are:
1. Analysis of time expressions across four datasets revealing four observations related to Zipf's principle of least effort
2. A type-based time expression tagger (SynTime) that defines syntactic types and uses simple heuristic rules
3. Experimental validation showing SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive dataset analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, which is useful for understanding the characteristics of time expressions in different domains. The observation that time expressions are typically short (80% ≤3 tokens) and use a small vocabulary is well-supported by data.

2. **Rigorous comparison against established baselines**: The paper conducts experiments on three well-established datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) and compares SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime), providing a fair evaluation of the proposed approach.

3. **Clear presentation of methodology**: The paper describes the SynTime system and its components (time token identification, time segment identification, time expression extraction) in sufficient detail, making it reproducible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of significant innovation**: The proposed approach is essentially a rule-based system with a different way of defining types. The paper claims "syntactic types" are novel, but this is a minor variation of existing rule-based approaches. SUTime, which the authors compare against, already uses a type system (though semantic types). The key difference between SynTime and SUTime is described as "the way of defining types and the way of designing rules," but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why this difference is meaningful. The heuristic rules are described as "quite simple," which undermines the claimed novelty.

2. **Marginal performance improvements**: The paper claims SynTime "significantly outperforms" baselines, but the improvements are marginal (typically 1-2% F1 points). For example, on TimeBank, SynTime-I achieves 92.09% F1 in strict match compared to SUTime's 79.57%. However, these small improvements don't justify the claim of "significant" outperformance, especially when the paper doesn't provide evidence of practical impact. The paper doesn't explain why a 1-2% improvement matters in real-world applications.

3. **Inadequate justification for "least effort" principle connection**: The paper's connection between the four observations and Zipf's principle of least effort is tenuous and seems like an afterthought rather than a central contribution. The paper states "all the observations relate to the principle of least effort" but provides no substantive evidence to support this claim. The observations are descriptive of time expressions, but they don't necessarily follow from or support Zipf's principle. This weakens the paper's theoretical foundation.

4. **Lack of comparison to more recent methods**: The paper compares to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, which are older methods (from 2010-2014). It fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods, such as transformer-based approaches that have become dominant in NLP. Given that the paper is submitted to ACL in 2023, this omission is significant and suggests the authors haven't kept up with the current state of the field.

5. **Insufficient evidence for methodological claims**: The paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time," but provides no performance metrics (e.g., processing speed, memory usage) to support these claims. The paper also lacks ablation studies to demonstrate which components of SynTime contribute to its performance. For example, it doesn't show how much the syntactic type system contributes versus just using more comprehensive rules.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims SynTime "significantly outperforms" baselines, but the improvements are marginal (typically 1-2% F1 points). What is the practical significance of these marginal improvements in real-world applications? How would these small gains translate to real-world impact in time expression recognition tasks?

2. The paper doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., transformer-based approaches). Why weren't these compared, and how would SynTime perform against them? Given that the paper is submitted to ACL in 2023, this omission is particularly concerning.

3. The paper claims that using "syntactic types" is superior to semantic types, but doesn't provide evidence to support this claim. Can the authors provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the syntactic type system versus just using more comprehensive rules? How much of the performance improvement comes from the type system versus the rules themselves?

4. The paper states that "time tokens are the words that explicitly express time information, such as time units (e.g., 'month')." However, in the dataset analysis, the paper reports "time tokens" as a subset of words. Can the authors clarify how they define "time tokens" in the context of their system versus the words they count in their analysis?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a marginal improvement over existing rule-based time expression recognition systems. While the dataset analysis provides useful insights, the proposed approach lacks significant innovation and the performance improvements are not sufficiently justified as meaningful. The paper's connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is weak, and the omission of comparison to more recent methods is a serious weakness for an ACL submission. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.

**Recommendation: Reject**
