

# ACL Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary
This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and makes four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion. They evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets and report that it outperforms the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, with comparable results on WikiWars.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Comprehensive analysis of time expressions** across multiple datasets demonstrating they follow the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), with strong empirical evidence for their four key observations.
2. **Proposed syntactic type system** for time expressions that differs from existing approaches by using syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning for type definitions.
3. **Lightweight rule-based system** (SynTime) that achieves state-of-the-art performance with simple heuristic rules, enabling real-time processing.

## Strengths

### 1. Strong, well-supported analysis of time expressions
The paper's most significant contribution is the comprehensive analysis of time expressions across four diverse datasets. The authors provide clear, data-driven evidence for their four observations (short length, small vocabulary, high time token occurrence, similar syntactic behavior) supported by tables and figures. This analysis is particularly compelling because it shows consistent patterns across different text types (news, Wikipedia, tweets), which strengthens their argument that these patterns reflect a general linguistic principle (the principle of least effort). The connection to Zipf's principle is well-justified and provides a strong theoretical foundation for their approach.

### 2. Effective and practical system design
SynTime is presented as a lightweight, real-time system that achieves state-of-the-art performance without requiring training data. The authors clearly explain how the syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic rules (boundary expansion) work together to recognize time expressions. The evaluation shows that SynTime achieves higher recall than all baselines on two datasets (TimeBank and Tweets), which is particularly impressive given their observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens. This high recall aligns with their analysis and demonstrates the practical value of their approach.

### 3. Thorough experimental evaluation
The evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) and comparing against three strong baselines. The results are presented clearly with tables showing precision, recall, and F1 scores for both strict and relaxed matching. The authors also provide a meaningful analysis of why SynTime performs well on Tweets (due to people's tendency to use short, formal time expressions), which directly connects their analysis to their system's performance. The addition of SynTime-E (expanded version) demonstrates how the system can be adapted to specific domains with minimal effort.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of clear novelty in the core approach
While the analysis is strong, the core approach of using syntactic types and simple heuristic rules is not sufficiently novel. The paper acknowledges that other rule-based systems like SUTime also use regular expressions and rule-based approaches. The key difference they claim (syntactic vs. semantic types) is not convincingly justified as a significant departure from existing work. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate why their syntactic approach is fundamentally better than semantic approaches used in other rule-based systems. Without this clear novelty, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

### 2. Insufficient comparison with related work
The paper compares SynTime to only three baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime), but doesn't adequately discuss how it relates to other rule-based approaches that might have used syntactic features. For example, the paper mentions that SUTime uses "fixed rules" while SynTime uses "heuristic rules," but doesn't explain why heuristic rules are better. The paper would be stronger if it compared SynTime to other rule-based systems that might have used syntactic information or if it provided a more detailed analysis of how their syntactic type system differs from existing approaches.

### 3. Lack of analysis of why the approach works
The paper demonstrates that SynTime outperforms baselines, but doesn't deeply analyze *why* it works better. For example, they could have provided specific examples of time expressions where SynTime succeeded while baselines failed, and connected those examples to their observations about time expressions (e.g., short length, small vocabulary). The paper mentions that SynTime performs well on Tweets because people use time expressions in minimum length, but this is superficial without concrete examples. A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution.

### 4. Limited discussion of limitations
The paper briefly mentions that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but this limitation is not thoroughly discussed. The authors don't address how SynTime would handle time expressions that don't follow the observed patterns (e.g., non-standard time expressions), nor do they discuss how well their approach would generalize to other languages or domains. The paper's future work section mentions applying their method to other languages, but doesn't provide any analysis of the limitations of their current approach in this context.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that SynTime's key difference from other rule-based taggers is the use of syntactic types rather than semantic types. Can you provide specific examples of time expressions where a semantic type approach would fail but a syntactic type approach succeeds, and explain why?

2. The paper states that "the size of time words is small" (Table 3), but the number of distinct time tokens (60-80) seems relatively large for a "small vocabulary." How does this align with your observation about the small vocabulary? Could you clarify the definition of "small" in this context?

3. The paper mentions that SynTime-E adds only a few time tokens and modifiers (Table 5), but doesn't provide examples of the specific tokens added. Could you provide a few examples of tokens added for each dataset (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) and explain why they were added?

4. The paper claims SynTime achieves "exceptionally good results on Tweets dataset" with 95.87% relaxed match recall. Given that tweets are informal and contain many abbreviations, how does SynTime handle time expressions like "2day" (which appears only 3% of the time in the Twitter word cluster for "today")? Did the authors include such abbreviations in their time token list?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a strong contribution with its comprehensive analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, providing compelling evidence for the principle of least effort in time expression usage. The proposed SynTime system is well-motivated by this analysis and achieves state-of-the-art results on two of three datasets. However, the core approach lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing rule-based systems, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related work and a deeper analysis of why the approach works well.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to strengthen the novelty argument and provide more detailed analysis of the approach's strengths and limitations. The paper has significant potential to inform future work on time expression recognition, but needs to better articulate how it advances the field beyond incremental improvements.

**Overall Score: 7 (Accept)**
