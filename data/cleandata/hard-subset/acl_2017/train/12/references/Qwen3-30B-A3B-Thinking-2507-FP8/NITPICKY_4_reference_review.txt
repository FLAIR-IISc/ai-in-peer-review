

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a rule-based time expression recognition system called SynTime that uses syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four observations: time expressions are short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, contain at least one time token in 93%+ of cases, and exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they define a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and propose a rule-based approach that identifies time tokens first, then expands boundaries to find modifiers and numerals. The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets and report improved performance, particularly on Tweets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. Comprehensive analysis of time expression characteristics across multiple datasets
2. A syntactic type system for time expression recognition that uses boundary expansion heuristic rules
3. A lightweight rule-based approach that achieves competitive results with minimal training

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive time expression analysis across multiple domains**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of time expressions across four distinct datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), revealing consistent patterns that could inform future work in the field. The four observations about time expression characteristics (short length, small vocabulary, high time token coverage, and similar syntactic behaviors) are well-supported by data and provide valuable insights about how humans express time information.

2. **Clear motivation for the approach**: The paper effectively argues that time expressions follow the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), which provides a compelling theoretical foundation for the approach. The connection between the observed patterns (short expressions, small vocabulary) and the principle of least effort is well-articulated, even if the theoretical connection could be strengthened.

3. **Good experimental evaluation**: The evaluation on three different datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) covers a range of domains and text types. The comparison against three strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) is appropriate, and the results show that SynTime achieves competitive or superior performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset where it achieves 95.87% relaxed match F1.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: While the paper claims a "type-based approach" that defines syntactic types, the approach is largely a modification of existing rule-based methods rather than a fundamentally new approach. The distinction between "syntactic types" and "semantic types" is not clearly justified, and the syntactic type system (15 time tokens, 5 modifiers) is very similar to existing approaches like SUTime. The "heuristic rules based on boundary expansion" are not novel or well-explained, and the paper fails to demonstrate how this differs substantially from existing rule-based methods.

2. **Inconsistencies in terminology and presentation**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies, including inconsistent capitalization ("Time expression" vs "time expression"), inconsistent citation styles (sometimes "Author et al., 2011" and sometimes "Author et al.,? 2011"), and inconsistent use of hyphens ("rule-based" vs "rulebased"). There are also minor grammatical errors throughout (e.g., "Time expression recognition main involves two kinds" should be "Time expression recognition mainly involves two kinds"). These inconsistencies undermine the paper's professionalism and make it difficult to follow the technical content.

3. **Overstated claims about significance**: The paper claims SynTime "significantly outperforms" baselines, but the improvements are marginal (e.g., 11.37% improvement in strict match on Tweets). The paper also claims "SynTime achieves the best recalls on all three datasets and exceptionally good results on Tweets dataset," but the recall improvements are modest (e.g., 2.08% recall improvement over SUTime on TimeBank). The claim that SynTime "runs in real time" is unsubstantiated without runtime comparisons.

4. **Weak theoretical justification for the principle of least effort**: The paper claims the observations support the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), but this connection is tenuous. The principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949) specifically refers to the relationship between word frequency and rank in language, not the length of time expressions. The paper's observations about time expression characteristics are not directly related to Zipf's principle, and this misapplication of theoretical foundations weakens the paper's contribution.

5. **Lack of clear innovation and ablation study**: The paper fails to provide a clear ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component (syntactic types vs semantic types, heuristic rules vs fixed-length rules). Without this, it's impossible to determine which aspects of the approach are actually responsible for the improvements. The paper also doesn't clearly explain why the syntactic approach is better than semantic approaches, despite claiming this is a key innovation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "SynTime defines types for tokens according to their syntactic behaviors" and that "other rule-based taggers define types for tokens based on their semantic meaning." However, SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) also uses part-of-speech information (syntactic information) in its rules. How is SynTime's use of syntactic information different from SUTime's use of part-of-speech information?

2. The paper states that "Gigaword dataset was not used in our experiments because the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." Can you provide evidence that the automatic labels are not ground truth, or at least explain why the automatic labels are unreliable for this task?

3. The paper claims that "SynTime is a rule-based tagger" but also states "SynTime designs rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion." What exactly is the "heuristic method" that is different from the fixed-length rules used in other rule-based taggers? Could you provide specific examples of these heuristic rules?

4. The paper claims that "the size of time words is small" (Table 3), but the number of distinct time tokens ranges from 64 to 80 across the datasets. Given that these numbers are not extremely small, how does this small size justify the claim that manual keyword addition "will not cost much"?

5. The paper states that "the average length of time expressions" is about two words, but Table 2 shows average lengths ranging from 1.51 to 2.38 words. How does this variation across datasets affect the applicability of the approach, and why does the average length differ so much between datasets?
