

# Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes "SynTime," a rule-based time expression recognition system that categorizes tokens into syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types. The authors claim their approach is inspired by part-of-speech tagging and uses "simple heuristic rules" based on four observations about time expressions: (1) they are short (80% ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary, (3) at least 93% contain time tokens, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. They claim SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with particularly strong recall.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Analysis of time expressions across four datasets showing they are short, use small vocabulary, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior
2. A rule-based time expression recognition system (SynTime) that uses syntactic types rather than semantic types
3. Experimental validation showing SynTime achieves competitive results against state-of-the-art baselines

## Strengths

1. **Clear empirical observations about time expressions**: The paper does provide some interesting empirical observations about time expressions across four different datasets, particularly regarding their short length and small vocabulary. The data in Tables 2 and 3 provide concrete evidence for these observations.

2. **Comprehensive comparison against multiple baselines**: The paper compares SynTime against three strong state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), which is good practice for a rule-based approach.

3. **High recall results**: The paper demonstrates that SynTime achieves consistently high recall (above 92% in relaxed match on all datasets), which aligns with their observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens. This is a meaningful result that suggests the approach captures most time expressions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful methodological novelty**: The paper's core contribution is not novel. The distinction between "syntactic types" and "semantic types" is largely semantic rather than methodological. Most rule-based time expression systems already use some form of syntactic information (like POS tags) to identify time expressions. The paper doesn't demonstrate how their "syntactic types" are fundamentally different from standard POS tagging or how they provide a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. In fact, they explicitly state that they "collect from SUTime only the tokens and the regular expressions over tokens, and discard its other rules," which suggests their approach is largely derivative.

2. **Overstated claims about the principle of least effort**: The paper claims their observations "provide evidence in terms of time expression for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949)," but this is a misapplication of the principle. The principle of least effort is a broad linguistic theory, and the observed patterns in time expressions could just as easily be explained by the inherent structure of time expressions themselves rather than a principle of linguistic efficiency. This theoretical justification appears to be an after-the-fact rationalization rather than a meaningful contribution.

3. **Marginal performance improvements without sufficient justification**: The performance improvements over baselines are marginal (e.g., 92.09% F1 for SynTime-I vs. 86.10% for UWTime on TimeBank strict match). The paper doesn't convincingly explain why this small improvement is significant enough to warrant a new approach. The authors claim their method is "more flexible, expansible, and extremely light-weight," but provide no evidence for these claims beyond stating they're rule-based.

4. **Insufficient validation of the "heuristic rules" approach**: The paper repeatedly emphasizes that their rules are "simple" and "heuristic," but doesn't provide evidence that this simplicity leads to better performance. In fact, their rules appear to be more complex than those in SUTime (which they cite as a source for their token regular expressions). The paper lacks an ablation study to determine which components of their approach are actually responsible for the improvements.

5. **Overstatement of scope and capabilities**: The paper claims SynTime "can recognize all time expressions," but acknowledges in the limitations section that it doesn't handle time expressions with descriptive words (e.g., "The time Arnold reached Quebec City") or some prepositions. This significantly limits the scope of their approach, which the paper doesn't adequately address.

## Questions to Authors

1. Given that you explicitly state you "collect from SUTime only the tokens and the regular expressions over tokens, and discard its other rules," how much of the performance improvement over SUTime is due to your "syntactic types" versus simply using SUTime's token regular expressions?

2. You claim your "syntactic types" are fundamentally different from standard POS tagging. Can you provide specific examples of time expressions that POS tagging would fail to identify but your syntactic types would successfully identify?

3. Your approach requires manual addition of tokens for domain adaptation (as shown in Table 5). How does this compare to the manual effort required for other rule-based approaches like SUTime or HeidelTime? What is the actual cost of maintaining SynTime across different domains?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a minor variation on existing rule-based time expression recognition systems, with marginal performance improvements over baselines. The claimed novelty is not supported by sufficient evidence, and the theoretical justification (principle of least effort) is misapplied. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I cannot endorse this submission as a significant contribution to the field. The paper would need to provide stronger evidence of methodological novelty and a more convincing explanation for why their approach is meaningfully better than existing rule-based methods before it could be considered for publication.

**Decision: Reject**
