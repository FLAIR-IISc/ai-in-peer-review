

# Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and makes four key observations: 1) time expressions are very short (80% contain no more than three tokens), 2) the vocabulary used for expressing time is very small, 3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and 4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. The authors propose SynTime, a type-based approach that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for time expression recognition.

Based on my assessment of the paper, the main contributions are:

1. Systematic analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets showing they follow the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), providing empirical evidence for linguistic patterns in time expression usage.

2. Proposal of a syntactic type system for time expressions that enables a simpler, more flexible rule-based recognition approach, distinct from existing rule-based methods that rely on semantic types.

3. Demonstration that SynTime achieves superior or competitive results compared to state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with particularly strong recall performance.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of time expressions across four datasets, with well-supported observations that reveal consistent patterns (short length, small vocabulary, high time token prevalence, similar syntactic behavior). The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is theoretically sound and well-motivated by the data, providing valuable insights into language usage patterns that could inform future work.

2. **Clear technical distinction from prior work**: The paper effectively differentiates SynTime from existing rule-based approaches (like SUTime and HeidelTime) by emphasizing the syntactic type system versus semantic types. The description of how SynTime's heuristic boundary expansion rules differ from fixed-length rules in other systems is clear and technically sound.

3. **Strong experimental results**: The evaluation demonstrates SynTime's effectiveness, particularly on the Tweets dataset where it achieves 95.87% recall in relaxed match (6.33% improvement over SUTime). The high recall aligns with the paper's observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing a coherent explanation for the results.

4. **Practical implementation details**: The paper provides clear information about SynTime's construction, expansion process, and how it handles different time expression structures. The discussion of the limited number of keywords needed for expansion (Table 5) demonstrates the system's practicality and low maintenance requirements.

5. **Real-world applicability**: The paper's focus on informal language (Tweets) and the demonstration that even in informal contexts people use formal time expressions (with only 3% informal variants) provides important insights for practical applications. The system's real-time capability is well-positioned for practical use cases.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims SynTime is novel without sufficiently differentiating it from Tabassum et al. (2016), who also used a type-based approach for time expression recognition (though focused only on dates and using learning). The paper acknowledges this prior work but fails to adequately explain how SynTime's syntactic type system and rule-based approach differ from Tabassum's method in a way that constitutes a meaningful technical contribution.

2. **Limited explanation of boundary expansion rules**: The heuristic rules for time segment identification (Section 4.2.2) are described as "simple" but lack sufficient detail about how ambiguous cases are resolved. For example, the paper states that POS information is used to filter ambiguous words but doesn't specify how many such cases exist or how often the POS tagging resolves them correctly.

3. **Lack of runtime evaluation**: The paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time" but provides no runtime measurements or comparisons to the baseline systems. Without this evidence, the claim of real-time performance cannot be objectively verified.

4. **Inadequate explanation of WikiWars results**: On the WikiWars dataset, SynTime achieves lower F1 scores than HeidelTime and SUTime (Table 4). The paper attributes this to "descriptive time expressions" that SynTime cannot recognize because it follows TimeML standards, but this explanation lacks supporting evidence or examples of the specific cases where SynTime fails.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper mentions that SynTime assumes accurate tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't quantify how much performance degrades with imperfect input. For example, it states that Stanford POS Tagger assigns VBD to "sat" in "friday or sat" when it should be NNP, but doesn't provide an error analysis showing the impact of such errors on the overall system performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the size of time words is small" (Table 3), but doesn't provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "time word" or how these were manually identified. Could the authors clarify the criteria used to determine which words were classified as time tokens?

2. The paper claims SynTime-E achieves better results than SynTime-I on WikiWars by adding 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers (Table 5), but doesn't provide examples of these added tokens. Could the authors provide a few representative examples of the tokens that were added for WikiWars?

3. The paper states that "SynTime could not fully recognize such kinds of time expressions because it follows TimeML and TimeBank," but doesn't explain how TimeML/TimeBank standards differ from the actual time expressions in WikiWars. Could the authors provide specific examples of time expressions in WikiWars that SynTime failed to recognize and explain why?

4. The paper claims that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words" based on Twitter word clusters, but doesn't provide the exact statistics or methodology for this claim. Could the authors clarify how they determined that 97% of time-related words in tweets are formal?

5. The paper mentions that "SynTime runs in real time" but doesn't provide any runtime measurements. Could the authors provide execution times for SynTime compared to the baseline systems on a standard machine?
