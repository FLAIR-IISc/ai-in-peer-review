- Paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer using syntactic types and simple heuristic boundary rules, grounded in analysis of four datasets.  
- Main contributions: dataset-driven analysis of time expressions, syntactic-type-based recognition, and strong empirical results (esp. Tweets).

- Strength: Thorough empirical analysis showing time expressions are short, use small vocabulary, usually contain time tokens, and have similar syntactic behavior.  
- Strength: Practical, lightweight system with no training required, real-time performance, and strong recall/F1 (notably 91.74% F1 on Tweets).  
- Strength: Clear link between linguistic observations and approach, with Twitter cluster analysis supporting the theoretical claim.

- Weakness: Novelty is questionable; distinction between syntactic vs. semantic types and heuristic rules is not convincingly differentiated from prior rule-based systems like SUTime.  
- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope—Gigaword excluded and broader benchmarks or recent datasets not fully addressed.  
- Weakness: Theoretical claim tying findings to the principle of least effort is underdeveloped and not strongly causal.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison with recent neural or state-of-the-art methods beyond the cited baselines.

- Question: Provide a concrete example where SynTime handles a time expression differently/better than SUTime and explain why.  
- Question: Quantify the manual keyword addition effort for new domains (how many keywords, time required), with examples from WikiWars/Tweets.  
- Question: Why are POS tags used only for filtering and not directly incorporated into the syntactic type system despite inspiration from POS definitions?

- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—practical and empirically strong but needs stronger novelty justification and broader evaluation.
