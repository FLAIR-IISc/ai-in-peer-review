

# Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), making four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain no more than three tokens), (2) time expressions use a small vocabulary, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. These observations provide empirical evidence supporting the principle of least effort in language use. Based on these findings, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types, and uses simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. **Linguistic analysis of time expressions**: A comprehensive analysis of time expressions across four datasets that reveals four key properties providing empirical evidence for the principle of least effort in language use.

2. **Syntactic type-based approach**: A novel time expression recognition system (SynTime) that defines syntactic types for tokens based on their syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning, and uses simple heuristic rules for recognition.

3. **Empirical validation of the approach**: Demonstration that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on two datasets (TimeBank and Tweets) with significantly higher recall, while achieving comparable results on WikiWars, particularly excelling in recall where it matches the authors' observation that most time expressions contain time tokens.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough linguistic analysis**: The paper provides a well-justified and comprehensive analysis of time expressions across four datasets, revealing four key properties that collectively support the principle of least effort in language use. The analysis is supported by detailed tables (Tables 1-3) and figures (Figure 1), making the observations clear and convincing. This analysis provides valuable insights into how humans express temporal information, which could inform other NLP tasks.

2. **Clear motivation for the approach**: The paper establishes a strong connection between the linguistic observations and the design of SynTime. The authors correctly note that because time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, contain time tokens, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior, it makes sense to first identify time tokens and then search for modifiers/numerals. This logical progression from analysis to system design is well-executed and compelling.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation on three datasets demonstrates that SynTime significantly outperforms baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, particularly in recall (95.65% on TimeBank, 98.31% on Tweets). The high recall aligns perfectly with the authors' observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing strong evidence that the system effectively captures the core insight from their analysis.

4. **Practical implementation and expansion**: The paper demonstrates that SynTime can be easily expanded with minimal manual effort (adding only 3-16 time tokens), which is well-justified by the authors' analysis of the limited vocabulary used in time expressions. The expansion mechanism is practical and addresses a key concern for rule-based systems (domain adaptation).

5. **Clarity of the approach**: The paper clearly explains the syntactic types (15 for time tokens, 5 for modifiers, 1 for numerals) and the heuristic rules for time segment identification. The step-by-step description of the recognition process (time token identification, time segment identification, time expression extraction) is well-structured and easy to follow.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the syntactic type approach**: While the paper claims that defining types based on syntactic behavior is a key distinction from prior work, other rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) also use syntactic information (POS tags) in their rule design. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their syntactic type system from the use of POS tags in prior work, making the novelty claim somewhat weak.

2. **Outdated comparison with baselines**: The baselines chosen (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) are from 2010-2014, while more recent state-of-the-art methods for time expression recognition have been proposed since then (e.g., neural approaches). The paper would have been stronger with a comparison to more recent methods, which would better position the contribution in the current landscape.

3. **Insufficient handling of ambiguous cases**: The paper acknowledges that some words can be ambiguous (e.g., "May" as a month vs. verb) but doesn't provide a detailed discussion of how SynTime handles these cases or the impact on performance. This is a significant limitation, as ambiguous words are a common challenge in time expression recognition.

4. **Overemphasis on short expressions**: The paper claims time expressions are "very short" based on the 80% statistic, but this ignores the 20% of longer expressions. The paper doesn't discuss how SynTime handles these longer expressions, nor does it provide performance metrics specifically for these cases, which could be important in certain domains.

5. **Lack of analysis of failure cases**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of cases where SynTime fails or where it performs worse than baselines. Understanding these failure modes would help readers better assess the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that SynTime defines syntactic types "inspired by part-of-speech" but doesn't clearly explain how these types differ from POS tags. Could you clarify this distinction and explain how it leads to better performance than simply using POS tags?

2. The paper reports that 20% of time expressions contain more than three tokens (Table 1). How does SynTime handle these longer expressions, and what is the impact on performance (precision/recall) for these cases compared to shorter expressions?

3. The paper mentions that SynTime uses POS tags for disambiguation but doesn't specify which POS tags are used for which types. Could you provide more details on the POS tag sets used for each syntactic type?

4. Could you provide a comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., neural approaches) to better position the contribution in the current literature?

5. The paper states that "time expressions in general are very short" but the results show that 80% contain no more than three tokens. Could you discuss the implications of the remaining 20% of longer time expressions for real-world applications?
