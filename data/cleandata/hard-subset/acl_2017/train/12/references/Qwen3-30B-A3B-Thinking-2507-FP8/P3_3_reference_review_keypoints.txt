- Summary: Proposes SynTime, a rule-based time-expression recognizer using four empirical observations (short length, small vocabulary, presence of time tokens, syntactic consistency) and syntactic-type heuristic rules.

- Strength: Strong empirical foundation—comprehensive statistics across four datasets showing time expressions are short and contain time tokens in 93–98% of cases.

- Strength: The Zipf/least-effort explanation is well-motivated and ties observed patterns (short expressions, small vocabulary, syntactic consistency) to communication efficiency.

- Strength: Strong empirical performance—large F1/recall gains on TimeBank and Tweets and comparable results on WikiWars, aligning with the token-presence observation.

- Weakness: Overstated novelty—the claimed syntactic-type distinction from prior POS-based systems (e.g., SUTime) is not clearly established.

- Weakness: Limited error/robustness analysis—no study of how tokenization or POS-tagging errors affect SynTime’s rule-based performance.

- Weakness: Poor domain generalization analysis—the manual domain-specific additions for WikiWars lack a scalable or automatic method for new domains.

- Weakness: Limited handling of complex/descriptive time expressions (e.g., “the time Arnold reached Quebec City”) with no proposed remedies.

- Question: Provide a concrete example where SUTime fails but SynTime succeeds due to the syntactic-type distinction.

- Question: Supply broader Twitter-formality statistics for other common time expressions (month, year, week) beyond the “today” example.

- Question: How would SynTime perform in non-English languages with weaker POS tagging, and would language-specific changes be required?

- Overall assessment: Valuable empirical contribution linking time expressions to least-effort principles, but methodological novelty is limited; recommend accept with minor revisions to clarify novelty, error analysis, and limitations.
