- Paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets and proposes SynTime, a rule-based recognizer using syntactic types and simple heuristic boundary expansion.  
- Key empirical observations: time expressions are very short (80% ≤3 tokens), have a small vocabulary, ≥93% contain time tokens, and words show similar syntactic behavior.  
- Contributions: comprehensive linguistic analysis supporting Zipf’s principle, a syntactic-type-based recognizer, and empirical gains on TimeBank and Tweets.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation across diverse datasets with high F1 and recall, notably 92.09% F1 on TimeBank and 91.74% F1 on Tweets.  
- Strength: compelling, data-supported linguistic analysis that provides a useful theoretical perspective.  
- Strength: SynTime is lightweight, real-time, requires no training data, and maintains performance with minimal vocabulary expansion.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—SynTime is largely a rule-based system similar to prior tools (e.g., SUTime, HeidelTime) with minor variations.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification that syntactic types outperform semantic types; distinction seems mainly conceptual and untested.  
- Weakness: underdeveloped analysis of limitations—no evaluation of robustness to tokenization/POS errors or ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May”).  
- Question: why are syntactic types superior to semantic types, and could standard POS-tagging achieve the same results?  
- Question: what are concrete runtime measurements and how does SynTime’s speed compare to other rule-based systems like SUTime?  
- Question: how does SynTime detect time expressions that lack explicit time tokens, given the 93.18% time-token statistic?  
- Overall: valuable linguistic contribution, but methodological novelty is marginal and the paper should better demonstrate advantages of the syntactic-type approach.  
- Recommendation: weak acceptance, with revisions to clarify novelty and evaluate robustness and runtime.
