- SynTime: a rule-based time-expression recognizer using syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and boundary-expansion heuristics, evaluated on four datasets.

Observations:
- Time expressions are very short: ~80% are ≤ 3 tokens.
- Time expressions use a small, recurring vocabulary of time-related keywords.
- Over 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token.
- Words within time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behaviors, consistent with Zipf’s least-effort principle.

Strengths:
- Thorough multi-dataset analysis (news, Wikipedia, tweets) with well-presented supporting data.
- Strong performance on informal text (Tweets) with 95.87% relaxed-match F1, outperforming baselines.
- Simple, lightweight, and extensible method using syntactic types and minimal boundary expansion.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty appears overstated; related syntactic methods (e.g., Lee et al. 2014 using CCG) are not adequately distinguished.
- Lacks analysis of error sources and sensitivity to tokenization/POS tagging errors and ambiguous tokens (e.g., "May").
- Limited evaluation scope and insufficient justification for excluding Gigaword/standard benchmarks.
- No computational complexity or runtime benchmarks provided to support real-time efficiency claims.

Questions to authors:
- Provide a detailed comparison between your syntactic type system and Lee et al. (2014)’s CCG-based approach.
- Why does SynTime-E yield only a 0.03% F1 improvement on Tweets despite adding time tokens—does this contradict your observations?
- Please supply timing benchmarks (e.g., on Gigaword) comparing SynTime to baselines to substantiate efficiency claims.

Overall assessment:
- Interesting and well-supported analysis with strong tweet results, but the paper needs clearer novelty positioning, error/efficiency analysis, and dataset-justification to be fully convincing.
