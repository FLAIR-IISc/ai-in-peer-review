

# Peer Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), making four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% have ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types, and uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion.

The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), claiming it outperforms them, especially on TimeBank and Tweets, with exceptional recall on Tweets (98.31% relaxed match).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Empirical analysis of time expression properties across multiple domains**: The paper provides a thorough, data-driven analysis of time expressions in diverse text types (news, Wikipedia, tweets), offering concrete evidence for the principle of least effort in language use. This analysis is well-supported and valuable for understanding time expression patterns.

2. **SynTime system with syntactic type system**: While the core approach is rule-based, the authors' focus on syntactic types (rather than semantic types) and simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion represents a practical refinement of existing rule-based methods.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness on challenging domain (tweets)**: The high recall on the Tweets dataset (98.31% relaxed match) is particularly compelling given the informal nature of Twitter text, which is a significant challenge for time expression recognition.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis**: The paper's analysis of time expressions across four datasets is thorough and well-supported with statistics. The observations about time expression shortness (80% ≤3 tokens), small vocabulary (60-80 distinct time tokens), and high time token coverage (≥93%) provide valuable insights that align with linguistic principles. This analysis is the paper's strongest contribution and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

2. **High performance on Tweets dataset**: The results on the Tweets dataset are impressive, with SynTime achieving 95.87% relaxed match F1. This is particularly significant because Twitter's informal language, abbreviations, and noise make time expression recognition challenging. The authors' explanation that people tend to use formal words even in informal settings (supported by their Twitter word cluster analysis) provides a compelling rationale for the high performance.

3. **Simplicity and efficiency**: SynTime's lightweight nature and real-time performance are practical advantages over machine learning-based approaches that require training and may be slower at inference. The simplicity of their heuristic rules (boundary expansion) makes the system easy to understand, implement, and adapt, which is valuable for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach**: The paper's main claim of a "type-based approach" using "syntactic types" is overstated. SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) and HeidelTime (Strötgen and Gertz, 2010) already use rule-based approaches with some form of type system, and their methods rely heavily on syntactic information (like POS tags). The distinction between "syntactic" and "semantic" types is not clearly articulated or justified. For example, SUTime's "frequency modifiers" and SynTime's "PREFIX modifiers" are both based on syntactic position, not semantic meaning. The authors fail to demonstrate how their approach differs significantly from existing systems.

2. **Outdated comparison with baselines**: The paper compares against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, but these are relatively dated methods (SUTime from 2012, UWTime from 2014). More recent state-of-the-art approaches, such as neural network-based methods (e.g., Deep Learning for Time Expression Recognition), are not included in the comparison. This weakens the claim that SynTime outperforms "state-of-the-art" methods, as the comparison is against older baselines.

3. **Questionable methodology for Tweets dataset**: The authors manually annotated 942 tweets containing time expressions but used SUTime to tag the remaining 3,058 tweets to identify 500 for manual annotation. They claim SUTime missed about 3% of time expressions in tweets, but they don't provide any error analysis of these missed examples or show representative cases. This makes their claim about the quality of their dataset somewhat questionable.

4. **Overstated claims about "principle of least effort"**: The paper claims their observations provide "evidence in terms of time expression for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949)." However, the principle of least effort is a general linguistic principle, and while their observations are consistent with it, they don't provide strong evidence that this is the *cause* of the observed patterns. The paper doesn't rule out alternative explanations (e.g., time expressions are short because they're commonly used in specific grammatical constructions).

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper briefly mentions that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but this is a significant limitation, especially for informal text like tweets. They don't provide any analysis of how errors in these components affect SynTime's performance, which is crucial for understanding the system's real-world applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify how your syntactic type system differs from the type systems used in SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) or HeidelTime (Strötgen and Gertz, 2010)? Specifically, how is your "syntactic" approach different from the POS-based approaches already used in these systems?

2. You state that "POS could not distinguish time expressions from common words," but then use POS to filter ambiguous words (e.g., "May" as month vs. modal verb). This seems contradictory. Can you clarify how POS information is used in your system, and why it's useful despite not being able to distinguish time expressions from common words?

3. The paper claims that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words," citing a Twitter word cluster analysis. However, you also mention that "2day" and "tday" are popular informal variants. How do you reconcile these two statements? Is the 3% informal rate (as mentioned for the 'today' cluster) sufficient to explain the high performance on Tweets, or are there other factors at play?

4. You mention that "Gigaword dataset was not used in our experiments because the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." However, you used SUTime to tag tweets to get a training set. Why is it acceptable to use SUTime's automatic labels for your training set but not for Gigaword?

5. The paper states that SynTime-E improves performance on WikiWars by adding 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers. Can you provide examples of these tokens and explain why they were necessary? This would help clarify whether the improvement is due to domain-specific knowledge or simply more comprehensive rules.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a solid empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple domains, which is valuable for the field. The SynTime system is a practical refinement of existing rule-based approaches, and its high performance on the challenging Tweets dataset is impressive. However, the novelty of the approach is overstated, and the comparison with baselines is outdated. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with recent methods and a clearer articulation of how their syntactic type system differs from existing approaches.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the novelty of the approach and the comparison with more recent baselines. The empirical analysis is strong, but the claimed contribution of a "type-based approach" needs to be better justified against existing work.
