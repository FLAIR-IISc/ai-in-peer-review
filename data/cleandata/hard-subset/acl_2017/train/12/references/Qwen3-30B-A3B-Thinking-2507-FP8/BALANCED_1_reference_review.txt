

# Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and makes four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words within time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose "SynTime," a rule-based time expression recognizer that uses syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion. The authors compare SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets, demonstrating that SynTime achieves high recall (92%+) across all datasets and outperforms baselines on TimeBank and Tweets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**
1. **Novel empirical analysis of time expressions** showing they follow the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), with compelling evidence from multiple datasets.
2. **Practical, lightweight rule-based approach** (SynTime) that achieves state-of-the-art results without training data and runs in real-time.
3. **Strong experimental validation** demonstrating SynTime's effectiveness, particularly its high recall across multiple datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical foundation with compelling empirical evidence:** The paper's most significant strength is its thorough analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, providing strong empirical support for the principle of least effort in language usage. The four observations (short expressions, small vocabulary, high occurrence of time tokens, similar syntactic behavior) are well-documented with clear statistics (Tables 2-3, Figure 1) and properly linked to linguistic theory. This analysis is genuinely novel and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

2. **Practical, lightweight solution with real-world applicability:** SynTime's simplicity is a major strength - it requires no training data, runs in real-time, and is extremely lightweight. The authors demonstrate that SynTime achieves state-of-the-art results without complex machine learning models, which is valuable for applications where training data is scarce or where computational resources are limited. The fact that SynTime-E (expanded version) shows only modest improvements on TimeBank and Tweets (Table 5) confirms the small size of time vocabulary, which is a key insight from the analysis.

3. **Strong empirical results with clear advantages:** The experimental results (Table 4) are impressive, with SynTime achieving F1 scores above 91% across all datasets and exceptionally high recall (92%+). The analysis of why SynTime performs particularly well on Tweets (short expressions, small vocabulary, formal language) is insightful and well-supported. The paper clearly demonstrates that SynTime's high recall aligns with their observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens (Table 2), which is a strong theoretical validation of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient articulation of novelty compared to existing rule-based approaches:** While the authors claim that SynTime differs from other rule-based taggers by using "syntactic types" rather than "semantic types," this distinction is not clearly established. SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) also uses part-of-speech information (a syntactic feature) in its rules. The paper needs to more clearly explain what makes their syntactic type system novel and why it leads to better performance. The claim that "other rule-based taggers design rules in a fixed way" is not sufficiently substantiated with specific examples.

2. **Lack of concrete failure cases and limitations analysis:** The paper mentions limitations (Section 5.3) but doesn't provide specific examples of where SynTime fails. Without concrete failure cases, it's difficult to assess the boundaries of the system's applicability. The authors state that "SynTime could not fully recognize time expressions in WikiWars that include lots of prepositions and quite a few descriptive time expressions," but don't provide examples to illustrate this limitation. This omission weakens the paper's credibility.

3. **Incomplete comparison with relevant prior work:** The paper compares SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, but doesn't adequately compare to the most relevant rule-based approaches. The authors reference SUTime as a state-of-the-art rule-based tagger, but don't explain why they didn't include newer versions of SUTime (e.g., the one used in TempEval-3) or other recent rule-based approaches. The comparison to machine learning approaches is also limited, as they don't compare to more recent neural approaches that might be more relevant to current NLP practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim that SynTime uses "syntactic types" rather than "semantic types," but SUTime also uses part-of-speech information (a syntactic feature) in its rules. Could you clarify the specific differences between your syntactic type system and the syntactic features used in SUTime? How does your type system lead to better performance?

2. In Section 5.2.1, the authors state that "for the rule-based time taggers, their rules are designed in a fixed way, lacking flexibility." Can you provide specific examples of time expressions that SynTime recognizes but SUTime cannot, to illustrate this flexibility? What specific time expressions does SynTime handle better due to its heuristic boundary expansion?

3. The paper mentions that "in tweets people tend to use formal words" based on Twitter word clusters. Can you provide specific examples of time expressions in tweets that use formal words versus informal variants (e.g., 'today' vs '2day')? How does SynTime handle cases where informal variants are used, and how many of these variants were added during SynTime expansion (Table 5)?

4. The authors state that "the size of time words is small" (Table 3), but don't provide a complete list of time tokens. Could you share the full list of time tokens used in SynTime to demonstrate the small vocabulary claim? How many of these tokens are specific to each dataset?

## Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of time expression patterns and provides a practical, lightweight solution that achieves strong results. The empirical analysis is thorough and well-supported, and the proposed SynTime system is genuinely useful for real-world applications.

However, the paper would benefit from strengthening the novelty claims (particularly clarifying how the syntactic type system differs from and improves upon existing approaches), providing concrete examples of failure cases, and expanding the comparison to more relevant prior work. With these improvements, the paper would be significantly stronger.

The main contributions of the paper are compelling, particularly the analysis of time expressions following the principle of least effort and the development of a lightweight, high-recall system. These contributions are valuable to the field, though the novelty claims could be better supported. The paper is well-written and the experiments are appropriately conducted, making it a strong candidate for acceptance with minor revisions.
