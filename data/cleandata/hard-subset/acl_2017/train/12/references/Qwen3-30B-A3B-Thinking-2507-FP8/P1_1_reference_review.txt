

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper proposes a new approach to time expression recognition called SynTime, which is based on analyzing time expressions across four different datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The authors make four key observations about time expressions: they are very short (80% have ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, at least 93% contain time tokens, and words within time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Inspired by part-of-speech tagging, they define a syntactic type system for time expressions with three main types (time tokens, modifiers, and numerals) and design simple heuristic rules based on token positions for boundary expansion. The paper evaluates SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets, showing it outperforms the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Thorough empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of time expressions from four different sources, revealing consistent patterns that support the principle of least effort in language usage. This analysis is well-executed and provides a strong foundation for their approach.

2. **Conceptual contribution of syntactic types for time expressions**: The paper introduces a novel framework for time expression recognition based on syntactic types (inspired by part-of-speech tagging) rather than semantic types used in previous work. This conceptual distinction is significant for the field.

3. **Simple, lightweight rule-based system with strong performance**: SynTime is a rule-based system that requires no training, runs in real-time, and achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three evaluation datasets, with exceptionally high recall rates.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis**: The paper's analysis of time expressions is comprehensive and well-supported by data across four datasets. The four observations they make are compelling and well-justified, providing a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is particularly insightful and well-articulated.

2. **Clear conceptual distinction between syntactic and semantic types**: The paper makes a valuable distinction between the syntactic type system used in SynTime (based on token behavior) and semantic type systems used in previous work. This conceptual contribution is significant and could influence future work in time expression recognition.

3. **Strong experimental results**: SynTime achieves the best recall on all three datasets (above 92% for relaxed match), which aligns perfectly with their observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens. The performance on Tweets (95.87% F1 in relaxed match) is particularly impressive and demonstrates the system's effectiveness in informal contexts.

4. **Practical advantages of the approach**: SynTime is lightweight, requires no training, and runs in real-time, making it highly practical for real-world applications. This is a significant advantage over learning-based approaches that require training data and computational resources.

5. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-written, with a logical flow from analysis to method to evaluation. The figures and tables are well-designed and effectively support the arguments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach**: While the syntactic type system is conceptually interesting, the actual implementation of SynTime appears to be a refinement of existing rule-based systems rather than a fundamentally new method. Many of the heuristic rules used are similar to those used in SUTime and other rule-based systems, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate SynTime from these existing approaches.

2. **Lack of direct comparison between syntactic and semantic types**: The authors claim that using syntactic types is better than semantic types, but they don't provide a direct comparison between the two approaches. A version of SynTime using semantic types would have strengthened their argument for the superiority of their approach.

3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is limited to three datasets, with Gigaword excluded due to automatic labels. More diverse evaluation across additional datasets would have strengthened the generalizability claims. Additionally, the paper doesn't address how SynTime handles time expressions that don't follow the observed patterns (e.g., more complex expressions in specific domains).

4. **Insufficient discussion of POS dependency limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime depends on correct POS tagging, which is a significant limitation in real-world applications. However, the authors don't adequately discuss how this dependency impacts performance or how it could be mitigated. This is a critical weakness for a rule-based system.

5. **Overstatement of the "principle of least effort" connection**: While the authors connect their observations to Zipf's principle of least effort, they don't provide sufficient evidence that these patterns are *specifically* due to the principle of least effort rather than other linguistic or pragmatic factors. The connection feels somewhat tenuous.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details on how you determined which tokens to add during SynTime expansion, and how this process would scale to other domains with different time expression patterns?

2. How would you handle time expressions that don't follow the "principle of least effort" patterns, such as highly descriptive time expressions in WikiWars? Would SynTime require significant modifications to handle these cases?

3. The paper claims SynTime's syntactic type system is superior to semantic type systems, but doesn't provide a direct comparison. Could you create a version of SynTime using semantic types (as used in SUTime) and compare the performance?

4. The paper mentions that in tweets, people tend to use formal words despite informal communication. How does this finding impact your approach to handling time expressions in social media, and how would you adapt SynTime for other informal contexts where people might use more creative or abbreviated time expressions?

5. The paper states that SynTime-E adds only a few tokens for most datasets, but more for WikiWars. Could you provide examples of these added tokens and explain why they weren't included in the initial version?

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution through its thorough empirical analysis of time expressions and the introduction of a syntactic type system for time expression recognition. The strong experimental results, particularly the high recall rates on all three datasets, validate the approach. While there are some limitations regarding novelty and evaluation scope, the paper's strengths outweigh these weaknesses, and the work provides valuable insights for the field of temporal information extraction.
