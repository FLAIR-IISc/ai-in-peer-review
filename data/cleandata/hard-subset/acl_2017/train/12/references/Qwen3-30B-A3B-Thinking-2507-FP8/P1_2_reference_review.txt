

# Peer Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain no more than three tokens), (2) a small vocabulary is used to express time information, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that uses syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types to define rules. They claim SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, especially in recall.

## Main Contributions

1. Comprehensive empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets revealing patterns consistent with Zipf's principle of least effort, providing valuable linguistic insights into how people express time.

2. Proposal of a syntactic type system for time expressions and the development of SynTime, a rule-based approach that leverages these types for recognition without requiring training data.

3. Strong empirical results demonstrating SynTime's superiority on the Tweets dataset (95.87% relaxed match F1) and competitive performance on TimeBank and WikiWars.

## Strengths

1. **Well-executed empirical analysis with strong theoretical grounding**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of time expressions across four diverse datasets, making four well-supported observations. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is intellectually compelling and well-justified. The authors demonstrate that time expressions follow linguistic patterns that minimize communication effort, which is a significant contribution to our understanding of time expression patterns.

2. **Strong performance on the challenging Tweets dataset**: SynTime achieves 95.87% relaxed match F1 on the Tweets dataset, significantly outperforming all baselines (by 6.33% in relaxed match). This is particularly impressive given the informal nature of tweets and the challenges of recognizing time expressions in social media. The authors effectively explain this success through their four observations (short expressions, small vocabulary, time tokens, and syntactic behavior).

3. **Practical advantages of the approach**: SynTime is extremely lightweight (no training required, runs in real-time) and can be easily expanded with domain-specific time expressions. This practical advantage over machine learning-based approaches (which require training data and computational resources) is well-documented and significant for real-world applications.

4. **Clear demonstration of the principle of least effort**: The paper provides concrete evidence that time expressions follow the principle of least effort in multiple ways (short expressions, small vocabulary, time tokens, and syntactic behavior), which is a valuable contribution to linguistic theory and NLP applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the syntactic type approach**: The paper claims that "other rule-based taggers define types for tokens based on their semantic meaning" while SynTime defines types based on "syntactic behaviors." However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate this distinction in practice. For example, SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) already uses POS tags in its rules, which are syntactic features. The authors fail to provide a clear example of how their syntactic types differ from semantic types used in prior work, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their approach.

2. **Limited evaluation scope and justification for dataset exclusion**: The authors explicitly exclude Gigaword from their experiments because "the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." This is a significant limitation, as Gigaword is a standard benchmark dataset in the field. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the automatic labels are particularly problematic for time expression recognition, and the exclusion weakens the generalizability of their claims. For example, they could have evaluated on a subset of Gigaword with manually verified labels.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't provide any analysis of how errors in these components affect performance. Given that the authors use Stanford POS Tagger, which has known limitations (e.g., assigning VBD to "sat" in "friday or sat" when it should be NNP), this is a significant practical concern that needs to be addressed. The paper should have included error analysis showing how tokenization/POS errors impact SynTime's performance.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares to SUTime (2012), HeidelTime (2010), and UWTime (2014), but doesn't include more recent approaches. For example, there have been several advances in neural methods for time expression recognition since 2014 that could provide a more comprehensive comparison. The exclusion of newer methods weakens the claim that SynTime outperforms "state-of-the-art" approaches.

5. **Overstated claims about the heuristic rules**: The paper claims that their "heuristic rules are quite simple that it makes SynTime much more flexible, expansible, and extremely light-weight." However, the rules described in Section 4.2.2 are straightforward boundary expansion rules that are not particularly novel. The paper doesn't provide convincing evidence that these rules are significantly more flexible or expandable than existing rule-based approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "other rule-based taggers define types for tokens based on their semantic meaning" while SynTime defines types based on "syntactic behaviors." Can you provide a concrete example of how the word "ago" would be classified differently by SynTime versus SUTime? For instance, does SUTime treat "ago" as a semantic modifier while SynTime treats it as a syntactic modifier? How does this classification difference lead to different recognition patterns?

2. The paper states that Gigaword was excluded because "the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." However, many researchers use automatically generated labels for evaluation when gold standard is unavailable. Can you provide specific examples of time expressions in Gigaword that were mislabeled by the automatic system, and explain why these errors would significantly impact the evaluation of SynTime?

3. The paper mentions that "SynTime is a rule-based tagger" but "designed rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion." How does this boundary expansion approach differ from the rule-based approach used in SUTime, which also expands from individual tokens to time expressions? The paper describes SUTime as using "fixed rules" while SynTime uses "heuristic rules," but the distinction is not clear.

4. In Table 4, SynTime achieves 95.87% relaxed match F1 on Tweets, which is significantly better than all baselines. However, the paper doesn't explain why SynTime is particularly effective on Tweets. Given that tweets use informal language and abbreviations, how does SynTime handle these challenges better than the baselines? The paper mentions that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words," but this seems counterintuitive for social media. Can you provide evidence supporting this claim?

5. The paper states that "the size of time tokens is small" (Table 3 shows 60-80 distinct time tokens across datasets). How did the authors determine which words should be considered "time tokens" versus regular words? For example, how did they distinguish between "month" as a time token versus "month" as part of a regular phrase like "the month of January"?
