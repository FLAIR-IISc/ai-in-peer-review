

# ACL Review: Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and makes four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain no more than three tokens), (2) the vocabulary for expressing time is small, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for recognition.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Insightful empirical analysis of time expressions**: The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing consistent patterns that are well-supported by data. This analysis forms a strong foundation for the proposed method.

2. **Novel type definition approach**: The paper proposes a syntactic type system for time expression recognition that differs from previous rule-based approaches by defining types based on syntactic behavior rather than semantic meaning.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates that SynTime achieves state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art performance on multiple benchmarks, particularly excelling on the Tweets dataset with high recall rates (95.87% on relaxed match).

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive and well-supported empirical analysis**: The paper's analysis of time expressions across four different datasets is thorough and well-documented. The four observations (shortness, small vocabulary, high proportion containing time tokens, and similar syntactic behavior) are clearly presented with supporting statistics and figures. This analysis provides genuine insight into time expression patterns that could be valuable for future work in the field. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is particularly interesting, even if somewhat speculative, and provides a theoretical framework for the observed patterns.

2. **Clear motivation for the syntactic type approach**: The paper effectively explains why the syntactic type approach is superior to semantic type approaches used in existing systems. The authors demonstrate how their method avoids the inflexibility of fixed-length rules in systems like SUTime by using heuristic boundary expansion. The examples showing how SynTime handles expressions like "year 1" (which SUTime misses) while still maintaining high precision are convincing.

3. **Strong empirical results with high recall**: The results on the Tweets dataset are particularly impressive, with SynTime achieving 95.87% recall on relaxed match and 91.74% F1 on strict match. This aligns perfectly with the paper's key observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, demonstrating that the authors' analysis directly translated into practical improvements. The fact that SynTime achieves the best recalls on all three datasets is a significant strength.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient differentiation from existing work**: While the authors claim their syntactic type approach is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this differs from existing rule-based systems. SUTime already uses POS tags to some extent, and the authors don't clearly articulate why their syntactic types are fundamentally different from the semantic types they claim to replace. The comparison between SUTime's "semantic modifier types" and SynTime's "syntactic modifier types" lacks sufficient detail to convince the reader of the novelty.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper analyzes Gigaword but excludes it from the evaluation, stating the labels "may not be the ground truth." This is a significant limitation because Gigaword is a standard benchmark dataset for time expression recognition. The authors don't provide a compelling reason for excluding it from evaluation, especially since they used it in their analysis. This omission weakens the paper's claim of being "state-of-the-art" as the evaluation is limited to three datasets rather than the standard benchmarks.

3. **Overstated theoretical connection to Zipf's principle**: The paper's connection between the observed patterns and Zipf's principle of least effort is tenuous. While the authors cite Zipf, they don't provide a strong theoretical or empirical link between the observed patterns and the principle. The connection seems more like a post-hoc justification rather than a well-supported theoretical contribution. The phrase "provide evidence in terms of time expression for the principle of least effort" is overstated—the paper shows patterns that *could* be explained by the principle, but doesn't provide evidence that the principle *caused* these patterns.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "Gigaword dataset was not used in our experiments because the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." However, the authors also analyzed Gigaword in their dataset analysis. Could they clarify why they believe the labels in Gigaword are unreliable for evaluation, and how this affects the validity of their analysis? Did they check the quality of the Gigaword labels against a gold standard?

2. The paper claims SynTime defines types based on syntactic behavior while other systems use semantic types. SUTime, however, does use POS information in its rules. Could the authors clarify the specific differences between their syntactic types and the POS-based rules used in SUTime, and why they consider this a meaningful distinction?

3. The paper states SynTime "runs in real time" but doesn't provide any timing measurements. How does SynTime's processing speed compare to the baselines? What is the average time per document or per time expression, and how does this compare to other systems?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from analysis to method to evaluation. The technical details of SynTime's operation are well-explained with helpful examples. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly regarding how the syntactic type approach differs from other rule-based systems that might use similar concepts.

The analysis of time expressions in tweets is particularly interesting and well-supported. The finding that even in informal communication like tweets, people tend to use formal time expressions (with only about 3% informal variants) is valuable and well-documented. This insight could have implications for other NLP tasks involving informal language.

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper makes a strong empirical contribution with its detailed analysis and effective system, the lack of clear novelty compared to existing work and the limited evaluation scope prevent it from being a compelling contribution to the field. The insights about time expression patterns are valuable, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how SynTime represents a meaningful advance beyond current state-of-the-art rule-based approaches. The connection to Zipf's principle is interesting but not sufficiently supported.

I recommend **rejection** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Expand the evaluation to include Gigaword dataset, or provide a convincing justification for its exclusion from evaluation (e.g., by comparing the automatically generated labels with a gold standard).

2. Provide a more detailed comparison of the syntactic type approach with existing systems to better establish the novelty of the method.

3. Strengthen the theoretical connection to Zipf's principle or consider removing this claim if it cannot be adequately supported.

4. Include timing measurements to demonstrate the "real-time" claim.

With these improvements, the paper would make a more substantial contribution to the field.
