

# ACL Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression recognition that defines syntactic types for tokens and uses simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), making four observations: (1) time expressions are very short, (2) time expressions use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they define a syntactic type system (15 time token types, 5 modifier types, and 1 numeral type) to recognize time expressions. They evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets, reporting that SynTime outperforms baselines in recall and achieves competitive F1 scores.

## Main Contributions
1. A systematic analysis of time expressions across four datasets revealing key properties that relate to the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949)
2. A syntactic type-based approach to time expression recognition that defines types based on syntactic behaviors rather than semantic meaning
3. A lightweight rule-based system (SynTime) that achieves high recall and competitive precision across multiple datasets

## Strengths
1. **Thorough analysis of time expressions**: The paper provides a detailed quantitative analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing consistent patterns (short length, small vocabulary, high percentage containing time tokens) that are valuable for time expression recognition.

2. **Practical lightweight system**: SynTime is presented as an extremely lightweight rule-based system that runs in real time without requiring training data, which is a practical advantage for many NLP applications.

3. **Strong recall performance**: The paper demonstrates that SynTime achieves the highest recall on all three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), which is particularly impressive given the paper's claim that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens. This consistency with their analysis is well-supported.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Numerous formatting and typographical errors (critical flaw)
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors that significantly undermine its professionalism and readability. Examples include:
- "Time expression recognition main involves two kinds" (should be "mainly involves")
- "recoginze" (should be "recognize")
- Inconsistent capitalization of "SynTime" (sometimes "SynTime", sometimes "SynTime")
- "Alonso et al.,? 2011" in references (should be "Alonso et al., 2011" without the question mark)
- "SUTime defines 5 semantic modifier types, such as frequency modifiers; 1 while SynTime defines 5 syntactic modifier types" (the semicolon and "1" are clearly typos)
- Inconsistent pluralization of "time token(s)" vs "time tokens"
- "Table 2: The percentage of time expressions that contain at least one time token, and the average length of time expressions." (should be "the percentage of time expressions containing at least one time token" for consistency)

These errors are pervasive throughout the paper and indicate a lack of careful proofreading, which is unacceptable for an ACL submission.

### 2. Technical contradictions and unclear explanations
The paper contains several technical contradictions and unclear explanations that weaken its core claims:

- The paper claims "words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors" (Introduction), but Observation 4 states "Part-of-speech (POS) could not distinguish time expressions from common words, but within time expressions, POS can help distinguish their constituents." These statements are contradictory - if words demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors, POS should not be able to distinguish their constituents.

- The paper states "TimeML (Pustejovsky et al., 2003a) and TimeBank (Pustejovsky et al., 2003b) do not treat most prepositions like 'on' as a part of time expressions. Thus SynTime does not collect those prepositions." However, in Figure 3(c), the example "January/MONTH 13/NUMERAL 1951/YEAR" shows a time token "1951/YEAR" as part of the shared boundary, which contradicts the claim about not collecting prepositions.

- The paper claims "SynTime designs rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion" but never clearly explains what "boundary expansion" means or how it differs from other rule-based approaches.

### 3. Lack of evidence for key theoretical claims
The paper claims that the four observations provide "evidence in terms of time expression for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949)," but provides no actual evidence or explanation for how the observations relate to this principle. The connection between the empirical observations and Zipf's theoretical principle is stated as fact without any supporting analysis or argument.

The paper also claims that "the size of time tokens is small" (Table 3 shows 60-80 distinct time tokens), but doesn't provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "time token" or how these tokens were identified. For example, should "month" and "months" be counted as one token or two? The paper doesn't clarify this.

### 4. Missing methodological details for reproducibility
The paper lacks several important methodological details that would be essential for reproducibility:

- No timing measurements are provided to support the claim that SynTime "runs in real time."
- No statistical significance tests are reported to show whether the performance improvements over baselines are statistically significant.
- The paper mentions "SynTime-E" (the expanded version) but doesn't provide a clear explanation of the expansion process beyond "we add keywords to SynTime-I." The specific criteria for adding keywords are vague.

### 5. Inconsistent dataset usage
The paper analyzes time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), but only uses three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) for evaluation. It states "Gigaword dataset was not used in our experiments because the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." However, it doesn't explain why Gigaword was used for analysis but not for evaluation, and why the analysis results from Gigaword are presented but not used in the evaluation.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do the four observations specifically provide evidence for the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949)? The paper states this as a fact without explaining the connection.

2. The paper claims "words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors" but Observation 4 states that "within time expressions, POS can help distinguish their constituents." Could you clarify this apparent contradiction?

3. Could you provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "time token" and how they were identified (including how variants like "month" vs. "months" were handled)?

4. What is the exact meaning of "boundary expansion" in the context of SynTime? Could you provide a more detailed explanation or example?

5. The paper claims SynTime "runs in real time" but doesn't provide any timing measurements or comparisons to other systems. Could you provide these details?

## Overall Recommendation
Reject

This paper presents an interesting approach to time expression recognition with some promising empirical results, but it contains too many critical flaws to be accepted for ACL. The numerous typographical and formatting errors, technical contradictions, lack of evidence for key theoretical claims, and missing methodological details significantly undermine the paper's credibility. While the analysis of time expressions is valuable, the paper fails to properly justify its theoretical foundation, explain its methodology clearly, and present itself professionally. 

As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these minor flaws collectively represent a substantial issue that outweighs the paper's contributions. The errors and contradictions are too pervasive to be considered mere "minor" issues. For a top-tier conference like ACL, the paper must demonstrate exceptional attention to detail and rigorous presentation, which this submission does not achieve.
