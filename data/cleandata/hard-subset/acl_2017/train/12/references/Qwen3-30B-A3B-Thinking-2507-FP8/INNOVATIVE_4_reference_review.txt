

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a new time expression recognition system called "SynTime" that uses a syntactic type system rather than semantic types. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets and make four observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) time expressions use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. They connect these observations to Zipf's principle of least effort and propose SynTime as a rule-based approach that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.

The paper compares SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets), reporting that SynTime outperforms the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, with comparable results on WikiWars.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The four observations about time expressions (shortness, small vocabulary, time tokens in most expressions, similar syntactic behaviors)
2. The syntactic type system for time expression recognition (time tokens, modifiers, numerals)
3. The experimental validation of SynTime against state-of-the-art baselines

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical connection to Zipf's principle of least effort**: While the connection is somewhat superficial, it provides a theoretical foundation for the observations about time expressions. The paper correctly notes that time expressions follow linguistic patterns that reduce communication effort, which is an interesting perspective.

2. **Practical lightweight system**: SynTime is indeed extremely lightweight and runs in real time, which is a valuable practical contribution for applications requiring fast time expression recognition.

3. **Clear experimental evaluation**: The paper conducts thorough experiments on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) against three state-of-the-art baselines, providing good evidence for the effectiveness of SynTime on two of the three datasets.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of true novelty**: The core idea of using syntactic information (POS tags) for time expression recognition is not novel. HeidelTime (Strötgen and Gertz, 2010) already uses POS tags to guide its rules. The paper's distinction between "syntactic types" versus "semantic types" is not meaningful - most rule-based approaches use some form of syntactic information, and the authors fail to demonstrate how their syntactic types are fundamentally different from standard POS-based approaches.

2. **Incremental contribution**: The paper presents a rule-based approach that is an incremental improvement over existing work rather than a truly novel direction. The improvements over baselines are modest (e.g., 11.37% improvement in strict match on Tweets), and the paper doesn't provide strong evidence that the syntactic type system is the main reason for the improvements. The approach could be seen as a refinement of existing rule-based methods rather than a new paradigm.

3. **Superficial theoretical connection**: The paper mentions Zipf's principle of least effort but doesn't deeply explore or leverage this connection to develop new insights or approaches. The observations about time expressions could have been made without this theoretical framework, and the paper doesn't show how this theoretical lens leads to genuinely new methods or understandings.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the fundamental difference between your "syntactic types" and the POS tags used in existing rule-based approaches like HeidelTime? How is your approach different from simply using POS tags to guide rules?

2. The paper states that "other rule-based taggers design rules in a fixed way, including fixed length and fixed position. In contrast, SynTime designs rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion." Can you provide concrete examples of how your heuristic rules differ from fixed-length rules, and why this boundary expansion approach is fundamentally better?

3. Given that your observations about time expressions (shortness, small vocabulary, etc.) could have been made without the syntactic type framework, how do you see this work leading to a fundamentally new direction in time expression recognition rather than just being an incremental improvement?

## Decision
Reject

## Justification
As an ACL reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be an incremental improvement rather than a truly novel contribution. While the paper makes some interesting observations about time expressions, these observations do not lead to a fundamentally new approach or direction in the field. The core idea of using syntactic information for time expression recognition is not novel, as existing rule-based approaches already use POS tags to guide their rules. The distinction between "syntactic types" and "semantic types" is not meaningful or well-justified, and the paper fails to demonstrate how this approach is fundamentally better than existing methods.

The paper's connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is superficial and doesn't lead to new insights or methods. The experimental results show modest improvements over baselines, but these improvements could be achieved through other means (e.g., adding more rules or examples) rather than through the syntactic type system.

Given my review criteria that prioritize novel ideas over methodological perfection, and the fact that this paper offers only incremental improvements rather than a significant new concept or direction, I recommend rejection. The paper would be more appropriate for a workshop or a conference with a lower novelty bar, but it does not meet the standards for ACL for a truly novel contribution.
