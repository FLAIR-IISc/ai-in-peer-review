- Paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets and finds most are short, use a small vocabulary, contain time tokens, and show similar syntactic behavior.  
- Authors propose SynTime, a rule-based recognizer using syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic boundary expansion.  
- SynTime is evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, claiming superior performance, notably high recall on Tweets.  

- Contribution: thorough empirical analysis of time expression properties across domains with supporting statistics.  
- Contribution: SynTime’s syntactic-type rule-based system as a practical refinement of existing rule-based methods.  
- Contribution: Demonstrated strong performance on the challenging Tweets domain.  

- Strength: Strong, data-driven empirical analysis that supports key observations about time expressions.  
- Strength: Very high Tweets performance (95.87% relaxed F1; 98.31% relaxed recall) despite informal text challenges.  
- Strength: System simplicity and efficiency make SynTime practical and easy to implement.  

- Weakness: Limited novelty—similar type- and rule-based ideas exist in SUTime and HeidelTime, and the syntactic vs. semantic distinction is not well-justified.  
- Weakness: Comparisons use outdated baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) and omit recent neural/learning-based methods.  
- Weakness: Questionable methodology for Tweets dataset construction—reliance on SUTime to preselect tweets and lack of error analysis for SUTime misses.  
- Weakness: Overstated claim about demonstrating the "principle of least effort" without ruling out alternative explanations.  
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion/analysis of limitations—no study of how tokenization/POS errors affect SynTime, especially for noisy text.  

- Q1: How does your syntactic type system concretely differ from type systems in SUTime/HeidelTime?  
- Q2: Clarify the apparent contradiction about POS usefulness—if POS can't distinguish time expressions, why is it used for filtering ambiguous tokens?  
- Q3: Reconcile the claim that tweets use formal words with the existence of informal variants like "2day"; is the low informal rate enough to explain high tweet performance?  
- Q4: Why is it acceptable to use SUTime-labeled tweets for dataset creation but not to use auto-labeled Gigaword data?  
- Q5: Provide examples of the 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers added in SynTime-E and explain whether gains are domain-specific or rule comprehensiveness.  

- Overall recommendation: Weak accept conditional on addressing novelty claims, updating comparisons with recent methods, and clarifying methodological issues.
