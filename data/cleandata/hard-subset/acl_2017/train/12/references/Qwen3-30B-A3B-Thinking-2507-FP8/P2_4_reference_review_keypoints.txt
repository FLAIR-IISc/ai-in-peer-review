- Observation: Time expressions are very short (80% ≤ 3 tokens).
- Observation: A small vocabulary of time-related keywords suffices for most expressions.
- Observation: At least 93% of time expressions contain explicit time tokens.
- Observation: Words in time expressions show consistent syntactic behaviors.
- Contribution: Proposes SynTime, a rule-based recognizer using syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics.
- Contribution: Provides cross-dataset empirical analysis supporting the principle of least effort in time expressions.
- Contribution: Presents an extremely lightweight system that outperforms baselines on two datasets.
- Strength: Empirical observations are strongly supported by multi-dataset statistics.
- Strength: Clear novelty claim in using syntactic types rather than semantic types for rules.
- Strength: Strong experimental results with high recall (≥92% relaxed match) and particularly good performance on Tweets.
- Weakness: Novelty not fully justified—insufficient differentiation from prior rule-based/POS-based approaches.
- Weakness: Limited evaluation—excludes Gigaword/standard TempEval benchmarks, reducing generalizability.
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion/quantification of impacts from tokenization and POS-tagging errors.
- Weakness: Lacks comparison with recent learning-based (neural/transformer) methods.
- Question: How exactly do syntactic types differ from standard POS tags and why is that distinction important?
- Question: How would including Gigaword affect performance and conclusions?
- Question: What are concrete runtime measurements (e.g., time to process an average document) compared to baselines?
- Overall assessment: Well-motivated, empirically grounded, technically sound paper with strong results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address novelty and evaluation gaps.
