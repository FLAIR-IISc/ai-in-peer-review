

# ACL Review: Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes "SynTime," a rule-based time expression recognition system that uses syntactic types rather than semantic types to identify time expressions. The authors claim four key observations about time expressions (short length, small vocabulary, high occurrence of time tokens, similar syntactic behavior) and present a type-based approach that defines syntactic types for tokens and uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion.

The main contributions as I see them (ordered by strength):
1. The analysis of time expressions across four datasets, revealing four consistent observations about their linguistic properties
2. The introduction of a syntactic type system for time expressions (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) that differs from existing semantic-based approaches
3. The experimental demonstration that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, particularly on the Tweets dataset

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to time expression recognition**: The paper's key contribution is the syntactic type system for time expressions, which represents a departure from the semantic-based type systems used in other rule-based approaches. The authors provide a clear explanation of how this differs from existing methods (e.g., SUTime's semantic modifiers vs. SynTime's syntactic modifiers).

2. **Strong empirical results**: SynTime achieves the best recall on all three datasets, with exceptional performance on the Tweets dataset (95.87% relaxed match recall). The high recall aligns with the authors' key observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the approach.

3. **Clear explanation of the principle of least effort**: The paper effectively connects their observations about time expressions to Zipf's principle of least effort, providing a compelling linguistic justification for their approach. This connection adds theoretical depth to the work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Multiple grammatical errors and inconsistent terminology**: The paper contains numerous grammatical errors that undermine its professionalism. The most egregious example appears in the introduction: "Time expression recognition main involves two kinds of methods" (should be "Time expression recognition **mainly** involves two kinds of methods"). Additionally, "rulebased" is consistently misspelled as one word instead of "rule-based" (e.g., "other rulebased taggers" appears multiple times). These are basic errors that should have been caught during proofreading.

2. **Inconsistent citation format throughout the paper**: The paper uses inconsistent citation formats that would be unacceptable in an ACL publication. Examples include:
   - "Parker et al.,? 2011" (should be "Parker et al., 2011")
   - "Pustejovsky et al.,? 2003b" (should be "Pustejovsky et al., 2003b")
   - "\Q2011\E" in citations (should be "2011")
   These inconsistencies suggest a lack of attention to detail that is critical for academic publishing.

3. **Confusing description of SynTime's LINKAGE modifier**: The paper claims "LINKAGE (4) link two time tokens" but then states "TimeML (Pustejovsky et al., 2003a) and TimeBank (Pustejovsky et al., 2003b) do not treat most prepositions like 'on' as a part of time expressions. Thus SynTime does not collect those prepositions." This is inconsistent with their use of "to" as a LINKAGE type (as in "8 to 20 days"), which is a preposition. If TimeML doesn't treat prepositions as part of time expressions, why does SynTime include "to" as a LINKAGE type? This contradiction undermines the paper's methodological credibility.

4. **Inconsistent description of the DECADE type**: In the SynTime construction section, the authors state "DECADE (-)" with "Number in '()' indicates the number of distinct tokens in this type, without counting variants." However, the "-" symbol is inconsistent with the numerical values provided for other types (e.g., "SEASON (5)"). This should be either clarified as "N/A" or replaced with a number, but the current presentation is confusing.

5. **Questionable methodology for Twitter dataset collection**: The paper states they "randomly sample 4000 tweets and use SUTime to tag them. 942 tweets of which each contains at least one time expression." Then they say "From the remaining 3,058 tweets, we randomly sample 500 and manually annotate them, and find that only 15 tweets contain time expressions." This suggests time expressions are extremely rare in tweets (only 15 out of 500 manually annotated tweets), yet they claim to have a dataset of 942 tweets with at least one time expression. This implies they may have cherry-picked the 942 tweets, which undermines the representativeness of their Twitter dataset and the generalizability of their claims about Twitter time expressions.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "TimeML (Pustejovsky et al., 2003a) and TimeBank (Pustejovsky et al., 2003b) do not treat most prepositions like 'on' as a part of time expressions." However, SynTime includes "to" as a LINKAGE type (as shown in Figure 3e). How does this align with TimeML's treatment of prepositions? Could you clarify the relationship between your LINKAGE type and TimeML's treatment of prepositions?

2. The DECADE type is listed as "DECADE (-)" with a hyphen, while all other types have numerical values in parentheses. What does the hyphen represent? Is it meant to indicate that the number varies (e.g., different decade expressions), and if so, why isn't this clarified in the text?

3. The paper claims "at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token" (Observation 3), but the lowest percentage in Table 2 is 93.18% for WikiWars. This is very close to the 93% threshold, suggesting the observation is marginal rather than robust. Could you elaborate on why this observation is significant given the marginal difference between 93% and 93.18%?

## Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents a potentially useful approach to time expression recognition with promising empirical results, the numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology, and citation formatting issues demonstrate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for an ACL publication. The minor methodological inconsistencies (particularly regarding the LINKAGE modifier type and TimeML) further undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues collectively indicate a lack of rigor in the paper's presentation that outweighs the paper's substantive contributions. These are the types of errors that should be caught during the proofreading stage before submission, and their presence suggests the authors may not have carefully reviewed their own work.
