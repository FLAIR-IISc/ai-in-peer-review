

# Peer Review: Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a rule-based time expression recognizer called SynTime, which is grounded in an analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The authors make four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) ≥93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they propose SynTime, which defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types, and uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion. The main contributions are: (1) comprehensive analysis of time expression patterns across diverse datasets, (2) the syntactic type-based approach for time expression recognition, and (3) experimental validation showing SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough empirical analysis**: The paper provides a rigorous analysis of time expression patterns across four distinct datasets, demonstrating consistent linguistic patterns (short length, small vocabulary, time token prevalence, similar syntactic behavior) that support the principle of least effort. This analysis is well-supported with statistical evidence (Tables 2-3) and provides a strong foundation for the proposed approach. The observation that ≥93% of time expressions contain time tokens is particularly compelling.

2. **Practical efficiency and performance**: SynTime achieves state-of-the-art results, particularly on the Tweets dataset (91.74% F1, 11.37% improvement over best baseline in strict match). Its lightweight nature (no training required, real-time performance) is a significant practical advantage over machine learning-based approaches, especially for resource-constrained environments. The high recall rates (95.65% on TimeBank, 98.31% on Tweets) directly align with the authors' observation about time token prevalence.

3. **Clear validation of theoretical foundation**: The paper successfully connects linguistic observations to a practical approach, demonstrating that the principle of least effort manifests in time expression patterns. The analysis shows that people tend to use minimal, formal expressions even in informal contexts (tweets), which explains SynTime's strong performance on this dataset. The Twitter word cluster analysis (Section 4.3) provides strong evidence for this claim.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty**: The paper's core contribution (syntactic types vs. semantic types) is not clearly differentiated from existing rule-based approaches. SUTime also uses part-of-speech information (as noted in the paper), and the distinction between "syntactic" and "semantic" types is not convincingly demonstrated. The heuristic rules described (boundary expansion) are similar to other rule-based systems, and the paper doesn't provide a clear example where SynTime handles expressions that other rule-based systems cannot.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to three datasets, with Gigaword excluded because its labels were automatically generated (not ground truth). This omission weakens the evaluation since Gigaword is a standard benchmark in temporal expression research. The paper should have addressed why Gigaword was excluded and provided additional analysis on the potential impact of this exclusion on the results.

3. **Underdeveloped theoretical contribution**: While the paper connects observations to the principle of least effort, this connection is superficial. The paper doesn't provide strong evidence that these linguistic patterns are *caused* by the principle of least effort rather than other factors. The theoretical contribution feels tacked on rather than central to the main technical contribution.

4. **Insufficient comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares with HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, but doesn't address more recent state-of-the-art methods that may have been published after the cited baselines. The paper states that UWTime is "a learning method," but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with more recent neural approaches that might outperform rule-based methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that SynTime defines types based on syntactic behavior while other rule-based taggers use semantic types. Can you provide a concrete example of a time expression that would be handled differently by SynTime versus SUTime, and explain why the syntactic approach is superior in this case?

2. The paper states that "the manual addition of keywords thus will not cost much," but doesn't quantify this cost. How many additional keywords would be needed for a new domain, and how long would it take to add them? Please provide specific numbers based on your experience with the WikiWars and Tweets datasets.

3. The paper mentions that POS tags are used for filtering ambiguous words, but doesn't use POS as a core part of the type system. Given that the paper claims to be inspired by part-of-speech definitions, why aren't POS tags directly incorporated into the syntactic type system?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated, empirically grounded approach to time expression recognition that achieves strong results, particularly on the Tweets dataset. The analysis of time expression patterns is thorough and provides valuable insights for the community. However, the novelty claim is not sufficiently supported, and the evaluation scope is limited. With stronger justification for the novelty and a more comprehensive evaluation, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it makes a practical contribution but doesn't significantly advance the theoretical or methodological understanding of time expression recognition. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty claims and evaluation limitations.
