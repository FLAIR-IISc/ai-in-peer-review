

# Peer Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel time expression recognition system called SynTime, grounded in a comprehensive analysis of time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets). The authors make four key observations: (1) time expressions are typically very short (80% ≤ 3 tokens), (2) time expressions use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) over 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words within time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. They argue these observations support Zipf's principle of least effort.

The main contribution is the SynTime system, which defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types to recognize time expressions. SynTime uses simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion rather than fixed-length rules. The authors claim this approach is more flexible, lightweight, and effective than existing rule-based and machine learning methods.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough dataset analysis with meaningful observations**: The analysis across four distinct datasets (news, Wikipedia, tweets) reveals consistent patterns in time expression structure that are both linguistically interesting and practically useful. The observation that time expressions follow Zipf's principle of least effort is compelling and provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. The data in Tables 2-3 is well-presented and supports their claims.

2. **Strong experimental results on informal text**: SynTime achieves exceptional performance on the Tweets dataset (95.87% relaxed match F1), significantly outperforming all baselines. This is particularly impressive because tweets are known to contain informal language, abbreviations, and non-standard time expressions. The authors correctly attribute this success to their observations about short expressions and small vocabulary in informal communication.

3. **Simplicity and practicality of the approach**: The paper's core innovation—using syntactic types rather than semantic types—leads to a system that is simple, lightweight, and easy to expand. The boundary expansion heuristic is elegantly simple and effectively handles the variability in time expression structures. The fact that SynTime requires minimal expansion (only 3-19 additional tokens across datasets) validates their core observation about the small vocabulary of time expressions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty and insufficient comparison with related work**: The paper claims "the key difference between SynTime and other rule-based taggers lies in the way of defining types and the way of designing rules." However, similar approaches have been explored in the literature. For example, Lee et al. (2014) use Combinatory Categorial Grammar to capture syntactic structure for time expression recognition. The paper fails to adequately distinguish their syntactic type approach from existing syntactic methods, making the novelty claim appear overstated.

2. **Insufficient analysis of error sources and limitations**: The authors acknowledge that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but they provide no analysis of how errors in these preprocessing steps affect their system's performance. Given that POS tagging errors (e.g., "sat" as VBD instead of NNP) are common in informal text like tweets, this is a significant limitation. The paper also doesn't address how SynTime handles ambiguous expressions (e.g., "May" as a verb vs. month) beyond basic POS filtering.

3. **Limited evaluation scope and dataset justification**: The authors exclude Gigaword because "the labels in the dataset were automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." However, they don't provide evidence that the automatic labels are unreliable or explain why they can't use the standard TempEval-3 evaluation on Gigaword. This omission weakens their claim about the robustness of their approach, as they're not evaluating on a standard benchmark.

4. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: While the paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time," it provides no concrete timing benchmarks or comparisons to other systems. Without this, the claim about efficiency remains unsubstantiated, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison between your syntactic type system and the Combinatory Categorial Grammar approach in Lee et al. (2014)? How does your method differ in handling syntactic structure?

2. Given your observation that people use formal words even in informal contexts (e.g., "today" vs. "2day"), why does SynTime-E show only a 0.03% improvement in F1 on Tweets despite adding 3 time tokens? This seems inconsistent with your own data.

3. Could you provide timing benchmarks showing SynTime's runtime compared to the baselines, particularly on large datasets like Gigaword? This would help validate your claim about real-time performance.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting analysis of time expressions and a practical rule-based approach to recognition. The core observations about time expression structure are valuable and well-supported, and the experimental results on tweets are compelling. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper lacks sufficient analysis of limitations and computational efficiency. With revisions addressing these concerns, this work could make a meaningful contribution to time expression recognition research. As it stands, the paper has strong empirical results but needs to better position its contribution within the existing literature and provide more rigorous analysis of its limitations.
