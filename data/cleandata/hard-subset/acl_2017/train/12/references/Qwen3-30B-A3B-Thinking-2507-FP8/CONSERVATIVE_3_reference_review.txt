

# ACL Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary
This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and proposes a rule-based time expression recognition system called SynTime that uses "syntactic types" rather than semantic types to define rules. The authors claim SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with comparable results on WikiWars. The paper argues that their approach is more flexible, lightweight, and runs in real-time.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Analysis of time expression patterns** across four datasets (weakest contribution)
2. **Proposed SynTime system** that uses syntactic types for time expression recognition (moderate contribution, but lacks novelty)
3. **Experimental results** showing performance improvements over baselines (moderate contribution)

## Strengths
1. **Clear analysis of time expression patterns**: The paper provides a useful analysis of time expression characteristics (length, vocabulary size, token composition) across multiple datasets, which is a reasonable contribution to the field.

2. **Demonstrated performance on Tweets dataset**: The results on the Tweets dataset are particularly strong, with SynTime achieving 95.87% relaxed match F1, which is a meaningful improvement over baselines. This suggests the approach may be valuable for social media applications.

3. **Practical implementation**: The system is indeed lightweight and designed to run in real-time, which is a practical advantage for certain applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The paper's main claim of using "syntactic types" rather than "semantic types" is not convincingly justified. SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) already uses POS tags (syntactic information) in its rule-based system. The distinction between "syntactic types" and "semantic types" is vague and not meaningfully different from existing approaches. The authors' claim that other rule-based taggers "design rules for each type based on their meanings" is inaccurate - SUTime uses token patterns and regular expressions with some syntactic constraints, not semantic types.

2. **Inadequate evaluation**: The paper excludes Gigaword dataset because "labels were automatically generated by other taggers which may not be ground truth." However, they don't provide evidence that these labels are unreliable or that the exclusion is justified. The evaluation is limited to three datasets, which is insufficient for a claim of broad effectiveness. The paper also lacks statistical significance tests for the performance improvements claimed.

3. **Overstated theoretical contribution**: The connection to "the principle of least effort" (Zipf, 1949) is tenuous. The observations about short expressions and small vocabulary are somewhat obvious and don't require a new theoretical framework to explain them. The paper doesn't provide strong evidence that these observations are novel or meaningful in the context of linguistic theory.

4. **Lack of concrete evidence for claimed advantages**: The paper claims SynTime is "more flexible" and "extremely light-weight" but provides no performance metrics to back up these claims. There are no speed comparisons with baseline systems, no analysis of how many rules are needed, or how easily the system can be expanded. The claim that the system is "light-weight" is unsubstantiated.

5. **Superficial treatment of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, but doesn't provide any error analysis showing how sensitive the system is to errors in these components. For example, they mention an error where "sat" is tagged as VBD instead of NNP, but don't analyze how often this would impact time expression recognition.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide a more concrete justification for why syntactic types are fundamentally better than semantic types for time expression recognition? What specific limitations of semantic types does your approach overcome that previous rule-based systems couldn't address?

2. Why was Gigaword excluded from the evaluation? The authors claim the labels were automatically generated, but they don't provide evidence that these labels are unreliable. If the labels are automatically generated, how did they verify the quality of these labels for evaluation purposes?

3. The paper claims SynTime is "lightweight" and runs in real-time, but doesn't provide any performance metrics (e.g., tokens per second) to support this. Can you provide speed comparisons with the baseline systems?

4. The authors claim that SynTime's "heuristic rules" are "much more flexible" than other rule-based approaches. Can you provide specific examples of time expressions that existing systems cannot recognize but SynTime can, beyond the "year 1" vs "1 year" example?

5. The paper states that "the size of time tokens is small" (Table 3), but the numbers (64-80 distinct tokens) don't seem particularly small for a time expression recognition system. How do you justify this claim in the context of NLP systems that typically handle thousands of tokens?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a modest contribution to the time expression recognition field, but the claimed novelty is overstated and the evaluation is insufficiently rigorous. The proposed method is not substantially different from existing rule-based approaches, and the theoretical contribution is weak. The results on the Tweets dataset are promising, but the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support the broader claims of superiority over existing methods. As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and established approaches, I find the paper's claims unsubstantiated and would recommend rejection.

The paper would be strengthened by: (1) providing a clearer justification for the novelty of the syntactic type approach, (2) expanding the evaluation to include Gigaword with proper validation of the automatically generated labels, (3) providing concrete performance metrics to support the "lightweight" claim, and (4) conducting a more thorough error analysis to understand the limitations of the approach.
