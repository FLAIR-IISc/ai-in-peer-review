

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary

This paper presents a new rule-based time expression recognition system called SynTime, which uses syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% have ≤3 tokens), (2) the vocabulary used is small, (3) at least 93% contain time tokens, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they propose SynTime, which defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic types used in existing rule-based approaches. The authors claim SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with comparable results on WikiWars.

## Main Contributions

1. **Insightful linguistic analysis of time expressions** grounded in Zipf's principle of least effort
2. **Syntactic type system for time expression recognition** (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) that differs from semantic type systems used in existing approaches
3. **Demonstrated effectiveness of the approach** with strong results, particularly on the Tweets dataset (95.87% F1 in relaxed match)

## Strengths

### 1. Comprehensive Linguistic Analysis
The paper provides a thorough, data-driven analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, demonstrating clear patterns that align with Zipf's principle of least effort. The four observations are well-supported by statistical evidence (Tables 2-3, Figure 1) and provide a compelling theoretical foundation for the proposed method. This analysis is significantly more detailed than typical time expression studies and offers valuable insights into the linguistic properties of time expressions that could inform future work.

### 2. Well-Justified Syntactic Type System
The authors' shift from semantic types to syntactic types is well-motivated by their analysis and the definition of part-of-speech from Manning and Schutze (1999). The distinction between syntactic types (e.g., PREFIX modifiers that appear before time tokens) versus semantic types (e.g., frequency modifiers) is theoretically sound and provides a more linguistically grounded foundation for the rule-based approach. This represents a meaningful contribution to the methodology of time expression recognition.

### 3. Strong Empirical Results
The results on the Tweets dataset are particularly impressive, with SynTime-E achieving 95.88% F1 in relaxed match, outperforming all baselines by over 6 percentage points. The authors provide a convincing explanation for this success that aligns with their analysis (short expressions, small vocabulary, and formal usage even in informal contexts). The high recall rates across all datasets (92%+ in relaxed match) are consistent with their observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing strong evidence for their core insight.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Overstated Novelty of Core Methodology
While the syntactic type system is a meaningful contribution, the actual recognition method (boundary expansion with heuristic rules) is not novel. Many existing rule-based systems use similar approaches. The paper claims SynTime "designs rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion" but fails to provide evidence that this is fundamentally different from existing approaches. For example, SUTime uses a cascade finite state approach with boundary expansion, which is conceptually similar. The paper needs to provide a more precise comparison of the heuristic rules used in SynTime versus existing systems.

### 2. Limited Evaluation Scope
The evaluation is limited to three datasets, with TimeBank being the only standard benchmark. The authors mention they didn't use Gigaword because labels were automatically generated, but they don't justify why they don't use the TempEval-3 or TempEval-4 benchmarks, which are standard for this task. Additionally, they don't compare with the most recent systems from the latest TempEval challenges (e.g., TempEval-4 systems), which would strengthen the evaluation.

### 3. Lack of Runtime Analysis
The paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time," but provides no runtime measurements or comparison to other systems. This is a significant claim that should be supported with empirical evidence. Without this, the claim remains unsubstantiated, and it's unclear how much faster SynTime is than the baselines.

### 4. Misrepresentation of Related Work
The paper misrepresents some related work. For instance, it states that "SUTime defines 5 semantic modifier types," but SUTime actually uses a cascade finite state approach where the types are defined by the regular expressions, not by semantic categories. The paper also claims that "other rule-based taggers design rules in a fixed way," which is inaccurate as SUTime's approach is quite flexible with its cascade of regular expressions.

### 5. Insufficient Analysis of Limitations
The paper mentions limitations briefly in Section 5.3 but lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases. It would be valuable to understand what specific types of time expressions SynTime struggles with, and how these cases relate to the authors' observations. For example, how does SynTime handle ambiguous expressions like "January 1st" (which could be a date or a time) or expressions with complex temporal relations?

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the heuristic rules used in SynTime versus those used in SUTime or HeidelTime? Specifically, what makes your heuristic rules "simple" and "heuristic" compared to existing approaches?

2. The paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time." Could you provide runtime measurements (e.g., time per sentence or per document) and compare them to the baselines?

3. The paper states that "words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors," but doesn't provide evidence that this is a better way to categorize time expressions than semantic categories. Could you provide specific examples where the syntactic type system outperforms semantic types?

4. The authors mention that SynTime does not collect prepositions like 'on' as part of time expressions (Section 4.1), but TimeML and TimeBank standards do include such prepositions. How does this affect the performance on datasets where these prepositions are common?

5. The paper claims "even in tweets people tend to use formal words," but the Twitter word clusters show informal variants like '2day' are used (though less frequently). How does SynTime handle these informal variants, and how does this affect performance?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution through its insightful linguistic analysis of time expressions and the introduction of a syntactically grounded type system. The empirical results, particularly on the Tweets dataset, are strong and well-supported by the analysis. However, the paper overstates the novelty of the core methodology and lacks critical details (runtime measurements, more nuanced comparison with related work) that would strengthen the claims.

I recommend **major revisions** to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly the need to more clearly articulate the novelty of the approach, provide runtime measurements, and offer a more precise comparison with existing work. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field.

## Rating

**Acceptable with major revisions** (This is a strong paper with minor issues that can be addressed with revisions, but the current version has significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before acceptance.)
