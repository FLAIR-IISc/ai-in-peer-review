

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) they use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, (3) at least 93% contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules for boundary expansion.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing patterns consistent with the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949)
2. The proposal of a syntactic type system for time expression recognition that differs from existing semantic-based approaches
3. The experimental demonstration that a lightweight rule-based approach using syntactic types can achieve state-of-the-art results on specific datasets

## Strengths
1. **Thorough empirical analysis**: The paper provides comprehensive analysis of time expressions across four distinct datasets, with concrete statistics (length distributions, vocabulary size, time token coverage) that consistently support the authors' observations. The connection to the principle of least effort is well-motivated and adds theoretical value beyond typical time expression analysis.

2. **Strong experimental results**: SynTime achieves significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets (11.37% improvement in strict match F1 on Tweets), and maintains competitive performance on WikiWars. The high recall rates (92%+ in relaxed match) align perfectly with the authors' observation that at least 93% of time expressions contain time tokens, providing strong validation for their approach.

3. **Well-motivated approach**: The syntactic type system is clearly defined and directly derived from the observed patterns in time expressions. The boundary expansion heuristic rules are simple yet effective, making the system lightweight and efficient without requiring training data. The paper demonstrates how the analysis directly informs the system design.

## Weaknesses
1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims SynTime is novel for using syntactic types rather than semantic types, but this distinction is not sufficiently justified as a major innovation. The approach still relies on manually crafted rules and regular expressions, which is standard practice in rule-based time expression recognition (e.g., SUTime, HeidelTime). The difference between "syntactic" and "semantic" types is not clearly articulated as a significant theoretical advancement.

2. **Limited scope of analysis**: The paper acknowledges that SynTime struggles with time expressions containing descriptive phrases or prepositions (e.g., in WikiWars), but doesn't analyze how common these cases are or provide concrete examples. The claim that "time expressions in general are very short" is contradicted by the WikiWars dataset, which contains many longer expressions with descriptive elements, limiting the generalizability of the observations.

3. **Unsubstantiated claims about efficiency**: The paper claims SynTime "runs in real time" but provides no runtime measurements or comparisons with other systems. Without concrete performance metrics, this claim lacks substance and makes it difficult to evaluate the practical advantages of the approach.

4. **Incomplete evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to three datasets, with Gigaword excluded because its labels were automatically generated. The paper doesn't justify why these three datasets are sufficient to demonstrate the approach's generalizability, nor does it compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond the three baselines selected.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that "at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token" (Table 2), but doesn't provide examples of the time expressions that don't contain time tokens. Could you provide specific examples of these cases and explain how they might be handled in future work?

2. The paper acknowledges that SynTime struggles with time expressions containing descriptive phrases (e.g., in WikiWars), but doesn't quantify how common these cases are. Could you provide the percentage of time expressions in WikiWars that contain descriptive phrases and how they affect SynTime's performance?

3. The paper claims SynTime "runs in real time" but provides no runtime measurements. Could you provide a comparison of SynTime's processing speed against the baselines on a standard dataset?
