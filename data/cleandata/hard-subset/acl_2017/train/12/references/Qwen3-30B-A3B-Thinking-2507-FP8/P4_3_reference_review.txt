

# Review of "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and makes four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), (2) time-related vocabulary is small, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain a time token, and (4) words in time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. Empirical analysis demonstrating that time expressions across different text types follow the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949), with evidence in terms of short expressions, small vocabulary, high occurrence of time tokens, and similar syntactic behaviors.

2. A novel rule-based time expression recognition approach (SynTime) that defines syntactic types for tokens rather than semantic types, and employs simple heuristic rules based on boundary expansion.

3. Comprehensive evaluation showing SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with particularly strong results on the Tweets dataset (6.33% improvement in relaxed match F1 over the best baseline).

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis with well-supported observations**: The paper makes a compelling case for the principle of least effort in time expression usage through detailed analysis across four diverse datasets. The four observations are well-justified with clear statistical evidence (Tables 2 and 3, Figure 1), and the connection to Zipf's principle is well-explained. This analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.

2. **Technical soundness of SynTime design**: The syntactic type system (15 time token types, 5 modifier types, 1 numeral type) is well-defined and clearly explained. The heuristic boundary expansion approach is simple yet effective, and the paper demonstrates how this design leads to a lightweight system that runs in real time. The evaluation on three datasets with multiple metrics (precision, recall, F1 for strict and relaxed match) provides convincing evidence of the system's effectiveness.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness on challenging datasets**: The paper shows particularly strong results on the Tweets dataset (95.87% relaxed match F1), which is a challenging domain due to informal language use. The authors provide a well-supported explanation for this success (short expressions, small vocabulary, use of formal words even in informal contexts), which aligns with their initial observations. This demonstrates the practical value of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach**: While the paper emphasizes the syntactic type system as novel, the distinction between syntactic and semantic types is not clearly articulated as a fundamental contribution. The heuristic boundary expansion rules described are similar to those used in existing rule-based systems like SUTime, which also expands boundaries. The paper doesn't clearly explain how the syntactic type approach fundamentally differs from or improves upon existing rule-based methods in a way that would be considered a significant novelty.

2. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims SynTime "significantly outperforms" the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, but does not provide statistical tests to support these claims. Without significance testing, it's difficult to determine whether the reported performance differences are meaningful or could be due to random variation in the datasets.

3. **Incomplete evaluation of limitations**: While the paper mentions that SynTime assumes correct tokenization and POS tagging, it doesn't provide any analysis of how errors in these components affect performance. The statement "the incorrect tokens and POS tags affect the result" is vague and doesn't quantify the impact or suggest potential mitigation strategies, which is important for a rule-based system that relies on these components.

4. **Inconsistent dataset usage**: The paper states that "Gigaword dataset was not used in our experiments because the labels in the dataset was automatically generated by other taggers which may not be the ground truth labels." However, Gigaword is a standard benchmark dataset in the TempEval series, and excluding it without clear justification weakens the evaluation. The paper should either include it with appropriate caveats or explain why it's not suitable for their evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims SynTime defines "syntactic types" rather than "semantic types," but the distinction between these types isn't clearly explained in terms of how it fundamentally changes the recognition process. Could the authors clarify how the syntactic type approach differs from semantic type approaches in a way that would be considered a meaningful contribution?

2. The paper states that SynTime "runs in real time," but doesn't provide specific runtime performance metrics (e.g., tokens per second or processing time per document) compared to the baselines. Could the authors provide these metrics to better demonstrate the system's efficiency?

3. The paper mentions that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words" (Section 4.3), yet tweets are known for informal language. How does the system handle cases where the same time expression appears in multiple informal variants (e.g., "today" vs. "tday"), and how does this affect the need for manual keyword expansion?
