

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that uses syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and make four observations about time expressions: they are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, most contain at least one time token (≥93%), and words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they propose a type-based approach that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses heuristic rules for boundary expansion.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper's main contribution is the syntactic type system for time expressions and the heuristic rules for boundary expansion, which forms the basis of SynTime.
2. The empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets provides evidence for the principle of least effort in language usage.
3. The experimental results demonstrate that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach to Time Expression Recognition**: The paper presents a clear distinction between syntactic and semantic type systems for time expression recognition. While other rule-based taggers (like SUTime) define types based on semantic meaning, SynTime defines types based on syntactic behavior, which is an interesting and potentially useful perspective.

2. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis**: The analysis across four different datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) provides valuable insights into time expression patterns. The observations about time expression shortness, small vocabulary, high percentage containing time tokens, and similar syntactic behaviors are well-supported by the data and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results show that SynTime achieves better performance than state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with particularly strong recall scores (above 92% on relaxed match). The paper's claim that SynTime outperforms existing methods is well-supported by the data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Typographical and Formatting Errors**: The paper contains numerous typographical errors and inconsistent formatting that undermine its credibility. For example:
   - "Time expression recognition main involves" (should be "mainly involves")
   - "rulebased" instead of "rule-based"
   - "the labels in the dataset was" (should be "were")
   - "the size of time tokens is small" (Table 3 shows 64-80 distinct time tokens, which isn't "very small" by any reasonable standard)
   - Inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Pustejovsky et al.,? 2003b" with a question mark)
   - Random "1" in the sentence: "SUTime defines 5 semantic modifier types, such as frequency modifiers; 1 while SynTime defines 5 syntactic modifier types"
   - "the most often used words are the formal words, and their frequencies are much greater than informal words'." (grammatical error)

2. **Lack of Technical Depth in Key Concepts**: The paper claims that "SynTime designs rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion" but fails to clearly explain what "boundary expansion" means or provide examples of these heuristic rules. This is a critical omission, as the core methodological innovation is not properly explained.

3. **Misleading Claims and Lack of Supporting Evidence**: 
   - The paper claims "the vocabulary used to express time information is very small" but Table 3 shows 64-80 distinct time tokens across datasets, which is not "very small" (for comparison, common English vocabulary is tens of thousands of words).
   - The paper claims "words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence beyond stating that certain POS tags are common in time expressions.
   - The paper claims SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "runs in real time" but provides no runtime analysis or computational complexity metrics to support these claims.

4. **Inconsistent Presentation of Results**: The paper claims "SynTime achieves the best recalls on all three datasets" but the results show that SynTime-E (not SynTime-I) achieves the best recall on WikiWars. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish between the two versions of SynTime in its main claims, creating confusion.

5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports performance improvements (e.g., "11.37% improvement in strict match") but doesn't provide statistical significance testing to confirm that these differences are not due to random chance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the size of time tokens is small" (Table 3 shows 64-80 distinct time tokens), but this is not "small" by any reasonable standard. Could you clarify what you mean by "small" and provide a comparison to the overall vocabulary size?

2. The paper claims "SynTime designs rules in a heuristic method based on the idea of boundary expansion" but doesn't define what "boundary expansion" means or provide examples of these rules. Could you clarify this key methodological concept?

3. The paper claims that "SynTime is much more flexible, expansible, and extremely light-weight" but provides no evidence for these claims beyond stating that it's rule-based. Could you provide runtime analysis or computational complexity metrics to support these claims?

4. The paper states "the vocabulary used to express time information is very small" but Table 3 shows 64-80 distinct time tokens. Could you explain why you consider this "very small" given that it's comparable to the size of many specialized vocabulary lists?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more rigorous presentation of its methodological innovations. The distinction between syntactic and semantic types is interesting but not clearly explained. The authors should provide concrete examples of how the syntactic type system works in practice, including specific rules and how they differ from semantic-based approaches.

The paper's claims about time expression patterns are interesting but could be strengthened with more comparative analysis (e.g., how these patterns differ from non-time expressions in the same corpora).

The experimental section would be more convincing with statistical significance testing for the reported performance differences.

## Overall Recommendation

Reject

The paper contains too many minor but critical errors and omissions that undermine the paper's credibility and quality. While the core idea of using syntactic types for time expression recognition has potential, the paper fails to properly explain the method, support its claims with evidence, and present its results clearly. The numerous typographical errors, inconsistent formatting, and technical omissions make it difficult to evaluate the work properly. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance given these substantial flaws.
