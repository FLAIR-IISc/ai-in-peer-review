

# ACL Review: "Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes "SynTime," a rule-based time expression recognition system that uses syntactic types rather than semantic types. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), making four observations about their properties (short length, small vocabulary, high occurrence of time tokens, similar syntactic behavior). Based on these observations, they define a syntactic type system for time expressions and design simple heuristic rules for recognition. The system is evaluated against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, showing improvements over baselines, especially on TimeBank and Tweets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets showing they are short, use a small vocabulary, contain time tokens, and have similar syntactic behaviors.
2. The proposed SynTime system that uses syntactic types rather than semantic types for time expression recognition.
3. The empirical evaluation showing SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on TimeBank and Tweets datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Thorough dataset analysis**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of time expressions across four diverse datasets, supporting their four key observations with concrete data (Tables 2 and 3, Figure 1). The analysis is well-justified and provides valuable insights into the nature of time expressions.

2. **Clear distinction between syntactic and semantic approaches**: The paper correctly identifies that most existing rule-based systems (like SUTime) use semantic types, while SynTime uses syntactic types based on POS behavior. This distinction is well-explained and provides a meaningful contribution to the field.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation shows consistent improvements over baselines, especially on the Tweets dataset (91.74% F1 in strict match), which is impressive. The results are presented clearly in Table 4.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Inconsistent naming and terminology (major flaw)
The paper uses inconsistent naming conventions throughout, which is a fundamental flaw in academic writing. Specifically:
- "SynTime" is sometimes capitalized as "SynTime" and sometimes as "synTime" (e.g., "SynTime is a rule-based tagger" vs. "synTime is initialized with regular expressions").
- "Time token" is inconsistently used as both singular and plural (e.g., "time tokens are the words" vs. "the time tokens usually have NN*").
- The paper uses "time token" and "time token" interchangeably without consistency.

This inconsistency is pervasive throughout the paper and indicates a lack of attention to detail. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot overlook this basic formatting flaw, which undermines the paper's credibility.

### 2. Ambiguous description of the key innovation (boundary expansion heuristic)
The paper claims that the "key difference" between SynTime and other rule-based taggers is the use of "simple heuristic rules based on the idea of boundary expansion," but fails to clearly explain what this means or how it differs from existing approaches. For example:

- In Section 4.2.2: "For the left searching, if encounter a PREFIX or NUMERAL or IN ARTICLE, then continue searching." (Grammatically incorrect - should be "if it encounters")
- The paper states: "The left searching will not exceed the previous time token; the right searching will not exceed the next time token." This is confusing - if we're scanning left from a time token, the "previous time token" would be the time token before the current one, but the search should stop at the previous word, not the previous time token.

This ambiguity makes it difficult to understand the core innovation of the paper, which is a significant flaw for a methodological contribution.

### 3. Technical flaws in the time segment identification rules
The paper describes rules for time segment identification that contain technical flaws that could affect the implementation:

- In Section 4.2.3: "If the word at the shared boundary is neither a COMMA nor a LINKAGE, then merge s1 and s2." But what if the shared boundary is a NUMERAL? The rule doesn't address this case, which could lead to incorrect merging of time segments.

- In Section 4.2.2: "For the left searching, if encounter a PREFIX or NUMERAL or IN ARTICLE, then continue searching." This is ambiguous - should it be "if it encounters" (grammatically correct), and should it be "continue searching" or "stop searching"? The description lacks clarity.

These technical inconsistencies undermine the paper's credibility as a methodological contribution.

### 4. Missing details about the expansion process
The paper claims that "SynTime can be easily expanded by adding time-related token regular expressions from training text," but provides insufficient details about this process:

- How many people annotated the words to be added? What was the inter-annotator agreement?
- How were the ambiguous words filtered out (e.g., "May" as a modal verb vs. month)?
- The paper mentions Table 5 showing the number of tokens added for each dataset, but doesn't explain how these numbers were derived.

Without these details, the claim that SynTime is "extremely light-weight" and "easily expanded" lacks support.

### 5. Inconsistent dataset usage
The paper states in Section 3.1 that they analyze time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), but in Section 5.1, they say they "use two benchmark datasets, TimeBank and WikiWars, and one manually labeled Tweets dataset." The paper should clarify that Gigaword was used in the analysis but not in the experiments, as it's mentioned in the analysis but not in the evaluation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the exact meaning of "boundary expansion" and how it differs from the fixed-length rule design used in other rule-based systems.
2. Please explain the grammatical error in "if encounter a PREFIX" and provide a corrected version.
3. Please explain how the ambiguous case of "the word at the shared boundary is a NUMERAL" is handled in the merging rules.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents some interesting observations and shows reasonable empirical results, the numerous minor flaws in terminology, inconsistent formatting, ambiguous method description, and technical inconsistencies are too significant for a paper to be accepted at ACL. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues indicate a lack of attention to detail that would make the paper difficult to reproduce and understand, which is unacceptable for a top-tier conference. The paper's core contribution is not sufficiently clarified due to these issues.
