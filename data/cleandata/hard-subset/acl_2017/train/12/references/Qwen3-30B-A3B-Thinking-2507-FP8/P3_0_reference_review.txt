

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that leverages syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identify four key observations: (1) time expressions are very short (80% have ≤3 tokens), (2) a small vocabulary is used for time expressions, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain a time token, and (4) words in time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they define a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, and numerals) and design a lightweight rule-based approach that identifies time tokens first, then expands to include modifiers and numerals. The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three datasets, reporting that SynTime outperforms the baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, with comparable results on WikiWars.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Insightful empirical analysis of time expressions**: The thorough analysis across multiple datasets revealing consistent patterns related to Zipf's principle of least effort provides valuable linguistic insights that could inform future work in temporal information extraction.
2. **Syntactic type system for time expressions**: The proposed type system inspired by part-of-speech tagging, while not entirely novel, provides a principled way to organize time expression rules that differs from existing semantic-based approaches.
3. **Lightweight and effective rule-based system**: SynTime demonstrates that a simple, rule-based approach can achieve competitive results with minimal computational cost, making it suitable for real-time applications.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical analysis with theoretical grounding**: The paper's analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets is thorough and well-supported by data. The four observations are compelling and provide a solid foundation for the proposed system. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is particularly insightful and adds theoretical depth to the work. The analysis of tweets is especially valuable as it addresses a challenging domain for time expression recognition.

2. **Practical and lightweight system**: SynTime's simplicity and efficiency are major practical advantages over more complex machine learning approaches. The paper convincingly demonstrates that a rule-based system can achieve state-of-the-art results while running in real-time, which is a significant advantage for many applications. The authors provide clear evidence of SynTime's efficiency through its high recall rates (over 95% on Tweets in relaxed match).

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation on three datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, Tweets) provides a good comparison against multiple baselines. The results showing SynTime's superiority on TimeBank and Tweets (with F1 scores of 91.74% and 95.87% in strict and relaxed match, respectively) are impressive for a rule-based system. The analysis of SynTime-E's performance improvement on WikiWars with minimal expansion (only 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers added) demonstrates the system's flexibility.

4. **Insight into informal language**: The analysis of time expressions in tweets is particularly valuable. The finding that even in informal communication (tweets), people tend to use formal time expressions (with only about 3% informal variants) explains SynTime's strong performance on this challenging dataset. This insight is crucial for NLP practitioners working with social media data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty in the core methodology**: The paper's main contribution is a rule-based system, but the key innovation claimed (using "syntactic types" instead of "semantic types") is not sufficiently differentiated from existing work. SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) also uses POS tags (a syntactic feature) to guide its rules. The paper doesn't adequately explain how the authors' syntactic types differ from the syntactic information already used in other rule-based systems. The heuristic boundary expansion approach is also not clearly novel compared to other rule-based methods.

2. **Insufficient justification for the "least effort" connection**: While the authors claim their observations support Zipf's principle of least effort, the connection is not rigorously established. The paper doesn't provide evidence that these patterns are the result of minimizing cognitive effort rather than other factors (e.g., grammatical constraints or historical language evolution). The connection appears to be more of a post-hoc interpretation than a well-supported theoretical claim.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper lacks a detailed analysis of where SynTime fails, which would be valuable for understanding the system's limitations. For example, it would be interesting to know if SynTime struggles with complex time expressions (e.g., "the day before yesterday" or "a year and a half ago") or specific domains (e.g., medical texts). Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess the full scope of SynTime's applicability.

4. **Inadequate comparison with related work**: The paper claims that SynTime differs from other rule-based taggers in the way types are defined, but it doesn't provide a clear comparison of how SynTime's type system differs from existing approaches. For example, it doesn't address how the syntactic types relate to the semantic modifiers used in SUTime (which the authors acknowledge). The paper also doesn't discuss how SynTime compares to GUTime or other rule-based systems that might use similar syntactic information.

5. **Questionable generalizability claims**: The authors claim that their approach "could be applied to other languages and other parts of language," but they provide no evidence or analysis to support this. The paper is focused solely on English time expressions, and there's no discussion of how the syntactic type system would need to be adapted for other languages or other linguistic phenomena.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that SynTime uses "syntactic types" while other rule-based systems use "semantic types." However, SUTime (Chang and Manning, 2012) also uses POS tags (a syntactic feature) to guide its rules. Can you clarify how your syntactic types differ from the syntactic features already used in SUTime and other rule-based systems?

2. The connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is intriguing but not well-supported. Can you provide more evidence that the observed patterns in time expressions are indeed the result of minimizing cognitive effort, rather than other factors (e.g., grammatical constraints or historical language evolution)?

3. The paper states that "Time expressions in WikiWars include lots of prepositions and quite a few descriptive time expressions. SynTime could not fully recognize such kinds of time expressions because it follows TimeML and TimeBank." Can you provide specific examples of these time expressions that SynTime fails to recognize, and explain why these examples are problematic for your approach?

4. The paper mentions that "even in tweets people tend to use formal words," citing a Twitter word cluster analysis showing "today" is used 1,220,829 times while "2day" is used only 34,827 times. However, this is just one example. Can you provide a more comprehensive analysis of informal time expressions in tweets to support your claim that the vocabulary size remains small?

5. The paper claims that SynTime "runs in real time" but doesn't provide any timing measurements. Can you provide actual runtime measurements to support this claim, including comparisons to the baseline systems?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a well-analyzed and practical approach to time expression recognition that demonstrates strong performance with a lightweight system. The empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets is valuable and provides useful insights for the field. However, the core methodology lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work, and the connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is not rigorously established.

I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the novelty of the approach and the justification for the theoretical connection to Zipf's principle. The paper has significant practical value and provides useful insights, but it needs to more clearly differentiate itself from existing work and provide stronger theoretical grounding for the claimed connection to linguistic principles.
