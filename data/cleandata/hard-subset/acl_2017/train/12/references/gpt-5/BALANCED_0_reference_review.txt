Summary and contributions
This paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition. Motivated by a descriptive analysis across four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), the authors observe that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, typically include at least one “time token,” and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime defines a syntactic type system (15 types of time tokens, 5 types of modifiers, and numerals) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics around identified time tokens to extract time expressions. Initialized using SUTime’s token regexes and optionally expanded with a small number of manually added entries, SynTime is evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset. SynTime attains high recall and competitive or superior F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, and comparable performance on WikiWars.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, interpretable rule-based recognizer that uses syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics to achieve strong recall and competitive F1 across domains.
2) A cross-corpus descriptive analysis of time expressions that informs the design and helps explain why a token-first, expansion-based approach can work well.
3) An empirical evaluation (including an expanded variant) showing that a very small set of added keywords suffices to adapt across domains, with particularly strong performance on tweets.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and especially high recall: SynTime(-I/-E) achieves best or second-best results in most metrics on TimeBank and Tweets; on Tweets, strict F1 ≈ 91.7% and relaxed F1 ≈ 95.9%, outperforming baselines by notable margins. The consistent high recall supports the central design choice to identify time tokens first and expand.
- Simplicity and interpretability: The system is compact, built from token regexes, a small syntactic type inventory, and a handful of heuristic merging rules. This is easy to implement, maintain, and extend, and offers predictable behavior compared to complex pipelines.
- Minimal domain adaptation cost: The expansion from SynTime-I to SynTime-E involves very few additions (e.g., only 3 time tokens and 2 modifiers for Tweets), yet yields tangible recall improvements where needed (notably in WikiWars). This suggests portability and practicality.
- Clear procedural description with examples: The paper describes identification, segment building, and merging with concrete boundary cases (e.g., adjacent/overlapping segments, commas, and linkages), making the approach straightforward to reproduce.
- Useful descriptive analysis: The cross-dataset observations (length distribution, small vocabulary, presence of time tokens, POS distributions) are informative and align with practitioners’ experience. They help justify design choices and explain why a token-first strategy yields high recall.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing rule-based systems: The system is initialized from SUTime token regexes and uses boundary expansion reminiscent of existing cascaded approaches. The shift from semantic to syntactic modifier types is interesting but not clearly demonstrated to be key; no ablations isolate the contribution of syntactic typing versus other design choices. Overall, the methodological advance feels incremental.
- Potential evaluation and fairness concerns: 
  • The Tweets dataset was constructed by first filtering 4k tweets with SUTime, then manually annotating the 942 tweets SUTime identified as containing time expressions. This introduces selection bias toward the kinds of expressions SUTime detects and may under-represent hard/atypical cases. The 3% miss-rate estimate from a small sample (500) does not fully mitigate this bias. 
  • SynTime is initialized with SUTime resources, raising questions about fairness when comparing to SUTime without similar adaptation. 
  • For TimeBank and WikiWars, train/test splits and alignment with prior evaluation setups are not fully specified, and some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior work while others are re-run, which risks mismatched conditions.
- Underdeveloped analysis and validation: 
  • Claims of “significant” outperformance lack statistical significance tests. 
  • No runtime or memory measurements are provided despite “real-time” claims. 
  • Error analysis is minimal; we see only a POS tagging anecdote. It would be valuable to quantify main error types (e.g., missed ranges/linkages, over-merging, ambiguous tokens like May, prepositions).
  • No ablations (e.g., without POS disambiguation, without heuristic merging, without syntactic-type distinctions) to validate the design choices.
- Annotation guideline mismatch and scope: The paper follows TimeML/TimeBank extents (excluding many prepositions), which partly explains lower performance on WikiWars where prepositions are common. This mismatch complicates cross-corpus comparison and may underestimate the difficulty of general time expression extraction in settings that include prepositions/descriptive expressions.
- Overstated theoretical framing: Linking the observations to Zipf’s principle of least effort is speculative in this context and not rigorously tested. The observations are descriptive and useful but not novel enough to support this theoretical claim without deeper analysis or cross-lingual validation.

Questions to authors
1) Tweet dataset construction: How do you address selection bias from prefiltering with SUTime? Can you provide results on a randomly sampled, fully annotated tweet set independent of any system’s outputs?
2) Resource sharing with SUTime: Precisely which SUTime resources were used (which token regex lists, any rule fragments)? Were any edits made? How do you ensure fairness in comparing SynTime to SUTime given shared resources?
3) Experimental setup details: 
   - What splits and versions were used for TimeBank and WikiWars? 
   - How was UWTime trained for each dataset (data, hyperparameters, train/dev/test splits)? 
   - Are all systems evaluated with the same guidelines and evaluation scripts?
4) Ablation and analysis: Please report performance when (a) disabling POS-based disambiguation, (b) using token identification only (no boundary expansion), and (c) replacing syntactic modifier types with semantic categories, to isolate contributions. An error breakdown by phenomenon would help.
5) Significance and efficiency: Provide statistical significance tests for major comparisons and report throughput/latency and memory for all systems under a common setup.
6) Guidelines consistency: For datasets with different inclusion of prepositions, can you provide variants of SynTime that adjust extents accordingly and re-run evaluations to quantify the impact?
7) Reproducibility: Will you release code, resources, and the fully annotated tweet dataset (constructed without prefilter bias), along with instructions to reproduce numbers?
