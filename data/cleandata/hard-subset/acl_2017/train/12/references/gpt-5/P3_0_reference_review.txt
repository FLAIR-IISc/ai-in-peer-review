Summary and claimed contributions
The paper introduces SynTime, a lightweight rule-based recognizer for temporal expressions that (i) defines syntactic “types” for time tokens, modifiers, and numerals, and (ii) applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics to merge adjacent/overlapping segments into full timex spans. The authors motivate the design by an analysis over four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and a Tweets dataset), arguing that timexes are short, use a small vocabulary, usually contain a “time token,” and show consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes, filters candidates with POS tags, and optionally expands its lexicon with a few manually added forms. Evaluations on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, reporting strong recall and competitive F1, especially on Tweets.

My view of the main contributions (in order of strength):
1) A simple boundary-expansion heuristic atop a syntactic type inventory that yields high recall timex spans, particularly on noisy tweets. The algorithmic simplicity and efficiency are attractive.
2) A descriptive analysis of timex statistics across corpora, used to justify a type-first, token-seeded approach.
3) An empirical comparison on standard datasets plus a newly annotated Tweets set.

Strengths
- High recall with very simple rules: SynTime consistently attains the best recall across all datasets under both strict and relaxed match (Table 4), aligning with the paper’s design goal of first finding “time tokens” and then modestly expanding. The dependent-segment handling (“8 to 20 days”) is a useful pragmatic addition that many rule systems miss.
- Practical, lightweight system design: The approach is easy to implement, interpretable, and potentially fast in production (regex + POS + local expansion). This is valuable for applications where normalization is outsourced or latency constraints preclude heavy models.
- Positive results on Tweets: On the noisy Twitter domain, SynTime achieves the best F1 and a notable recall gain over strong rule baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime). The small amount of domain-specific expansion (Table 5) suggests that limited manual effort can pay off.
- Clear articulation of types and rules: The type inventory (time tokens, prefix/suffix/linkage modifiers, numerals) and the merging logic for adjacent/overlapping segments are spelled out clearly, which aids reproducibility and future extensions.
- Sensible positioning relative to prior work: The paper situates SynTime against SUTime/HeidelTime (rule-based) and UWTime (grammatical/learned), arguing for a type- and position-driven rather than purely semantic class or grammar-based approach.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited relative to existing rule systems: SUTime already uses token-level regex, chunk expansion, and cascaded patterns, and HeidelTime also performs context-aware expansion leveraging POS. SynTime’s boundary-expansion idea and local merging rules feel incremental, and the distinction between “syntactic” vs “semantic” types is not shown to translate into qualitatively different coverage. A stronger case would need ablations demonstrating that the proposed typing—and not merely the SUTime-lexicon seeding—drives the gains.
- Potential bias in the Tweets dataset construction: The Tweets set was created by first selecting tweets that SUTime marked as containing a timex (942 positives), then annotating them, with a small sample (500) from the remainder to estimate miss rate. This procedure induces selection bias toward SUTime’s lexicon and tokenization choices. Because SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes, this can inflate both SynTime’s and SUTime’s apparent coverage and hamper fair comparison with other methods. A cleaner construction would annotate a random sample independent of any baseline tagger and report IAA.
- Comparability and completeness of baselines: Some results for HeidelTime and UWTime are borrowed from prior papers (Table 4), which can be problematic due to different resource versions, configuration (news vs cross-domain settings), and evaluation scripts. In addition, no comparisons are made to modern neural sequence taggers (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BERT-based span taggers) adapted to timex recognition, which have become strong baselines in recent years.
- Limited rigor in the “principle of least effort” analysis: The four observations (shortness, small vocabulary, presence of time token, similar syntactic behavior) are descriptive statistics; linking them to Zipf’s least-effort principle is speculative without stronger statistical testing across domains and languages. The analysis is useful empirically, but the theoretical claim is overreached.
- Missing ablations, diagnostics, and runtime evidence: There is no ablation quantifying the contribution of POS filtering, dependent-segment handling, comma/linkage rules, or the small lexicon expansions. No speed or memory measurements are provided to substantiate “real time.” Error analysis is minimal; for example, the impact of POS errors is acknowledged but not measured.

Questions to authors
- Dataset construction: Can you re-annotate a random sample of tweets independent of any tagger filtering and report performance there (with IAA), to remove selection bias? Alternatively, can you show results on existing Twitter timex benchmarks, if any?
- Ablations: Please provide ablation studies quantifying the impact of (a) POS filtering; (b) dependent-segment logic; (c) comma/linkage merge conditions; (d) expansion vs initial lexicon. Also, what happens if you initialize with an empty lexicon and add only the few expansions you used?
- Baseline parity: Did you run and tune HeidelTime and SUTime yourselves under the same tokenization/POS pipeline and TimeML conventions used for SynTime, especially on Tweets and WikiWars? If not, please do so and report.
- Modern baselines: Can you compare against at least one strong neural sequence tagger fine-tuned for timex recognition (e.g., RoBERTa/BERT token-classification or span-classification with light domain adaptation)?
- Efficiency: Please include throughput and latency numbers under typical settings to substantiate the “real-time” claim.

Additional comments
- Releasing code, resources, and the Tweets annotations (with guidelines and adjudication details) would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Consider a finer-grained error analysis by time token type (e.g., DATE vs DURATION), length bins, and constructions with prepositions, to understand where SynTime underperforms on WikiWars and how to mitigate it (perhaps via optional preposition handling without violating TimeML).
- The claim that other rule systems use “fixed length and fixed position” patterns is too strong; please nuance this discussion and, if possible, show concrete cases where SynTime’s boundary expansion captures patterns those systems miss.
