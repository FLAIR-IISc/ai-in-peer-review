Summary and Contributions
This paper studies time expression recognition and proposes SynTime, a rule-based tagger that groups tokens into syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics to form time expressions. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four datasets indicating that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, usually contain an explicit time token, and show consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime is initialized with token regular expressions (largely from SUTime) and can be expanded with a small number of manually added variants. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset show SynTime achieves high recall and competitive or superior F1 compared to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

As seen by this review, the main contributions are:
1) A simplified, type-based rule framework (SynTime) that uses syntactic types and lightweight boundary-expansion heuristics to recognize time expressions with strong recall and competitive precision across domains.
2) An empirical characterization of time expressions across multiple corpora (length distribution, vocabulary size, presence of explicit time tokens, and syntactic tendencies) that motivates the design.
3) A comparative evaluation on three datasets including a new annotated Tweets set, demonstrating robustness and suggesting low-cost domain expansion.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime(-I/-E) achieves best or second-best results on most strict/relaxed metrics; notably superior on Tweets (e.g., 91.7% strict F1, 95.9% relaxed F1). High recall (>92% relaxed across datasets) aligns with the design choice to anchor on time tokens.
- Methodological simplicity and clarity: The type system (15 time token types, 5 modifier types, numerals) and heuristic boundary-expansion rules are easy to understand and implement. The assumptions and stopping conditions (e.g., handling commas, linkages) are clearly specified and grounded in corpus observations.
- Lightweight and extensible: Initialization from token regexes and minimal manual expansion (e.g., only a handful of additions for TimeBank/Tweets; moderate additions for WikiWars) suggest low adaptation cost. The approach plausibly runs in real time, making it attractive for practical systems.
- Analysis that guides design: The cross-corpus analysis (length, vocabulary size, time-token frequency, POS distributions) is useful, provides concrete statistics, and directly motivates the architecture (anchor on time tokens, then expand).
- Competitive to both rule-based and learning baselines: Performance matches or surpasses established rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a learned CCG-based approach (UWTime) without training, highlighting robustness across text types.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over prior rule-based systems: The use of POS cues, token-level regex resources, and boundary expansion is common in existing taggers (e.g., SUTime’s cascaded finite-state approach). The shift from “semantic” to “syntactic” types is presented as the key differentiation, but the practical distinction and its unique benefits are not rigorously substantiated. Many token categories overlap with established resources; initialization explicitly reuses SUTime token regexes.
- Evaluation fairness and comparability concerns: 
  • Tweets dataset selection is seeded by SUTime (only tweets with at least one SUTime-identified time expression are retained), introducing potential selection bias and coupling with the initialization resource. The estimate that SUTime misses ~3% is based on a small sample and does not fully mitigate the bias concern. 
  • Some baseline results are borrowed from prior papers, which may not be strictly comparable (different configurations, versions, or preprocessing), especially for TimeBank/WikiWars. 
  • SynTime-E benefits from manual expansion, but analogous tuning of HeidelTime/SUTime for the target domains is not reported; this could understate baseline performance.
- Incomplete analysis of errors and ablations: The paper lacks ablation studies to quantify the contribution of syntactic typing vs. heuristic rules vs. resource initialization, and lacks detailed error analysis (e.g., categories missed, over-segmentation, ambiguous tokens, preposition handling). No statistical significance tests are reported for performance differences.
- Dependence on POS quality and annotation scheme: The approach relies on POS tags to disambiguate ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May”). POS tagging errors, especially in noisy text, can degrade performance; this is acknowledged but not quantified. Adherence to TimeML/TimeBank’s exclusion of many prepositions limits coverage on WikiWars (where prepositional time expressions are common). This design choice may not match downstream needs where prepositions are included.
- Reproducibility and resource clarity: The exact token regex inventory used for initialization (from SUTime) and the final SynTime resources (including added tokens) are not provided. Configuration details for baselines (versions, language/domain resources, parameters) and for POS tagging are also insufficiently specified.

Questions to Authors
1) Novelty and design: Can you concretely differentiate SynTime’s “syntactic types + boundary expansion” from SUTime/HeidelTime rules beyond terminology? For example, provide side-by-side rule templates or coverage analyses showing cases your method handles that others’ fixed rules miss.
2) Evaluation fairness: 
   - How were HeidelTime and SUTime configured (versions, resources, domain settings)? Were they allowed domain-specific tuning comparable to SynTime-E? 
   - For numbers taken from prior work, are the data splits and preprocessing identical to those used in your experiments?
3) Tweets dataset construction: Using SUTime to preselect tweets likely biases the sample. Can you provide results on a randomly sampled tweets test set independent of SUTime prefiltering, or quantify the bias more rigorously?
4) Resource release and reproducibility: Will you release SynTime code, the full list of token regexes used (initialization) and all added tokens/modifiers per dataset, as well as the annotated Tweets corpus and evaluation scripts?
5) Robustness and error analysis: 
   - Can you report error categories and their proportions (e.g., ambiguous month names, overlapping segments with commas/linkages, preposition-including expressions)? 
   - How sensitive is SynTime to POS tagging errors in noisy text (e.g., by perturbing tags or switching taggers)?
6) Statistical testing and ablations: Can you provide significance tests for F1 improvements, and ablation experiments that isolate the contributions of (i) type system vs. (ii) boundary rules vs. (iii) SUTime-initialized resources vs. (iv) manual expansion?
7) Scope and generality: Given the stated link to the principle of least effort, do the observations and the approach generalize to other languages or domains with richer time syntax (e.g., clinical notes), and to recognition schemes that include prepositions as part of spans?
