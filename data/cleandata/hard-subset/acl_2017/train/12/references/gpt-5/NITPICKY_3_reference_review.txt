Summary
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based time expression recognizer that groups tokens into syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics to form time expressions. Motivated by an analysis across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset, the authors claim most time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, contain at least one “time token,” and show consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes, optionally expanded with a small set of additional tokens, and evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A pragmatic, type-based heuristic tagger (SynTime) emphasizing token-type definitions and boundary-expansion rules, with strong recall and competitive F1 in reported experiments.
2) An empirical analysis suggesting short length, small lexicon, and syntactic regularity of time expressions across domains, albeit with methodological caveats.
3) A manually annotated tweets test set (though its construction is biased by SUTime pre-filtering and lacks IAA).

Strengths
- High recall and competitive F1: SynTime achieves consistently high recall across datasets, particularly on Tweets, aligning with the stated design goal of covering most time tokens. The approach is simple, interpretable, and appears robust to the dominant surface patterns observed.
- Simplicity and potential efficiency: The method is extremely lightweight (regex + POS + local expansion). While no runtime is reported, heuristic rules plus token-type filtering plausibly support real-time use and easy domain adaptation by adding a handful of tokens.
- Clear, intuitive type system: The division into time tokens, modifiers (with positional subtypes), and numerals is conceptually clean, easy to extend, and more syntactically grounded than some prior semantic-focused rule sets. The merging logic is simple to implement.
- Cross-domain descriptive analysis: The paper attempts a cross-genre analysis (news, Wikipedia, Twitter) and draws consistent qualitative trends (short expressions, limited lexicon). Even if unsurprising, this can help motivate lightweight approaches over more complex statistical models.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The core idea—regex-based identification of time tokens and boundary expansion to form spans—closely resembles existing rule-based systems (e.g., SUTime’s cascade over token regexes). The paper frames novelty as “syntactic types” and “heuristic expansion,” but comparable notions exist (e.g., SUTime’s modifiers, boundary expansion; UWTime’s linguistically structured lexicons). The contribution risks being incremental without deeper algorithmic innovations, new coverage categories, or substantially superior generalization.
- Methodological bias in Tweets dataset: The Tweets dataset was pre-filtered using SUTime to select tweets “with at least one time expression,” then used for evaluation against SUTime. This introduces a selection bias that favors patterns SUTime can detect and distorts precision/recall comparisons for all systems. The post hoc estimate that SUTime misses ~3% based on a 500-tweet sample is weak and may underestimate bias. Moreover, no inter-annotator agreement is reported, and annotation guidelines beyond “TimeML/TimeBank standard” are not detailed.
- Baseline comparability and tuning: Some baseline numbers appear borrowed from prior papers (Table 4) with potentially different preprocessing, versions, or settings. It is unclear whether HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime were tuned for the same tokenization/POS tagging configurations, especially for Twitter. Using Stanford POS Tagger (WSJ-trained) on tweets likely penalizes all systems that depend on POS; a Twitter-specific POS tagger should have been used, or at least controlled across methods.
- Reproducibility and missing details: Code/resources for SynTime are not provided. The lists of initial token regexes (from SUTime) and the expanded tokens (Table 5) are not released; the exact POS constraints used to disambiguate ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May”) are not enumerated; the precise merging and stopping conditions are only partially specified (e.g., handling nested or nested-with-prepositions cases, windowing). No ablations quantify the contribution of type definitions vs. expansion rules vs. POS filters. No error analysis is provided. Claims of “real time” lack empirical measurements.
- Data analysis methodology issues: The “small vocabulary” claim is based on manual normalization rules that are not described, and numerals are excluded despite being central to time expressions, biasing Table 3. Observation 4 on POS utility is vague (no per-tag distributions, confusion analyses, or statistical tests). The principle-of-least-effort linkage is speculative without rigorous evidence.
- Presentation and citation quality: The paper contains numerous typos, inconsistencies, and formatting issues that impede readability and credibility. Examples: mixed diacritics and spellings (Strötgen/Strotgen/Strötgen), “Rulebased,” “natruallanguage,” “Cambride,” “Engilish,” “Prager,” mismatched/misplaced footnotes (e.g., “4” before JodaTime, “SEASON (5)” where “(5)” is ambiguous), “PREFIXT” in Figure 3b, inconsistent citation years and question marks in references, missing Table 1 and Figure 1, and references lacking complete bibliographic information. These are pervasive.

Questions to Authors
- Tweets dataset: Please detail the selection protocol precisely. How many tweets were initially sampled? What proportion of positive tweets did SUTime identify? What is the exact estimated false negative rate with confidence intervals? Did you compute inter-annotator agreement? Will you release the raw tweets, annotations, and train/test splits?
- Baselines: Did you run the same versions of HeidelTime and SUTime on your exact pre-processing pipeline for all datasets? If numbers were taken from prior work, why, and how do you control for confounds? For UWTime, what training details (hyperparameters, lexicons) were used on Tweets and TimeBank?
- POS tagging: Why not use a Twitter POS tagger? How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors? Please provide an ablation with gold POS (where available) or a Twitter-specific tagger.
- Resources and rules: Will you release the full SynTime rules, the exact POS constraints for each type, and the added tokens/modifiers per dataset? Can you provide runtime benchmarks and memory footprint?
- Analysis: How exactly was manual normalization performed for Table 3? Please report examples, guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement for normalization. Can you include an error analysis and ablation to substantiate which heuristic rules matter?

Recommendation: Weak reject. The approach is practical and yields strong recall, but the novelty is limited, the Tweets evaluation is biased, baseline comparability is unclear, and the paper’s presentation and rigor need substantial improvement for ACL standards.
