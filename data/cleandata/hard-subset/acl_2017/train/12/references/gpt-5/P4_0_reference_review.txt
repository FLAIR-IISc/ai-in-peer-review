Summary
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that operates over syntactic “types” (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time expressions. The authors first analyze four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and report: (i) time expressions are short; (ii) the vocabulary of time tokens is small; (iii) >93% of expressions contain at least one time token; and (iv) within expressions, constituents show consistent syntactic behaviors. SynTime initializes token regexes from SUTime’s resources, identifies time tokens via regex and POS, expands left/right to attach modifiers/numerals, merges adjacent/overlapping segments, and optionally allows manual lexicon expansion. Evaluations on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets set compare SynTime with HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime. SynTime achieves the best F1 on TimeBank and Tweets and competitive results on WikiWars, with consistently high recall.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, transparent rule-based recognizer (SynTime) that uses syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics, delivering high recall and strong F1 on standard benchmarks and tweets with minimal engineering.
- Empirical analysis across four corpora quantifying length, token coverage, and syntactic patterns of time expressions, which motivates SynTime’s design.
- A new annotated Tweets dataset for recognition evaluation and evidence that modest, manual lexicon expansion yields robust performance across domains.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with notably high recall. SynTime-I/E achieve relaxed recall ≥92% on all datasets; e.g., on TimeBank relaxed F1 94.96 (SynTime-I) vs 91.40 (UWTime) and on Tweets relaxed F1 95.88 (SynTime-E) vs 89.54 (SUTime) and 86.92 (UWTime). Strict F1 on TimeBank is 92.47 (SynTime-E) vs 83.10 (UWTime), and on Tweets 91.77 (SynTime-E) vs 80.37 (HeidelTime) and 76.99 (SUTime).
- Method simplicity and interpretability. The type system (time token, modifier, numeral) and boundary-expansion rules are easy to understand and implement, reducing brittleness associated with long hand-crafted patterns. The merging rules for adjacent/overlapping segments are clearly specified.
- Practicality and portability. Initialization reuses public SUTime token resources; expansion is lightweight (e.g., Tweets: +3 time tokens and +2 modifiers) yet yields excellent cross-domain performance. The approach appears resource-lean and does not require supervised training.
- Motivating analysis. Cross-corpus statistics (expression length distributions, small token vocabularies, >93% containing time tokens) provide concrete justification for a token-first, boundary-expansion strategy, and explain the consistently high recall.
- Careful adherence to TimeML boundary conventions. The paper clarifies decisions (e.g., generally excluding prepositions) to align with TimeBank/TempEval guidelines, supporting fair comparison on those datasets.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior rule-based systems. SUTime and HeidelTime also start from token inventories and expand spans via patterns; SynTime primarily reframes types as “syntactic” vs “semantic” and uses simpler, position-based heuristics. Given that SynTime initializes with SUTime’s token regexes, the incremental novelty is modest.
- Evaluation rigor and comparability issues. Some baseline results are borrowed from prior papers, while others seem re-run; configuration parity (e.g., HeidelTime settings, document-creation time handling, domain tuning) is not detailed. There are no statistical significance tests to substantiate claims of “significant” improvements.
- Tweets dataset construction may introduce bias. Tweets containing time expressions were pre-filtered using SUTime and only a small sample of the “non-hit” remainder was manually checked. Although the authors estimate ~3% misses, the selection pipeline risks biasing the distribution toward expressions SUTime can find, potentially affecting comparative fairness.
- Reproducibility and resource release. Manual lexicon expansion (“cannot cause ambiguity and should be generic”) is subjective; the paper does not specify whether the code, expanded lexicons, and the annotated Tweets dataset will be released, nor quantify annotation/expansion effort.
- Lack of ablations and error analysis. The paper does not quantify the contribution of (i) POS disambiguation, (ii) each modifier type, or (iii) the merging rules; nor analyze prominent error types (e.g., with LINKAGE/COMMA, holidays, relative expressions). This limits insight into failure modes and generalizability.
- Unsubstantiated efficiency claim. The “real-time” assertion is plausible given rule simplicity, but no runtime benchmarks or throughput comparisons are provided.
- Theoretical framing is speculative. The link to the principle of least effort is interesting but not essential to the method; the paper does not empirically test the hypothesis beyond descriptive statistics.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: How were HeidelTime and SUTime configured for each dataset (e.g., language resources, domain-specific rules, DCT handling)? Were any tuning options used for WikiWars/TimeBank to ensure strong baselines comparable to those reported in prior work?
- Tweets dataset: Will the full annotated dataset be released? Can you provide details on sampling, annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, and the impact of SUTime pre-filtering (e.g., distributional comparison between filtered and unfiltered subsets)?
- Reproducibility: Will you release SynTime code, the initial and expanded lexicons, and a scriptable pipeline? Can you formalize the criteria for manual expansion (with examples of accepted vs rejected candidates) to reduce subjectivity?
- POS tagging: Which POS tagger/models were used for tweets (general Stanford tagger or a tweet-specific model)? How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors (e.g., ablation or using alternate taggers)?
- Error analysis and ablation: Can you provide breakdowns of major error categories and ablation results (e.g., removing LINKAGE/COMMA rules, ignoring POS disambiguation, or varying the merging conditions)?
- Efficiency: Can you report processing speed (docs/sec) and memory footprint relative to SUTime/HeidelTime to substantiate “real-time” claims?
