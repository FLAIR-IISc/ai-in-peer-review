- Proposes SynTime: a lightweight rule-based time expression recognizer using a small set of “syntactic types” and boundary-expansion heuristics.
- Design motivated by corpus analysis showing time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, and follow POS-driven regularities.
- Evaluated vs HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset, showing strong recall and SOTA/comparable F1, especially on Tweets.
- Claims a type-based, position-aware heuristic approach is a simpler, low-engineering-cost alternative with real-time applicability.

- Clear, simple method grounded in empirical observations with easy-to-implement span-formation rules.
- Achieves strong recall and competitive F1 across domains, notably high F1 on Tweets.
- Uses type abstractions to collapse surface variation and reduce rule combinatorics.
- Practical and extensible: small per-domain additions, interpretable, and maintainable.
- Cross-corpus empirical analysis provides useful design guidance.

- Limited methodological novelty relative to SUTime/HeidelTime; distinction between “syntactic” and “semantic” types is not convincingly new.
- Tweets dataset likely biased by preselection with SUTime and small manual sampling, threatening external validity of tweet gains.
- No ablation studies or statistical significance tests to show which components drive improvements.
- Experimental clarity and fairness issues: unclear baseline configurations, suboptimal tweet preprocessing, and no modern neural sequence-labeling baselines.
- Efficiency and generality claims unsubstantiated: no runtime/memory benchmarks or quantitative analysis of annotation effort and ambiguity handling.

- Clarify Tweets dataset pipeline, whether only SUTime-flagged tweets were annotated, mitigation of selection bias, and data release plans.
- Specify versions/resources and tuning used for HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime training/preprocessing details.
- Report whether a Twitter-specific tokenizer/POS tagger was considered and effects of substituting one.
- Provide ablations isolating token identification, left/right expansion, comma/linkage rules, and POS disambiguation.
- List which SUTime token regexes were reused or modified and provide exact added tokens/modifiers per dataset with examples.
- Provide throughput (docs/sec) and memory usage on the three corpora and comparisons to SUTime/HeidelTime.

- Add error analysis by category (e.g., false merges, missed descriptives, numeral ambiguities) and by token type.
- Report statistical significance for F1 differences.
- Clarify span extent alignment with TimeML conventions and any evaluation-script adjustments if normalization is out of scope.
- Add a modern neural sequence-labeling baseline for recognition-only to contextualize the rule-based approach.
