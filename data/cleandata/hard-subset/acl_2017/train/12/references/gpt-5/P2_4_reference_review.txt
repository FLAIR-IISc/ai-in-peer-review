Summary and contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer driven by a small set of syntactic token types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors first analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and report regularities in time expressions (short length, small vocabulary, high incidence of explicit time tokens, and consistent POS behavior). SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes, filters with POS to reduce ambiguity, then expands around identified time tokens using positional modifier/number types to form segments and merge them into final spans. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets dataset show strong recall and competitive-to-state-of-the-art F1, particularly on Tweets.

In my view, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A practical, simple rule-based recognizer that achieves very high recall and strong F1 across domains, especially tweets, while being lightweight and training-free.
2) An empirical characterization of time expressions across domains that motivates the design (short spans, small lexicon, token-centric patterns).
3) A clear, type-oriented formulation for rule writing (syntactic token types and positional modifier types) that seems to simplify rule design relative to more complex cascades.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and high recall across datasets:
  The method achieves best recall on all three datasets and top F1 on TimeBank and Tweets (e.g., strict F1 92.09–92.47 on TimeBank and 91.74–91.77 on Tweets; relaxed F1 ~95–96 on both). These results corroborate the paper’s central claim that a token-first, boundary-expansion approach is effective and robust, especially in noisy, short texts where time expressions are often minimal.

- Simplicity and practicality:
  The approach uses a compact set of syntactic types and a small number of position-based heuristics rather than long, brittle rule cascades. This is likely easier to maintain and adapt, and it plausibly explains the generalization to tweets with minimal manual additions (Table 5 shows very few added tokens for TimeBank/Tweets). The lack of training and the local nature of rules make the method attractive for real-time pipelines.

- Sensible, data-driven design rationale:
  The corpus analysis—shortness of spans, small keyword set, heavy reliance on explicit time tokens, and characteristic POS patterns—provides an intuitive, testable foundation for the algorithmic choices (identify the time token first, then expand). Even if the “least effort” framing is more conceptual than causal, the measured distributions (e.g., >93% spans containing time tokens) directly support the core pipeline.

- Clear algorithmic description:
  The three-step pipeline and the merging rules are described concretely with illustrative examples (Figure 3). The heuristics are easy to reproduce and reason about, which aids adoption and potential extension.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems:
  SUTime and HeidelTime also rely on token categories, POS cues, and boundary expansion via cascaded patterns. SynTime’s “syntactic types” and positional modifier classes appear to be an incremental reorganization of similar ideas rather than a substantive new paradigm. The distinction between “semantic” vs “syntactic” types is not convincingly shown to be the source of the gains (no ablation to isolate it). Without ablations, it is difficult to attribute improvements to type design vs implementation choices.

- Questionable fairness and potential biases in evaluation:
  • Initialization from SUTime’s token regexes creates a resource asymmetry; SynTime benefits from SUTime’s lexicon while SUTime is not allowed comparable domain adaptation in experiments. SynTime-E further adds manual tokens per dataset, while it is unclear whether comparable domain-specific tuning was applied to HeidelTime/SUTime (e.g., HeidelTime “colloquial” resources for tweets).
  • The Tweets dataset is constructed by sampling tweets for which SUTime already finds time expressions, then adding a small set of misses (~3%). This selection risks biasing the evaluation distribution and makes absolute performance less generalizable. A truly random sample independent of any system is preferable.

- Insufficient experimental detail and controls:
  • Splits: For TimeBank and WikiWars, the paper does not specify train/dev/test splits or whether manual expansions for SynTime-E were restricted to training only; leakage would inflate performance.
  • Baseline configurations and versions: It is unclear how HeidelTime and SUTime were configured for each domain, whether their latest resources were used, or whether any tuning was allowed. Some baseline scores are “borrowed” from prior work, which may not be strictly comparable to the current setup.
  • No statistical significance testing is reported for improvements.

- Missing ablations and error analysis:
  There is no quantitative ablation of (i) POS filtering, (ii) the syntactic type inventory, (iii) the merging heuristics, or (iv) the effect of manual expansion. An error analysis would clarify remaining failure modes (e.g., prepositional/descriptive expressions in WikiWars), and whether precision drops in relaxed match are due to overexpansion.

- Claims not fully substantiated:
  The claim of “real-time” performance lacks runtime benchmarks. The normalization task is not addressed (acknowledged), which limits downstream utility; at minimum, a brief experiment showing compatibility with an existing normalizer would strengthen the practical impact.

Questions to authors
1) Data splits and leakage: For TimeBank and WikiWars, what splits were used? Were SynTime-E additions derived strictly from training (not test) data? Please clarify to rule out leakage.
2) Baseline tuning: How were HeidelTime and SUTime configured for each dataset? For Tweets, did you enable HeidelTime’s colloquial settings or add domain resources? If not, why is manual expansion allowed for SynTime but not for baselines?
3) Ablations: Can you provide ablations isolating (a) POS filtering, (b) the syntactic modifier types vs a simpler undifferentiated modifier set, and (c) the merging rules vs a simpler adjacent-concatenate strategy?
4) Tweets dataset: Can you release the annotations and construct an evaluation set independently sampled from Twitter (not prefiltered by SUTime) to avoid selection bias?
5) Runtime: Please report throughput and latency comparisons with SUTime/HeidelTime on standard hardware.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a clear, useful, and effective rule-based recognizer with strong empirical results and a compelling simplicity/coverage tradeoff, especially valuable for noisy text. However, the novelty over established rule-based systems is limited, and the evaluation setup raises fairness and selection-bias concerns that need clarification. With stronger experimental controls (splits, tuning parity, ablations) and resource release, this could be a solid contribution. At present, I am borderline-positive if the authors can address the evaluation and ablation concerns.
