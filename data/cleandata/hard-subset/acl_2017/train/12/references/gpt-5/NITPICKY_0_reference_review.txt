Summary and main contributions
- A rule-based time expression recognizer (SynTime) that defines a syntactic type inventory (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time expressions from identified time tokens. The system is initialized with SUTime’s token regexes and can be “expanded” by manually adding domain-specific variants.
- A descriptive analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) yielding unsurprising observations: time expressions are short; a small vocabulary accounts for most expressions; most expressions include at least one time token; POS is not discriminative globally but informative within expressions.
- An empirical comparison against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweet set, reporting strong recall and top F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, and comparable results on WikiWars.

Strengths
- Simple, fast, and practical approach: The boundary-expansion heuristics on a small syntactic type system are easy to implement and plausibly efficient. This could be attractive for real-time or resource-constrained settings, although runtime numbers are missing.
- Strong recall across datasets: The method systematically prioritizes token identification and boundary growth, which aligns with the reported high recalls on all corpora. This design choice is coherent with the analysis that most expressions contain a time token.
- Good performance on Tweets: The method substantially improves strict and relaxed F1 on the Tweet test set, which is a challenging domain for many rule-based systems, and requires minimal expansion (few added tokens/modifiers).
- Clear articulation of rules: The high-level rules (token identification with POS filtering, left/right expansion constraints, merging logic for adjacent/overlapping segments) are stated concretely enough to understand the approach.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and heavy dependence on SUTime resources: SynTime reuses SUTime’s lexicon and token regexes; the “syntactic types” largely mirror categories already present in rule-based taggers; and SUTime already performs token-to-chunk expansion. The main novelty—heuristic boundary expansion with a few position-based modifier types—seems incremental relative to existing cascaded FST/regex approaches.
- Serious evaluation biases on Tweets: The Tweet corpus was preselected by SUTime (by design, almost all tweets contain a SUTime-detected timex), and the authors estimate SUTime misses ~3%—but this still yields a strong selection bias that disadvantages systems that detect different expressions and advantages a system initialized with SUTime’s vocabulary. Moreover, SynTime expansion uses the training portion; it’s unclear how independence was maintained. Results on Tweets are therefore difficult to interpret as fair head-to-head comparisons.
- Inconsistent and potentially unfair baseline comparisons: Some baseline numbers are “borrowed from their original papers,” while others appear to be re-run, likely with different preprocessing, splits, and evaluation pipelines. This is not apples-to-apples. WikiWars and TimeBank have multiple variants and annotation conventions (e.g., inclusion of prepositions); the paper explicitly excludes prepositions per TimeML, which may deviate from WikiWars annotation practice—again complicating fair comparison with prior reported results.
- Missing key details and ablations: The paper does not provide the full type definitions, POS constraints used to disambiguate ambiguous tokens (e.g., “May”), the exact regex lists, the expansion catalog added per dataset, or any ablation isolating the contribution of (i) POS filtering, (ii) type system, (iii) boundary-expansion strategy. No statistical significance testing is reported. Claims of “real-time” performance lack runtime or memory metrics.
- Weaknesses in analysis and claims: The observations (shortness, small vocabulary, presence of time tokens) are intuitive and have been implicitly/explicitly reflected in prior rule-based systems. The link to Zipf’s principle is speculative and not substantiated empirically. Error analysis is minimal; corner cases (e.g., prepositions, nested/coordinate expressions, cross-sentence phenomena) are not rigorously examined.
- Presentation quality and numerous technical/formatting issues: The manuscript has many typos, inconsistent author names and diacritics, malformed references, inconsistent tables/figures, dangling footnotes/URLs in text, and calculation issues (e.g., SUTime WikiWars strict F1 appears miscomputed given reported P/R). These undermine credibility.

Questions to Authors
- How was fairness ensured on Tweets given SUTime-based preselection? Can you re-collect a random Tweet sample independent of any system and report all systems’ performance?
- Did you re-run HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime under a unified pipeline and same splits for all three datasets? If not, can you provide re-run results under identical settings and tools?
- Please release the full SynTime resources (type definitions, POS constraints, regexes, expansion lists) and code. Without this, reproducibility is limited.
- Provide ablations: (a) remove POS filtering; (b) replace your boundary expansion with SUTime-like fixed rules; (c) remove specific modifier types; report deltas.
- Report runtime and memory, and significance tests. Also clarify the training/development/test protocol for TimeBank and WikiWars (what data was used to “expand” SynTime?).

Additional Comments
- Methodological details unclear: how do you handle ambiguous tokens beyond POS (e.g., “march,” “may,” “spring”)? Which tagset? Did you use a tweet-specific tagger for Tweets (Stanford POS tagger is ill-suited for Twitter; CMU ARK POS would be more appropriate)?
- Annotation standards: Please clarify whether you matched each corpus’s native TIMEX annotation policies (e.g., inclusion of prepositions in WikiWars) or enforced TimeML consistently across datasets. This impacts comparability to published numbers.
- Table/numbering issues: SUTime strict F1 on WikiWars appears inconsistent with P/R; several table captions and figure references are malformed; “PREFIXT” appears in Fig. 3; “s1 s2 e1 s1” artifacts appear; ensure correct compilation.
- Typos and style: numerous (e.g., “main involves,” “Particulaly,” “recoginize,” “Cambride,” “natruallanguage,” “Engilish,” inconsistent “Strotgen/Strötgen,” inconsistent hyphenation “rule based/rule-based,” inconsistent use of diacritics).
- Claims of “evidence for least effort” need more rigorous statistical support or should be toned down.
- Consider providing an error taxonomy and qualitative examples where SynTime fails vs. baselines, especially for prepositions, lists/ranges, and descriptive/narrative time expressions.

Overall, while the system is simple and achieves high recall, the methodological novelty is limited, the Tweet evaluation is biased, key details are missing, and the presentation needs substantial polishing.
