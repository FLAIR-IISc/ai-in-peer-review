Summary and contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that organizes token patterns into a small set of syntactic “types” (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time expressions from detected time tokens. The design is motivated by an analysis across four corpora showing that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, nearly always contain at least one time token, and have regular POS patterns. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a Tweets dataset compare SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, type-based rule formulation for time expression recognition that achieves strong recall and competitive/better F1 than established systems on standard datasets, particularly in tweets.
2) An empirical analysis across four corpora that motivates design choices (token-centric rules, small lexicon, syntactic types) and offers data-driven regularities of time expressions.
3) A lightweight, easily extensible rule framework that can be adapted with minimal manual lexicon additions for new domains.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with consistently high recall: SynTime-I and SynTime-E achieve the top or second-best performance on most metrics across three datasets. In particular, recall in relaxed match exceeds 92% on all datasets; Tweets results are especially strong (e.g., 95.87–95.88 F1 relaxed with 98%+ recall), demonstrating the approach’s robustness to informal text.
- Simplicity and interpretability: The system is transparent—types are intuitive (15 time-token types, 5 modifier types, numerals), and the boundary-expansion heuristic is easy to understand and debug (left/right expansion, simple merging rules for adjacency/overlap, careful handling of commas/linkers). This simplicity can be a major practical advantage compared to complex grammars or ML pipelines.
- Plausible, data-driven motivation: The corpus analysis (length distributions, token coverage, POS patterns) is useful and aligns well with the algorithm’s design. The observation that 93%+ of time expressions contain a time token directly supports a token-first recognition strategy, explaining SynTime’s recall.
- Lightweight domain adaptation: Minimal manual additions (e.g., 16 time tokens and 19 modifiers for WikiWars) yield meaningful recall gains, indicating an inexpensive path to improve coverage in specialized domains. This supports claims of easy extensibility.
- Clarity of task focus: The paper is explicit about only addressing recognition (not normalization) and justifies this choice by pointing to existing normalization resources, keeping scope focused.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and dependence on prior resources: The approach largely recasts established practices (token regexes and chunk expansion) with syntactic labels. Initialization reuses SUTime’s token regexes, and the expansion heuristic resembles common chunking/regex cascades. The distinction between “syntactic types” here and the “semantic types” in HeidelTime/SUTime is not convincingly shown to yield qualitatively new capabilities beyond what careful rule writing already does. An ablation showing that the syntactic typing (vs. flat lexicons) materially improves performance would help.
- Potential evaluation bias in the Tweets dataset: The Tweets corpus is built by first selecting tweets SUTime tagged as containing time expressions; only a small sample from the remainder was annotated to estimate a 3% miss rate. This creates a risk of selection bias and reduces the challenge for recall. Moreover, the test set is drawn from the preselected positives, not a random sample of tweets with gold labels. A fairer setup would annotate a random sample of tweets independently of any system’s predictions and then evaluate all systems on that gold set.
- Missing significance testing and limited error analysis: Claims of “significant” gains are not supported with statistical tests. The paper provides minimal qualitative error analysis, no per-type performance, and no ablations (e.g., effect of POS filtering, merging rules, comma/linkage heuristics, or manual lexicon expansion). This limits insight into where SynTime helps or fails and why.
- Reproducibility and resource availability: Key assets (code, full type inventories/regexes post-initialization, added tokens/modifiers per dataset, the annotated Tweets dataset) are not released. Without these, reproducibility and adoption are hindered. Also, important implementation details (e.g., exact POS filters per type, tokenization assumptions) are only sketched.
- Claims about efficiency and generality lack evidence: The paper asserts real-time speed and broad applicability but provides no runtime measurements, memory footprint, or throughput comparisons. Generality is also constrained by adherence to TimeML/TimeBank treatment that excludes many prepositions; this likely contributes to weaker results on WikiWars and may limit portability to applications that include prepositional cues within time spans.
- Baselines not fully up-to-date: The paper does not compare against strong neural sequence labeling baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF or transformer-based taggers) that are standard for span detection, even without normalization. While the paper’s era may predate some models, at least a modern CRF/LSTM-CRF tagger with character and gazetteer features would strengthen the evaluation.

Questions to authors
- How much of the performance advantage remains if you do not initialize from SUTime’s token regexes? Can you quantify the dependence on SUTime versus your type system and heuristics (e.g., build from scratch or from a reduced lexicon)?
- Can you provide significance testing for the reported improvements? If not, please add bootstrap tests or randomization tests.
- What is the exact POS mapping per type, and how sensitive is performance to POS tagging errors? An ablation without POS constraints (or using a different tagger) would be informative.
- For Tweets, can you release a randomly sampled, fully annotated test set independent of any system’s prefiltering? Alternatively, can you re-evaluate on a random gold subset to address selection bias?
- Do you have runtime measurements (tokens/second) and a comparison to SUTime/HeidelTime on the same hardware?
- Could you provide error analysis by type (e.g., dates vs durations vs times), and typical failure modes (e.g., prepositional phrases, descriptive spans, ambiguous tokens like May)?

Overall, this paper presents a practical, transparent approach with strong empirical recall and competitive F1. Addressing evaluation bias, adding significance tests/ablations, and releasing resources would substantively strengthen the work.
