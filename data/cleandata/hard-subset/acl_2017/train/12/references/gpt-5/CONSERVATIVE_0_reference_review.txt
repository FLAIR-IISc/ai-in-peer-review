Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that (i) defines syntactic types for time tokens, modifiers, and numerals, and (ii) uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time segments into full expressions. Initialization uses token regexes drawn from SUTime; optional manual expansion adds domain-specific variants.
- The authors analyze four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and report that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, usually contain a “time token,” and show consistent syntactic behavior. They argue these align with the principle of least effort.
- Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a small manually annotated Tweets set show high recall and strong F1 versus HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; SynTime-E adds a small number of tokens and further improves recall.

My view of the contributions (strongest first):
1) An engineering simplification of rule-based temporal tagging that achieves strong recall and competitive F1 with minimal resources and without training, especially on tweets.
2) An empirical characterization of time expressions across multiple corpora that motivates a simple lexicon+boundary expansion strategy.
3) A practical recipe (type inventory + heuristics) that is lightweight and appears to run in real time.

Strengths
- Strong recall and competitive F1 across datasets: SynTime(-I/-E) obtains ≥92% relaxed recall on all three benchmarks and best or second-best F1 in most settings. The gains on the Tweets set are particularly notable (strict F1 ≈91.7%).
- Simplicity and practicality: The method is easy to implement, requires no training, and appears computationally cheap. For many downstream IR/NLP pipelines, a robust, high-recall time tagger with low latency is valuable.
- Minimal manual expansion: The expansion step adds very few tokens per domain (Table 5), which is consistent with the “small vocabulary” observation and suggests good portability at low annotation cost.
- Clear, reproducible heuristics: The boundary expansion rules and merging criteria are described concretely with examples; this transparency is welcome in a space often characterized by opaque rule cascades.
- Sensible error sources acknowledged: The paper notes the dependency on tokenization and POS tagging and illustrates concrete failure modes.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established rule-based systems: SUTime already identifies tokens then expands to larger chunks via cascaded token regex; HeidelTime also leverages POS and curated lexicons. The main claimed novelty—syntactic vs. semantic modifier types and a heuristic expansion process—seems incremental. The work reads as a thoughtful re-engineering of known ideas rather than a conceptual advance.
- Evaluation fairness and potential bias:
  - SynTime is initialized with SUTime’s token/regex inventory. This gives SynTime a strong lexical foundation while SUTime is evaluated “as-is.” It is unclear whether comparable tuning (e.g., domain lexicon updates) was permitted for the baselines.
  - The Tweets dataset was pre-filtered using SUTime to select tweets containing time expressions; only a small sample of discarded tweets was manually checked to estimate misses (~3%). This data construction risks biasing the tweet corpus toward SUTime-detectable forms and may overstate performance and/or understate the difficulty of recall for other systems.
  - Some baseline numbers are “borrowed from prior papers,” which may entail different preprocessing, splits, or evaluation configurations, undermining apples-to-apples comparison.
- Lack of ablations and statistical rigor:
  - No ablation to quantify the contribution of (i) POS constraints, (ii) syntactic typing vs. a simpler token gazetteer, (iii) boundary-expansion rules vs. fixed templates, or (iv) manual expansion. Without ablations, the source of gains is unclear.
  - No statistical significance testing, and no per-category analysis (e.g., DATE vs TIME vs DURATION) or detailed error analysis.
  - Claims of “real-time” operation lack runtime/throughput measurements on standard hardware.
- Domain and annotation mismatches:
  - The approach explicitly excludes many prepositions following TimeML/TimeBank guidelines, yet WikiWars is known to contain more descriptive, preposition-rich TEs. The paper acknowledges poorer results there but does not reconcile annotation differences or provide a domain-specific evaluation protocol.
  - Tweets are POS-tagged with the Stanford POS tagger trained on news; there is no comparison to a Twitter-specific tagger nor sensitivity analysis to POS errors.
- Dataset and reproducibility concerns:
  - The Tweets dataset is small (942 tweets), lacks inter-annotator agreement, and is partly derived via a system-dependent filtering pipeline.
  - No code, lexicon, or annotation release is indicated; given the rule-based nature, public resources are important for impact and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Initialization and fairness: Since SynTime imports SUTime’s token regexes, did you also allow SUTime and/or HeidelTime any domain-specific resource expansion on the same training data used for SynTime-E? If not, why is this a fair comparison?
- Tweets dataset construction: Please quantify the exact filtering pipeline, the false-negative rate estimate with confidence intervals (not just 15/500), and whether any SUTime-unseen phenomena are sufficiently represented. Will you release the tweet IDs and annotations?
- Evaluation protocol: For TimeBank and WikiWars, did you use the exact same splits and preprocessing as the baselines whose numbers you report? If not, please re-run baselines (or clearly mark non-comparable results) and provide a unified evaluation with the TempEval-3 script.
- Ablations and error analysis: Can you provide ablations isolating POS constraints, syntactic types, and the boundary-expansion heuristic? Also, a breakdown by TE category and a small error taxonomy would help substantiate the claimed advantages.
- Runtime: Please report throughput (docs/sec or tokens/sec) and memory footprint versus SUTime/HeidelTime on the three datasets.
- POS tagging on tweets: Did you try a Twitter POS tagger? How sensitive is SynTime to POS accuracy, and can you quantify the delta in performance?
- Annotation conventions: Given that some corpora include/exclude prepositions differently, how does SynTime adapt? Can you toggle inclusion of specific function words without extensive rule work?

Overall, while the paper delivers a solid, practical system with strong recall and a clean description, the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation has fairness and rigor gaps. Addressing the questions above—especially fair baseline tuning, ablations, and unified evaluation—would substantially strengthen the case.
