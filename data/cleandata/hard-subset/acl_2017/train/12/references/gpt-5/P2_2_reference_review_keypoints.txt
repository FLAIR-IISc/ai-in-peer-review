- Proposed SynTime: a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer using token-level syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics, initialized from SUTime regexes with optional manual lexicon expansion.  
- Corpus analysis: four datasets show timexes are short, lexicon is small, most contain explicit time tokens, and POS patterns are consistent, tied to Zipf’s least-effort idea.  
- Evaluation results: high recall and strong F1; outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on two datasets and is comparable on WikiWars.  
- Main contributions: a practical high-recall rule-based recognizer, a corpus-driven analysis motivating the design, and a small annotated tweets dataset with comparative evaluation.  
- Strength—performance: achieves >91 strict F1 on TimeBank and Tweets with best recall across datasets, supporting the design choices.  
- Strength—simplicity/efficiency: implementation is simple (regex + POS + local heuristics) and suitable for deployment with scarce training data or domain drift.  
- Strength—data-driven motivation: empirical observations align with design and explain high recall.  
- Strength—domain expansion: small manual addition of lexical variants yields noticeable recall gains, supporting small-lexicon claims.  
- Strength—baselines: includes relevant rule-based and learning-based baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime).  
- Weakness—novelty: limited conceptual novelty over prior rule-based systems (SUTime/HeidelTime); lacks ablation showing unique advantages.  
- Weakness—resource dependence: initialization reuses SUTime regexes, blurring method superiority and requiring ablation of this dependence.  
- Weakness—tweets dataset bias/size: tweets were prefiltered with SUTime, risking bias toward SUTime-detectable cases, and the test set is small (200 tweets).  
- Weakness—evaluation gaps: no statistical significance testing and limited qualitative error analysis to characterize remaining errors.  
- Weakness—scope/generalization: English-only recognition (no normalization), no cross-lingual or runtime/latency evidence, and Zipf claim remains anecdotal.  
- Questions for authors: clarify novelty vs SUTime chunking, provide ablations (time-token only, no POS filtering, no merging rules), quantify dependence on SUTime resources, provide unbiased tweet results or release dataset, detail baseline configurations, and report runtime/memory comparisons.  
- Overall ACL assessment: originality moderate–low, generally sound but weakened by evaluation choices, practical significance decent but limited scientific novelty, clarity mostly good though some issues, reproducibility unclear due to unspecified code/resources.  
- Recommendation: leaning borderline reject; could become a solid practical contribution with ablations, clearer novelty evidence, unbiased tweets evaluation, and significance tests.
