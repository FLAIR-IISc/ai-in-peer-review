Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that (a) defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) rather than semantic classes, and (b) uses simple heuristic “boundary expansion” rules to form time segments and merge them into time expressions. The authors motivate the design with an analysis of four corpora, arguing most time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, frequently include explicit time tokens, and show similar syntactic behavior. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets set report strong recall and competitive or superior F1 against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, lightweight rule set for time expression recognition using syntactic types and simple positional heuristics that attains high recall on several datasets.
2) A cross-corpus descriptive analysis of time expressions (length distributions, token vocabulary size, POS tendencies).
3) An empirical comparison against established baselines, including a Twitter dataset annotation and a minimal “expansion” procedure.

Strengths
- Strong recall and competitive F1 across datasets: The reported relaxed recalls above 92% on all three datasets are notable, and strict F1 on TimeBank and Tweets surpasses published baselines in the table provided. The simplicity of the rules relative to the performance is appealing for practical use.
- Simple, interpretable method with minimal resources: The type definitions and boundary-expansion heuristics are easy to implement, tune, and deploy. This is attractive for real-time systems or low-resource settings where full ML pipelines are impractical.
- Descriptive analysis across domains: The cross-corpus observations (brevity of expressions, small time-token vocabulary, co-occurrence of explicit time tokens, syntactic regularities) are useful for practitioners and help explain why a token-first, boundary-expansion approach can be effective.
- Domain adaptability via light expansion: The expansion step appears to require relatively few additions (Table 5) and yields gains on WikiWars, suggesting the approach can be adapted to specific domains with modest effort.

Weaknesses
- Questionable novelty relative to existing rule-based systems: SUTime also identifies token-level regular expressions and then expands to chunks; the proposed “syntactic types” and boundary expansion are conceptually close to prior cascaded rule frameworks. Using SUTime’s token regexes for initialization further blurs the novelty. The distinction (syntactic vs. semantic types) is not clearly shown to matter empirically via ablations.
- Potential experimental design issues and data leakage: It is unclear how SynTime-E was expanded for TimeBank and WikiWars. Unlike Tweets (where a train/test split is described), there is no explicit training split for these corpora. If expansion drew from the same data used for evaluation, results would be inflated. Similarly, UWTime training details for TimeBank are not sufficiently specified—training on TimeBank and evaluating on TimeBank would overestimate performance. These issues need clarification.
- Tweet dataset selection bias: Tweets were pre-filtered by SUTime to contain at least one time expression, and only a small random sample of the remainder was manually checked. This almost certainly biases the dataset toward expressions SUTime can detect and systematically underrepresents missed phenomena. The claim that SUTime “misses about 3%” is based on a small subsample and may not be reliable. This selection protocol may distort both baseline and SynTime performance comparisons.
- Baseline comparability and significance: Some baseline numbers are taken from prior papers; others seem to be re-run. Differences in preprocessing (POS tagging, tokenization), evaluation sets, or parameterization can materially affect scores. No statistical significance testing is reported. Without standardized setups or significance tests, the reported improvements are difficult to assess conclusively.
- Limited scope and evaluation coverage: Only recognition is addressed; normalization is not evaluated, though it is critical for downstream temporal applications. There is limited error analysis (e.g., prepositions in WikiWars, ambiguous tokens, POS errors) and no detailed ablations (e.g., effect of POS constraints, boundary rules, or type choices). Claims of “real-time” and “light-weight” lack runtime or memory measurements.

Questions to authors
1) Expansion protocol: For TimeBank and WikiWars, did SynTime-E’s added tokens/modifiers come from a held-out training split, or from the same data used for evaluation? Please detail splits and procedures to rule out leakage.
2) UWTime training: What exact data was used to train UWTime for evaluation on TimeBank and WikiWars? If TimeBank was used for both training and testing, please provide proper cross-validation or a held-out split.
3) Baseline configurations: Which versions of HeidelTime and SUTime were used, with what language resources and settings? Were tokenization/POS identical across systems? If not, please standardize or report the impact.
4) Tweets selection bias: Given SUTime pre-filtering, how do you ensure the Tweets test set is representative of tweets with time expressions beyond SUTime’s coverage? Can you provide an evaluation on an unbiased sample (e.g., all tweets in a random sample with exhaustive manual annotation)?
5) Ablations and sensitivity: Can you report ablations for (a) removing POS constraints, (b) removing specific modifier types, and (c) replacing syntactic types with semantic groupings as in SUTime, to isolate the contribution of your type system and boundary rules?
6) Runtime and resources: Please provide runtime and memory statistics on the three datasets, and compare to SUTime and HeidelTime to substantiate the “real-time” claim.
7) Release: Will SynTime code, resources, and the annotated Tweets dataset be released to facilitate replication?

Overall, while the method is practical and shows promising recall, the novelty is limited and the evaluation raises methodological concerns that must be addressed for a robust claim of superiority over established systems.
