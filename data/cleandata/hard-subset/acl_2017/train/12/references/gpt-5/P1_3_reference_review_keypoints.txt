- Paper proposes SynTime: a simple rule-based temporal mention recognizer using a few syntactic token types and boundary-expansion heuristics, initialized from SUTime regexes.  
- Evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, WikiWars, and Tweets vs. HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime; shows strong recall and competitive-to-best F1, with large gains on tweets.  
- Contribution: a high-recall, type-based rule system that achieves strong F1 on TimeBank and Tweets relative to established taggers.  
- Contribution: cross-corpus empirical analysis (span length, vocabulary size, time-token frequency, POS behavior) that motivates the design.  
- Contribution: a lightweight domain adaptation approach—small lexicon additions (few tokens) substantially boost recall.  
- Strength: consistently high recall (>92% relaxed) and top-tier F1, especially large strict/relaxed gains on tweets.  
- Strength: simple, interpretable, training-free approach with a tiny ruleset and minimal manual lexicon expansion.  
- Strength: data-driven design explains robustness on noisy inputs (tweets) where expressions are short and tokenized.  
- Strength: demonstrated low-effort domain adaptation (e.g., WikiWars recall improvement with few tokens).  
- Strength: clear algorithmic description and pipeline (token identification → segment expansion → merging), with edge-case examples.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—core ideas (token classes, regexes, expansion) closely resemble SUTime/HeidelTime and may be incremental.  
- Weakness: Tweet dataset likely biased—tweets were pre-filtered by SUTime before annotation, risking favorable evaluation for SUTime-derived methods.  
- Weakness: no statistical significance tests reported and limited qualitative error analysis to explain remaining failures or rule impact.  
- Weakness: scope and reproducibility gaps—focuses on recognition only, English-only evaluation, no runtime/ablation studies, and missing details on POS constraints and added lexicon.  
- Weakness: baseline comparison fairness unclear—baselines appear off-the-shelf while SynTime-E received manual per-dataset adaptation.  
- Question: will you release a tweet corpus constructed without SUTime pre-filtering or results on a random fully annotated tweet sample to remove selection bias?  
- Question: which versions/configs of HeidelTime and SUTime were used and were baselines given comparable light tuning as SynTime-E?  
- Question: which POS tagger was used for tweets, how sensitive is SynTime to POS errors, and can you provide ablation without POS filtering?  
- Question: are F1 improvements statistically significant, and can you add an error breakdown by phenomenon (prepositions, ranges, holidays, etc.)?  
- Question: will you release code, type definitions/regex lists (including added tokens), and runtime numbers to support reproducibility and the “real-time” claim?
