- Paper introduces SynTime: a lightweight rule-based timex recognizer using syntactic “types” and boundary-expansion heuristics, seeded from SUTime regexes and optionally expanded manually.
- Authors motivate design via analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) claiming timexes are short, use small vocab, usually contain a time token, and show consistent syntactic behavior.
- Main claimed contributions: simple high-recall boundary-expansion algorithm, descriptive timex statistics across corpora, and empirical comparisons including a newly annotated Tweets set.
- Strength: achieves highest recall across datasets (strict and relaxed), with useful dependent-segment handling (e.g., “8 to 20 days”).
- Strength: practical, interpretable, and likely fast design (regex + POS + local expansion) suitable for production/low-latency settings.
- Strength: strong performance on noisy Twitter domain, with small manual lexicon expansion yielding notable gains.
- Strength: clear specification of type inventory and merging logic that aids reproducibility and extension.
- Strength: well-positioned relative to prior rule-based (SUTime/HeidelTime) and learned/grammar-based (UWTime) systems.
- Weakness: limited novelty—techniques are incremental relative to SUTime/HeidelTime and the syntactic/semantic type distinction isn’t shown to give qualitative gains.
- Weakness: Tweets dataset construction is biased (selected by SUTime outputs), advantaging SUTime-seeded SynTime and impairing fair comparison; IAA not reported.
- Weakness: baseline comparisons may be inconsistent—some results borrowed from prior work and no tuning under shared tokenization/POS; no neural baselines included.
- Weakness: theoretical “least effort” claim is speculative and not rigorously tested across domains/languages.
- Weakness: missing ablations and diagnostics quantifying contributions of POS filtering, dependent-segment logic, comma/linkage rules, and lexicon expansions.
- Weakness: no runtime/throughput/latency measurements or detailed error analysis (e.g., impact of POS errors).
- Requested: re-annotate a random sample of tweets independent of tagger filtering (report IAA) or use existing Twitter timex benchmarks to remove selection bias.
- Requested: provide ablation studies for POS filtering, dependent-segment logic, comma/linkage merging, and lexicon initialization (including empty-lexicon experiment).
- Requested: run and tune HeidelTime/SUTime under the same preprocessing/evaluation and compare; add at least one modern neural sequence-tagging baseline (BERT/RoBERTa).
- Requested: include efficiency numbers (throughput/latency) to substantiate “real-time” claims.
- Additional suggestion: release code, resources, and Tweets annotations with guidelines and adjudication.
- Additional suggestion: perform finer-grained error analysis by timex type, length, and prepositional constructions to identify weaknesses and improvements.
- Additional suggestion: temper claims that other rule systems use “fixed length/position” patterns and provide concrete examples where SynTime’s expansion uniquely succeeds.
