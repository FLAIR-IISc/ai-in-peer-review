Summary and key contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and applies simple heuristic boundary-expansion rules to extract time expressions. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora showing that temporal mentions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, usually contain explicit time tokens, and exhibit similar POS patterns. SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes and disambiguated with POS; it outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and a new Tweets dataset, and is comparable on WikiWars.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, type-driven heuristic method for temporal mention recognition with strong recall and competitive F1, especially on tweets.
2) A corpus analysis highlighting compactness, limited temporal lexicon, and syntactic regularities that motivate the design (although these observations are not entirely novel).
3) A lightweight expansion mechanism via small manual additions per domain.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall, particularly on noisy text: SynTime-I/E achieves the best or second-best F1 on most metrics, and clearly leads in recall across all datasets. Gains on the Tweets dataset are substantial (e.g., strict F1 ~91.7 vs 76.99–80.37 for SUTime/HeidelTime/UWTime), aligning with the claim that minimalistic, type-based heuristics are robust to informal text and token variations.

- Methodological simplicity and interpretability: The approach is easy to understand and implement: identify time tokens; expand left/right for modifiers/numerals; merge segments with small boundary rules. This contrasts with more complex hand-written grammars and learned parsers, and offers practical utility (likely fast, low-resource, easier to maintain).

- Clear articulation of a design principle grounded in corpus patterns: The paper links empirical observations to a concise design (types + boundary expansion), making the rationale traceable. The POS-based filtering for ambiguous tokens (e.g., May) is pragmatic and effective.

- Small domain adaptation cost: The expansion step requires very few additions (Table 5), suggesting portability. The results on WikiWars improve notably with small additions, indicating the approach adapts with minimal manual effort.

- Coverage-focused error profile: High recall is valuable in downstream temporal relation extraction and IR; the system recovers most time mentions even if boundary precision is slightly weaker in relaxed match.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited and partially derivative: SynTime is heavily initialized from SUTime’s token regexes, and the overall pipeline (token recognition → chunk expansion → merging) resembles prior rule-based systems (e.g., cascaded finite-state methods). The “syntactic types” are close to what existing systems already encode (token classes, modifier categories), and the boundary-expansion heuristics overlap with standard chunk-expansion strategies. The conceptual contribution beyond reorganization and simplification is modest.

- Potential methodological leakage/tuning on test sets: Table 5 indicates SynTime-E adds tokens/modifiers using TimeBank and WikiWars. It is unclear whether these additions were derived from their test splits or from held-out training portions. Since TimeBank/WikiWars are typically used as test corpora for recognition, adding entries based on those corpora risks overfitting and inflating reported performance. The paper must clarify splits and prevent using gold test text for resource construction.

- Fairness of the Tweets dataset filtering and evaluation: Tweets were selected by first running SUTime and keeping tweets that contained at least one SUTime-detected time expression (then later estimating ~3% miss rate from a small sample). This introduces selection bias and likely disadvantages learning baselines and favors dictionary-heavy methods; it also underestimates recall errors on truly hard cases. In addition, no inter-annotator agreement is reported for the manual tweet annotations, making label quality uncertain.

- Lack of ablations and error analysis: There is no breakdown of where gains come from: token dictionary vs POS disambiguation vs boundary rules vs merging logic. A comparison to “tokens-only” or “no POS” variants would be informative. Likewise, there is no detailed error analysis (e.g., failures on prepositions in WikiWars, descriptive time mentions, ambiguous numerals). Without ablations, the claim that “type-based heuristic expansion” is the main driver is weaker.

- Missing runtime and resource comparisons: The paper asserts that SynTime runs in real time and is extremely lightweight, but provides no speed, memory, or complexity measurements relative to HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime. Given the emphasis on simplicity and efficiency, empirical evidence is needed.

- Comparative setup and statistical rigor: Some baseline numbers are taken from prior work; it is unclear whether configurations, resources, and document time settings are aligned across systems. No statistical significance tests are reported. UWTime training details are sparse (features, hyperparameters), and only one slightly-better configuration is presented.

Questions to authors
- Clarify train/test protocol: For TimeBank and WikiWars, what splits were used? Were any tokens/modifiers added to SynTime using the same test sets? If not, what training data supported the additions listed in Table 5?

- Tweets dataset construction: How exactly were tweets sampled? What fraction of the 4,000 tweets were annotated independently of SUTime filtering? Did you compute inter-annotator agreement? Will you release the dataset?

- UWTime and HeidelTime/SUTime configurations: Were these tools run locally with the same document creation time settings and options? If some numbers are from prior papers, why not re-run everything under a unified setup?

- Ablation and error analysis: Can you provide results for ablations (remove POS filtering; remove merging rules; use SUTime tokens vs your additions) and an error analysis by category (e.g., linkage, commas, descriptive mentions, prepositions)?

- Runtime: What are throughput numbers (docs/sec) and memory footprint relative to SUTime and HeidelTime?

Additional comments
- The “principle of least effort” framing is interesting but feels speculative; consider tempering claims or adding citations that specifically study temporal expression economy.
- There are many typos and reference inconsistencies; please revise the bibliography and formatting.
- Consider evaluating on additional domains (e.g., clinical notes, forum data) to further support portability claims.
