Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic token types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to grow time segments around identified time tokens. The authors analyze four corpora to motivate design choices (short spans, small vocabulary, high presence of “time tokens,” similar POS patterns), and evaluate SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset. They report strong recall and state-of-the-art F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, and comparable performance on WikiWars.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple syntactic-type-based, boundary-expansion method for time expression recognition that achieves consistently high recall and competitive F1.
2) An empirical analysis of time expressions across corpora highlighting short lengths and limited lexical variety.
3) A new, manually annotated Tweets dataset (if released; the paper does not clearly state availability).

Strengths
- Strong recall and solid F1 across domains: SynTime consistently attains the highest recall (≥92% relaxed match across all datasets), leading to strong or best F1 on TimeBank and Tweets. The boundary expansion around time tokens is an effective, simple heuristic that aligns with the observation that >93% of spans contain a time token.

- Simplicity and potential practicality: The approach is straightforward, transparent, and appears lightweight (“real time,” although no timings are reported). It requires only POS tagging and a small inventory of token regexes. The minimal rule set may ease maintenance compared to large hand-crafted grammars.

- Empirical observations are plausible and useful: The corpus analysis (short spans; small vocabulary; POS patterns) is consistent with practitioner intuition for temporal tagging, and the resulting design (token-first, then local expansion) is sensible.

- Cross-domain evaluation including informal text: Evaluating on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets is welcome; many systems degrade sharply on noisy, informal text. SynTime’s strong performance on Tweets is noteworthy.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty; heavy dependence on SUTime resources: The method initializes its token regex inventory from SUTime and then applies fairly standard left/right expansion and merging rules. SUTime itself uses token-level regexes and chunk expansion (tokensregex; cascaded automata). The claimed novelty (“syntactic types” and heuristic boundary expansion) seems incremental relative to prior rule-based taggers and to SUTime’s expansion pipeline. A clearer, formal differentiation and ablation showing why SynTime’s typing/heuristics are superior is missing.

- Potentially biased Tweet dataset and evaluation: The Tweets corpus was pre-filtered by SUTime to find tweets “with at least one time expression,” then only 500 of the remainder were spot-checked to estimate miss rate (~3%). This sampling design risks (i) missing tweets that SynTime could detect but SUTime could not, and (ii) inflating relative performance, notably against SUTime, because the test set was implicitly selected by a baseline. Furthermore, details on annotation protocols and quality are lacking: inter-annotator agreement is not reported, guidelines beyond “TimeML-like” are not provided, and dataset release is not stated.

- Non-uniform baseline comparison and reproducibility gaps: Some baseline numbers are “borrowed from their papers,” which likely used different preprocessing, tokenization, and even evaluation setups. For a fair comparison, all systems should be run with the same tokenization, POS tagging, and evaluation scripts on the same splits. There is no statistical significance testing. Code, rules, and data availability are not specified; the manual “expansion” step lacks precise documentation, making reproduction difficult.

- Possible test-set tuning/data leakage: Table 5 shows tokens and modifiers were added for TimeBank and WikiWars, but the paper does not define training/dev/test splits for these corpora. If the expansions were informed by the same data used for evaluation, results are over-optimistic. For Tweets, a train/test split is described, but the expansion procedure and its scope (which data it used) should be specified.

- Incomplete/missing experimental details and analyses: No runtime measurements despite “real time” claims; no ablations (e.g., without POS filtering, without certain modifier types, or with reduced token inventories); no error analysis beyond noting POS errors; no breakdown by phenomena (e.g., ranges, holidays, relative dates). The method’s handling of nested/overlapping expressions is described informally but could benefit from more rigorous specification.

- Annotation/schema mismatch on WikiWars: The paper acknowledges WikiWars often includes prepositions and descriptive spans, while SynTime follows TimeML/TimeBank excluding many prepositions. This schema mismatch likely depresses strict scores and complicates claims of “comparable” performance. Either adapt to the dataset’s conventions or clearly separate evaluations by schema.

- Writing, formatting, and references: Numerous typos, encoding issues, inconsistent author spellings, dangling “?” in years, malformed citations, and orphaned footnotes/URLs. Figures/tables are referenced but not always sufficiently described; some footnotes interrupt the flow. This detracts from clarity and polish.

Questions to authors
- Dataset and code: Will you release SynTime code/rules and the annotated Tweets dataset? Please specify license and availability.

- Fairness of Tweet evaluation: How exactly were tweets selected for annotation after SUTime pre-filtering? Can you provide an evaluation on a truly random sample (without SUTime pre-filter) to eliminate selection bias?

- Baselines: Can you re-run HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime with identical preprocessing (tokenizer, POS tagger, casing) and the same evaluation toolkit/splits? Please report significance tests.

- Splits and expansion: For TimeBank and WikiWars, did you define training/dev/test splits before manual expansion? If yes, which standard splits? If not, how did you avoid test-set leakage during expansion?

- Ablations: What is the effect of (a) removing POS-based disambiguation; (b) removing specific modifier types (PREFIX/SUFFIX/LINKAGE); (c) reducing token inventories to top-k items; (d) disabling comma/linkage-specific merges?

- Runtime: Please provide throughput (docs/sec) and memory footprint for SynTime vs SUTime/HeidelTime on each dataset.

- Schema alignment: For WikiWars, can you evaluate under its native span conventions (including prepositions) and/or provide a mapping so that comparisons are like-for-like?

- Error analysis: What are the dominant error sources (e.g., ambiguous month names, ranges, ordinals, holidays)? How often do POS errors cause misses, especially on Tweets?

Overall, the approach is practical and yields high recall, but the contribution over prior rule-based systems appears modest, and there are significant concerns about evaluation rigor, dataset selection bias, and reproducibility.
