Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based tagger for time expression recognition that organizes token patterns into syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers by relative position, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble full expressions. The method is initialized with token-level regexes largely imported from SUTime and can be “expanded” via modest manual additions from training text. The authors also present an empirical analysis of four corpora to motivate design choices (short span length, small vocabulary, high coverage by time tokens, similar syntactic behavior), and evaluate on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually labeled Tweets dataset.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A practical, type-based boundary-expansion approach for time expression recognition that achieves high recall and strong F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, with simple rules and no statistical training.
2) An empirical analysis of time expressions across four datasets that underscores small lexicon size and frequent presence of explicit time tokens, informing rule design.
3) A minimal, low-cost domain adaptation procedure via small lexicon additions that boosts recall in a domain like WikiWars.

Strengths
- Strong empirical recall and competitive F1 with a very simple method: SynTime-I achieves 92.09 F1 (strict) on TimeBank and 91.74 on Tweets, outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime in those settings, while maintaining relaxed-match recall >95% on TimeBank and >98% on Tweets. The boundary-expansion rules seem effective at capturing local context around time tokens, including dependent segments (e.g., “8 to 20 days”).
- Lightweight and fast by construction: The approach avoids heavy grammars or supervised models, uses a small set of interpretable types and a single-pass merge strategy, and is likely to be robust across domains where tokenization and POS tagging are available. The design is easy to reimplement and debug.
- Clear, transparent rule design: The paper precisely specifies token types, modifier types by relative position (PREFIX/SUFFIX/LINKAGE/COMMA/IN-ARTICLE), and merging conditions (including special handling for commas and linkers). This level of detail improves reproducibility and facilitates replication/adaptation.
- Useful cross-corpus analysis: The observations (short spans, small lexicon, coverage by time tokens, consistent syntactic behavior) are intuitively convincing and align with what many systems exploit implicitly. The quantitative breakdowns help justify the focus on time token detection and local expansion.
- Simple domain adaptation with good effect: Adding a small number of tokens/modifiers (e.g., 16/19 for WikiWars) yields measurable recall gains, supporting the claim that the lexicon needed is small and manageable.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to established rule-based systems: SUTime already uses a tokens-to-chunks expansion pipeline over token regexes, and HeidelTime exploits POS/context cues. SynTime’s main difference—grouping modifier types by relative position and applying simple boundary-expansion/merge heuristics—appears incremental. Moreover, SynTime imports SUTime’s token regexes, which blurs attribution and limits claims of originality.
- Potential evaluation bias in Tweets dataset construction: Tweets were first filtered by SUTime to ensure at least one time expression, then a small sample of non-selected tweets was checked to estimate a 3% miss rate. This design risks favoring systems similar to SUTime (including SynTime, which inherits SUTime’s token regexes) and may underrepresent expressions SUTime tends to miss. A cleaner protocol would annotate a random sample without preselection, or report performance on both preselected and truly random sets.
- Incomplete fairness and clarity across datasets/guidelines: The paper states SynTime excludes most prepositions following TimeML/TimeBank, yet WikiWars annotations often include prepositions. This mismatch could penalize or advantage systems depending on their treatment. The paper should explicitly align extraction scope with each gold standard and report matched vs unmatched scope results.
- Missing ablations and error analysis: There is no ablation of key design choices (POS filtering, dependent-segment handling, comma/linker rules) or analysis of which types drive gains. An error breakdown by type (e.g., DATE vs DURATION, holiday names, ordinal constructions) and by cause (POS errors, tokenization, lexical gaps) would strengthen the case and guide future improvements.
- Overstated theoretical framing: The “least effort” argument is anecdotal and not statistically grounded; the observations are plausible but largely known in prior work. This framing does not substantively advance theory and reads as ex post rationalization of empirical regularities.
- Claims of runtime/real-time performance lack evidence: No timing, memory, or throughput measurements are provided against baselines. Given the emphasis on being “extremely light-weight,” empirical runtime benchmarks would be valuable.

Questions to authors
- Tweets corpus construction: How many tweets in total were annotated end-to-end without SUTime preselection? Could you release metrics on a truly random, fully annotated test set to remove selection bias?
- Scope alignment: For WikiWars and Tweets, do your extracted spans include/exclude prepositions in the same way the gold does? If not, can you re-run with guidelines-aligned settings or report adjusted scores?
- Resource dependence: Precisely which SUTime token regexes did you import and which did you modify? Can you quantify SynTime performance without SUTime-initialized resources (e.g., starting from an empty lexicon plus your expansion)?
- Ablation and analyses: Please report ablations removing (i) POS disambiguation, (ii) comma/linker-specific rules, (iii) dependent-segment handling, and (iv) manual expansion. Also include error categories and examples.
- Runtime: Provide throughput (docs/s or tokens/s) and memory for SynTime vs SUTime/HeidelTime on each corpus, and clarify hardware/software settings.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, the expanded lexicons (the added tokens/modifiers), and the Tweets annotations? What is the inter-annotator agreement for the Tweets labels?

Overall, this is a solid, practical contribution with clear engineering value and strong results on two datasets, but the novelty is modest and the evaluation design has issues that should be addressed.
