Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that organizes token classes by syntactic behavior (time tokens, modifiers partitioned by positional behavior, numerals) and uses a simple boundary-expansion heuristic to compose full spans. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora showing that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, nearly always contain an explicit time token, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior—interpreted through Zipf’s principle of least effort. SynTime initializes its lexicon with SUTime’s token regexes and adds minimal domain-specific expansions. On TimeBank and Tweets, SynTime outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; on WikiWars it is comparable and has best recall.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A conceptually simple, syntactic-type and position-aware rule framework for time expression recognition using uniform boundary expansion, yielding strong recall and competitive F1 across domains with minimal engineering.
2) A cross-corpus empirical analysis of time expressions (length, vocabulary size, presence of time tokens, syntactic regularities) that informs the design and explains why lightweight rules can work well, especially on informal text.
3) Practical evidence that a minimal, extensible rule set (small, manually added lexicon expansions) can achieve state-of-the-art performance and run in real time.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplification with practical impact: Recasting rules around syntactic types (prefix/suffix/linkage modifiers and numerals) plus a single boundary-expansion heuristic is elegant and lowers the rule-writing burden. It offers a clear alternative to fixed-length, fixed-position rules without requiring learning or heavy resources.
- Strong empirical results and recall: SynTime-I/E achieves best or second-best in 25/36 cells, with especially high recall (>92% relaxed across all datasets) and large gains on Tweets (strict F1 ≈ +11 over next best). This aligns with the paper’s observations and supports the claim that finding time tokens first is highly effective.
- Minimal domain adaptation cost: The expansion step adds very few tokens (e.g., 3–5 for TimeBank/Tweets; 16/19 for WikiWars), yet improves performance, demonstrating portability and ease of maintenance.
- Clear algorithmic description: The segmentation and merging logic is easy to follow (Figure 3), making the approach reproducible and auditable—valuable for downstream systems needing determinism and efficiency.
- Useful corpus analysis: The four observations, quantified across diverse sources, are informative and likely to generalize to related extraction tasks, offering a design principle for simple taggers.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior rule-based systems: SUTime also performs token-level identification followed by expansion to chunks/expressions; the proposed types map closely to existing semantic categories (e.g., JodaTime classes). The main differences—syntactic vs semantic grouping and a more uniform boundary-expansion heuristic—are interesting but incremental. An ablation isolating gains from syntactic typing vs inherited token lists would strengthen the novelty claim.
- Evaluation comparability and potential bias: Some baseline numbers are “borrowed” from prior papers, risking mismatch in settings, resources, and preprocessing. Tweets were prefiltered using SUTime before annotation, and the estimated 3% miss rate is based on a small sample; this could bias distribution and comparability. A clean, independently sampled tweet set and re-running all baselines under identical configs would solidify conclusions.
- Dependence on POS tagging without robustness analysis: SynTime relies on POS to disambiguate tokens (e.g., May, march, second), yet no experiments quantify sensitivity to tagging errors, especially on noisy tweets. This is a key risk for practical deployment.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics: No breakdown by expression type (DATE vs DURATION), length, or modifier/linkage patterns; no study of the impact of omitting modifier types or the merging rules; no runtime measurements despite “real time” claims. These would help validate the design choices and the efficiency claim.
- Scope and claims: The Zipf/least-effort discussion is suggestive but speculative; cross-lingual generalization is not tested. Normalization is deferred; end-to-end utility for temporal reasoning is thus unmeasured.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Baselines: Did you re-run HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime on the exact same splits and preprocessing for TimeBank and WikiWars? If not, can you provide unified re-runs or clarify configuration differences? For Tweets, can you provide results on an independently sampled test set not prefiltered by SUTime?
- Ablations: 
  a) Replace the syntactic modifier types with SUTime-style semantic categories while keeping your boundary expansion—what changes? 
  b) Remove POS disambiguation—how much does performance drop, especially on Tweets? 
  c) Disable merging of overlapping segments or linkage handling—what are the effects?
- Robustness: How does performance change when using a Twitter-specific POS tagger vs Stanford POS Tagger? Can you report error breakdowns for ambiguous tokens like May/march/second?
- Efficiency: Please report runtime (tokens/sec) and memory footprint on each dataset to substantiate “real time.”
- Resources: Will you release SynTime code, the added token/modifier lists, and the annotated Tweets dataset to enable fair replication and future comparisons?
- WikiWars guidelines: You mention excluding most prepositions due to TimeML/TimeBank. WikiWars often includes them. Did this guideline mismatch depress your performance there, and if so, can you quantify?

Overall, I find the idea of a syntactic-type, position-aware, boundary-expansion framework a neat simplification with clear practical value and strong empirical recall, especially on tweets. With clearer, fairer comparisons and a small set of ablations/robustness checks, this could be a solid contribution. I lean toward acceptance given its conceptual simplicity, portability, and empirical gains, despite the modest novelty over prior rule-based systems.
