Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based temporal expression recognizer built around syntactic “types” (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and a simple boundary-expansion heuristic to assemble full spans. The system is initialized from SUTime’s token regex resources and can be “expanded” by manually adding domain-specific tokens. The authors motivate the design with a corpus analysis across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and tweets, and evaluate on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets set. They report higher F1 (especially recall) than HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and Tweets, and competitive results on WikiWars.

Contributions as I see them:
1) An engineering simplification of rule-based time expression recognition using syntactic grouping and boundary expansion, yielding high recall.
2) An empirical evaluation across three datasets with results competitive with strong baselines, particularly on tweets.
3) A descriptive analysis of temporal expression distributions that, while unsurprising, helps motivate a high-coverage rule inventory.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical recall and competitive F1 on established datasets. SynTime (both initial and expanded) attains very high relaxed-match recall (≥92%) across all datasets and strong strict-match F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, outperforming or matching HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime by notable margins in the reported tables. High recall is especially valuable in downstream temporal reasoning pipelines.

2) Simple, interpretable, and lightweight approach. The boundary-expansion heuristic and type inventory are easy to understand and implement, which is attractive for practitioners needing robust components without complex training. The reliance on token-level patterns and relative positions aligns with proven strategies in mature systems.

3) Pragmatic use of existing resources and modest expansion effort. Initializing from SUTime’s token regexes ensures good coverage from the start; the expansion step appears to require relatively few additions (Table 5) to improve domain coverage, which is practical.

4) Clear description of the rule system. The paper provides concrete typing (15 token types, 5 modifier types, numerals) and explicit merging heuristics (including comma/linkage conditions), aiding reproducibility and comparative analysis.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty beyond established rule-based systems. The core ideas—token-level categories similar to JodaTime/SUTime and left/right chunk expansion—are close to existing practice (e.g., SUTime’s cascades and chunk growth). The shift from “semantic” to “syntactic” modifier types is not shown to lead to qualitatively different behavior, and many categories mirror prior work. Given the initialization from SUTime token regexes, the new contribution seems incremental.

2) Tweet dataset construction risks selection bias and limits generality. Tweets were first filtered using SUTime to find those “containing” time expressions; only a small sample of the remainder was checked to estimate a 3% miss rate. This procedure likely shapes the distribution of expressions in the resulting dataset and may bias evaluation (even if SUTime does not dominate). The test set is small (200 tweets), and it is unclear whether annotations (and expansion lexicons) will be released, which hinders reproducibility and external validation.

3) Baseline comparability and statistical rigor are insufficiently addressed. Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers; others are re-run, but versions/configurations are not specified. There is no statistical significance testing despite frequent claims of “significant” improvements. No ablations quantify the contribution of POS disambiguation, modifier typing, or specific merging rules. The reliance on a general Stanford POS tagger for tweets is questionable; no sensitivity analysis is provided.

4) Scope limited to recognition; claims about generality are untested. Normalization is not attempted, whereas many state-of-the-art systems jointly address recognition and normalization. The approach is tuned to TimeML/TimeBank span conventions (e.g., excluding prepositions), which partially explains weaker results on WikiWars; generalization to other annotation schemes is unclear. The “least effort”/Zipfian argument is descriptive and not central to the method’s effectiveness.

5) Missing runtime and resource usage evidence. The paper claims “real-time” performance and “extremely lightweight,” but provides no measurements (latency, throughput, memory) or comparisons to SUTime/HeidelTime. Given the POS tagging dependency, the overall runtime may be dominated by the tagger; this should be quantified.

Questions to authors
- Tweet dataset: Please detail the exact construction protocol. How many expressions are in the 200-tweet test split? Were the 200 test tweets chosen independently of SUTime detections? Will the tweet annotations and SynTime resources be released?

- Baselines: What exact versions/configurations of HeidelTime and SUTime were used? Were they tuned (e.g., HeidelTime resources) as in their best reported settings? How was UWTime trained (training instances, features, and hyperparameters)?

- Significance and ablations: Are improvements statistically significant (e.g., bootstrap tests on F1)? Can you provide ablations isolating (i) removal of POS filters, (ii) alternative modifier typing (semantic vs syntactic), and (iii) merging without the comma/linkage special cases?

- Runtime: Please provide throughput (docs/sec or tokens/sec) and latency on each dataset, with and without POS tagging, and compare to SUTime/HeidelTime.

- Expansion effort: How much human time was required to curate the added tokens/modifiers per dataset? What criteria ensured “no ambiguity,” and how reproducible is this curation across annotators (any agreement metrics)?

- Robustness: How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors, especially on noisy tweets? Would a tweet-specific POS tagger improve performance, and by how much?

Overall, while the engineering is sensible and results are promising, the contribution seems incremental and the evaluation lacks rigor in dataset construction, baseline comparability, and statistical testing. Strengthening these aspects would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
