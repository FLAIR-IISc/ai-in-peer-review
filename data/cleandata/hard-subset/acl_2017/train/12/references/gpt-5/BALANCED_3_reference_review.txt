Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that defines a small set of syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time spans. The system is initialized from token-level regexes (largely sourced from SUTime) and can be manually expanded. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), report four empirical observations (short spans, small vocabulary, high presence of “time tokens,” similar syntactic behavior), and evaluate SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets. SynTime achieves high recall and strong F1, especially on Tweets.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A simple, high-recall, type-based heuristic system (SynTime) for time expression recognition, with competitive or superior performance to strong baselines on multiple datasets, particularly in noisy Twitter text.
2) An empirical analysis of time expressions across multiple corpora that motivates a compact syntactic type system and boundary-expansion strategy.
3) A small manual expansion procedure demonstrating that limited domain-specific additions can improve recall with low effort.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall and competitive F1 across datasets. SynTime (both initial and expanded variants) achieves the best recall on all three evaluation datasets and top-tier F1 on TimeBank and Tweets. The gains on Tweets (11.37% F1 improvement over the best baseline for strict match) are notable given the challenges of informal text.
- Simple, transparent design that is easy to extend and runs in real time. The type definition (time token, modifier, numeral) and the boundary expansion heuristics are straightforward, interpretable, and inexpensive. The ablation between SynTime-I and SynTime-E shows that minimal manual additions can yield measurable benefits (e.g., WikiWars recall).
- Cross-domain evaluation and analysis-driven design. The analysis across datasets (short spans, small vocabulary, token presence, syntactic regularities) is convincing and clearly motivates the system’s focus on token-first detection and local boundary expansion. The POS-driven filtering to reduce ambiguity is sensible and grounded in the observed distributions.
- Clear formulation of syntactic types and operational rules. The separation into 15 time-token types and 5 modifier types, plus treatment of linkage/comma cases and dependent segments (e.g., ranges), provides a compact yet reasonably comprehensive coverage of common constructions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing rule-based systems and heavy reliance on SUTime resources. The main methodological differences (syntactic vs. semantic “types,” and heuristic boundary expansion vs. fixed patterns) are incremental. The token lexicon and regexes are largely borrowed from SUTime, and the system still requires manual expansion per domain. The paper would benefit from a clearer ablation disentangling the contribution of the new heuristics vs. the reused token inventory.
- Evaluation design raises potential bias and comparability concerns, especially for Tweets. The Tweets dataset was preselected by SUTime, which risks biasing the pool toward expressions SUTime already detects (authors estimate ~3% miss rate from a small sample). This can distort both dataset composition and comparative performance. Several baseline numbers are “borrowed from their original papers,” potentially involving different setups or versions. It is unclear if HeidelTime/SUTime were run/tuned under identical conditions and guidelines. Statistical significance testing is absent.
- Guidelines mismatch and coverage ambiguities. SynTime follows TimeML/TimeBank conventions that often exclude prepositions (e.g., “on”), whereas WikiWars includes many preposition-rich and descriptive expressions. The paper acknowledges this but still compares on WikiWars without fully aligning annotation policies. This can penalize SynTime and complicate fairness. More broadly, the handling of nested expressions, ranges, and compositional structures is described only briefly (e.g., LINKAGE/COMMA rules), with limited error analysis.
- Claims about speed and generality are not substantiated empirically. While “real-time” performance is plausible, there are no runtime or memory measurements. The claim that manual expansion is low effort is reasonable but would benefit from quantification on additional domains beyond the three tested, and from assessing portability to other languages (only English is considered).
- Reproducibility gaps. The paper does not clearly state whether code, types/lexicons, and the Tweets annotations will be released. POS dependency is acknowledged as a limitation, but there is no sensitivity analysis to POS errors or alternatives.

Questions to Authors
- Tweets dataset and preselection: Please quantify more rigorously how SUTime prefiltering affected dataset composition and system comparisons. Can you provide results on (a) a random sample not prefiltered by SUTime, and/or (b) a reweighted evaluation accounting for the estimated 3% SUTime miss rate?
- Baselines and fairness: Were HeidelTime and SUTime run locally with the same versions, resources, and parameter settings? If some results are taken from prior papers, can you re-run them under your setup for comparability? Did you tune them on your training sets?
- Annotation guidelines: How did you reconcile the TimeML/TimeBank standard with WikiWars’ more inclusive spans (e.g., prepositions)? Did you re-annotate or harmonize guidelines across systems? Could you report category-level performance (e.g., ranges, anchored dates, relative expressions with prepositions)?
- Ablations and error analysis: Can you isolate the gain from your boundary-expansion heuristics vs. token inventory (e.g., replacing your heuristics with SUTime-style fixed rules but keeping the same lexicon)? Please include an error breakdown by type and typical failure cases (e.g., nested spans, ambiguous “May,” ranges).
- Runtime and robustness: Please provide timing and memory metrics on all datasets. How sensitive is SynTime to POS tagging errors? Would character-level or lexicon-based disambiguation help reduce POS dependency?
- Resources: Will you release code, the syntactic type definitions, and the Tweets annotations? This would strengthen the paper’s impact and reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a practical, interpretable system with strong empirical results. To meet the bar for a premier venue, the authors should strengthen claims of novelty with careful ablations, ensure evaluation fairness (especially on Tweets), provide runtime/statistical evidence, and release resources.
