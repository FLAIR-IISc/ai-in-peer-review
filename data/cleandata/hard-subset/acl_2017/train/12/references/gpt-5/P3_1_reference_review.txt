Summary and contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer grounded in a syntactic type system and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and observe: (i) time expressions are short, (ii) a small vocabulary of time tokens covers most cases, (iii) at least 93% of time expressions contain a time token, and (iv) constituents exhibit similar POS behavior. SynTime initializes a lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes, identifies time tokens with POS filtering, expands to segments using local heuristics, and merges segments into final spans. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets set compare against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; SynTime often achieves the best recall and competitive or superior F1, especially on Tweets.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, type-based rule framework with boundary-expansion heuristics that yields strong recall and competitive F1 on standard datasets, with particularly strong performance on tweets.
2) An empirical cross-corpus analysis of time expression properties that motivates the design (short spans, small lexicon, token-centric recognition).
3) A small domain adaptation recipe (“expansion”) that adds a few domain-specific tokens to boost recall; limited new annotated tweets resource (though availability is unclear).

Strengths
- Strong performance with a simple method: SynTime achieves high recall (>92% relaxed across datasets) and competitive or state-of-the-art F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, with small manual expansions (Table 5 shows only a handful of added tokens per domain). The approach is practical and easy to port.
- Clear, linguistically-motivated design: The syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers by relative position, numerals) and the boundary-expansion/merging rules are intuitive, compact, and well justified by the corpus analysis (Observations 1–4). The token-centric recognition strategy aligns with the empirical finding that most time expressions include an explicit time token.
- Good cross-domain coverage: Results on news (TimeBank), encyclopedic text (WikiWars), and social media (Tweets) show the method is robust, particularly on noisy tweets where concise expressions and limited lexicons dominate.
- Minimal dependence on training: The system runs without supervised training and can be adapted via small lexicon additions. This is attractive for low-resource or real-time scenarios.
- Transparent comparison to rule-based and learning baselines: Including HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime (with Tweets training) provides a reasonable landscape of alternatives.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to SUTime/HeidelTime: SUTime also starts from token regexes and performs cascaded expansion; HeidelTime uses POS and handcrafted rules. SynTime’s “syntactic vs. semantic” type distinction and the boundary-expansion heuristic appear incremental rather than conceptually new. A more rigorous comparison of rule expressivity and error types versus SUTime/HeidelTime is needed to substantiate novelty.
- Evaluation concerns on Tweets: The tweets dataset is constructed by pre-filtering with SUTime, then manually annotating only tweets predicted to contain times (and a small negative sample). This risks selection bias that favors SUTime-detectable expressions and may distort recall estimates and method comparisons. Inter-annotator agreement is not reported. Availability of the tweets resource is not stated.
- Use of SUTime resources: SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes and then compares against SUTime. This advantages SynTime (it inherits SUTime coverage but changes boundary logic). To ensure fairness, either (i) compare against a SUTime variant that uses the same lexicon snapshot, or (ii) ablate the overlap to show gains are due to the new rules rather than lexicon differences.
- Lack of statistical testing and ablations: No significance tests, confidence intervals, or per-phenomenon breakdowns are provided. Ablations (e.g., removing POS filtering; changing merge rules; using only token types without modifiers; removing expansion) are needed to isolate which design choices drive gains. Runtime claims (“real time”) are not supported by measurements.
- Incomplete methodological details and reproducibility: The exact POS constraints per type, the full token lists, and the precise merge heuristics (edge cases beyond Figure 3) are not fully enumerated. It is unclear whether code, lexicons, and the annotated tweets will be released. References are inconsistent and some details (e.g., “DAY TIME (27)”) are ambiguous.
- Scope limitations: The system focuses on recognition only; normalization is not evaluated, though it is central in TempEval tasks. The WikiWars underperformance is attributed to TimeML decisions about prepositions, but this also highlights a practical limitation: many downstream applications expect spans with prepositions. The “principle of least effort” argument is interesting but empirically underdeveloped (no statistical tests, limited to English).

Questions to authors
- Lexicon overlap and fairness: What exact SUTime version/rule set did you use to initialize SynTime? How much overlap exists between SynTime’s lexicon and the SUTime baseline used in experiments? Did you ensure both systems used identical token resources?
- Tweets dataset: How exactly were tweets sampled and annotated? Please report inter-annotator agreement, release plans, and statistics on expressions missed by SUTime that appear in your dataset. Can you re-evaluate on a tweet sample not pre-filtered by SUTime to avoid selection bias?
- POS constraints: Please provide the mapping from POS tags to each token/modifier type and the ambiguity resolution rules (e.g., “May” as modal vs. month). How sensitive is performance to POS errors on tweets?
- Ablations and significance: Can you report ablations for (i) no POS filtering, (ii) no merge-over-comma rule, (iii) no dependent segments (ranges), and (iv) no expansion? Also provide statistical significance tests across runs.
- Runtime and scalability: Please provide wall-clock throughput (docs/sec) and memory usage versus SUTime/HeidelTime on each corpus.
- Normalization and span policy: Do you plan to include prepositions when task definitions require them (e.g., for certain IE tasks)? How would your rules adapt? Any preliminary normalization accuracy when paired with an off-the-shelf normalizer?

Overall, this is a solid, practical paper with strong empirical performance from a simple design. However, novelty over established rule-based systems is limited, and the evaluation—particularly on tweets—needs stronger methodological rigor and ablations. I would lean toward acceptance if the authors address fairness, dataset bias, and provide clearer methodological details with statistical and runtime evidence.
