Summary and contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines a small set of “syntactic types” (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics around detected time tokens to form time mentions. The authors also provide a descriptive analysis of time expressions across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and an in-house Tweets dataset, noting short length, small vocabulary, high rate of explicit time tokens, and consistent POS patterns. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical rule-based recognizer that achieves strong recall and competitive F1, particularly on tweets, using a lightweight, interpretable boundary-expansion procedure.
2) A cross-corpus descriptive analysis of time expressions (length, vocabulary size, token presence, POS tendencies) that helps motivate a token-first tagging strategy.
3) A small demonstration that limited manual lexicon expansion (SynTime-E) yields domain adaptability.

Strengths
- High recall with a simple method: The approach attains consistently high relaxed-match recall (>92% across datasets) and strong strict F1 on TimeBank and tweets. The emphasis on time-token detection plus local boundary expansion is a robust, easily implemented strategy that aligns with long-standing practices in information extraction and appears effective.

- Lightweight and transparent: SynTime is simple to understand, tune, and deploy (no training, deterministic rules, “real-time” operation). This is valuable for applications requiring interpretability and fast adaptation without labeled data.

- Cross-dataset analysis is useful: The empirical characterization (short spans, small lexicon, frequent explicit time tokens) reinforces known intuitions with numbers and motivates the design choices. Even if not novel, this analysis is practical and informative.

- Domain adaptation with minimal effort: The SynTime-E results show that adding a small number of tokens/modifiers can improve recall in a domain like WikiWars, supporting the claim that a small vocabulary covers most mentions.

- Sensible evaluation metrics: Using TempEval-3 strict/relaxed match and reporting precision/recall/F1 is standard and appropriate for the task.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established rule-based systems: The “syntactic type” framing is not clearly distinct from the semantic classes already used in HeidelTime/SUTime (e.g., MONTH, TIME UNIT). The main procedural novelty—heuristic boundary expansion around tokens—resembles common chunk expansion in cascaded rule systems. Given that the lexicon and token regexes are taken directly from SUTime, the methodological contribution appears incremental.

- Experimental design concerns on tweets: The tweet corpus was constructed by prefiltering with SUTime, then manually annotating only tweets SUTime marked as positive (with a small sample to estimate missed positives). This introduces selection bias and weakens claims of superiority or recall estimation. A fair evaluation requires random sampling without prefiltering (or at least a controlled correction), otherwise systems are evaluated on an enriched subset defined by one baseline.

- Insufficient ablations and significance tests: The paper lacks ablations that isolate the contribution of (a) POS filtering, (b) boundary-expansion rules vs. alternative fixed patterns, and (c) the SUTime-initialized lexicon. No statistical significance testing is reported despite claims of “significant” improvements. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the proposed design rather than to lexicon coverage or dataset idiosyncrasies.

- Comparability and reproducibility issues: Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers, potentially with different setups/splits. Precise versions, configurations, and parameter settings for HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime are not detailed. Code, resources, and the tweet annotations do not appear to be released, limiting reproducibility and the community’s ability to validate results.

- Narrow scope (recognition only) and overextended claims: Recognition without normalization limits impact for end-to-end temporal processing scenarios common in IR/IE. The digression to the principle of least effort and language universals is interesting but speculative and not rigorously tested; it does not materially strengthen the technical contribution.

Questions to authors
- Tweets dataset construction: Can you provide results on a truly random sample of tweets (no SUTime prefilter) to remove selection bias? If not possible, can you quantify and correct for the bias with a larger negative sample? Will you release the tweet annotations?

- Fairness and setup details: What exact versions and configurations of HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime were used? Were any domain adaptations applied (e.g., tweet-specific settings)? For UWTime, what training splits and hyperparameters were used on TimeBank/WikiWars?

- Ablations: Please report ablations for (i) using the SUTime lexicon with a trivial longest-match baseline (no boundary heuristics), (ii) removing POS disambiguation, and (iii) replacing positional modifier types with simple fixed patterns. How much does each component contribute?

- Statistical testing: Are the observed F1 differences significant (e.g., approximate randomization, bootstrap over documents)?

- POS robustness: On tweets, the Stanford POS tagger (news-trained) is known to underperform. How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors? What happens if you use a Twitter-specialized POS tagger?

- Resource release: Will you release SynTime code, the exact token regexes used (post-expansion), and scripts to reproduce the reported results?

Overall, while the system is practical and achieves strong recall, the methodological novelty appears limited, and the evaluation (especially on tweets) needs stronger controls and ablations to substantiate the claims. I encourage the authors to tighten the experimental protocol, add ablations and significance testing, and release resources.
