Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer. It defines syntactic “types” (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics guided by POS tags to assemble spans. It initializes its lexicon and token regexes from SUTime and optionally expands them manually per domain.
- The authors present a descriptive analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) to motivate their design, emphasizing short spans, a small vocabulary of time tokens, and recurring syntactic behavior.
- Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a new Tweets dataset compare against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; the authors report higher recall and competitive or better F1 on two datasets.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A pragmatic, type-based rule formulation with boundary-expansion heuristics that yields high recall and competitive F1 across domains with minimal manual effort.
2) An empirical observation study across multiple corpora characterizing time expressions’ length, vocabulary, and POS distributions, used to justify the approach.
3) A Tweets dataset annotated for time expressions (though its construction raises concerns; see weaknesses).

Strengths
- Strong recall and competitive F1 against established baselines on standard corpora. The method achieves the best recall across datasets and state-of-the-art F1 on TimeBank and Tweets. Using the TempEval evaluation script lends credibility to the reported metrics.
- Simplicity and interpretability. The type system and boundary-expansion heuristics are easy to understand and implement, which is attractive for practitioners who need a fast, rule-based recognizer without heavy dependencies or training data.
- Cross-domain evaluation. Results on both formal (news, Wikipedia) and informal (tweets) text are provided. The tweets results are particularly strong, aligning with the claim that short spans and a small lexicon are sufficient in that domain.
- Minimal manual expansion. The reported number of added tokens/modifiers is small, suggesting reasonable portability; the approach’s “lightweight” claim seems plausible.
- Useful perspective on types vs. semantics. The choice to define types by syntactic behavior (rather than semantic categories) and design position-aware, flexible rules provides a concrete alternative to more rigid rule sets.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems and heavy reliance on SUTime resources. The initialization imports SUTime’s token regexes, and the main algorithm is a straightforward boundary expansion around anchor tokens. Without a deeper formalism or new linguistic insight, the contribution feels incremental. A direct ablation showing performance with and without SUTime lexicon (and with an independently derived lexicon) is needed to isolate the novelty’s value.
- Potential bias in the Tweets dataset construction. Tweets were prefiltered using SUTime before manual annotation. This likely underrepresents expressions SUTime systematically misses and may inflate results for SUTime-like token inventories (and for SynTime, which inherits SUTime’s tokens). The claim that SUTime misses “about 3%” is based on a small subsample; a truly random sample or a stratified sampling with full manual annotation is necessary to avoid selection bias.
- Fairness of comparisons and adaptation. SynTime-E benefits from manual domain-specific expansion, but it is unclear whether comparable tuning was done for HeidelTime and SUTime (both support resource tuning) or for UWTime (e.g., domain lexicon augmentation). Moreover, some reported baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers with possibly different settings/splits. A controlled, re-run comparison under the same preprocessing and evaluation is necessary.
- Methodological details and reproducibility gaps. The paper lacks a full specification of the token/type lists, the exact regexes, the POS filters per type, and the added tokens per dataset. There are no statistical significance tests, no runtime measurements despite “real-time” claims, and no ablation studies (e.g., removing POS filtering, varying boundary rules, or excluding LINKAGE/COMMA handling). These omissions make it difficult to assess robustness and generality.
- Questionable general claims (Zipf/least effort) without rigorous testing. The observation section is descriptive and intuitive but does not provide statistical tests or causal analysis linking the principle to performance gains. Also, the choice to exclude prepositions to follow TimeML, while baselines may include them in spans, can confound strict/relaxed span comparisons.
- Missing stronger modern baselines. The paper does not compare to contemporary sequence labeling models (e.g., CRF with character/word features from TempEval-3, BiLSTM-CRF, or contextual encoders), which have been competitive for span recognition. Even if normalization is out of scope, comparing recognition spans against a strong neural tagger would better situate SynTime.

Questions to authors
- Tweets dataset: Please report performance on a truly random sample of tweets annotated from scratch (no SUTime prefilter), and clarify the exact sampling protocol. How sensitive are results to the SUTime prefilter? Can you provide per-type performance on tweets?
- Baselines: Were HeidelTime and SUTime run with their latest English resources and tuned for each dataset? Did you allow domain adaptation akin to SynTime-E (e.g., adding WikiWars-specific lexicon) for them? For UWTime, please detail training data, hyperparameters, and whether any domain lexicon was allowed.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, the full type inventory with regexes, and the exact added tokens/modifiers per dataset? Please also share the tweet annotations and splits.
- Ablations and significance: Can you include ablation experiments isolating (a) POS filtering, (b) LINKAGE/COMMA rules, (c) left vs. right boundary stop conditions, and (d) removal of SUTime-initialized tokens? Also report statistical significance of improvements.
- Runtime: Please provide measured throughput (docs/sec or tokens/sec) and memory usage relative to SUTime/HeidelTime on the same hardware.
- Span policy: Given differences in handling prepositions and punctuation across systems, can you quantify how many errors are attributable to span boundary conventions vs. genuine misses?

Overall, the approach is practical and delivers strong recall with low engineering cost, but the novelty is modest and the evaluation has important threats to validity that should be addressed before acceptance.
