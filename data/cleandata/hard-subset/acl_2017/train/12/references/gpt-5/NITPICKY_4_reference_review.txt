Summary and contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer built around a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic boundary-expansion rules. The method initializes its lexicon by reusing SUTime’s token/regex resources, adds a small amount of manually curated domain-specific variants, and uses POS filtering to disambiguate. The authors report strong results on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset.

Contributions as I see them (in order):
1) A simple, type-driven rule system for time expression recognition that achieves high recall and competitive F1 across three datasets, with particularly strong results on tweets.
2) A compact analysis of time expressions across four corpora highlighting short length, small vocabulary, token prevalence, and consistent syntactic behavior.
3) A practical recipe for lightweight domain expansion via manual keyword augmentation.

Strengths
- Strong empirical recall and competitive F1: SynTime consistently achieves very high recall (≥92% relaxed) and competitive or better F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, sometimes surpassing baselines. The approach appears robust and practical, especially for short/noisy texts.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The boundary-expansion heuristics and small type inventory yield an interpretable, easily extensible system that plausibly runs in real time—attractive for deployment compared to heavier grammars or ML pipelines.
- Sensible use of syntactic regularities: Defining types by syntactic behavior (e.g., positional modifier types, POS-informed disambiguation) is a reasonable design choice that helps with generalization across surface forms and supports the reported recall.
- Minimal supervision for domain adaptation: The manual addition of a small number of tokens/modifiers to adapt to WikiWars and Tweets is pragmatic and low-cost, and the presented counts suggest good coverage with little effort.

Weaknesses
- Serious dataset construction bias for Tweets: The 942-tweet dataset was selected by first running SUTime and keeping tweets that SUTime marks as containing time expressions. This selection procedure biases the test set toward SUTime-detectable vocabulary and patterns (and SynTime reuses SUTime’s token regexes). The subsequent estimate that SUTime misses only ~3% (15/500) is based on a small sample and does not correct the biased test selection. This undermines the fairness and generalizability of the claimed gains on Tweets.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair baseline comparisons: Some baseline scores are “borrowed” from prior papers, likely under different preprocessing, splits, toolkit versions, and evaluation conventions. UWTime is retrained, but for SUTime/HeidelTime it is unclear whether they were rerun under exactly the same conditions as SynTime. No statistical significance tests are reported. This weakens the claim of “significant” improvements.
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems: SUTime also uses token-level regexes, cascaded expansion, and POS hints; HeidelTime uses lexicons plus linguistic cues. The novelty claim of “syntactic types and heuristic boundary expansion” reads as an incremental rearrangement of well-known ideas (position-based modifiers, merging adjacent chunks). The paper does not provide ablations showing that the type design (vs existing semantic categories) or the specific merging heuristics are responsible for the gains.
- Methodological details are underspecified: 
  - No formal algorithm or pseudocode is given for segment expansion/merging; rules are described informally, leaving room for ambiguity (e.g., treatment of DURATION/PERIOD “no need to further search” seems wrong for constructions like “two days ago”).
  - POS tagging for tweets appears to use the Stanford PTB tagger (not a Twitter POS tagger), which is known to underperform on social media; yet the system reportedly excels on tweets. The impact of tagging errors is not quantified.
  - No runtime or memory measurements are provided despite the “real-time” claim.
  - The exact lists of token types, regexes, and added keywords are not provided; no code or resources are released, limiting reproducibility.
- Evaluation gaps and lack of analysis:
  - No per-category breakdown (dates, times, durations), no length-based or boundary error analysis, and no ablations (e.g., without POS filtering, without type-based merging, without added keywords).
  - No cross-domain generalization test where expansion is not allowed, and no robustness checks to OOV time tokens.
- Numerous presentation and formatting issues:
  - Many typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “main involves,” “perfermance,” “Engilish,” “Cambride,” “v.s.”).
  - Broken or misplaced footnotes, inconsistent diacritics (Strötgen/Strotgen), inconsistent citation formatting and missing years/page ranges, malformed page ranges (e.g., 269–198), inconsistent reference entries, and figures/tables referenced but not clearly presented here. These detract materially from readability and polish.

Questions to authors
1) Tweets dataset: Please clarify the complete construction pipeline. How many tweets were truly randomly sampled and fully annotated independent of SUTime? Can you provide results on a fully random, fully annotated test set not filtered by SUTime, and report inter-annotator agreement?
2) Baseline comparability: Did you re-run SUTime and HeidelTime on your exact splits and evaluation toolkit? If not, can you rerun them and report statistical significance for all comparisons?
3) Resources and reproducibility: Will you release code, the SynTime type inventories, regexes, and the added keyword lists per domain? Without these, it is hard to verify the claims.
4) Ablations: Can you provide ablations quantifying the impact of (a) POS filtering, (b) syntactic vs semantic modifier types, (c) boundary-merge rules, and (d) expansion keywords?
5) Runtime: Please report throughput and latency on the three datasets, and compare to SUTime/HeidelTime.
6) Tagging accuracy on tweets: Did you try a Twitter POS tagger? How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors?

Overall, while the approach is practical and results are promising, the combination of dataset bias, insufficiently controlled comparisons, lack of ablations, and numerous presentation issues currently prevents me from recommending acceptance.
