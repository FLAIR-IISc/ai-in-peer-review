Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes SynTime, a light-weight, rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic types over tokens (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time segments into full time expressions. Initialization relies on SUTime’s token-level regex resources; optional manual expansion adds domain-specific variants.
- The authors present a descriptive analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and derive four observations: timexes are short; the lexicon is small; most timexes contain at least one “time token”; and constituents show consistent POS patterns. They connect these to Zipf’s principle of least effort.
- Evaluation on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a small tweets dataset shows high recall and strong F1, outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on two datasets and being comparable on WikiWars.

Main contributions (ordered by strength)
1) A practical, simple rule-based recognizer with high recall and competitive F1, achieved with a small set of syntactic types and generic boundary-expansion heuristics.
2) A corpus-driven descriptive analysis that motivates the design (short length, small lexicon, prevalence of explicit time tokens), with concrete statistics across datasets.
3) A small, manually annotated tweets dataset and comparative evaluation including both rule-based and learning-based baselines.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and high recall: On TimeBank and Tweets, SynTime-I/E achieves >91 F1 (strict) and >94 F1 (relaxed), with best recall on all datasets. This directly supports the claim that recognizing time tokens and expanding boundaries is effective and robust.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The approach is easy to implement (token regex matching + POS filters + short, local merging heuristics) and plausibly real-time. This makes SynTime attractive for practical deployment where training data are scarce or domain drift is expected.
- Clear, data-driven motivation: The four observations are useful and actionable, especially the prevalence of explicit time tokens and short spans. The design aligns well with these empirical patterns and explains the high recall.
- Sensible handling of domain expansion: The manual addition of a small number of lexical variants (e.g., tweet abbreviations) is modest in effort yet provides noticeable recall gains on WikiWars, supporting the “small lexicon” claim and the practicality of manual updates.
- Breadth of baselines: Including HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime is appropriate and gives both rule-based and learning-based points of comparison.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over prior rule-based systems: SUTime and HeidelTime already leverage token regexes, POS cues, and expansion/chunking. The “syntactic types” and “heuristic boundary expansion” appear close to existing notions (e.g., SUTime’s token cascades and chunk growth). The paper does not convincingly demonstrate conceptual novelty beyond reorganizing and simplifying existing ideas. An ablation contrasting “semantic vs syntactic typing,” or showing concrete failure cases of fixed-length rules that SynTime uniquely handles, would help.
- Dependence on SUTime resources and fairness: SynTime initialization reuses SUTime’s token regexes. While practical, this blurs the line between methods and complicates claims of superiority—SynTime benefits from SUTime’s curated lexicon without its more complex patterns. The paper should discuss this more explicitly and evaluate how much performance remains if initialized without SUTime resources (or provide an ablation of added value beyond the shared lexicon).
- Tweets dataset construction risk of bias: Tweets were prefiltered using SUTime to find tweets with at least one timex. Although the authors estimate a ~3% miss rate via a small negative sample, this process can bias the dataset toward SUTime-detectable contexts and away from truly hard negatives. The small test size (200 tweets) also limits the strength of conclusions. A random sample of tweets with exhaustive annotation (without prefiltering) would be more convincing.
- Missing statistical significance and limited error analysis: The paper claims significant improvements but does not report significance testing. There is also minimal qualitative error analysis beyond noting POS errors. Understanding major remaining error types (e.g., embedded prepositional phrases, narrative/descriptive timexes, ambiguous tokens like May) would add credibility and guide future work.
- Scope and generalization: SynTime supports English only and recognition only; normalization is left to future work. The claim about Zipf/least effort, while interesting, remains largely anecdotal. No cross-lingual experiments or runtime/latency measurements are provided, limiting claims about generality and efficiency.

Questions to authors
- Novelty clarification: In concrete terms, how does SynTime’s boundary expansion differ from SUTime’s multi-stage chunking? Can you provide categories of time expressions that SUTime/HeidelTime systematically miss due to fixed patterns but SynTime captures, with counts?
- Ablations: What is the performance using (a) only time-token identification (no expansion), (b) without POS filtering for ambiguous tokens, and (c) without the COMMA/LINKAGE merging rules?
- Resource dependence: How much performance drops if SynTime is initialized without SUTime’s token regexes (e.g., using only a minimal seed lexicon)? Please quantify the contributions of initialization vs manual expansion.
- Tweets dataset: Can you provide results on a truly random tweet sample annotated regardless of SUTime detection, or release the dataset to allow independent verification?
- Baseline configurations: Which versions/configurations of HeidelTime and SUTime were used, and were they tuned to match TimeML/TimeBank guidelines comparable to SynTime’s choices (e.g., treatment of prepositions)?
- Efficiency: Do you have runtime and memory measurements vs SUTime/HeidelTime on the evaluated corpora?

Overall assessment with respect to ACL criteria
- Originality: Moderate to low; the framing via syntactic types is tidy but close to prior rule-based paradigms.
- Soundness/correctness: Generally sound; rules are simple and transparent. Some evaluation design choices (tweet prefiltering) weaken claims.
- Significance: Practical significance is decent (high recall with simple rules). Broader scientific significance is limited without stronger novelty or generalization.
- Clarity: Mostly clear, but there are typos/incomplete references and limited error analysis.
- Resources/reproducibility: Code/resource release is not stated; this limits reproducibility.

Recommendation: Leaning borderline reject. With ablations, stronger evidence of novelty, a less biased tweets evaluation, and significance tests, this could be a solid practical contribution.
