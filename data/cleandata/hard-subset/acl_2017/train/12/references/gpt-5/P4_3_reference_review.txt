Summary
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for recognizing time expressions. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) that finds time expressions are typically short, rely on a small set of “time tokens,” almost always contain at least one such token, and show consistent POS patterns. SynTime defines syntactic types for tokens (15 time-token types, 5 modifier types, 1 numeral type) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics around identified time tokens to form time segments and merge them into full time expressions. Initialization uses SUTime’s token-level lexicon/regexes, with optional manual expansion per domain. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; SynTime reports high recall and strong F1, particularly on Tweets.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, type-based rule system with positional/syntactic modifier categories and heuristic boundary-expansion that achieves high recall and competitive/better F1 across three datasets, especially in noisy tweets, while being lightweight and fast.
2) A quantitative cross-corpus analysis of time expressions (length distribution, token inventory size, presence of explicit time tokens, POS distributions) that motivates the design.
3) An empirical comparison with strong baselines across domains; the paper also describes a manually annotated Tweets set used for evaluation (release status unclear).

Strengths
- High recall and strong overall performance: SynTime attains best or second-best scores on most metrics across TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets. Notably, strict F1 of ~92 on TimeBank and ~92 on Tweets, and relaxed recalls >92% on all datasets, support the claim that time-token-first identification plus boundary expansion is effective. The consistently high recall aligns with the Observation 3 that most time expressions contain explicit time tokens.
- Simplicity and extensibility: The system relies on a small, clearly defined type inventory and straightforward expansion rules (search left/right for specific modifier/numeral types with clear stopping conditions). This makes the approach easy to implement, adapt, and run in real time, with minimal linguistic resources beyond POS tagging and token regexes.
- Empirical motivation: The analysis across corpora quantifies properties (short spans, small vocabulary, frequent explicit time tokens, typical POS patterns) that are intuitively known but not always documented; this provides concrete design guidance and can inform both rule-based and learning-based methods.
- Comparisons include both rule-based and learning-based baselines: Evaluating against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime provides a broad view. Including both strict and relaxed metrics using the TempEval-3 toolkit aligns with common practice and facilitates comparison.
- Domain adaptation via minimal additions: SynTime-E shows that small, targeted lexicon expansions (e.g., for WikiWars) can boost recall without heavy engineering, suggesting practical adaptability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The overall pipeline—identify token-level lexical triggers, expand boundaries with positional heuristics, and merge segments—resembles prior rule-based taggers (e.g., SUTime’s cascaded regex/finite automata). Framing modifier categories as “syntactic” rather than “semantic” is a modest re-labeling; the contribution is primarily an engineering simplification and empirical validation rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic idea.
- Potential bias in Tweets dataset construction: Tweets were prefiltered by SUTime to select those with at least one time expression, and only a small sample of the remainder was inspected to estimate missed cases (~3%). This likely underrepresents expressions not detectable by SUTime and could bias evaluation. Although the authors estimate a small miss rate, the sampling is limited and may not capture long-tail phenomena; this threatens the validity of cross-system comparisons on Tweets.
- Fairness and comparability of baselines: SynTime-E benefits from manual lexicon expansion using training data; it is unclear whether HeidelTime/SUTime received analogous domain tuning for WikiWars/Tweets. Some baseline numbers are “borrowed” from original papers, potentially under different tokenization, preprocessing, or evaluation setups. These factors can materially impact strict-match scores and complicate claims of superiority.
- Reproducibility and specification gaps: The system depends on SUTime’s token regexes plus unspecified manual additions; detailed POS constraints per type and disambiguation logic (e.g., exact tag sets used to allow/forbid matches like “May”) are not fully enumerated. The paper does not state whether code, resources, or the manually annotated Tweets dataset will be released, limiting verification.
- Overstated theoretical framing: The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is suggestive but not rigorously tested; it does not materially affect the method or results and risks distracting from the empirical contribution.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Baseline configurations: What exact versions/settings of HeidelTime and SUTime were used on each dataset? Were they allowed domain-specific resource tuning comparable to SynTime-E’s manual expansions?
- Evaluation consistency: For results “borrowed from original papers,” were tokenization, preprocessing, and evaluation (strict/relaxed definitions, sentence segmentation) identical to those used for SynTime? If not, can you rerun baselines under your pipeline or provide aligned evaluations?
- Tweets dataset: Will the annotated tweets and SynTime resources be released? If not, can you provide additional details to enable reproduction (annotation guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, sampling protocol for estimating SUTime’s miss rate, and a larger audit of the unselected tweets)?
- POS usage: Please provide the full mapping from SynTime types to allowed POS tags and the disambiguation rules (e.g., for “May,” “sat/Saturday”). How sensitive is performance to POS errors on tweets? Any ablation removing POS filters?
- Efficiency: Can you report processing throughput and memory footprint to substantiate “real-time,” and include a comparison to SUTime/HeidelTime?

Overall, the work presents a clear and effective simplification of rule-based time recognition with strong empirical results, but the novelty is incremental and some evaluation and reproducibility issues should be addressed.
