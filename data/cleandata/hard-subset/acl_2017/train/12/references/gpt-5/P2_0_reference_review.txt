Summary and contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a light-weight, rule-based time expression recognizer. The core idea is to define syntactic types over tokens (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and apply simple heuristic “boundary expansion” rules around detected time tokens to form time expressions. The authors also provide descriptive analyses of four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) supporting observations about length, vocabulary size, and POS patterns of time expressions. SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token-level regexes and optionally expanded with a small set of manually added tokens. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare SynTime with HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple rule-based recognizer structured around syntactic token types and boundary-expansion heuristics that achieves strong recall and competitive F1 across domains, especially on tweets.
2) A descriptive analysis of time expressions across datasets showing short length, small vocabulary, and consistent POS tendencies, which motivates the type-based approach.
3) An engineering finding that a minimal, flexible rule set with token-type-based heuristics can match or outperform heavier rule systems/learned models for recognition.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with particularly high recall: Across all three datasets, SynTime(-I/E) achieves the best recall for relaxed match and competitive/best F1 on TimeBank and Tweets. The approach appears robust to domain variation and aligns with the observation that most time expressions contain a time token. The performance on tweets (F1 > 91% strict, > 95% relaxed) is a practical contribution for a difficult domain.
- Method simplicity and efficiency: The approach is easy to implement, requires no training, and the rules are interpretable. The boundary-expansion strategy is intuitive, compact, and likely to be fast; the promise of “real-time” recognition is appealing for downstream systems.
- Clear design anchored in empirical observations: The syntactic-type design (time token, modifier types, numeral) is well-motivated by cross-corpus statistics (short spans, small lexicon, POS regularities), and the decisions around using POS to disambiguate ambiguous tokens (e.g., May) are sensible.
- Domain adaptability via small lexicon expansion: The paper shows that adding a small number of domain-specific tokens (e.g., for WikiWars) improves recall, suggesting the approach scales to new domains with modest annotation cost.
- Comparisons to strong baselines: HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime are reasonable baselines representing rule-based and learning-based paradigms; using the TempEval-3 toolkit for evaluation is appropriate.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited relative to existing rule-based systems (notably SUTime): SUTime already operates over token-level regexes with cascaded expansion from tokens to chunks to expressions. The proposed “syntactic types” and boundary-expansion rules are conceptually close to SUTime’s token classes and chunk expansion. The paper claims other rule-based systems use “fixed rules” while SynTime is “heuristic and flexible,” but it does not concretely demonstrate capabilities that SUTime/HeidelTime cannot encode (e.g., specific constructions that SynTime captures but SUTime cannot). An ablation or side-by-side rule comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.
- Potential selection bias in the Tweets dataset: Tweets containing time expressions were identified by first running SUTime and keeping only tweets it flagged, with a small audit estimating ~3% miss rate. This prefiltering risks biasing the dataset toward SUTime’s vocabulary/coverage and away from expressions it misses, undermining claims of superior performance on tweets. A fairer setup would annotate a random sample exhaustively and then evaluate all systems on the same set. As it stands, both initialization (borrowing SUTime’s token regexes) and tweet selection may advantage SynTime in this domain.
- Fairness and transparency of resources: SynTime is initialized with SUTime’s token regexes, and in expansion uses manual additions. That makes comparative gains harder to interpret: are improvements due to better rules over a similar lexicon, or lexicon differences? Exact token lists, added items per dataset, and code are not provided, limiting reproducibility and making it hard to assess fairness of the baseline configurations (e.g., were SUTime/HeidelTime tuned or used with recommended domain settings?).
- Missing analyses and diagnostics: There is no ablation (e.g., without POS filtering, without COMMA/LINKAGE rules, without expansion), no error analysis, and no runtime measurements despite “real-time” claims. These would clarify which components matter, typical failure modes (e.g., prepositional phrases in WikiWars), and efficiency benefits versus SUTime/HeidelTime.
- Scope and claims: The “principle of least effort” framing is interesting but speculative; the presented statistics are descriptive and do not constitute strong evidence for cognitive/linguistic claims. Also, the system handles recognition only; in many applications, normalization is crucial. While out of scope, this should be emphasized to avoid overstating applicability.

Questions to authors
- Tweets dataset construction: Can you provide a version of the tweets test set drawn from a random sample without SUTime prefiltering? If not, please quantify the potential bias more rigorously (beyond a 500-tweet spot check), and report results on a truly random test subset.
- Baseline configurations: Which versions and language resources were used for HeidelTime and SUTime? Were any domain-specific resources enabled (e.g., colloquial forms for Twitter)? Did you tune parameters? Please ensure comparability with SynTime-E, which includes domain expansions.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, the exact token-type lexicon (including manual additions), and the annotated tweets? Without these, it is difficult to reproduce and verify results.
- Ablations: Can you report ablations quantifying the impact of POS filtering, COMMA/LINKAGE rules, and the “dependent time segment” handling? Also, how much does performance drop if you initialize without SUTime’s regexes (e.g., from a minimal seed)?
- Efficiency: Please report throughput or latency versus SUTime/HeidelTime on the evaluation corpora to substantiate “real-time.”

Overall, this is a solid engineering paper with practical value and strong recall, but the novelty over existing rule-based systems is modest, and the tweets evaluation methodology raises fairness concerns. Addressing dataset bias, adding ablations and runtime, and clarifying baseline parity would significantly strengthen the paper.
