Summary and contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer built around syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors motivate the design with an analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), observing that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, usually contain at least one “time token,” and show consistent POS/syntactic behaviors. SynTime initializes its token/regex inventory largely from SUTime, then (optionally) expands it with a small number of manually added items. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets report high recall and strong F1 (especially on Tweets) compared to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, type-driven rule formulation for time expression recognition with strong recall, particularly on short, informal text (Tweets).
2) An empirical analysis across several corpora quantifying length, lexical diversity, and POS patterns of time expressions.
3) An extremely lightweight instantiation that runs in real time and requires little manual expansion to adapt across domains.

Strengths
- High recall and competitive F1 across datasets, with especially strong performance on Tweets. The reported recalls (>92% relaxed across datasets) align with the observation that most time expressions contain an identifiable time token. The strict/relaxed F1 gains on Tweets (e.g., 91.74/95.87 for SynTime-I) suggest the approach is robust to noisy, short inputs where simple patterns dominate.
- Simplicity and practicality. The approach is transparent, easy to implement, and domain-adaptable by a small amount of manual expansion. Table 5 shows only a handful of additions per dataset. This makes the method appealing for applications that value interpretability and maintainability.
- Clear design rationale grounded in corpus analysis. The paper connects observed distributional facts (short spans, small vocabularies, POS regularities) to a minimal rule set that exploits these regularities. This separation of token types and positional heuristics plausibly explains the robustness.
- Focused scope with clear error modes. By clearly excluding normalization and focusing on recognition, the paper isolates a meaningful subproblem and shows where POS/tokenization and preposition-inclusion conventions impact performance.
- Potentially useful insights about coverage vs. ambiguity trade-offs. The POS-aware disambiguation of tokens like “May” and the decision to exclude prepositions (consistent with TimeML/TimeBank) are well articulated and actionable for other systems.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems. SUTime already defines regular expressions over tokens and performs staged expansion; HeidelTime uses POS and handcrafted resources. The proposed type system and boundary-expansion heuristics feel like incremental reorganizations rather than a qualitatively new modeling idea. A clearer contrast (e.g., ablations showing the value of syntactic types vs. semantic categories, or of the boundary-expansion heuristic vs. fixed-length patterns) is missing.
- Potential selection bias in the Tweets dataset. Tweets for annotation were prefiltered using SUTime, then a small random subset of SUTime-negative tweets was annotated to estimate misses (~3%). This pipeline is likely to bias the dataset toward SUTime-detectable expressions and underrepresent cases SUTime systematically misses, complicating fair comparison and generalization claims. A fully random sample with exhaustive annotation is needed.
- Incomplete experimental details and fairness concerns. It is unclear whether POS/tokenization are consistent across systems; whether baselines were tuned equally for Tweets; whether SynTime expansion used only training data and how train/dev/test splits were defined for TimeBank and WikiWars (where no training split is standard). The paper borrows SUTime’s token/regex inventory but does not describe how this impacts comparative fairness or run SUTime with the same updated resources. No statistical significance testing is reported.
- Scope and baseline coverage. The paper focuses only on recognition, but the literature often evaluates recognition+normalization. Moreover, there is no comparison to contemporary neural sequence labeling baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF/transformer taggers) that have shown strong performance on similar span-identification tasks, including on social media text.
- Limited analysis of errors and design choices. The paper does not quantify the impact of POS errors, provide breakdowns by expression category (e.g., ranges, holidays, relative expressions), or present head-to-head examples where SynTime succeeds and baselines fail. The “least effort”/Zipf tie-in is interesting but not strongly validated; the observations seem expected and are reported without statistical tests.

Questions to authors
- Tweets data construction: Please detail the exact pipeline. How many SUTime-positive tweets were annotated? How were SUTime-negative tweets sampled and annotated to estimate the 3% miss rate? Can you provide performance on a fully random, exhaustively annotated sample? Could you release the tweets and annotations?
- Splits and training: For TimeBank and WikiWars, what are the splits? If SynTime-E “expands” using training data, what is the source of training data for those corpora? How did you prevent leakage? For UWTime, did you follow the original training protocol and hyperparameters?
- Resource parity: SynTime initializes from SUTime’s token/regex resources. Did you evaluate SUTime with the same refreshed lexicon to ensure a fair comparison? If not, please justify.
- Tokenization/POS: Which tokenizer and POS tagger were used, and were they applied uniformly to all methods or only SynTime? Do baseline systems internally retokenize? Please quantify the sensitivity of SynTime to POS errors.
- Heuristic choices: Can you provide ablations for (a) removing POS disambiguation, (b) changing the boundary-expansion rules, and (c) excluding COMMA/LINKAGE handling? What are the most common failure modes (with examples)?
- Significance, speed, and reproducibility: Are the improvements statistically significant (e.g., bootstrap on F1)? What are throughput/latency metrics compared to baselines? Will you release code, resources, and configuration to replicate results?

Decision
Borderline reject. The approach is elegant and shows strong practical performance with high recall, especially on tweets. However, the novelty over existing rule-based systems is limited, the Tweets dataset construction likely introduces selection bias, and key experimental details and ablations are missing. Strengthening the empirical methodology (unbiased datasets, significance tests, fair resource parity), adding modern baselines, and providing deeper error/ablation analyses would materially improve the submission.
