Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition that groups tokens into a small set of “syntactic types” (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics to form spans. The design is motivated by an analysis of four corpora showing that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, and share POS-driven regularities.
- It evaluates SynTime against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset, reporting strong recall and state-of-the-art or comparable F1, especially on Tweets.
- It argues that a type-based, position-aware heuristic approach offers a simpler alternative to fixed finite-state rule sets or feature-heavy ML systems, with real-time performance and low engineering cost.

Strengths
- Clear, simple method grounded in empirical observations. The span-formation rules (left/right prefix/suffix/numeral expansion with punctuation/linkage constraints) are easy to implement and reason about, and they directly operationalize the observed short length and small-vocabulary properties of time expressions.
- Strong recall and competitive F1 across domains. SynTime achieves the best recalls on all three datasets and notably high F1 on Tweets. This supports the claim that a small, high-coverage token inventory combined with boundary heuristics can be robust.
- Sensible use of type abstractions for generalization. Collapsing surface variation into types (e.g., MONTH NUMERAL COMMA YEAR) addresses some of the brittleness that hurts token- or character-level features, and the position-sensitive modifier types reduce rule combinatorics.
- Practicality and extensibility. The expansion story is plausible: the number of additions required per domain is small, and the system remains interpretable and maintainable. The approach is attractive for settings where normalization is handled elsewhere and where realtime extraction is needed.
- Empirical analysis is useful. The cross-corpus statistics (length distribution, coverage by time tokens, POS patterns) are informative and provide actionable design guidance for both rule-based and learning-based approaches.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to SUTime/HeidelTime. SynTime reuses SUTime’s token regex inventory and performs a token-centered boundary expansion with positional constraints. SUTime’s TokensRegex + cascaded FSTs already perform expansion; HeidelTime also uses POS-informed rules. The claimed distinction between “syntactic” vs “semantic” types is not fully convincing: categories like YEAR/MONTH/TIME UNIT/HOLIDAY are primarily semantic, while the truly syntactic part is limited to modifier position (prefix/suffix/linkage). The paper should more clearly demonstrate behaviors not captured by existing systems, beyond anecdotal examples (e.g., “year 1”).
- Potential bias in the Tweets dataset construction. Tweets with time expressions were preselected using SUTime, and only a small sample (500 of 3,058) from the remainder was manually checked, yielding a 3% miss estimate. This selection can bias the distribution of expressions and vocabulary toward SUTime’s coverage and penalize competitors differently. The resulting 3% figure is statistically underpowered and not clearly incorporated into evaluation. This threatens the external validity of the apparently large gains on Tweets.
- Missing ablations and significance tests. There is no analysis of which components matter most (POS gating, left vs right expansion, comma/linkage handling, treatment of dependent segments). There are no statistical significance tests on F1 improvements. Without ablations, it is difficult to assess whether the gains stem mainly from token coverage (reused from SUTime), from the positional heuristics, or from domain-specific additions.
- Experimental clarity and fairness gaps. Details about baseline configurations (resource versions, domain tuning, whether HeidelTime was tuned for English as in prior work) are limited. For Tweets, the use of the Stanford POS tagger is suboptimal; a Twitter-specific tokenizer/POS tagger would likely help both SynTime and learning baselines. There is no comparison to modern neural sequence taggers (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF or transformer-based span models), which are standard for sequence labeling and could serve as strong baselines for recognition-only evaluation.
- Claims about efficiency and generality are not substantiated. The paper asserts “real-time” performance but provides no runtime or memory benchmarks. The generalization story relies on manual expansion, yet there is no quantitative analysis of annotation effort vs gain (beyond counts of added tokens), or a principled strategy for avoiding ambiguity when adding forms (especially in noisy text).

Questions to authors
- Tweets dataset construction: Please clarify the full pipeline. Did you include in the final annotated Tweets set only those 942 tweets that SUTime flagged as containing time expressions? If so, how do you mitigate selection bias? Will you release the tweets, offsets, and guidelines to enable replication?
- Baselines: Which versions and resources were used for HeidelTime and SUTime? Were any parameters tuned on the target domains (as in published configurations)? For UWTime, what training details (hyperparameters, training sizes) and preprocessing were used?
- Preprocessing for Tweets: Did you consider a Twitter-specific tokenizer and POS tagger? If not, can you report performance changes when substituting a Twitter POS tagger and tokenization?
- Ablation: Can you report ablations isolating (a) token identification only, (b) + left expansion, (c) + right expansion, (d) + comma/linkage rules, and (e) without POS disambiguation? This would help assess where gains arise.
- Resources and additions: How many SUTime token regexes were reused verbatim, and how many were modified? Can you provide the exact lists of added tokens/modifiers per dataset and examples where they helped?
- Efficiency: Please provide throughput (docs/sec) and memory usage on the three corpora, and contrast with SUTime/HeidelTime.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider reporting error analyses by category (false merges across clauses, missed descriptive expressions, numeral ambiguities) and by token type, to make the limitations concrete.
- Report statistical significance for F1 differences.
- If normalization is out of scope, clarify how your span extents align with TimeML conventions (e.g., excluding prepositions), and whether evaluation scripts were adjusted accordingly.
- If possible, add a modern neural sequence-labeling baseline for recognition-only, to contextualize the advantage of a lightweight rule-based approach.
