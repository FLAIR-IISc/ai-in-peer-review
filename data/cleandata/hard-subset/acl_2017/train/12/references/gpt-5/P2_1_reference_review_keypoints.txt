- Summary: Proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based timex recognizer that groups token-level patterns into syntactic types and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics; initialized from SUTime and evaluated on TimeBank, WikiWars, and an annotated Tweets set with strong recall and competitive F1.

- Contribution 1: Presents a streamlined, type-based, rule-driven recognizer with simple merge heuristics that is interpretable, efficient, and shows strong cross-domain recall, especially on tweets.

- Contribution 2: Provides a cross-corpus empirical analysis of timexes (length, lexicon size, presence of time tokens, POS tendencies) motivating the design.

- Contribution 3: Introduces a manually annotated tweet dataset (with selection caveats) and corresponding evaluation.

- Strength: Achieves high recall (>92% relaxed) and top/competitive F1 across datasets, with particularly large gains on noisy tweet data.

- Strength: Lightweight, interpretable, and extensible system using compact token regexes and a few merging heuristics that are easy to audit and adapt.

- Strength: Clear, data-driven motivation linking corpus statistics to system design (identify time token then expand).

- Strength: Demonstrates that modest domain-specific token additions improve recall (SynTime-I vs SynTime-E).

- Weakness: Limited novelty over existing rule-based taggers (SUTime/HeidelTime); design resembles prior positional/grammar rules and reuses SUTime tokens.

- Weakness: Tweet dataset construction is biased by pre-filtering with SUTime, risking selection bias that may inflate performance estimates.

- Weakness: Baselines and reporting are incomplete or misaligned (imported numbers, unclear versions/settings), lacking significance tests and comparisons to modern neural taggers.

- Weakness: Reproducibility gaps: full regex lists, lexicon, rule set, and code/dataset release are not provided; heuristics are informally specified.

- Weakness: Limited scope and generality: focuses on recognition not normalization, follows TimeML conventions that may mismatch some corpora (e.g., preposition handling in WikiWars), and theoretical claims are weakly substantiated.

- Question: Will the authors release code, full SynTime rules, and the annotated Tweets dataset with guidelines to enable reproducibility?

- Question: Can the authors provide an independently annotated random tweet sample (not pre-filtered by SUTime) and report results on it?

- Question: What versions/configurations of HeidelTime and SUTime were used, and were domain-specific resources enabled for fairness?

- Question: How sensitive is SynTime to POS tagging errors in noisy text, and were Twitter-specific POS taggers evaluated?

- Question: Can the authors provide detailed ablations for syntactic vs semantic typing, each merge heuristic, and POS constraints?

- Question: Have the authors compared SynTime to contemporary neural sequence-labeling baselines (BiLSTM-CRF, BERT) or can they justify their omission?

- Overall assessment: SynTime is a simple, effective, and practical reorganization of rule-based timex recognition with strong recall, but novelty is modest and experimental, baseline, and reproducibility issues must be addressed for it to be a complete contribution.
