Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, rule-based time expression recognizer (SynTime) that uses a small set of “syntactic” token types (time tokens, modifiers by relative position, numerals) and a boundary-expansion heuristic to form spans. Initialization reuses SUTime’s token regexes; optional manual expansion is allowed.
- An empirical analysis across four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) showing: (i) time expressions are short, (ii) a small vocabulary covers most occurrences, (iii) >93% contain at least one “time token,” and (iv) POS alone doesn’t separate time expressions from non-temporal spans but helps within-span roles.
- An evaluation on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets set, comparing against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, showing high recall and strong F1 on TimeBank and Tweets; competitive on WikiWars.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and recall. On TimeBank, SynTime-I achieves 92.09 F1 (strict) and 94.96 F1 (relaxed), exceeding HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime in Table 4. On Tweets, SynTime-I reaches 91.74 (strict) and 95.87 (relaxed), with best recall across all datasets. The recall advantage aligns with the observation that most expressions contain time tokens, suggesting the approach is effective at high coverage.
- Practical, lightweight method with minimal supervision. The approach runs without training and uses a small, understandable rule set. Manual expansion adds only a handful of tokens/modifiers per domain (Table 5), supporting the claim that the vocabulary is compact and domain adaptation cost is low.
- Clear, testable design hypothesis tied to corpus evidence. The boundary-expansion heuristic and the focus on time tokens are directly motivated by the corpus observations (short spans, token prevalence). The analysis is simple but actionable, and the system design follows from it.
- Broad, relevant baselines and metrics. The paper evaluates against two strong rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a learned system (UWTime), and reports both strict and relaxed TempEval metrics, which helps situate results within prior work.
- Domain diversity including social media. The Tweets evaluation fills a gap where many systems struggle; the reported gains there are particularly compelling for practitioners dealing with noisy text.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to SUTime and similar rule-based systems. The core ideas—token-level regex detection, chunk expansion, and rule-based merging—are close to SUTime’s cascade. Although the paper frames types as “syntactic,” the main token types (YEAR, MONTH, DATE, etc.) are semantic categories (even borrowed from JodaTime). The novelty seems to rest mainly on (i) classifying modifiers by surface position and (ii) simpler, more flexible boundary-expansion rules. This is incremental; a crisper differentiation (and ablation) is needed to establish conceptual novelty.
- Incomplete methodological detail and lack of ablations. The boundary-expansion and merging rules are described informally, with several special cases (commas, linkages, dependent segments), but no full specification or pseudocode. There is no ablation to quantify the contribution of: token identification vs. boundary rules; “syntactic” modifier types vs. a simpler positional heuristic; or the added lexicon in SynTime-E. Without this, it’s hard to attribute where the gains come from.
- Potential bias and limited rigor in Tweets dataset construction. Tweets were pre-filtered using SUTime to contain at least one time expression; only a small sample of the remainder was annotated to estimate miss rate. This introduces selection bias toward SUTime-positive tweets and could distort comparative precision/recall (even if the miss rate is estimated low). A more principled construction (random sampling and exhaustive annotation) is needed, or at least a careful bias analysis.
- Fairness and reproducibility concerns. SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes, making comparisons partly dependent on SUTime resources. It’s unclear whether code and the added lexicons are released. Several baseline numbers are taken from prior papers, which may not match this paper’s exact setup or preprocessing (POS tagger version, tokenization). No statistical significance tests are reported. The real-time claim is not supported by runtime measurements.
- Theoretical claims (principle of least effort) are suggestive but underdeveloped. While the descriptive stats are interesting, the link to Zipf’s principle is asserted rather than tested; a more rigorous analysis (e.g., cross-corpus statistical tests, cross-lingual validation) would make this claim more than an anecdotal framing.

Questions to authors
- Novelty vs. SUTime: Concretely, how does SynTime’s boundary-expansion differ from SUTime’s cascaded finite-state rules? Can you provide side-by-side examples where SUTime fails due to “fixed rules” but SynTime succeeds due to flexibility?
- Formalization/reproducibility: Can you provide a full algorithmic specification (or code) of the boundary-expansion and merging logic, including handling of punctuation, prepositions, overlapping spans, and dependent segments?
- Ablation: What is the performance if (a) you remove modifier position types and keep only token detection plus symmetric expansion; (b) you initialize without SUTime token regexes; (c) you disable added lexicon (SynTime-E) per dataset?
- Tweets dataset: Please clarify the selection pipeline and potential bias from SUTime-based prefiltering. Can you release a truly random, fully annotated subset, or re-evaluate on an existing Twitter temporal corpus to validate results?
- Significance and efficiency: Are the reported improvements statistically significant? What are the runtime and memory compared to SUTime and HeidelTime on the evaluated corpora?
- Scope: Since WikiWars includes prepositions in spans, can SynTime be configured to match that annotation style? How sensitive are results to the TimeML-style exclusion of prepositions?

Overall assessment
- Soundness: Method is sound and practically useful but underspecified in places; evaluation is broadly convincing but weakened by the Tweets selection procedure and lack of ablations/significance tests.
- Novelty: Incremental over established rule-based systems; clearer differentiation is needed.
- Impact: Potentially valuable for practitioners needing a simple, high-recall recognizer, especially on social media; impact improves with released code/resources.

Recommendation: Borderline reject without stronger novelty evidence and cleaner evaluation; could become a solid accept with clarified algorithm, ablations, unbiased Twitter evaluation, significance tests, and released code/data.
