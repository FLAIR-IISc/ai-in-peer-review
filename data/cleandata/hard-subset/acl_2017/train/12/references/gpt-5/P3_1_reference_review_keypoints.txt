- Proposes SynTime: a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer using a syntactic type system and boundary-expansion heuristics.
- Corpus analysis findings motivating design: time expressions are short, a small token vocabulary covers most cases, ≥93% contain a time token, and constituents show similar POS behavior.
- Main contributions: simple type-based rule framework with strong recall, cross-corpus empirical analysis, and a small domain-adaptation (“expansion”) recipe plus a tweets annotation effort.
- Strong performance: high recall (>92% relaxed) and competitive or state-of-the-art F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, with few added domain tokens.
- Linguistically motivated, compact design: time-token/modifier/number types and boundary-expansion rules are intuitive and justified by corpus observations.
- Cross-domain robustness: effective on news, encyclopedic text, and especially noisy social media (tweets).
- Minimal supervised training required and easy portability via small lexicon additions.
- Transparent baseline comparisons including HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.
- Limited novelty relative to SUTime/HeidelTime; proposed distinctions and heuristics seem incremental.
- Tweets evaluation may be biased: dataset pre-filtered by SUTime, selective annotation, missing inter-annotator agreement, and unclear release plans.
- Potential unfair advantage from initializing lexicon with SUTime regexes; needs controlled comparison or ablation.
- Missing statistical tests and ablation studies to isolate which components drive gains; runtime claims lack measurements.
- Reproducibility gaps: incomplete POS constraints, full token lists, detailed merge heuristics, and unclear code/lexicon release.
- Scope limitations: focuses only on recognition (not normalization), issues with span policy for prepositions (WikiWars), and limited empirical development of broader claims.
- Question on SUTime overlap and fairness: which SUTime version/rules were used and how much lexicon overlap exists?
- Question on tweets dataset: sampling/annotation procedure, inter-annotator agreement, release plans, and evaluation on an unbiased sample.
- Question on POS constraints: provide POS-to-type mappings, ambiguity resolution rules (e.g., “May”), and sensitivity to POS errors.
- Request for ablations and significance testing: no POS filtering, no merge-over-comma, no dependent segments, no expansion, and statistical significance.
- Request for runtime and scalability metrics: wall-clock throughput and memory vs SUTime/HeidelTime.
- Question on normalization and span policy: plans to include prepositions when required, adaptation strategy, and any preliminary normalization accuracy.
