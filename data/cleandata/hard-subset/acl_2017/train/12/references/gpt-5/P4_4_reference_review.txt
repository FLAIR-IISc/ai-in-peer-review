Summary
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition that uses a syntactic type inventory and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors analyze four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) to motivate design choices: time expressions are short, rely on a small lexicon, almost always contain at least one “time token,” and exhibit consistent POS patterns. SynTime initializes its lexicon from SUTime’s token regexes, defines syntactic types for time tokens, modifiers (categorized by position), and numerals, and then identifies time segments via left/right expansion rules around detected time tokens. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset compare SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, reporting strong recall and state-of-the-art F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, and competitive results on WikiWars.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, type-based rule system for time expression recognition that emphasizes modifier categories by syntactic position and boundary-expansion heuristics, yielding consistently high recall and strong overall F1 across domains, especially on tweets.
2) A cross-corpus descriptive analysis of time expressions (length distributions, lexicon size, and POS patterns) that supports the design of a compact rule system.
3) A practical demonstration that a minimal, easily extensible resource, initialized from existing token lexicons and with modest manual expansion, can perform competitively with heavier rule systems and learned parsers.

Strengths
- High recall and strong F1 across datasets: SynTime(-I/-E) achieves the best recalls on all three datasets and top F1 on TimeBank and Tweets (Table 4). The relaxed-match recalls above 92% confirm that the token-centric approach captures the majority of mentions, matching the Observation 3 finding that >93% of expressions contain a time token.
- Simplicity and transparency: The system uses a small number of interpretable types and straightforward expansion rules. The core algorithm (Sections 4.2.1–4.2.3, Fig. 3) is clearly specified and easy to reimplement, making the contribution practical and reproducible in principle.
- Cross-domain robustness with minimal effort: The expansion step adds few tokens for TimeBank/Tweets (Table 5) while maintaining high performance. This supports the claim that time expressions draw on a small lexicon and that small, domain-specific additions suffice.
- Clear description and qualitative motivation: The analysis (Section 3) is explicit about length distributions, token coverage, and POS tendencies (Tables 2–3), and ties these to design decisions (identify time tokens first, then expand). The constraints on merges (comma and linkage handling) are precise.
- Sensible scope: The paper explicitly targets recognition only, avoiding confounds with normalization. Evaluation follows TempEval-3 metrics and toolkit.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule systems: The type inventory for time tokens (e.g., MONTH, YEAR, DATE) is semantic in nature and closely mirrors existing resources (e.g., SUTime, JodaTime classes). The main differences are (a) positional modifier types and (b) a boundary-expansion procedure, both of which resemble chunk expansion in prior systems (e.g., SUTime’s cascaded regex over tokens). An ablation is needed to isolate what is new and what drives gains.
- Dependence on SUTime resources and potential evaluation bias on Tweets: SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes. The Tweets dataset was constructed by first selecting tweets that SUTime tagged as containing time expressions, then sampling a subset from the remainder to estimate misses (~3%). This selection process can bias the distribution of expressions towards the SUTime token inventory and may underrepresent phenomena SUTime cannot detect. As SynTime shares SUTime’s token lexicon, this raises fairness concerns and may inflate performance estimates.
- Inconsistent and/or under-specified experimental protocol for baselines: Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior work, potentially with different preprocessing and parameter settings (Table 4). Versions and configurations of HeidelTime/SUTime on Tweets are not detailed. UWTime’s training regimen is adjusted (TimeBank+Tweets), but comparable tuning/expansion for rule baselines is unclear. No statistical significance tests are reported despite claims of “significant” improvements.
- Lack of ablations and error analysis: The paper does not quantify the contribution of POS filtering, positional modifier types, or merging rules. No comparison is made to a simpler window-based expansion or to SUTime augmented with the same added tokens. Error analysis is minimal, making it hard to assess failure modes (e.g., WikiWars prepositions, complex ranges).
- Missing runtime and robustness evidence: While claimed to be “real-time” and “extremely light-weight,” no speed benchmarks or resource footprints are provided. The approach relies on POS tagging quality; the choice of tagger for Tweets (standard Stanford POS vs. Twitter-optimized taggers) and sensitivity to tagging errors are not evaluated.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
1) Tweets dataset construction: How many of the 3,058 “negative” tweets were actually false negatives in total (beyond the 500-sample estimate)? Could you provide results on a tweet sample independent of SUTime preselection to remove selection bias?
2) Baselines: Which versions/configurations of HeidelTime and SUTime were used on each dataset, especially Tweets? Were any domain adaptations or custom resources enabled? For UWTime, did you use the authors’ released implementation, and did you tune hyperparameters?
3) Ablations: Can you provide ablations for (a) removing POS constraints, (b) using semantic modifier types instead of positional ones, (c) simpler fixed-window expansions, and (d) SUTime tokens vs. a reduced lexicon? How much does each component contribute to recall/precision?
4) Runtime: What are measured throughput and latency on each dataset (sentences/sec), including POS tagging time? What is the rule set size and memory footprint?
5) Resource release: Do you plan to release SynTime code, rule files, and the annotated Tweets dataset? This would materially impact reproducibility and utility.

Additional Comments
- The analysis is useful but largely confirms known properties (short mentions, small lexicon). Consider citing prior quantitative characterizations, if any, and clarifying how your “time token” definition avoids circularity when measuring “% of mentions containing a time token.”
- WikiWars evaluation may be penalized by TimeML’s exclusion of prepositions; a supplementary evaluation that includes prepositions could clarify upper bounds in that domain.
- Several references have formatting issues (author names, years). Clarify whether some results in Table 4 are directly comparable given differing setups.
