Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based recognizer for time expressions that (i) defines a small set of syntactic “types” (time token, modifier, numeral, with finer-grained subtypes) and (ii) uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble spans around detected time tokens. The system is initialized using token-level regex resources from SUTime and can be “expanded” by manually adding missing lexemes. The authors also analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and report patterns about time expression length, vocabulary size, token presence, and POS distributions. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime (recognition only).

My view of contributions (in order of strength):
1) A simple, high-recall, rule-based time-expression recognizer with heuristic boundary expansion and competitive F1 on TimeBank and Tweets.
2) An empirical analysis of time-expression statistics across four corpora (shortness, limited lexicon, token presence, within-span POS regularities) that motivates the design.
3) A small, manually annotated tweets dataset and experiments suggesting strong performance in informal text.

Strengths
- High recall and competitive F1 on standard corpora: SynTime achieves strong recall (>92% relaxed on all datasets) and best or competitive F1 on TimeBank and Tweets in both strict and relaxed metrics. The performance on Tweets is particularly strong, which is valuable for noisy text settings.

- Simplicity and efficiency: The method is extremely lightweight (regex + POS constraints + local boundary expansion), requires no training, and appears easy to maintain/extend. This is practical for latency-sensitive and resource-constrained scenarios.

- Sensible design grounded in corpus observations: The empirical findings (most spans are short; small lexicon; most spans contain a time token; within-span POS regularities) provide a clear rationale for focusing on token-first detection plus local modifier/numeral aggregation.

- Domain adaptability with limited manual effort: The “expansion” step demonstrates that modest, curated additions can improve recall in domain-specific corpora (WikiWars). The reported numbers added are small relative to total vocabulary and show decent portability.

- Clear heuristic rules and error modes: The paper describes concrete, testable rules (e.g., left/right expansion; handling of comma/linkage; dependent segments like “8 to … days”), which makes the approach interpretable and potentially useful as a baseline or component in larger systems.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty vs. existing rule-based systems: The “type-based” framing and boundary expansion closely resemble prior systems (e.g., SUTime’s cascaded token regex and chunk expansion; HeidelTime’s lexicons + POS constraints). SynTime’s lexicon is initialized from SUTime, and the proposed types (time token/modifier/numeral with subtypes) largely map to categories used in the literature. The methodological novelty over prior rule systems is insufficiently articulated and empirically disentangled.

- Potential bias and methodological issues in the Tweets dataset: Tweets were first filtered by SUTime, and only a small sample of the “negatives” was manually checked. This likely biases the dataset composition and the distribution of expressions toward those detectable by SUTime’s lexicon, which SynTime reuses. The claim that SUTime misses ~3% is based on 500 sampled “negatives” with only 15 positives—too small for a robust estimate—and the final dataset contains only tweets with at least one time expression, limiting generalization and making precision estimates sensitive to selection bias.

- Incomplete and uneven evaluation: The paper evaluates recognition only, while TempEval emphasizes normalization as well; many applications require both. Baseline numbers are partly borrowed from prior papers rather than re-run under identical conditions/splits, risking configuration mismatches (e.g., DCT handling, language resources, genre settings). Details on baseline configurations (HeidelTime/SUTime options, models for Twitter), split protocols, and statistical significance are missing.

- Insufficient methodological detail and ablations: The exact token/type inventories (e.g., membership of the 15 time token types, what “TIMELINE,” “DAY TIME,” etc. include) are not listed, hampering reproducibility. The effect of POS constraints, of different POS taggers (news vs. Twitter), and of the merging heuristics are not ablated. Edge cases (coordination, discontinuous spans, nested expressions, “between … and …”, “every second Tuesday of the month”, prepositions) are under-specified. The exclusion of prepositions to follow TimeML may reduce generality (and likely contributes to lower WikiWars scores).

- Writing, formatting, and citation quality: Numerous typos, grammar errors, inconsistent diacritics (Strotgen/Strötgen), malformed references (years with “?”, stray punctuation), and missing figure/table details undermine clarity. Some tables/figures are referenced but not fully described; POS distribution stats are discussed but not tabulated. Claims such as “runs in real time” lack empirical runtime benchmarks. Reproducibility is unclear: no code/data release for SynTime or the Tweets dataset is stated.

Questions to authors
1) Novelty and relation to SUTime/HeidelTime: Please concretely differentiate SynTime from SUTime beyond terminology. What can SynTime recognize that SUTime and HeidelTime fundamentally cannot (with examples and counts)? Can you provide ablations showing the incremental gains due to your expansion/merging rules on top of the SUTime-initialized lexicon?

2) Tweets dataset construction: How many tweets in total truly contain time expressions in the unfiltered 4k sample (with full manual annotation)? Can you report results on an unbiased sample (no SUTime prefilter) and/or release the dataset with negatives so precision is meaningful?

3) Baseline configurations and fairness: Did you re-run HeidelTime and SUTime on your exact splits with comparable settings (e.g., DCT, language/genre configs)? Which Stanford POS tagger/model was used (news vs. Twitter) and for which datasets? How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors on tweets?

4) Reproducibility: Will you release the full type inventories (token lists per type), code, and the added expansions per dataset? Without the exact lexicon and rules, it is difficult to verify or build on your work.

5) Coverage and error analysis: Please provide a detailed error analysis (top false positives/negatives by pattern) and report coverage of each type across datasets. How does SynTime handle coordinated/discontinuous expressions and complex patterns (“between 3 and 5 pm”, “every other week starting next Monday”)?

6) Runtime: Please provide latency and throughput measurements vs. SUTime/HeidelTime on the three corpora to substantiate the “real time” claim.

7) Normalization: Although out of scope, can you comment on how well SynTime’s segmentations align with standard normalization pipelines, and whether recognition choices (e.g., excluding prepositions) hinder normalization quality?
