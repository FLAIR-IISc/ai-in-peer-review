Summary and contributions
- A lightweight, type-based rule framework (SynTime) for time expression recognition that defines syntactic token types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time expressions. The method is positioned as a syntactic alternative to existing semantics-driven rule systems.
- An empirical analysis across four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) yielding compact observations (short spans, small vocabulary, near-universal presence of a time token, and consistent syntactic behavior), which directly inform the design and explain SynTime’s high recall.
- Strong empirical results on TimeBank and Tweets (state-of-the-art or better F1; consistently highest recall on all datasets), with minimal resources and real-time speed; plus a simple, low-cost expansion protocol to adapt to new domains.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with a fresh angle: Recasting timex rules around syntactic types and boundary expansion is a clean, general lens. Compared to classic finite-state cascades, the “type + expansion” view is easy to reason about, flexible in span assembly (adjacent/overlapping merging), and is likely to be robust and maintainable in practice.
- Strong results with minimal machinery: SynTime-I (without expansion) already outperforms HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and Tweets (e.g., strict F1 92.09 on TimeBank and 91.74 on Tweets; relaxed recall ≥95% on both), and is comparable on WikiWars. This supports the core claim independent of expansion. Consistently highest recalls across all datasets suggest the type inventory covers most productive forms (matching Observation 3).
- Lightweight, real-time, and extensible: The expansion procedure adds only a handful of tokens/modifiers per domain (Table 5) and yields further accuracy on WikiWars, illustrating low human cost and practical portability. This stands in contrast to learned models needing labeled data and feature engineering, and to large handcrafted rule inventories.
- Data-driven observations that generalize intuitions: The cross-corpus analysis is valuable on its own. The “least effort” perspective neatly rationalizes why a small lexicon and short patterns suffice and why a type-first strategy should work well—particularly on noisy text like tweets, where SynTime excels.
- Clear articulation of rules and types: The paper specifies type inventories and merging heuristics clearly, enabling replication and inspection. This transparency is an asset compared to opaque pipelines and large rule sets.

Weaknesses
- Risk of test-set leakage in expansion: SynTime-E adds tokens/modifiers per dataset, but for TimeBank and WikiWars there is no stated train/test split. If additions were derived from the same test corpora, SynTime-E results are inflated. While SynTime-I is strong and supports the core claim, the SynTime-E gains (especially on WikiWars) need a clean protocol (e.g., cross-validation or domain-held-out lexicon construction).
- Novelty relative to SUTime may be narrower than claimed: SUTime also starts from token regexes and expands to chunks/expressions via cascades. The distinction between “semantic types” vs “syntactic types,” and between “fixed rules” vs “heuristic boundary expansion,” feels incremental without stronger ablation. A head-to-head analysis showing that the re-typing and boundary heuristics, not lexicon differences, drive gains would strengthen the novelty claim.
- Dependence on POS tagging with limited robustness analysis: The method relies on POS to disambiguate tokens like May and to find modifiers/numerals. On tweets, general-purpose POS taggers can be brittle; the paper acknowledges errors but does not quantify their impact or test tweet-specific taggers. An ablation (no POS vs POS; standard vs tweet POS) would clarify robustness.
- Evaluation protocol and comparability: Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers, others are re-run, which can introduce inconsistencies. It is unclear whether HeidelTime/SUTime were tuned; SUTime was also used to pre-filter tweets (even if a 3% miss rate is estimated), which can bias the sample distribution. Additional details (versions, parameters, consistent re-evaluation) are needed for fair comparison.
- Scope limited to recognition: Normalization is deferred, yet many applications and TempEval tasks hinge on normalization quality. While not fatal, it reduces end-to-end utility; at least a minimal normalization experiment or a plug-in demonstration would make the contribution more complete.

Questions to authors
- Expansion protocol: For TimeBank and WikiWars, were added tokens/modifiers derived from the same evaluation corpora? If so, can you provide results where additions are learned from a separate corpus (or via cross-validation) to avoid leakage?
- Ablation for novelty: Can you report results controlling for lexicon (e.g., using exactly the SUTime lexicon in both systems), to isolate the effect of your syntactic typing and boundary rules? Similarly, can you show performance when treating SUTime’s categories as “syntactic” vs “semantic” to clarify the practical difference?
- POS robustness: Which POS tagger was used for tweets? Can you quantify performance changes with a tweet-optimized tagger or with relaxed POS constraints? How often do POS errors cause missed or spurious timexes?
- Baseline parity: What versions and configurations of HeidelTime/SUTime were used? Were domain settings (e.g., news vs wiki vs social media) matched? Can you re-run all baselines under a unified protocol rather than mixing published numbers?
- Reproducibility and resources: Will you release SynTime code, the type inventories, and the tweet annotations? This would significantly increase impact.

Overall, I find the reframing around syntactic types and simple boundary-expansion heuristics both elegant and pragmatically impactful. Despite concerns about expansion protocol and novelty relative to SUTime, the strong SynTime-I results and the clarity/simplicity of the approach make this a compelling contribution.
