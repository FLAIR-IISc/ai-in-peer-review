Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that relies on a small set of syntactic token types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors first analyze time expressions in TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and a newly annotated Tweets dataset, observing that most time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, almost always contain at least one “time token,” and exhibit similar syntactic behavior. SynTime initializes its token inventory from SUTime’s token regexes and disambiguates with POS tags. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; SynTime reports best F1 on TimeBank and Tweets and comparable results on WikiWars, with notably high recall.

As I see them, the contributions (ordered by strength) are:
1) A simple, type-based heuristic tagger that achieves strong performance and very high recall across multiple datasets, especially on Tweets, while remaining lightweight and fast.
2) A practical analysis of time expressions across domains highlighting short lengths, small vocabulary, frequent presence of time tokens, and consistent syntactic patterns, used to motivate the design.
3) A manually annotated tweets dataset and an empirical comparison against strong baselines across three datasets.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime consistently achieves the best or second-best F1 across datasets and the highest recall in relaxed match on all datasets, with particularly impressive gains on Tweets. The approach seems effective at capturing varied surface forms without heavy engineering.
2) Simplicity and clarity of approach: Defining a small number of syntactic types and using boundary expansion is easy to understand, implement, and extend. The system’s initialization from token regexes and minimal expansion aligns with the observations (small vocabularies).
3) Practical domain adaptability: The expansion step adds only a handful of tokens/modifiers per dataset, yet yields measurable gains (notably on WikiWars). This supports the claim that domain adaptation costs are low.
4) Useful cross-domain analysis: The paper provides concrete statistics on length distributions, token coverage, and POS tendencies across disparate sources (news, Wikipedia, tweets), reinforcing design decisions and offering insights that may transfer to other tasks.
5) Competitive against strong baselines: Comparisons to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime (trained where applicable) demonstrate the viability of a lightweight rule-based method relative to both hand-engineered and learned systems.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to SUTime and other rule-based systems: The system heavily reuses SUTime’s token regexes and differs mainly in the organization of “types” and the form of boundary-expansion heuristics. The analysis (short, token-limited, POS-regular expressions) largely confirms well-known properties exploited by existing taggers. The conceptual contribution feels incremental.
2) Evaluation and reproducibility gaps: Important experimental details are missing. There is no code release, no full specification of the token/type inventories beyond counts, and no explicit rule lists. Baseline configurations (versions, language resources, domain settings) are not described in enough detail to ensure fairness. No statistical significance tests are reported. The “real-time” claim lacks runtime measurements.
3) Potential bias in the Tweets dataset construction and limited annotation reporting: Tweets were prefiltered using SUTime; while the authors estimate only ~3% missed expressions, this pipeline can bias the dataset composition and baseline comparisons. Inter-annotator agreement, adjudication protocol, and detailed annotation guidelines are not reported. This weakens the reliability of the large reported gains on Tweets.
4) Missing ablations and error analysis: There is no ablation on key components (e.g., POS disambiguation, modifier/NUMERAL handling, linkage/comma rules, or the effect of the SUTime-initialized lexicon). Error analysis across categories (e.g., relative vs absolute dates, multi-span expressions, descriptive forms) and by dataset would clarify strengths and failure modes.
5) Scope restricted to recognition without normalization and limited discussion of preposition policy: The choice to exclude prepositions following TimeML conventions seems to hurt on WikiWars; exploring this or analyzing its impact more deeply would be valuable. While recognition-only is a valid scope, normalization is central in temporal extraction benchmarks, and lack of integration limits end-to-end utility.

Questions to authors
- What exact versions and configurations of HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime were used? Were any domain-specific resources enabled (e.g., colloquial settings for tweets)? How did you ensure comparable tokenization/POS across systems?
- Can you release the full SynTime resources (token/type regexes, rules) and code? If not, can you provide a deterministic, complete description sufficient for reproduction?
- Please report runtime and memory usage to substantiate “real-time.”
- How many rules are there, and how complex are they? A small catalog with examples would help assess novelty vs SUTime’s cascades.
- Provide ablations: (a) without POS disambiguation; (b) without modifier/NUMERAL expansion; (c) without LINKAGE/COMMA merging; (d) without expansion (I vs E is partially shown); and (e) using a different initialization than SUTime to assess dependence.
- Tweets dataset: report inter-annotator agreement, guideline deviations from TimeML, and evidence that SUTime-based prefiltering does not bias the test set. Can you validate on a truly random tweet sample (without prefiltering) to corroborate the 3% miss estimate?
- Do results hold against stronger modern neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, transformer-based sequence labeling) trained for recognition only?

Additional comments
- Clarify the modifier taxonomy (you mention 5 types, but later detail 3 plus 2 special types).
- Several references are incomplete or malformed; please correct formatting and citations.
- Consider discussing how to extend SynTime cross-lingually and what changes to the type inventory would be required.

Overall recommendation
The paper presents a practical and effective system with solid empirical results, but the methodological novelty over established rule-based systems appears limited, and the evaluation lacks important details for reproducibility and fairness. I encourage a revision that strengthens novelty claims, adds ablations and error analysis, clarifies baseline settings, reports IAA and runtime, and releases code/resources. With these improvements, the work could make a stronger case for acceptance.
