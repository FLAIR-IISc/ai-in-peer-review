Summary and contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer built around a syntactic type system (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors support the design with an analysis of four corpora, observing that time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic behaviors. SynTime is initialized with token regexes (largely from SUTime), applies POS-aware token identification, expands locally to form time segments, and merges adjacent/overlapping segments with simple rules. On TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually labeled Tweets set, SynTime achieves high recall and competitive-to-strong F1, especially on tweets.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A clean, syntactic-type-driven rule design with simple, position-based boundary expansion that achieves notably high recall and strong F1 with very lightweight implementation and real-time speed.
2) An empirical cross-dataset analysis of time expressions (length, vocabulary size, token coverage, syntactic behavior) that motivates the design and links to the principle of least effort.
3) A manually annotated tweet test set for temporal expression recognition (value contingent on release).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and re-framing of rule design: The move from semantic classes to syntactic types plus boundary-expansion heuristics is a neat, broadly applicable abstraction. It reduces rule brittleness (fixed-length/position patterns) and appears to generalize across domains. This is a bold, minimalist design decision compared to more baroque rule cascades.

- Strong recall and competitive SOTA performance: SynTime achieves the best recalls on all three datasets and the best or comparable F1 on TimeBank and Tweets (e.g., strict F1 91.74–91.77 on Tweets). The results support the claim that targeting time tokens first and expanding by position captures the bulk of mentions.

- Real-time, lightweight system with minimal manual effort: Initialization via harvested token regexes and a small number of manual expansions (e.g., only 3–16 time tokens and 2–19 modifiers added) is attractive. This makes SynTime practical for low-resource or streaming settings.

- Domain robustness and extensibility: The heuristic expansion strategy and syntactic type definitions lend themselves to easy domain adaptation. The improvement on WikiWars after modest expansion supports this.

- Useful empirical observations: The corpus analysis (length distributions, token coverage, POS tendencies) is informative for the community and likely to transfer to other languages/domains, aligning with a human-effort perspective.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate relative to prior rule-based systems: While the syntactic-type framing is interesting, SUTime and HeidelTime already expand from token cues, use POS, and merge spans. The boundary-expansion rules here are simpler rather than fundamentally new. The contribution risks being viewed as a more elegant engineering of existing ideas rather than a new modeling concept.

- Tweet dataset construction may bias evaluation: The tweet set was constructed by filtering 4k tweets with SUTime to retain 942 containing time expressions, then annotating those; only a small sample of negatives was annotated. This likely biases the test distribution toward SUTime-detectable expressions and under-represents misses/edge cases. A fully independent sample with exhaustive annotation (positives and negatives) is needed for fair comparison and recall estimation.

- Partial task coverage and limited diagnostic analysis: The work focuses on recognition only, leaving normalization aside, which limits practical impact relative to TempEval tasks. There is no ablation to quantify the impact of POS filtering, each modifier type, linkage/comma rules, or expansion depth limits. Error analyses are minimal, especially for hard cases (ambiguous tokens, nested/relative times, prepositional structures).

- Baseline comparability and resource reuse: SynTime initialization uses SUTime’s token regexes, yet comparisons to SUTime are reported without clarifying versioning, configuration, or whether SUTime was tuned similarly for Twitter. Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers while others are rerun, which can confound fairness. Please unify experimental conditions.

- Overreliance on POS accuracy and TimeML choices: The approach assumes good tokenization and POS; tweets violate this frequently. Also, excluding many prepositions per TimeML reduces coverage for corpora like WikiWars where such tokens are included, potentially limiting generality.

Questions to authors
- Dataset: Will you release the annotated tweet dataset (including negatives) and the exact train/test splits? Can you provide an unbiased test set not pre-filtered by SUTime? If not, can you estimate the selection bias’s impact via an additional evaluation?

- Reproducibility: Will you release SynTime code and the exact token/modifier lists for SynTime-I and SynTime-E? How many regexes were imported from SUTime vs. newly added?

- Ablations and diagnostics: 
  - What is the performance without POS filtering? 
  - How sensitive are results to boundary expansion limits (e.g., search window)? 
  - What are the main error categories on each dataset (missed tokens vs. over-merging vs. boundary errors)?

- Speed claims: Can you quantify runtime (docs/sec) and memory footprint versus SUTime/HeidelTime?

- Generalization: Have you tried SynTime on other languages or on domains with more descriptive temporal mentions (e.g., clinical notes)? How would you adapt type definitions there?

Recommendation
Weak accept. While the novelty is moderate, the paper offers a crisp, generalizable perspective on rule design with strong empirical results and high recall, especially on noisy text, and a lightweight, practical system. Addressing dataset bias, baseline parity, and releasing resources would strengthen the contribution.
