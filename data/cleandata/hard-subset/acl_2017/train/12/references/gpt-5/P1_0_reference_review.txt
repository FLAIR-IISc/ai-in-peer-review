Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that organizes lexical items into syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and applies simple heuristic “boundary expansion” rules to assemble time segments into complete mentions. The authors motivate their design via an analysis of four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets), claiming that time expressions are typically very short, use a small vocabulary, almost always contain an explicit time token, and show consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s token regexes, optionally expanded with a few domain-specific additions, and evaluated against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A pragmatic, lightweight rule-based recognizer (SynTime) that attains strong recall and competitive F1 across three datasets, especially on tweets, with simple syntactic typing and boundary-expansion rules.
2) A cross-corpus analysis of time expressions highlighting short length, small vocabulary, and the prevalence of explicit time tokens, offering design guidance for rule systems.
3) Evidence that minimal, targeted lexicon expansion can yield robust domain adaptation (WikiWars) without heavy engineering.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime (both initial and expanded) achieves best or second-best F1 on most settings and the best recall across all datasets and match regimes. The gains on Tweets are substantial (strict F1 ≈ 91.7 vs. 80–89 for baselines; relaxed F1 ≈ 95.9 vs. 86–92), supporting the claim that the system captures short, token-centric informal expressions effectively.
- Simple, transparent rules with clear rationale: The “type + boundary expansion” approach is well motivated by the empirical observations (e.g., >93% mentions contain a time token; expressions are short), and the rules are easy to inspect and extend. This transparency is attractive for real-time or resource-constrained settings and for practitioners who require controllable behavior.
- Minimal supervision/engineering for domain transfer: The expansion step adds very few tokens/modifiers (e.g., 16+19 for WikiWars) yet improves recall notably (WikiWars strict recall +3.25pp; relaxed recall +2.98pp). This suggests a cost-effective path to adapt to domain idiosyncrasies, which is a genuine practical advantage over larger ML setups.
- Sensible, widely used baselines and standard evaluation: Comparing against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime using the TempEval-3 toolkit and both strict/relaxed match provides a familiar and credible evaluation framework.
- Useful corpus-level analysis: Although intuitive, the quantitative confirmation (length distributions, type coverage, POS tendencies) is well presented and aligns with the observed performance characteristics (e.g., why recall is high and tweets are “easy” for SynTime).

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and significant dependence on SUTime resources: SynTime’s token inventory and regexes are taken from SUTime, and the type inventory (e.g., TIME_UNIT, HOLIDAY) is largely semantic in nature despite the “syntactic” framing. The key novelty seems to be the positional modifier types and the boundary-expansion merge rules. This is an incremental contribution relative to established rule-based systems.
- Potential bias in the Tweets dataset construction: Tweets were prefiltered using SUTime to identify tweets with time expressions; only a small random sample from the remainder was annotated to estimate SUTime’s miss rate (~3%). This procedure likely biases the distribution toward SUTime-detectable expressions and may not reflect a truly random sample of time mentions in tweets. Given that SynTime is initialized from SUTime’s lexicon, this may inflate apparent generalization.
- Annotation mismatch and evaluation caveats on WikiWars: The paper notes WikiWars contains more prepositions and descriptive expressions, while SynTime follows TimeML/TimeBank guidelines that exclude many prepositions. This guideline mismatch can disadvantage SynTime (and others) under the WikiWars gold and complicates interpretation of strict-match results. A clearer reconciliation (e.g., adapting rules to WikiWars annotation) or a per-phenomenon breakdown would strengthen claims.
- Lack of ablations and significance testing: It is hard to attribute gains to specific design choices. For example, what is the impact of the POS-based filtering? Of the specific merge heuristics? How much does expansion contribute per dataset? No significance tests are reported for differences across systems, which are sometimes small.
- Claims about efficiency and “real-time” not substantiated: There are no runtime or memory measurements, nor throughput comparisons with HeidelTime/SUTime/UWTime. Given the practical emphasis, quantitative efficiency data are important.
- Presentation quality and reproducibility: The paper has numerous typos and citation formatting issues. Core resources (code, rules, added lexicon lists) are not provided, limiting reproducibility and adoption.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify runtime and memory usage compared to HeidelTime and SUTime on the three datasets? What is throughput (docs/sec) and latency per sentence?
- How sensitive is SynTime to POS errors? Have you tried running without POS filtering or with a different tagger? Please provide an ablation.
- Can you release the exact added tokens/modifiers for SynTime-E per dataset and the final rule set to enable replication?
- For Tweets, can you report results on a truly random sample independent of SUTime filtering, or provide a more thorough estimate of SUTime’s miss rate (beyond 500 sampled tweets), to mitigate selection bias?
- On WikiWars, can you adapt rules to include prepositions per its guidelines and report updated performance, or provide an error breakdown showing which categories account for the gap?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, pragmatic systems paper with convincing empirical performance and a clear, usable design. The novelty over prior rule-based work is modest, and the tweets dataset construction raises concerns. With added rigor (ablations, significance, efficiency data) and improved clarity/reproducibility, this would be a valuable contribution for time expression recognition, especially in informal domains.
