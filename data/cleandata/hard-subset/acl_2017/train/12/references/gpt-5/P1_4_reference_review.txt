Summary and contributions
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that defines a small set of syntactic token types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple, position-based heuristic “boundary expansion” to assemble time segments into time expressions. The authors first analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets set) and observe that time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, typically include at least one time token, and exhibit consistent POS patterns. SynTime is initialized using SUTime’s token regex resources, applies POS gating to disambiguate ambiguous tokens, expands around time tokens to find modifiers/numerals, and merges adjacent/overlapping segments via simple rules. Evaluations on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets compare against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, showing strong recall and high F1, with especially large gains on Tweets.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A very simple, type- and position-aware rule framework for time expression recognition that achieves strong recall and competitive or superior F1 across three datasets, particularly on Tweets.
2) A descriptive cross-corpus analysis quantifying the length, vocabulary size, and POS tendencies of time expressions, which motivates the design (though the “principle of least effort” framing is largely rhetorical).
3) A small manually annotated Tweets dataset and an evaluation showing robustness on informal text.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime-I/E achieve best or second-best F1 on most settings, and highest recall across all datasets (e.g., 95–98% relaxed recall on Tweets). The merging heuristics and boundary expansion seem to recover many mentions that fixed-length/fixed-position rules miss.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The method is light-weight, rule-based, does not require training, and is easy to extend by adding token regexes. This is attractive for real-time or low-resource scenarios and for deployment where learning-based models are impractical.
- Clear, reproducible high-level algorithm: The paper clearly lays out the type inventory, the three-step pipeline (time token identification, segment identification, expression extraction), and the merging conditions (including special cases for comma/linkage), which is helpful for reimplementation.
- Cross-domain evaluation, including informal text: Results on both formal (news, Wikipedia) and informal (tweets) data highlight the robustness of the approach, and the reported gains on Tweets are practically significant for social media applications.
- Insightful qualitative motivation: The analysis (short length, small vocabulary, token presence, syntactic regularity) is consistent with practitioners’ intuition and helps justify the design choice to center rules on time tokens and simple local structure.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems: The notion of defining token categories and expanding around them is central to existing systems (e.g., SUTime’s cascade/FST over token regex, HeidelTime’s resource-driven rules). SynTime’s “syntactic types” vs “semantic types” distinction feels slight, and the core rules (left/right expansion, merging on punctuation/linkage) are incremental rather than conceptually new. Moreover, the system reuses SUTime’s token lexicon, so improvements likely stem from different merging heuristics rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
- Potential bias and weak methodology in Tweets data collection: Tweets with time expressions were first selected using SUTime, then annotated; only a small sample of the remainder was checked to estimate miss rate (~3%). This procedure risks selection bias toward SUTime-detectable expressions, potentially affecting comparative fairness and limiting the representativeness of the test set.
- Baseline comparability and tuning details are unclear: Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers; others are run by the authors. It is not clear that all systems are evaluated under identical preprocessing, tokenization, POS tagging, and dataset splits. For TimeBank and WikiWars, mixing reported numbers can introduce confounds. No evidence is given that baseline rules/resources were tuned for the same annotation conventions (e.g., inclusion/exclusion of prepositions).
- Reproducibility and resource release: The paper does not state whether SynTime code, the exact rule set, and the list of added tokens/modifiers are released. Key implementation details (e.g., the exact POS constraints per type, the regex definitions used/added) are not fully specified, making replication difficult.
- Limited analysis and ablations: There is little error analysis beyond POS tagging errors, and no ablations to quantify the contribution of each heuristic (e.g., merging rules, POS gating, handling of commas/linkage) or of the manual expansions. The “principle of least effort” claim is not operationalized beyond descriptive statistics.

Questions to authors
- Fairness of comparisons: Did you run HeidelTime and SUTime yourselves on the same splits with identical preprocessing and evaluation scripts? If not, can you report results under a fully controlled, uniform setup rather than mixing in numbers from prior papers?
- Tweets data collection: Can you quantify the selection bias introduced by preselecting with SUTime? Would results hold on a randomly sampled tweet test set annotated independently of any system? Can you provide per-type performance on Tweets to show robustness beyond SUTime’s coverage?
- Resources and reproducibility: Will you release the SynTime code, the full rule set (including POS/type constraints), and the exact list of added tokens/modifiers per dataset? Without these, how should others replicate the results?
- Ablations: What is the impact of (a) removing POS gating, (b) disabling specific merging rules (overlap vs adjacency vs comma/linkage), and (c) using only SUTime’s token regex without your merging heuristics? These would help isolate where the gains come from.
- Scope decisions: You exclude many prepositions following TimeML/TimeBank conventions, which hurts on WikiWars. Can your framework be extended to handle such cases without sacrificing precision? What are the trade-offs?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed engineering paper with strong practical results and a clear, simple method, but with limited novelty and some methodological concerns around data collection and baseline comparability. Clarifying fairness, releasing resources, and adding ablations/error analysis would substantially strengthen the submission.
