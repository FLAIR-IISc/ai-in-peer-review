Summary
This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that organizes token-level patterns into syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time spans. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four corpora showing that time expressions are short, use a small lexicon, and almost always contain at least one “time token.” SynTime is initialized with SUTime’s token regexes and augments them manually when needed. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets set compare SynTime to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; SynTime reports strong recall and top or competitive F1, particularly on Tweets.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A streamlined, type-based, rule-driven recognizer with very simple boundary heuristics that achieves high recall and competitive F1 across domains, notably in tweets, while remaining interpretable and efficient.
2) A cross-corpus empirical characterization of time expressions (length distribution, lexicon size, presence of time tokens, POS tendencies) that motivates the design choices.
3) A new manually annotated tweet dataset and corresponding evaluation (with caveats about selection).

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with simple machinery: SynTime (both initial and expanded) attains high recall (>92% relaxed) on all datasets and top F1 on TimeBank and Tweets (strict and relaxed). The large gains on Tweets—where short forms and lexical variety are expected—suggest the type-and-boundary strategy is robust to noise while remaining efficient.
- Lightweight, interpretable, and extensible: The system relies on a compact set of token regexes and a few merging heuristics (prefix/suffix/linkage/comma/article, adjacency/overlap rules). This makes it easy to audit, adapt to new domains by adding a handful of tokens, and deploy at low cost.
- Clear, data-driven motivation: The corpus analysis (short spans, small vocabulary, >93% containing a time token, POS regularities within timexes) is convincing and directly informs the system’s design (identify a time token first, then expand by local modifiers/numerals).
- Sensible cross-domain results and ablation via expansion: The SynTime-I vs SynTime-E comparison demonstrates that modest, domain-specific token additions can improve recall (notably on WikiWars), aligning with the “small lexicon” observation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing rule-based taggers: SUTime and HeidelTime already use token regexes, POS clues, and multi-stage chunking/expansion. SynTime’s “syntactic types” (e.g., prefix/suffix/linkage) and boundary merge rules resemble prior systems’ positional/grammar rules; the conceptual difference (syntactic vs semantic categories) is incremental. Moreover, SynTime is initialized with SUTime’s token set, which blurs the line between novelty and reconfiguration of existing resources.
- Experimental design concerns for Tweets: The tweet dataset is constructed by first filtering 4K tweets with SUTime and keeping only those where SUTime found at least one time expression (then fully re-annotated). This conditioning on SUTime likely biases corpus composition and could affect both recall and precision estimates for all systems. Although the authors sampled 500 of the “negatives” and estimate ~3% missed timexes, this does not remove the selection bias from the main tweet corpus used for evaluation. A fairer design would annotate a random sample independent of any system.
- Incomplete/misaligned baselines and reporting: Some baseline numbers (e.g., for WikiWars) are imported from prior work; splits, versions, and settings may not match the present evaluation, risking apples-to-oranges comparisons. There are no statistical significance tests or confidence intervals. It is unclear whether HeidelTime/SUTime were tuned appropriately for each domain (especially Tweets). The paper also omits comparison to more recent neural sequence-tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BERT-based taggers) that are standard for span recognition tasks.
- Reproducibility and specification gaps: The exact regex lists, expansion lexicon, and full rule set are not provided; code and datasets are not stated as released. The “heuristic” rules are described informally, with several special cases (e.g., comma/linkage merge) but without a complete deterministic specification that others can implement. The manual expansion criteria (“generic,” “no ambiguity”) are subjective; inter-annotator or curation protocols are not described.
- Scope and generality: The system addresses recognition only, not normalization, and adheres to TimeML conventions that exclude most prepositions—hurting performance on corpora like WikiWars where prepositions are often annotated as part of timexes. The “principle of least effort” claim is speculative; while the descriptive statistics are helpful, the theoretical link is weakly substantiated.

Questions to authors
- Can you release the code, the full SynTime rule set (regexes, type inventories, POS constraints, merge rules), and the annotated Tweets dataset with guidelines? Without this, reproducibility is limited.
- How exactly was the Tweets dataset constructed and split? Can you report results on a fully random, independently annotated sample (not pre-filtered by SUTime) to remove selection bias?
- What versions and configurations of HeidelTime and SUTime did you use, and were any domain-specific resources enabled (e.g., colloquial lexicons for Tweets)? If not, can you rerun with appropriate settings to ensure fairness?
- How sensitive is SynTime to POS tagging errors in noisy text? Did you try a Twitter-specific POS tagger, and if so, how did performance change?
- Can you provide ablations quantifying the contribution of (a) syntactic vs semantic typing, (b) each heuristic (e.g., comma vs linkage merging), and (c) POS constraints for ambiguity resolution?
- Have you compared against contemporary neural sequence labeling baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BERT fine-tuned for timex spans)? If not, please include them or justify their omission.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a simple, well-motivated, and practically effective reorganization of rule-based timex recognition that achieves strong recall and competitive F1 with low complexity. However, the methodological novelty over existing rule-based systems is modest, the tweet evaluation is potentially biased, and the baseline and reproducibility gaps limit confidence in the generality of the reported gains. With stronger experimental rigor (unbiased tweet corpus, modern baselines, significance testing), fuller rule specification, and resource release, this would be a solid contribution as a robust, lightweight baseline. As it stands, it is promising but not yet meeting the bar for novelty and experimental thoroughness.
