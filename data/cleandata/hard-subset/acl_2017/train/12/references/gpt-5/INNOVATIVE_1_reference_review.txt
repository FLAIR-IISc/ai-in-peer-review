Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based time expression recognizer that defines syntactic token types (time tokens, modifiers by relative position, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to compose time expressions. The system is initialized from token regexes largely sourced from SUTime, filters with POS tags, and merges adjacent/overlapping segments via a small set of rules (including handling commas/linkage). It targets recognition only (not normalization).
- The authors present a cross-corpus analysis (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) yielding four empirical observations: time expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, almost always contain at least one time token, and show similar internal syntactic behavior. They connect these to the principle of least effort.
- Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a small Twitter dataset show strong recall and competitive-to-better F1 versus HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; especially high performance on Tweets. The approach is zero-training and easily extensible with a handful of domain tokens.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A surprisingly effective, extremely simple rule set for time expression recognition with high recall across domains, especially on informal text, demonstrating that a light syntactic-typing plus boundary-expansion approach can rival established systems.
2) A useful empirical characterization of time expressions across corpora (length, vocabulary size, presence of a time token), which motivates the design and may inform future methods.
3) Practical system-level insights: minimal manual expansion suffices for domain adaptation; the system runs in real time and is interpretable.

Strengths
- Simplicity with strong results: The boundary-expansion heuristic operating over a small syntactic type inventory achieves state-of-the-art or competitive F1 with the best recall on all datasets. The tweets results are particularly compelling and practically relevant.
- Zero training and easy extensibility: SynTime-I performs very well out of the box; SynTime-E adds only a handful of tokens/modifiers yet improves recall on WikiWars. This suggests favorable maintenance and deployment characteristics.
- Clear, interpretable rules: The modifier categories by position (prefix/suffix/linkage) and merge criteria (handling commas/linkers, overlapping segments) are easy to understand, audit, and transfer.
- Empirical analysis that informs design: The four observations are plausible, quantified, and directly connected to the system’s decisions (e.g., prioritize time-token detection and local expansion).
- Competitive baselines and standard evaluation: Comparisons to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and WikiWars with the TempEval toolkit help situate the contribution.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based systems: SUTime and HeidelTime already identify token-level elements and expand to chunks using cascaded automata/fixed rules; several systems use type-like abstractions. The shift from semantic to “syntactic” types and the boundary-expansion heuristic feels like an incremental design change, not a fundamentally new concept or modeling paradigm.
- Heavy reliance on SUTime resources and lack of ablations: SynTime’s lexicon/regexes are sourced from SUTime. It’s unclear how much of the gains come from the new rules vs. the inherited coverage. There is no ablation on (a) without POS filtering, (b) without the special comma/linkage rules, (c) using semantic vs. proposed syntactic modifier types, or (d) replacing the SUTime lexicon.
- Potential bias in the Tweets dataset: The tweet corpus was preselected by running SUTime and keeping tweets it tagged, then manually annotating those; only a small sample of the remainder was inspected. This procedure likely biases the dataset toward SUTime-detectable expressions and risks skewing recall/precision comparisons. A clean, fully annotated random sample would be more convincing.
- Scope limited to recognition and English: Normalization is out of scope; cross-lingual claims are speculative. Given that normalization is integral to downstream use, not addressing it reduces end-to-end utility. No evidence of generalization beyond English or to more diverse domains.
- Evaluation/reporting gaps: No statistical significance testing, no runtime numbers to substantiate the “real-time” claim, and limited error analysis. Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior papers; it’s unclear if all systems were run under identical settings and versions.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablation studies quantifying the contribution of: (i) POS filtering, (ii) the comma/linkage merge rules, (iii) syntactic vs. semantic modifier typing, and (iv) using a neutral lexicon (not SUTime) to isolate the effect of your rules?
- For the Tweets dataset, can you release a fully annotated, unbiased random sample (without prefiltering by SUTime) and re-run the comparisons? If not, can you at least quantify how many Gold time expressions in the test split were originally missed by SUTime during prefiltering?
- Did you re-run HeidelTime and SUTime yourself with current resources and appropriate settings, or are some numbers taken from literature? If borrowed, how do you ensure apples-to-apples comparability (same corpora splits, versions, tokenization/POS)?
- Can you report runtime throughput and memory usage for SynTime vs. baselines to support the real-time claim?
- How sensitive is SynTime to POS tagging errors? Do you have a fallback for ambiguous tokens (e.g., May) in noisy settings?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is a well-executed engineering effort with an appealingly simple idea and solid empirical performance, particularly on tweets. However, the methodological novelty over existing rule-based systems appears limited, and the tweets dataset construction raises fairness concerns. With stronger ablations, unbiased tweet evaluation, runtime numbers, and clearer differentiation from SUTime/HeidelTime, this could be a strong short paper or system report. As a long-paper contribution, I find the conceptual advance too incremental for ACL.
