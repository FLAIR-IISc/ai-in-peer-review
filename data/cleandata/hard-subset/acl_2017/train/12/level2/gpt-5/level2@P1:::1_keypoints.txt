- SynTime: a lightweight, rule-based temporal expression recognizer using syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics.
- Empirical analysis across TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets: temporal expressions are short, use a small vocabulary, usually contain a “time token,” and have consistent syntactic behavior.
- Implementation: defines syntactic types for time tokens/modifiers/numerals, initializes token regexes largely from SUTime, and expands spans around identified time tokens.
- Experimental claim: strong recall and competitive-to-state-of-the-art F1 vs HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime, with large gains on Tweets.
- Contribution 1: a simple type-based rule framework with boundary-expansion heuristics achieving high recall and strong F1, especially on Tweets.
- Contribution 2: cross-genre empirical analysis motivating the design and providing actionable modeling insights.
- Contribution 3: pragmatic domain adaptation via small lexicon additions yielding measurable improvements.
- Strength: consistently high relaxed-match recall across datasets, supporting the type-first expansion strategy.
- Strength: simplicity, efficiency, and ease of implementation/extension, suitable for real-time applications.
- Strength: insightful “time token first” perspective justified by >93% coverage in analysis.
- Strength: practical, low-effort domain adaptation (few lexicon items) improves portability.
- Strength: clear statement of design choices and error model (e.g., POS disambiguation, exclusion of prepositions per TimeML).
- Weakness: limited novelty—many components borrowed from SUTime and rule-based paradigm remains similar to prior work.
- Weakness: lacks ablation isolating lexicon vs rule contributions; needs comparisons like SUTime lexicon + SynTime rules, etc.
- Weakness: potential evaluation bias—Tweets sampled using SUTime detections and SynTime tuned on training data may favor SynTime; baseline configurations are under-specified.
- Weakness: missing statistical significance testing and some baseline results borrowed from prior work with unclear comparability.
- Weakness: reproducibility gaps—full type inventories, POS constraints, precise merging heuristics, and baseline versions/configs are not provided.
- Weakness: sensitivity to POS tagging/tokenization and annotation conventions; no robustness study and TimeML-based exclusion of prepositions may limit portability.
- Weakness: scope limited to recognition; interactions with normalization or end-to-end normalization performance are not evaluated.
- Questions: provide ablation isolating lexicon vs heuristics, baseline versions/configs, dataset splits, and statistical tests.
- Questions: detail Tweets sampling process, provide SUTime-independent test set, TimeML variant, and inter-annotator agreement.
- Questions: supply exact POS constraints (tagset, capitalization rules), handling of colloquialisms (’til, tmrw, mon), modifier/linker types, and merging/stopping conditions.
- Questions: describe handling of nested/coordinated expressions beyond two segments.
- Suggestions: add error analysis by category and length, consider a learning variant using syntactic types as features, report runtime/memory, and release code/lexicons/datasets for reproducibility.
- Overall: practical, clear paper with strong recall and excellent Tweets results, but limited novelty and concerns about evaluation fairness and reproducibility that should be addressed.
