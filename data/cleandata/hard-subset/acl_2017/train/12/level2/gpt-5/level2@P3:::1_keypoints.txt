Proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based temporal tagger using syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics.  
Empirical cross-corpus analysis showing temporal mentions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, usually include a “time token,” and have consistent syntactic behavior.  
Claims a minimal, extensible type inventory and lightweight rules achieve real-time, adaptable performance with little manual effort.  

High recall and strong F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, with competitive performance on WikiWars.  
Corpus-backed motivation: statistics support design choices (mention length, time-token prevalence, compact token sets).  
Simple, interpretable, and low-maintenance design with few expansions needed for new domains.  
Fair comparison against strong rule-based and learning baselines across multiple datasets including social media.  
Transparent discussion of error sources and limitations, notably POS-tag dependency.  

Novelty is incremental relative to prior rule systems (lexicon largely from SUTime; main changes are type grouping and heuristics).  
Tweets dataset may have selection bias because SUTime was used to prefilter positive examples.  
No ablation studies or statistical significance testing to isolate which components drive gains.  
Evaluation mismatch on WikiWars due to differing annotation policies (prepositions), likely penalizing strict matches.  
“Real time” and reproducibility claims lack throughput metrics and release of exact regexes/POS constraints/expansions.  

Clarify the contribution split between boundary-expansion heuristics and the SUTime-initialized lexicon via ablation.  
Provide exact POS-to-type mappings (table of allowed POS patterns per type) for reproducibility.  
Recompute Tweets results on a fully random, exhaustively annotated sample (or report performance on the 500 manually checked negatives).  
Report statistical significance for main F1 improvements on TimeBank and Tweets.  
Provide throughput (tokens/sec or docs/sec) and comparisons to HeidelTime/SUTime.  

Add ablation experiments (remove POS filtering, merge rules, LINKAGE/COMMA handling, reduce lexicon).  
Release reproducibility package: token regex lists per type, POS constraints, small expansion lexicons, and code.  
Report statistical significance and runtime throughput to support claims.  
Harmonize WikiWars evaluation with its annotation conventions or report with/without prepositions.  
Address Tweets sampling bias by expanding exhaustive manual annotation on a random sample.  

Overall: clear, well-motivated, and effective interpretable rule approach with strong recall; needs ablations, significance tests, and dataset/reproducibility fixes to strengthen claims.
