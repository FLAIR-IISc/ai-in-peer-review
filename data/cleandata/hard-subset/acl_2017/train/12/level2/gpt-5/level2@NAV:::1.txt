Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based time expression recognizer that groups tokens into syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and applies simple boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors claim: (1) an empirical analysis showing time expressions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, and share syntactic behaviors; (2) a type-based design using syntactic rather than semantic categories; (3) state-of-the-art recognition results on TimeBank and Tweets with best recall across all datasets.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A pragmatic, simple rule architecture that achieves strong recall and competitive F1, particularly on Tweets. 2) A useful empirical characterization of time expressions across four corpora that motivates a high-recall, type-first strategy. 3) An extensible lexicon+heuristic framework that can be adapted via small, targeted additions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance: SynTime consistently attains best recall and top-tier F1 on TimeBank and Tweets; the gains on Tweets are substantial (e.g., strict F1 ≈91.7 vs. 79–80 for baselines), supporting the claim that a type-first approach handles informal text well.
- Simplicity and practicality: The method is easy to implement, runs in real time, and requires minimal training data—attractive for low-resource or rapidly evolving domains.
- Clear, data-driven motivation: The cross-corpus analysis (length, vocabulary size, POS distribution) compellingly justifies the design choices (time-token first, boundary expansion).
- Extensibility demonstrated: Small, manual lexicon additions notably improve WikiWars recall, suggesting good domain adaptation with low cost.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The approach refines established rule-based paradigms (e.g., SUTime’s expansion) with syntactic types; the conceptual advance is incremental.
- Evaluation threats: The Tweets dataset is self-annotated; details on inter-annotator agreement and baseline tuning are sparse. No statistical significance tests or ablations (e.g., effect of types vs. SUTime token lists).
- Reproducibility: The full rule set, lexicons, and expansion lists are not released; runtime claims lack quantitative benchmarks.
- Scope: Focuses only on recognition (not normalization). Reliance on POS tagging introduces brittleness; exclusion of prepositions (per TimeML) may undercount expressions in domains like WikiWars.

Questions to authors
- Please provide ablations: (a) without syntactic typing, (b) using only SUTime tokens, (c) without POS filtering. Which components drive recall?
- How were HeidelTime/SUTime configured for Tweets? Any domain adaptation for fairness?
- What are runtime measurements (docs/sec) and memory footprint?
- Do you have inter-annotator agreement for Tweets? Any error analysis by category (missed tokens vs. segmentation)?
- Will you release code, rules, and expanded lexicons to ensure reproducibility?
