Summary and contributions
This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for time expression recognition based on syntactic types and boundary-expansion heuristics. The authors first analyze four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and observe that (i) temporal expressions are short, (ii) a small vocabulary expresses most time information, (iii) most expressions contain a “time token,” and (iv) tokens inside time expressions show consistent syntactic behavior. Building on these insights, SynTime defines syntactic types for time tokens, modifiers, and numerals, initializes token regexes largely from SUTime, and then applies simple heuristic rules that expand around identified time tokens to construct full spans. Experiments against HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets show strong recall and competitive-to-best F1, with notable gains on Tweets.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, type-based rule framework with boundary-expansion heuristics for temporal expression recognition that achieves strong recall and state-of-the-art or competitive F1 across multiple datasets, especially on Tweets.
2) An empirical analysis of temporal expressions across genres that motivates the design (short spans, small vocabulary, presence of a time token, consistent syntactic behavior), offering actionable insights for model design.
3) A pragmatic approach to domain adaptation via small, targeted lexicon additions, demonstrating gains with minimal manual effort.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with high recall: SynTime achieves best or second-best results in most metrics, with particularly strong gains on Tweets (e.g., strict F1 ~91.7%). The consistently high relaxed-match recall across datasets supports the claim that the type-first, boundary-expansion strategy covers a large fraction of real-world expressions.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The approach is easy to understand, implement, and extend; the heuristics are minimalistic yet effective. Real-time operation and low engineering overhead make the method attractive for practitioners.
- Insightful analysis: The cross-genre analysis is useful and convincingly connected to the system design. The “time token first” perspective is well-motivated by the finding that >93% of expressions contain a time token.
- Practical domain adaptation: The expansion procedure adds few items (e.g., 3–19 per dataset) yet yields measurable improvements, especially on WikiWars, suggesting good portability to new domains.
- Clear error model and design choices: The paper clearly states the reliance on POS disambiguation for ambiguous tokens (e.g., May) and avoids prepositions as per TimeML, which helps readers align expectations with annotation practices.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing rule-based systems: Much of SynTime’s initialization (token regexes) is borrowed from SUTime, and the method still follows a rule-based expansion paradigm. The key difference—syntactic vs semantic grouping and flexible boundary expansion—needs stronger empirical isolation. An ablation comparing (a) SUTime lexicon + SUTime rules, (b) SUTime lexicon + SynTime rules, and (c) SynTime lexicon-only variants would clarify what contributes to the gains.
- Potential evaluation bias and fairness concerns: The Tweets dataset was sampled using SUTime detections, then augmented with a small sample of missed cases. Even with the estimate that ~3% were missed, this pipeline may bias distributional characteristics of the corpus toward SUTime’s coverage. Moreover, SynTime is expanded using training data while baselines may not be similarly tuned to the same domain; configuration details for HeidelTime and SUTime (e.g., domain/adaptation settings) and UWTime training regimes are brief. Statistical significance tests are missing. Some baseline numbers are borrowed from prior work, which may not use identical splits or tool versions.
- Reproducibility and detail gaps: The paper does not provide the exact type inventories (full lists), POS constraints used to filter ambiguous tokens, or the precise heuristic rules for merging (beyond a few examples), making replication harder. It is also unclear which versions of the baseline systems were used and with what configurations.
- Sensitivity to tagging and annotation conventions: The approach depends on accurate POS tagging and tokenization; no robustness study is provided. Excluding prepositions per TimeML may limit portability to datasets that annotate spans differently (e.g., including “on” or “at”).
- Scope limited to recognition: While recognition alone is a valid subtask, many applications require normalization; discussions of how well SynTime’s spans interact with normalizers (or simple pairing with an off-the-shelf normalizer) are not empirically demonstrated.

Questions to authors
- Ablation and fairness:
  - Can you report an ablation isolating the effect of your type-based heuristics from the lexicon by comparing SUTime’s lexicon with SynTime’s rules vs SUTime’s full engine? Likewise, what happens if you restrict SynTime to its own lexicon (without SUTime-derived items)?
  - Did you tune HeidelTime and SUTime for each dataset (e.g., rule sets, domain options)? Please provide versions and configuration flags. Are your splits identical to those used for any results borrowed from prior papers?
  - Please add statistical significance testing for key comparisons.
- Tweets dataset:
  - Could you detail the exact sampling process and annotate a random, SUTime-independent test set to rule out sampling bias?
  - Which TimeML variant/guidelines were used for Tweets? Can you share inter-annotator agreement?
- Method details:
  - What are the precise POS constraints for filtering ambiguous tokens (tagset, capitalization handling, e.g., May vs may)? How do you handle colloquialisms like “’til,” “tmrw,” “mon”?
  - Please describe the full set of modifier and linkage types and the exact stopping/merging conditions (especially for commas and multiple linkers).
  - How do you handle nested or coordinated expressions beyond two segments (e.g., “June 5–7, 1990 and March 2001”)?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide an error analysis by category (e.g., missed descriptive dates, POS errors, preposition span mismatches) and by length, to guide future improvements.
- Consider a learning variant that uses your syntactic types as features within a sequence model; this could address some brittleness while preserving interpretability.
- Report runtime and memory usage to substantiate the “real-time” claim.
- If possible, release code, lexicons, and the annotated Tweets dataset to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall, this is a clear, practical paper with strong recall and excellent results on Tweets. The design is appealingly simple and useful. Its primary limitations lie in novelty relative to existing rule-based systems and in evaluation fairness/reproducibility. Addressing the ablations, clarifying baseline configurations, and strengthening the tweets evaluation would significantly improve the paper.
