Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies temporal expression recognition and proposes SynTime, a lightweight rule-based tagger that uses syntactic “types” (time token, modifier, numeral) and simple boundary-expansion heuristics to assemble time expressions. A cross-corpus analysis (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) motivates the approach with four observations: temporal mentions are short, rely on a small vocabulary, almost always include a “time token,” and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. SynTime initializes its lexicon from token-level regexes (largely derived from SUTime) and applies position-aware heuristics to expand and merge segments. Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and a manually annotated Tweets dataset show strong recall and competitive or superior F1 to HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime.

Key contributions, as seen here, ordered by strength:
1) A simple, interpretable rule framework (syntactic types + boundary-expansion heuristics) that achieves strong recall and state-of-the-art F1 on TimeBank and Tweets, and competitive results on WikiWars.
2) An empirical analysis across four corpora identifying compact lexical coverage and syntactic regularities of temporal expressions, informing the design.
3) Evidence that a minimal, extensible type inventory and lightweight rules can run in real time and be adapted with little manual effort via keyword expansion.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with high recall: On TimeBank, SynTime-I reaches 92.09 F1 (strict) and 94.96 (relaxed), exceeding HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime; on Tweets, SynTime-I attains 91.74/95.87 F1 (strict/relaxed), a substantial improvement over baselines. Recalls exceed 92% across datasets (relaxed), consistent with the “time token” observation and suggesting broad coverage of temporal lexicon.
- Clear, corpus-backed motivation: The four observations are supported by corpus statistics (e.g., 80% of mentions ≤ 3 tokens; ≥93% contain a time token; compact token sets in Table 3), connecting the design to observable language behavior rather than ad-hoc rules.
- Lightweight and extensible design: The type system and boundary-expansion rules are simple, interpretable, and easy to modify. The expansion step adds very few items for TimeBank/Tweets (Table 5), suggesting low maintenance cost for new domains.
- Fair breadth of baselines and datasets: Comparisons include two strong rule-based systems (HeidelTime, SUTime) and a learning-based system (UWTime) on two standard corpora and an additional social media domain.
- Transparent error factors and limitations: The paper acknowledges reliance on POS tagging and gives concrete cases where POS errors cause misses, which is helpful for future improvements.

Weaknesses
- Novelty vs. prior rule-based systems is incremental: The system’s lexicon and token regexes are largely sourced from SUTime, and the main difference lies in syntactic type grouping and boundary-expansion heuristics. The paper argues semantic vs. syntactic type definitions and heuristic vs. fixed rules, but a deeper comparative analysis of rule expressivity and coverage would strengthen claims of novelty.
- Dataset construction for Tweets risks selection bias: Tweets with temporal expressions were initially detected using SUTime, and only a sample of the “negatives” was manually checked (finding ~3% misses). This pipeline risks overrepresenting SUTime-detectable forms. Although SynTime still outperforms SUTime on this set, a fully random sample with exhaustive manual annotation would strengthen robustness claims.
- Lack of ablations and significance tests: The paper attributes gains to type-based heuristics and boundary expansion, but does not quantify contributions of components (e.g., POS filtering, merging strategies, handling of LINKAGE/COMMA, expansions). No statistical significance testing is reported for F1 improvements (e.g., on TimeBank and Tweets), yet the text claims significant outperformance.
- Annotation-policy mismatch on WikiWars: The system intentionally excludes many prepositions (following TimeML/TimeBank), yet WikiWars annotations often include them, likely hurting strict match. While this is discussed, the evaluation would be fairer if adapted to WikiWars conventions or if results were reported with/without prepositions to isolate the effect.
- Reproducibility and efficiency claims: “Real time” performance is asserted without throughput measurements or complexity analysis. Reproducibility would benefit from releasing the exact token regex lists, POS constraints, and expansion lists used per dataset.

Questions to clarify during author response
- How much of the improvement stems from the boundary-expansion heuristics vs. the SUTime-initialized lexicon? An ablation removing SUTime lexicon or replacing it with HeidelTime’s tokens (or a minimal seed) would help quantify rule vs. lexicon contributions.
- What is the exact mapping from POS tags to type instantiations? A table enumerating allowed POS patterns per type would aid reproducibility and clarity about ambiguity handling (e.g., “May”).
- Can results on Tweets be recomputed on a fully random sample with exhaustive manual annotation (no prefiltering by SUTime) to eliminate selection bias? Alternatively, report performance on the 500 randomly sampled “negatives” that were manually checked.
- Were statistical significance tests performed for the main F1 improvements on TimeBank and Tweets?
- Are throughput numbers available (e.g., tokens/sec or docs/sec) compared to HeidelTime and SUTime?

Suggestions for improvement (feasible within a short revision cycle)
- Add an ablation study: (a) without POS filtering, (b) without merge-overlap rules, (c) without LINKAGE/COMMA special handling, and (d) with a reduced lexicon. This would empirically isolate which heuristics drive the gains.
- Provide a reproducibility package: token regex lists per type, POS constraints, and the small expansion lexicons used for each dataset. If possible, release code.
- Report statistical significance for key comparisons and provide runtime throughput to support “real time” claims.
- Harmonize the WikiWars evaluation with its annotation conventions (or bifurcate reporting to show with/without prepositions) to ensure fair comparison.
- Reassess the Tweets evaluation on a fully random subset to address potential sampling bias; the existing 500-tweet check could be expanded or complemented with a new randomly sampled test set.

Overall, the work presents a clear, well-motivated, and effective rule-based approach that is easy to understand and extend, with strong recall and competitive F1. Addressing the concerns above—especially ablations, significance, and dataset bias—would materially strengthen the contribution and its claims of generality.
