Summary and contributions
- The paper presents SynTime, a lightweight rule-based system for time expression recognition that organizes token-level patterns into “syntactic” types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple boundary-expansion heuristics to form time expressions. Unlike prior rule-based systems that rely on fixed patterns, SynTime focuses on position-based modifier types and local merging heuristics.
- The authors analyze four corpora (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and report four empirical observations: time expressions are short; a small vocabulary dominates; most time expressions contain at least one “time token”; and constituents show similar POS distributions. These motivate SynTime’s design.
- Experiments on TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets show high recall and strong F1, outperforming HeidelTime, SUTime, and UWTime on TimeBank and Tweets and being competitive on WikiWars. The system requires no training and claims real-time speed.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance, especially recall: SynTime achieves the best recall across all datasets and particularly impressive F1 on Tweets (strict F1 ~91.7, relaxed F1 ~95.9). The high recall supports the core hypothesis that recognizing time tokens first and then expanding is effective.
- Simple, transparent method likely to be robust and easy to adapt: The left/right boundary expansion with small position-based modifier types is intuitive and easier to extend than long, fixed templates. The paper demonstrates minimal effort to adapt to new domains (few keywords added to match WikiWars and Tweets).
- Useful corpus analysis connecting design to data: The observations (length distributions, token coverage, POS distributions) are quantified and align with the principle of least effort, providing a compelling rationale for prioritizing token-first detection and local expansion.
- Clear positioning with respect to prior work: The paper contrasts its position-based “syntactic” types with SUTime’s semantic categories and discusses differences with both rule-based systems and learning-based systems (e.g., UWTime).
- Practicality: No training required, compatible with common NLP preprocessing (POS tagging), and designed to run in real time. This makes the approach appealing for deployment.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to SUTime and other rule-based systems: SynTime reuses SUTime’s token lexicons/regexes and differs primarily in how it groups modifier types and applies boundary-expansion heuristics. While this design is neat, it feels incremental rather than a conceptual leap; the “syntactic” vs. “semantic” distinction is not deeply justified linguistically, and the rule differences may be seen as engineering refinements.
- Potential bias in Tweets dataset creation: Tweets were pre-filtered using SUTime to select tweets containing a time expression; the authors estimate SUTime misses ~3%, but this still risks biasing the dataset toward SUTime’s coverage. This could affect fairness of comparisons and inflate gains, especially when SynTime reuses SUTime token resources.
- Incomplete experimental controls and reporting: Baseline configurations are under-specified (e.g., HeidelTime domain setting, SUTime rule sets, UWTime training details). No statistical significance tests are reported. There is no ablation to isolate contributions of POS filtering, modifier types, or merging heuristics, nor analysis of failure cases beyond POS tagging errors.
- Real-time claim not substantiated: The paper asserts real-time performance but provides no runtime benchmarks, memory footprint, or throughput numbers compared to baselines.
- Scope and generalization: By adhering to TimeML guidelines that exclude many prepositions from time expressions, SynTime underperforms on WikiWars where prepositions and descriptive spans are common. This raises questions about generalization to domains with richer compositional temporal phrases. Cross-lingual applicability, though mentioned as future work, is not evidenced.

Questions to authors
- Tweets dataset construction: Can you quantify the exact recall and precision of SUTime on the initial 4,000 tweets rather than estimating a ~3% miss rate from a 500-sample? How sensitive are your results to this pre-filtering step? Could you re-evaluate on a randomly sampled, fully annotated tweet set without SUTime pre-filtering?
- Baseline configuration details: Which HeidelTime configuration (domain/language resources) and SUTime rule sets were used? For UWTime, what training hyperparameters and feature sets were employed, and how was the Tweets training integrated with TimeBank? Did you tune baselines on development data comparable to SynTime-E’s expansion?
- Ablations: What is the performance of SynTime without POS disambiguation? Without the COMMA/LINKAGE-specific merge rules? With only PREFIX vs SUFFIX vs LINKAGE types ablated? An ablation would clarify where gains come from.
- Runtime: Please provide throughput (docs/sec or tokens/sec) and memory usage, and compare with SUTime and HeidelTime under the same hardware.
- Error analysis and coverage: What are the dominant error types per dataset (e.g., false positives from ambiguous tokens like “spring,” long descriptive spans, preposition-related spans)? How often do true time expressions lack any time token, and how does SynTime handle them?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider an alternative Tweets dataset without pre-filtering by SUTime or at least report a second evaluation on such a set to address potential selection bias.
- Provide per-type results (e.g., DATE vs DURATION vs TIME, or patterns like MONTH NUMERAL YEAR), which would illuminate where SynTime’s heuristics are strongest/weakest.
- Strengthen comparisons by enabling/aligning preposition handling to better match WikiWars guidelines or by reporting both TimeML-adherent and inclusive spans.
- Report statistical significance for F1 differences, especially where improvements are modest (e.g., TimeBank relaxed F1).
- Clarify the relationship between “syntactic” types and traditional linguistic syntax; consider more precise terminology (e.g., “position-based modifier categories”) if the types are defined by relative position rather than syntactic constituency.
- Improve reference formatting and fix typos; release code and resources to support reproducibility.
