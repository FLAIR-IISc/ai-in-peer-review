

Summary: This paper presents SynTime, a rule-based approach for time expression recognition that leverages syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), making four key observations: time expressions are short (80% have ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, most contain at least one time token (93%+), and words in time expressions exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they define three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and design heuristic rules for time expression recognition. The authors report that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets datasets, with particularly strong results on Tweets.

Main Contributions:
1. Comprehensive analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets, revealing systematic patterns that support Zipf's principle of least effort.
2. A novel syntactic type system for time expression recognition that differs from semantic-based approaches in the literature.
3. A lightweight, rule-based time expression tagger (SynTime) that achieves strong performance while being simple to expand.

Strengths:
1. The paper's analysis of time expressions is thorough and well-supported by data from multiple sources. The four observations provide strong empirical evidence for the design of SynTime, and the connection to Zipf's principle of least effort is insightful and well-explained.

2. SynTime demonstrates impressive performance, particularly on the Tweets dataset where it achieves 91.74% F1 (11.37% improvement over the best baseline in strict match). The authors convincingly argue that the simplicity and small vocabulary of time expressions in informal communication (like tweets) make SynTime especially effective in these contexts.

3. The paper provides a clear, step-by-step explanation of how SynTime works, with helpful examples (Figure 3) that illustrate the time segment identification and extraction processes. The expansion mechanism is practical and well-justified, with evidence that only a small number of additional keywords are needed for different domains.

4. The authors acknowledge limitations appropriately, particularly regarding dependency on accurate tokenization and POS tagging, and provide a reasonable explanation for why SynTime performs well on Tweets despite their informal nature.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the syntactic type system is not sufficiently differentiated from existing rule-based approaches. While the paper claims SynTime differs from SUTime in its type definition and rule design, the differences aren't clearly demonstrated or emphasized. The authors should more explicitly contrast their approach with SUTime's semantic-based types.

2. The paper lacks runtime measurements to support the claim that SynTime "runs in real time." For a system claiming to be lightweight and efficient, providing concrete runtime data would strengthen this claim significantly.

3. The evaluation on WikiWars is notably weaker than on other datasets (SynTime achieves only 80.11% F1 in strict match), yet the paper doesn't provide detailed error analysis to understand why. A more thorough discussion of where SynTime fails would help readers understand the limitations of the approach.

4. The paper doesn't adequately address how SynTime handles time expressions that don't follow the observed patterns. For example, how would SynTime handle unusual time expressions like "the day after tomorrow" or "the third Wednesday of next month," which might not fit neatly into the syntactic types defined?

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details on how SynTime handles time expressions with multiple overlapping patterns (e.g., "the first week of January 2020")? The paper mentions overlapping time segments but doesn't elaborate on how these are resolved.

2. The paper states that SynTime "can be easily expanded by simply adding keywords," but doesn't specify how this is done in practice. Could you provide more concrete examples of the expansion process, particularly for a domain like medical texts where time expressions might differ significantly from news or tweets?

3. The paper claims that POS tagging errors affect SynTime's performance, but doesn't quantify this impact. Could you provide an analysis of how different levels of POS tagging errors affect SynTime's accuracy?
