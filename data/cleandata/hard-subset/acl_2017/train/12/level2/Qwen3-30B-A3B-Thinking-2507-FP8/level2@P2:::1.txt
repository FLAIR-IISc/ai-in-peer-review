

Summary of the paper: The authors analyze time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets), making four key observations: 1) time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), 2) the vocabulary used is small, 3) at least 93% contain time tokens, and 4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, they propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that uses syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and simple heuristic rules. They claim SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and Tweets.

Main contributions: 
1) Comprehensive analysis of time expression patterns across multiple domains, providing empirical support for the principle of least effort in language usage.
2) The SynTime system, which uses syntactic types rather than semantic types for rule-based time expression recognition.
3) Empirical demonstration of high recall (92%+) and competitive precision on benchmark datasets.

Strengths:
1) The analysis of time expressions is thorough and provides valuable insights into time expression patterns. The four observations are well-supported by data and provide a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.
2) The connection to the principle of least effort (Zipf, 1949) is insightful and provides a compelling theoretical justification for the observed patterns.
3) The empirical results are impressive, particularly the high recall rates (above 92% in relaxed match on all datasets) and exceptional performance on Tweets (F1 of 95.87% in relaxed match).
4) The system is lightweight and runs in real-time, which is a significant practical advantage for applications requiring fast processing.

Weaknesses:
1) The paper claims SynTime "outperforms state-of-the-art methods" without providing statistical significance tests for the results, making it difficult to assess whether the performance differences are statistically meaningful.
2) The paper doesn't adequately address why SynTime performs well on TimeBank and Tweets but less well on WikiWars (where it has lower precision and recall compared to some baselines), despite noting that "Time expressions in WikiWars include lots of prepositions and quite a few descriptive time expressions."
3) The comparison with state-of-the-art baselines is limited to three systems, and the paper doesn't explain why other relevant systems (e.g., those that performed well in TempEval-3) weren't included, which weakens the claim of superiority.
4) The claim that SynTime "can be easily expanded" is not substantiated with a thorough analysis of the effort required for expansion or the impact on performance beyond the limited examples provided in Table 5.
5) The paper doesn't adequately address how the system handles ambiguous cases (e.g., "May" as a month vs. a modal verb), which is a significant challenge in time expression recognition.

Questions to authors:
1) Could you provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences between SynTime and the baselines?
2) Could you elaborate on the specific challenges that WikiWars presents to SynTime and how the approach could be improved to handle these challenges better?
3) Why were other state-of-the-art systems not included in the comparison, and how would their inclusion affect the conclusions of the paper?
