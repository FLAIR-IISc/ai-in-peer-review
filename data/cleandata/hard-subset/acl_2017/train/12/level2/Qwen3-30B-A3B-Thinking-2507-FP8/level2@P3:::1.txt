

Summary: The paper analyzes time expressions from four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, and Tweets) and identifies four key observations: time expressions are very short (80% contain ≤3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, at least 93% contain time tokens, and words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, the paper proposes SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognition system that defines three syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and uses simple heuristic rules for recognition. The approach is designed to be lightweight, real-time, and easily expandable.

Main contributions:
1. The detailed analysis of time expressions across multiple datasets provides empirical evidence supporting the principle of least effort in language usage, revealing key structural properties of time expressions.
2. The novel type-based approach (SynTime) defines syntactic types for time-related tokens rather than semantic types, leading to a more flexible and lightweight rule-based system compared to prior work.
3. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets, particularly achieving exceptional performance on the informal Tweets dataset.

Strengths:
1. The paper's analysis is thorough and provides meaningful insights into how time expressions are structured in language, supporting the principle of least effort with compelling empirical evidence. The observations about time expression structure (short length, small vocabulary, high occurrence of time tokens, and similar syntactic behavior) are well-supported by data across multiple datasets.

2. The proposed SynTime approach is genuinely novel in its use of syntactic types rather than semantic types for rule-based time expression recognition. The heuristic rules based on boundary expansion are simple yet effective, and the system's lightweight nature and real-time performance are significant practical advantages over both rule-based and machine learning approaches.

3. The experimental results are strong and well-justified, showing consistent improvements over three state-of-the-art baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) across three datasets. The exceptional performance on the Tweets dataset is particularly impressive and well-explained through the paper's own analysis of informal language use.

4. The paper provides a clear and practical demonstration of how the system can be expanded for new domains through simple keyword additions, with supporting evidence that this requires minimal effort due to the limited vocabulary of time-related words.

5. The discussion of why SynTime performs well on informal text (tweets) is insightful and directly connects the paper's analysis to the experimental results, showing how the observed properties of time expressions (short length, formal word usage) align with the system's strengths.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is limited to three baselines, which is insufficient for a claim of outperforming "state-of-the-art" methods. More recent or alternative approaches (e.g., neural models) should be included for a more comprehensive evaluation.

2. The paper claims SynTime "runs in real time" but provides no concrete runtime measurements or comparisons to other systems. Without specific performance metrics, this claim lacks supporting evidence.

3. The paper mentions that SynTime can be easily expanded by adding keywords, but doesn't provide detailed analysis of how many additional keywords are needed for different domains or how performance scales with each addition. The limited expansion data (Table 5) shows minimal improvements on TimeBank and Tweets but significant gains on WikiWars, suggesting domain-specific adaptation needs more thorough exploration.

4. The paper does not adequately address how the system handles ambiguous cases (e.g., "May" as a month vs. modal verb), which is a critical challenge in time expression recognition. The brief mention of POS tagging in Section 4.2.1 is insufficient for addressing this limitation.

5. The paper's analysis of syntactic behavior within time expressions (Observation 4) is valuable but could be more deeply explored to better inform the type system design. A more detailed examination of how different syntactic types interact in various time expression patterns would strengthen the foundation of the proposed approach.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can the authors provide specific runtime measurements for SynTime compared to the baselines to support the claim of "real-time" performance?
2. How many additional keywords would be needed to achieve comparable performance on datasets with highly domain-specific time expressions, and what is the marginal benefit of each additional keyword?
3. What is the system's performance on ambiguous cases where words can serve multiple roles (e.g., "May" as a month or modal verb), and how does the system handle such cases?
