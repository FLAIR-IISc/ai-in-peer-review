

Summary: This paper analyzes time expressions across four datasets (TimeBank, Gigaword, WikiWars, Tweets) and makes four key observations: time expressions are short (80% ≤ 3 tokens), use a small vocabulary of time-related keywords, contain time tokens in ≥93% of cases, and exhibit similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, the authors propose SynTime, a rule-based time expression recognizer that defines three syntactic types (time tokens, modifiers, numerals) and uses simple heuristic boundary expansion rules for recognition.

Main Contributions:
1. The analysis of time expressions providing empirical evidence for Zipf's principle of least effort in language usage.
2. The introduction of a syntactically motivated type system for time expression recognition, differing from semantic-based approaches.
3. The lightweight SynTime system that achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets.

Strengths:
1. The paper's analysis provides valuable insights into time expression patterns, with strong empirical evidence supporting the four observations. The connection to Zipf's principle adds theoretical depth.
2. SynTime demonstrates significant improvements on the Tweets dataset (91.74% F1), which aligns with the paper's observations about short expressions and small vocabulary in informal communication.
3. The system's simplicity and real-time performance are compelling advantages over learning-based approaches that require training data and computational resources.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the approach is limited, as it builds directly on existing rule-based methods like SUTime without sufficiently differentiating the syntactic type system from conventional POS-based approaches.
2. The paper lacks sufficient detail on the heuristic rules for reproducibility, making it difficult to assess the exact implementation of boundary expansion.
3. The evaluation is limited to three datasets, omitting Gigaword despite its use in the analysis, and does not compare with recent state-of-the-art methods like those using neural networks.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do the syntactic types in SynTime specifically outperform POS tags for time expression recognition, given that the paper states POS cannot distinguish time expressions from common words?
2. What is the exact implementation of the boundary expansion heuristic rules, and how were they tuned for optimal performance?
3. Why was Gigaword excluded from the evaluation despite being used in the analysis phase?
