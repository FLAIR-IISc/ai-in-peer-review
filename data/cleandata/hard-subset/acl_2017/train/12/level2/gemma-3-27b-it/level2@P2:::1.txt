This paper presents SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The authors observe characteristics of time expressions across several datasets – short length, small vocabulary, high prevalence of “time tokens”, and consistent syntactic behavior – and leverage these observations to build a lightweight and extensible system. SynTime defines three syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and uses simple heuristic rules for recognition. The experiments demonstrate that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets, and achieves comparable results on WikiWars.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Time Expressions:** The thorough analysis of time expressions across four datasets provides valuable insights into their linguistic properties. The observations about expression length, vocabulary size, and syntactic consistency are well-supported by the presented data. This is the strongest contribution, forming a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
2. **Type-Based Rule Definition:** The introduction of a syntactic type system for time expressions, as opposed to semantic types used in other systems, is a novel idea. Defining rules based on token types and their relative positions allows for a more flexible and extensible approach.
3. **Effective Time Expression Recognition System:** SynTime achieves competitive or superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly on the challenging Tweets dataset. The system’s lightweight nature and real-time performance are also notable advantages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated by the observed properties of time expressions. The principle of least effort provides a compelling rationale for the design choices.
2. **Clear Explanation of Method:** The SynTime approach is described clearly and concisely, making it easy to understand the key components and how they interact. The use of Figure 2 to illustrate the workflow is particularly helpful.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments cover a diverse set of datasets, including both benchmark corpora and a real-world dataset (Tweets). The comparison with strong baselines provides a good assessment of SynTime’s performance.
4. **Practical Advantages:** The system's lightweight nature, real-time performance, and easy extensibility are practical advantages that make it appealing for real-world applications.
5. **Good Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section provides a good overview of existing methods for time expression recognition, highlighting the differences between SynTime and previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Rule-Based Approach:** While the type-based rule definition is a valuable contribution, the overall approach remains fundamentally rule-based. This limits the potential for generalization to unseen data or domains where the rules may not be readily applicable. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation, but the extent of this limitation could be further discussed.
2. **Reliance on POS Tagging:** The system's performance is dependent on the accuracy of the POS tagger. Errors in POS tagging can propagate to the time expression recognition process. While this is acknowledged, the impact of POS tagging errors on performance is not thoroughly investigated.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of errors that SynTime makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific types of time expressions that the system consistently fails to recognize?
4. **Expansion Methodology:** While the authors mention expanding SynTime by adding keywords, the methodology for selecting which keywords to add is not fully explained. A more systematic approach to keyword selection could improve the system’s performance and reduce the need for manual effort.
5. **Impact of Dataset Quality:** The Gigaword dataset was discarded due to concerns about label quality, but this decision isn’t fully justified. A brief discussion of the issues with the Gigaword labels and the rationale for excluding it would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the methodology used for selecting keywords to expand SynTime, beyond simply adding words that are not covered by the initial rules?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime's performance to errors in the POS tagger? Have you conducted any experiments to assess the impact of POS tagging accuracy on the overall results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of time expressions that SynTime struggles to recognize?
4.  What are the computational costs of SynTime compared to the baseline methods, beyond simply stating that it runs in “real time”?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections could benefit from further clarification. For example, the explanation of the heuristic rules for time segment identification could be more detailed.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the key differences between SynTime and the baseline methods.
