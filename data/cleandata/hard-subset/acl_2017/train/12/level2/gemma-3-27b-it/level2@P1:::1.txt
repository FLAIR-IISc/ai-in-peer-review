This paper proposes a type-based approach, SynTime, for time expression recognition. The authors analyze time expressions in four datasets and observe that they are typically short, use a limited vocabulary, and exhibit consistent syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three syntactic types – time token, modifier, and numeral – and employs simple heuristic rules for recognition. The experiments demonstrate that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and tweets.

**Contributions:**

1. **Analysis of Time Expressions:** The thorough analysis of time expressions across four datasets, highlighting their brevity, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic patterns, is a significant contribution. This provides valuable insights into the characteristics of time expressions and justifies the design choices made in SynTime.
2. **Type-Based Approach:** The introduction of a type-based approach for time expression recognition, focusing on syntactic types rather than semantic meaning (as in previous work like SUTime), is a novel idea. This simplification, combined with heuristic rules, allows for a lightweight and efficient system.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation on three diverse datasets (TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets), demonstrating that SynTime outperforms existing methods, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The especially good results on the Tweets dataset are notable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime consistently achieves superior performance, particularly in recall, across the evaluated datasets. This is a key strength of the paper. The consistently high recall indicates the system effectively identifies most time expressions.
2. **Lightweight and Efficient:** The paper clearly articulates the efficiency and real-time capability of SynTime, which is a direct result of the simplified type-based approach and heuristic rules. This is a practical benefit that sets SynTime apart from more complex methods.
3. **Well-Motivated Design:** The design of SynTime is strongly motivated by the insightful analysis of time expressions. The authors convincingly argue that the observed characteristics warrant a type-based approach.  The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort adds theoretical grounding.
4. **Clear and Concise Explanation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, design choices, and experimental setup. The explanations of the type definitions and heuristic rules are easy to follow.
5. **Effective Expansion Strategy:**  The method for expanding SynTime by adding keywords based on analysis of training data is practical and effective, as shown by the performance improvements on the WikiWars dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on POS Tagging:** The reliance on accurate POS tagging is a potential limitation. The paper acknowledges this but does not fully explore the impact of POS tagging errors on the performance of SynTime.  A more detailed analysis of this dependency, perhaps with experiments using different POS taggers, would strengthen the paper.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, a more in-depth error analysis would be valuable. Identifying common error cases could suggest areas for improvement in the type definitions or heuristic rules. What types of time expressions does SynTime still struggle with?
3. **Comparison to More Recent Methods:** The baseline comparison primarily includes methods from 2010-2014. While these are established baselines, it would be beneficial to compare SynTime to more recent, potentially deep learning-based, approaches to time expression recognition to provide a more current assessment of its performance.
4. **Limited Discussion of Normalization:** The paper explicitly states it focuses only on recognition, but a brief discussion of how normalization could be integrated with SynTime, given its type-based structure, would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is SynTime's performance to errors in the POS tagger? Could you provide an analysis of the impact of POS tagging accuracy on the final results?
2. Can you provide examples of time expressions that SynTime frequently misidentifies? What are the common characteristics of these error cases?
3. Have you considered evaluating SynTime against more recent state-of-the-art time expression recognition systems, including those based on deep learning? If so, what were the results?
4. Could you elaborate on how the type-based structure of SynTime could facilitate time expression normalization?



**Additional Comments:**

* The figure illustrating the SynTime workflow is clear and helpful.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* The notation and terminology are consistent throughout the paper.
* The GitHub link to SUTime is appreciated.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that presents a novel and effective approach to time expression recognition. The strong empirical results, coupled with the clear motivation and well-defined methodology, make this a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding the analysis of POS tagging dependency and inclusion of more recent baselines, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
