## Review of “Time Expression Analysis and Recognition Using Syntactic Types and Simple Heuristic Rules”

This paper presents SynTime, a novel rule-based approach for time expression recognition. The authors begin with an insightful analysis of time expressions across four datasets, observing their short length, limited vocabulary, and consistent syntactic behavior. Leveraging these observations, SynTime defines three syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and employs simple heuristic rules for recognition. The core contribution lies in shifting the focus from semantic-based rules (common in existing systems like SUTime) to a syntactic type-based approach, coupled with a boundary expansion heuristic, aiming for simplicity, flexibility, and speed.

The paper’s strengths are its thorough analysis of time expression characteristics, which strongly motivate the design of SynTime. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, even outperforming state-of-the-art methods on TimeBank and Tweets, particularly in recall. The light-weight nature of SynTime, enabling real-time processing, is also a significant advantage. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the rationale behind the design choices. 

However, the paper has weaknesses. While the syntactic type approach is interesting, the novelty isn’t groundbreaking. The idea of grouping tokens by syntactic behavior aligns with established linguistic principles. The performance gains, while present, are not overwhelmingly significant and could benefit from more detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of each design component. The paper also lacks a comprehensive error analysis; understanding *where* SynTime fails would be valuable.  The limitation regarding reliance on accurate POS tagging is acknowledged but could be explored further – how robust is SynTime to POS tagging errors, and are there strategies to mitigate this?  The dependence on SUTime for initial token regular expressions also raises a question of independence. 

Finally, while the authors highlight the efficiency of SynTime, a more detailed comparison of processing time with existing methods would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors SynTime makes and suggesting potential improvements?
2.  How sensitive is SynTime’s performance to errors in the POS tagging stage? Have you experimented with different POS taggers?
3.  To what extent is SynTime's performance dependent on the initial regular expressions borrowed from SUTime? Would a completely independently derived set of rules impact the results significantly?
