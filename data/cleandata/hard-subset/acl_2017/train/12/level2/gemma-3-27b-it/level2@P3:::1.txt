This paper introduces SynTime, a type-based rule-based approach to time expression recognition. The core idea stems from observations regarding the characteristics of time expressions across four datasets: their short length, small vocabulary, frequent presence of time tokens, and consistent syntactic behavior. The authors define syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and design simple heuristic rules based on these types for time expression recognition. The evaluation demonstrates that SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets and Twitter data.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the novel application of a syntactic type system to rule-based time expression recognition. Existing rule-based methods often rely on semantic meaning or fixed patterns, whereas SynTime focuses on syntactic behavior and employs a flexible boundary expansion heuristic. This approach leads to a lightweight and efficient tagger capable of running in real time. A secondary contribution is the thorough analysis of time expressions across diverse datasets, which provides empirical support for the design choices within SynTime and highlights the principle of least effort in language usage related to temporal expressions. The experimental results, demonstrating performance gains over existing systems, provide further validation of the approach.

Strengths:

1. **Novelty of the Type-Based Approach:** The use of syntactic types as the foundation for a rule-based time expression tagger is a significant departure from traditional methods. This allows for a more flexible and extensible system, as demonstrated by the ease of expansion through the addition of keywords.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** SynTime consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across multiple datasets, including the challenging Twitter dataset. The substantial improvements in recall are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Thorough Data Analysis:** The detailed analysis of time expressions provides valuable insights into their characteristics and justifies the design choices made in SynTime. The connection to Zipf’s principle of least effort is a compelling observation.
4. **Efficiency and Scalability:**  The lightweight nature of SynTime and its ability to run in real-time are substantial practical advantages. This makes it suitable for applications where speed and resource constraints are critical.
5. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and experimental results are presented in a logical and coherent manner.

Weaknesses:

1. **Dependence on POS Tagging:** The performance of SynTime relies heavily on the accuracy of the underlying POS tagger. Errors in POS tagging can propagate through the system and negatively impact its performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation but could benefit from a more detailed error analysis investigating the impact of POS tagging errors.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents overall performance metrics, a more granular error analysis would be helpful. Identifying specific types of errors that SynTime makes could provide insights into areas for future improvement.
3. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Learning-Based Methods:** The baseline comparisons primarily focus on established rule-based systems. Including comparisons to more recent, state-of-the-art learning-based methods (e.g., those utilizing deep learning) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of SynTime’s performance.
4. **Limited Discussion of Normalization:** The paper explicitly states that it focuses solely on recognition and leaves normalization for future work. However, a brief discussion of potential normalization strategies that could be integrated with SynTime would be beneficial.
5. **Expansion Data Size:** The reported number of added tokens for expansion (Table 5) is relatively small. It would be useful to investigate whether performance improvements plateau with larger expansion datasets.

Questions to Authors:

1.  What is the impact of POS tagging errors on the performance of SynTime? Could the system be made more robust to such errors?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of errors that SynTime makes?
3.  Have you considered incorporating more advanced machine learning techniques for any part of the system, such as learning the weights for the heuristic rules?
4.  What are your plans for addressing time expression normalization? Are there any specific challenges that you anticipate?
