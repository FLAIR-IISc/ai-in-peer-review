**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper presents SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based system for recognizing time expressions from text. The authors first conduct an analysis of time expressions across four datasets (news, Wikipedia, Twitter), observing that they are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and almost always contain a core "time token" (e.g., 'day', 'month', 'Monday'). Based on these observations, SynTime employs a simple and effective three-step strategy: 1) identify core time tokens in a sentence, 2) expand their boundaries left and right to form "time segments" by including adjacent modifiers and numerals, and 3) merge adjacent or overlapping segments to form the final time expressions. A key aspect of the proposed method is its use of "syntactic types" (e.g., `PREFIX` modifier, `TIME_UNIT` token) to define the rules for boundary expansion, which the authors contrast with the semantic types used in other systems. The system is evaluated against strong baselines on three datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on news (TimeBank) and social media (Tweets) data, particularly in terms of recall.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A simple, effective, and high-recall method for time expression recognition.** The core "identify-then-expand" heuristic is intuitive and proves to be very effective across different genres, especially on informal text. The system's consistently high recall is a significant result that suggests its coverage is excellent.
2.  **A clean and interpretable framework for a rule-based tagger.** By conceptualizing rules around token types based on their position and function relative to core time tokens, the authors present a system that is easy to understand, and likely easy to debug and extend.
3.  **An empirical analysis of time expression characteristics across different domains.** The preliminary analysis provides a solid, data-driven motivation for the system's design and offers useful insights into the nature of time expressions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method achieves impressive results, outperforming strong rule-based and machine-learning baselines on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The performance on Tweets is particularly noteworthy, with an F1 score over 91% in the strict match setting, demonstrating the robustness of the simple heuristic approach on noisy, informal text where other systems may struggle.
2.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and transparent. The three-step recognition process is easy to follow, and the heuristic rules for expanding and merging segments are intuitive. This stands in contrast to more complex cascaded automata or black-box machine learning models, making SynTime highly interpretable and likely easier to maintain.
3.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** The paper makes a credible claim that SynTime is lightweight and runs in real time. The reliance on efficient string/regex matching and simple heuristics, rather than complex grammars or statistical inference, supports this. Furthermore, the approach of bootstrapping the system with token lists from an existing resource (SUTime) and then providing a clear path for manual expansion is a very practical strategy for developing a robust tagger for new domains.
4.  **Well-Motivated Design:** The system's architecture is directly motivated by the insightful data analysis presented in Section 3. The observations that most time expressions are short and contain an identifiable "time token" provide a strong justification for the "identify-then-expand" strategy, grounding the system design in empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Novelty of the "Syntactic Types" Framing:** The paper's central claim of novelty rests on the distinction between its "syntactic types" and the "semantic types" of systems like SUTime. This distinction feels somewhat overstated. A rule that identifies a token as a `PREFIX` modifier because it appears before a time token seems functionally similar to a pattern in another system that explicitly matches that same token in that position. It would strengthen the paper to provide a more concrete example of how this syntactic typing leads to a fundamentally simpler or more powerful rule-writing paradigm compared to, for instance, SUTime's TokensRegex.
2.  **Dependence on an Existing System for Initialization:** SynTime-I is initialized with token lists extracted from SUTime. This makes the direct performance comparison against SUTime potentially less clear-cut, as SynTime's success is partly dependent on the quality and coverage of SUTime's lexical resources. The contribution of SynTime's novel rule mechanism could have been isolated more effectively with an ablation study, for example, by showing the performance when initialized with a smaller, independently created set of core time tokens.
3.  **Manual and Subjective Expansion Process:** The proposed method for domain adaptation (SynTime-E) relies on a manual process of adding new keywords. While effective, this process is not easily scalable and may be subjective. The guideline that "added words can not cause ambiguity and should be generic" relies on human expertise and is not a formal procedure. A more detailed discussion of this process—for example, the time/effort required for the WikiWars expansion, or guidelines for resolving potential ambiguities—would be beneficial.
4.  **Exclusion of Time Normalization:** The paper focuses exclusively on the recognition task. While this is a reasonable scoping decision, time expression processing in most applications requires both recognition and normalization (i.e., resolving an expression to a specific value). A brief discussion on how the proposed syntactic types and recognition framework might aid or complicate the subsequent normalization task would make the paper more complete and increase its potential impact.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you further clarify the distinction between your "syntactic types" and the rule mechanisms in other systems? For example, SUTime's cascaded patterns also implicitly encode positional information. Is the primary advantage of your system the simplicity of the rule engine itself (i.e., a simple expansion loop vs. a more complex finite-state machine), rather than the typing system?
2.  Regarding the manual expansion for SynTime-E on the WikiWars dataset, could you provide a rough estimate of the manual effort involved (e.g., number of documents reviewed or hours spent) to identify the 16 new time tokens and 19 new modifiers? This would help readers gauge the practicality of applying this expansion method to new domains.
3.  Your system uses a POS tagger for disambiguation (e.g., for "May"). Have you analyzed the system's sensitivity to POS tagging errors, particularly on the Tweets dataset where standard POS taggers are known to perform less reliably?
