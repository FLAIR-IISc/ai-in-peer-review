- SynTime is a lightweight, rule-based time-expression recognizer using "syntactic types" and an expand-and-merge algorithm.
- Motivated by corpus analysis showing time expressions are short and built from a small set of core "time tokens" plus modifiers.
- Three-stage pipeline: identify core tokens → expand boundaries with adjacent modifiers/numerals → merge segments into final expressions.
- Evaluated on news, Wikipedia, and Twitter, showing particularly strong performance on informal Twitter data.
- Contribution: a simple, interpretable rule-based architecture that offers an alternative to complex ML or large hand-crafted rule systems.
- Contribution: strong empirical results (state-of-the-art or competitive), notably high recall across datasets and big gains on Tweets.
- Contribution: useful cross-domain corpus analysis highlighting the central role of time tokens and informing the system design.
- Strength: conceptual simplicity and transparency make the system understandable, debuggable, and extensible.
- Strength: robust quantitative performance and domain robustness, outperforming baselines on multiple datasets.
- Strength: consistently high recall (anchoring on time tokens present in ~93% of expressions) achieved without large precision loss.
- Strength: efficient, lightweight, real-time capable, and easy to extend by adding terms to dictionaries.
- Weakness: insufficient differentiation from prior rule-based systems (e.g., SUTime); novelty and practical impact not fully substantiated.
- Weakness: dependence on external resources (POS tagger and SUTime-derived lexicons) is not quantified; ablation needed to isolate algorithmic contribution.
- Weakness: lack of detailed qualitative error analysis to characterize false positives, false negatives, and systematic failure modes.
- Question: request a concrete example where SynTime succeeds but SUTime fails due to the syntactic-type distinction.
- Question: ask how the system performs when initialized with a minimal, manually curated lexicon to isolate the algorithm's effect.
