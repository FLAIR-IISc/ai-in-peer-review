- SynTime is a lightweight, rule-based time-expression recognizer using an "identify core time token → expand boundaries → merge segments" strategy.  
- It relies on "syntactic types" (e.g., PREFIX, TIME_UNIT) to drive boundary expansion rather than semantic labels.  
- Evaluated on multiple domains, SynTime achieves strong performance, with notably high recall and >91% F1 on Tweets (strict match).  
- Contribution: a simple, effective, high-recall identify-then-expand method for time recognition across genres.  
- Contribution: a clean, interpretable rule-based framework centered on token positional/function types.  
- Contribution: empirical cross-domain analysis showing time expressions are short, use small vocabularies, and contain core time tokens.  
- Strength: strong empirical results, outperforming baselines on TimeBank and Tweets, especially robust on noisy informal text.  
- Strength: conceptual simplicity and transparency make rules easy to understand, debug, and extend.  
- Strength: efficient and practical—real-time capable, uses regex/string matching, and can be bootstrapped from existing resources.  
- Strength: design is well-motivated by data analysis that justifies the identify-then-expand approach.  
- Weakness: the claimed novelty of "syntactic types" vs. existing semantic/pattern approaches (e.g., SUTime) may be overstated.  
- Weakness: initialization from SUTime token lists blurs the comparison and may mean SynTime benefits from SUTime's lexical coverage.  
- Weakness: manual domain adaptation (SynTime-E) is subjective, potentially unscalable, and lacks detail on effort and ambiguity handling.  
- Weakness: paper omits time normalization—recognition-only focus limits discussion of downstream resolution impacts.  
- Question: please clarify how syntactic types fundamentally differ from positional/pattern encodings in other systems and whether the rule engine simplicity is the main advantage.  
- Question: provide an estimate of manual effort (documents/hours) to find the 16 tokens and 19 modifiers for WikiWars to assess practicality.  
- Question: have you analyzed sensitivity to POS-tagging errors (e.g., on Tweets) given reliance on POS for disambiguation like "May"?
