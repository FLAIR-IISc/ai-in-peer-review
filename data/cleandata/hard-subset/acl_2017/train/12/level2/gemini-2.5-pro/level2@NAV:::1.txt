**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes SynTime, a lightweight, rule-based method for time expression recognition. The approach is motivated by an analysis of four datasets, which reveals that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and contain at least one core "time token." Based on these observations, the authors define a system of syntactic types (time token, modifier, numeral) and apply simple, heuristic boundary-expansion rules to identify expressions. The process involves identifying time tokens, searching their surroundings for modifiers/numerals to form segments, and finally merging these segments.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A simple yet highly effective heuristic method for time expression recognition. The novelty lies in its use of syntactic types and boundary-expansion rules, which proves to be a fast and efficient alternative to complex, fixed-rule systems or machine learning models.
2.  A clear empirical analysis of the structural properties of time expressions across varied datasets (news, Wikipedia, tweets), which provides strong justification for the system's design and links it to established linguistic principles like Zipf's law.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art or superior performance compared to strong baselines on the TimeBank and Tweets datasets. The exceptionally high recall across all datasets is particularly notable and serves as strong evidence for the validity of the core idea: identifying a central "time token" is sufficient to capture most expressions.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Simplification:** The approach of using syntactic roles and simple expansion heuristics, rather than complex semantic rules, is a well-motivated simplification. This design makes the system lightweight, fast, and easily expandable, which are significant practical advantages.
3.  **Strong Methodological Grounding:** The design of SynTime is directly justified by the insightful data analysis presented in Section 3. The observations about expression length, vocabulary size, and component roles provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on Existing Resources:** The system is initialized with token regular expressions from SUTime, a key baseline. While the proposed rule system is novel, the underlying lexical resources are not. This makes it difficult to disentangle how much of the performance gain comes from the novel heuristics versus the high quality of the pre-existing SUTime lexicons.
2.  **Inconsistent Performance and Analysis:** While performance on TimeBank and Tweets is excellent, the strict F1 score on the WikiWars dataset is notably lower than that of the baselines. The paper briefly attributes this to "descriptive time expressions," but a more detailed error analysis is needed to understand the failure modes of the heuristic approach on more complex or domain-specific language.
3.  **Manual Expansion Process:** The improved SynTime-E version relies on manually adding keywords from a training set. This process is subjective and not scalable, limiting the method's adaptability to new domains without significant human effort.

**Questions to Authors**

Could the authors provide an analysis or ablation study that helps disentangle the performance contribution of the novel heuristic rules from the contribution of the lexical resources borrowed from SUTime? For instance, how would a simplified SUTime (using only its lexicons with basic matching) perform in comparison? This would help clarify the precise source of the performance gains.
