**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces SynTime, a rule-based system for recognizing time expressions in text. The method is motivated by a linguistic analysis of four datasets, which finds that time expressions are typically short, use a small vocabulary, and almost always contain a core "time token" (e.g., 'month', 'Tuesday'). Based on these observations, SynTime employs a simple three-step process: 1) it identifies these core time tokens in a sentence using a predefined lexicon and POS tags; 2) it applies heuristic rules to expand the boundaries around each time token to include adjacent modifiers and numerals, forming "time segments"; 3) it merges adjacent or overlapping time segments to form the final time expressions. The authors evaluate their system on newswire, Wikipedia, and Twitter data, showing that it outperforms several state-of-the-art systems.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1.  The SynTime system itself, which is a simple, efficient, and highly effective rule-based method for time expression recognition. Its strong performance, particularly its high recall and excellent results on noisy Twitter data, is a significant achievement.
2.  A clear and useful analysis of the properties of time expressions across different text genres. This analysis provides a solid, data-driven motivation for the architectural choices made in the SynTime system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** SynTime achieves state-of-the-art or superior performance against strong baselines (HeidelTime, SUTime, UWTime) on three distinct datasets. The F1 scores on TimeBank (92.47 strict) and especially Tweets (91.77 strict) are excellent. The system's consistently high recall across all datasets validates its core design principle of anchoring the search on "time tokens."
2.  **Effectiveness on Informal Text:** The system's performance on the Tweets dataset is a major strength. Many NLP systems trained on clean, formal text struggle with the noise and variation in social media data. SynTime's ability to achieve over 91% F1 score demonstrates its robustness and practical utility for a challenging and important domain.
3.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to understand. In an era of increasingly complex and computationally expensive models, the paper presents a lightweight, efficient, and easily extensible system. The idea of identifying a seed token and then expanding its boundaries is an elegant and effective heuristic.
4.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The design of SynTime is directly and convincingly motivated by the preliminary data analysis in Section 3. The observations that time expressions are short and anchored by a time token provide a clear rationale for the system's architecture. This grounding makes the paper methodologically sound and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated Novelty of "Syntactic Types":** The paper's primary claim to novelty lies in its "syntactic" type system, which it contrasts with the "semantic" types of other rule-based systems like SUTime. This distinction is not clearly substantiated and feels more like a re-labeling of similar concepts. For example, categorizing a modifier by its position (e.g., PREFIX) is not necessarily more "syntactic" than categorizing it by its function (e.g., frequency modifier). The core novelty of SynTime appears to be its simple, dynamic boundary expansion heuristic, rather than a fundamentally new way of typing temporal words. The paper would be stronger if it focused on the novelty of its rule application mechanism.
2.  **Dependence on SUTime's Lexicon:** The SynTime system is initialized with token regular expressions directly from SUTime. This makes it difficult to disentangle the performance contribution of SynTime's heuristic rules from the contribution of SUTime's expertly curated and comprehensive lexicon. The high performance could be partially attributed to starting with an excellent set of seed tokens. An ablation study, for instance by building a lexicon from scratch from the training portion of the datasets, would be necessary to demonstrate the independent power of the SynTime framework.
3.  **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper provides a high-level overview of performance but lacks a qualitative error analysis. What are the primary failure modes of the boundary expansion and merging rules? For example, how does it handle complex or conjoined expressions like "Monday and Tuesday of next week"? A discussion of specific cases where the heuristics fail would provide deeper insight into the system's limitations and offer avenues for future work.
4.  **Simplified View of Learning-based Methods:** The paper's critique of machine learning methods (Section 5.2.1) is overly simplistic. It claims that learning systems struggle to generalize between patterns like ‘June 30, 1990’ and ‘October 12, 2008’. However, standard features for sequence labeling models (e.g., CRFs) have long included abstractions like character shapes (e.g., 'Xxxx dd, dddd'), word type information (e.g., 'is-a-month', 'is-a-4-digit-number'), and gazetteers to handle precisely this type of variation. While SynTime's performance is impressive, the justification for why it outperforms the learning-based UWTime system is not fully convincing.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you further elaborate on the fundamental difference between your "syntactic types" and the token-level categories used in systems like SUTime? The key innovation seems to be the boundary expansion algorithm itself. Would it be more accurate to frame the contribution around the rule mechanism rather than the type system?
2.  Your system's lexicon is bootstrapped from SUTime. How crucial is this specific initialization to the final performance? Would it be possible to learn the initial sets of time tokens, modifiers, and numerals from an annotated training corpus, and how do you think that would affect the system's performance and generality?
3.  Regarding the comparison with UWTime, a learning-based system, your results are very strong. Given that well-featured sequence models can capture abstract patterns, what specific types of time expressions do you believe SynTime's heuristic approach handles better than a learned model, and why?
