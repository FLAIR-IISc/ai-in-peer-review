**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to time expression recognition, called SynTime, which is based on syntactic types and simple heuristic rules. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations: (1) time expressions are very short, (2) only a small group of time-related keywords are used, (3) more than 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Inspired by part-of-speech, the authors define a syntactic type system for time expressions and propose SynTime, which defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules based on the idea of boundary expansion. The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets and show that SynTime outperforms the baselines, including rule-based time taggers and machine learning-based time taggers.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in time expression recognition.
2. The authors propose a novel approach to time expression recognition, called SynTime, which is based on syntactic types and simple heuristic rules.
3. The authors define a syntactic type system for time expressions and design simple heuristic rules based on the idea of boundary expansion.

**Strengths**

1. The authors provide a thorough analysis of time expressions from four datasets, which provides insights into the characteristics of time expressions.
2. The authors propose a novel approach to time expression recognition that is based on syntactic types and simple heuristic rules, which is different from existing approaches.
3. The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets, which shows the effectiveness of SynTime.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors assume that words are tokenized and POS tagged correctly, which may not always be the case in reality.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by SynTime, which would be helpful in understanding the limitations of the approach.
3. The authors do not compare SynTime with other machine learning-based approaches, which would be helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of SynTime.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by SynTime, including the types of errors and the frequency of each type of error?
2. How does SynTime handle time expressions that are not matched in any explicit rule?
3. Can you compare SynTime with other machine learning-based approaches, such as those using deep learning techniques?
4. How does SynTime handle time expressions in languages other than English?
5. Can you provide more details on the syntactic type system for time expressions and how it is used in SynTime?
