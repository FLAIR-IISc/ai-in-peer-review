**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a type-based approach, named SynTime, for recognizing time expressions in free text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations: (1) time expressions are very short, (2) only a small group of time-related keywords are used, (3) at least 93% of time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behaviors. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions. The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a type-based approach, SynTime, for recognizing time expressions, which defines syntactic types for tokens and designs simple heuristic rules based on the idea of boundary expansion.
2. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations that provide evidence for the principle of least effort in time expression.
3. The authors evaluate SynTime against three state-of-the-art baselines on three datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1. The authors provide a thorough analysis of time expressions from four datasets, which provides insights into the characteristics of time expressions.
2. The proposed approach, SynTime, is simple and effective, and its performance is comparable to or better than state-of-the-art baselines.
3. The authors provide a detailed description of the SynTime approach, including its construction, time expression recognition, and expansion.
4. The evaluation of SynTime on three datasets demonstrates its robustness and effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors assume that words are tokenized and POS tagged correctly, which may not always be the case in reality.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of SynTime with other machine learning-based methods, which may be more effective for certain datasets.
3. The authors do not discuss the limitations of the SynTime approach, such as its inability to recognize descriptive time expressions.
4. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the SynTime approach can be extended to other languages or domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the SynTime approach to other languages or domains?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of SynTime with other machine learning-based methods?
3. How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the SynTime approach, such as its inability to recognize descriptive time expressions?
4. Can the authors provide more details on the manual annotation process for the Tweets dataset?
