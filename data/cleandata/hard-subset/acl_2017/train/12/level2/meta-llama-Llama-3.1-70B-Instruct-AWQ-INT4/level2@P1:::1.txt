**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach for recognizing time expressions in free text, called SynTime. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and observe that they are generally short, expressed using a small vocabulary, and exhibit similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, they define a syntactic type system for time expressions and propose a type-based time expression tagger. SynTime uses simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions and achieves state-of-the-art performance on three benchmark datasets.

**Contributions**

1. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of time expressions from four datasets, which sheds light on the characteristics of time expressions in free text.
2. They propose a novel type-based approach for recognizing time expressions, which is inspired by part-of-speech tagging.
3. They demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime on three benchmark datasets, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of time expressions, which is a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. The proposed approach, SynTime, is simple and efficient, making it suitable for real-time applications.
3. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SynTime, which is a significant improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that words are tokenized and POS-tagged correctly, which may not always be the case in real-world applications.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of SynTime with other machine learning-based methods, which would be useful for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
3. The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed approach, such as its ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words or domain-specific time expressions.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of incorrect tokenization and POS-tagging, which may affect the performance of SynTime?
2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of SynTime with other machine learning-based methods, including their strengths and weaknesses?
3. How do you plan to extend SynTime to handle out-of-vocabulary words and domain-specific time expressions?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed approach. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors have done a good job of demonstrating the effectiveness of SynTime. However, there are some areas that require further clarification and discussion, such as the limitations of the proposed approach and its potential applications.
