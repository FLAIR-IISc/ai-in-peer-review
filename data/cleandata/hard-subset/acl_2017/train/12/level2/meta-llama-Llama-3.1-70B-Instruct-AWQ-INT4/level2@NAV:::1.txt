**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a type-based approach, named SynTime, to recognize time expressions in free text. The authors analyze time expressions from four datasets and make four observations: (1) time expressions are short, (2) a small group of words are used to express time information, (3) most time expressions contain at least one time token, and (4) words in time expressions demonstrate similar syntactic behavior. Based on these observations, SynTime defines three main syntactic types (time token, modifier, and numeral) and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions. The approach is evaluated on three datasets and outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Analysis of time expressions**: The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of time expressions from four datasets, which provides insights into the characteristics of time expressions.
2. **SynTime approach**: The authors propose a type-based approach, SynTime, which defines syntactic types and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions.
3. **Evaluation on multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate SynTime on three datasets, including TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets, and demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: SynTime is a novel approach that defines syntactic types and designs simple heuristic rules to recognize time expressions, which is different from existing rule-based and machine learning-based methods.
2. **Good performance**: SynTime outperforms state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets, including TimeBank, WikiWars, and Tweets.
3. **Lightweight and flexible**: SynTime is an extremely lightweight rule-based tagger that runs in real-time and can be easily expanded by adding keywords.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scope**: SynTime focuses only on time expression recognition and does not address time expression normalization.
2. **Assumes perfect tokenization and POS tagging**: SynTime assumes that words are tokenized and POS tagged correctly, which may not always be the case in reality.
3. **May not generalize to other languages**: The authors do not evaluate SynTime on other languages, and it is unclear whether the approach will generalize to other languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend SynTime to address time expression normalization?
2. How do you plan to handle cases where tokenization and POS tagging are not perfect?
3. Have you considered evaluating SynTime on other languages, and if so, what are the results?
