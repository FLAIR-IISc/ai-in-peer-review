- Paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel as similarity.  
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks show the method is effective.  
- Strength: Problem is clearly motivated and defined.  
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty.  
- Strength: The max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity supports efficient training.  
- Strength: Paper is mostly well written.  
- Question: Why was the number of Gaussian components k set to 2, what criteria determined this, and does increasing k hurt performance?  
- Question: How does the learned distribution look for words with only one dominant meaning?  
- Question: All experiments use spherical covariances—was this purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect performance?  
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.  
- Minor: Reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
