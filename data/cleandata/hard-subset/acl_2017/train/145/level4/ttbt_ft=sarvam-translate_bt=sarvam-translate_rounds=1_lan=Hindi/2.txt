This work utilizes Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates its ability to capture the meanings of multiple words for polysemy. The training process is conducted based on a maximum-margin objective. The expected probability kernel is used as the similarity between the distribution of two words. The results of experiments on word similarity and entailment functions demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed work.

- Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and defined. Gaussian mixtures are much more expressive than deterministic vector representations. It can potentially capture the meanings of different words by its modes with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work represents a significant contribution to word embedding.
This work proposes a maximum-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measures for efficient training.
This paper is mostly well-written.

- Weaknesses:
See below for some questions.
- General Discussion:
In a Gaussian mixture model, the number of Gaussian components (k) is usually an important parameter. In the experiments in this paper, k is set to 2. What is your criterion for selecting k? Does increasing k harm the performance of this model? What does the learned distribution look like for a word that has only one popular meaning?
I noticed that you use a spherical case in all the experiments (the covariance matrix is reduced to a single number). Is this purely for computational efficiency?
I wonder what the performance is like using a normal diagonal covariance matrix. Since in this more general case, the Gaussian mixture defines different degrees of uncertainty in different directions in the semantic space, which seems more interesting.
