- Clearly motivated problem: polysemy framed well.  
- GMMs are significantly more expressive than deterministic vectors, capturing multiple word senses via mixture modes, masses, and uncertainty.  
- Well-designed max-margin learning objective with a closed-form expected likelihood kernel enables efficient training.  
- Empirical results on word similarity and textual entailment support the model’s effectiveness.  
- Unjustified choice of number of mixture components (k); experiments fix k=2 without explanation and no analysis of larger k or overfitting risk.  
- Lack of analysis for words with a single dominant sense—need to show learned distributions adapt (e.g., “apple”).  
- Use of spherical covariances for efficiency limits expressiveness; diagonal (or full) covariances could model directional uncertainty and might improve performance.  
- Authors should evaluate whether the covariance simplification harms results and provide experiments with more general covariance structures.  
- Minor: Table 4 is cited but not discussed.  
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. missing publication year.  
- Overall: Promising and well-written contribution, but credibility and generalizability are weakened by missing justifications for k and covariance choices; addressing these would strengthen the paper.
