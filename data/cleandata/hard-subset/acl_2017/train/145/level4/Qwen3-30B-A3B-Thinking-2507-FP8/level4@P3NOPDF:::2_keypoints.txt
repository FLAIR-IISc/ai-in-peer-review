- Proposes modeling polysemy via Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians for multiple senses.  
- Uses a max-margin learning objective with an expected likelihood kernel for efficient closed-form similarity computation.  
- Empirical results show improvements on word similarity and textual entailment tasks compared to baseline embeddings.  
- Strength: Clearly motivated framework addressing polysemy, with GMMs offering greater expressiveness than deterministic vectors.  
- Strength: GMMs capture distinct senses, probability mass, and uncertainty, representing a meaningful advancement in embeddings.  
- Strength: Max-margin objective and closed-form similarity enable efficient training, suitable for large-scale use.  
- Strength: Paper is well-written and logically structured.  
- Weakness: Choice of mixture components k fixed at 2 lacks justification; need analysis of varying k, per-word tuning, and effects on monosemous words.  
- Weakness: Use of spherical (isotropic) covariances limits directional uncertainty modeling; should evaluate diagonal covariances for richer semantics.  
- Minor: Table 4 is not cited or discussed in the text.  
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.  
- Overall: Compelling idea with solid empirical validation, but evaluation weakened by missing hyperparameter (k) and covariance-structure sensitivity analyses; recommend acceptance pending clarifications.
