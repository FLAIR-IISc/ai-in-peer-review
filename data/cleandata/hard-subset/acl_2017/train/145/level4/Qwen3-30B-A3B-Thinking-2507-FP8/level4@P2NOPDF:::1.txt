

Review: Multimodal Word Distributions  
**Strengths:** This is an excellent paper presenting a well-motivated, technically sound contribution. The core idea—extending Vilnis and McCallum’s (2014) unimodal Gaussian word representation to a multimodal Gaussian mixture model (WMG) for capturing polysemy—is compelling and clearly articulated. The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to method, experiments, and analysis. The empirical evaluation is thorough, correctly implemented, and the qualitative results in Table 1 align precisely with the model’s intended capabilities. Overall, this work makes a significant, principled advance in modeling polysemy through distributional semantics.  

**Weaknesses:** The primary opportunity for improvement lies in the comparative analysis. While the paper effectively demonstrates WMG’s advantages over unimodal baselines (W2G), the discussion of related work and comparative results could be strengthened by more explicitly contextualizing WMG within the broader landscape of multi-sense embedding approaches.  

**Specific Comments for Clarification:**  
1.  **Clarify Distinction from Tian et al. (2014):** The paper should briefly explain how WMG differs from Tian et al.’s (2014) mixture model approach for multi-prototype word representation. While both use mixture models, WMG’s use of Gaussian mixtures for *distributional* representations (vs. Tian’s discrete prototypes) and its direct integration with the Vilnis & McCallum framework are key distinctions warranting clarification in the related work or method section.  
2.  **Expand Related Work and Comparisons:** The related work section would benefit from citing and discussing three relevant papers:  
    *   Neelakantan et al. (EMNLP 2014) on *Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word*.  
    *   Li and Jurafsky (EMNLP 2015) on *Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?*  
    *   Liu et al. (AAAI 2015) on *Topical Word Embeddings*.  
    Including these establishes a stronger foundation and clarifies WMG’s position relative to existing techniques. Crucially, the results from these approaches should be compared in Tables 3 and 4 (or a new table) to demonstrate WMG’s relative performance and niche.  
3.  **Address SWCS Performance Discrepancy:** The paper notes that WMG underperforms W2G on the SWCS dataset (Table 3). The authors should explicitly discuss this finding. What factors might explain this counterintuitive result? Is it due to the specific nature of the SWCS task, the clustering strategy, or a limitation of the multimodal representation for that particular context? A brief analysis would significantly strengthen the discussion.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong, high-quality paper that advances multimodal word representation effectively. The core contribution is novel, well-executed, and supported by robust experiments. The suggested revisions are minor and primarily focused on enhancing the contextualization of the work within the field and clarifying specific points for the reader. Addressing the points above—particularly adding the cited works, integrating their results into the comparison tables, and clarifying the SWCS finding—will elevate an already excellent paper to an outstanding one. The authors have clearly demonstrated a deep understanding of the problem and a sound technical approach. The work is highly relevant to ACL audiences and makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics and polysemy modeling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
