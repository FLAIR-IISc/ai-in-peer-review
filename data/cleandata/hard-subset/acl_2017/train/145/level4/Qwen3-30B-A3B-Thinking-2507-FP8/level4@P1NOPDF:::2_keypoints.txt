- Paper proposes modeling polysemy with Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) for word representations, showing effectiveness in capturing multiple meanings.
- Uses a max-margin learning objective and a closed-form expected likelihood kernel for efficient training.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the approach's efficacy.
- Strength: Problem is clearly motivated and well-defined.
- Strength: GMMs provide greater expressiveness than deterministic vectors, capturing multiple senses, probability mass, and uncertainty.
- Strength: The method is an important contribution to word embeddings and is generally well-written.
- Weakness: Number of GMM components k is fixed to 2 without justification—unclear criteria for this choice and effects of increasing k.
- Weakness: Unclear model behavior for words with a single dominant meaning and what learned distributions look like in those cases.
- Weakness: Use of spherical covariance is assumed for all experiments—question whether this is solely for efficiency and what benefits a diagonal covariance would bring.
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
