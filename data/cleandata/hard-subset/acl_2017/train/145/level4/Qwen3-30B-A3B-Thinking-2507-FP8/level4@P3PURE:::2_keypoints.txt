- Paper proposes using Gaussian mixture models for word representations to capture multiple senses, trained with a max-margin objective and an expected likelihood kernel similarity.
- Strength: Clearly motivated problem and well-defined approach.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures provide greater expressiveness than deterministic vectors, allowing distinct sense components, probability mass, and uncertainty modeling.
- Strength: Max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Strength: Experimental results on word similarity and entailment validate the method; paper is well-written.
- Weakness: All experiments fix the number of mixture components k=2 without justification; unclear how k selection affects performance.
- Weakness: Unclear model behavior for words with a single dominant sense and what the learned distribution looks like in such cases.
- Weakness: Use of spherical covariances only—question whether this is solely for efficiency and what gains/insights diagonal or full covariances would provide.
- Minor: Table 4 is not cited in the text.
- Minor: Reference for Luong et al. is missing a publication year.
- Note: The authors’ response to these points has been considered.
