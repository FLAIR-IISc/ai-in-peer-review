

This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling polysemy using Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) for word representations, where each word is represented as a mixture of Gaussians capturing multiple meanings. The authors employ a max-margin learning objective with an expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity computation between word distributions. Experiments on word similarity and textual entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of this method compared to baseline embeddings.

**Strengths:**  
The work addresses a critical limitation of standard word embeddings—handling polysemy—through a well-motivated and clearly defined framework. GMMs offer significantly greater expressiveness than deterministic vector representations, enabling the model to capture distinct word senses via mixture modes, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty. This constitutes a meaningful advancement in the field of word embeddings. The max-margin objective with closed-form similarity computation ensures efficient training, a practical advantage for large-scale applications. The paper is also well-written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses and Key Questions:**  
While the approach is promising, two critical aspects require clarification:  
1. **Choice of mixture components (k):** The paper consistently sets *k* = 2 across experiments. The authors should justify this choice. Is *k* = 2 optimal for all word types, or should it be tuned per word? Crucially, how does performance change with higher *k* (e.g., *k* = 3 or 4)? For words with a single dominant sense (e.g., "apple" as fruit, not company), what does the learned GMM distribution look like? If the model forces a second component, does this degrade performance for unambiguous words?  
2. **Covariance structure:** All experiments assume a spherical covariance matrix (i.e., isotropic Gaussians). This choice appears driven by computational efficiency, but it limits the model’s ability to represent directional uncertainty in semantic space. For instance, a general diagonal covariance matrix would allow varying uncertainty along different semantic axes (e.g., "bank" as river vs. financial institution might have distinct uncertainties in "water" vs. "money" directions). The authors should report results with diagonal covariances to assess whether this richer representation improves performance or captures more nuanced semantics.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Table 4 is not cited or discussed in the main text.  
- The reference to Luong et al. omits the publication year.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The core idea of using GMMs for polysemy is compelling and well-executed in principle, with strong empirical results on standard benchmarks. However, the lack of discussion around hyperparameter sensitivity (*k* and covariance structure) weakens the evaluation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. The authors’ response to the above queries should clarify whether *k* = 2 is a heuristic or empirically justified, and whether diagonal covariances yield meaningful gains. If the spherical assumption is indeed a limitation without analysis, this should be acknowledged as a constraint of the current work.  

Given the novelty of the approach and solid experimental validation, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. With clarifications on the hyperparameters and covariance assumptions, it would be suitable for publication. I recommend acceptance pending satisfactory addressing of the above points in the final version.
