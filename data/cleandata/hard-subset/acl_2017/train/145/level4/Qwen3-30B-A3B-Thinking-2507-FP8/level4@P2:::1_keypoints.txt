- Introduces a Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings to capture multiple word senses via multimodal distributions.  
- Proposes an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for tractable training of Gaussian mixtures.  
- Provides empirical validation showing improvements over unimodal methods on word similarity and entailment tasks.  

- Novel multimodal representation learns interpretable semantic components for polysemous words (e.g., "rock" → stone vs. music).  
- Expected likelihood kernel gives a closed-form energy between mixtures, avoiding KL instability and supporting scalable, stable training.  
- Strong empirical gains over unimodal baselines and competitive multi-prototype methods on benchmarks without external lexical resources.  

- Does not adequately compare to other state-of-the-art multi-prototype approaches (e.g., Tian et al. 2014, Chen et al. 2014), leaving unclear the source of advantages.  
- Fails to explain the unexpected performance drop of w2gm vs. w2g on SCWS despite overall better results elsewhere.  
- Insufficient discussion and concrete metrics on scalability and runtime to substantiate claims of training on billions of tokens.  

- How does w2gm's SCWS performance compare directly to Tian et al. (2014) and Chen et al. (2014)?  
- Why does w2gm outperform on entailment but slightly underperform on SCWS—window size, dataset characteristics, or hyperparameters?  
- Is K=2 generally sufficient given little gain from K=3, and what are the trade-offs between model complexity and performance?  

- Recommend citing recent multi-sense embedding work (Li & Jurafsky 2015; Neelakantan et al. 2014) to better position the contribution.  
- Suggest briefly contrasting the method with Tian et al.'s EM-based approach to clarify modeling and inference differences.  
- Advise including computational cost comparisons (e.g., training time vs. word2vec) to enhance practical relevance.
