

This work employs Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to represent words, demonstrating their potential for capturing polysemy through multiple semantic modes. The approach trains word distributions using a max-margin objective, with the expected likelihood kernel measuring similarity between word distributions. Experiments on word similarity and textual entailment tasks confirm the model's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**  
The problem of polysemy is clearly motivated and framed. GMMs offer significantly greater expressiveness than deterministic vector representations, enabling the model to capture distinct word senses via mixture modes, associated probability masses, and uncertainty distributions. This represents a meaningful contribution to word embedding research. The paper proposes a well-designed max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training. The writing is generally clear and well-structured.

**Weaknesses:**  
The choice of Gaussian mixture components (*k*) requires justification. The experiments fix *k* = 2 without explanation. What criteria guided this selection? Does increasing *k* improve performance, or does it risk overfitting? Crucially, how does the model handle words with a single dominant sense (e.g., "apple" as fruit vs. company)? The learned distribution for such words should be examined to validate the model's adaptability.  

The paper exclusively uses spherical Gaussians (covariance matrix reduced to a scalar), citing computational efficiency. However, this choice limits the model's expressiveness. A diagonal covariance matrix would allow directional uncertainty in semantic space—e.g., higher uncertainty along certain semantic axes—which could better reflect nuanced meaning. The authors should address whether this simplification impacts performance and provide results for a more general covariance structure.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Table 4 is cited in the text but not discussed.  
- Reference to Luong et al. lacks a publication year.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This work presents a promising GMM-based approach to polysemy, with strong theoretical motivation and effective empirical results. However, the lack of justification for key hyperparameters (*k* and covariance structure) weakens the model’s credibility and generalizability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable step toward richer word representations. I have reviewed the authors' response.
