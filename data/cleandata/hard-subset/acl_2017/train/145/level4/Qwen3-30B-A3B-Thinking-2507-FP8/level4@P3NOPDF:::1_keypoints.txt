- Extends unimodal Gaussian word embeddings to multimodal Gaussian mixture models to model polysemy by representing words as sets of Gaussians.  
- Core innovation: sense-specific Gaussians per word, allowing multiple word senses in a unified embedding space.  
- The approach is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented with a transparent training procedure.  
- Experimental evaluation shows consistent improvements over unimodal baselines across multiple datasets.  
- Qualitative analysis (Table 1) demonstrates intuitive sense clusters that align with expectations.  
- Strength: elegant, principled solution to polysemy with rigorous validation, ablation studies, and clear writing.  
- Weakness: related work comparison is insufficiently detailed, weakening the novelty framing.  
- Suggestion: explicitly clarify distinctions from Neelakantan et al. (2014) regarding mixture usage and sense modeling.  
- Suggestion: expand related work citations to include Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).  
- Suggestion: include comparative results from those prior multi-sense embedding papers in Tables 3–4 for contextualized performance.  
- Suggestion: analyze and explain the W2GM performance drop versus W2G on the SWCS dataset (dataset characteristics, hyperparameters, or limitations).  
- Conclusion: strong contribution with minor revisions to related work and comparative analysis; recommendation for acceptance.
