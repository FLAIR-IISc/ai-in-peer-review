

This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling polysemy using Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) for word representations, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing multiple word meanings. The method employs a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure (the expected likelihood kernel) to efficiently train the model. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks confirm the approach's efficacy.

**Strengths:**  
The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. GMMs offer significantly greater expressiveness than deterministic vector representations, enabling the model to capture distinct word senses through multiple modes, alongside associated probability mass and uncertainty. This work makes an important contribution to the field of word embeddings. The max-margin objective with closed-form similarity provides an efficient training mechanism, and the paper is generally well-written.

**Weaknesses:**  
The paper raises several critical questions that require clarification:

1.  **GMM Component Selection (k):** The experiments set the number of Gaussian components (k) to 2. What criteria guided this choice? Does increasing k degrade model performance, and if so, why? Additionally, how does the model behave for words with only a single dominant meaning (i.e., minimal polysemy)? What does the learned distribution look like in such cases?

2.  **Covariance Assumption:** The authors use a spherical covariance matrix (where the covariance reduces to a scalar) across all experiments. Is this choice solely for computational efficiency? What would be the impact of using a general diagonal covariance matrix instead? A diagonal covariance would allow for varying uncertainty along different semantic dimensions, potentially capturing richer structural properties of word meaning—a point of significant interest.
