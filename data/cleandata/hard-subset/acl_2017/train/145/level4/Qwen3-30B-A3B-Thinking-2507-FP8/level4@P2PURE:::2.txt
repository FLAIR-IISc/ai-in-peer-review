

Review:  
This work employs Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates their potential for capturing multiple word meanings in polysemy. Training utilizes a max-margin objective, with the expected likelihood kernel measuring similarity between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks confirm the approach's effectiveness.  

- Strengths:  
The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures offer significantly greater expressiveness than deterministic vector representations, enabling the capture of distinct word senses through mixture components, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty. This work constitutes a meaningful contribution to word embedding research. The proposed max-margin learning objective, featuring a closed-form similarity measure for efficient training, is well-motivated. The paper is generally well-written.  

- Weaknesses:  
Key questions remain regarding hyperparameter choices:  
1. The number of Gaussian components (k) is typically critical in mixture models. The experiments fix k=2—what criteria guided this selection? Does increasing k degrade performance?  
2. The experiments use a spherical covariance matrix (reduced to a scalar) solely for computational efficiency? How would performance change with a general diagonal covariance matrix, which allows direction-specific uncertainty in semantic space—a more semantically nuanced representation?  
3. What does the learned distribution resemble for a word with only one dominant meaning?  

- Minor Comments:
