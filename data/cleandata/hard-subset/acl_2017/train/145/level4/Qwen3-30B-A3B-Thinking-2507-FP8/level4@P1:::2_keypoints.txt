- Summary: Introduces w2gm, a Gaussian mixture word-embedding model capturing multiple senses via mixture components, trained with an expected-likelihood kernel and max-margin objective, showing empirical gains on similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Strength 1: Strong motivation—multimodal mixtures address polysemy better than unimodal Gaussians or deterministic vectors, with illustrative examples and visualization.  
- Strength 2: Practical, efficient training—expected likelihood kernel gives closed-form energy, combined with max-margin loss and numerical tricks to scale to large corpora.  
- Strength 3: Strong empirical results—consistent improvements over w2g and word2vec on similarity and entailment benchmarks, attributed to multimodality.  
- Weakness 1: No justification for choosing K=2—unclear how K was selected and how performance changes for K>2.  
- Weakness 2: Over-reliance on spherical covariance—no exploration of diagonal covariance or discussion of trade-offs for direction-specific uncertainty.  
- Weakness 3: Insufficient analysis of unimodal words—unclear whether w2gm collapses to unimodal representations or unnecessarily splits single-sense words.  
- Question 1: For single-dominant-sense words (e.g., "apple"), does the learned mixture behave like a unimodal distribution or split across components?  
- Question 2: How does using spherical covariance affect semantic interpretation and would diagonal covariance improve entailment/asymmetric tasks?  
- Additional comment 1: Table 4 is referenced but not discussed—please clarify its relevance.  
- Additional comment 2: Bibliography issue—"Luong et al." lacks a publication year.
