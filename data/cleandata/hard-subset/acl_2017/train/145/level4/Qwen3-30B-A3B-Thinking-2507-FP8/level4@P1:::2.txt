

Summary: This paper introduces a Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) for word embeddings that explicitly represents multiple word senses through multimodal distributions, contrasting with unimodal Gaussian embeddings (w2g) and deterministic vectors. The core contributions are: (1) a multimodal representation capturing distinct word meanings via mixture components, (2) an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel for efficient training, and (3) empirical validation showing superior performance on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

Strengths:
1. **Compelling motivation for multimodal representations**: The paper effectively argues that unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) fail to model polysemy due to overly diffuse uncertainty, while deterministic embeddings cannot capture multiple meanings. The w2gm model directly addresses this by using mixture components to isolate distinct senses (e.g., "rock" for stone vs. music), as demonstrated in Table 1 and the interactive visualization. This represents a significant conceptual advance over prior probabilistic approaches.
2. **Practical and efficient training framework**: The use of the expected likelihood kernel enables closed-form computation of the energy function for Gaussian mixtures—a critical advantage over KL-based alternatives. Combined with the max-margin objective and numerical stabilizations (e.g., diagonal covariance, log-space optimization), the model scales to billion-word corpora in days. This makes the approach both theoretically sound and practically viable.
3. **Strong empirical results**: w2gm consistently outperforms w2g and word2vec across multiple word similarity datasets (e.g., +2.3 Spearman on WS-R, +4.8 on MEN) and achieves state-of-the-art results on word entailment (Table 5). Crucially, the gains are attributed to the multimodal structure (e.g., cosine similarity between rock and stone improves from 0.029 in w2g to 0.586 in w2gm), not just hyperparameter tuning.

Weaknesses:
1. **Lack of justification for K=2**: The paper uses two mixture components throughout experiments but provides no rationale for this choice. How was K selected? Does performance degrade with K>2 (e.g., for words with three senses like "cell" in Table 1)? The brief mention of K=3 results (Section 4.2) lacks quantitative comparison, leaving uncertainty about optimal component count.
2. **Over-reliance on spherical covariance**: The model uses spherical covariance (a single scalar per component) for computational efficiency but does not justify this choice or explore diagonal covariance. Generalizing to diagonal matrices would allow direction-specific uncertainty (e.g., varying confidence along semantic axes), which could better model nuanced word meanings—yet the paper offers no ablation study or discussion of this trade-off.
3. **Insufficient analysis of unimodal words**: The paper claims w2gm handles polysemy but does not evaluate how it represents words with a single dominant meaning (e.g., "apple" in Table 1). Does the model default to a unimodal distribution for such words, or does it force unnecessary components? This gap affects the model’s generality.

Questions to Authors:
1. For words with a single dominant sense (e.g., "apple"), how does the model’s learned distribution (e.g., mixture weights, covariance) compare to w2g? Does it maintain the unimodal structure, or does it unnecessarily split the representation?
2. How does the spherical covariance assumption impact the semantic interpretation of the embedding space? Could diagonal covariance (with direction-specific uncertainty) yield measurable improvements on entailment tasks, where asymmetric uncertainty matters (e.g., "aircraft" → "vehicle" vs. "vehicle" → "aircraft")?
