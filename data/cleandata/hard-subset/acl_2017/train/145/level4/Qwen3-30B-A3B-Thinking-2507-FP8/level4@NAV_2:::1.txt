

Review: Multimodal Word Distributions
Summary: This paper introduces a novel approach for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings, extending Vilnis and McCallum's (2014) unimodal Gaussian representation. The main contributions are: (1) a multimodal representation that effectively captures polysemy through Gaussian mixture models, (2) an energy-based max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel for training, and (3) improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks compared to existing methods.

Strengths: 
1. The model successfully addresses polysemy by learning multiple meaningful components per word, as demonstrated in Table 1 where distinct meanings of polysemous words are captured by different mixture components.
2. The experimental results show consistent and significant improvements over baseline methods (word2vec skip-grams and Gaussian embeddings) across multiple word similarity datasets, particularly on WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, and RW.
3. The qualitative analysis provides clear evidence that the model learns interpretable representations, with each mixture component corresponding to a distinct meaning of the word, as verified through nearest neighbor analysis.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison against similar approaches could be extended to include more detailed analysis of the relationship between the proposed method and existing multi-prototype embedding approaches, such as Tian et al. (2014).
2. The related work section omits several relevant papers that propose similar multi-prototype embedding techniques, including Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li and Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).
3. The paper does not adequately explain the performance drop of w2gm compared to w2g on the SWCS dataset, which appears contradictory to the general trend of improvements.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the relationship between your approach and Tian et al. (2014), particularly regarding the differences in their mixture model implementation and training objectives?
2. Why does the w2gm model perform worse than w2g on the SWCS dataset, despite consistently outperforming w2g on other evaluation metrics?
