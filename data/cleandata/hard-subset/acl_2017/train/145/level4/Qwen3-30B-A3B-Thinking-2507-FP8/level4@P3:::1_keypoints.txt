- Proposal: Introduces a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) representing words as multimodal distributions to capture multiple meanings.  
- Method: Uses an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel allowing closed-form similarity computation and efficient training.  
- Empirical claim: Shows improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks versus word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.  
- Strength: Effectively models polysemy—learns distinct Gaussian components for different senses (e.g., "rock", "bank") as shown qualitatively.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation via the expected likelihood kernel, enabling analytic tractability for Gaussian mixtures.  
- Strength: Convincing empirical gains on multiple similarity datasets and superior entailment performance (average precision and F1).  
- Weakness: Novelty overstated—fails to adequately acknowledge prior multi-prototype work (Tian et al. 2014; Huang et al. 2012; Nalisnick & Ravi 2015).  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art multi-prototype methods; missing results from Huang (2012) and Tian (2014).  
- Weakness: No explanation for why multimodal model (w2gm) underperforms single-prototype (w2g) on the SCWS dataset.  
- Question: Clarify relationship between w2g and the original Vilnis & McCallum (2014) Gaussian embedding and why w2g outperforms it.  
- Question: Explain why w2gm fails on SCWS and whether this is dataset-specific or a general limitation; suggest fixes.  
- Question: Address vocabulary cutoff (discarding words <100 occurrences) and its impact on capturing rare word meanings.
