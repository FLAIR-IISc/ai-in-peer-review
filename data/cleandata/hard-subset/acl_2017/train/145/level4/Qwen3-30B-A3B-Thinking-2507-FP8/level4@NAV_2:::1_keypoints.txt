- Introduces Gaussian mixture models to learn multimodal word distributions that capture multiple word meanings.
- Proposes an energy-based max-margin objective using an expected likelihood kernel for training.
- Shows improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks versus existing methods.
- Effectively models polysemy by learning multiple meaningful mixture components per word (Table 1).
- Consistently and significantly outperforms baselines (word2vec skip-grams and Gaussian embeddings) on many similarity datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW).
- Qualitative nearest-neighbor analysis demonstrates interpretable components corresponding to distinct word senses.
- Comparison to other multi-prototype embedding methods (e.g., Tian et al. 2014) is limited and could be expanded.
- Related work omits several relevant multi-prototype papers (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li and Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015).
- Paper fails to explain why w2gm performs worse than w2g on the SWCS dataset despite general improvements.
- Request clarification on differences between this approach and Tian et al. (2014) in mixture implementation and training objectives.
- Ask for an explanation of the w2gm performance drop on SWCS compared to w2g.
