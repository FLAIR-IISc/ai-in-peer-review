

This work employs Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates their potential for capturing multiple word senses in polysemy. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, with the expected likelihood kernel serving as the similarity measure between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks validate the approach's effectiveness.

- Strengths:
  The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures offer significantly greater expressiveness than deterministic vector representations, enabling the capture of distinct word senses through mixture components, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty around these modes. This represents a meaningful contribution to the field of word embeddings. The authors propose a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training. The paper is well-written overall.

- Weaknesses:
  The paper sets the number of Gaussian components (k) to 2 in all experiments. What criteria guided this choice? Does increasing k beyond 2 degrade performance? Additionally, how does the model behave for words with only a single dominant sense—what does the learned distribution look like in such cases?
  The experiments exclusively use spherical Gaussians (where the covariance matrix reduces to a scalar multiple of the identity matrix). Is this constraint solely for computational efficiency? What performance gains or insights would arise from using a general diagonal covariance matrix, which permits varying uncertainty directions in semantic space—a more nuanced representation?

- Minor Comments:
