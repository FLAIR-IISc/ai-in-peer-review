- Summary: Proposes Gaussian mixture word embeddings to model multiple senses per word, trained with a max-margin objective and expected likelihood kernel, showing gains on similarity and entailment tasks.

- Contribution 1: Introduces multimodal (Gaussian mixture) embeddings that capture distinct meanings of polysemous words.

- Contribution 2: Develops an energy-based training objective using the expected likelihood kernel for tractable learning of Gaussian mixtures.

- Contribution 3: Provides empirical validation showing consistent improvements over point-based and unimodal probabilistic embeddings.

- Strength 1: Effectively resolves unimodal variance issues by assigning separate mixture components to different senses, with clear qualitative examples.

- Strength 2: The expected likelihood kernel is well-motivated, yields closed-form computations, and the paper includes numerical-stability implementation details.

- Strength 3: Strong empirical results, including state-of-the-art similarity scores and improved entailment performance, especially for polysemous words, plus a visualization tool.

- Weakness 1: Insufficient comparison to recent multi-sense/multi-prototype embedding methods; lacks comprehensive empirical comparisons and explanations.

- Weakness 2: No analysis explaining why the multimodal model underperforms the unimodal model on SCWS despite SCWS focusing on polysemy.

- Weakness 3: Fails to clearly differentiate technical novelty from prior mixture-based multi-sense approaches like Tian et al. (2014) and Huang et al. (2012).

- Question 1: Why does w2gm underperform w2g on SCWS despite SCWS emphasizing polysemous words?

- Question 2: How does performance vary as the number of mixture components K increases beyond 2, and what are the complexity/performance trade-offs?

- Question 3: Can the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art multi-sense embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., Tian et al.) to substantiate superiority?
