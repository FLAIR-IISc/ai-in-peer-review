- Introduces a Gaussian mixture word embedding model to represent multiple meanings per word, addressing polysemy limitations of unimodal embeddings.
- Proposes an expected likelihood kernel energy with a max-margin objective enabling closed-form similarity computation and efficient training.
- Empirically shows consistent improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks, especially for datasets testing polysemy.

- Motivation convincingly demonstrates multimodal need (e.g., "bank") and qualitative results show distinct learned senses.
- The expected likelihood kernel is theoretically sound and allows scalable closed-form similarity computations; trained on 3.5B tokens.
- Thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets with notable gains on polysemy-focused sets (e.g., SCWS).
- Qualitative nearest-neighbor analysis shows interpretable components separating senses (e.g., "rock" into stone and music).

- No adequate justification for choosing K=2 and limited analysis of how varying K (e.g., K=3+) affects performance.
- Using spherical covariances limits expressiveness; no comparison with diagonal or full covariances is provided.
- Lack of analysis on words with a single dominant meaning to assess whether multimodality adds unnecessary complexity or noise.
- Insufficient exploration of training sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters; robustness not demonstrated.

- Why was K set to 2—was this chosen by systematic validation, and how does performance change for K=3 or 4?
- What is the impact of using diagonal or full covariance matrices versus spherical on expressiveness and performance?
- How does the model behave for mostly monosemous words—do component weights/uncertainty avoid introducing noise?
- Given limited data for extra senses, how do authors recommend addressing data sparsity for words with >2 meanings?

- Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
- The citation for "Luong et al." is missing a publication year.
- Numerical stability discussion in the supplement is useful but should be more integrated into the main paper.
