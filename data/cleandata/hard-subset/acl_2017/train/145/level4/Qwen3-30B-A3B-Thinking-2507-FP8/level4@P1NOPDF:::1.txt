

Review: Multimodal Word Distributions  
- Strengths: Overall, this is a strong paper with clear contributions.  
- Weaknesses: The comparison with related work could be strengthened.  

The paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word meanings—representing each word as a set of Gaussian components rather than a single distribution. This extends Vilnis and McCallum’s (2014) unimodal Gaussian approach, effectively addressing polysemy. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the experiments are sound. Table 1’s qualitative analysis yields results consistent with the model’s design, demonstrating its effectiveness. There are few substantive criticisms; the following points aim to enhance clarity and context.  

**Specific Comments:**  
1.  **Clarify distinctions with Tian et al. (2014):** The paper would benefit from a brief note contrasting the current approach with Tian et al.’s mixture-based prototype method. Both split word representations into multiple components, but the mechanisms and objectives differ.  
2.  **Expand related work citations:** The related work section should cite key prior efforts on multi-sense embeddings:  
    - Neelakantan et al. (2014) on efficient non-parametric multiple embeddings.  
    - Li and Jurafsky (2015) on whether multi-sense embeddings improve NLU.  
    - Liu et al. (2015) on topical word embeddings (AAAI).  
3.  **Include comparative results in Tables 3–4:** Adding results from the cited approaches (Neelakantan et al., Li and Jurafsky, Liu et al.) would contextualize the current model’s performance more effectively.  
4.  **Address SWCS performance discrepancy:** The authors observe that W2GM underperforms W2G on SWCS. Could they elaborate on the factors they attribute to this difference?  

The paper’s core contribution is compelling and well-executed. The suggested additions would further solidify its position within the literature and strengthen the narrative.
