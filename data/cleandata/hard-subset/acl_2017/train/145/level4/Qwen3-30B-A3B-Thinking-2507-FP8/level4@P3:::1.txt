

Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) for word embeddings that represents words as multimodal distributions rather than point vectors or unimodal Gaussians. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel probabilistic model for capturing multiple word meanings through Gaussian mixtures; (2) an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel that enables analytic tractability and efficient training; and (3) empirical validation demonstrating superior performance on word similarity and entailment tasks compared to alternatives.

Strengths
1. The paper provides a compelling solution to the polysemy problem by using multiple Gaussian components per word, which is clearly demonstrated through qualitative analysis (Table 1). For polysemous words like "rock" and "bank," the model successfully learns distinct components corresponding to different meanings (e.g., "rock" as stone vs. rock music, "bank" as river vs. financial institution), addressing a key limitation of unimodal approaches.

2. The theoretical foundation is strong, particularly the use of the expected likelihood kernel for computing similarity between distributions. This choice enables closed-form computation for Gaussian mixtures, which is a significant advantage over alternatives like KL divergence that would require approximations. The authors provide a clear derivation of this kernel in the supplementary material, demonstrating good analytical rigor.

3. The empirical results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over both word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings across multiple word similarity datasets (Table 2). The model's ability to capture entailment relationships (Table 5) is particularly impressive, with the multimodal version significantly outperforming unimodal approaches on both average precision and F1 scores.

Weaknesses
1. The claim that this is the "first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings" overlooks several relevant prior works. The related work section should better acknowledge Tian et al. (2014), Huang et al. (2012), and Nalisnick and Ravi (2015), which all explore multi-prototype representations. This weakens the novelty claim and requires clarification of how this work differs from these approaches.

2. The comparison with state-of-the-art multi-prototype approaches is insufficient. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including results from Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) in Tables 2 and 3. The current comparison primarily focuses on word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings, which doesn't adequately demonstrate the relative advantages of the proposed approach.

3. The paper reports that w2gm underperforms w2g on the SCWS dataset (Table 4), but provides no explanation for this counterintuitive result. The authors should address why the multi-prototype model fails on this specific dataset and whether this is a general limitation of their approach or an idiosyncrasy of the SCWS data.

Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims w2gm outperforms w2g on most datasets, but w2g (a single-prototype version of the same model) outperforms the original Vilnis and McCallum (2014) Gaussian embedding. Could the authors clarify the relationship between their w2g model and the original Vilnis and McCallum model, and explain why the single-prototype version of their model outperforms the original Gaussian embedding?

2. The paper reports that w2gm underperforms w2g on the SCWS dataset. What do the authors attribute this to, and how might the model be improved to address this issue? Is this limitation specific to the SCWS dataset or indicative of a broader problem with the multimodal approach?

3. The paper states that the model has been trained on 3.5 billion tokens, but the vocabulary size is limited to 314,129 words (discarding words occurring fewer than 100 times). Could the authors comment on how the vocabulary size limitation might impact the model's ability to capture rare word meanings?

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of word embeddings, with clear theoretical justification and strong empirical results. While the novelty claim requires refinement to properly acknowledge prior work on multi-prototype embeddings, the model's ability to capture polysemy through multimodal distributions represents a meaningful advancement over unimodal approaches. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with the supplementary material providing sufficient technical detail for replication. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, this would be a strong contribution to ACL 2017.
