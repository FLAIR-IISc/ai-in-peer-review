

This work employs Gaussian mixtures to model word representations and demonstrates their potential for capturing multiple word senses in polysemy. Training utilizes a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel serves as the similarity measure between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks validate the proposed approach.

- Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and defined. Gaussian mixtures offer greater expressiveness than deterministic vector representations, enabling the capture of multiple word meanings through distinct modes, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty. This represents a significant contribution to word embedding research.
The work proposes a max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measurement, facilitating efficient training.
The paper is largely well-written.

- Weaknesses:
See below for specific questions.

- General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is typically a critical parameter. The experiments use k=2; what criteria guided this choice? Does increasing k degrade model performance? How does the learned distribution appear for a word with only one predominant meaning?
The experiments exclusively use spherical Gaussians (covariance matrix reduced to a scalar). Is this solely for computational efficiency? How would performance change with a general diagonal covariance matrix, which allows direction-specific uncertainty in semantic space—a potentially more informative representation?
