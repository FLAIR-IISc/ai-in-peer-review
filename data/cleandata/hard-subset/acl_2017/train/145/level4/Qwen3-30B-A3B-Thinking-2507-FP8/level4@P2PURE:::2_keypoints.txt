- Paper summary: Models words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple senses, trained with a max-margin objective using an expected likelihood kernel; shows strong results on similarity and entailment tasks.

- Strength: Clear motivation and problem definition.

- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are far more expressive than deterministic vectors, capturing distinct senses, probability mass, and uncertainty.

- Strength: Meaningful contribution to word embedding research.

- Strength: Max-margin objective with a closed-form similarity measure is well-motivated and enables efficient training.

- Strength: Paper is generally well-written.

- Weakness: Choice of number of Gaussian components (k) is unclear—experiments fix k=2 without justification; impact of larger k unknown.

- Weakness: Use of spherical covariances for computational efficiency questioned; effect of using diagonal covariances (direction-specific uncertainty) is unknown.

- Weakness: Unclear what the learned distribution looks like for words with a single dominant meaning.

- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text; Luong et al. citation missing publication year.
