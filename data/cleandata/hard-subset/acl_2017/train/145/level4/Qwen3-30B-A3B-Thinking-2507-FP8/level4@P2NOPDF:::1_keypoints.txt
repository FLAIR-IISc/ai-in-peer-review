- Extends unimodal Gaussian word representations to a multimodal Gaussian mixture model (WMG) to capture polysemy — compelling and technically sound.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-structured with a clear flow from problem to method, experiments, and analysis.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough and correctly implemented; qualitative results align with model goals.  
- Makes a significant, principled advance in distributional semantics for modeling polysemy.  
- Main weakness: comparative analysis is weak — needs clearer contextualization among multi-sense embedding approaches.  
- Clarify distinction from Tian et al. (2014): WMG uses Gaussian mixtures for distributional representations vs. Tian’s discrete prototypes and integrates Vilnis & McCallum’s framework.  
- Expand related work to cite/discuss Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).  
- Add comparative results from those prior multi-sense methods to Tables 3 and 4 (or a new table) to show WMG’s relative performance.  
- Explain why WMG underperforms W2G on SWCS (Table 3) — analyze task specifics, clustering strategy, or representation limitations.  
- Overall assessment: strong, high-quality paper with minor revision suggestions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
