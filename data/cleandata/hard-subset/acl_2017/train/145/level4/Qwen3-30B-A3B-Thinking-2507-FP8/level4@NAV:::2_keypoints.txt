- Paper uses Gaussian mixtures to model word representations and shows they can capture multiple senses and uncertainty.
- Training uses a max-margin objective; expected likelihood kernel is used as a closed-form similarity measure.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks support the proposed approach.
- Clear motivation and definition of the problem.
- Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors, enabling distinct modes for different meanings and modeling uncertainty.
- Max-margin learning with closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Paper is largely well-written.
- Key methodological questions: why choose k=2 components, how does performance change with larger k, and how do distributions look for mainly single-sense words?
- Methodological question about covariance choice: experiments use spherical Gaussians—was this only for efficiency, and would diagonal/general covariances improve performance by capturing direction-specific uncertainty?
- Minor issues: Table 4 is not referenced; reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
