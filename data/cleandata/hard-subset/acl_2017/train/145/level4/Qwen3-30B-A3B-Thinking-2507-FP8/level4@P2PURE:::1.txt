

Review: Multimodal Word Distributions  
- Strengths: Overall, this is a very strong paper.  
- Weaknesses: The comparison against similar approaches could be extended.  
- General Discussion:  
The paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word meanings—i.e., representing a word as a set of multiple Gaussian distributions. This approach extends Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By employing a multimodal representation, the current method addresses the problem of polysemy.  
Overall, this is a well-structured and clear paper. The experimentation is sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 yields expected results. There is little to criticize; all comments below aim to enhance the paper’s clarity.  

Some comments:  
- It would be valuable to briefly clarify the differences between the approach in Tian et al. (2014) and the current work. Both utilize mixture models to split single-word representations into multiple prototypes.  
- The related work section should include the following missing citations:  
  * Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space (Neelakantan et al., EMNLP 2014)  
  * Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? (Li and Jurafsky, EMNLP 2015)  
  * Topical Word Embeddings (Liu et al., AAAI 2015)  
- Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would strengthen the comparison.  
- A question for the authors: What accounts for the performance drop of w2gm relative to w2g in the SWCS analysis?
