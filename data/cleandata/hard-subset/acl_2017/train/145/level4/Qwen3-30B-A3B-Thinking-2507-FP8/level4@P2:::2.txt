

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixture models (w2gm) to represent words with multiple distinct meanings, uncertainty, and entailment relationships. The primary contribution is the development of a Gaussian mixture representation that captures polysemy through multiple modes, each corresponding to a distinct word sense. The second contribution is the energy-based max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel for efficient training, which provides a closed-form solution for similarity between distributions. The third contribution is empirical validation demonstrating consistent performance improvements over strong baselines on word similarity (e.g., MEN, WS-R) and entailment tasks.

**Strengths**  
1. The paper effectively addresses a significant limitation in current word embedding approaches: the inability to model polysemy with unimodal distributions. The Gaussian mixture representation provides a theoretically sound and interpretable solution, as demonstrated in Table 1 where distinct mixture components capture different word senses (e.g., "rock" for stone vs. music). This is a meaningful advancement over point embeddings and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

2. The proposed energy-based objective with expected likelihood kernel offers both theoretical elegance and practical efficiency. Unlike previous methods using KL divergence (which lacks closed form for Gaussian mixtures), the expected likelihood kernel enables tractable optimization. The authors provide a clear derivation and practical implementation details (e.g., diagonal covariance reduction), making the approach scalable to large corpora.

3. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The paper demonstrates consistent performance gains across multiple word similarity datasets (Table 2) and shows qualitative improvements in capturing entailment relationships (Table 5). The comparison with Huang et al. (2012) on WordSim-353 is particularly convincing, showing w2gm substantially outperforms prior multi-prototype approaches.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The choice of k=2 mixture components lacks justification and is not explored systematically. The paper does not investigate whether k=2 is optimal or whether increasing k (e.g., to 3) would further improve performance, especially for words with more than two meanings. The brief mention of k=3 results (Section 4.2) is insufficient to assess this critical hyperparameter's impact.

2. The restriction to spherical covariance matrices (reducing covariance to a scalar) appears purely for computational simplicity, but the paper does not explore the potential benefits of more expressive diagonal or full covariance matrices. As noted in the paper, "the covariance matrix contains uncertainty information," yet the authors do not investigate how richer uncertainty representations might improve performance or interpretability.

3. The paper does not adequately address how the model handles words with only one dominant meaning (non-polysemous words). For such words, the Gaussian mixture approach may introduce unnecessary complexity compared to simpler representations. The authors should clarify whether the model automatically collapses to a unimodal distribution for non-polysemous words or requires manual tuning.

4. The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training with Gaussian mixtures relative to standard approaches. While the authors claim "scalable learning procedure, capable of training on a corpus with billions of words in days," the computational complexity of the expected likelihood kernel for Gaussian mixtures (which involves O(K²) terms) is not analyzed, making it difficult to assess practical feasibility.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper uses k=2 for all experiments. What criteria guided this choice, and how was it validated? Would increasing k improve performance for words with more than two meanings, or would it lead to overfitting?

2. The spherical covariance assumption (Section 4.2) seems to limit the model's expressiveness. How would the performance change if diagonal or full covariance matrices were used? Could this better capture directional uncertainty in semantic spaces?

3. For words with a single dominant meaning (e.g., "apple" as fruit), does the model automatically collapse to a unimodal representation, or does it maintain two components that could degrade performance? What evidence supports this?

**Additional Comments**  
- Table 4 is referenced in the supplementary material but not in the main text. Please clarify its relevance.
- The reference to "Luong et al." in Table 2 lacks a publication year; please provide the full citation.
- The paper's claim that "the model is able to automatically discover multiple meanings" (Section 4.2) is supported by qualitative results but could be strengthened with quantitative analysis of the number of words with multiple meaningful components.
