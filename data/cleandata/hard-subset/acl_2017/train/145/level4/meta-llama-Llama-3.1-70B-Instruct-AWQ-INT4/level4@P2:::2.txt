This work proposes a novel approach to word representation using Gaussian mixtures, allowing for the capture of multiple word meanings and uncertainty in a more expressive and nuanced manner. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is used as the similarity measure between two words' distributions.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors introduce a Gaussian mixture model for word representations, which can capture multiple distinct meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information. The model is trained using a max-margin energy-based ranking objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is used as the energy function. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The proposal of a Gaussian mixture model for word representations, which can capture multiple distinct meanings and uncertainty information.
2. The development of a max-margin energy-based ranking objective for training the model.
3. The use of the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function, which allows for efficient training and captures the similarity between two words' distributions.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem is clearly motivated and defined, and the use of Gaussian mixtures is well-justified.
2. The proposed approach is more expressive and nuanced than traditional deterministic vector representations.
3. The max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measurement allows for efficient training.
4. The paper is mostly well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The choice of the number of Gaussian components (k) is not clearly justified, and it is unclear how the performance of the model would be affected by increasing k.
2. The use of the spherical case (reducing the covariance matrix to a single number) is not fully explained, and it is unclear whether this is purely for computational efficiency or if there are other benefits to this approach.
3. The performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix is not explored, which could potentially capture different degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is your criteria for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k), and how does the performance of the model change when k is increased?
2. Can you provide more insight into the choice of the spherical case, and whether this is purely for computational efficiency or if there are other benefits to this approach?
3. Have you explored the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix, and if so, what were the results?
