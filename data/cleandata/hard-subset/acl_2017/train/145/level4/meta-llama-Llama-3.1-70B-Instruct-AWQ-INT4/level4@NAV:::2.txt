This paper proposes the utilization of Gaussian mixtures for word representation, showcasing its capability in capturing multiple meanings of polysemous words. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, with the expected likelihood kernel employed as a measure of similarity between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Strengths:
- The problem statement is clear and well-defined.
- Gaussian mixtures offer enhanced expressiveness compared to deterministic vector representations, enabling the capture of distinct word meanings through their modes, probability mass, and associated uncertainty.
- This work contributes significantly to the field of word embeddings by introducing a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, facilitating efficient training.
- The paper is generally well-written.

Weaknesses:
Please refer to the questions below.

General Discussion:
- In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is a crucial parameter. This paper sets k to 2; could the authors elaborate on the criteria used to select this value? Would increasing k negatively impact the model's performance? Additionally, what does the learned distribution look like for a word with a single dominant meaning?
- The experiments employ the spherical case, reducing the covariance matrix to a single value. Is this choice solely driven by computational efficiency? It would be interesting to explore the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix, as it allows for varying degrees of uncertainty in the semantic space.
- Minor comments:
- Table 4 is not referenced in the text.
- The reference to Luong et al. lacks the publication year.
