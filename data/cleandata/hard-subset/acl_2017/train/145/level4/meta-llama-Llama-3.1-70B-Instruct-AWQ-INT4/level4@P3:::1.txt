**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings. The approach extends the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By using a multimodal distribution, the current approach addresses the problem of polysemy, where a single word can have multiple meanings.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a new model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures, which can capture multiple word meanings and uncertainty information.
2. The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective for learning the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model, which can be optimized using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent.
3. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-structured and clear introduction to the problem of polysemy and the proposed solution.
2. The experimentation is thorough, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 shows results as expected from the approach.
3. The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with existing approaches, including word2vec and Gaussian embeddings.

**Weaknesses**

1. The comparison against similar approaches could be extended to include more recent works, such as Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space (Neelakantan et al., 2014) and Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? (Li and Jurafsky, 2015).
2. The inclusion of results from those approaches in tables 3 and 4 could be interesting to see how the proposed model compares to other multimodal word embedding approaches.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the loss of performance of w2gm against w2g in the analysis of SWCS.

**Questions to Authors**

1. What do you attribute the loss of performance of w2gm against w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
2. Can you provide more details on the optimization process, including the choice of hyperparameters and the convergence of the algorithm?
3. How do you plan to extend the proposed model to capture more complex semantic relationships, such as word sense induction and lexical semantic relations?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly presented. The use of tables and figures is effective in illustrating the results and providing a clear understanding of the proposed model. The paper is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing and has the potential to impact various applications, including language modeling, sentiment analysis, and machine translation.
