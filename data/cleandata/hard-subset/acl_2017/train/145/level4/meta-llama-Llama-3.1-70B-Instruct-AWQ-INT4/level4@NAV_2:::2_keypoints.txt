- Proposes a Gaussian mixture model for word representations to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty.  
- Trains the model with a max-margin objective using a closed-form expected likelihood kernel as similarity.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  
- Problem is well motivated; Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors.  
- Model can represent multiple senses via mixture modes and encode uncertainty/probability mass.  
- Paper is a meaningful contribution to word embedding literature and is mostly well written.  
- No clear criteria or analysis provided for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k) or its impact on performance.  
- All experiments use spherical covariances; unclear whether this choice was for efficiency or if diagonal/full covariances were evaluated.  
- Table 4 is not referenced in the text and the Luong et al. citation lacks a publication year.  
- What criteria were used to select k, and does increasing k degrade performance?  
- How does the learned distribution appear for a word with a single dominant meaning?  
- Is the spherical covariance choice solely for computational efficiency, and were diagonal covariances tested?  
- Please provide more implementation details for the max-margin learning objective and the closed-form similarity measurement.
