- Proposes Gaussian mixture word representations to capture multiple senses and uncertainty.
- Trains the model with a max-margin energy-based ranking objective.
- Uses the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity/energy function for efficient training.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on benchmark word similarity and entailment tasks.
- Main contributions: Gaussian mixture model, max-margin training objective, and expected likelihood kernel.
- Strength: Clear motivation and well-justified use of Gaussian mixtures.
- Strength: More expressive and nuanced than deterministic vector embeddings.
- Strength: Max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Strength: Paper is mostly well-written and easy to follow.
- Weakness: No clear justification or analysis for choosing the number of Gaussian components (k).
- Weakness: Spherical covariance assumption is not fully explained (computational vs. other benefits unclear).
- Weakness: Performance with general diagonal covariance matrices is not explored.
- Question: What criteria determine the choice of k and how does performance change with larger k?
- Question: Is the spherical covariance purely for computational efficiency or are there other reasons?
- Question: Were diagonal covariance models tried, and what were the results?
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
