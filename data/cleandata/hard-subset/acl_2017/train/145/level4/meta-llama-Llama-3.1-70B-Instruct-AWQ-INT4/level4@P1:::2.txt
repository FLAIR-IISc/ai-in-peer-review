This work uses Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates its potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is done based on a max-margin objective. The expected likelihood kernel is used as the similarity between two words' distributions. Experiment results on word similarity and entailment tasks show the effectiveness of the proposed work.

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

The paper proposes a novel approach to word representation using Gaussian mixtures, allowing for the capture of multiple word meanings and uncertainty. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A Gaussian mixture model for word representation, which can capture multiple word meanings and uncertainty.
2. A max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measurement for efficient training.
3. Experiment results on word similarity and entailment tasks that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem is clearly motivated and defined. Gaussian mixtures are much more expressive than deterministic vector representations, and can potentially capture different word meanings by their modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes.
2. The proposed approach represents an important contribution to word embedding.
3. The paper proposes a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measurement for efficient training.
4. The paper is mostly well-written.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The number of Gaussian components (k) is an important parameter in the Gaussian mixture models. However, the paper does not provide a clear criteria for selecting k, and it is unclear how the increase of k affects the performance of the model.
2. The paper uses the spherical case in all experiments, which reduces the covariance matrix to a single number. It is unclear whether this is purely for computation efficiency, and what the performance would be like using a general diagonal covariance matrix.
3. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of what the learned distribution looks like for a word that only has one popular meaning.

**Questions to authors:**

1. What is the criteria for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k) in the Gaussian mixture models?
2. How does the increase of k affect the performance of the model?
3. What does the learned distribution look like for a word that only has one popular meaning?
4. Is the use of the spherical case purely for computation efficiency? What would be the performance like using a general diagonal covariance matrix?
