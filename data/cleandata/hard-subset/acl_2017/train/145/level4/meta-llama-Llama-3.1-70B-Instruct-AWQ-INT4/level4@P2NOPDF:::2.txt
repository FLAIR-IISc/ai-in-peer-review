This work employs Gaussian mixtures to represent words, showcasing its potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, utilizing the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity measure between two words' distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Strengths:

The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. The use of Gaussian mixtures is a significant improvement over deterministic vector representations, as it can potentially capture different word meanings through its modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work represents an important contribution to word embedding. The proposed max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measurement enables efficient training. The paper is mostly well-written.

Weaknesses:

Several questions and concerns are raised below.

General Discussion:

In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is a crucial parameter. However, in the experiments presented, k is set to 2. It would be beneficial to know the criteria used to select this value. Does increasing k negatively impact the model's performance? Furthermore, it would be interesting to see the learned distribution for a word with only one popular meaning.

The use of the spherical case in all experiments, where the covariance matrix reduces to a single number, appears to be motivated by computational efficiency. However, it is worth investigating the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix. This more general case allows the Gaussian mixture to define different degrees of uncertainty along various directions in the semantic space, making it a more intriguing approach.

Minor comments:

Table 4 is not referenced in the text. Additionally, the reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
