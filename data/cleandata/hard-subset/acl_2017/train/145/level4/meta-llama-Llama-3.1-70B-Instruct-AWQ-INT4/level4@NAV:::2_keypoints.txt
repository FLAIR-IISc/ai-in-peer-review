- Proposes Gaussian mixture word representations to capture multiple meanings of polysemous words.  
- Trains models with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure.  
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness.  
- Strength: clear and well-defined problem statement.  
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures provide greater expressiveness than deterministic vectors, capturing modes, probability mass, and uncertainty.  
- Strength: Novel max-margin objective with a closed-form similarity measure enables efficient training.  
- Strength: Paper is generally well-written.  
- Question: Why is the number of mixture components k set to 2—what criteria determined this choice and how does increasing k affect performance?  
- Question: How does the learned distribution look for a word with a single dominant meaning?  
- Question: Spherical covariance was used—was this solely for computational efficiency, and would diagonal (general) covariances improve results?  
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.  
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. lacks the publication year.
