- Proposes Gaussian mixture word representations to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty.
- Trains models with a max-margin objective and uses the expected likelihood kernel as a closed-form similarity.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.
- Strength: Clear motivation—Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can model modes and uncertainty.
- Strength: Represents a meaningful contribution to word embedding methods.
- Strength: Max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Strength: Paper is mostly well-written.
- Weakness: No clear criterion for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k).
- Weakness: Unclear how increasing k affects model performance.
- Weakness: Experiments use spherical covariances only; unclear if this is for efficiency and how diagonal/full covariances would perform.
- Weakness: No clear depiction or explanation of the learned distribution for words with a single dominant meaning.
- Question: What criteria should be used to select k?
- Question: How does performance change as k increases?
- Question: What does the learned distribution look like for a unimodal (single-meaning) word?
- Question: Is the spherical covariance choice purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal covariances affect results?
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.
- Minor: Reference "Luong et al." is missing the publication year.
