This paper proposes a novel approach to representing words using Gaussian mixtures, demonstrating its potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is used as the similarity between two words' distributions. The experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks show the effectiveness of the proposed work.

Strengths:

The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. The use of Gaussian mixtures is a significant improvement over deterministic vector representations, as it can potentially capture different word meanings by its modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work represents an important contribution to word embedding. The proposed max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measurement enables efficient training. Additionally, the paper is mostly well-written.

Weaknesses:

Several questions and concerns arise from the paper, which are discussed below.

General Discussion:

In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is a crucial parameter. However, in the experiments presented in this paper, k is set to 2. It would be beneficial to know the criteria used to select this value. Furthermore, it is essential to investigate whether increasing k would hurt the performance of this model. Another interesting aspect to explore is the learned distribution for a word that only has one popular meaning.

The use of the spherical case in all experiments, where the covariance matrix reduces to a single number, raises questions about computational efficiency. Was this choice purely made for efficiency reasons? It would be interesting to see the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix, as it allows the Gaussian mixture to define different degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space.

Minor Comments:

Table 4 is not referred to in the text and could be removed or referenced to maintain consistency. Additionally, the reference to Luong et al. lacks the publication year, which should be included for completeness.

Overall, this work presents a promising approach to word representation using Gaussian mixtures. Addressing the raised questions and concerns will further strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.
