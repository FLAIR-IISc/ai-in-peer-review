- Proposes Gaussian mixture word representations to capture polysemy.  
- Trains with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as similarity.  
- Experiments show effectiveness on word similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Strength: problem is clearly motivated and well-defined.  
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures offer significant improvement over deterministic vectors by modeling multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty.  
- Strength: Represents an important contribution to word embedding literature.  
- Strength: Max-margin learning with closed-form similarity allows efficient training.  
- Strength: Paper is mostly well-written.  
- Concern: Number of Gaussian components k is fixed to 2 with no justification; need criteria for choosing k and effects of larger k.  
- Concern: Would like to see learned distributions for words with only one dominant meaning.  
- Concern: All experiments use spherical covariances for efficiency; should evaluate diagonal covariances to capture directional uncertainty.  
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.  
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
