**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings. This approach extends the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. The current approach addresses the problem of polysemy by using a multimodal distribution to represent a word as a set of many Gaussian distributions.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Multimodal Word Distributions**: The paper proposes a new model for learning multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings.
2. **Extension of Vilnis and McCallum (2014)**: The approach extends the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions.
3. **Addressing Polysemy**: The current approach addresses the problem of polysemy by using a multimodal distribution to represent a word as a set of many Gaussian distributions.

**Strengths**

1. **Overall a very strong paper**: The paper is well-structured and clear, with correct experimentation and qualitative analysis.
2. **Effective Addressing of Polysemy**: The approach effectively addresses the problem of polysemy by using a multimodal distribution to represent a word as a set of many Gaussian distributions.
3. **Good Qualitative Analysis**: The qualitative analysis made in Table 1 shows results as expected from the approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison to Similar Approaches**: The comparison against similar approaches could be extended to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed model.
2. **Missing Citations**: Some missing citations, such as Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space (Neelakantan et al., 2014) and Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? (Li and Jurafsky, 2015), could be mentioned in the related work section.
3. **Inclusion of Additional Results**: The inclusion of the results from those approaches in tables 3 and 4 could be interesting and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed model.
