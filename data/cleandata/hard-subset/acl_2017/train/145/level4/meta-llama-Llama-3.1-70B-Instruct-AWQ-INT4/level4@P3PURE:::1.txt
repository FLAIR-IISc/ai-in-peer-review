Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall, this is a very strong paper, well-structured and clear in its presentation.

- Weaknesses: One potential area for improvement is the comparison against similar approaches, which could be extended to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed model's strengths and weaknesses.

- General Discussion:

The primary focus of this paper is the introduction of a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings. In essence, this approach represents a word by a set of multiple Gaussian distributions, thereby extending the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By employing a multimodal approach, the current model effectively addresses the problem of polysemy.

Overall, the paper is well-structured and clear, with correct experimentation and a qualitative analysis in Table 1 that yields expected results from the approach. The paper is robust, and the comments below are intended to provide suggestions for additional clarity.

Some comments:

* It may be interesting to include a brief explanation of the differences between the approach presented by Tian et al. (2014) and the current one. Both approaches split single word representations into multiple prototypes using a mixture model.
* There are some missing citations that could be mentioned in the related work section, including:
	+ Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space by Neelakantan et al. (EMNLP 2014)
	+ Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? by Li and Jurafsky (EMNLP 2015)
	+ Topical Word Embeddings by Liu et al. (AAAI 2015)
* The inclusion of results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 could also be interesting and provide a more comprehensive comparison.
* A question to the authors: What do you attribute the loss of performance of w2gm against w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
