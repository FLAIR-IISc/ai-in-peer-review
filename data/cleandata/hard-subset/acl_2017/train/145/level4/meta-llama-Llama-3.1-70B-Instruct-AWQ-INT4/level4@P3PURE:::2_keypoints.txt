- Paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel as similarity.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment show the proposed method is effective.
- Strength: problem is clearly motivated and well-defined.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors, capturing multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty.
- Strength: Important contribution to word embedding research.
- Strength: Max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables efficient training; paper is mostly well-written.
- Weakness: Number of mixture components k is set to 2 without justification; need criteria for choosing k and analysis of larger k.
- Weakness: Should show learned distributions for words with a single dominant sense to evaluate behavior on unimodal words.
- Weakness: All experiments use spherical covariances for efficiency; should investigate diagonal (anisotropic) covariances to capture direction-specific uncertainty.
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced and should be removed or discussed.
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
