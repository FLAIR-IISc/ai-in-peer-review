This work uses Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates its potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is done based on a max-margin objective. The expected likelihood kernel is used as the similarity between two words' distributions. Experiment results on word similarity and entailment tasks show the effectiveness of the proposed work.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions:**

The paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model to represent words, allowing for the capture of multiple word meanings and uncertainty. The model is trained using a max-margin objective with a closed-form similarity measurement. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A Gaussian mixture model for word representation, which can capture multiple word meanings and uncertainty.
2. A max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measurement for efficient training.
3. Experimental results showing the effectiveness of the proposed model on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem is clearly motivated and defined. Gaussian mixtures are much more expressive than deterministic vector representations.
2. The proposed model can potentially capture different word meanings by its modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes.
3. This work represents an important contribution to word embedding.
4. The paper is mostly well written.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The number of Gaussian components (k) is usually an important parameter in Gaussian mixture models. However, the paper does not provide a clear criteria for selecting k and does not investigate the impact of increasing k on the model's performance.
2. The paper uses the spherical case in all experiments, which reduces the covariance matrix to a single number. It is unclear whether this is purely for computation efficiency or if the authors have investigated the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix.
3. Table 4 is not referred to in the text, and the reference Luong et al. lacks the publication year.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the criteria for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k) in the proposed model? Does the increase of k hurt the performance of this model?
2. What does the learned distribution look like for a word that only has one popular meaning?
3. Is the use of the spherical case in all experiments purely for computation efficiency? Have the authors investigated the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix?
4. Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the max-margin learning objective and the closed-form similarity measurement?
