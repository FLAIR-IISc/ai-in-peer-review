This paper proposes a novel approach to representing words using Gaussian mixtures, demonstrating its potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is employed as the similarity measure between two words' distributions. The experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures offer a more expressive representation than deterministic vector representations, allowing them to potentially capture different word meanings through their modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work represents an important contribution to word embedding research. The authors propose a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measurement, enabling efficient training. The paper is mostly well-written.

Weaknesses:
Several questions and concerns are raised below.

General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is typically a crucial parameter. In the experiments presented in this paper, k is set to 2. The authors should provide their criteria for selecting k and discuss whether increasing k would negatively impact the model's performance. Additionally, it would be interesting to see what the learned distribution looks like for a word with only one popular meaning.

The use of the spherical case in all experiments, where the covariance matrix reduces to a single number, appears to be motivated by computational efficiency. However, it would be beneficial to investigate the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix. This more general case allows the Gaussian mixture to define different degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space, which seems more intriguing.

Minor Comments:
Table 4 is not referenced in the text and should be either removed or discussed in the narrative. Furthermore, the reference to Luong et al. lacks the publication year, which should be added for completeness.
