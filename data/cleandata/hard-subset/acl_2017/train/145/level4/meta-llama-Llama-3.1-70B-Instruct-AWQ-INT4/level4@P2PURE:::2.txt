This work leverages Gaussian mixtures to represent words, showcasing their potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, utilizing the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity measure between two words' distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures offer a more expressive representation than deterministic vector representations, potentially capturing different word meanings through their modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work constitutes an important contribution to word embedding. A notable aspect is the proposal of a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measurement, facilitating efficient training. The paper is mostly well-written.

Weaknesses:
Please see below for some questions and concerns.

General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is typically a crucial parameter. In the experiments presented, k is set to 2. Could the authors elaborate on their criteria for selecting k? Does increasing k negatively impact the model's performance? Furthermore, it would be interesting to visualize the learned distribution for a word with only one popular meaning.

The use of the spherical case in all experiments, where the covariance matrix reduces to a single number, raises questions. Is this choice purely driven by computational efficiency? The authors may want to consider investigating the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix, as this would allow the Gaussian mixture to define different degrees of uncertainty along various directions in the semantic space, making the approach more intriguing.

Minor comments:
Table 4 is not referenced in the text. Additionally, the reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
