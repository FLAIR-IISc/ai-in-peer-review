Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures, allowing for the representation of multiple word meanings. The authors extend the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. The proposed approach effectively addresses the problem of polysemy, where a single word can have multiple related meanings.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A new model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures.
2. An extension of the Vilnis and McCallum (2014) model, which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions.
3. A robust and scalable learning procedure capable of training on large corpora.

**Strengths**

Overall, this is a very strong paper. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their work. The experimentation is thorough, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 demonstrates the expected results from the approach.

**Weaknesses**

One potential weakness is that the comparison against similar approaches could be extended. Specifically, it would be beneficial to include a brief explanation of the differences between the approach from Tian et al. (2014) and the current one, as both use mixture models to split single-word representations into multiple prototypes.

Additionally, some missing citations could be mentioned in the related work section, such as:

* Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space (Neelakantan et al., 2014)
* Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? (Li and Jurafsky, 2015)
* Topical Word Embeddings (Liu et al., 2015)

Including the results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 could also provide a more comprehensive comparison.

**Questions to Authors**

One question arises from the analysis of the SCWS dataset: What do the authors attribute the loss of performance of w2gm against w2g to?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the experimentation is thorough. However, the inclusion of additional comparisons and explanations, as mentioned above, would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.
