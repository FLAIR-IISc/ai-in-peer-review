This work leverages Gaussian mixtures to represent words, demonstrating its potential in capturing multiple word meanings for polysemy. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, utilizing the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity measure between two words' distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks showcase the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. The use of Gaussian mixtures is more expressive than deterministic vector representations, allowing for the potential capture of different word meanings through its modes, along with probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work represents a significant contribution to word embedding research. Furthermore, the proposed max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measurement enables efficient training. The paper is also well-written, making it easy to follow.

Weaknesses:
Several questions and concerns are raised below.

General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is typically a crucial parameter. However, in the experiments presented in this paper, k is set to 2. It would be beneficial to know the criteria used to select this value. Additionally, does increasing k negatively impact the model's performance? It would also be interesting to see what the learned distribution looks like for a word with only one popular meaning.

I noticed that the spherical case is used in all experiments, where the covariance matrix reduces to a single number. Is this choice solely driven by computational efficiency? I am curious to know how the model performs when using a general diagonal covariance matrix. In this more general case, the Gaussian mixture model can define different degrees of uncertainty along various directions in the semantic space, which seems more intriguing.

Minor comments:
Table 4 is not referenced in the text. Additionally, the reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
