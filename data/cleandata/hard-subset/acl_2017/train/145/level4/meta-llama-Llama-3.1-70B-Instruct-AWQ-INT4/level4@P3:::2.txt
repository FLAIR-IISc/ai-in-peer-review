**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings, representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution. The authors argue that this representation is more expressive than traditional deterministic vector representations and can capture multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information. They introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model and use the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure between word distributions.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Gaussian Mixture Word Embeddings**: The authors propose a new approach to word embeddings, representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution, which can capture multiple word meanings and uncertainty information.
2. **Energy-Based Max-Margin Objective**: The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model, which is efficient and effective for training.
3. **Expected Likelihood Kernel**: The authors use the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure between word distributions, which is a closed-form solution and can be efficiently computed.

**Strengths**

1. **Expressive Representation**: The Gaussian mixture representation is more expressive than traditional deterministic vector representations and can capture multiple word meanings and uncertainty information.
2. **Efficient Training**: The energy-based max-margin objective and the expected likelihood kernel enable efficient training of the Gaussian mixture model.
3. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on several word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Selection of Number of Gaussian Components**: The authors do not provide a clear criteria for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k) and do not investigate the impact of increasing k on the performance of the model.
2. **Spherical Case**: The authors use the spherical case in all experiments, which reduces the covariance matrix to a single number. It is unclear whether this is purely for computation efficiency or if it is a limitation of the approach.
3. **Lack of Analysis on Learned Distributions**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the learned distributions for words with different meanings and uncertainty information.

**Questions to Authors**

1. What is the criteria for selecting the number of Gaussian components (k), and how does it impact the performance of the model?
2. Why did you choose to use the spherical case in all experiments, and what is the performance of using a general diagonal covariance matrix?
3. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the learned distributions for words with different meanings and uncertainty information?
