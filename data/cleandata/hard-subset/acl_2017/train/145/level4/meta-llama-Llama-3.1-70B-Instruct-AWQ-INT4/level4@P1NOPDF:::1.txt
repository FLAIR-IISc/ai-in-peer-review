Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall, this is a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison against similar approaches could be extended.

General Discussion:
The primary focus of this paper is the introduction of a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, which are formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings. In essence, this approach represents a word by a set of multiple Gaussian distributions, thereby extending the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By leveraging a multimodal approach, the current model effectively addresses the issue of polysemy.

Overall, the paper is well-structured and clear, and the experimentation is sound. The qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 yields results that are consistent with expectations from this approach. There is little to criticize, and the comments below are intended to provide suggestions for further clarification.

Some comments:

* It would be interesting to include a brief explanation of the differences between the approach presented by Tian et al. (2014) and the current one. Both approaches split single-word representations into multiple prototypes using a mixture model.
* There are some missing citations that could be mentioned in the related work section, such as:
  + Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space by Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, and McCallum (EMNLP 2014)
  + Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? by Li and Jurafsky (EMNLP 2015)
  + Topical Word Embeddings by Liu, Liu, Chua, and Sun (AAAI 2015)
* Including the results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 could also be informative.
* A question to the authors: What do you attribute the loss of performance of w2gm against w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
