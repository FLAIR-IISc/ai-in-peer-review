This work uses Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates the potential of polysemy to capture the meaning of multiple words. The training process is based on a maximum-margin objective. The expected probability kernel is used as a similarity between two word distributions. Test results on word similarity and entrainment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed work.

- Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and defined. Gaussian mixtures are much more expressive than deterministic vector representations. It can potentially capture the meaning of various words through its modes, as well as the probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work represents a significant contribution to word embedding.
This work proposes a maximum-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity measurements for efficient training.
This paper is well-written for the most part.

- Weaknesses:
See below for some questions.
- General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is usually an important parameter. In this paper's experiment, k is assumed to be 2. What is your criterion for choosing k? Does increasing k affect the performance of this model? How does the learned distribution for a word that has only one popular meaning look?
I noticed that you used a spherical case in all the experiments (the covariance matrix is reduced to a single number). Is this only for computational efficiency? I'm wondering what the performance would be like using a general diagonal covariance matrix. Since this is a more general case, Gaussian mixtures define different degrees of uncertainty in semantic space in different directions, which seems more appealing.
