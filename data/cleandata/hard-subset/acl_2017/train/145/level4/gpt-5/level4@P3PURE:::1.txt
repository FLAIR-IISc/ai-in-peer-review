Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison against similar approaches could be extended.

General Discussion:
The paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word meanings, i.e., representing a word as a set of Gaussian components. The approach extends the model of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By moving to a multimodal representation, the proposed method directly addresses the problem of polysemy.

Overall, this is a very strong, well-structured, and clear paper. The experiments are sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 aligns with expectations for this approach. There is little to fault; the comments below are intended to improve clarity and completeness.

Comments:
- It would be helpful to include a brief explanation of the differences between the approach of Tian et al. (2014) and the current one. Both split a single word representation into multiple prototypes using a mixture model, but clarifying the distinctions would aid readers.
- Some related work appears to be missing and could be cited:
  • Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space. Neelakantan, A., Shankar, J., Passos, A., McCallum, A. EMNLP 2014
  • Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? Li, J., and Jurafsky, D. EMNLP 2015
  • Topical Word Embeddings. Liu, Y., Liu, Z., Chua, T.-S., and Sun, M. AAAI 2015
- Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would strengthen the comparison.
- A question for the authors: What do you attribute as the cause of the loss of performance of w2gm relative to w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
