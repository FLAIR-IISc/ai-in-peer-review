- Paper models words as Gaussian mixture distributions to capture multiple senses, uses a max-margin objective and expected likelihood kernel, and shows strong results on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Strength: Problem is clearly motivated; Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than vectors and capture distinct meanings with uncertainty.
- Strength: Proposes a max-margin learning objective and a closed-form similarity measure enabling efficient training.
- Strength: Paper is mostly well written.
- Weakness/Question: Number of mixture components k is fixed at 2—what was the selection criterion, does increasing k hurt performance, and how do distributions look for single-meaning words?
- Weakness/Question: Experiments use spherical covariances—was this for efficiency, and how does performance change with diagonal or full covariances that model anisotropic uncertainty?
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
