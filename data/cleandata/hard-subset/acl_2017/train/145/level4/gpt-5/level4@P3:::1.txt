Summary
This paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), yielding multimodal probabilistic embeddings that can capture multiple senses, uncertainty, and asymmetric relations such as entailment. Training uses a skip-gram–style, energy-based max-margin objective. The key design choice is an analytic, tractable energy function based on the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel), which admits closed-form computation for Gaussian mixtures. Experiments on large corpora show improved performance over point-vector baselines (word2vec) and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on several intrinsic benchmarks, with qualitative analyses illustrating clear separation of senses for polysemous words.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Probabilistic multi-prototype word embeddings via Gaussian mixtures that explicitly model polysemy and uncertainty, with interpretable component-wise senses and reduced variance inflation compared to unimodal Gaussians.
2) An energy-based training framework pairing a max-margin ranking objective with the expected likelihood kernel, providing closed-form, scalable training for mixtures (contrasting with KL, which lacks a closed form for mixtures).
3) Empirical evidence—both qualitative (nearest neighbors by sense) and quantitative (word similarity and lexical entailment)—demonstrating competitive or superior performance to strong baselines, and showing advantages of uncertainty-aware modeling.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent modeling innovation with sound motivation. Moving from unimodal to multimodal distributions is a natural and well-argued step for handling polysemy. The paper explains how mixtures avoid the variance inflation and mean “pull” issues observed in unimodal Gaussian embeddings, and the qualitative examples convincingly show sense separation (e.g., bank: river bank vs. financial bank; rock: stone vs. music).
- Careful choice of tractable energy function enabling scalable learning. The expected likelihood kernel yields a closed-form energy between mixtures and integrates both location and uncertainty, addressing a practical obstacle with KL for mixtures. The training objective is straightforward and well aligned with the distributional hypothesis, and the paper provides useful implementation details for numerical stability and scalability.
- Strong empirical results with diverse similarity metrics. Across multiple similarity datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MTurk, RW), the mixture model often outperforms word2vec and unimodal Gaussians, with sensible behavior across metrics (e.g., cosine vs. Euclidean) and a clear advantage in lexical entailment when using asymmetric KL at test time.
- Qualitative interpretability and diagnostic analyses. Component-level nearest neighbors and variance comparisons illustrate that the mixture representation not only improves scores but also yields interpretable senses and better-calibrated uncertainty. The public visualization (anonymous) adds to the paper’s transparency.
- Scale and practicality. Training on billions of tokens with a simple optimizer and diagonal/spherical covariances suggests the approach is feasible for large-vocabulary settings and fits existing workflows that expect pretrained embeddings.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete comparison to recent multi-sense baselines. While the paper compares to Huang et al. (2012), it omits stronger contemporaneous multi-prototype or sense-selection models (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014; Li & Jurafsky, 2015; topical embeddings: Liu et al., 2015). Including these (even if some use external lexicons) would clarify where probabilistic mixtures stand relative to deterministic multi-sense methods and nonparametric approaches.
- Limited ablation on modeling choices. The paper largely fixes K=2 (with brief comments on K=3) and uses spherical/diagonal covariances. More systematic analysis of K, covariance structure (spherical vs. diagonal), and mixture weight learning would help isolate which components drive gains and how robust the method is to these choices.
- Metric selection and evaluation protocol. Results depend on metric choice (maximum cosine, minimum Euclidean, expected likelihood), and different datasets prefer different metrics. While reasonable, it raises concerns about tuning to each benchmark. A consistent primary metric or a principled selection criterion would strengthen the claims. For SCWS specifically (contextual similarity), the paper does not clearly explain why the mixture sometimes underperforms the unimodal variant; a context-aware sense selection at evaluation time might be necessary for a fair test of multi-sense embeddings.
- Baseline fairness and reproducibility details. The unimodal Gaussian baseline (w2g) here appears stronger than reported in prior work due to implementation differences. Clearer accounting of training data, window sizes, negative sampling rates, and hyperparameter tuning across all baselines (including word2vec) would ensure fairness. It would also be useful to quantify compute and training time vs. baselines.
- Mostly intrinsic evaluation. While lexical entailment is a valuable intrinsic test, there are no downstream extrinsic tasks (e.g., sentence classification, NLI, QA, sequence labeling) to demonstrate benefits in real applications, especially where uncertainty could matter.

Questions to authors
- SCWS: Why does w2gm sometimes trail w2g on SCWS? Is the evaluation context-sensitive (i.e., does it select the most likely component given the sentence context), or does it only apply MaxSim/AveSim over sense components without disambiguation? Would a simple context-conditioned sense selection improve SCWS results?
- Energy function: Did you try training directly with asymmetric energies (e.g., an approximation to KL for mixtures) for entailment, or multi-task training that combines ELK and a KL-based auxiliary objective?
- Model capacity: How sensitive are results to K? Do you observe diminishing returns beyond K=2? Any automatic model selection (e.g., nonparametric mixing or pruning of unused components)?
- Covariance structure: What is the performance difference between spherical vs. diagonal covariances? Do component covariances meaningfully correlate with sense specificity (e.g., more general senses having larger variance)?
- Baselines: Can you add comparisons to Neelakantan et al. (2014) and Tian et al. (2014), and, if feasible, Chen et al. (2014) and Li & Jurafsky (2015)? Even partial comparisons (same corpora/settings) would be informative.

Additional comments
- Please add discussion contrasting your approach with Tian et al. (2014) and nonparametric multi-sense methods (Neelakantan et al., 2014), highlighting the benefits of probabilistic mixtures (uncertainty, entailment) vs. deterministic prototypes.
- Consider reporting a small extrinsic evaluation to illustrate practical gains and whether uncertainty helps downstream.
- Minor: unify notation for w2g/w2gm; clarify when KL is used (test-time entailment) vs. ELK (training); and provide trained embeddings/visualization upon publication.
