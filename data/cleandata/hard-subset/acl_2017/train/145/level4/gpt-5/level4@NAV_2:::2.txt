This paper models words as Gaussian mixture distributions and demonstrates their ability to capture polysemy. Training is performed with a max-margin, energy-based objective, using the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity between word distributions. Results on word similarity and lexical entailment benchmarks indicate the approach is effective.

- Strengths:
The problem is well motivated and clearly scoped. Mixture distributions are strictly more expressive than single point embeddings, enabling distinct senses to be represented by separate modes and providing meaningful uncertainty and probability mass around those senses. This constitutes a notable contribution to word representation learning.
The paper introduces a margin-based learning objective paired with a closed-form distributional similarity, enabling efficient and scalable training.
The manuscript is generally clear and well presented.

- Weaknesses:
Primarily requests for clarification; see questions below.

- General Discussion:
The number of mixture components (K) is a key hyperparameter. In the experiments, K is fixed to 2. What criteria are used to choose K? Does increasing K degrade performance, and if so, why? For words that are effectively monosemous, what form do the learned distributions take?
All experiments use the spherical covariance case. Is this choice purely for computational efficiency? How does performance change with general diagonal covariances? Allowing dimension-wise uncertainty could better reflect anisotropic semantic variability and seems compelling.
