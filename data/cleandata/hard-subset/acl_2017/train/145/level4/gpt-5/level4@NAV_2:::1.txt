Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: A very strong submission overall.
- Weaknesses: The empirical comparison to closely related methods could be broadened.

General Discussion:
This paper proposes a new model that learns multimodal word distributions by representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians, thereby supporting multiple senses, entailment, and richer uncertainty. It extends Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which modeled each word as a single Gaussian; by moving to mixtures, the method addresses polysemy directly. The paper is well organized and clear. The experiments are sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 aligns with what one would expect from a multi-prototype formulation. There is little to fault; the comments below are intended to further improve clarity.

Some comments:
- It would be helpful to briefly articulate the differences between Tian et al. (2014) and this work. Both produce multiple prototypes per word via a mixture-like mechanism, but the modeling choices and training objectives differ.
- A few related works appear to be missing from the Related Work section and could be cited:
  • Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, McCallum. “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space,” EMNLP 2014.
  • Li and Jurafsky. “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?,” EMNLP 2015.
  • Liu, Liu, Chua, Sun. “Topical Word Embeddings,” AAAI 2015.
- Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would strengthen the comparative analysis.
- Question to the authors: What explains the drop in performance of w2gm relative to w2g on the SCWS evaluation?
