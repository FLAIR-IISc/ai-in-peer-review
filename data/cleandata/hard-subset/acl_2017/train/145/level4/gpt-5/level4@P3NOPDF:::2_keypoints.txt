- Summary: Represents words as Gaussian mixture embeddings to capture polysemy and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin objective and compared via an expected likelihood kernel; shows gains on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Strength: Clear motivation—distributions naturally encode multiple senses, uncertainty, and probability mass.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures increase expressiveness over point embeddings and provide intuitive handling of polysemy via components.
- Strength: Max-margin training plus closed-form similarity enables efficient training and inference.
- Strength: Well written with empirical improvements on standard benchmarks; substantive contribution to probabilistic embeddings literature.
- Weakness/Question: Mixture size k fixed at 2—needs justification and an ablation (k=1,2,3,4) to assess performance, overfitting, and training cost.
- Weakness/Question: For monosemous words, unclear whether extra components collapse, get negligible weight, or model noise/sub-senses.
- Weakness/Question: Covariance limited to spherical—unclear if this is for efficiency or regularization; compare to diagonal covariances to check benefits vs. overfitting.
- Weakness/Question: Lacks qualitative examples of learned mixtures for polysemous (e.g., bank, rock, cell) and monosemous words (means, weights, variances) to support interpretability claims.
- Weakness/Question: Training details missing—sensitivity to margin, negative sampling strategy, and presence of regularizers to prevent degenerate covariances or vanishing variances.
- Weakness/Question: Efficiency claims need asymptotic and empirical training/inference time comparisons and memory overhead as k increases versus baselines (point or single-Gaussian embeddings).
- Clarity: Exposition is clear but should better connect the expected likelihood kernel to mixture geometry and discuss limitations (e.g., sensitivity to variance scaling).
- Minor: Table 4 not referenced; add discussion or remove. Luong et al. missing publication year. Ensure consistent notation for mixture weights and covariances across sections.
