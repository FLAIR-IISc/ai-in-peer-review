Summary and contributions
This paper proposes probabilistic, multimodal word embeddings in which each word is modeled as a Gaussian mixture. The approach aims to capture multiple senses, asymmetry for entailment, and calibrated uncertainty. Training uses an energy-based max-margin ranking objective with an analytically tractable similarity: the expected likelihood kernel between distributions. Experiments on word similarity and lexical entailment show gains over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings, alongside qualitative evidence of sense separation.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A multimodal probabilistic representation (Gaussian mixtures) that models polysemy, uncertainty, and entailment within a single framework.
2) An efficient, closed-form energy function (expected likelihood kernel) paired with a max-margin objective, enabling scalable training.
3) Empirical evidence—both qualitative (nearest neighbors, visualizations) and quantitative (word similarity, entailment)—that the approach is competitive or superior to strong baselines.

Strengths
- Expressive representation of polysemy and uncertainty: Modeling each word as a mixture allows distinct senses to be localized in different modes, with covariances reflecting uncertainty. The qualitative examples (e.g., rock and bank) show clear sense separation, and the method avoids the variance inflation often seen in unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemous words.
- Sound and efficient learning setup: The expected likelihood kernel admits closed-form computation for Gaussian mixtures, which integrates naturally with the max-margin ranking loss. The paper provides practical stability techniques and scalable training details, achieving learning on billion-token corpora—evidence that the method is not only principled but also usable at scale.
- Empirical improvements on key benchmarks: The mixture model outperforms skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on multiple word similarity datasets (e.g., WS-353 and MEN) and achieves better lexical entailment performance, especially when using asymmetric KL metrics. These results support the claim that multimodality and uncertainty modeling are beneficial.
- Clarity and interpretability: The paper is generally well written, and the visualizations and nearest-neighbor analyses make the learned senses interpretable. The discussion of reduced variances for polysemous words helps explain why the model can improve entailment.

Weaknesses
- Limited exploration of the number of mixture components: Most experiments fix K=2, with only brief qualitative mention of K=3. There is no clear criterion for choosing K, little analysis of performance sensitivity to K, and limited discussion of how the model behaves for monosemous words (e.g., whether extra components collapse or split spurious senses).
- Restrictive covariance structure: All experiments use spherical covariances for computational efficiency. This limits the ability to model directionally anisotropic uncertainty. The paper does not report results with diagonal covariances nor quantify the trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency.
- Minor presentation issues: Table 4 is not cited in the text, and one reference (Luong et al.) is missing a publication year. These should be fixed for completeness and reproducibility.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Choosing K: How do you select the number of mixture components in practice? Please provide quantitative results varying K (e.g., 1, 2, 3) on the main benchmarks, and discuss criteria (validation metrics, BIC/AIC, held-out likelihood, or early stopping behavior). Does increasing K beyond 2 help, hurt, or saturate performance? Are there signs of overfitting or instability as K grows?
- Monosemous words: For words with a single dominant sense, how do the learned mixtures behave? Do extra components naturally collapse (e.g., low weights, overlapping means), or do they split the sense unnecessarily? Some illustrative examples or distributional statistics would help.
- Covariance structure: Is the choice of spherical covariances purely for computational reasons? Could you report results with diagonal covariances to assess whether modeling directional uncertainty yields measurable gains, and at what computational cost? If you tried diagonal and saw no benefit, a brief negative result would be informative.
- Practical guidance: Given your experience, what default settings (K, covariance type, dimension, window size) would you recommend for new users, and what failure modes should they watch for?
