Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison against similar approaches could be expanded.

General Discussion:
This paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word senses—that is, representing each word as a set of Gaussian components. The approach extends the model of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By moving to a multimodal formulation, the paper directly addresses polysemy.

Overall, this is a strong, well-structured, and clear paper. The experiments are sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 aligns with what one would expect from the proposed approach. There is little to fault; my suggestions below are intended to improve clarity and strengthen the empirical comparison.

Comments:
- It would be helpful to include a brief explanation of how your approach differs from Tian et al. (2014). Both methods split a single word representation into multiple prototypes via a mixture model; clarifying the modeling, training, or objective differences would aid readers.
- A few related works appear to be missing and should be cited:
  - Neelakantan, A., Shankar, J., Passos, A., & McCallum, A. (2014). Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space. EMNLP 2014.
  - Li, J., & Jurafsky, D. (2015). Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? EMNLP 2015.
  - Liu, Y., Liu, Z., Chua, T.-S., & Sun, M. (2015). Topical Word Embeddings. AAAI 2015.
- Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would provide a more comprehensive comparison and help position your contributions relative to prior multisense embedding methods.
- A question for the authors: What do you attribute the drop in performance of w2gm relative to w2g in the SWCS analysis?
