Review- Review: Multimodal Word Distributions
- Strengths: Overall, a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison with related approaches could be expanded.
- General Discussion:
The paper’s primary contribution is a new model for learning multimodal word distributions via Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple meanings—i.e., representing a word as a set of Gaussian components. The method extends Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which modeled words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By moving to a multimodal formulation, the approach addresses polysemy.
Overall, this is a strong, well-structured, and clear paper. The experiments are solid, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 shows results consistent with the method’s aims. There is little to criticize; the comments below are intended to further enhance clarity.
Some comments:
_ It would be useful to briefly explain how this approach differs from Tian et al. (2014). Both split a single word representation into multiple prototypes using a mixture model.
_ Some citations that could be added to Related Work include:
Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space. Neelakantan, A., Shankar, J., Passos, A., McCallum. EMNLP 2014
Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? Li and Jurafsky, EMNLP 2015
Topical Word Embeddings. Liu Y., Liu Z., Chua T., Sun M. AAAI 2015
_ Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 could be informative.
_ A question for the authors: What explains the performance drop of w2gm relative to w2g in the SWCS analysis?
