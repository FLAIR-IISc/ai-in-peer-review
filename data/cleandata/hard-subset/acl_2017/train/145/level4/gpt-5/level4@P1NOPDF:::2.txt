This paper represents words as Gaussian mixture distributions and demonstrates their potential to capture multiple senses for polysemous words. Training uses a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is employed as the similarity measure between two word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks support the effectiveness of the approach.

- Strengths:
  - The problem is clearly motivated and well defined. Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture different word meanings via distinct modes, with associated probability mass and uncertainty. This represents an important contribution to word embeddings.
  - The paper proposes a max-margin learning objective together with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training.
  - The paper is mostly well written.

- Weaknesses:
  - See below for several questions.

- General Discussion:
  - In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is typically a crucial parameter. In your experiments, k is fixed at 2. What criteria did you use to select k? Does increasing k hurt performance? What does the learned distribution look like for a word with essentially a single predominant meaning?
  - I notice that you use the spherical case in all experiments (the covariance reduces to a single scalar). Is this purely for computational efficiency? How does performance change if you use a general diagonal covariance matrix? In this more general case, the mixture can model different degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space, which seems potentially more informative.

- Minor comments:
  - Table 4 is not referenced in the text.
  - In the references, Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
