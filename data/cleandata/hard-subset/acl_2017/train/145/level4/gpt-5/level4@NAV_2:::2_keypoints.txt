- Proposes modeling words as Gaussian mixture distributions to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin energy-based objective using the expected likelihood kernel; shows strong results on similarity and entailment benchmarks.
- Well-motivated and clearly scoped problem; mixtures are more expressive than point embeddings and can represent distinct senses and uncertainty.
- Introducing a margin-based learning objective with a closed-form distributional similarity enables efficient, scalable training.
- Manuscript is generally clear and well presented.
- Main weaknesses are requests for clarification rather than substantive flaws.
- Key hyperparameter K (number of mixture components) is fixed to 2 in experiments; reviewer asks how K is chosen, whether larger K degrades performance, and what distributions look like for monosemous words.
- All experiments use spherical covariances; reviewer questions if this is only for efficiency and asks how performance would change with diagonal (anisotropic) covariances.
- Minor issues: Table 4 is not cited in the main text and Luong et al. reference is missing the publication year.
