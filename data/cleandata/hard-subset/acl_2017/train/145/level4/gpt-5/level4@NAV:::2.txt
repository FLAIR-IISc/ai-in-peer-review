This paper models words as Gaussian mixtures and demonstrates the approach’s ability to capture multiple senses for polysemous terms. Training is performed with a max-margin objective. The expected likelihood kernel serves as the similarity measure between two word distributions. Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks show the method’s effectiveness.
- Strengths:
The problem is well motivated and clearly defined. Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vector embeddings. By leveraging multiple modes, they can represent distinct word senses along with the associated probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This work constitutes a meaningful contribution to word embeddings.
The paper introduces a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training.
The writing is generally clear.
- Weaknesses:
See below for some questions.
- General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is typically an important hyperparameter. In this paper’s experiments, k is fixed at 2. What criterion do you use to choose k? Does increasing k degrade performance? What does the learned distribution look like for a word with essentially a single prevalent meaning?
I notice that all experiments use the spherical case (the covariance collapses to a single scalar). Is this purely for computational efficiency? How does performance change if you use a general diagonal covariance matrix? In that more flexible setting, the mixture can express different levels of uncertainty along different directions in semantic space, which seems particularly appealing.
