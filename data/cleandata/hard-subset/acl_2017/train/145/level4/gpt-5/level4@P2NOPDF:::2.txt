This paper represents words as Gaussian mixture distributions, enabling multiple senses for polysemous terms. Training is performed with a max-margin objective, and similarity between word distributions is computed using the expected likelihood kernel. Experiments on word similarity and lexical entailment tasks indicate strong performance and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

- Strengths:
  - The problem is well motivated and clearly defined.
  - Mixture-of-Gaussians embeddings are more expressive than point vectors, capturing multiple meanings via distinct modes and encoding probability mass and uncertainty around those modes.
  - The combination of a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure supports efficient training.
  - The work represents a meaningful contribution to word representation.
  - The paper is generally well written.

- Weaknesses:
  - No major issues; my main concerns are clarifications and additional analysis.

- Questions and discussion:
  - Number of components (k): In the experiments, k is fixed at 2. What criteria did you use to choose k? How sensitive is performance to k, and does increasing k degrade results (e.g., via overfitting or mode redundancy)? For words that are effectively monosemous, how do the learned mixtures behave—do they collapse to a single dominant component or distribute mass in another systematic way?
  - Covariance structure: All experiments use spherical covariances (a single variance per component). Was this choice primarily for computational efficiency or stability? Have you evaluated diagonal covariances? Allowing anisotropic uncertainty could capture direction-specific variability in semantic space and may yield richer representations; results or discussion on this trade-off would be valuable.

- Minor comments:
  - Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
  - In the references, the Luong et al. entry is missing the publication year.
