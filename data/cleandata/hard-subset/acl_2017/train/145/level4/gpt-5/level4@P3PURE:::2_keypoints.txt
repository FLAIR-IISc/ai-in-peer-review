- Paper represents words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and compared using the expected likelihood kernel; shows effectiveness on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Problem is clearly motivated; Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture multiple senses, probability mass, and uncertainty.
- Proposes a max-margin learning objective and a closed-form expected likelihood kernel that enable efficient training.
- Paper is mostly well written.
- Overall an important contribution to word embeddings.
- Main weaknesses are unresolved experimental/methodological questions raised by the reviewer.
- Why is the number of mixture components k fixed to 2; what criterion chose k; does increasing k hurt performance; what does the learned distribution look like for words with a single predominant meaning?
- Why use spherical covariances—purely for efficiency—and how does performance change with diagonal (or full) covariances that allow per-dimension uncertainty?
- Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
- Luong et al. reference is missing the publication year.
- Reviewer states they have read the authors’ response.
