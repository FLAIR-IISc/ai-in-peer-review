- Paper introduces multimodal word representations using Gaussian mixtures to model word senses, extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings.  
- Well written, clearly structured, with sound experiments and convincing qualitative analysis.  
- Strong motivation: Gaussian mixtures are a natural, effective way to capture polysemy.  
- Principled extension of prior unimodal work (Vilnis & McCallum 2014).  
- Good presentation and helpful qualitative examples.  
- Empirical results appear carefully conducted and supportive of claims.  
- Limited comparisons to existing multi-sense/multi-prototype embedding methods.  
- Related work section omits several relevant multi-sense embedding citations.  
- Request to clarify differences with Tian et al. (2014) across modeling, objectives, covariance parameterization, mixture weights, and sense induction.  
- Ask to add and compare to Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015), explaining sense induction mechanisms and evaluation differences.  
- Suggest strengthening empirical comparisons by including or reporting numbers from prior multi-sense methods in key tables.  
- Recommend adding more qualitative examples and failure cases to illustrate behavior across homonymy vs. related senses.  
- Advise consistent nomenclature for w2g (unimodal) and w2gm (mixture) and brief reminders in experiments.  
- Request discussion of trade-offs: computational overhead, training stability, number of components, initialization, and regularization.  
- Ask diagnostic analysis for SWCS performance drop of w2gm versus w2g (over-segmentation, sparsity, metric nature, optimization issues, component utilization/sense purity).  
- Overall: strong, principled contribution; accept contingent on improving related work discussion and expanding comparative results.  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer’s assessment remains unchanged.
