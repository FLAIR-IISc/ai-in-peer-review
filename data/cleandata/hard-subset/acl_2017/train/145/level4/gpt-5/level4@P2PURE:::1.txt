Review: Multimodal Word Distributions
- Strengths: Overall, a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison with related approaches could be expanded.

- General discussion:
The paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word meanings—that is, each word is represented by a set of Gaussian components. This approach extends Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By moving to a multimodal representation, the method addresses polysemy. The paper is well structured and clear. The experiments are sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 aligns with expectations for the proposed approach. There is little to fault; the comments below are intended to further improve clarity.

Some comments:
- It would be helpful to include a brief explanation of the differences between the approach of Tian et al. (2014) and the current method. Both split a single word representation into multiple prototypes via a mixture model.
- A few related-work citations appear to be missing and could be included:
  Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space. Neelakantan, A., Shankar, J., Passos, A., McCallum, A. EMNLP 2014
  Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? Li, J., and Jurafsky, D. EMNLP 2015
  Topical Word Embeddings. Liu, Y., Liu, Z., Chua, T.-S., and Sun, M. AAAI 2015
- Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would also be informative.
- A question for the authors: What explains the drop in performance of w2gm relative to w2g in the SWCS analysis?
