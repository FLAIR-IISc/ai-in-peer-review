- Proposes modeling words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple senses and uncertainty.
- Uses a max-margin training objective and the expected likelihood kernel as a closed-form similarity measure.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment show the method is effective.
- Well-motivated problem and clearly defined approach.
- Gaussian mixtures offer more expressiveness than deterministic embeddings by representing distinct senses and associated uncertainty.
- The max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Writing is generally clear.
- Hyperparameter k (number of mixture components) is fixed at 2—no criterion given for choosing k or analysis of larger k and its effect on performance.
- Unclear how words with a single prevalent meaning are represented under the fixed-k setup.
- All experiments use spherical covariances—question whether this choice is for efficiency and how results change with diagonal (more flexible) covariances.
- Minor issues: Table 4 is not cited, and Luong et al. reference is missing the publication year.
- Reviewer indicates they have read the authors’ response.
