Summary:
This paper represents words as Gaussian mixtures and demonstrates their potential to capture polysemy by modeling multiple senses as distinct modes. Training is performed with a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is used to measure similarity between two word distributions. Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks indicate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Strengths:
- The problem is clearly motivated and well defined. Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vector representations and can capture different word meanings through multiple modes, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty.
- The work proposes a max-margin learning objective and a closed-form similarity measure (the expected likelihood kernel) that together enable efficient training.
- The paper is mostly well written.
- Overall, this is an important contribution to word embedding.

Weaknesses:
- Primary concerns are captured in the questions below.

General discussion and questions:
- In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is typically an important parameter. In your experiments, k is fixed to 2. What criterion did you use to select k? Does increasing k hurt performance? What does the learned distribution look like for a word with only one predominant meaning?
- You use spherical covariances in all experiments (i.e., each covariance reduces to a single scalar). Is this purely for computational efficiency? How does performance change when using a general diagonal covariance matrix? In this more general case, the mixture could express different degrees of uncertainty along different dimensions in the semantic space, which seems particularly interesting.
