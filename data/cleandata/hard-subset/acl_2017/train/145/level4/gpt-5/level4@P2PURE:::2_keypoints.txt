- Paper models words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple senses and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin objective and compared using the expected likelihood kernel; shows strong results on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Strength: Problem is clearly motivated and well defined.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors, modeling distinct senses and associated uncertainty—an important contribution.
- Strength: The max-margin objective plus closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Strength: Paper is generally well written.
- Weakness/Question: Number of mixture components k is fixed at 2—what criteria determined k, does increasing k hurt performance, and how are single-meaning words represented?
- Weakness/Question: All experiments use spherical covariances—was this for efficiency, and how would diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect performance by capturing direction-specific uncertainty?
- Minor: Table 4 is not cited in the text.
- Minor: Luong et al. reference is missing the publication year.
