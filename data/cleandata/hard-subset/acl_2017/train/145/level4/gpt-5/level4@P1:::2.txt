Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution to model polysemy, entailment, and uncertainty. Training uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity function.
- Primary contributions (as I see them):
  1) A multimodal probabilistic embedding (Gaussian mixtures) that can capture multiple senses per word, along with interpretable uncertainty.
  2) An analytically tractable training setup combining a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel, enabling efficient large-scale learning.
  3) Empirical evidence (qualitative and quantitative) that the approach improves over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and entailment, and discovers distinct senses in an unsupervised manner.

Strengths
- Expressive representation of meaning and uncertainty: Modeling words as Gaussian mixtures is a well-motivated and powerful extension of point embeddings and unimodal Gaussian embeddings. The use of multiple modes directly addresses polysemy, while covariances capture uncertainty and facilitate interpretations such as entailment via inclusion.
- Tractable, scalable learning: The expected likelihood kernel provides a closed-form similarity between mixtures, fitting naturally into a max-margin ranking objective. The paper includes sensible practical choices (e.g., diagonal/spherical covariance, stability tricks, initialization) and demonstrates scalability to billions of tokens, which is crucial for adoption.
- Empirical gains on multiple benchmarks: The model outperforms skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on several word similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW) and on entailment (improved AP/F1 with KL-based scoring), indicating benefits beyond qualitative interpretability.
- Clear qualitative evidence of sense separation: Nearest-neighbor analyses show distinct components aligning with different senses (e.g., “rock” → geology vs. music; “bank” → river vs. finance). This addresses a known limitation of unimodal representations whose variances often inflate to cover disparate senses.
- Generally well written and organized: The motivation, method, and experiments are clearly presented, making the contribution easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Choice of number of components (K): All main experiments use K=2, but the paper does not provide a clear criterion for selecting K, a sensitivity analysis, or a trade-off discussion. It remains unclear whether increasing K helps or hurts overall performance, how common multi-sense words with more than two senses are handled in practice, and how the model behaves for monosemous words (e.g., whether extra components collapse or split noise).
- Restriction to spherical covariance: All experiments use spherical covariances. While this yields efficiency and stability, it limits the model’s ability to represent direction-dependent uncertainty in semantic space. The paper does not report results with diagonal covariances, which would be a natural middle ground between expressivity and tractability.
- Limited analysis of monosemous cases: The paper focuses on polysemy, but does not show learned distributions for words with a single dominant meaning. It would be helpful to see whether extra components naturally downweight or merge in these cases, and whether variance remains compact compared to unimodal baselines.
- Minor presentation issues: Some referencing and cross-referencing issues remain (see below), which slightly detract from polish.

Questions to the authors
- Model capacity and selection of K:
  • What criterion did you use to set K=2? Did you try K>2 systematically, and how did it affect similarity and entailment performance, training stability, and overfitting?
  • For words with a single common meaning, what do the learned mixtures look like (e.g., one dominant component with near-zero weights on others, or multiple similar components)? Please provide examples or statistics.
- Covariance structure:
  • Is the choice of spherical covariance purely for computational efficiency? Did you experiment with diagonal covariances, and if so, what were the training/runtime costs and performance differences?
  • Conceptually, diagonal covariances could model different uncertainty along different semantic axes. Do you expect performance gains from this added flexibility, or did you observe diminishing returns?
- Entailment scoring:
  • You report better entailment with minimum KL divergence than with cosine similarity. Given that KL does not have a closed form for mixtures, how did you approximate or compute KL in practice for w2gm? Please clarify the computational method and its cost.
- Stability and component behavior:
  • As training progresses, do mixture weights tend to concentrate on a single component for many words? Any regularization or annealing strategies used to avoid degenerate solutions?

Additional comments

- The paper is mostly well written; addressing the above minor issues would improve polish.
