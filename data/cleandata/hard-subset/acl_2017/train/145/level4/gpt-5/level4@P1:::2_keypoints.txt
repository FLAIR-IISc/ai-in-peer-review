- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixture distributions to model polysemy, entailment, and uncertainty using a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel.
- Contribution: multimodal probabilistic embeddings (Gaussian mixtures) that capture multiple senses per word and provide interpretable uncertainty.
- Contribution: an analytically tractable training setup combining a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for scalable learning.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that the method improves over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on similarity and entailment and discovers distinct senses unsupervised.
- Strength: expressive representation—multiple modes address polysemy and covariances capture uncertainty and entailment via inclusion.
- Strength: tractable, scalable learning with closed-form similarity and sensible practical choices, demonstrated on billions of tokens.
- Strength: empirical gains across multiple word similarity benchmarks and improved entailment performance (AP/F1 with KL-based scoring).
- Strength: clear qualitative evidence of sense separation in nearest-neighbor analyses (e.g., “rock”, “bank”).
- Strength: generally well written and organized with clear motivation, method, and experiments.
- Weakness: K selection unclear—experiments use K=2 without criteria, sensitivity analysis, or discussion of trade-offs and behavior for words with >2 senses.
- Weakness: restriction to spherical covariances limits modeling of direction-dependent uncertainty; no reported results for diagonal covariances.
- Weakness: limited analysis of monosemous words—no evidence whether extra components collapse or remain noisy compared to unimodal baselines.
- Weakness: minor presentation and referencing issues that reduce polish.
- Question: What criterion determined K=2, were K>2 tried systematically, and how did it affect performance, stability, and overfitting?
- Question: For monosemous words, do learned mixtures show one dominant component or multiple similar components—please provide examples/statistics.
- Question: Was spherical covariance chosen solely for efficiency, were diagonal covariances tried, and what were the runtime/performance trade-offs?
- Question: Do you expect diagonal covariances to yield meaningful gains or diminishing returns in practice?
- Question: How was KL computed or approximated for mixtures for entailment scoring given no closed form, and what is the computational cost?
- Question: Do mixture weights tend to concentrate on single components during training and were any regularization or annealing strategies used to avoid degeneracy?

- Additional: Addressing the minor issues would improve the paper’s polish.
