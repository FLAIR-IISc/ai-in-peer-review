Summary:
This paper represents words as Gaussian mixture distributions in embedding space to capture polysemy and uncertainty. Training uses a max-margin objective, and similarity between two word distributions is computed via the expected likelihood kernel, enabling closed-form comparisons. Experiments on word similarity and lexical entailment demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and problem formulation. Modeling words as distributions is a natural way to encode multiple senses, uncertainty, and relative probability mass.
- Using Gaussian mixtures increases expressiveness over deterministic vector embeddings and offers an intuitive handle on polysemy via mixture components.
- The proposed max-margin learning objective combined with a closed-form similarity measure supports efficient training and inference.
- The paper is generally well written and the empirical results indicate meaningful gains on standard benchmarks.
- The work makes a substantive contribution to the literature on probabilistic word embeddings.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Choice of mixture size (k): In all reported experiments, k is fixed to 2. What criteria guided this choice (e.g., development-set performance, computational budget, stability)? Please provide an ablation over k (e.g., k=1,2,3,4) to show how performance and overfitting trade off with increased capacity. Do larger k values hurt performance or just increase training time? For words that are effectively monosemous, what does the learned distribution look like—do extra components collapse, receive negligible weight, or model sub-senses/noise?
- Covariance structure: All experiments use spherical covariances. Is this primarily for computational efficiency or for regularization/stability? A comparison to diagonal covariances would be informative: does allowing dimension-wise uncertainty improve word similarity or entailment, or does it overfit? Even a small-scale study would clarify whether the added flexibility is beneficial.
- Qualitative analysis: The approach invites qualitative inspection. Please include examples of learned mixtures for clearly polysemous words (e.g., “bank,” “rock,” “cell”) and for monosemous words, with component means, weights, and variances. This would help validate the interpretability claims.
- Training details: The margin-based objective is sensible, but more detail would help reproducibility and interpretation. How sensitive is performance to the margin hyperparameter and negative sampling strategy? Are there regularizers on mixture weights or covariances to prevent degenerate solutions (e.g., vanishing variances)?
- Efficiency: The paper emphasizes efficiency via closed-form similarity. Could you provide asymptotic and empirical training/inference time comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., standard embeddings or single-Gaussian embeddings), and memory overhead as k increases?

Clarity and presentation:
- The exposition is generally clear and well organized. It would be helpful to more explicitly connect the expected likelihood kernel to the geometry of mixtures (e.g., what aspects of overlap it emphasizes) and to discuss any limitations (e.g., sensitivity to variance scaling).

Minor comments:
- Table 4 is not referenced in the main text; please add a discussion or remove the table.
- In the references, Luong et al. is missing the publication year; please add it.
- Please ensure notation is consistent when referring to mixture weights and covariance parameters across sections.
