- Proposes Gaussian-mixture word embeddings to model multiple senses, uncertainty, and asymmetric relations.
- Introduces a max-margin energy-based training objective using an analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel for mixtures.
- Presents qualitative sense separation and quantitative gains on word similarity and entailment benchmarks with scalable implementation details.

- Principled probabilistic treatment of polysemy: mixtures separate senses and avoid inflated variances of unimodal Gaussians.
- Sound objective and energy choice: closed-form energy for mixtures, covariances capture uncertainty, and regularization behavior is well discussed.
- Strong empirical performance: often outperforms skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings across similarity and entailment tasks.
- Practical and scalable: includes useful implementation tricks and trains on multi-billion-token corpora, aiding reproducibility.
- Clear presentation and aligned qualitative analyses.

- Limited comparisons to other multi-sense methods (e.g., Tian et al. 2014; Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015).
- Mixture model underperforms unimodal model on SCWS in some settings without analysis of causes.
- Limited analysis of the choice of K (number of components) and its effects on sense granularity, collapse, or fragmentation.
- Metric-objective mismatch: training energy has Euclidean structure while cosine often performs best; needs ablations.
- Evaluation scope narrow: relies on intrinsic tasks and lacks extrinsic downstream evaluations.

- Please clarify differences from Tian et al. (2014) and Neelakantan et al. (2014) beyond using probabilistic mixtures.
- Explain the w2gm drop versus w2g on SCWS and whether context-sensitive selection or probability-weighted averaging was tried.
- How should K be chosen in practice? Any observed component collapse or fragmentation and validation heuristics?
- How sensitive are results to covariance type (spherical vs diagonal), margin, initialization, and embedding dimensionality?
- For entailment, did you try training with an asymmetric energy or hybrid objective to encourage inclusion?

- Expand related work to include Neelakantan et al. 2014, Li & Jurafsky 2015, Tian et al. 2014, and Liu et al. 2015.
- Add comparisons or discussion against these methods (especially on WordSim-353 and SCWS) to strengthen results.
- Report statistical significance and clarify preprocessing (e.g., stopword removal) for fair comparisons.
- Minor corrections: fix “SWCS” → “SCWS” and briefly document how sense selection is performed for nearest neighbors in the visualization.
