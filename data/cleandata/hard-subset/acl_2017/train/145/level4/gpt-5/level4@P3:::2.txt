Summary and contributions
This paper proposes multimodal word embeddings, representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm) to capture multiple senses, uncertainty, and entailment. Training uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as a closed-form similarity between distributions. Experiments on word similarity and lexical entailment aim to show both quantitative improvements over word2vec skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings (w2g), and qualitative interpretability.

Main contributions (as I see them):
- A probabilistic, multimodal embedding of words via Gaussian mixtures trained with a scalable, analytic energy function (ELK) and a max-margin objective, yielding interpretable sense-separated representations.
- Empirical evidence that mixtures improve over single-Gaussian and word2vec baselines on several word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment, with qualitative demonstrations of polysemy handling and reduced variance for polysemous words.
- Practical training details (initialization, stability, subsampling/negative sampling) enabling large-scale training on billions of tokens.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and model expressivity: Modeling words as Gaussian mixtures naturally addresses polysemy by allocating distinct modes to different senses while retaining uncertainty information. The qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses (e.g., “rock,” “bank,” “cell”) convincingly show that different components capture distinct senses; the K=3 example for “cell” further supports multi-sense capacity.
- Sound, tractable energy and objective: Using the ELK yields a closed-form similarity for Gaussian mixtures and allows efficient max-margin training, avoiding the intractability of KL between mixtures. The analysis of the partial energies clarifies how mean separation and covariance interact and how the log-det term regularizes against variance inflation.
- Empirical improvements and breadth of evaluation: On many benchmarks (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW), w2gm outperforms w2g and often skip-gram. The entailment results show consistent gains for w2gm over w2g and further improvements when using asymmetric KL at test time, which is appropriate for entailment. The reduction in variance for polysemous words is a desirable qualitative behavior aligning with the stated motivation.
- Scalability and reproducibility: Training on ukWaC+WaCkypedia with billions of tokens and providing detailed hyperparameters, sampling schemes, optimization choices, and numerical-stability tricks demonstrate a robust, scalable pipeline. The public visualization is a nice addition for interpretability.
- Writing and presentation: The paper is generally clear and well structured. The contrast with unimodal Gaussians and deterministic multi-prototype models is well situated in the related work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior probabilistic embeddings: The move from unimodal Gaussians (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) to mixtures with an ELK-based energy is a natural extension. While the combination is well executed, the paper could better differentiate its methodological novelty beyond increased expressivity and closed-form energy, and more deeply analyze how mixture modeling interacts with the energy to yield the observed gains.
- Incomplete analysis of the number of components (K): Most experiments fix K=2, with a brief K=3 qualitative example. There is no principled criterion for choosing K, no ablation on performance vs. K, and no discussion of per-word adaptivity (e.g., component collapse for monosemous words). Without this, it is hard to assess robustness, overparameterization risk, or how the model handles words with one dominant sense.
- Covariance restrictions and uncertainty modeling: All experiments use spherical covariances for efficiency. This limits the model’s ability to capture anisotropic uncertainty and fine-grained entailment structure. The claim of “rich uncertainty” is weakened without results for diagonal covariances. An empirical comparison (spherical vs. diagonal) would strengthen the argument and test whether ELK’s log-det regularization sufficiently controls variance without collapsing interpretive structure.
- Metric selection and evaluation consistency: The paper reports results using multiple similarity measures (ELK, max cosine, min Euclidean), selecting the best per dataset. This risks metric-dependent gains and makes comparisons less clean. SimLex scores lag strong baselines. The entailment evaluation uses asymmetric KL at test time despite symmetric ELK in training, introducing a mismatch between training and evaluation objectives that is not fully addressed.
- Baselines and fairness of comparison: While w2gm is compared to w2g and word2vec, comparisons to other strong baselines (e.g., GloVe, more recent multi-sense embeddings) are limited or absent. Details on matching corpora, dimensions, and tuning for all baselines are crucial to ensure fairness. Similarly, runtime/memory comparisons for K=1 vs. K=2, and against word2vec, would clarify the practical trade-offs of the mixture approach.

Questions to authors
- Choice of K: How do you select K in practice, and how sensitive are results to K? Do components collapse for monosemous words (e.g., via low mixture weights), and do you observe consistent patterns of unused components? Would a data-driven per-word K (e.g., sparsity or nonparametric prior) be feasible?
- Covariance structure: What is the empirical trade-off between spherical and diagonal covariances (accuracy, speed, memory)? Does diagonal covariance improve entailment or SimLex where fine-grained similarity matters?
- Training–evaluation mismatch for entailment: Since ELK is symmetric and KL is asymmetric, did you consider training with an asymmetric energy (e.g., approximate KL surrogates) or adding an entailment-specific objective? How robust is entailment performance to the choice of ELK vs. cosine during training?
- Metric selection protocol: Were similarity metrics and thresholds chosen on a development set, and are the reported test results fixed by that selection? Could you report a canonical metric (e.g., cosine for all models) for apples-to-apples comparisons?
- Computational cost: What are the training time and memory overheads of K=2 vs. K=1 at D=50, and how do they scale with larger D (e.g., 200–300)? Any convergence differences or instability with larger K?
