Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison with closely related approaches could be expanded.

General Discussion:
This paper introduces a model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures, representing each word as a set of Gaussian components to capture multiple senses. It extends the approach of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which modeled each word as a single (unimodal) Gaussian. By adopting a multimodal formulation, the proposed method directly addresses polysemy.

The paper is well structured, clearly written, and technically sound. The experiments are appropriate, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 aligns with the model’s expected behavior. There is little to fault; the comments below aim to further improve clarity and contextualization.

Comments:
- It would be helpful to briefly explain how this approach differs from Tian et al. (2014). Both split a single word representation into multiple prototypes using mixture models; clarifying the modeling, training, or objective differences would strengthen the positioning.
- Please consider adding the following related work, which is highly relevant to multi-sense embeddings and topic-aware embeddings:
  - Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, and McCallum. “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space.” EMNLP 2014.
  - Li and Jurafsky. “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?” EMNLP 2015.
  - Liu, Liu, Chua, and Sun. “Topical Word Embeddings.” AAAI 2015.
- Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would make the empirical comparison more comprehensive.
- Question to the authors: What explains the performance drop of w2gm relative to w2g on the SWCS analysis?
