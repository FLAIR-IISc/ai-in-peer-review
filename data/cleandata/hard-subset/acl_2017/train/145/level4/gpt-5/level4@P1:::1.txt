Summary and Contributions
- This paper introduces a probabilistic, multimodal word embedding model that represents each word as a mixture of Gaussians. This extends Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which used a single Gaussian per word, and directly addresses polysemy by allowing multiple modes for distinct senses.
- The authors propose an energy-based max-margin training objective with an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function, enabling analytic, tractable similarity computation between mixtures while incorporating uncertainty.
- Empirically, the model discovers multiple senses in an unsupervised way and achieves strong results on word similarity and entailment benchmarks, with qualitative analyses that align with expectations.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated advancement over unimodal Gaussian embeddings: Modeling words with Gaussian mixtures is a natural and principled way to capture multiple senses while retaining uncertainty information. The paper convincingly argues why unimodal Gaussians can become overly diffuse for polysemous words and demonstrates how mixtures mitigate this.
- Sound training objective and energy function: The max-margin ranking objective combined with the expected likelihood kernel is technically well-justified and analytically tractable for mixtures. The discussion of how the partial energies evolve during training shows insight into how the model aligns senses across words.
- Strong qualitative evidence for sense separation: Table 1 shows compelling nearest-neighbor patterns where different components of words like rock and bank align with distinct senses (e.g., geological vs. musical, river vs. financial), which matches the stated goals of the approach.
- Solid empirical performance on standard benchmarks: The model outperforms strong baselines such as word2vec skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on multiple datasets (e.g., WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW). The entailment results also improve when using KL-based metrics, supporting the claim that uncertainty matters for asymmetric relations.
- Practical scalability and robustness: The paper provides useful details on numerical stability, covariance parameterization, initialization, and optimization, and reports training on multi-billion-token corpora. This improves reproducibility and demonstrates that the approach is feasible at scale.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete comparison to closely related multi-prototype baselines: While the paper contrasts with deterministic multi-sense methods and unimodal Gaussian embeddings, the experimental section would benefit from direct comparisons to recent multi-prototype approaches that also split a word into multiple senses. In particular, including Tian et al. (2014) and other multi-sense embeddings would strengthen the empirical case.
- Missing related work and absent baselines in tables: Important prior work relevant to multi-sense embeddings is not cited or compared against, including:
  - Neelakantan et al., “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space” (EMNLP 2014)
  - Li and Jurafsky, “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?” (EMNLP 2015)
  - Liu et al., “Topical Word Embeddings” (AAAI 2015)
  Including these in Related Work and, where possible, in the comparison tables (e.g., Tables 3 and 4) would give a more complete picture.
- Clarify differences from Tian et al. (2014): Both approaches produce multiple prototypes per word, but the precise modeling and training distinctions (e.g., EM-based latent sense assignment vs. probabilistic mixtures with uncertainty) are not clearly delineated. A concise comparison would aid readers.
- SCWS performance gap: The model appears to underperform the unimodal variant (w2gm vs. w2g) on SCWS in some settings. This is surprising given the model’s explicit handling of polysemy, and it warrants discussion (e.g., sensitivity to the choice of K, context selection, or similarity metric).

Questions to Authors
- What explains the loss of performance of w2gm relative to w2g on the SCWS dataset? Is this due to the number of mixture components K, the similarity metric (e.g., MaxSim vs. AveSim or KL-based measures), or training hyperparameters?
- Could you provide a brief, explicit comparison with Tian et al. (2014), highlighting key modeling and optimization differences, and ideally include their method as a baseline?
- Would it be possible to add results or at least discussion for the following multi-sense baselines: Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li and Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015)? If not, please comment on expected performance differences and the feasibility of integrating their comparison strategies into your framework.
