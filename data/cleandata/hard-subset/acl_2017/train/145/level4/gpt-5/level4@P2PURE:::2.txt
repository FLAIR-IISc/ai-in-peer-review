This paper represents words as Gaussian mixtures and shows their potential to capture multiple senses for polysemous words. Training is conducted with a max-margin objective, and similarity between two word distributions is measured using the expected likelihood kernel. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness.

- Strengths:
The problem is clearly motivated and well defined. Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vector representations, with the ability to model multiple senses via distinct modes, as well as the associated probability mass and uncertainty around those modes. This represents an important contribution to word embeddings.
The paper proposes a max-margin learning objective together with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training.
The paper is generally well written.

- Weaknesses:
Please see the questions below.

- General Discussion:
In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is typically a key parameter. In this paper’s experiments, k is fixed at 2. What criteria did you use to choose k? Does increasing k degrade performance? What does the learned distribution look like for a word with essentially a single dominant meaning?
I note that all experiments use spherical covariances (the covariance reduces to a single scalar). Is this choice purely for computational efficiency? How would performance change with a general diagonal covariance? Allowing diagonal covariances would capture different degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space, which seems particularly informative.
