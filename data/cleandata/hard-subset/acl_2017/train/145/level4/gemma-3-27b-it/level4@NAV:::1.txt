Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

**Strengths:** This is a notably strong paper overall.

**Weaknesses:** The evaluation could be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to related work.

**General Discussion:**
This paper presents a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, employing Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings – effectively representing a word with a set of Gaussian distributions. The work builds upon the unimodal Gaussian approach of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), addressing the challenge of polysemy through this multimodal extension. 

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and presents a strong methodological foundation. The experimental results appear sound, and the qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 aligns with expectations given the proposed approach. The work is of high quality, and the following comments are offered solely to potentially enhance clarity and completeness.

Specific comments:
* A brief discussion clarifying the distinctions between the current approach and that of Tian et al. (2014) would be beneficial, as both utilize mixture models to represent words with multiple prototypes.
* The related work section could be enriched by including citations to: “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space” (Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, & McCallum, EMNLP 2014), “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?” (Li & Jurafsky, EMNLP 2015), and “Topical Word Embeddings” (Liu, Liu, Chua, & Sun, AAAI 2015).
* Incorporating results from these related approaches into Tables 3 and 4 would further contextualize the presented findings.
* I would be interested to hear the authors’ perspective on the observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g in the SWCS analysis – what factors do they attribute to this outcome?
