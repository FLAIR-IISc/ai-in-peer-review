The main focus of this paper is the introduction of a new model for learning multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings. i.e., representing a word by a set of many Gaussian distributions. The approach extends the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By using a multimodal representation, the current approach addresses the problem of polysemy.

Overall, a very strong paper, well-structured and clear. The experimentation is sound and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 shows results as expected from the approach. There is not much that can be faulted, and all my comments below are meant to help the paper gain additional clarity. 

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a Gaussian mixture model for representing words, enabling the capture of multiple meanings and nuanced uncertainty. This is a significant improvement over unimodal Gaussian embeddings, as demonstrated both qualitatively and quantitatively. A second, valuable contribution is the use of an energy-based max-margin objective, coupled with the expected likelihood kernel, for learning the model parameters. This allows for analytic tractability and efficient training on large corpora. Finally, the comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons to existing methods on several benchmark datasets, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Effective Modeling of Polysemy:** The Gaussian mixture model demonstrably improves the representation of polysemous words, as shown in Table 1 and the interactive visualization. The ability to disentangle different meanings within a single word is a significant advance. This is supported by the qualitative examples provided, clearly showing components clustering around distinct semantic concepts (e.g., ‘bank’ separating into financial and river-related meanings).
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model consistently outperforms existing methods, including word2vec and the Gaussian embedding approach of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), on several word similarity and entailment datasets (Tables 2, 3, 5). The gains are particularly noticeable on tasks that benefit from a richer representation of semantic relationships.
3.  **Analytical Tractability and Scalability:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel enables analytic tractability, which is crucial for efficient training on large datasets. The authors report training on corpora with billions of words in days, indicating the scalability of the approach.
4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation using a variety of datasets and metrics, providing strong evidence for the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method. The inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative results strengthens the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Recent Multi-Prototype Methods:** While the comparison to Vilnis and McCallum (2014) is relevant, the paper could benefit from a more extensive comparison to other recent methods that also employ multi-prototype representations, such as those cited in the original review (Neelakantan et al., 2014; Li & Jurafsky, 2015; Liu et al., 2015). Including these baselines would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed approach.
2.  **Performance Drop on SCWS Dataset:** The performance drop of w2gm compared to w2g on the SCWS dataset is not fully explained (Table 5). A more in-depth analysis of this phenomenon would be beneficial. It’s unclear whether this is a limitation of the model or an artifact of the dataset/evaluation setup.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, such as the number of Gaussian components (K). Investigating the impact of these parameters on performance would provide valuable insights into the model’s behavior.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for the performance drop of w2gm compared to w2g on the SCWS dataset? Are there specific characteristics of this dataset that might explain this observation?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of the number of Gaussian components (K)? Have you explored the impact of different values of K on the results?
3.  How does the computational cost of training and inference scale with the size of the vocabulary and the number of Gaussian components?
