This work introduces a novel approach to word embedding using Gaussian mixtures, aiming to capture multiple word meanings and inherent uncertainty more effectively than traditional methods. The model is trained with a max-margin objective leveraging the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity computation. Experimental results demonstrate improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multimodal Word Representations:** The paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures, enabling the modeling of multiple distinct meanings and associated uncertainties – a significant improvement over single-vector embeddings. This is the primary contribution, offering a more nuanced and expressive semantic representation.
2. **Scalable Training with Expected Likelihood Kernel:** The authors present a practical and scalable training procedure based on a max-margin objective and the analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel. This facilitates learning from large corpora, as demonstrated in the experiments.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The work provides comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks, showing that the proposed model outperforms existing methods, including word2vec and previous Gaussian embedding approaches, on tasks sensitive to semantic nuance, such as word similarity and entailment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Novelty:** The motivation for moving beyond point embeddings to distributions is well-articulated, and the Gaussian mixture approach offers a compelling way to represent semantic ambiguity and uncertainty. This addresses a limitation of prior work.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient method for comparing probability distributions, enabling scalable training.
3. **Positive Empirical Results:** The consistently improved performance across multiple datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method and validates the benefits of modeling words as mixtures of Gaussians. The qualitative examples in Table 1, showing distinct meanings captured by different components, are particularly convincing.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Parameter Sensitivity (K):** The choice of the number of Gaussian components (K) is a critical parameter. The paper sets K=2 across most experiments, but a more thorough investigation of the impact of varying K, including its effect on performance for words with varying degrees of polysemy (including monosemous words), is needed. A justification for choosing K=2 would strengthen the analysis.
2. **Covariance Simplification:** The exclusive use of a spherical covariance matrix (diagonal with identical values) limits the model’s ability to capture directional variations in semantic uncertainty. Exploring the performance of a full diagonal covariance matrix, which allows for different levels of uncertainty along different dimensions, would be valuable. While acknowledged as a simplification for computational efficiency, the impact of this choice on representation quality should be discussed.
3. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares against several baselines, a more focused ablation study to isolate the contribution of the Gaussian mixture model itself (compared to the training objective or kernel choice) would be beneficial. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What criteria were used to select K=2 for the Gaussian mixtures? How does performance change with different values of K, and how does the model behave for words with only one prominent meaning when K>1?
2. The paper mentions computational efficiency as the motivation for using a spherical covariance matrix. Could you provide some quantitative results (e.g., training time) comparing the spherical case to a full diagonal covariance matrix?
3. Table 4 is present in the paper but is not referenced in the text. Please clarify its relevance or remove it.
