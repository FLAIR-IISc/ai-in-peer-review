This work leverages Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates their potential for capturing multiple word meanings, addressing the issue of polysemy. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is employed to measure similarity between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures offer a more expressive representation compared to deterministic word vectors. They have the potential to capture distinct word meanings through their modes, along with associated probability masses and measures of uncertainty. This work represents a significant contribution to the field of word embeddings.

The authors propose a max-margin learning objective coupled with a closed-form similarity measurement, enabling efficient training. 

The paper is, for the most part, well-written.

**Weaknesses:**

See the General Discussion section below for specific questions.

**General Discussion:**

In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is a crucial parameter. In the experiments presented, k is fixed at 2. What criteria were used to select this value? Does increasing k negatively impact model performance? Furthermore, what does the learned distribution look like for a word that predominantly has only one common meaning?

I observed that all experiments utilize the spherical case, where the covariance matrix is reduced to a single scalar value. Is this solely for computational efficiency? I am curious about the performance that could be achieved with a general diagonal covariance matrix. Allowing for different degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space—as a general covariance matrix would permit—seems potentially more insightful and powerful.
