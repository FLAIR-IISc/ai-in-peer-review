- Strong paper: clear motivation, well-described method, convincing experiments, and easy-to-follow structure.  
- Introduces multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy.  
- Builds on unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum 2014) to represent multiple prototypes per word.  
- Qualitative analysis (Table 1) and experiments consistently support benefits of the multimodal approach.  
- Weakness: comparisons could be strengthened by including recent, highly relevant methods.  
- Request to expand related work, especially clarifying differences from Tian et al. (2014).  
- Missing citations to include: Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), Liu et al. (2015).  
- Recommend adding quantitative results from those related works in Tables 3 and 4 for fairer comparison.  
- Ask for deeper analysis explaining w2gm’s performance drop vs w2g on SWCS—dataset-specific or general cause?  
- Post-rebuttal: authors’ responses were thorough, addressed concerns, and strengthened the paper.
