## Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

This is a strong paper overall.

The main focus of this paper is the introduction of a new model for learning multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures for multiple word meanings – representing a word by a set of multiple Gaussian distributions. The approach extends the model introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as unimodal Gaussian distributions. By using a multimodal representation, the current approach addresses the problem of polysemy.

Overall, this is a well-structured and clear paper. The experimentation is sound, and the qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 demonstrates expected results from the proposed approach. There is little to fault, and the following comments are intended to further clarify the work.

Some comments:

* It may be interesting to include a brief explanation of the differences between the approach of Tian et al. (2014) and the current one. Both split a single word representation into multiple prototypes using a mixture model.
* Several related works could be mentioned in the related work section:
    * Neelakantan et al. (2014) – Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space.
    * Li and Jurafsky (2015) – Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?
    * Liu et al. (2015) – Topical Word Embeddings.
* Including results from these approaches in Tables 3 and 5 would be valuable.
* Question for the authors: What do you attribute the loss of performance of w2gm against w2g in the analysis on the SCWS dataset?



**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this paper is the introduction of a Gaussian mixture model for representing word embeddings, enabling the capture of multiple word meanings and uncertainty. This is a significant improvement over previous approaches that rely on unimodal distributions.  A second contribution is the development of an energy-based max-margin objective function, combined with the expected likelihood kernel, for efficiently learning the parameters of this mixture model. This provides an analytically tractable and scalable learning procedure. Finally, the empirical validation on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, outperforming several baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of the Approach:** The use of Gaussian mixtures to model word distributions is a conceptually strong idea, directly addressing the limitations of unimodal embeddings for polysemous words. The theoretical grounding of the approach, using the expected likelihood kernel and max-margin objective, appears solid.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on multiple datasets, demonstrating statistically significant improvements over existing methods. The inclusion of both intrinsic (word similarity) and extrinsic (entailment) evaluations strengthens the findings.
3. **Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 and the interactive visualization website effectively illustrate the ability of the model to capture different word senses. This helps to build intuition about the learned representations.
4. **Scalability and Practicality:** The authors demonstrate that the proposed method can be trained on a large corpus (billions of words) in a reasonable time frame, highlighting its practicality for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Closely Related Work:** While the authors acknowledge related work on multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014), the comparison is somewhat limited. Including results from these methods in the tables would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
2. **Performance Drop on SCWS Dataset:** The observation that w2gm performs worse than w2g on the SCWS dataset is concerning and requires further investigation. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of this phenomenon in their response.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not extensively discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters such as the number of Gaussian components (K) or the margin (m). A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter tuning could strengthen the robustness of the findings.
