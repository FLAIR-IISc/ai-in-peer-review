- Uses Gaussian mixture models to represent words, aiming to capture multiple meanings and polysemy.  
- Training uses a max-margin objective and similarity is measured with the expected likelihood kernel.  
- Experiments show effectiveness on word similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Strength: problem is clearly motivated and well-defined.  
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors, capturing modes, probability mass, and uncertainty.  
- Strength: Max-margin objective plus closed-form similarity enables efficient training; paper is well-written.  
- Weakness/Question: Number of Gaussian components k fixed at 2—no justification given; unclear impact of increasing k on performance.  
- Question: How does the model represent words with a single dominant sense?  
- Simplification concern: all experiments use spherical covariances—unclear if for efficiency only and how diagonal/general covariances would affect performance and interpretability.  
- Minor comments: Table 4 not referenced; Luong et al. missing publication year; reviewer has read authors' response.
