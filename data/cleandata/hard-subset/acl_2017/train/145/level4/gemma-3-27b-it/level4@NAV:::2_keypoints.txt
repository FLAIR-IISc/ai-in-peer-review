- Paper proposes Gaussian-mixture word representations to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel for similarity.  
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment show the method is effective.  
- Strength: Clearly motivated problem and valuable contribution to word embedding research.  
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures increase expressiveness over deterministic vectors by representing multiple meanings, component probabilities, and uncertainty.  
- Strength: Efficient training via a max-margin objective and a closed-form similarity measure; manuscript is well written.  
- Question: Why is the number of Gaussian components k fixed at 2, and does increasing k harm performance?  
- Question: How does the learned distribution look for words with a single dominant meaning?  
- Question: Experiments use spherical covariances—was this for efficiency, and how would diagonal (per-dimension) covariances affect performance and uncertainty modeling?  
- Minor: Table 4 is not cited in the text.  
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
