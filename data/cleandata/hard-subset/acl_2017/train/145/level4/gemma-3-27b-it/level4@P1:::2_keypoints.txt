- Proposes Gaussian mixture word representations to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel; shows strong results on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Clearly motivated problem and well-defined approach.
- Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can model multiple senses, probability mass, and uncertainty.
- Important contribution to word embedding literature.
- Max-margin objective plus closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.
- Key weakness: number of Gaussian components k is fixed at 2 with no justification or analysis of different k values.
- Unclear how the model handles words with a single dominant meaning and what their learned distributions look like.
- Using spherical covariances for efficiency raises questions about potential gains from diagonal covariances that model directional uncertainty.
- Questions for authors: why k=2, how does performance vary with k, and what is the impact of diagonal/full covariances versus spherical?
- Additional comments: Table 4 is not referenced in the text; the Luong et al. citation is missing a publication year.
