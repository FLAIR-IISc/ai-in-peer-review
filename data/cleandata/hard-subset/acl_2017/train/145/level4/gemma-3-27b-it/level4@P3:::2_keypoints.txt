- Proposes Gaussian mixture word embeddings to model polysemy and uncertainty more effectively than point or unimodal embeddings.
- Learns distributions with a max-margin objective using the analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel for scalable similarity computations.
- Empirical results show improved performance on word similarity and entailment benchmarks, with qualitative visualizations supporting interpretability.
- Strong motivation and clear framing of the problem of capturing polysemy and uncertainty in embeddings.
- Technically novel and sound combination of Gaussian mixtures, max-margin learning, and the expected likelihood kernel enabling analytical tractability.
- Thorough experimental evaluation comparing to baselines (e.g., word2vec, prior Gaussian embeddings) with quantitative and qualitative analyses.
- Paper is well-written and well-organized, making methodology and results easy to follow.
- Insufficient justification for fixing the number of mixture components to K=2 and lack of analysis across words with varying polysemy; unclear behavior at K=1.
- Restrictive spherical covariance assumption limits expressiveness; no comparison with diagonal covariance to assess representational benefits versus computational cost.
- Limited analysis of the chosen negative sampling strategy and its effect on performance across different settings.
- Table 4 is included but never referenced or discussed in the text.
- Requests clarification on how performance varies with the number of components (K), especially for words with different polysemy levels.
- Asks for comparison of diagonal versus spherical covariance matrices and the computational/representational trade-offs.
- Seeks sensitivity analysis of model performance to negative sampling parameters.
- Minor edits requested: add publication year for Luong et al., include visualizations contrasting highly polysemous and unambiguous words, and discuss limitations/alternatives to the expected likelihood kernel.
