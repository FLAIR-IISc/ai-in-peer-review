## Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

This is a strong paper presenting a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings. The approach effectively extends the unimodal Gaussian model proposed by Vilnis and McCallum (2014) to address the challenges posed by polysemy. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the experimentation appears sound. The qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 demonstrates results consistent with the proposed methodology. While the work is of high quality, the following comments are offered to potentially enhance the paper’s clarity and impact.

The core contribution lies in representing a word as a collection of Gaussian distributions, allowing the model to capture the nuances of words with multiple meanings. This is a compelling approach, and the authors should be commended for a well-executed implementation.

However, the comparison to related work could be strengthened. Specifically, a brief discussion outlining the key differences between this work and Tian et al. (2014) would be beneficial. Both approaches utilize mixture models to represent words with multiple prototypes; highlighting the distinguishing features of the current method will further clarify its unique contributions.

Furthermore, the related work section would benefit from including several relevant citations. Specifically, the authors should consider referencing: 

*   Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, and McCallum (2014) – “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space”
*   Li and Jurafsky (2015) – “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?”
*   Liu, Liu, Chua, and Sun (2015) – “Topical Word Embeddings.”

Including results from these comparable approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would provide a more comprehensive benchmark and further contextualize the performance of the proposed model. This would allow readers to more easily assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of different methods.

Finally, I have a question for the authors regarding the analysis of SWCS: What factors do you attribute to the observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g? A deeper understanding of this result would be valuable.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have presented a well-reasoned and thoroughly evaluated model. Addressing the minor points outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its overall impact.
