- Represent words as Gaussian mixtures to model multiple meanings and uncertainty beyond single-vector embeddings.
- Scalable training via a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity computation.
- Empirical results show improvements over word2vec and prior Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and entailment tasks.
- Strong motivation and novelty in moving from point embeddings to distributional mixtures to capture semantic ambiguity.
- Technical soundness: the expected likelihood kernel provides a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient comparison of distributions.
- Positive empirical validation with consistent gains across datasets and convincing qualitative examples of distinct component meanings.
- Clear presentation and well-explained methodology and experimental setup.
- Sensitivity to the number of mixture components K: K=2 is used without sufficient justification or analysis across different K.
- Covariance simplification (spherical covariance) may limit modeling directional uncertainty; impact of diagonal/full covariance not evaluated.
- Limited ablation study: need to isolate the contribution of the Gaussian mixture model versus the training objective or kernel choice.
- What criteria motivated selecting K=2, and how does performance change with different K values and for monosemous words?
