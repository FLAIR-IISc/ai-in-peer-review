Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall, a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison to similar approaches could be expanded.
- General Discussion:

This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, utilizing Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings – effectively representing a word as a collection of Gaussian distributions. The approach extends the unimodal Gaussian distribution model for words proposed by Vilnis and McCallum (2014). By adopting a multimodal representation, the current work directly addresses the issue of polysemy.

Overall, this is a strong, well-structured, and clear paper. The experiments are sound, and the qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 demonstrates results consistent with the approach’s expectations. There are few significant weaknesses, and the comments below are intended to further enhance the paper’s clarity.

Specific comments:

* It would be beneficial to include a concise explanation of the key differences between the approach of Tian et al. (2014) and the present one. Both methods utilize mixture models to split a single word representation into multiple prototypes.
* The related work section would be strengthened by including the following citations:
    * Neelakantan, A., Shankar. J. Passos, A., & McCallum. Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space. EMNLP 2014.
    * Li and Jurafsky. Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? EMNLP 2015.
    * Liu Y., Liu Z., Chua T., & Sun M. Topical Word Embeddings. AAAI 2015.
* Including results from these related approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would be valuable for a more comprehensive comparison.
* I have a question for the authors: What factors do you attribute to the performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
