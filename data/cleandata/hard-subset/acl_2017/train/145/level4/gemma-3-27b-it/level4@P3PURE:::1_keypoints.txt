- Introduces a novel multimodal word distribution model using Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple senses per word.  
- Extends the unimodal Gaussian embedding model of Vilnis & McCallum (2014) to address polysemy.  
- Overall assessment: very strong paper—well-structured, clear, with strong experimental validation and consistent qualitative results.  
- Main weakness: empirical comparison against similar multi-prototype approaches is limited and should be extended.  
- Request to clarify key differences between the current method and Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models for word prototypes.  
- Suggests adding related citations: Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).  
- Recommends including results from those related approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for a more comprehensive comparison.  
- Asks authors to explain why w2gm loses performance compared to w2g in the SWCS analysis.
