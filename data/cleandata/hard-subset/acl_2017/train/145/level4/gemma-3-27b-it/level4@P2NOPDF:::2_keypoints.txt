- Proposes Gaussian mixture embeddings to model word meanings as distributions, addressing polysemy rather than using single vectors.
- Trains the model with a max-margin objective and measures similarity with an expected likelihood kernel.
- Reports effective results on word similarity and entailment tasks.
- Strength: Clear motivation and well-defined problem.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic embeddings and can capture multiple senses.
- Strength: Mixture modes provide sense probabilities and uncertainty, a promising direction.
- Strength: Max-margin learning plus closed-form similarity yields efficient training; paper is well-written.
- Weakness/Question: Number of components k is fixed at 2 without justification; impact of larger k is not explored.
- Weakness/Question: Unclear how the learned distribution behaves for words with a single dominant meaning.
- Weakness/Question: Experiments use spherical covariances only; need evaluation with diagonal covariances for anisotropic uncertainty.
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text; Luong et al. citation is missing the year.
