Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: This is a very strong paper overall.
- Weaknesses: The comparison to related work could be broadened.

General Discussion:
This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, utilizing Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings – effectively representing a word with a set of Gaussian distributions. The approach extends the unimodal Gaussian distribution model proposed by Vilnis and McCallum (2014) and addresses the issue of polysemy through this multimodal representation. 

Overall, the paper is strong, well-structured, and clearly written. The experimentation is sound, and the qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 demonstrates expected results consistent with the proposed approach. The paper is largely without fault; the following comments are intended to further enhance its clarity and impact.

Specific Comments:
_ It would be beneficial to briefly explain the key differences between the approach presented in Tian et al. (2014) and the current work. Both utilize mixture models to represent a single word with multiple prototypes.
_ The related work section could be enriched by citing the following papers: Neelakantan et al. (2014) – “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space”; Li and Jurafsky (2015) – “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?”; and Liu et al. (2015) – “Topical Word Embeddings.”
_ Including the results of these related approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would be informative.
_ A question for the authors: What factors do you attribute to the observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
