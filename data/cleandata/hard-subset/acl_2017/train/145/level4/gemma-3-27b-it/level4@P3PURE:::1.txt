Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- Strengths: Overall, a very strong paper.
- Weaknesses: The comparison against similar approaches could be extended.

General Discussion:
This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, utilizing Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings – effectively representing a word with a set of Gaussian distributions. The approach extends the unimodal Gaussian distribution model presented by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), addressing the challenge of polysemy through a multimodal representation. 

Overall, this is a well-structured and clear paper with strong experimental validation. The qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 yields results consistent with the proposed approach. There are few significant weaknesses, and the comments below are offered to further enhance the paper’s clarity and impact. 

Specific Comments:

* It would be beneficial to include a brief explanation of the key differences between the approach of Tian et al. (2014) and the current work. Both employ mixture models to represent a single word with multiple prototypes.
* The related work section could be strengthened by including the following relevant citations: Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, and McCallum (2014) – “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space”; Li and Jurafsky (2015) – “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?”; and Liu, Liu, Chua, and Sun (2015) – “Topical Word Embeddings.”
* Including the results of these related approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would be of interest and provide a more comprehensive comparison.
* Question for the authors: What factors do you attribute to the observed loss of performance of w2gm compared to w2g in the analysis of SWCS?
