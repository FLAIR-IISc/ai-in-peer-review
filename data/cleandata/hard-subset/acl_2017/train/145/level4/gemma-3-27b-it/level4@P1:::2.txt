This work uses Gaussian mixtures to represent words and demonstrates its potential in capturing multiple word meanings, addressing polysemy. The training process is performed using a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is employed as a similarity measure between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures are significantly more expressive than deterministic vector representations. They have the potential to capture different word meanings through their modes, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty. This work represents an important contribution to the field of word embeddings.

The authors propose a max-margin learning objective coupled with a closed-form similarity measurement, enabling efficient training.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

The choice of the number of Gaussian components, *k*, is a crucial parameter in Gaussian mixture models. In the experiments presented, *k* is fixed at 2. What criteria were used to select this value? Does increasing *k* negatively impact performance? Furthermore, what does the learned distribution look like for a word with only one dominant meaning?

The authors employ the spherical case (a single variance value for the covariance matrix) in all experiments, seemingly for computational efficiency. However, I wonder about the performance achievable with a general diagonal covariance matrix. This more general formulation allows the Gaussian mixture to define varying degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space, which appears more promising.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   What criteria were used to select *k*=2? How does performance change with different values of *k*?
*   What is the performance impact of using a full diagonal covariance matrix compared to the spherical case?
*   Can you provide examples of learned distributions for words with a single, dominant meaning?
