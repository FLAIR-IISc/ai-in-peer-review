- Introduces Gaussian mixture model for multimodal word distributions to capture polysemy, extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014).
- Employs an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for analytically tractable, scalable learning.
- Empirical results show improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks and scalability to training on large corpora.
- Qualitative analysis and interactive visualization (Table 1, website) convincingly demonstrate the model capturing different word senses.
- Suggests clarifying differences between this approach and Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models for multiple prototypes.
- Recommends citing and comparing with Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).
- Requests inclusion of results from those related multi-prototype methods in Tables 3 and 5 for a more comprehensive evaluation.
- Flags that w2gm underperforms w2g on the SCWS dataset and asks for analysis of this performance drop.
- Notes limited comparison to closely related work on multi-prototype embeddings and urges expanded evaluation.
- Points out lack of thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (number of components K, margin m) and suggests investigating tuning effects.
