- Proposes Gaussian-mixture word embeddings to capture multiple senses and uncertainty, offering a more expressive alternative to deterministic vectors.
- Introduces an analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel within a max-margin framework, enabling efficient training.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over baselines on word similarity and entailment, supported by qualitative nearest-neighbor and visualization analyses.
- Sensitivity to the number of Gaussian components (K) is not thoroughly explored; effects for mono- vs. highly polysemous words remain unclear.
- Restricting covariances to diagonal/spherical form for efficiency limits the ability to model anisotropic uncertainty; impact of full covariances is untested.
- Paper lacks a detailed ablation study to quantify the contribution of components like the max-margin objective and the expected likelihood kernel.
- What criteria justified choosing K=2, and how was sensitivity to other K values evaluated?
- Were full covariance matrices evaluated, and if so, what performance differences were observed?
- What is the computational cost of using full covariances, and which approximations could mitigate that cost?
- How does the model handle rare or unseen words, and are there techniques used to address limited-data challenges?
