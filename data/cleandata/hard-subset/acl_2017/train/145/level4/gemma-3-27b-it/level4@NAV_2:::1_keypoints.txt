- Introduces a Gaussian mixture model for multimodal word distributions to represent polysemy, extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014).  
- Uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for tractable, efficient learning.  
- Strong, clear paper with sound experiments and qualitative results supporting the approach.  
- Primary contribution: multimodal Gaussian embeddings capture multiple meanings and nuanced uncertainty better than unimodal Gaussians.  
- Secondary contribution: analytic tractability and scalable training via the expected likelihood kernel.  
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing improvements on multiple benchmark datasets.  
- Strength: effectively disentangles polysemous senses (e.g., “bank” → financial vs. river meanings).  
- Strength: outperforms baselines (word2vec, unimodal Gaussian embeddings) on several similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Strength: demonstrated scalability (training on corpora with billions of words in days).  
- Strength: thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluations across datasets and metrics.  
- Weakness: limited comparisons to other recent multi-prototype methods (e.g., Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky, Liu et al.).  
- Weakness: unexplained performance drop of w2gm versus w2g on the SCWS dataset.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., number of Gaussian components K).  
- Question: What explains the w2gm performance drop on SCWS—dataset artifacts or model limitation?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the number of Gaussian components K; were different K values explored?  
- Question: How does computational cost for training and inference scale with vocabulary size and number of components?
