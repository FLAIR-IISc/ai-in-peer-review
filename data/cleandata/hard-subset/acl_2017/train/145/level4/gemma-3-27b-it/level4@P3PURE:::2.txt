This work utilizes Gaussian mixtures to represent words, demonstrating their potential for capturing multiple word meanings and addressing polysemy. The training process is based on a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is employed to measure the similarity between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined. Gaussian mixtures are significantly more expressive than deterministic vector representations, offering the potential to capture distinct word meanings through their modes, along with associated probability mass and uncertainty. This work represents an important contribution to the field of word embeddings. 

Furthermore, the authors propose a max-margin learning objective coupled with a closed-form similarity measurement, enabling efficient training. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

See the “General Discussion” section below for specific questions and concerns.

**General Discussion:**

In Gaussian mixture models, the number of Gaussian components (k) is a crucial parameter. In the experiments presented, k is fixed at 2. What criteria were used to select this value? Does increasing k negatively impact model performance? Additionally, how does the learned distribution appear for a word that primarily possesses a single, dominant meaning?

I observed that all experiments utilize the spherical case, reducing the covariance matrix to a single scalar value. Is this simplification solely for computational efficiency? I am curious about the performance achievable with a general diagonal covariance matrix. In this more general case, the Gaussian mixture could define varying degrees of uncertainty along different directions in the semantic space, which seems potentially more insightful.
