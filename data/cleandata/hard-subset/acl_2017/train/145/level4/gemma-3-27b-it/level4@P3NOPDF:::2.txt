This work introduces a novel approach to word representation learning using Gaussian mixtures, demonstrating a promising ability to model polysemy by capturing multiple word meanings. The model is trained with a max-margin objective, and employs the expected likelihood kernel to efficiently measure similarity between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and textual entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

The motivation for this work is clear and the problem well-defined. Traditional word embeddings represent each word as a single, deterministic vector, limiting their ability to represent words with multiple meanings. This paper addresses this limitation by leveraging the greater expressiveness of Gaussian mixtures. The use of multiple modes in the Gaussian mixtures offers the potential to capture distinct word senses, alongside representing the probability mass and associated uncertainty for each sense. This represents a potentially significant contribution to the field of word embedding research. 

A key strength lies in the formulation of a max-margin learning objective coupled with a closed-form similarity measurement, enabling efficient training. The paper is, for the most part, well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

The choice of the number of Gaussian components, *k*, is a crucial parameter in Gaussian mixture models. In the presented experiments, *k* is fixed at 2. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the criteria used for this selection. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand how performance is affected by increasing *k*. Furthermore, analyzing the learned distributions for words with a predominantly single meaning would provide insight into the model’s behavior and the effectiveness of using a mixture even in such cases.

The paper consistently employs the spherical Gaussian assumption (i.e., restricting the covariance matrix to a single scalar value) throughout the experiments, ostensibly for computational efficiency. While understandable, this simplification warrants further investigation. A comparison against using a general diagonal covariance matrix would be valuable. Allowing for different degrees of uncertainty along different dimensions in the semantic space – as a diagonal covariance matrix would permit – appears conceptually more nuanced and potentially beneficial.

**General Discussion & Questions:**

The proposed Gaussian mixture model provides a compelling alternative to existing word embedding techniques, particularly for handling polysemous words. However, several points require further clarification.

1. **Component Selection (k):** What is the rationale for choosing *k*=2? Was any experimentation performed with different values of *k*, and if so, what were the results? How does the model perform when *k* is increased, potentially to capture more subtle semantic distinctions?

2. **Covariance Matrix Simplification:** The use of a spherical covariance matrix significantly reduces computational complexity. However, what impact does this simplification have on the model's expressive power? Is there a trade-off between computational efficiency and the ability to capture more complex semantic relationships? Were experiments conducted with a diagonal covariance matrix to assess this trade-off? If not, could the authors discuss the potential benefits and challenges of using a more general covariance structure?

3. **Distribution Analysis:** Could the authors provide examples of the learned Gaussian mixture distributions for a few representative words – both polysemous and monosemous? Visualizing these distributions would help illustrate the model’s behavior and validate its ability to capture different word senses.
