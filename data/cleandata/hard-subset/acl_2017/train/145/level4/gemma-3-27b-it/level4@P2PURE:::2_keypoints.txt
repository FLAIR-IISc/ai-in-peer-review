- Proposes Gaussian mixture word representations to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel; experiments on word similarity and entailment validate the approach.
- Strength: Well-motivated problem and clearly defined.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors.
- Strength: Multiple modes with associated masses and uncertainties can capture distinct word senses.
- Strength: Max-margin learning plus a closed-form similarity measure enables efficient training.
- Strength: Paper is generally well-written.
- Question: Why is the number of Gaussian components k fixed at 2—how was this chosen and does increasing k hurt performance?
- Question: How does the learned distribution look for words with a single dominant meaning?
- Question: Experiments use spherical covariances—is this solely for efficiency, and how would diagonal covariances (per-dimension uncertainty) affect performance?

- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
