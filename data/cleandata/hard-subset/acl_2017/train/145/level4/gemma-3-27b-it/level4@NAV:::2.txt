This paper introduces a novel approach to word representation using Gaussian mixtures, effectively modeling polysemy by capturing multiple word meanings. The model is trained with a max-margin objective, utilizing the expected likelihood kernel to measure similarity between word distributions. Experimental validation on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrates the method's efficacy.

**Strengths:**

The research addresses a well-defined and motivated problem. Employing Gaussian mixtures offers a significant advantage in expressiveness compared to deterministic word vectors, with the potential to represent distinct word meanings through its mixture components, associated probabilities, and uncertainty quantification. This work constitutes a valuable addition to the field of word embedding research. 
Furthermore, the paper proposes an efficient training procedure based on a max-margin learning objective coupled with a closed-form similarity measure. 
The manuscript is generally well-written and clear.

**Weaknesses:**

Please see questions below.

**General Discussion:**

The number of Gaussian components (*k*) is a crucial parameter in Gaussian mixture models. In this work, *k* is fixed at 2. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this selection?  Does increasing *k* negatively impact performance?  Additionally, how does the learned distribution appear for a word possessing a predominantly single, dominant meaning?

The experiments consistently employ the spherical covariance case (reducing the covariance matrix to a scalar value). Is this a deliberate simplification for computational efficiency? I am curious about the performance achievable with a general diagonal covariance matrix.  Such a configuration would allow the Gaussian mixture to represent varying levels of uncertainty across different dimensions of the semantic space, which appears potentially more insightful.
