- Uses Gaussian mixtures to represent words to capture polysemy and multiple meanings.
- Trains model with a max-margin objective and uses the expected likelihood kernel for measuring similarity.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks show the proposed approach is effective.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation; Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic embeddings and can model modes, probability masses, and uncertainty.
- Max-margin objective plus closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Paper is well-written.

Weaknesses / open questions:
- The number of mixture components k is fixed at 2 — no justification given; unclear how k was chosen and how larger k affects performance.
- Unclear how the model represents predominantly monosemous words (what learned distribution looks like).
- All experiments use spherical covariances — is this for computational reasons, and how would diagonal or full covariances affect results?

Minor comments:
- Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
- Missing publication year for the Luong et al. reference.
