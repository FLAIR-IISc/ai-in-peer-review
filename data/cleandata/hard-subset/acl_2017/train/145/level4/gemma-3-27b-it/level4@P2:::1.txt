## Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

This is a strong paper overall, presenting a novel approach to word representation learning. The core contribution lies in extending the Gaussian embedding model of Vilnis and McCallum (2014) to utilize Gaussian mixtures, enabling the representation of multiple word meanings and addressing the limitations of unimodal representations in capturing polysemy. The authors effectively demonstrate the benefits of this multimodal approach through comprehensive experimentation and insightful qualitative analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of the Approach:** The use of Gaussian mixtures to model word distributions is a conceptually sound extension of previous work. Representing words as distributions, rather than single points, allows for a more nuanced capture of semantic information, including uncertainty and multiple meanings. The energy-based max-margin objective, combined with the expected likelihood kernel, provides an analytically tractable and effective learning mechanism.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on various word similarity and entailment tasks. The proposed model (w2gm) consistently outperforms the baseline Gaussian embedding (w2g) and achieves competitive performance compared to established methods like word2vec. The improvements observed on datasets requiring the disambiguation of polysemous words are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 effectively illustrates the ability of the model to capture distinct meanings for polysemous words. The visualization tool, linked in the paper, further enhances understanding of the learned representations and provides a valuable resource for exploring the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Mixture Models:** While the paper acknowledges related work employing mixture models for word embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014), the comparative analysis could be strengthened. A more direct comparison with these methods, including their inclusion in Tables 3 and 4, would provide a clearer understanding of the proposed approach's relative advantages.
2. **Performance Dip on SCWS Dataset:** The observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g on the SCWS dataset warrants further investigation. While a question is posed to the authors, elaborating on potential reasons within the review itself (e.g., potential overfitting, sensitivity to hyperparameter settings) could strengthen the assessment.
3. **Missing Citations:** The review correctly identifies missing citations of relevant work in the related work section, such as Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li and Jurafsky (2015) and Liu et al. (2015). While not a critical flaw, including these would strengthen the positioning of the work within the existing literature.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What factors contribute to the observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g on the SCWS dataset?  Specifically, did you observe any sensitivity to hyperparameter settings when evaluating on this dataset?
2. Could you elaborate on the specific differences between your approach and that of Tian et al. (2014), given that both utilize mixture models to represent multiple word prototypes?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the methodology and understand the results.
* The inclusion of implementation details and a link to a visualization tool are commendable and contribute to the reproducibility and accessibility of the work.
* The use of both expected likelihood kernel and cosine similarity for evaluating the embeddings is a strong methodological choice, providing a comprehensive assessment of the learned representations.
