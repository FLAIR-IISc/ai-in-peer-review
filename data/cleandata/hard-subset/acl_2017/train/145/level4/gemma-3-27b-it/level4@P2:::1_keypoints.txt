- Extends Gaussian embeddings to Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, using an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel.  
- Represents words as distributions (not points), capturing uncertainty and multiple meanings.  
- w2gm shows strong empirical gains over w2g and competitive performance with word2vec, especially on tasks requiring word-sense disambiguation.  
- Qualitative analysis (Table 1) and linked visualization convincingly demonstrate distinct sense representations for polysemous words.  
- Comparative analysis to other mixture-model embedding works is limited; direct comparisons (e.g., Huang et al. 2012; Tian et al. 2014) should be included in result tables.  
- w2gm performs worse than w2g on the SCWS dataset, which needs investigation (possible overfitting or hyperparameter sensitivity).  
- Missing citations of relevant prior work (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) weaken the related-work positioning.  
- Asks authors to explain factors behind SCWS performance drop and any hyperparameter sensitivity observed.  
- Requests clarification on specific differences from Tian et al. (2014) despite both using mixture models.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, aiding comprehension.  
- Inclusion of implementation details and visualization tool supports reproducibility.  
- Using both expected likelihood kernel and cosine similarity provides a comprehensive evaluation of embeddings.
