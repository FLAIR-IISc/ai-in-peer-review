## Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

**Overall Score:** Strong Accept

**Strengths:** This is a strong paper presenting a valuable extension to existing work on word representation. The motivation is clear, the method is well-described, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:** While the comparison to existing methods is solid, it could be further strengthened by including results from several recent and highly relevant approaches. 

**General Discussion:**

This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings. The approach builds upon the unimodal Gaussian representation introduced by Vilnis and McCallum (2014) and directly addresses the challenge of polysemy by modeling words as distributions over multiple Gaussian prototypes. This allows for a more nuanced representation of word meaning, capturing the variety of contexts in which a word can appear. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly articulates the core idea and its implementation. The experiments are appropriately designed and executed, and the qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 effectively demonstrates the expected behavior of the proposed model. The results consistently support the benefits of the multimodal approach. My comments below are intended to refine the presentation and further strengthen the paper’s impact.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Related Work Expansion:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, specifically approaches that also leverage mixture models for representing multiple word prototypes.  A brief explanation of the key differences between this work and Tian et al. (2014) would be particularly helpful, as both decompose single word representations into multiple prototypes.  

* **Missing Citations:** The related work section would be more comprehensive with the inclusion of several key publications:
    * Neelakantan et al. (2014), “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space.”
    * Li and Jurafsky (2015), “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?”
    * Liu et al. (2015), “Topical Word Embeddings.”

* **Comparative Results:**  Including quantitative results from the aforementioned related work in Tables 3 and 4 would provide a more thorough comparative analysis and highlight the advantages of the proposed multimodal Gaussian mixture model. This would more convincingly demonstrate the contribution of this work.

* **SWCS Analysis – Performance Dip:** The authors are asked to provide further insight into the observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g in the analysis of SWCS. A more detailed explanation of the potential causes for this difference would be valuable. Is this specific to the SWCS dataset, or a more general phenomenon? What specific characteristics of SWCS might contribute to this outcome?



**Post-Rebuttal Remarks:**

I have reviewed the authors’ response to my comments and find it to be thorough and insightful. The authors adequately address my concerns regarding related work and provide a reasonable explanation for the observed performance differences in the SWCS analysis. The clarifications and additions provided strengthen the paper.
