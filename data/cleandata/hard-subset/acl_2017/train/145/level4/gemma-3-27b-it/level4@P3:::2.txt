This work introduces a novel approach to word embedding using Gaussian mixtures, aiming to capture multiple word meanings and inherent uncertainty more effectively than traditional methods. The authors propose learning these distributions via a max-margin objective, leveraging the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity calculations. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multimodal Word Representation:** The core contribution is the use of Gaussian mixtures to represent words, enabling the modeling of polysemy and providing a richer representation than single-point embeddings or unimodal Gaussian distributions. This allows for capturing multiple distinct meanings and associated uncertainties for each word.
2. **Scalable Learning with Expected Likelihood Kernel:** The authors propose a practical and scalable learning objective based on maximizing the margin between related and unrelated word distributions, facilitated by the use of the analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel. This addresses the computational challenges associated with more complex probabilistic models.
3. **Empirical Validation of Multimodal Benefits:** Through comprehensive experiments on standard benchmarks, the paper provides evidence that the proposed Gaussian mixture model outperforms existing methods, particularly in tasks requiring nuanced semantic understanding like word similarity and entailment. The qualitative analysis and visualization further support the model's ability to learn interpretable representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding techniques and establishes a compelling motivation for exploring multimodal representations. The problem of capturing polysemy and uncertainty is well-defined and relevant to the field.
2. **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The combination of Gaussian mixtures, a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is a technically sound and novel approach. The choice of the kernel ensures analytical tractability, which is a significant advantage for scaling the model to large datasets.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conducted a thorough evaluation of their model on multiple benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to established methods like word2vec and previous Gaussian embedding approaches. The inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative analyses strengthens the findings.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology and understand the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Justification for Fixed Number of Components (K=2):** The choice of using only two Gaussian components (K=2) throughout most experiments is not sufficiently justified. The paper briefly mentions that increasing K doesn’t consistently improve performance, but a more detailed analysis exploring the impact of K on different types of words (e.g., words with varying degrees of polysemy) is needed. What is the behavior of the model when K=1 (essentially a standard Gaussian embedding)?
2. **Spherical Covariance Assumption:** The exclusive use of a spherical covariance matrix (diagonal elements are equal) limits the expressiveness of the model. While the authors acknowledge this is for computational efficiency, the potential benefits of allowing different variances along different semantic dimensions are not fully explored. A comparison with a diagonal covariance matrix would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Analysis of Negative Sampling:** The paper mentions using a specific negative sampling strategy, but a discussion on its impact compared to other negative sampling methods would be valuable. Does the performance significantly change with different negative sampling parameters?
4. **Table 4 is Unused:** Table 4 is present in the paper but is never referenced or discussed in the text. This is a minor point but should be addressed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the number of Gaussian components (K) on model performance, particularly for words with different levels of polysemy?
2.  What is the performance impact of using a general diagonal covariance matrix compared to the spherical covariance assumption?  Do you observe a trade-off between computational cost and representational power?
3.  How sensitive is the model to the parameters used in the negative sampling strategy?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please add the publication year to the reference for Luong et al.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the learned distributions for words with varying degrees of polysemy (e.g., a highly polysemous word vs. a word with a single clear meaning) to further illustrate the model’s capabilities.
*   A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the expected likelihood kernel and potential alternatives would be beneficial.
