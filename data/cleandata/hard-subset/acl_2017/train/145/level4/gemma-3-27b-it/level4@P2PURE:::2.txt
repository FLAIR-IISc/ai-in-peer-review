This work leverages Gaussian mixtures to model word representations, demonstrating their ability to capture multiple meanings inherent in polysemous words. Training is performed via a max-margin objective, with the expected likelihood kernel employed to quantify similarity between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and textual entailment tasks validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

- Strengths:
The problem is well-motivated and clearly defined. Gaussian mixtures offer a more expressive representation compared to deterministic word vectors. The use of multiple modes, alongside associated probability masses and uncertainty measures, allows the potential to capture distinct word senses. This work constitutes a valuable contribution to the field of word embeddings.
Furthermore, the authors propose a max-margin learning objective coupled with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training.
The paper is generally well-written.

- Weaknesses:
See questions below.

- General Discussion:
The number of Gaussian components, *k*, is a crucial parameter in Gaussian mixture models. In this paper, *k* is fixed at 2. What criteria were used to select this value? Does increasing *k* negatively impact performance? Additionally, how does the learned distribution appear for a word with a predominantly single, common meaning? 
I observed that all experiments utilize the spherical case, restricting the covariance matrix to a scalar value. Is this solely for computational efficiency? I am curious about the performance achievable with a general diagonal covariance matrix. Such a configuration would allow the Gaussian mixture to model varying degrees of uncertainty across different dimensions of the semantic space, which seems potentially more informative.
