This paper explores the use of Gaussian mixtures to represent word meanings, effectively addressing polysemy by modeling words as distributions rather than single vectors. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, and word similarity is measured using an expected likelihood kernel. Results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach.

**Strengths:**

The motivation for this work is clear and the problem well-defined. Gaussian mixtures offer a significant advantage over deterministic word embeddings by providing a more expressive representation capable of capturing multiple meanings. The ability to represent different word senses through mixture modes, alongside associated probabilities and uncertainty, is a promising direction for word embedding research. This work represents a valuable contribution to the field. 

The proposed max-margin learning objective, coupled with a closed-form similarity measurement, offers an efficient training approach. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

See specific questions below.

**General Discussion:**

A crucial parameter in Gaussian mixture models is the number of components, *k*. In the experiments presented, *k* is fixed at 2. What criteria were used to select this value? Furthermore, has the impact of increasing *k* on model performance been investigated? It would be insightful to understand how the learned distribution manifests for words possessing primarily a single, dominant meaning.

The experiments consistently employ the spherical covariance case, simplifying the covariance matrix to a single scalar value. While understandable for computational efficiency, it would be beneficial to know the performance implications of using a more general diagonal covariance matrix. Allowing for differing degrees of uncertainty along different semantic dimensions – as a diagonal covariance matrix would permit – seems potentially more expressive and interesting.
