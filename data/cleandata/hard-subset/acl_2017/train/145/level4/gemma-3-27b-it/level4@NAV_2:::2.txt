This work introduces a novel approach to word embedding using Gaussian mixtures to represent words as multimodal distributions, aiming to capture multiple word meanings and inherent uncertainty. The method is trained via a max-margin objective employing the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity measurement. Empirical results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

The core idea of representing words with Gaussian mixtures is a strong contribution, offering a more expressive alternative to traditional deterministic word vectors. This allows the model to capture nuanced semantic information, including polysemy and varying degrees of certainty associated with different meanings, which is a significant advancement in the field of word representation learning.

The development of an analytically tractable energy function (expected likelihood kernel) within the max-margin framework is a key strength. This enables efficient training of the model, overcoming potential computational challenges associated with more complex probabilistic models. This practical consideration enhances the applicability of the proposed method.

The empirical evaluation provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The model consistently outperforms baseline methods on standard word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating its ability to learn meaningful and informative word representations. The qualitative analysis, including nearest neighbor examples and the interactive visualization, further supports the claim that the model captures distinct word senses.

**Weaknesses:**

The sensitivity of the model to the choice of the number of Gaussian components (K) is not fully explored. While the paper justifies K=2, a more thorough investigation into the impact of varying K on performance, particularly for words with varying degrees of polysemy, is needed. How does the model behave when K=1 for monosemous words, and does increasing K beyond 2 yield diminishing returns or improved performance for highly polysemous words?

The decision to restrict the covariance matrices to be diagonal (spherical case) solely for computational efficiency is a limitation. While understandable, it sacrifices the potential to model anisotropic uncertainty in the semantic space. An analysis of the performance impact of using full covariance matrices, even with computational approximations, would strengthen the argument for the current implementation choice.

The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study. While comparisons are made to existing methods, it is unclear how much each component of the proposed approach (e.g., the max-margin objective, the expected likelihood kernel) contributes to the overall performance. A more detailed analysis would clarify the relative importance of different design choices.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What criteria were used to select K=2 for the Gaussian mixture model, and how was the sensitivity of the model to different values of K investigated?
2.  Was the performance impact of using full covariance matrices (rather than diagonal matrices) explored, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with using full covariance matrices, and what approximations might be employed to mitigate this cost?
4.  How does the model handle rare words or words not seen during training? Are any specific techniques employed to address the challenges associated with limited data?
