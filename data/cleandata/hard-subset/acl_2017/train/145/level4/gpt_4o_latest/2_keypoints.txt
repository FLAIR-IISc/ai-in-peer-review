- Paper proposes Gaussian-mixture word embeddings to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel for similarity.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment show the approach is effective.
- Strength: Clear motivation and well-defined problem statement.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty—more expressive than deterministic vectors.
- Strength: Max-margin objective plus closed-form similarity enables efficient training.
- Strength: Paper is generally well written.
- Weakness/Question: Number of components k fixed at 2 — unclear selection criteria and effect of increasing k on performance.
- Weakness/Question: How do distributions behave for words with a single dominant meaning?
- Weakness/Question: Experiments use spherical covariances only — was this for computational reasons, and how would diagonal/general covariances affect performance?
- Minor: Table 4 is not cited in the text.
- Minor: Reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
