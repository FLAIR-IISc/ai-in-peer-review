- Paper is strong: well-structured, clearly written, novel, well-motivated model with claims supported by sound experiments.
- Introduces multimodal word distributions: represents each word as a mixture of Gaussians to capture multiple meanings.
- Extends Vilnis & McCallum (2014) by moving from unimodal to multimodal word representations.
- Multimodal representation effectively addresses polysemy.
- Experimental setup is sound; qualitative analysis (Table 1) is convincing and aligns with objectives.
- Main weakness: empirical comparison to other multi-prototype embedding models is insufficient.
- Recommend explicitly discussing differences with Tian et al. (2014), since both use mixture models for multiple prototypes.
- Recommend strengthening related work by citing Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).
- Suggest including results from those approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for a more comprehensive empirical comparison.
- Query for authors: explain the performance drop of w2gm compared to w2g on the SWCS dataset.
