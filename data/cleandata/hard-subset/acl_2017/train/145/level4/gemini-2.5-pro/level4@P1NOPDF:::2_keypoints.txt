- Problem is clearly motivated and defined.
- Representing words as Gaussian mixtures is more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture distinct senses, probability mass, and uncertainty; this is an important contribution.
- The paper proposes a max-margin objective with a closed-form similarity (expected likelihood kernel), enabling efficient training.
- The paper is generally well-written.
- Why was the number of components set to k=2 for all experiments; what criterion guided this choice, how does performance change as k increases, and how do two components behave for monosemous words?
- Experiments use spherical covariances only—was this for efficiency, and would diagonal covariances better capture varying uncertainty across semantic dimensions?
- Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
- The Luong et al. reference is missing its publication year.
