This paper proposes a novel method for learning word representations by modeling each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). This approach extends prior work on single Gaussian embeddings, allowing a word's distribution to have multiple modes to capture distinct meanings (polysemy), as well as richer uncertainty information. To train the GMM parameters, the authors employ an energy-based max-margin objective, using the expected likelihood kernel as a tractable, closed-form similarity measure between word distributions. The method is evaluated on standard word similarity and lexical entailment tasks, where it demonstrates improved performance over point-based and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and expressive word embedding model that represents each word as a Gaussian Mixture. This provides a principled probabilistic framework for capturing polysemy, where different mixture components can learn distinct word senses.
2.  A scalable training procedure that combines a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel. This formulation allows for efficient, end-to-end learning of the GMM parameters despite the model's complexity.
3.  An empirical demonstration that the proposed model successfully learns disentangled word meanings and outperforms strong baselines on several word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Expressive Model.** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of both deterministic and unimodal probabilistic word embeddings, particularly in their failure to adequately represent polysemous words. The proposed GMM representation is a natural and powerful extension that can model multiple distinct meanings through its modes, while also capturing the uncertainty and scope of each meaning through the component covariances. This is a significant step towards more nuanced and interpretable word representations.
2.  **Effective and Tractable Training Framework.** A key strength is the design of a practical learning algorithm for this complex model. By pairing the GMM representation with the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function, the similarity between any two-word distributions has a convenient closed-form solution. This allows the use of a standard max-margin ranking objective, making the entire framework scalable to large corpora via stochastic gradient descent.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Qualitative Analysis.** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed model. The qualitative analysis in Table 1 and the interactive visualization clearly show that the model learns to separate the distinct senses of words like "rock" and "bank." This is further supported by strong quantitative performance on a wide range of word similarity and entailment tasks, where the multimodal approach consistently outperforms its unimodal counterpart and other baselines.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation.** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, from the model definition to the training objective and practical implementation details.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Number of Mixture Components (K).** A critical hyperparameter in any mixture model is the number of components, K. The experiments are conducted almost exclusively with K=2, with a brief qualitative mention of K=3. The paper provides no principled criteria or sensitivity analysis for selecting K. It is unclear how performance changes as K increases, or if a larger K could hurt performance by overfitting on words with fewer meanings. This lack of exploration leaves open important questions about the model's robustness and general applicability.
2.  **Simplification to Spherical Covariance.** For computational efficiency, all experiments use spherical covariance matrices, where the uncertainty is isotropic. This simplification significantly constrains the model's expressiveness. A key motivation for probabilistic embeddings is to capture rich uncertainty, and a diagonal or full covariance matrix would allow a component to be narrow along certain semantic axes but broad along others. The paper does not explore this trade-off or report results with the more general diagonal case, which, despite being more computationally intensive, would be a better match for the model's stated goals.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the selection of K=2 for the experiments? How does the model's performance on key downstream tasks (e.g., WordSim-353, SCWS) change as K increases? Furthermore, for a word with only one dominant meaning, do the K components effectively collapse into a single representation, or do they diverge in a non-meaningful way?
2.  The use of spherical covariance is noted as a simplification for efficiency. Could you provide some insight, perhaps from preliminary experiments, into the performance impact of using diagonal covariance matrices? While computationally more expensive, this would allow the model to represent varying degrees of uncertainty along different semantic dimensions, which seems central to the motivation for using probabilistic embeddings.
