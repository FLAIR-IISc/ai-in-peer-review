- Proposes multimodal word embeddings by representing each word as a Gaussian mixture model to capture multiple senses, extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014).

- Paper is clear, well-structured, and the methodology is sound.

- Qualitative results (Table 1) effectively demonstrate the model captures distinct word meanings.

- Main contribution is an elegant, well-motivated solution to polysemy in word embeddings.

- Main weakness: insufficient comparisons to contemporary multi-sense embedding models.

- Requires a brief discussion clarifying differences from Tian et al. (2014)’s mixture-model approach.

- Related work should explicitly cite and discuss Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).

- Empirical evaluation should include results from those prior multi-sense models in Tables 3 and 4 for stronger contextualization.

- Question for authors: why does unimodal w2g outperform multimodal w2gm on the SWCS dataset—dataset/task mismatch versus a potential model limitation?
