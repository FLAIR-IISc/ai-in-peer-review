- Introduces Gaussian-mixture word embeddings to model polysemy, trained with an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel.

- Contributions: a probabilistic multi-prototype model (Gaussian mixtures) and empirical evidence that it disentangles senses and often outperforms unimodal and point-vector baselines.

- Strength: Clear, well-motivated, elegant extension of prior unimodal probabilistic embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum 2014).

- Strength: Strong qualitative results — nearest-neighbor examples show distinct components capturing different senses of polysemous words.

- Strength: Thorough quantitative evaluation — consistent improvements over strong baselines (word2vec, w2g) across many similarity and entailment benchmarks.

- Weakness: Related work section is incomplete, omitting several relevant multi-prototype/multi-sense embedding papers (e.g., Tian et al. 2014; Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015).

- Weakness: Empirical comparison is limited — lacks direct benchmarking against alternative multi-prototype methods on polysemy-focused datasets like SCWS and WordSim-353.

- Question to authors: Why does the multimodal w2gm underperform the unimodal w2g on SCWS, a dataset designed to test polysemy — what factors might explain this result?
