- Summary: Proposes modeling words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to capture polysemy, semantic entailment, and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin objective and an expected likelihood kernel; shows qualitative sense separation and quantitative improvements.

- Contribution 1: Introduces GMM-based word embeddings to represent multiple distinct word senses within a single probabilistic embedding.

- Contribution 2: Provides a scalable training framework using a max-margin objective plus a closed-form expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity computation between GMMs.

- Strength 1: Representation is highly expressive and well-motivated; demonstrates clear sense disentangling for polysemous words.

- Strength 2: Learning procedure is principled and scalable, avoiding costly sampling through closed-form similarity calculations.

- Strength 3: Strong empirical validation with superior results on word similarity and notable gains on entailment benchmarks.

- Weakness 1: No systematic analysis of the number of mixture components K; experiments mostly use K=2 and selection/impact of K is unclear.

- Weakness 2: Experimental covariances are simplified to spherical form, reducing expressive power; no comparison to diagonal or full covariances.

- Weakness 3: Missing results on the SCWS multi-prototype benchmark (Table 4), leaving out a key comparison to multi-prototype baselines.

- Question 1: Why was K=2 chosen, is there sensitivity analysis over K, and do unused components collapse for monosemous words?

- Question 2: What are the performance vs. computational trade-offs of using diagonal (or fuller) covariance matrices instead of spherical ones?

- Question 3: Please provide the missing SCWS results and comparisons to the multi-prototype baselines mentioned.

- Additional comment 1: Inconsistent abbreviation ("W2MG" vs "w2gm")—use a single consistent name.

- Additional comment 2: Bibliographic entry for "Luong et al." is incomplete (missing year/venue).
