This paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, employing the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach.

- Strengths:
The problem of representing polysemous words is well-motivated and clearly defined. The proposed use of Gaussian mixtures offers greater expressivity than standard deterministic vector representations. This approach can potentially capture distinct word senses through the modes of the mixture, along with the probability mass and uncertainty associated with each sense. This represents an important contribution to the field of word embeddings.
The authors propose an efficient training scheme based on a max-margin objective that leverages a closed-form similarity measure.
The paper is generally well-written.

- Weaknesses:
The main weaknesses concern the justification for key hyperparameter choices and modeling decisions, which are elaborated upon in the questions below.

- General Discussion:
The number of Gaussian components, k, is a critical hyperparameter. The experiments exclusively use k=2. What was the criterion for this choice? The authors should investigate the model's sensitivity to k and report whether performance is affected as k increases. Furthermore, a qualitative analysis would be beneficial: for instance, what does the learned distribution for a monosemous word (i.e., a word with only one dominant meaning) look like?

All experiments use spherical Gaussian components, where the covariance matrix is simplified to a single scalar. Was this choice made solely for computational efficiency? An ablation study using a diagonal covariance matrix would be highly valuable. This more general case is compelling, as it would allow the model to learn different degrees of uncertainty along different semantic axes.
