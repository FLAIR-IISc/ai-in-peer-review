Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

- **Strengths:** This is a strong, well-executed paper.

- **Weaknesses:** The paper could be strengthened by a more extensive comparison to related methods.

- **General Discussion:**
This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions. It extends the work of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words with unimodal Gaussian distributions, by representing each word as a Gaussian mixture model. This multimodal approach allows the model to capture polysemy by learning distinct representations for a word's different meanings.

Overall, the paper is well-structured and clearly written. The experimental setup is sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 provides convincing results that align with the model's goals. The paper has few weaknesses; the following comments are minor suggestions intended to further improve its clarity.

Some comments:
- It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the differences between the proposed approach and that of Tian et al. (2014). Both models use a mixture model to learn multiple prototypes for each word, so a direct comparison would be insightful.

- The related work section could be strengthened by citing other relevant work on multi-sense embeddings, such as:
    - Neelakantan et al. (EMNLP 2014), "Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space"
    - Li and Jurafsky (EMNLP 2015), "Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?"
    - Liu et al. (AAAI 2015), "Topical Word Embeddings"

- Following the point above, including experimental comparisons against these methods in Tables 3 and 4 would provide valuable context for the paper's results.

- A question for the authors: To what do you attribute the performance drop of the proposed w2gm model compared to the w2g baseline on the SWCS dataset?
