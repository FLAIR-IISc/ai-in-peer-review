- Summary: Proposes representing words as mixtures of Gaussians to model polysemy, trained with an energy-based max-margin objective using an expected likelihood kernel, and shows qualitative and quantitative gains on similarity and entailment tasks.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a multimodal probabilistic word embedding (Gaussian mixture) to capture distinct senses and uncertainty per word.

- Contribution 2: Provides empirical validation showing interpretable sense components and competitive or state-of-the-art performance against point and unimodal Gaussian baselines.

- Strength 1: Clear motivation and elegant, principled model combining mixture representations with an analytically tractable similarity kernel.

- Strength 2: Strong qualitative nearest-neighbor evidence and consistent quantitative improvements on multiple benchmarks (similarity, relatedness, entailment).

- Strength 3: Well-written paper with detailed implementation/hyperparameter notes and promised code release aiding reproducibility.

- Weakness 1: Limited experimental comparison to other multi-prototype/multi-sense methods (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014, Liu et al. 2015, Li & Jurafsky 2015), especially on SCWS.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of model choices, including sensitivity to number of mixture components K, automatic selection of K per word, and trade-offs of diagonal/spherical vs full covariances.

- Question 1: Request clarification of key modeling and objective differences from Tian et al. (2014), since both use mixture approaches.

- Question 2: Ask for clarification and intuition about SCWS results (possible w2gm drop vs w2g) and analysis of cases where unimodal models might outperform multi-prototype ones.
