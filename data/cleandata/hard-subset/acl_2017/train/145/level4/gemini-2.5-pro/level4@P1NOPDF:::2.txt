This paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures and demonstrates this method's potential for capturing polysemy. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, and the similarity between word distributions is measured with the expected likelihood kernel. Experimental results on word similarity and textual entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**
*   The problem is clearly motivated and defined.
*   Gaussian mixtures offer a more expressive representation than deterministic vectors. They have the potential to capture distinct word meanings through their modes, along with the probability mass and uncertainty associated with each sense. This work represents an important contribution to the field of word embeddings.
*   The paper proposes a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, which enables efficient training.
*   The paper is generally well-written.

**Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors:**
*   In Gaussian mixture models, the number of components (k) is a critical hyperparameter. The paper sets k=2 for all experiments. What was the criterion for selecting this value? Does model performance degrade as k increases? Furthermore, for a monosemous word (i.e., a word with a single dominant meaning), what does the learned two-component distribution typically look like?
*   I noticed that the experiments are limited to the spherical case, where the covariance matrix simplifies to a single scalar. Was this choice made purely for computational efficiency? It would be interesting to see the performance when using a more general diagonal covariance matrix. This more general case seems compelling, as it would allow the model to capture different degrees of uncertainty along different semantic dimensions.

**Minor Comments:**
*   Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
*   The reference for Luong et al. is missing its publication year.
