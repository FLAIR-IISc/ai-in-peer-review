### **Review: Multimodal Word Distributions**

#### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions by representing words as Gaussian mixtures, where each component of the mixture is intended to capture a distinct word meaning. The approach is a direct extension of the unimodal Gaussian embedding model proposed by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), and it is specifically designed to address the challenge of polysemy by allowing for multiple representations per word. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a solid experimental evaluation to support its claims.

#### **Strengths**

This is a very strong paper with several notable strengths:

1.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written. The motivation for moving from unimodal to multimodal distributions is well-argued, and the model is presented in an accessible manner.
2.  **Valuable Contribution:** The extension of Gaussian embeddings to a multimodal setting is a valuable and logical next step for this line of research. It effectively tackles the important and challenging problem of representing words with multiple senses.
3.  **Solid Experimentation:** The experimental setup is sound, and the evaluations are conducted rigorously. The qualitative analysis presented in Table 1, in particular, provides compelling and intuitive evidence for the model's ability to capture distinct word senses as expected.

#### **Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its comparison to other contemporary approaches for learning multi-sense embeddings. While the comparison to the unimodal baseline (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) is essential and well-executed, the discussion of and comparison with other related work is limited. Several other prominent models that also learn multiple embeddings per word are not included in the experimental comparison, which makes it difficult to fully assess the state-of-the-art performance of the proposed method.

#### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

The paper is of high quality, and my comments below are primarily intended to help further strengthen the work and improve its clarity.

*   **Comparison with Tian et al. (2014):** It would be beneficial to include a more explicit discussion of the differences between the proposed model and the approach of Tian et al. (2014). Since both models utilize a mixture model to create multiple prototypes for each word, clarifying the key distinctions in methodology or objective would further highlight the unique contributions of this work.

*   **Related Work and Experimental Baselines:** The related work section and experimental comparisons would be significantly strengthened by including and discussing several highly relevant papers on multi-sense embeddings. I suggest considering the following:
    *   Neelakantan, A., Shankar, J., Passos, A., & McCallum, A. (2014). *Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space*. EMNLP.
    *   Li, J., & Jurafsky, D. (2015). *Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?* EMNLP.
    *   Liu, Y., Liu, Z., Chua, T. S., & Sun, M. (2015). *Topical Word Embeddings*. AAAI.

    Including results from these, or similar, multi-sense embedding models in Tables 3 and 4 would provide a more comprehensive comparison and better contextualize the performance of your model within the field.

*   **Question on SWCS Results:** In the analysis on the SWCS dataset, the multimodal `w2gm` model underperforms the unimodal `w2g` baseline. What is your hypothesis for this performance decrease? Is it possible that the nature of the SWCS task or dataset characteristics inadvertently favors a single, averaged representation over distinct sense-specific ones?
