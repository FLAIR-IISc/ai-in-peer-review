- Proposes representing each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to capture polysemy and richer uncertainty.  
- Trains GMM embeddings with an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as a closed-form similarity.  
- Demonstrates improved performance over point and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and lexical entailment tasks.  
- Main contribution: expressive probabilistic embedding that models distinct senses via mixture components.  
- Main contribution: scalable end-to-end training combining expected likelihood kernel with a max-margin ranking loss.  
- Main contribution: empirical and qualitative evidence that the model disentangles word meanings and outperforms strong baselines.  
- Strength: well-motivated extension addressing limitations of deterministic and unimodal embeddings for polysemy.  
- Strength: tractable similarity computation (closed-form) enabling scalable SGD training despite model complexity.  
- Strength: strong quantitative results and qualitative analyses (e.g., sense separation for “rock” and “bank”).  
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation and methodological explanation.  
- Weakness: no principled justification or sensitivity analysis for the number of mixture components K (experiments mainly use K=2).  
- Weakness: experiments use spherical covariances only, limiting expressiveness and not exploring diagonal/full covariance trade-offs.  
- Question: how was K=2 chosen, how does performance vary with K, and do components collapse for single-sense words?  
- Question: any preliminary results on using diagonal covariances and their impact on performance vs. computational cost?  
- Additional: Table 4 is cited but missing; Luong et al. reference lacks a publication year.
