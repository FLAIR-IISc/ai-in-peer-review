- Proposal: Represent words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to capture polysemy, entailment, and semantic uncertainty via multiple component means, covariances, and mixture weights.  
- Training: Learn GMM parameters with a skip-gram style, energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel for efficient closed-form similarity between GMMs.  
- Qualitative/Quantitative Evidence: Shows component senses align with real-world meanings (e.g., "rock", "bank") and outperforms word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on similarity and entailment benchmarks.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces a novel, expressive multi-prototype probabilistic word representation (GMMs) extending single-vector and unimodal probabilistic embeddings.  
- Contribution 2: Provides a scalable, tractable training framework combining a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel to train GMMs on large corpora.  
- Contribution 3: Delivers strong empirical validation with interpretable qualitative examples and competitive/state-of-the-art quantitative results, notably on entailment.  
- Strength 1: Clear motivation and elegant model that naturally generalizes existing embeddings and captures sense prevalence and uncertainty.  
- Strength 2: Practical, scalable training design (expected likelihood kernel + max-margin) enabling large-vocabulary optimization.  
- Strength 3: Comprehensive, convincing experiments including qualitative visualizations and improvements across multiple tasks.  
- Weakness 1: Unclear strategy for choosing the number of components K (mostly set to 2) and lack of analysis on sensitivity or behavior for monosemous words.  
- Weakness 2: Limited exploration of covariance structure—experiments use spherical covariances, restricting anisotropic uncertainty modeling and lacking trade-off analysis with diagonal/full covariances.  
- Question 1: Please justify K=2, report experiments with different K values, and explain how extra components behave for monosemous words.  
- Question 2: Please discuss performance vs. computational trade-offs of spherical vs. diagonal/full covariances and provide any experimental comparisons.
