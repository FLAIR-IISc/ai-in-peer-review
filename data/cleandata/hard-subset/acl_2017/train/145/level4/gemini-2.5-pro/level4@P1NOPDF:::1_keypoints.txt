- Introduces multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word senses, extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014).  
- Represents each word as a set of Gaussian distributions to address polysemy.  
- Paper is strong, well-motivated, clearly written, and well-structured.  
- Experimental setup is sound and qualitative analysis (Table 1) shows compelling, consistent results.  
- Weakness: experimental comparison against similar multi-sense approaches is not comprehensive.  
- Suggest briefly explaining key theoretical differences from Tian et al. (2014), since both use mixture-based multiple prototypes.  
- Recommend strengthening related work by discussing Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).  
- Recommend including results from those multi-sense embedding models in Tables 3 and 4 for a more comprehensive empirical comparison.  
- Query for authors: explain why w2gm underperforms unimodal w2g on the SCWS task and possible reasons for this drop.
