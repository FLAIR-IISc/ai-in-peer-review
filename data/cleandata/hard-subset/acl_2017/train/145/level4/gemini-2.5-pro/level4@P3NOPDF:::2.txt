This paper introduces a novel method for learning word embeddings by representing each word as a Gaussian mixture model. The core motivation is to capture the polysemous nature of words, a limitation of traditional single-vector embeddings. The models are trained using a max-margin objective, and the similarity between two word distributions is calculated using the expected likelihood kernel. The authors evaluate their approach on standard word similarity and textual entailment tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of these probabilistic representations compared to deterministic vector embeddings.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper is well-written, with a clearly motivated and well-defined problem statement. It effectively addresses a known shortcoming of standard word embeddings—their inability to model multiple word senses.

2.  **Expressive Representation:** The primary strength of this work lies in its move beyond single, deterministic vector representations for words. By employing Gaussian mixtures, the authors propose a representation with significantly greater expressive power. This probabilistic approach can potentially capture not only the multiple meanings of a polysemous word through the modes of the mixture but also the probability mass and uncertainty associated with each sense. This represents an important and promising direction for research in word embeddings.

3.  **Efficient Training Framework:** The proposed max-margin learning objective, combined with a closed-form similarity measure (the expected likelihood kernel), provides an efficient and elegant framework for training these complex models.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

My main concerns relate to certain modeling choices and the lack of ablation studies, which would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

1.  **Choice of Gaussian Components (k):** The number of Gaussian components is a critical hyperparameter in mixture models. The experiments consistently use k=2. It would be beneficial for the authors to elaborate on the criteria for this choice. For instance, how sensitive is model performance to an increase in k? Does a larger k lead to overfitting or other performance degradation? An analysis of the learned distributions would also be insightful. For example, what does the learned mixture look like for a monosemous word—does the model successfully collapse to a single significant component, or does it learn spurious modes?

2.  **Covariance Matrix Structure:** The authors use a spherical covariance matrix in all experiments, where the matrix reduces to a single variance parameter. While this choice is understandable for computational efficiency, it limits the model's flexibility. It would be highly informative to see an analysis of performance using a more general diagonal covariance matrix. This would allow the model to capture different degrees of uncertainty along different semantic axes, which seems like a compelling advantage of the Gaussian framework and could lead to more nuanced representations. A discussion of the trade-offs between computational cost and representational power for different covariance structures would be a valuable addition.
