**Review: Multimodal Word Distributions**

**Strengths:**
This is a strong, well-motivated, and clearly written paper.

**Weaknesses:**
The experimental comparison against similar approaches could be more comprehensive.

**General Discussion:**
This paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word senses. In this framework, each word is represented by a set of Gaussian distributions. The approach extends the model of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as single, unimodal Gaussian distributions. By adopting a multimodal representation, the proposed model is able to address the problem of polysemy.

Overall, this is a very strong paper that is well-structured and clearly presented. The experimental setup is sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 shows compelling results that are consistent with the model's motivations. The paper is of high quality, and the following comments are intended as constructive suggestions to further improve its clarity and impact.

**Comments and Suggestions:**

*   The paper would benefit from a brief explanation of the key differences between the proposed model and the one by Tian et al. (2014). Since both approaches split a single word representation into multiple prototypes using a mixture model, a direct comparison of their theoretical underpinnings would be helpful for the reader.

*   The related work section could be strengthened by including and discussing the following relevant papers on multi-sense embeddings:
    *   Neelakantan, A., Shankar, J., Passos, A., & McCallum, A. (2014). Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space. In *EMNLP*.
    *   Li, J., & Jurafsky, D. (2015). Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding? In *EMNLP*.
    *   Liu, Y., Liu, Z., Chua, T. S., & Sun, M. (2015). Topical Word Embeddings. In *AAAI*.

*   Furthermore, including results from these or similar multi-sense embedding models in the comparison tables (Tables 3 and 4) would provide a more comprehensive empirical evaluation and better contextualize the performance of the proposed model.

*   A question for the authors: To what do you attribute the performance drop of the proposed w2gm model compared to the unimodal w2g on the SCWS task? What might be the reasons for this particular result?
