Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

**Strengths:**
This is a very strong paper that is well-structured, clearly written, and presents a novel, well-motivated model. The claims are supported by sound experimentation.

**Weaknesses:**
The empirical comparison to other multi-prototype embedding models could be strengthened.

**General Discussion:**
This paper introduces a new model for learning multimodal word distributions, where each word is represented by a mixture of Gaussian distributions to capture its multiple meanings. The proposed approach extends the model of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), which represented words as a single unimodal Gaussian distribution. By adopting a multimodal representation, the model effectively addresses the problem of polysemy.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper. The experimental setup is sound, and the qualitative analysis in Table 1 provides convincing results that align with the model's objectives. There is little to fault in the work; the comments below are primarily suggestions for improving the paper's clarity and completeness.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion of the differences between the proposed approach and that of Tian et al. (2014). Since both methods learn multiple prototypes for each word using a mixture model, an explicit comparison would help clarify the unique contributions of this work.

*   The related work section could be strengthened by including the following relevant papers on multi-sense embeddings:
    *   *Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space.* Neelakantan, A., Shankar. J. Passos, A., McCallum. EMNLP 2014.
    *   *Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?* Li and Jurafsky, EMNLP 2015.
    *   *Topical Word Embeddings.* Liu Y., Liu Z., Chua T., Sun M. AAAI 2015.

*   Including results from the aforementioned approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would provide a more comprehensive empirical comparison and better situate the paper's contributions.

*   A question for the authors: To what do you attribute the performance drop of the w2gm model compared to w2g on the SWCS dataset?
