- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixture models to capture multiple senses and uncertainty.  
- Trains embeddings with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as a closed-form energy function.  
- Shows empirical improvements over word2vec and single-Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Contribution: a novel multi-prototype embedding where each word is a Gaussian mixture, modeling distinct senses and uncertainty.  
- Contribution: a scalable, stable training framework combining a max-margin objective with a closed-form expected likelihood kernel.  
- Contribution: thorough qualitative and quantitative evaluation demonstrating that embeddings capture multiple meanings and outperform strong baselines.  
- Strength: clear and compelling motivation—Gaussian mixtures naturally address polysemy and uncertainty in word meaning.  
- Strength: sound, practical training method justified by the tractable expected likelihood kernel.  
- Strength: strong experimental results, with interpretable sense components for polysemous words and quantitative gains.  
- Strength: paper is well-written and easy to follow.  
- Weakness: overall strong but some model configuration and experimental details need clarification.  
- Question: justification and effect of choosing k=2 Gaussian components; how performance scales with larger k and risk of overfitting.  
- Question: behavior for words with a single dominant sense—do components become redundant or does one get negligible weight?  
- Question: rationale for using spherical covariances and whether diagonal covariances improve modeling of uncertainty across dimensions.
