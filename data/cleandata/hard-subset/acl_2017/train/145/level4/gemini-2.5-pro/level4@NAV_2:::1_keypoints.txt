Introduces multimodal word embeddings: each word is a mixture of Gaussians, trained with a max-margin energy objective using the expected likelihood kernel, evaluated on word similarity and entailment.

Contribution: novel probabilistic Gaussian-mixture model that explicitly represents polysemy and richer uncertainty versus point/unimodal embeddings.

Contribution: strong empirical validation—competitive or state-of-the-art quantitative results and qualitative evidence that components disentangle senses.

Strength: well-motivated, expressive model; expected likelihood kernel gives a tractable closed-form objective.

Strength: excellent qualitative analysis—nearest-neighbor tables and visualizations show distinct senses for words like "rock", "bank", "cell".

Strength: thorough quantitative evaluation across many benchmarks where w2gm often outperforms word2vec and unimodal w2g.

Strength: clear writing and reproducibility; methodology and training details are well-documented.

Weakness: limited comparison to other multi-prototype methods (e.g., Tian et al., Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky); few quantitative baselines from that literature.

Weakness: incomplete analysis on SCWS (a key polysemy benchmark); results and discussion are missing or insufficient.

Question: please clarify key conceptual and methodological differences from other multi-prototype embedding approaches (Tian et al., Neelakantan et al., etc.).

Question: please provide and discuss SCWS results—how does w2gm compare to w2g on this polysemy-focused task, and explain any unexpected underperformance.
