This paper proposes representing words using Gaussian mixture models to capture the polysemous nature of words by modeling their multiple meanings. Model training is performed using a max-margin objective function, and the similarity between word distributions is calculated via the expected likelihood kernel. The proposed method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on standard word similarity and textual entailment benchmarks.

- Strengths:
The paper offers a clear motivation and a well-defined problem statement. The use of Gaussian mixtures provides a more expressive representation compared to standard deterministic word vectors. This approach has the potential to capture distinct word senses through the modes of the mixture, while also modeling the probability and uncertainty associated with each sense. This research is a valuable contribution to the field of word embeddings.
The authors introduce an efficient training procedure by combining a max-margin objective with a closed-form similarity metric.
The manuscript is generally well-written and easy to follow.

- Weaknesses:
Please see the detailed comments and questions below.

- General Discussion:
The number of components (k) in a Gaussian mixture model is a critical hyperparameter. The experiments consistently use k=2. Could the authors elaborate on the criteria for this choice? Was the effect of increasing k investigated, and if so, does it lead to performance degradation? Furthermore, for a monosemous word (i.e., a word with a single dominant meaning), what form does the learned distribution typically take?

The paper appears to use a spherical covariance matrix (reducing it to a scalar variance) in all experiments. Was this choice made solely for computational efficiency? It would be interesting to see the performance when using a more general diagonal covariance matrix. This more general case is theoretically appealing as it would allow the model to capture varying degrees of uncertainty along different semantic axes.
