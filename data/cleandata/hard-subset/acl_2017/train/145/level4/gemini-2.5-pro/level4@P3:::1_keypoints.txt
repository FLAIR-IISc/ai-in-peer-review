- Models words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) so each component captures a distinct word sense (addresses polysemy).
- Trains GMM embeddings with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel, which has a closed-form and keeps training tractable.
- Shows interpretable multi-sense representations and strong quantitative results on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.
- Contribution: proposes a novel multimodal GMM word representation to explicitly model multiple meanings per word.
- Contribution: provides a tractable training framework by combining GMMs with an expected likelihood kernel and max-margin loss.
- Contribution: delivers thorough empirical and qualitative evaluation demonstrating disentangled senses and competitive performance.
- Strength: well-motivated and elegant model that aligns representation choice with a principled energy function.
- Strength: compelling qualitative nearest-neighbor examples (e.g., “rock”, “bank”) and an interactive visualization supporting interpretability.
- Strength: competitive or superior results vs. word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings across multiple datasets, including entailment.
- Weakness: limited comparison to other multi-prototype/multi-sense embedding methods (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015).
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of the number of components K (mostly K=2); lacks sensitivity analysis and downstream-task impact.
- Weakness: unclear and incomplete evaluation on the SCWS dataset; missing results table and possible surprising underperformance vs. unimodal model.
- Question: Did w2gm underperform w2g on SCWS, and if so, what explains this counterintuitive result?
- Question: What are the key technical differences between this model and the mixture-model approach of Tian et al. (2014)?
- Question: Why was K=2 chosen for most experiments; were methods explored to validate or select optimal K per word or task?
