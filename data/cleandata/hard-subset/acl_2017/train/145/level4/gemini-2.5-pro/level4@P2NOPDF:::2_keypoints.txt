- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to better capture polysemy than single-vector embeddings.
- Trains with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity measure.
- Experiments on word similarity and textual entailment demonstrate the method’s effectiveness.
- Well-motivated problem and generally clear, well-written presentation.
- Gaussian mixtures are theoretically more expressive, modeling multiple senses, probability mass, and uncertainty.
- Efficient training framework with a closed-form similarity makes the approach practical.
- Key hyperparameter k (number of mixture components) is fixed at 2 with little justification; needs analysis of performance vs k and behavior on monosemous words.
- Covariances are restricted to spherical form; lacks discussion/comparison with diagonal covariances that could model axis-wise uncertainty.
- Minor issues: Table 4 not referenced in the text; Luong et al. citation missing publication year.
