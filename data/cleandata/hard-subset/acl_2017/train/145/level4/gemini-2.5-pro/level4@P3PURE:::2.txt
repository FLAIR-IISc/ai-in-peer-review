This paper proposes representing words with Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, and the expected likelihood kernel is employed as a similarity measure between word distributions. Experimental results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**
*   The problem is clearly motivated and defined.
*   Gaussian mixtures are significantly more expressive than deterministic vector representations. They can potentially capture different word meanings through their modes, along with the probability mass and uncertainty associated with each mode. This work represents an important contribution to the field of word embeddings.
*   The paper proposes a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, which allows for efficient training.
*   The paper is generally well-written.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The choice of the number of Gaussian components (k) is not well-motivated. The paper sets k=2 for all experiments without discussing the criteria for this selection. It would be valuable to see an analysis of how performance is affected by different values of k, and whether a larger k harms performance. Furthermore, a qualitative analysis of the learned distributions for words with only one dominant meaning would be insightful.
*   The experiments are limited to spherical Gaussian components, where the covariance matrix is simplified to a single scalar. The paper should clarify if this choice was made purely for computational efficiency. An ablation study using a more general diagonal covariance matrix would strengthen the paper, as this would allow the model to capture different degrees of uncertainty along different semantic dimensions, which is a compelling aspect of this approach.

**Minor Comments:**
*   Table 4 is not referenced in the main text.
*   The reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
