### Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

**Strengths:**
This is a high-quality submission that presents a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
The experimental comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.

**General Discussion:**
This paper introduces a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, representing words as a mixture of Gaussian distributions to capture multiple word meanings. The approach extends the unimodal Gaussian model from Vilnis and McCallum (2014), with the key innovation being the use of a multimodal framework to explicitly address the challenge of polysemy.

Overall, this is a very strong paper. It is well-structured, clearly written, and the experimental methodology is sound. The qualitative analysis in Table 1 effectively demonstrates the model's capabilities, yielding results that align with the method's goals. The following comments are minor suggestions intended to further enhance the paper's clarity and completeness.

**Comments:**
*   It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion clarifying the key differences between the proposed method and the work of Tian et al. (2014), as both employ mixture models to generate multiple word prototypes.
*   The related work section could be strengthened by incorporating several highly relevant papers:
    *   *Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space* by Neelakantan, A., Shankar. J. Passos, A., McCallum (EMNLP 2014)
    *   *Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?* by Li and Jurafsky (EMNLP 2015)
    *   *Topical Word Embeddings* by Liu Y., Liu Z., Chua T., Sun M. (AAAI 2015)
*   To provide a more thorough evaluation, I would encourage the authors to include results from these related methods in the comparison Tables 3 and 4.
*   A question for the authors: Could you offer an explanation for the performance degradation of the w2gm model compared to w2g in the SWCS analysis?
