This paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures, a method designed to capture polysemy more effectively than standard single-vector embeddings. The model is trained using a max-margin objective, employing the expected likelihood kernel to measure the similarity between word distributions. The experiments, conducted on word similarity and textual entailment tasks, demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses a well-motivated and clearly defined problem: the limitation of deterministic vector representations in modeling words with multiple meanings.
*   The use of Gaussian mixtures is theoretically appealing, as they are significantly more expressive than single-vector embeddings. They can potentially model different word senses through their modes, while also capturing the probability mass and uncertainty associated with each sense. This represents an important contribution to the field of word representation.
*   The authors propose an efficient training framework based on a max-margin objective with a closed-form similarity measure, which makes the approach practical.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weaknesses of the paper relate to a lack of analysis regarding key model design choices. A more thorough discussion of the following points would substantially strengthen the work:

*   **Number of Mixture Components (k):** A critical hyperparameter in Gaussian mixture models is the number of components, `k`. The paper sets `k=2` across all experiments but provides limited justification for this choice. The paper would be improved by a discussion on the criteria for selecting `k`. For instance, how does the model's performance change as `k` increases? An analysis of whether a larger `k` leads to overfitting or performance degradation would be valuable. Furthermore, it would be insightful to see what the learned distribution looks like for a monosemous word (a word with a single dominant meaning). Does the model correctly learn to place most of the probability mass in a single component?
*   **Form of the Covariance Matrix:** The experiments exclusively use spherical covariance matrices, where the covariance for each component simplifies to a single variance parameter. While this choice is computationally efficient, the paper would benefit from a discussion of this simplification. A comparison with a model using diagonal covariance matrices would be particularly interesting. A diagonal covariance structure would allow each Gaussian component to model different degrees of uncertainty along different semantic dimensions, which is a theoretically powerful property that could lead to more nuanced representations.
