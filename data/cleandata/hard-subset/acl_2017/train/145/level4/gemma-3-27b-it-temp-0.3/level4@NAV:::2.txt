This paper introduces a novel approach to word representation using Gaussian mixtures, effectively modeling polysemy by capturing multiple word meanings. The model is trained with a max-margin objective, leveraging the expected likelihood kernel to quantify similarity between word distributions. Empirical evaluation on word similarity and textual entailment tasks demonstrates the method’s efficacy.

**Strengths:**

The research addresses a well-defined and significant problem in natural language processing. Employing Gaussian mixtures offers a substantial improvement in expressiveness compared to traditional deterministic word vectors, allowing for the representation of distinct word senses through individual mixture components, alongside associated probabilities and uncertainty estimates. This work constitutes a valuable contribution to the field of word embedding research. Furthermore, the proposed max-margin learning objective, coupled with a closed-form similarity measure, enables efficient model training. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**General Discussion:**

A crucial parameter in Gaussian mixture models is the number of components, *k*. In the presented experiments, *k* is fixed at 2. What was the rationale behind this choice? Has the impact of increasing *k* on model performance been investigated? Additionally, how does the learned distribution manifest for words possessing a single dominant meaning? 

The experiments consistently utilize a spherical covariance structure, simplifying the covariance matrix to a single scalar value. Was this decision driven solely by computational considerations? I am curious about the performance implications of employing a general diagonal covariance matrix, which would allow the Gaussian mixture to model varying degrees of uncertainty across different dimensions of the semantic space – a potentially more nuanced and informative representation.
