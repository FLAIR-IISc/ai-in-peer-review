Review: Multimodal Word Distributions

**Strengths:** This is a notably strong paper overall.

**Weaknesses:** The comparison to related work could be broadened for a more comprehensive evaluation.

**General Discussion:**
This paper presents a novel model for learning multimodal word distributions, utilizing Gaussian mixtures to represent the multiple meanings inherent in words – effectively representing a word through a collection of Gaussian distributions. The work builds upon the unimodal Gaussian distribution model proposed by Vilnis and McCallum (2014), addressing the challenge of polysemy through a multimodal approach. 

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and presents a strong methodological foundation. The experimental results are sound, and the qualitative analysis presented in Table 1 aligns with the expected outcomes of the proposed approach. The work is of high quality, and the following comments are offered to further enhance the paper’s clarity and impact.

Specific comments:

*   Consider briefly outlining the key distinctions between the approach presented in Tian et al. (2014) and the current work. Both utilize mixture models to decompose single word representations into multiple prototypes.
*   The related work section would benefit from including citations to: Neelakantan, Shankar, Passos, & McCallum (2014) – “Efficient Non-parametric Estimation of Multiple Embeddings per Word in Vector Space”; Li & Jurafsky (2015) – “Do Multi-Sense Embeddings Improve Natural Language Understanding?”; and Liu, Liu, Chua, & Sun (2015) – “Topical Word Embeddings.”
*   Including results from these related approaches in Tables 3 and 4 would provide valuable context for comparison.
*   I would appreciate the authors’ insight into the observed performance decrease of w2gm compared to w2g during the SWCS analysis.
