- Paper models words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple meanings of polysemous words.
- Training uses a max-margin objective and the expected likelihood kernel as a closed-form similarity measure.
- Experiments on word similarity and entailment show the approach is effective.
- Strength: problem is well-defined and clearly motivated.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures provide more expressive representations than deterministic vectors, capturing modes, probability mass, and uncertainty.
- Strength: The method is a significant contribution to word embeddings.
- Strength: Max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables efficient training; paper is well-written.
- Weakness/queries: Number of mixture components k is fixed at 2 — need justification and analysis of larger k’s effect.
- Weakness/queries: Suggest visualizing distributions for words with a single dominant meaning.
- Weakness/queries: All experiments use spherical covariances — ask if this is for efficiency and recommend exploring diagonal covariances for directional uncertainty.
- Minor: Table 4 is not cited in the text.
- Minor: Reference to Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
- Note: The authors’ response to the initial review has been considered.
