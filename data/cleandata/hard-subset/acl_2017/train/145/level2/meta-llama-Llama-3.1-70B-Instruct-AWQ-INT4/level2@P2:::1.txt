**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings by representing words as multimodal distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures. The authors claim that this approach can capture multiple distinct meanings of words, entailment, and rich uncertainty information. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A Gaussian mixture model for word representations, which allows for highly expressive distributions over words.
2. An energy-based max-margin objective for learning the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model, which can capture the affinity between word distributions.
3. An expected likelihood kernel energy function, which can be evaluated in a closed form given the choice of probabilistic embedding.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of word embeddings and the limitations of existing approaches.
2. The proposed Gaussian mixture model is a significant improvement over existing word embedding methods, as it can capture multiple distinct meanings of words and entailment.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks.
4. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its advantages.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The authors do not provide a thorough comparison of their approach with other multimodal word embedding methods, such as the work by Huang et al. (2012).
2. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the number of Gaussian components (K) is chosen, and how it affects the performance of the model.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their approach, which may be important for large-scale applications.
4. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the expected likelihood kernel energy function is related to other energy functions, such as the KL divergence.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you choose the number of Gaussian components (K) for a given word, and how does it affect the performance of the model?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your approach, and how it compares to other word embedding methods?
3. How does your approach handle out-of-vocabulary words, and can you provide any insights on how to improve its performance on such words?
4. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the expected likelihood kernel energy function is related to other energy functions, such as the KL divergence?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of word embeddings. However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed, such as providing a more detailed comparison with other multimodal word embedding methods and a clearer explanation of how the number of Gaussian components is chosen. Overall, the paper is a strong contribution to the field and has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in word embeddings.
