**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as multimodal distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures. This allows for the capture of multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information. The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture, using an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function. The model is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks, and is shown to outperform alternative methods.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Multimodal Word Embeddings**: The authors propose a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as multimodal distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures. This allows for the capture of multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information.
2. **Energy-Based Max-Margin Objective**: The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture, using an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function.
3. **Improved Performance on Benchmark Datasets**: The authors evaluate the model on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks, and show that it outperforms alternative methods.

**Strengths**

1. **Expressive Word Representations**: The multimodal word embeddings proposed in this paper allow for the capture of multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information, making them more expressive than traditional word embeddings.
2. **Improved Performance on Polysemous Words**: The authors show that their model performs well on polysemous words, which are often challenging for traditional word embeddings.
3. **Scalability**: The authors demonstrate that their model can be trained on large datasets, making it a scalable solution for word embedding tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Increased Computational Complexity**: The authors' model requires more computational resources than traditional word embeddings, which may be a limitation for some applications.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning**: The authors' model requires careful tuning of hyperparameters, which may be challenging for some users.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: While the authors evaluate their model on several benchmark datasets, they do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of their model's performance on a wide range of tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

2. **Can the authors provide more guidance on hyperparameter tuning for their model?**
