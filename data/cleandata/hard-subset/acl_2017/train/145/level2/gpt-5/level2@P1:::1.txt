Summary and contributions
This paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), learned with a max-margin energy-based objective using the expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as the similarity measure. This extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings by allowing multiple senses per word and encoding uncertainty via component covariances. The authors show qualitative sense separation and report quantitative gains on several word similarity benchmarks and lexical entailment.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A scalable multimodal probabilistic embedding for words using Gaussian mixtures trained with an ELK-based max-margin objective, enabling polysemy and uncertainty to be captured in a principled and tractable way.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach improves over strong baselines (skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings) on multiple word similarity datasets and lexical entailment, with interpretable qualitative sense neighborhoods.
3) Practical training details for stability and scale (diagonal/spherical covariances, initialization, subsampling, negative sampling, and an efficient formulation of ELK for mixtures).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and modeling advantage for polysemy: The move from unimodal Gaussians to mixtures directly addresses the over-diffuse variance problem and the mean-pulling issue for polysemous words. The qualitative examples (e.g., rock, bank, cell) convincingly show distinct modes aligning with different senses and are more compelling than unimodal alternatives.

- Elegant, tractable energy function: Using the expected likelihood kernel yields a closed-form expression for pairwise energies between mixtures, avoiding the intractability of KL for mixtures. The analysis of partial energies and the role of covariance and determinant terms is informative and supports the training design.

- Empirical improvements on standard benchmarks: The model delivers consistent gains on WordSim-353 (including similarity/relatedness splits), MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, and RW, and improves entailment metrics (AP and F1) compared to unimodal Gaussian embeddings. Reporting multiple similarity metrics (max cosine, ELK, min Euclidean) strengthens the evaluation.

- Scalability and implementation detail: Training on 3.5B tokens with mini-batch Adagrad and diagonal/spherical covariances demonstrates practicality. The paper provides helpful details (initialization, numerical stabilization via log-sum-exp trick for ELK, covariance positivity via log parameterization) that others can reproduce.

- Interpretability via uncertainty: The discussion of how covariances modulate sensitivity to mean differences and the observed reduction in variances for polysemous words (compared to unimodal models) align with the intended semantics of uncertainty and entailment.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior Gaussian embeddings: Extending to mixtures is a natural next step, and the training objective and sampling follow established recipes (skip-gram-like negatives, max-margin). The main technical novelty lies in combining mixtures with ELK; while appropriate, ELK for mixtures is known. Emphasizing unique theoretical insights or new inference/learning techniques would strengthen the contribution.

- Limited breadth of evaluation: The benchmarks are primarily intrinsic (similarity, entailment). There is no downstream task evaluation (e.g., NER, parsing, entailment at sentence level, QA) to demonstrate impact when used as features or initializations. Given the extra parameters and probabilistic structure, downstream gains would bolster the case for adoption.

- Comparability and significance: Word2vec baseline performance is sensitive to dimension and hyperparameters; using D=50 across models may underplay skip-gram (commonly D=300). It is unclear whether dimensionality was tuned fairly for baselines, and no statistical significance testing is reported for the often modest correlation differences. Ablations (e.g., K=1 vs K=2 vs K=3, effect of ELK vs cosine in training) are limited.

- Sense selection at evaluation time: The use of max-over-components similarity (e.g., max cosine, min KL) can pick spurious pairs and may inflate scores without reflecting robust sense disambiguation. Alternatives such as probability-weighted similarities (AveSim) are mentioned but not reported broadly. A deeper analysis of how often the “winning” component aligns with the intended sense in context would be valuable.

- Covariance behavior and regularization: While the paper argues ELK prevents variance inflation, there is potential for covariance collapse (overly small variances) for frequent words with near-duplicate senses, given the −log det term. The paper does not report variance distributions, floors/ceilings, or regularization strengths beyond an epsilon for stability. This makes it hard to assess whether uncertainty truly reflects semantics vs. optimization artifacts.

- Anonymity concern: The interactive visualization is linked via a public IP, which risks deanonymization under a double-blind policy. This should be removed or moved to the author response with a blinded proxy.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the embedding dimension? Can you report comparisons at D=100/200/300, and ensure baselines are comparably tuned? Do gains persist at higher dimensions where skip-gram is typically strongest?

- Can you provide SCWS results (the text states a comparison, but numbers are not shown here) and clarify the metrics used (MaxSim vs AveSim vs ELK) and whether context is leveraged at test time?

- What is the runtime/throughput and wall-clock training time for K=2 and K=3, and how does it scale with K (given O(K^2) pairwise component interactions)?

- Do you observe covariance collapse or explosion? Please include histograms/quantiles of learned variances across frequency bins and senses, and discuss any explicit regularization (e.g., priors, bounds).

- For entailment, how often does the asymmetric KL direction match gold directionality (w1 |= w2 vs w2 |= w1)? Can you report directionality accuracy and qualitative examples where mixtures help over unimodal Gaussians?

Additional comments
- It would help to include an ablation showing the effect of training with ELK versus cosine-based energy on the same mixture architecture.

- Consider evaluating probability-weighted component similarities (e.g., AveSim-ELK) alongside max-based metrics to assess robustness.

- Please ensure the code and hyperparameters for baselines (word2vec, unimodal Gaussian) are matched as closely as possible and report variance across multiple runs.

- For camera-ready, remove the public demo link or provide a blinded version to comply with double-blind reviewing.
