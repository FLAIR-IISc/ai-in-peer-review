Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a probabilistic, multi-prototype word embedding model in which each word is represented as a mixture of Gaussians. Training uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as a closed-form similarity between distributions, enabling scalability and stable optimization. Experiments on large corpora show qualitative sense separation and competitive or improved performance on multiple word similarity benchmarks and lexical entailment.

Contributions, as assessed here:
1) A tractable, multimodal probabilistic embedding formulation (Gaussian mixtures) trained with a max-margin objective and ELK energy, including practical stabilization techniques. This provides an analytically convenient alternative to KL-based energies for mixtures.
2) Empirical evidence that mixture embeddings better capture polysemy and yield gains on several benchmarks (e.g., WS, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW) and on lexical entailment, along with interpretable qualitative neighbors and visualizations.
3) A scalable training recipe with concrete implementation details (spherical/diagonal covariances, subsampling, negative sampling, initialization, and optimization), trained on billions of tokens.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance with solid technical grounding: Extending probabilistic embeddings from unimodal Gaussians to Gaussian mixtures is a natural, well-motivated step for representing polysemy and uncertainty. Using ELK yields closed-form energies for mixtures, preserving tractability that would be lost with KL. The derivations and intuition for the energy terms are clear and helpful.
- Expressivity and interpretability: Qualitative analyses (e.g., rock/bank/Apple/cell) convincingly show distinct mixture components aligning with distinct senses. The observation that mixtures reduce the excessive variance seen in unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemous words is intuitive and evidenced (e.g., rock variance reduction).
- Empirical performance: On multiple similarity datasets the model is competitive or better than strong baselines; notably, improvements on WS/WS-R/MEN/MC/RG/YP/RW are consistent. The entailment evaluation shows that mixture embeddings combined with an asymmetric metric (min KL at test time) outperform the unimodal Gaussian baseline across window sizes and metrics (best AP/F1).
- Practicality and scalability: Training on UKWAC+Wackypedia at large scale with CPU-based asynchronous Adagrad, with explicit choices for covariance parameterization and numerical stabilization, makes the approach reproducible and usable. The inclusion of a visualization tool is valuable.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written, with helpful figures, concrete equations, and extensive implementation details in the supplement.

Weaknesses
- Baseline comparability and fairness: Some comparisons rely on numbers reported in prior work (e.g., sg*, w2g* from Vilnis & McCallum) rather than re-training under identical data, dimensionality, and hyperparameters. Word2vec is often reported at higher dimensions than D=50 used here, which may understate its performance. Re-running skip-gram and GloVe on the same corpus/settings would strengthen claims.
- Metric selection and consistency: Results are reported using multiple scoring functions (ELK, max cosine, min Euclidean) and the best per dataset is highlighted. This can blur conclusions about which similarity function is appropriate and may introduce metric selection bias. A primary, pre-specified similarity measure (with secondary analyses) and statistical significance testing would increase rigor.
- Limited context-sensitive evaluation: The SCWS dataset is designed for sense disambiguation in context. The paper mentions SCWS and sense-aware metrics (MaxSim/AveSim), but the main text lacks a detailed table and methodology for incorporating context (e.g., how contexts determine component selection or weighting). More thorough SCWS reporting would better demonstrate polysemy handling.
- Fixed K and covariance constraints: The model mostly uses K=2 (with brief K=3 illustrations) and spherical/diagonal covariances. There is no principled model selection criterion for K, nor a mechanism to prevent component collapse/duplication beyond the training dynamics. While reasonable for scalability, discussing or testing simple selection strategies (e.g., validation-based K choice) would help.
- Entailment objective asymmetry: Training uses a symmetric ELK energy, while entailment evaluation relies on asymmetric KL at test time. This mismatch seems suboptimal for entailment; even if KL has no closed form for mixtures, approximate or bounded asymmetric objectives (e.g., Monte Carlo estimates, variational bounds) could be explored or at least discussed empirically to gauge potential gains.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can results be provided for re-trained skip-gram (and possibly GloVe) on the same corpus, dimensionality (D=50 and a higher-D setting), window size, and preprocessing for a fair comparison? How do w2gm gains look at D=200/300?
- Similarity metric: Which single similarity score should be considered the primary evaluation metric for distributional embeddings in this setting? Are the conclusions stable if one metric (e.g., ELK or MaxSim) is fixed across all datasets?
- SCWS protocol: How is context used to select or weight mixture components for SCWS? Please report detailed SCWS results (MaxSim, AveSim) and describe the context-based scoring.
- Covariance parameterization: Were experiments ultimately spherical or diagonal? The supplement mentions both; please clarify what is used in each result table, and whether diagonal offers measurable gains over spherical.
- Computational profile: What are training times, memory usage, and throughput as functions of K and D? Are there observed stability issues as K increases?
- Component specialization: Do components ever collapse or remain redundant? Are there regularizers (e.g., entropy on mixture weights, repulsion between means) that improve specialization?

Suggestions for improvement (feasible)
- Re-train and report strong baselines under matched settings; add significance testing. This is essential.
- Standardize on a primary similarity metric, with secondary analyses clearly labeled, and discuss when each metric is preferable.
- Expand SCWS results and clarify the context-aware scoring protocol.
- Report sensitivity to K and D, training time, and memory; include a brief ablation on spherical vs diagonal covariances.
- Discuss or briefly test an asymmetric training objective for entailment (even approximate), or explain practical barriers.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically useful extension of probabilistic embeddings, with clear qualitative benefits and solid quantitative results. Addressing baseline fairness and metric consistency would significantly strengthen the empirical claims.
