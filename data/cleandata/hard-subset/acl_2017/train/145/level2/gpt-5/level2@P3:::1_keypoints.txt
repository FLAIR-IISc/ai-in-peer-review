- Paper proposes a scalable probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding: words as Gaussian mixtures trained with a max-margin objective using the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK).
- Claimed contribution: tractable multimodal embedding formulation (Gaussian mixtures + ELK) with practical stabilization techniques as an analytic alternative to KL for mixtures.
- Claimed contribution: empirical evidence that mixture embeddings better capture polysemy and improve performance on multiple word similarity benchmarks and lexical entailment.
- Claimed contribution: scalable training recipe with concrete implementation details (covariance choices, subsampling, negative sampling, initialization) trained on billions of tokens.
- Strength: Conceptual and technical advance—extending unimodal to multimodal probabilistic embeddings with closed-form ELK energy preserves tractability and clarity.
- Strength: Expressivity and interpretability—qualitative analyses show mixture components align with distinct senses and reduce excessive variance for polysemous words.
- Strength: Empirical performance—competitive or improved results across multiple similarity datasets and superior entailment performance when combined with asymmetric metrics at test time.
- Strength: Practicality and scalability—large-scale training details, numerical stabilization, and a visualization tool make the approach reproducible and usable.
- Strength: Clarity—well-written paper with clear derivations, helpful figures, and extensive supplemental implementation details.
- Weakness: Baseline comparability—some baselines use reported numbers rather than re-training under identical corpus, dimensionality, and hyperparameters, potentially biasing comparisons.
- Weakness: Metric selection and consistency—multiple scoring functions are used and the best per-dataset is highlighted, risking metric selection bias and unclear primary evaluation metric.
- Weakness: Limited context-sensitive evaluation—SCWS reporting and the methodology for using context to select/weight mixture components are insufficiently detailed.
- Weakness: Fixed K and covariance constraints—mostly K=2 and spherical/diagonal covariances with no principled model selection for K or mechanisms to prevent component collapse/duplication.
- Weakness: Entailment objective asymmetry—training uses symmetric ELK while entailment evaluation uses asymmetric KL, creating a training/evaluation mismatch that could be suboptimal.
- Question: Can skip-gram and GloVe be re-trained on the same corpus, dimensionalities (D=50 and higher), and preprocessing to provide fair baseline comparisons?
- Question: Which single similarity metric should be chosen as the primary evaluation for distributional embeddings, and are conclusions stable if fixed across datasets?
- Question: How is context used for SCWS to select or weight mixture components; please report detailed SCWS results (MaxSim, AveSim) and protocol.
- Question: Were experiments spherical or diagonal covariances in each result table, and does diagonal offer measurable gains over spherical?
- Question: What are training times, memory usage, and throughput as functions of K and D, and are there stability issues as K increases?
- Question: Do components collapse or remain redundant, and would regularizers (entropy on weights, repulsive priors) improve specialization?
- Suggestion: Re-train and report strong baselines under matched settings and include statistical significance testing.
- Suggestion: Standardize on a primary similarity metric with clearly labeled secondary analyses and discuss metric preferences.
- Suggestion: Expand SCWS reporting and clarify the context-aware scoring protocol.
- Suggestion: Report sensitivity analyses for K and D, training time, memory, and ablation on spherical vs diagonal covariances.
- Suggestion: Discuss or test an asymmetric training objective for entailment (even approximate) or explain practical barriers.
- Overall: A well-motivated, technically sound, and practical extension of probabilistic embeddings with clear qualitative benefits; addressing baseline fairness and metric consistency would strengthen empirical claims.
