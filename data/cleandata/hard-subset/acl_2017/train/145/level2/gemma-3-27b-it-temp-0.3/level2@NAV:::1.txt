## Review of "Multimodal Word Distributions"

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, moving beyond point representations to model words as Gaussian mixtures. The core idea – capturing multiple word meanings and uncertainty through a mixture of Gaussians – is promising and addresses limitations of existing methods like word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings. The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for learning these distributions, demonstrating improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of Gaussian mixtures to word embeddings, coupled with a tractable learning objective. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this approach can capture polysemy and represent uncertainty more effectively than existing methods. A secondary contribution lies in the practical considerations for training, including techniques for numerical stability and initialization, enabling scalability to large corpora. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets provides strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Strengths of the paper include the clear motivation, well-defined methodology, and comprehensive experimental results. The qualitative examples, including nearest neighbor analysis and visualization, effectively illustrate the model's ability to capture distinct word meanings. The comparison to existing methods, including the original Gaussian embedding approach, is well-executed and highlights the benefits of the proposed mixture model. The inclusion of implementation details and discussion of optimization techniques enhances reproducibility.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The discussion of the choice of the expected likelihood kernel over other potential energy functions (like KL divergence) could be more detailed. While the authors mention the advantages of the chosen kernel, a more in-depth comparison would be beneficial. Additionally, the impact of the number of Gaussian components (K) on performance is somewhat limited. While K=2 is explored, a more systematic investigation of different K values and their trade-offs would strengthen the analysis. Finally, the paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational cost associated with the Gaussian mixture model compared to simpler embedding techniques.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for choosing the expected likelihood kernel over alternative energy functions, such as KL divergence?
2. How does the computational cost of training and using the Gaussian mixture embeddings scale with the number of Gaussian components (K) and the vocabulary size?
3.  The paper mentions using a separate output embedding. Could you elaborate on the motivation behind this choice and its impact on performance?
