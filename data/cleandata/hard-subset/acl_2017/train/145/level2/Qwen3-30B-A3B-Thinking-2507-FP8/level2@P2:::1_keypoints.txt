- Proposes multimodal word embeddings using Gaussian mixture models to represent multiple senses and entailment.  
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for analytic, tractable training of GMM parameters.  
- Claims empirical improvements over unimodal Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  

- Effectively separates meaningful senses for polysemous words (e.g., "rock" as stone vs. music), supported by Table 1 and visualizations.  
- Provides a theoretically grounded way to model uncertainty via mixture components and derives a closed-form expected likelihood kernel for GMMs.  
- Quantitative results show consistent improvements over Gaussian embeddings and competitive performance with word2vec on multiple datasets.  

- Incorrectly claims to be the first probabilistic model for multiple meanings; prior multi-prototype methods (Huang et al. 2012; Tian et al. 2014) exist and should be distinguished.  
- Performance gains are marginal in many cases (often 2–3 Spearman points), and the practical significance of such gains is not well justified.  
- Lacks thorough analysis of the effect of mixture component count K; K=1 and K>2 results are not adequately reported or discussed in main experiments.  
- Scalability/training-efficiency claims are unsupported by concrete training time or resource usage details for large-scale corpora.  

- Question: How does this probabilistic GMM approach differ, in learning mechanism and representation, from deterministic multi-prototype methods (Huang et al., Tian et al.)?  
- Question: Beyond word similarity and entailment, which downstream NLP tasks would most benefit from the multimodal representation given marginal gains?  
- Question: Please provide training time and resource usage for large-scale experiments (UKWAC + Wikipedia) to substantiate scalability claims.
