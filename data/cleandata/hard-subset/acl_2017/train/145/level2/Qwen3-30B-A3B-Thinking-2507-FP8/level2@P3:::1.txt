

Summary: This paper introduces a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixture models (w2gm) to represent words with multiple meanings, extending previous Gaussian embedding work. The main contributions are: (1) a Gaussian mixture model that captures multiple word senses through multiple components; (2) an energy-based max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel for training; and (3) demonstrated improvements over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and word2vec on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

Strengths: The paper provides a compelling motivation for multimodal representations to address the limitations of unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemous words. The qualitative analysis (Table 1, embedding visualization) effectively demonstrates how the model captures distinct meanings for words like "rock" and "bank," with the 0th component of "rock" associating with "stone" and the 1st component with "music." The theoretical foundation using the expected likelihood kernel is sound, with a closed-form solution for Gaussian mixtures that enables tractable training. The experimental results consistently show improvements over prior methods across multiple word similarity datasets (Table 2) and entailment tasks (Table 5), with w2gm outperforming w2g on 8 of 10 similarity datasets and demonstrating superior entailment performance. The implementation details for numerical stability (diagonal covariance, log-determinant calculations) are thorough and practical.

Weaknesses: The novelty claim requires more precise contextualization with prior work. While the paper states this is "the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings," Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) have proposed multiple deterministic embeddings for polysemy. The paper should more clearly distinguish the probabilistic nature of this approach from previous deterministic multi-prototype methods. The computational complexity analysis is limited—while the paper mentions scalability to billions of words, it lacks concrete comparisons of training time or resource requirements versus simpler alternatives like word2vec. The exploration of mixture components (K) is superficial; the paper uses K=2 as default but only briefly discusses K=3 without demonstrating its impact on performance or interpretability. The evaluation focuses narrowly on word similarity and entailment benchmarks without demonstrating improvements on broader downstream NLP tasks, limiting the evidence for the approach's practical utility.

Questions to Authors: How does the training time scale with the number of mixture components K (e.g., K=2 vs. K=3)? What is the computational overhead compared to word2vec for equivalent corpus size? The paper claims this is the first probabilistic word embedding for multiple meanings, but Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) proposed multi-prototype deterministic approaches. How does the probabilistic nature of the Gaussian mixture fundamentally differ from these methods in a way that justifies the novelty claim? The results on WordSim-353 show w2gm outperforms Huang et al. (2012), but the paper doesn't explain why their multi-prototype approach with external lexical resources (WordNet) underperforms.
