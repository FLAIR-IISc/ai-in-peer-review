

This paper introduces a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixture models (w2gm) to represent multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty. The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for training, demonstrating improvements over unimodal Gaussian embeddings (w2g) and word2vec on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The main contributions are: (1) a Gaussian mixture model for capturing multiple word meanings, addressing the limitation of unimodal distributions for polysemous words; (2) an energy-based training objective with expected likelihood kernel enabling analytic tractability while capturing uncertainty; and (3) empirical validation showing superior performance on word similarity benchmarks, particularly for polysemous words.

Strengths: The qualitative evaluation (Table 1) compellingly demonstrates how different mixture components correspond to distinct word meanings (e.g., "rock" components align with "stone" and "music"). The model consistently outperforms baselines on multiple word similarity datasets (Table 2), especially for polysemous words. The theoretical justification for using expected likelihood kernel over KL divergence is strong, as it provides analytic tractability while incorporating uncertainty.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks statistical significance testing for performance improvements, making it difficult to assess whether observed gains are meaningful. Computational complexity analysis is missing, which is crucial for practical adoption. The comparison with Huang et al. (2012) is superficial; a more thorough analysis of how w2gm differs from their multiple-prototype approach would strengthen the novelty claim.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to probabilistic word embeddings, but would benefit from statistical validation of results, more detailed computational analysis, and clearer differentiation from prior work on multiple prototypes. The visualization tool is particularly impressive for demonstrating the model's interpretability.

Questions to Authors: Could you provide statistical significance tests for the performance improvements? How does the training time and memory requirements compare to word2vec and Gaussian embeddings on the same hardware? Could you elaborate on how your approach differs from Huang et al. (2012) beyond the use of Gaussian mixtures?
