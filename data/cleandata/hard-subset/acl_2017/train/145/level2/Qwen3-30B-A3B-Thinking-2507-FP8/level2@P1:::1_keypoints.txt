- Introduces w2gm: multimodal word embeddings using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple senses, entailment, and uncertainty.  
- Main contributions: Gaussian mixture model for word senses; max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel; strong empirical validation on similarity and entailment.

- Addresses polysemy by learning separate Gaussian components for distinct senses, supported by qualitative examples (e.g., "rock" = stone vs. music).  
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple standard datasets showing w2gm outperforms baselines (word2vec, Vilnis & McCallum 2014, Huang et al. 2012).  
- Clear motivation and tractable math for the expected likelihood kernel; careful implementation details for stability and optimization.  
- Strong qualitative analysis and visualization demonstrating meaningful sense representations and entailment relationships.

- Novelty is limited since the approach extends Vilnis & McCallum (2014) and is not clearly distinguished from prior multi-prototype methods.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability (training time, memory) compared to strong baselines.  
- Insufficient exploration of the effect of number of mixture components K and no dynamic per-word K selection or analysis for words with >2 senses.  
- Omits analysis of failure cases and limitations (subtle sense distinctions, rare words, when unimodal models might be preferable).

- Why is the expected likelihood kernel superior to alternatives (e.g., KL divergence) for this task?  
- Are the improvements of w2gm over w2g statistically significant (p-values or confidence intervals)?  
- Can the authors provide detailed analysis of how polysemous-word variance changes across models and how that impacts similarity performance?
