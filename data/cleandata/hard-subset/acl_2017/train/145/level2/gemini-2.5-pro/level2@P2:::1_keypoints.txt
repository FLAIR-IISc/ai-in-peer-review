- Contribution: Proposes a novel probabilistic word embedding that represents each word as a Mixture of Gaussians (GMM) to model polysemy.  
- Contribution: Introduces a tractable learning framework combining a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function, enabling closed-form similarity between GMMs.  
- Contribution: Presents qualitative and quantitative evidence that GMM components correspond to distinct senses and that GMM embeddings outperform word2vec and single-Gaussian baselines on several benchmarks.

- Strength: Novel and significant extension of Gaussian embeddings to multimodal GMMs, with the expected likelihood kernel cleverly avoiding intractable divergences.  
- Strength: Convincing qualitative results showing interpretable nearest neighbors for different mixture components (e.g., rock, bank, Apple).  
- Strength: Thorough quantitative evaluation with consistent improvements over strong baselines on relatedness and entailment tasks.

- Weakness: Missing SCWS experiment results and apparent formatting error in Section 4.4, weakening support for claims about handling polysemy.  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of model complexity and hyperparameters—K is fixed (usually 2) for all words with little sensitivity analysis or discussion of per-word K.  
- Weakness: Vague scalability claims without rigorous time/memory complexity analysis or direct training-time comparisons to word2vec and single-Gaussian baselines.

- Question: Please provide the missing SCWS evaluation results referenced in Section 4.4.  
- Question: Have you considered or tested adapting the number of mixture components K per word, and can you provide sensitivity results for K={1,2,3,4,5}?  
- Question: Please report concrete training-time figures (hardware, wall-clock time) comparing w2gm (K=2), w2g (K=1), and standard word2vec on the same corpus.
