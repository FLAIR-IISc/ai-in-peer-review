Summary: Proposes modeling words as Gaussian Mixture Models trained with a skip-gram-like max-margin objective using the Expected Likelihood Kernel to capture multiple senses, with qualitative and quantitative gains shown.

Contribution 1: Introduces a novel, well-motivated multi-prototype probabilistic embedding framework (words as GMMs) using an analytically tractable ELK energy.

Contribution 2: Provides comprehensive empirical evidence that the multimodal embeddings disentangle senses and outperform point-based and unimodal probabilistic baselines on similarity and entailment tasks.

Strength 1: Strong motivation and expressiveness—GMMs naturally model polysemy and the qualitative results convincingly separate word senses.

Strength 2: Sound and elegant methodology—the ELK yields closed-form similarity measures, avoiding sampling and making training stable.

Strength 3: Thorough evaluation—extensive experiments show consistent improvements over w2g and word2vec, including variance reduction for polysemous words and better entailment performance.

Strength 4: Clarity and reproducibility—the paper is well-written, provides implementation details and hyperparameters, and commits to releasing code.

Weakness 1: Incomplete SCWS evaluation—Section 4.4 introduces the task and baselines but omits reporting or discussing the model's SCWS results, a critical gap.

Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of number of components K—largely uses K=2 with little exploration of sensitivity, behavior for monosemous words, or strategies to select K.

Weakness 3: Limited discussion of computational cost—lacks quantitative comparison of complexity and training time versus baselines despite GMMs being more parameter-heavy.

Question 1: Please provide the missing SCWS results for Section 4.4 to validate the model's handling of polysemy.

Question 2: How does the model behave for monosemous words when K>1 (redundant components vs. finer-grained senses), and was per-word or automatic selection of K explored?
