- Summary: Proposes representing words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel, and shows qualitative and quantitative improvements.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a novel probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding by modeling each word as a GMM to represent multiple senses and uncertainty.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a scalable training framework pairing GMMs with the expected likelihood kernel for tractable max-margin learning on large corpora.

- Contribution 3: Provides empirical validation with qualitative sense disentanglement and competitive/state-of-the-art results on word similarity and entailment tasks.

- Strength 1: Strong motivation and novelty—natural, non-trivial extension addressing polysemy beyond point and unimodal embeddings.

- Strength 2: Compelling qualitative results—component nearest neighbors clearly separate senses for examples like "rock", "bank", and "Apple".

- Strength 3: Thorough quantitative evaluation—compares to strong baselines (word2vec, single Gaussian) across many datasets, with w2gm often improving performance.

- Strength 4: Clear presentation—well-structured paper, informative figure, and helpful implementation details for reproducibility.

- Weakness 1: Incomplete polysemy evaluation—SCWS results/discussion are missing, preventing direct comparison to prior multi-prototype methods.

- Weakness 2: Limited analysis of the number of components K—experiments mainly use K=2 without sensitivity analysis or per-word K selection guidance.

- Weakness 3: Cosine similarity dominance—maximum-cosine (means-only) often outperforms distributional metrics, questioning the added value of covariances and mixture weights.

- Weakness 4: Insufficient computational-cost detail—no clear runtime or complexity comparison to baselines despite K^2 component interactions.

- Question 1: Please provide SCWS results and discussion to validate polysemy handling versus prior multi-prototype models.

- Question 2: Please explain the choice of K=2, report results for K>2, and offer guidance on selecting K or per-word component counts.

- Question 3: Please clarify the practical benefits of learning covariances and mixture weights given max-cosine’s strong performance and identify tasks where full distributions matter.
