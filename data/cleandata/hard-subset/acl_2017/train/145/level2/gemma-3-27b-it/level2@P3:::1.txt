This paper introduces a method for representing words as Gaussian mixtures, aiming to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty more effectively than single Gaussian or point embeddings. The core contribution lies in learning these mixtures via an energy-based max-margin objective, utilizing the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability. The work demonstrates improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing Limitations of Existing Methods:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing word embedding approaches, specifically the inability of single Gaussian embeddings to represent polysemy and the potential for biased distributions. The proposed Gaussian mixture model directly addresses these issues.
2. **Novel Energy-Based Objective:** The use of an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel is a solid contribution. This approach offers a principled way to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture and maintains analytic tractability, simplifying the learning process.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods like word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings on various tasks, including word similarity and entailment. The qualitative analysis, visualized in Table 1 and further supported by the interactive visualization, strengthens these findings by illustrating the model’s ability to capture distinct word senses. 
4. **Practical Considerations:** The paper addresses practical challenges such as numerical stability and initialization, presenting techniques to ensure robust and scalable training on large corpora. This attention to detail increases the applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares against relevant baselines (word2vec, Vilnis & McCallum 2014), the comparison to more recent and sophisticated methods for handling polysemy (e.g., contextualized word embeddings like BERT, ELMo) is lacking.  How do the proposed Gaussian mixtures compare in performance and computational cost to these current methods?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The influence of key hyperparameters, such as the number of Gaussian components (K), is not thoroughly investigated. The paper primarily focuses on K=2, with limited discussion of performance variations with different K values. A more comprehensive analysis of K’s impact is warranted.
3. **Complexity of the Energy Function:** While analytic tractability is mentioned as a benefit, the description of the expected likelihood kernel and its derivation (Appendix A.1) is dense and potentially difficult for readers unfamiliar with the underlying mathematical concepts. A clearer explanation, possibly with more intuitive motivations, would improve accessibility.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper would benefit from an ablation study to isolate the contribution of different components of the proposed method. For instance, what is the performance gain achieved by using the expected likelihood kernel versus other energy functions? How much does the specific regularization technique contribute to the observed improvements?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the proposed Gaussian mixture embeddings compare to contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo) in terms of performance on the evaluated tasks and computational cost?
2. What is the computational cost of training and inference with the proposed model, especially as the number of Gaussian components (K) increases?
3. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the expected likelihood kernel, highlighting its benefits in the context of capturing word semantics and uncertainty?
4. Do you have any insights into why the Euclidean distance performs better than cosine similarity on the MC and RW datasets?
5. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the margin parameter *m* in the max-margin objective?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The interactive visualization is a valuable addition and greatly enhances understanding of the learned embeddings.
*  The detailed discussion of implementation details and optimization techniques in the supplementary material is commendable.
*  The writing is generally clear and concise, but could be further improved by breaking down complex concepts into smaller, more digestible pieces.
