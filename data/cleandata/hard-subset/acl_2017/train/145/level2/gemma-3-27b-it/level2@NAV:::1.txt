## Review of "Multimodal Word Distributions"

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, moving beyond point representations to model words as Gaussian mixtures. The core idea – representing words with multiple Gaussian components to capture polysemy and uncertainty – is promising and addresses limitations of existing methods like word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings. The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for learning these distributions, demonstrating improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The primary contribution is the introduction of Gaussian mixture models for word embeddings and the associated learning framework. A significant supporting contribution is the demonstration of performance gains on standard benchmark datasets compared to baseline methods, specifically highlighting the ability to better capture multiple word meanings. The empirical evaluation, including qualitative examples of learned representations, adds further weight to the approach.

Strengths include the clear motivation for using multimodal distributions, the elegant use of the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability, and the strong empirical results. The detailed discussion of implementation considerations (optimization, numerical stability) is particularly valuable. The comparison against both word2vec and prior Gaussian embedding work provides a good context for the contributions. The inclusion of qualitative examples and a link to an interactive visualization are also helpful. 

However, the paper has weaknesses. The improvements over existing methods, while statistically significant, appear relatively modest. More extensive ablation studies are needed to fully understand the impact of different design choices (e.g., number of Gaussian components, choice of kernel). The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (BERT, etc.).  While the authors acknowledge limitations, a discussion of the computational cost of the method compared to simpler embeddings would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper doesn't fully explore the potential of the learned distributions beyond the specific tasks considered.

Questions to Authors:

1. How does the performance of the model scale with vocabulary size and corpus size?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study varying the number of Gaussian components (K) and its impact on performance and computational cost?
3. How does the model compare to more recent contextualized word embedding methods (e.g., BERT, ELMo) on the evaluated tasks?



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a well-motivated and technically sound approach.  Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty and promising results warrant acceptance. Encouraging the authors to address the questions above and conduct more thorough ablation studies would further strengthen the work.
