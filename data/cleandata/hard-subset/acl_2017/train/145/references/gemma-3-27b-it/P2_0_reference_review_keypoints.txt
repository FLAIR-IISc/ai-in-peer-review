- Proposes word embeddings as Gaussian mixtures (w2gm) to model polysemy and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin energy-based objective using the expected likelihood kernel.  
- Demonstrates scalability and practical implementation details (stability, initialization, Adagrad) for training on large corpora.  
- Empirically outperforms prior methods on word similarity and entailment, with qualitative analyses and interactive visualizations supporting findings.  

Main contributions:
- Introduces Gaussian Mixtures for more expressive, multi-prototype word representations.  
- Provides a scalable training procedure addressing GM computational challenges.  
- Delivers thorough empirical validation showing gains over baselines.

Strengths:
- Directly tackles polysemy, improving semantic representation fidelity.  
- Strong, consistent quantitative improvements, especially on entailment tasks.  
- Clear, detailed methodology aiding reproducibility.  
- Qualitative nearest-neighbor examples and visualization corroborate distinct sense learning.  
- Theoretical grounding via the expected likelihood kernel for tractable similarity computation.

Weaknesses:
- Individual components (GMs, energy-based learning) are not novel; the novelty is mainly in their combination.  
- Insufficient analysis of sensitivity to the number of components K, with focus mainly on K=2.  
- Lacks comparison to recent contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo) as stronger baselines.  
- Unclear generalization of gains to more complex entailment scenarios (e.g., sentence-level).

Questions for authors:
- What is the computational cost of w2gm vs w2g and how does it scale with K?  
- How sensitive is performance to the max-margin margin m and was it tuned systematically?  
- Have you tried full covariances (vs diagonal) and what are the performance/cost trade-offs?  
- Can you provide more examples showing subtle semantic distinctions beyond Table 1?  
- Do you expect greater benefits in languages or domains with higher polysemy?

Additional comments:
- Paper is well-written and organized.  
- Interactive visualization link is a valuable addition.  
- Supplementary material is important for reproducibility.

Overall assessment:
- Strong paper addressing a key limitation in embeddings; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
