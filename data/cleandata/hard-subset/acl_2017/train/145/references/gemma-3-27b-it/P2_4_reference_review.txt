## Review for ACL Submission

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel word embedding method, "Word to Gaussian Mixture (w2gm)", which represents each word as a mixture of Gaussian distributions instead of a single point embedding or a single Gaussian. The authors argue this allows for capturing multiple meanings, representing uncertainty, and improving performance on tasks like word similarity and entailment. They introduce an energy-based learning objective using the expected likelihood kernel for analytical tractability and demonstrate improved performance compared to existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled approach to multi-sense word embeddings:** The paper presents a mathematically sound and well-motivated method for representing words with multiple meanings using Gaussian mixtures. This is a genuine contribution, addressing a limitation of existing methods.
2. **Effective use of the Expected Likelihood Kernel:** Employing the expected likelihood kernel provides an analytically tractable way to train the model, allowing for scalability to large datasets. This is a significant practical consideration.
3. **Empirical validation demonstrating improvements:** The extensive experiments on several datasets show consistent improvements over baseline methods, supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding methods in capturing polysemy and uncertainty, providing a strong motivation for the proposed approach. The introduction effectively lays out the problem and the rationale behind using Gaussian mixtures.
2. **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The use of Gaussian mixtures, combined with the expected likelihood kernel for training, is a novel and technically sound approach. The mathematical formulation appears correct, and the explanations are clear and concise. The analytical tractability is a significant advantage.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on a variety of datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks.  The comparisons to established methods like word2vec and the Gaussian embedding from Vilnis & McCallum (2014) are well-executed. The ablation studies presented (different similarity metrics) are also valuable.
4. **Demonstrated Qualitative Benefits:** The visualization and analysis of nearest neighbors (Table 1) provides compelling qualitative evidence that the model effectively captures different word senses. The example with "rock" and "bank" is particularly convincing.
5. **Scalability:** The authors highlight the ability to train the model on billions of words, suggesting practical applicability. This is an important consideration for real-world NLP systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention some hyperparameters, there isn't a detailed analysis of how the performance is affected by different settings (e.g., number of Gaussian components, margin in the max-margin objective). A more thorough hyperparameter study would strengthen the paper.
2. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Embedding Methods:** The comparison primarily focuses on earlier methods like word2vec and the Vilnis & McCallum (2014) Gaussian embedding.  It would be beneficial to compare against more recent contextualized word embedding models (e.g., BERT, ELMo), even if a direct comparison is challenging due to the different modeling paradigms.  Acknowledging the limitations of not comparing to these models would be appropriate.
3. **Complexity of Implementation & Reproducibility:** While the authors state that code will be made available, the implementation details, particularly around numerical stability and optimization, are complex.  Concerns about reproducibility could arise if the implementation isn’t carefully documented and released.
4. **Over-reliance on Correlation Metrics:** The paper heavily relies on Spearman correlation as the primary evaluation metric. While informative, correlation doesn't fully capture the nuances of semantic relationships. Reporting additional metrics or conducting a qualitative error analysis would provide a more complete picture.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the number of Gaussian components (K)?  Did you observe diminishing returns with increasing K, and what factors influence the optimal choice of K for different datasets?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training the w2gm model compared to simpler embedding methods like word2vec, particularly in terms of memory requirements and training time?
3.  How does the choice of the negative sampling strategy affect the performance of the model?  Did you experiment with different negative sampling methods?
4. Given the recent advancements in contextualized embeddings (BERT, ELMo, etc.), how do you see your method complementing or being integrated with these models? Are there potential benefits to combining the strengths of both approaches?



**Additional Comments:**

* The supplementary material is helpful in providing implementation details.
* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
* The reference list is comprehensive and up-to-date.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated approach to word embedding. The technical soundness, comprehensive evaluation, and demonstrated qualitative benefits make a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed method. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above.
