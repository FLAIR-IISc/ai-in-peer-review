## Review for ACL Submission

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel word embedding method, representing words as Gaussian Mixtures (GMs) instead of single Gaussian distributions or point embeddings. The authors argue that GMs can better capture polysemy and uncertainty in word meaning. They present a training method based on a maximum margin energy-based ranking objective using the expected likelihood kernel, demonstrating improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled approach to modeling word polysemy with probabilistic distributions:** While the idea of representing words with distributions isn't entirely new, the use of Gaussian Mixtures provides a more flexible and theoretically sound way to capture multiple meanings compared to single Gaussians.
2. **Demonstration of empirical improvements on multiple tasks:** The paper shows consistent performance gains on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks, providing evidence that the proposed method is effective.
3. **Practical considerations for training:** The authors address potential numerical instability issues and provide details on initialization and optimization, making the method more readily applicable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding methods in representing polysemy and uncertainty. The motivation for using Gaussian mixtures is well-justified, making the work relatable and understandable.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments on a variety of datasets, including both intrinsic (word similarity) and extrinsic (entailment) tasks. The comparison with several baselines, including the previous Gaussian embedding approach, strengthens the validity of the results.
3. **Technical Soundness:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel provides a theoretically grounded way to compare distributions. The discussion of practical considerations (numerical stability, initialization) demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges involved in training such a model.
4. **Qualitative Analysis Supports Findings:** The visualization and nearest neighbor examples convincingly demonstrate the ability of the model to capture different word senses.  The example with "rock" and "bank" is particularly effective.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly state their intention to release the code and provide details about hyperparameters and training procedures, which is crucial for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Concepts:**  The use of Gaussian mixtures itself isn't a fundamentally new idea. The core contribution lies in applying this to word embeddings *with* a specific energy function and training objective. While the combination is valuable, the individual components have been explored in other contexts.
2. **Complexity of Implementation & Hyperparameter Tuning:** Gaussian mixtures introduce more parameters compared to simpler embedding models. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t fully discuss the computational cost of training and the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameter settings, particularly the number of mixture components (K). While they do explore K=2, a more systematic investigation of K might be warranted.
3. **Entailment Results Need Further Scrutiny:** While improvements on entailment are reported, the gains aren't as substantial as those on word similarity. More detailed analysis of *why* the model performs better on entailment could be beneficial (e.g., error analysis, comparison of specific examples).
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  It would be beneficial to see an ablation study isolating the contributions of different aspects of the proposed method – for example, the expected likelihood kernel versus other possible energy functions, or the maximum margin objective versus other training criteria. This would help clarify which components are most crucial for the observed performance gains.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training the model, particularly as the vocabulary size and number of Gaussian components increase?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the number of Gaussian components (K)? Did you perform a more extensive hyperparameter search on K? If so, could you share the results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered during training (e.g., numerical instability) and how the proposed solutions addressed these challenges?
4.  Regarding the entailment results, can you provide specific examples where your model outperforms the baselines and explain why this improvement occurs?  Are there cases where it still struggles?
5.  Would you be willing to share some insights on the initialization scheme and its impact on convergence and final performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the interactive visualization is a great addition and helps demonstrate the model's capabilities.
*   The inclusion of related work is comprehensive, placing the paper within the broader context of word embedding research.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution that proposes a principled and effective method for representing words with mixtures of Gaussians. The experimental results are convincing, and the authors address practical considerations for training. While the individual components aren't entirely novel, the combination and the demonstrated performance improvements make this a valuable addition to the field of word embeddings.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
