This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) for word embeddings, aiming to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty better than existing approaches like single Gaussian embeddings or point-based embeddings. The authors introduce an energy-based learning objective using the expected likelihood kernel and detail practical considerations for stable training. They demonstrate improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The primary contribution is the exploration of a GMM representation for word embeddings. While the idea of using mixtures isn't entirely novel in the embedding space, the application of the expected likelihood kernel within a maximum margin ranking framework is a reasonable contribution. A secondary contribution is the detailed engineering effort to make the training of this model scalable and stable, particularly the focus on numerical stability and initialization. The experimental evaluation, showcasing improved performance on several benchmarks, provides some support for the effectiveness of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Improved Representation of Polysemy:** The qualitative examples (Table 1 and Figure 1) convincingly demonstrate that the GMM embeddings are better at capturing multiple meanings of words compared to the single Gaussian embedding baseline. The ability to disentangle meanings like “rock” (stone vs. music) is a tangible benefit.
2. **Theoretical Soundness:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel provides a theoretically grounded approach to comparing probability distributions, offering a principled way to incorporate uncertainty into the embedding space.  The derivation in the supplementary material is a plus.
3. **Scalability & Practicality:** The authors address practical challenges in training GMMs, such as numerical stability and initialization. The reported ability to train on billions of words in days is significant and makes the approach more viable for real-world applications.
4. **Solid Empirical Results:** The results on word similarity and entailment tasks consistently show improvements over the single Gaussian embedding baseline and are competitive with existing methods. While the gains aren’t massive, they are statistically meaningful and suggest the proposed model is a worthwhile direction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:**  The core idea of using mixtures of distributions for word embeddings isn't groundbreaking. Prior work has explored similar concepts (e.g., multiple deterministic embeddings), and the paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from this existing landscape beyond the specific kernel choice. The authors should more thoroughly discuss and contrast their work with these related approaches.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly regarding the number of Gaussian components (K). The results primarily focus on K=2, and the discussion of K=3 is brief. A more thorough exploration of how K affects performance is needed.
3. **Evaluation Metric Justification:** The choice of evaluation metrics (Spearman correlation, AP, F1) feels somewhat standard without a strong justification for *why* these metrics are particularly well-suited to evaluating the nuances captured by the GMM embedding. The authors should explain how these metrics specifically measure the benefits of their approach.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study is missing. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the GMM itself versus the expected likelihood kernel versus the specific training procedure details.  Breaking down the contribution of each component would be beneficial.
5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, spacing issues around equations). Although minor, they detract from the overall professionalism of the presentation. The figure captions are too verbose and repeat information already in the text.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of your model change with different choices of K (number of Gaussian components)?  Please provide results on additional datasets with varying K values.
2.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study that isolates the contribution of the GMM structure, the expected likelihood kernel, and the training procedure improvements?
3.  Why are the chosen evaluation metrics (Spearman, AP, F1) particularly well-suited to capturing the benefits of your multimodal embeddings?
4.  You mention the computational cost of Adam. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the training time and performance between Adagrad and Adam?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please address the minor formatting issues throughout the paper.
*   Streamline the figure captions to be more concise and focus on highlighting key observations.
*   The reference list is quite long. Consider prioritizing the most relevant and impactful works.
