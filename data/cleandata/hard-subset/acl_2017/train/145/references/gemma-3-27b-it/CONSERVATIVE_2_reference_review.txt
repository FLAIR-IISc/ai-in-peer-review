This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) for word embeddings, aiming to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty more effectively than single Gaussian or point-based embeddings. The authors introduce an expected likelihood kernel-based training objective and demonstrate improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The primary contribution is the application of GMMs to word embeddings, motivated by the limitations of unimodal representations for polysemous words. A secondary contribution is the proposed training methodology using an expected likelihood kernel with practical enhancements for stability and scalability. The empirical validation on several datasets is also a notable aspect of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Motivation is sound:** The paper correctly identifies a limitation of existing word embedding methods – their inability to adequately represent polysemy and uncertainty. The intuition behind using a mixture model to address this is logical and well-motivated.
2. **Technical approach is reasonably well-developed:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel provides a theoretically grounded and analytically tractable approach to training the GMM. The authors also demonstrate awareness of potential numerical issues and address them with practical considerations like adding a small constant for stability.
3. **Empirical results demonstrate improvement:** The results on word similarity and entailment tasks suggest that the proposed method outperforms several baselines, including the original Gaussian embedding approach (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) and word2vec. While the gains aren't dramatic, they are consistent across multiple datasets.
4. **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of qualitative examples (Table 1) and visualizations (mentioned but not included in the submission) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental novelty:** While the application of GMMs to word embeddings is a reasonable idea, the core novelty is limited. GMMs are a well-established technique in machine learning, and simply applying them to this domain doesn’t represent a substantial breakthrough. The primary novelty resides in the specific training objective, but the expected likelihood kernel is also a known concept.
2. **Limited comparison to strong baselines:** The comparison to word2vec feels somewhat dated. More recent and powerful embedding methods (e.g., BERT, GloVe) should be included as baselines to better assess the contribution of the proposed approach. The lack of comparison to these methods weakens the claims of significant performance improvement.  The authors' choice to implement their own Gaussian embedding baseline also introduces a potential for bias.
3. **Insufficient analysis of mixture components:** The paper states that each Gaussian component should represent a distinct meaning, but the analysis of whether this actually happens in practice is limited. While Table 1 provides some anecdotal evidence, a more systematic evaluation of the learned components is needed, perhaps through human evaluation or automated clustering analysis. How reliably do the components separate into meaningful semantic clusters?
4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper does not adequately discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters, such as the number of mixture components (K) or the margin (m) in the max-margin objective. A more thorough hyperparameter study is necessary to demonstrate the robustness of the method.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide ablation studies showing the effect of different hyperparameters (e.g., K, m) on performance?
2.  How do the learned mixture components compare to clusters derived from context vectors, as in Huang et al. (2012)?  Have you performed any analysis to assess the semantic coherence of the learned components?
3.  Could you include a comparison to more recent and competitive word embedding methods like BERT or GloVe?
4.  The visualization website is mentioned but not included. Please provide access to it or include representative visualizations in the paper.
5. The description of the negative sampling strategy seems somewhat vague. Could you provide more detail on how negative samples are selected?
