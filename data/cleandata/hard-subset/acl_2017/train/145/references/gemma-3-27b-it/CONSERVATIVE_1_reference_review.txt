This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) for word embeddings, motivated by the limitations of single Gaussian distributions in capturing polysemy and nuanced meaning. The authors introduce an energy-based learning objective, utilizing the expected likelihood kernel for tractability, and demonstrate improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The primary contribution of this work is a technically sound implementation of a GMM for word embeddings, coupled with a practical training procedure. While the idea of using mixtures isn’t novel, the specific choice of the expected likelihood kernel for the energy function, and the attention to numerical stability, represent a solid engineering effort. A secondary contribution is a comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the benefits of the GMM approach, particularly in representing polysemous words and improving performance on specific downstream tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Rigor:** The paper presents a well-defined model with clear mathematical foundations. The use of the expected likelihood kernel is a reasonable choice given the goal of analytical tractability, and the authors address practical concerns like numerical stability. This detailed approach is commendable.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model across a range of datasets – word similarity and entailment – and compare it to several baselines, including word2vec and the previous Gaussian embedding approach. This thoroughness helps establish the effectiveness of the proposed method.
3. **Qualitative Results & Visualization:** The examples of nearest neighbors (Table 1) and the interactive visualization demonstrate the model's ability to capture distinct meanings of polysemous words. This provides compelling evidence supporting the core motivation of the work.
4. **Performance Gains:** While not overwhelming, the reported improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks, particularly when using the KL divergence metric, provide evidence that the GMM approach offers a tangible benefit over simpler models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of using a mixture model to represent word meanings is not entirely new. Previous work has explored similar concepts using different techniques. The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself in terms of conceptual innovation, primarily focusing on a refined implementation rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Over-reliance on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The performance gains appear somewhat sensitive to the choice of similarity metric (cosine similarity vs. KL divergence). This suggests the method might require significant hyperparameter tuning for optimal performance, which isn't always practical. The justification for the KL divergence performing better in the entailment task feels somewhat superficial.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper would benefit from an ablation study to determine the individual contributions of different components of the proposed method. For example, how much of the performance gain is due to the GMM itself, versus the specific energy function, or the numerical stability improvements? Without this, it's hard to isolate the key drivers of success.
4. **Computational Cost:** While the authors claim scalability, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with training and using the GMM embeddings, especially compared to simpler models like word2vec. This is a critical consideration for practical adoption.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of your model compared to word2vec, particularly as the vocabulary size and embedding dimension increase?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the number of Gaussian components (K)? Did you explore values of K significantly larger than 2, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you elaborate on the choice of the expected likelihood kernel versus other potential kernel functions (e.g., a Gaussian kernel)? What specific considerations led you to this choice?
4. Would a more extensive ablation study to dissect the contributions of different elements (GMM, energy function, stability improvements) be feasible for the author response period?
