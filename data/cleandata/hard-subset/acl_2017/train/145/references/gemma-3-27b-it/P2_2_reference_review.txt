## Review for ACL Submission

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel word embedding method that represents each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) instead of a single point embedding or a single Gaussian. The authors argue that this allows for a more expressive representation capable of capturing multiple word senses, uncertainty, and entailment relationships. They introduce an energy-based learning objective using the Expected Likelihood Kernel for efficient training, alongside techniques for numerical stability and scalability.  The model is evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating improved performance compared to existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of GMMs to Word Embeddings:** The core contribution lies in successfully applying Gaussian Mixture Models to the task of learning word embeddings, which allows for representing multiple meanings within a single word vector. This is a conceptually sound idea that addresses a known limitation of traditional embedding methods.
2. **Scalable Training via Expected Likelihood Kernel:**  The use of the Expected Likelihood Kernel within a max-margin ranking objective provides a computationally tractable way to train the GMM-based embeddings on large corpora (billions of words). This practical consideration is crucial for the method's usability.
3. **Empirical Validation of Multi-Sense Representation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the GMM embeddings can capture multiple meanings of polysemous words, as evidenced by nearest neighbor analysis and improved performance on tasks like word similarity and entailment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core Limitation of Word Embeddings:**  Traditional word embeddings struggle with polysemy, forcing a single vector to represent multiple meanings. This paper directly tackles this issue with a well-motivated approach. The qualitative examples in Table 1 and the visualization are compelling evidence of the model's ability to separate meanings.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The quantitative results consistently show performance improvements over several baseline methods on a variety of tasks. The improvements are statistically meaningful and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The comparison with Huang et al. (2012) on WordSim353 is particularly strong.
3. **Technical Soundness and Scalability:** The choice of the Expected Likelihood Kernel and the described implementation details (handling numerical stability, initialization, and optimization) showcase a strong understanding of the technical challenges involved and offer a practical solution for scaling the method to large datasets.  The speed of training is also highlighted as a benefit.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The inclusion of Figure 1 and Table 1 aids in understanding the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of GMMs and the Expected Likelihood Kernel is effective, the individual components are not entirely novel. GMMs are a well-established technique, and the Expected Likelihood Kernel has been previously used in related fields. The primary novelty lies in the application and combination, rather than groundbreaking new algorithms.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Tuning:** The paper doesn’t provide extensive discussion regarding hyperparameter tuning.  The number of Gaussian components (K=2) is justified, but further analysis of the impact of different K values, window sizes, and optimization parameters could strengthen the robustness of the findings. What is the trade-off of choosing larger K?
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include more ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed method. For example, what is the performance gain from using the Expected Likelihood Kernel versus a simpler dot product? This would help to isolate the key factors driving the improved performance.
4. **Entailment Results Could Be More Detailed:** The entailment results (Table 5) are presented, but lack a deeper analysis. It would be useful to provide more examples of correctly and incorrectly predicted entailment pairs to gain further insights into the model’s strengths and weaknesses.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding hyperparameter tuning: How sensitive is the performance to the number of Gaussian components (K)? Did you explore values significantly different from 2?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the training procedure, specifically regarding the scaling with vocabulary size and corpus size?
3.  How does the performance of your model compare to more recent and sophisticated embedding methods, such as those based on transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) on the same tasks? (Even if they are not directly comparable, a brief discussion of the limitations of your method in the context of these modern approaches would be valuable).
4.  Could you provide a few examples where your model *fails* to capture distinct meanings, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?

**Additional Comments:**

* The link to the interactive visualization website is great!
* The supplementary material seems comprehensive and well-organized.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong submission that addresses an important problem in word representation learning with a well-motivated and effective approach. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the combination and practical implementation are significant contributions.  The experimental results are convincing, and the paper is generally well-written. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
