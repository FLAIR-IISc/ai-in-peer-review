### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a novel method for learning word representations, modeling each word not as a point vector or a single probability distribution, but as a mixture of Gaussians (GMM). This approach is a direct and significant extension of recent work on unimodal Gaussian embeddings, with the explicit goal of capturing polysemy—the phenomenon of words having multiple distinct meanings. The core idea is that each component in a word's GMM can learn to represent a different sense of the word. To make this model trainable, the authors employ an energy-based max-margin objective, cleverly using the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function, which provides a closed-form, tractable similarity measure between two GMMs.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A new conceptual model for word embeddings:** The primary contribution is the bold step of representing words as multimodal probability distributions. This provides a far more expressive and theoretically appealing way to handle polysemy than existing multi-prototype vector models, as it captures not just the distinct meanings (means) but also the uncertainty and subtleties around each meaning (covariances).
2.  **A tractable learning framework for multimodal distributional embeddings:** The paper's second key contribution is demonstrating that this complex representation can be learned effectively. The specific choice of the expected likelihood kernel is a crucial technical insight that makes the training of GMMs within a skip-gram-like framework feasible and scalable.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Appeal:** This work is an excellent example of advancing the field in a meaningful new direction. The progression from point vectors (word2vec) to unimodal distributions (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) and now to multimodal distributions is a compelling and natural evolution. The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of most embedding models—their inability to handle polysemy—with an elegant, probabilistic solution. This kind of conceptually ambitious work is precisely what our community should encourage.
2.  **Extremely Compelling Qualitative Results:** The qualitative analysis in Section 4.2 and Table 1 is a powerful demonstration of the model's success. The ability to cleanly separate the "geological" and "musical" senses of "rock," or the "financial" and "geographical" senses of "bank," is striking. These examples provide strong, intuitive evidence that the model is not just fitting noise but is genuinely learning the distinct semantic clusters it was designed to capture. The provided interactive visualization is also a commendable effort in transparency and interpretability.
3.  **Sound Methodological Choices:** The formulation of the learning problem is well-thought-out. While the max-margin objective is inherited from prior work, the choice of the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function is non-trivial and critical to the paper's success. It sidesteps the intractability of using KL-divergence for mixtures and provides a principled inner product in a Hilbert space, which works well in the max-margin framework. This demonstrates strong technical command and makes the overall approach robust.
4.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** Beyond the qualitative examples, the model demonstrates strong performance on a range of quantitative tasks, including word similarity and entailment. The fact that it outperforms its unimodal predecessor (w2g), particularly on tasks sensitive to polysemy (e.g., WS-R) and entailment, further validates the benefits of the more expressive GMM representation.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The Fixed Number of Components (K):** A significant limitation of the proposed model is that the number of mixture components, K, is a fixed hyperparameter for the entire vocabulary. This is a very strong simplification, as the degree of polysemy varies dramatically across words ('rock' vs. 'poutine'). The paper focuses on K=2 and briefly mentions K=3, noting a lack of general improvement. This suggests the model may struggle with words that have only one meaning (potentially wasting parameters) or more than K meanings. A more sophisticated approach where K could be learned or adapted per-word would make the model far more powerful and convincing.
2.  **Lack of Analysis on Computational Cost:** The model is substantially more complex than its predecessors. The energy calculation between two words involves a summation over K^2 pairs of Gaussian components, and the number of parameters per word scales linearly with K. While the authors claim the method is scalable, there is no analysis of the computational overhead in terms of training time or memory relative to the w2g baseline or word2vec. This practical consideration is important for adoption.
3.  **Incomplete Comparison on SCWS:** The paper introduces the SCWS dataset in Section 4.4, which is specifically designed to evaluate multi-prototype embeddings. However, the results and the corresponding table for this experiment appear to be missing from the submitted draft. This is a critical omission, as it represents the most direct comparison against competing multi-prototype models (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014) on a task for which this model was explicitly designed.

### Questions to Authors
1.  On the topic of the fixed hyperparameter K: Have you explored how the model behaves for clearly monosemous words? For such words, does one component dominate (e.g., receiving a mixture weight close to 1.0), or do the components split the probability mass, potentially modeling noise? Could you envision a principled way to select K per word, perhaps via a non-parametric Bayesian approach or a model selection criterion during training?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the training time and computational complexity as K increases? For instance, how much longer does it take to train the w2gm model with K=2 compared to the w2g baseline (K=1) on the same corpus?
3.  Could you please provide the results for the SCWS dataset comparison mentioned in Section 4.4? This seems like a very important piece of the evaluation.
