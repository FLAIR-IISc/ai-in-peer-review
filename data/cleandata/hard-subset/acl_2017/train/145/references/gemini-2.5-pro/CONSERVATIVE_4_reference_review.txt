### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes an extension to probabilistic word embeddings by representing each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) rather than a single Gaussian distribution. The motivation is to better capture polysemy, where a single word can have multiple distinct meanings. The authors adapt the energy-based max-margin training objective from Vilnis and McCallum (2014) but replace the KL-divergence energy function with the expected likelihood kernel, which is analytically tractable for GMMs. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **Model Extension:** The main contribution is the extension of the unimodal Gaussian embedding framework (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) to a multimodal one using a GMM for each word representation. This is a conceptually straightforward yet logical step to address the known limitations of unimodal distributions in capturing polysemy.
2.  **Methodological Adaptation:** The paper successfully integrates the GMM representation into a scalable training procedure by employing the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function. This choice allows for a closed-form computation of the similarity between two GMMs, making the max-margin objective feasible.
3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The authors provide both qualitative and quantitative evidence suggesting their model (w2gm) can learn distinct word senses and shows performance improvements over single Gaussian embeddings on several standard word similarity and entailment tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper addresses a well-known and significant limitation of standard word embeddings (both point-based and unimodal probabilistic models)—their inability to explicitly model multiple word senses. The proposed GMM approach is a natural and theoretically grounded solution to this problem.
2.  **Sound Methodological Framework:** The methodological approach is sound. Building upon the established energy-based training framework and substituting the energy function with one that is compatible with the proposed GMM representation (the expected likelihood kernel) is a reasonable and well-executed technical choice. The paper also provides practical details on initialization and numerical stability, which are important for reproducibility.
3.  **Compelling Qualitative Results:** The qualitative analysis in Section 4.2 and Table 1 provides compelling evidence that the model successfully learns distinct sense-specific components for polysemous words. For example, the clear separation of the "geological" and "musical" senses of 'rock' demonstrates the model's primary capability. This is a strong argument in favor of the model's expressiveness.
4.  **Comprehensive Evaluation on Standard Tasks:** The authors conduct a broad empirical evaluation across a wide range of standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks. The results generally show an advantage for the proposed multi-prototype model over its unimodal counterpart, suggesting the added complexity is beneficial.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The primary weakness of this work is its incremental nature. The proposed model is a direct extension of the Gaussian embedding framework of Vilnis and McCallum (2014). The training objective, skip-gram context sampling, and overall max-margin setup are retained. The core innovations are the switch from a single Gaussian to a GMM and the corresponding change in the energy function. While technically sound, this does not represent a significant departure from existing work.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Relevant Baselines:** The paper's main claim is its ability to model multiple prototypes. However, the comparison to other multi-prototype embedding methods is insufficient. While Huang et al. (2012) is included in one table for one dataset, a more thorough comparison against other contemporary models that explicitly handle polysemy (e.g., Tian et al., 2014, mentioned in related work) is conspicuously absent from the main results tables. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed probabilistic approach offers a clear advantage over established deterministic multi-prototype methods.
3.  **Ambiguous Significance of Quantitative Gains:** The quantitative improvements shown in Table 2, while consistent across several datasets, are often modest. The paper does not report significance tests, making it difficult to ascertain whether the gains of 'w2gm' over the authors' own strong 'w2g' baseline are statistically meaningful or simply due to variance in training runs. Furthermore, the fact that the authors' reimplemented unimodal 'w2g' model significantly outperforms the original 'w2g*' from Vilnis and McCallum (2014) suggests that hyperparameter tuning and implementation details play a crucial role, potentially confounding the comparison and slightly weakening the claim that the GMM structure is the sole reason for the improved performance.
4.  **Clarity in Experimental Reporting:** There are several issues with the experimental reporting. Section 4.4 discusses a comparison on the SCWS dataset against multiple multi-prototype models, which would be a critical evaluation for this paper's claims. However, the corresponding results table appears to be missing; Table 5 presents entailment results instead. This omission makes it impossible to verify the claims made in that section. Additionally, the entailment evaluation in Section 4.6 uses a KL-divergence metric, which is inconsistent with the expected likelihood kernel used for training. While KL-divergence is asymmetric and suitable for entailment, the paper should justify why a metric not optimized during training is the best choice for evaluation.
5.  **Limited Analysis of Model Complexity:** The paper sets the number of components K=2 for most experiments and briefly mentions that K=3 offers no general performance difference. This justification is anecdotal. A more rigorous analysis of model performance and computational cost as a function of K would strengthen the paper. It remains unclear how to choose K for a given vocabulary or task, and what trade-offs exist between model expressiveness and the risk of overfitting or increased training time.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Section 4.4 discusses the SCWS dataset and compares the proposed method with models from Huang et al. (2012), Tian et al. (2014), and Chen et al. (2014). Could you please provide the results table for this comparison? This seems to be one of the most relevant evaluations for your core claim of superior polysemy modeling.
2.  Could you provide a more direct and extensive quantitative comparison against other established multi-prototype embedding methods, such as that of Tian et al. (2014), across the main word similarity tasks presented in Table 2?
3.  For the word entailment task (Section 4.6), you use KL divergence as the similarity metric for evaluation, whereas the model was trained using the expected likelihood kernel. Could you elaborate on the motivation for this choice and perhaps show results using the training metric (expected likelihood) for comparison?
4.  The performance of your reimplemented unimodal `w2g` is substantially better than the original `w2g*`. Could you clarify which factors (e.g., hyperparameters, optimizer, initialization) contributed most to this improvement? This is important for understanding how much of the final `w2gm` performance is due to the GMM structure versus improved training methodology.
