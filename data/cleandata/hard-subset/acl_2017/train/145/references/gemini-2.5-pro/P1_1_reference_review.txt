### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a new method for learning word embeddings, representing each word not as a single point vector, but as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The central motivation is to explicitly model polysemy, where each Gaussian component in the mixture can capture a distinct meaning of a word. This work extends the idea of representing words as single Gaussian distributions (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) to a multimodal case. To train the model, the authors employ a max-margin ranking objective inspired by skip-gram, using the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function to measure the similarity between two GMMs. This choice of kernel is critical as it has a closed-form solution for GMMs, making the model tractable to train. The paper demonstrates through qualitative and quantitative experiments that the resulting embeddings can successfully disentangle word senses and achieve strong performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **The proposed model:** The primary contribution is the novel formulation of word embeddings as Gaussian Mixture Models combined with a tractable training objective using the expected likelihood kernel. This is a well-motivated and elegant extension of prior work on probabilistic embeddings that directly addresses the key limitation of unimodality.
2.  **Strong empirical validation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation that includes compelling qualitative examples (e.g., nearest neighbors for components of 'rock' and 'bank') and quantitative results across a wide range of word similarity and entailment benchmarks. The consistent improvements over the unimodal Gaussian baseline (w2g) effectively demonstrate the value of the multimodal approach.
3.  **Detailed practical implementation:** The authors provide significant detail on the practical aspects of training, such as the use of diagonal covariances, numerical stability tricks, and hyperparameter choices. This attention to detail, along with the promise of releasing code and an interactive visualization, makes the work highly reproducible and a valuable contribution to the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Elegant Formulation:** The paper addresses a well-understood and important problem in word representation: polysemy. The progression from point embeddings to single Gaussians and finally to Gaussian mixtures is logical and easy to follow. The proposed GMM representation is a natural fit for the problem, and the choice of the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function is a key technical insight that makes the approach computationally feasible.
2.  **Compelling Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative results in Section 4.2 and Table 1 are a major strength. The examples of nearest neighbors for different components of words like 'rock', 'bank', 'Apple', and 'cell' clearly and intuitively demonstrate that the model is successfully learning to disentangle distinct word senses. This provides strong evidence that the model is behaving as intended. The inclusion of an interactive visualization website is an excellent addition that further enhances the interpretability and impact of the work.
3.  **Comprehensive Quantitative Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on a wide array of standard datasets, covering both general word similarity (Table 2) and the more specific task of lexical entailment (Table 5). The model (w2gm) shows strong performance, often outperforming both word2vec and its direct predecessor, the single Gaussian model (w2g). The analysis is strengthened by the inclusion of their own w2g implementation, which helps isolate the benefits of the multi-prototype approach from other implementation differences.
4.  **Good Analysis of Model Properties:** Beyond standard benchmarks, the paper investigates specific properties of the model. For example, Section 4.5 confirms the hypothesis that moving to a GMM reduces the artificially large variance required by single Gaussian models for polysemous words. Furthermore, the use of an asymmetric measure (KL divergence) for the asymmetric task of entailment is well-reasoned and leads to improved results, demonstrating a thoughtful approach to evaluation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Comparison on a Key Task:** Section 4.4 introduces the SCWS dataset, which is specifically designed to evaluate multi-prototype embeddings on polysemous words. The text sets up a comparison with several other multi-prototype models (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014) but then fails to present any results (Table 4 appears to be missing). This is a significant omission, as this comparison is crucial for positioning the proposed model against its most direct competitors and validating its central claim of superior polysemy handling.
2.  **Lack of Analysis on the Number of Components (K):** The number of mixture components, K, is a critical hyperparameter, yet its selection is not discussed. The experiments primarily use K=2 and only briefly mention K=3 for a single example ('cell'). The paper would be substantially stronger with an analysis of how performance varies with K. Is K fixed for all words in the vocabulary? If so, this is a limitation, as some words are more polysemous than others. A discussion of potential methods for choosing or learning K per word would be valuable.
3.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Training Objective Metric:** In the word similarity results (Table 2), the maximum cosine similarity (`mc`) between component means almost always outperforms the expected likelihood kernel (`el`), which is the similarity measure used in the training objective. This raises the question of the role of the learned covariances in these tasks. If the uncertainty information they encode does not help in similarity evaluation, is their primary role simply as a regularizer during training? The paper would benefit from a discussion of this discrepancy.
4.  **Scalability Claims Could Be Better Substantiated:** The paper claims the method is "scalable," but the training time of "days" on a 12-core CPU suggests it is significantly more computationally expensive than methods like word2vec or GloVe. While the choice of Adagrad over Adam for speed is justified, a more direct comparison of training times or computational complexity against these standard baselines would provide a clearer picture of the practical trade-offs involved.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The results and discussion for the SCWS dataset, as mentioned in Section 4.4, appear to be missing from the paper. Could you please provide these results and elaborate on how your model compares to other multi-prototype embeddings on this task, which is specifically designed to test for polysemy?
2.  Could you elaborate on the selection of the number of mixture components, K? Was K=2 chosen based on a validation set, or for simplicity? Have you explored the model's sensitivity to K, and have you considered methods for adapting K for different words based on their degree of polysemy?
3.  On the word similarity tasks, why do you think the maximum cosine similarity metric consistently outperforms the expected likelihood kernel, given that the latter is more closely related to your training objective and incorporates the learned uncertainty? Does this suggest the learned covariances are more beneficial for tasks like entailment than for similarity?
