**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes an extension to probabilistic word embeddings by representing each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) rather than a single Gaussian distribution or a point vector. The primary motivation is to better capture polysemy, where a single word can have multiple distinct meanings. The authors introduce a training procedure based on a max-margin objective, similar to that of Vilnis and McCallum (2014), but adapt it for GMMs by using the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK) as a tractable energy function measuring the similarity between two distributions. The model is evaluated on qualitative sense-discovery tasks, standard word similarity benchmarks, and a word entailment task, where it is shown to offer improvements over a single-Gaussian baseline.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  A method for learning multi-prototype word embeddings where each prototype is a full Gaussian distribution, extending the single-Gaussian framework of Vilnis and McCallum (2014).
2.  The application of the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK) as a closed-form, analytically tractable objective function for learning these GMM-based embeddings.
3.  Empirical results, including compelling qualitative examples, that demonstrate the model's ability to disentangle word senses and show modest quantitative gains over a single-Gaussian baseline on several benchmarks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Sound and well-motivated approach.** The paper addresses a well-known limitation of standard word embeddings—the inability to model polysemy. Extending the single-Gaussian model to a mixture model is a logical and principled next step. The qualitative results presented in Table 1 are convincing and provide strong intuitive support for the model's effectiveness in separating the distinct meanings of words like 'rock' and 'bank'.

2.  **Methodologically sound choice of energy function.** The use of the Expected Likelihood Kernel is a key technical contribution. While KL divergence is a natural choice for comparing distributions, it lacks a closed form for GMMs. The authors' choice of the ELK cleverly sidesteps this issue, allowing for an exact and efficient gradient computation without resorting to approximations. This makes the proposed training scheme elegant and robust.

3.  **Reasonable experimental setup.** The authors conduct a variety of experiments to validate their model, including both qualitative and quantitative analyses. Comparing their GMM model ("w2gm") directly against their own re-implementation of a single-Gaussian model ("w2g") is good practice, as it helps isolate the benefits of using a mixture versus a single component. The performance gains on several word similarity and entailment tasks over this strong baseline suggest the multi-prototype approach has merit.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient comparison to established baselines.** The primary quantitative comparison in Table 2 is against the authors' own single-Gaussian implementation and word2vec numbers cited from a previous paper. This is not a sufficient validation of a new representation method. A thorough evaluation must include direct comparisons against other well-established multi-prototype methods (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014) across a range of relevant tasks, particularly polysemy-focused ones like the SCWS benchmark (which is mentioned but for which results are not shown). Furthermore, high-performing and widely adopted point embeddings like GloVe and FastText should be included as baselines to gauge the practical advantage of this more complex model. Without these comparisons, the claims of superiority are not well-supported.

2.  **The utility of the full probabilistic model is questionable.** In the word similarity experiments (Table 2), the best performance is often achieved using the "maximum cosine similarity" metric, which considers only the mean vectors of the components (`µ_w,i`). This metric completely discards the learned covariance matrices and mixture weights, which are central components of the proposed representation. If a simple cosine similarity between the component means is the most effective way to use these embeddings, it calls into question the value of learning the full, complex GMM structure. The experiments fail to convincingly demonstrate that the probabilistic aspects (covariance) of the model provide a tangible benefit on standard tasks over a simpler multi-vector approach.

3.  **Lack of hyperparameter analysis and model ablation.** The model's complexity is significantly higher than that of standard embeddings, with K times the number of mean vectors plus covariance and weight parameters. The paper arbitrarily sets K=2 for most experiments with only a brief qualitative mention of K=3. There is no analysis of how performance changes as K varies, nor is there a discussion on how to select an appropriate K for a given vocabulary or task. This is a critical omission. A sensitivity analysis of the model's performance with respect to the number of components is necessary to understand the trade-offs between complexity and representational power.

4.  **Claims of scalability are not substantiated.** The paper claims the learning procedure is "scalable," but this is not demonstrated with evidence. Training a GMM for every word in a large vocabulary is computationally expensive compared to standard methods. A concrete comparison of training time and memory requirements against baselines like word2vec or GloVe on the same hardware and dataset is needed to support claims of scalability. Stating that training takes "days" is too vague to be meaningful.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The evaluation of multi-prototype embeddings would be greatly strengthened by results on the SCWS dataset. Could you provide these results and compare them against other prominent multi-prototype methods, such as those by Tian et al. (2014) and Huang et al. (2012)? Additionally, can you justify the omission of strong point-embedding baselines like GloVe and FastText from your main word similarity evaluation in Table 2?

2.  The "maximum cosine similarity" metric, which often yields the best results, ignores the learned covariances and mixture weights. Does this imply that the primary benefit of your model comes simply from learning multiple mean vectors per word, rather than from the full probabilistic representation? Have you considered comparing against a simpler non-probabilistic multi-prototype baseline to isolate this effect?

3.  Could you provide an analysis of how model performance on a key benchmark (e.g., WS-R or SimLex-999) varies with the number of components, K? This would provide valuable insight into the model's sensitivity and the cost-benefit trade-off of increasing its complexity. How does one choose an optimal K in practice?
