### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper proposes a probabilistic word embedding model that represents each word as a Mixture of Gaussians (GMM), where each component is intended to capture a distinct word sense. This approach, termed Word to Gaussian Mixture (w2gm), extends prior work on unimodal Gaussian embeddings, which struggle to model polysemous words. The authors pair the GMM representation with an energy-based max-margin training objective, critically using the expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as the energy function. The ELK, which measures the inner product between two probability distributions, has a convenient closed-form solution for GMMs, making the model tractable to train on a large scale. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that the learned components successfully disentangle different word meanings and that the model outperforms strong baselines on a suite of word similarity and entailment tasks.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A tractable and effective method for learning multi-prototype probabilistic word embeddings.** The primary contribution is the novel combination of representing words as GMMs and using the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity function within a max-margin framework. This pairing elegantly solves the problem of measuring similarity between multimodal distributions in a computationally efficient manner, which was a key obstacle for extending probabilistic embeddings to handle polysemy.
2.  **Comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating the model's ability to capture polysemy.** The paper provides compelling qualitative evidence (e.g., nearest neighbor analysis for individual components of words like 'rock' and 'bank') that the model successfully learns distinct, interpretable word senses. This is supported by strong quantitative results, where the proposed model outperforms both deterministic (word2vec) and unimodal probabilistic baselines across numerous standard benchmarks.
3.  **A valuable analysis of similarity metrics for GMM embeddings.** The authors systematically evaluate three different similarity measures for their multi-prototype representations (maximum cosine similarity, minimum Euclidean distance, and the ELK). This analysis provides useful insights into which metrics are best suited for different downstream tasks and is a helpful contribution for future research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model design.** The paper clearly articulates a significant limitation of prior embedding methods—their inability to handle polysemy—and proposes a natural and expressive solution. The use of GMMs is a logical step forward from single Gaussian embeddings. The choice of the expected likelihood kernel is a clever technical contribution that ensures the model remains analytically tractable, avoiding the need for approximation methods that would be required for other divergences (e.g., KL). This results in a principled and robust learning procedure.
2.  **Strong and convincing qualitative results.** The qualitative evaluation in Section 4.2 is a major strength. The nearest neighbor examples in Table 1 effectively showcase the model's ability to disentangle the senses of polysemous words. For instance, distinguishing the 'geological' and 'musical' senses of "rock" provides clear, intuitive evidence that the model is working as intended. The inclusion of an interactive visualization is a commendable step towards transparency and allows for deeper exploration of the learned representations.
3.  **Solid quantitative improvements on a wide range of benchmarks.** The proposed `w2gm` model is rigorously evaluated on 11 word similarity datasets and an entailment task. It shows consistent and often significant improvements over the unimodal Gaussian baseline (`w2g`) and word2vec. The gains on relatedness-focused datasets (e.g., WS-R) and the entailment task are particularly noteworthy, suggesting that the model's richer, multimodal representation of uncertainty and meaning leads to tangible performance benefits.
4.  **High clarity and potential for reproducibility.** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain their methodology, from the high-level objective to the low-level implementation details. The supplementary material provides a thorough description of hyperparameters, initialization strategies, and numerical stability tricks, which are crucial for reproducing this work. The commitment to release the code further strengthens the paper's contribution to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The choice of the number of mixture components (K) is not well-justified.** The paper primarily uses a fixed K=2 for all words, with a brief mention of K=3 for one example ('cell'). This is a critical hyperparameter, yet there is no systematic analysis of its impact or a principled method for its selection. A fixed K for all words seems suboptimal, as some words are not polysemous while others may have more than two distinct meanings. The paper would be much stronger if it included an ablation study on the effect of K on downstream task performance, and a discussion on potential strategies for choosing K (e.g., as a function of word frequency or using non-parametric approaches). The assertion that K=3 offers no general performance benefit is presented without empirical evidence.
2.  **Comparison to other multi-prototype embeddings could be more comprehensive.** While the paper compares against the unimodal Gaussian model and Huang et al. (2012), the field of multi-prototype embeddings has evolved. A discussion contextualizing this work with respect to more contemporary approaches would be beneficial. For example, contextualized embedding models (e.g., ELMo, BERT), though different in their dynamic nature, also address polysemy. A brief discussion of the relative advantages of a static, interpretable multi-prototype model like w2gm versus dynamic, context-dependent models would strengthen the paper's positioning.
3.  **Analysis of the learned mixture weights and covariances is underdeveloped.** The model learns a mean, covariance, and mixture weight for each component, but the experimental analysis focuses almost exclusively on the mean vectors. The rich information contained in the mixture weights (`p_w,i`) and covariances (`Σ_w,i`) is largely unexplored. For example, do the learned mixture weights correlate with the real-world frequency of different word senses? Do the covariance matrices reveal interesting properties about the semantic space (e.g., do abstract concepts have higher variance)? A deeper analysis of these learned parameters would provide stronger evidence for the benefits of a full probabilistic representation and is a missed opportunity to showcase the model's full descriptive power.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The number of mixture components, K, appears to be a fixed hyperparameter for the entire vocabulary. Could you elaborate on how K=2 was chosen? How sensitive are the results on the main word similarity tasks to this choice? Furthermore, does forcing a fixed K for all words potentially harm the representations of monosemous words by unnecessarily splitting their probability mass?
2.  In Section 4.4, you discuss evaluating on the SCWS dataset and compare your model to several other multi-prototype methods (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014). However, the corresponding results table appears to be missing from the submitted paper. Could you please provide these results in your author response and clarify the experimental setup for that comparison?
3.  Could you provide more analysis on the learned mixture weights (`p_w,i`)? For a polysemous word like 'bank', do the learned weights for the "financial" versus "river" components reflect their relative frequencies in the training corpus? This would be a compelling validation of the model's ability to capture sense distributions.
