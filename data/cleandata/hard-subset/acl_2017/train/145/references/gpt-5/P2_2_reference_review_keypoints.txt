- Proposal: represent words as Gaussian mixture models (probabilistic multi-prototype embeddings) to model polysemy, uncertainty, and entailment more expressively than unimodal Gaussians.
- Method: introduce an ELK (expected likelihood kernel) energy with closed form for Gaussian mixtures, optimized via a max-margin ranking loss with negative sampling.
- Implementation: provide practical training details (covariance choices, log-sum-exp stabilization, initialization, subsampling, negative sampling, Adagrad) and report qualitative sense separation and quantitative gains on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Main contribution 1: a scalable, tractable formulation of probabilistic multi-sense embeddings (GMM + ELK) trained with a skip-gram–style objective.
- Main contribution 2: empirical evidence that multimodal GMMs outperform unimodal Gaussians and sometimes word2vec on multiple benchmarks, and disentangle senses qualitatively.
- Main contribution 3: a practical training recipe for stability and efficiency on large corpora.
- Strength: ELK provides a mathematically clean, closed-form energy for mixtures and includes a regularizer against variance inflation; derivation and motivation are correct.
- Strength: effectively addresses unimodal Gaussian limitations by separating senses, supported by nearest-neighbor analyses and variance measurements.
- Strength: shows quantitative improvements across several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment (with asymmetric KL aiding entailment).
- Strength: includes many reproducibility details and demonstrates practical scalability (training on billions of tokens feasible).
- Strength: conceptually significant as a natural extension from point and unimodal Gaussian embeddings toward probabilistic multi-sense representations.
- Weakness: limited baseline coverage — omits several strong multi-prototype and recent multi-sense baselines, risking unfair comparisons and apples-to-oranges numbers.
- Weakness: incomplete/unclear evaluations — SCWS results missing and the KL scoring for mixtures is not clearly defined (min-pair KL vs mixture KL).
- Weakness: limited ablations and sensitivity analyses — mostly K=2, D=50 with no systematic study of K, dimensionality, covariance type, or training-vs-evaluation metric mismatches.
- Weakness: clarity/presentation issues — inconsistent notation/terminology, malformed references/tables, and confusing entailment table placement.
- Weakness: limited evidence of downstream/task-specific benefits despite higher training cost relative to point embeddings.
- Question: precisely how is KL computed for mixtures (min component-pair KL or approximation)? formalize and justify the choice.
- Question: provide SCWS results (MaxSim/AveSim) and comparisons to multi-sense baselines trained under comparable settings.
- Question: report sensitivity to K and D (e.g., K=1,2,3; D=50,100,200), training time/memory, and stats on component collapse or mixture weight entropy.
- Question: how do covariance choices (spherical vs diagonal) affect performance and interpretability?
- Question: have you compared to more recent/nonparametric multi-sense methods; if not, position expected gains relative to those.
- Overall: originality moderate, method generally sound but KL clarification needed, clarity hampered by presentation issues, significance promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and downstream results.
- Recommendation: weak accept conditional on clarifying entailment KL, adding SCWS results, and expanding baselines/ablations.
