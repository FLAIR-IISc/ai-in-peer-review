Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes representing each word as a Gaussian mixture (w2gm), extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) to capture polysemy and uncertainty. Training uses a skip-gram-style negative sampling with a max-margin ranking loss and an energy function based on the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel), which remains closed-form for Gaussian mixtures.
- The authors claim: (1) improved handling of polysemy via multiple modes; (2) an analytically tractable similarity (energy) for mixtures enabling scalable training; (3) empirical gains on several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment; and qualitative interpretability.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A straightforward extension of Gaussian word embeddings to Gaussian mixtures with an L2 inner-product energy for analytic tractability.
2) An empirical study suggesting modest improvements on several similarity datasets and entailment, and qualitative demonstrations of sense separation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated extension: The move from a single Gaussian to a Gaussian mixture is a natural remedy for the unimodality limitation in Vilnis & McCallum. The choice of the expected likelihood kernel for mixtures is technically sensible (closed-form, simple gradients), and the derivations are correct and clear.
- Qualitative sense separation: The nearest-neighbor analyses for rock/bank/cell/Apple are compelling and show that different mixture components align with distinct senses. This addresses an oft-cited weakness of unimodal embeddings.
- Practical training details: The paper carefully discusses stability (log-sum-exp, epsilon in variances), parameterization (log-variances, softmax weights), and optimization choices. This level of detail is useful for reproducibility and indicates engineering care.
- Entailment evaluation: Using asymmetric KL for entailment is appropriate, and w2gm’s improvements over unimodal w2g on AP/F1 are consistent with the intuition that mixtures avoid variance inflation for polysemous words.
- Scalability claim: Although not fully quantified, the design (diagonal/spherical covariances, closed-form energy) is plausibly scalable. The O(K^2) pairwise mixture interaction with K=2 is practical.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond a direct extension: Modeling words with Gaussian mixtures and using probability product kernels are established ideas. The training objective mirrors Vilnis & McCallum (max-margin, negative sampling). The methodological increment (from 1 Gaussian to K Gaussians with EPK energy) is relatively modest, and related multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014) make this feel incremental. The claim of “first probabilistic multi-prototype” needs stronger positioning vs. contemporaneous probabilistic/sense-aware work.
- Baseline fairness and completeness: Table 2 mixes numbers from prior work (sg*, w2g*) with the authors’ new runs (w2g, w2gm) trained on different corpora and settings. There is no strong, controlled comparison to word2vec/GloVe trained on the same corpus, same dimension, and same preprocessing. Important baselines are missing or underdeveloped:
  - Multi-sense skip-gram variants (e.g., Neelakantan et al.) are not reported.
  - Strong point baselines (SGNS/GloVe) at comparable dimensions (D=50) on the same data are absent.
  - A multi-prototype point baseline with the same training objective (no covariances) is not provided, making it hard to disentangle gains due to “mixture” vs. “probabilistic” aspects.
- Metric selection/cherry-picking risk: Results are reported using three different similarity measures (expected likelihood, max cosine, min Euclidean), and the best per-dataset is highlighted. This risks ex post selection. A single primary metric decided a priori (or selection validated on a dev set) would be more convincing.
- Evaluation gaps for sense-specific benchmarks: For SCWS and other sense-sensitive datasets, the paper references multiple metrics (MaxSim/AveSim) but does not provide clear numerical results in the main text. A context-aware disambiguation evaluation (e.g., lexical substitution) would better test the model’s sense representations. Current evidence is largely qualitative.
- Statistical rigor and analysis: No significance testing is reported. Improvements on some datasets are modest, and SimLex (a stricter similarity set) degrades. There is limited analysis of variance calibration (besides a single rock example), component usage (weight distributions, collapse), or sensitivity to K. The claim that K=3 offers little benefit is unquantified. The scalability claim lacks concrete throughput and wall-clock comparisons to baselines.

Questions to authors
- Baseline parity: Can you provide results for word2vec and GloVe trained on the same UKWAC+Wackypedia corpus with D=50 and the same preprocessing? Also include a multi-sense skip-gram (e.g., Neelakantan et al.) baseline under the same conditions.
- Ablations: (a) Compare w2gm against a multi-prototype point-embedding model trained with the same objective (no covariances) to isolate the effect of probabilistic modeling. (b) Fix a single primary similarity metric (e.g., expected likelihood or cosine) selected on a dev set and report that consistently on test.
- SCWS and context-sensitive evaluation: Please provide full SCWS results with MaxSim/AveSim and, if possible, a lexical substitution or word sense disambiguation task to validate sense utility in context.
- Component behavior: Do components collapse or duplicate? Provide statistics on mixture weights (entropy), inter-component distances, and variance distributions across the vocabulary. How often do both components of a word get used for nearest neighbors?
- Sensitivity to K and D: Report performance for K ∈ {1,2,3} and D ∈ {50,100,200,300}, with compute trade-offs. Does K=3 help on words known to have ≥3 senses? Any impact on entailment?
- Computational cost: Provide wall-clock times, tokens/sec, and memory usage compared to SGNS and Vilnis & McCallum on the same hardware.

Overall recommendation
While the paper is clearly written and the approach is sensible, the methodological novelty is incremental, and the empirical validation lacks fair, strong baselines and consistent evaluation protocols. Without stronger controlled comparisons and more rigorous sense-specific evaluations, I am not confident that this should be accepted. I recommend rejection at this time, contingent on addressing the baseline and evaluation concerns.
