Summary and contributions
This paper proposes representing each word type as a mixture of Gaussian distributions (w2gm), learning means, covariances, and mixture weights from large corpora with an energy-based max-margin objective akin to skip-gram. The similarity (energy) between two words is the probability product kernel (expected likelihood kernel), which is closed-form for Gaussian mixtures. The approach aims to address polysemy, capture uncertainty, and support entailment via asymmetric measures.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A probabilistic multi-prototype embedding: modeling each word as a Gaussian mixture and training it end-to-end at scale with an energy-based ranking objective.
2) A practical, tractable energy function for mixtures: adopting the expected likelihood kernel for GMMs, with stability tricks enabling large-scale training.
3) Empirical evidence that mixtures better capture polysemy and improve over single-Gaussian embeddings and word2vec on several word similarity datasets and entailment.

Strengths
- Addresses a real limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings: The paper motivates and empirically shows that single Gaussians inflate variance for polysemous words, whereas mixtures produce more compact, interpretable modes. Table 1’s neighbors plausibly separate senses (e.g., rock: geology vs music; bank: river vs finance).
- Sound and tractable probabilistic formulation: Using the probability product kernel yields a closed-form energy for GMMs, avoiding intractable KL for mixtures (and employing a sensible log-sum-exp stabilization). The analysis of the ξi,j term and its effect on means vs covariances is informative.
- Scalable training details: The authors lay out initialization, spherical/diagonal covariance parameterization, stability constants, subsampling/negative sampling, and the use of separate input/output embeddings. This level of detail suggests the method can be reproduced and scaled.
- Broad evaluation across similarity and entailment: The model attains competitive or superior performance on multiple datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) versus word2vec and unimodal Gaussians. The use of componentwise similarity metrics (max cosine, min Euclidean) aligns with multi-sense evaluation practice.
- Qualitative interpretability: The visualization and qualitative nearest neighbors provide intuitive support for the approach and facilitate understanding of the learned senses.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/ambiguous comparisons and missing baselines: Strong multi-sense baselines (e.g., Multi-Sense Skip-gram/NP-MSSG, Reisinger & Mooney) are not compared. GloVe results are not reported despite being widely used. Only D=50 embeddings are used by default, which may understate word2vec/GloVe performance; fair comparisons should match dimensions and training settings. No statistical significance is reported.
- SCWS section is incomplete: Section 4.4 mentions SCWS and competing multi-prototype methods but reports no results. This is a major omission given SCWS is a standard sense-aware benchmark.
- Entailment KL metric is underspecified/inconsistent: The text claims KL for mixtures is intractable (correct), yet uses “minimum KL divergence” for entailment without clarifying whether KL is computed between component Gaussians (Ni||Nj) with min over i,j, or some approximation to KL between mixtures. The formula d(f,g)=min_{i,j} KL(f||g) as written is incorrect/ambiguous. Exact definition and implementation details are necessary.
- Covariance parameterization inconsistency and regularization concerns: The paper alternately states diagonal covariances are used, then that “the spherical case is used in all our experiments.” Please clarify which is used where. Also, the hinge loss + expected likelihood kernel can encourage large variances; the paper notes −log det regularization arises from the kernel term, but there is no explicit regularizer on covariances or means. Empirical analysis of variance growth, component collapse, and mixture weights (e.g., entropy of p_i) is missing.
- Evaluation protocol can inflate scores: Using max over component pairs (e.g., max cosine, min Euclidean) on sense-agnostic similarity datasets can overestimate performance by cherry-picking senses post hoc. This limitation should be acknowledged and balanced with sense-labeled evaluation (SCWS) and/or aggregation strategies (e.g., weighted by mixture weights or context-conditioned senses).
- Clarity/notation issues and formatting errors: There are numerous typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “polysemys,” “compliments” vs “complements,” extra spaces/punctuation, inconsistent capitalization). Table 5 formatting is unclear (two numbers per row but columns not labeled in-line). The definition of w2g is confusing (“w2g refers to our implementation of w2gm with one mixture component”). The colon annotations in Table 1 (e.g., basalt:1) are unexplained. References are severely malformed (duplicate authors, missing years “?,” truncated lines, LaTeX remnants). The “anonymous” visualization via a fixed IP/port raises anonymity concerns.

Questions to authors
1) Entailment KL: Exactly how is KL computed? Is it KL between Gaussian components, with min over i,j, i.e., min_{i,j} KL(N(μf,i,Σf,i) || N(μg,j,Σg,j))? If so, please provide the explicit formula used and justify min vs other aggregations (e.g., soft-min).
2) Covariance parameterization: Are experiments run with spherical covariances for all words/components, or diagonal? Are covariances learned per component or tied/shared? Any constraints or regularizers beyond the kernel-induced term? Please report average learned variances and mixture weight entropy to show no collapse.
3) Fair baselines: Can you add comparisons to strong multi-sense baselines (e.g., MSSG/NP-MSSG) and GloVe, matching embedding dimension and corpus? Also report results for D=200/300 to ensure fairness to word2vec/GloVe, and include significance tests.
4) SCWS results: Please provide full SCWS Spearman correlations (MaxSim/AveSim variants) against prior multi-sense methods.
5) Sensitivity/ablation: Effects of K (1,2,3), of the energy choice (expected likelihood vs alternatives), negative samples count, window size, and fixed vs learned covariances? Runtime/memory as a function of K?
6) Table 1 annotation: What do the “:0/1” suffixes next to neighbors denote? Are they the component indices of the neighbor word chosen by the matching rule?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is reasonable and the formulation is sound, with promising qualitative and some quantitative gains. However, the submission has multiple methodological ambiguities (KL entailment, covariance setting), missing key baselines and SCWS results, and numerous formatting/reference issues that undermine clarity and reproducibility. With clarified methods, stronger/fairer comparisons, completed SCWS/ablation analyses, and polished writing, this work would be much stronger.
