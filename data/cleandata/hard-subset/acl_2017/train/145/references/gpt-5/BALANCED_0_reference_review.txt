Summary and contributions
This paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians and training these probabilistic multi-prototype embeddings with a max-margin energy-based objective. The energy is the expected likelihood kernel (ELK), which remains closed-form for Gaussian mixtures, enabling scalable training. The model aims to capture polysemy more faithfully than unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) and to better encode uncertainty and entailment relations.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A probabilistic multi-sense embedding model: Gaussian Mixture word embeddings trained via a closed-form ELK energy and max-margin objective, with practical stability tricks. This extends prior Gaussian embeddings to multimodal distributions while retaining analytic tractability.
2) Empirical evidence that mixtures improve qualitative sense separation and quantitative performance on several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment, along with a practical, scalable training recipe.
3) Analysis and observations that mixtures mitigate variance inflation in unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemous words, and a set of similarity/entailment scoring choices for mixtures (max cosine, min Euclidean, min KL).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance with tractable probabilistic multi-sense modeling: Moving from unimodal Gaussians to Gaussian mixtures directly addresses the known limitation of single-mode uncertainty for polysemous words. Using the ELK maintains closed-form training signals even for mixtures, which is a thoughtful design choice given KL’s intractability for mixtures. The objective is simple and compatible with large-scale training.

- Clear qualitative evidence of sense separation and entailment: Nearest neighbor lists convincingly show disambiguated components for classic polysemous words (rock, bank, Apple, cell, left). The entailment evaluation (using min KL) shows consistent gains over unimodal Gaussian embeddings across window sizes and metrics, bolstering the claim that multimodality provides better inclusion structure.

- Practicality and reproducibility: The paper includes many crucial training details (negative sampling, subsampling, diagonal/spherical covariance, stability tricks, Adagrad schedule, log-sum-exp for ELK), an interactive visualization, and states plans for releasing code. The ability to train on 3.5B tokens with a relatively modest CPU setup, together with constrained covariance forms, demonstrates scalability.

- Empirical improvements on multiple benchmarks: The model often outperforms both skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian baselines on WS-353, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, and RW. The consistency of improvements across both similarity and entailment tasks suggests the approach is broadly useful, not just for niche metrics.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond a natural extension: The core idea—replacing unimodal Gaussians with mixtures and swapping the energy function to ELK to retain tractability—while useful, is a relatively incremental extension of Vilnis & McCallum (2014). The training objective and general setup (max-margin ranking with negative sampling) remain largely unchanged. Additional conceptual advances (e.g., asymmetric training for entailment, adaptive K per word) would strengthen novelty.

- Evaluation protocol may bias results: For each model the paper reports the best of multiple scoring metrics (mc/el/me) per dataset, effectively tuning on test sets. Baseline numbers (e.g., word2vec, original w2g) are single scores. This mismatch can inflate gains. A fair protocol (pre-fixing one scoring metric or using a dev set for metric selection) and reporting with confidence intervals or significance tests would improve credibility.

- Missing or limited comparisons to competitive multi-sense baselines: The paper compares against Huang et al. (2012), Tian et al. (2014), and a WordNet-aided method, but omits strong neural multi-sense baselines such as MSSG (Neelakantan et al., 2014). Without these, it is hard to position the contribution against the most relevant prior art in multi-prototype learning.

- Under-exploration of K and covariance structure: Most experiments fix K=2 and use spherical covariances, which weakens claims about nuanced uncertainty modeling and anisotropy. There is only a qualitative glance at K=3. A more thorough ablation on K (including selection strategies and trade-offs), and diagonal vs spherical covariance, would clarify when and why mixtures help, and whether directional uncertainty matters.

- Training-objective mismatch for entailment: Entailment is evaluated with an asymmetric measure (KL) while training uses a symmetric ELK. The paper argues KL is intractable for mixtures, but approximate training with Monte Carlo, variational bounds, or other asymmetric inclusion scores could be explored. At minimum, an empirical comparison of ELK vs other symmetric energies for mixtures would help justify the choice.

- Runtime and resource claims lack quantification: The paper states “days” on CPU, but does not provide concrete wall-clock times, memory usage, or speedups/slowdowns relative to word2vec and unimodal w2g, nor scalability with K. Given the increased parameter count and pairwise component interactions in ELK, these numbers are important for practical adoption.

Questions to authors
- Metric selection: How were mc/el/me chosen per dataset? Was a development set used? Please report results using a single pre-specified metric across datasets, or a dev-tuned metric fixed before testing, to avoid test-set tuning.

- Baselines: Can you add comparisons to MSSG (Neelakantan et al., 2014) under matched settings (same corpus, dimensions, window size), and any other competitive multi-sense embeddings from a similar timeframe?

- Effect of K: Please provide quantitative results for K ∈ {1,2,3,4} and a simple K-selection heuristic (e.g., based on frequency or model selection criteria). Does K correlate with polysemy? How does performance and training cost scale with K?

- Covariance structure: Since experiments use spherical covariances, can you report results with diagonal covariances and analyze whether anisotropy helps? Do mixtures compensate for lack of directional uncertainty?

- Entailment objective: Did you try asymmetric training signals (e.g., margin on an approximate KL or inclusion score)? Even simple surrogates (e.g., ELK with temperature or one-sided penalties) might improve entailment; any results?

- Runtime/memory: Please provide wall-clock time, epochs, and peak memory vs skip-gram and w2g on the same hardware, and scaling with K. This will help assess practicality.

- Dimensionality: Many baselines use 200–300D. Have you tested higher dimensions? Do the gains persist or improve?

- Sense disambiguation evaluation: Beyond SCWS correlations, can you evaluate sense selection accuracy on contextual WSD benchmarks (e.g., SemEval) by selecting the component conditioned on context? This would directly test the multi-sense claim.

Overall, this is a well-written, practical, and empirically solid extension of probabilistic embeddings to mixtures. Addressing the evaluation protocol, adding strong baselines, and deepening the analysis of K/covariances and runtime would significantly strengthen the paper.
