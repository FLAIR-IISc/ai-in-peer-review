- Paper proposes probabilistic multi-prototype word embeddings (Gaussian mixtures) with a closed-form expected likelihood kernel and a max-margin skip-gram-style training objective.
- Contribution 1: Scalable, expressive multi-sense probabilistic embeddings moving from unimodal Gaussians to Gaussian mixtures.
- Contribution 2: Practical training design (spherical/diagonal covariances, numerical stability, initialization) enabling large-scale training.
- Contribution 3: Empirical evidence: qualitative polysemy capture and quantitative gains on several word similarity sets and an entailment task.
- Strength: Analytically tractable formulation — closed-form energy for GMMs with clear gradients and principled distributional extension of SGNS.
- Strength: Addresses polysemy while preserving uncertainty — multiple modes reduce over-dispersion and yield interpretable sense clusters.
- Strength: Empirical improvements over unimodal Gaussians and reported SGNS baselines on multiple datasets; asymmetric metrics help entailment.
- Strength: Reproducibility — many implementation and training details disclosed and kernel derivation provided.
- Strength: Interpretability — covariances and mixture weights enable reasoning about uncertainty, specificity, and entailment.
- Weakness: Baselines may be unfair/weak — comparisons often use reported numbers from different corpora/dimensions; lacks same-corpus, same-dim SGNS/GloVe and stronger 300D baselines.
- Weakness: Entailment metric is heuristic — using min over componentwise KL ignores mixture weights and cross-component coverage and lacks justification/analysis.
- Weakness: Limited ablations — little study of K, weight collapse, covariance choices, alternative energy functions, and training hyperparameter impacts.
- Weakness: Scalability claims under-supported — no wall-clock times, throughput, memory, or complexity/empirical scaling vs SGNS given the O(K^2 D) cost.
- Weakness: Clarity/presentation issues — typos, inconsistent notation, possible truncated tables, messy references, and anonymity-risky demo URL.
- Question: Provide same-corpus/same-dimension SGNS/GloVe and multi-sense baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al., Tian et al.) and results at 200–300D?
- Question: Why use min componentwise KL for entailment rather than mixture-aware asymmetric scores or bounds, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- Question: Do mixture weights collapse for monosemous words; report distribution of effective K and examples where K>1 helps?
- Question: What are training times per epoch, memory usage, and scaling behavior with K compared to w2g and SGNS?
- Question: Do you perform context-based sense selection at test time (e.g., SCWS), and how does contextualized similarity perform?
- Overall: Technically sound and interesting, but empirical evaluation (fair baselines, ablations, entailment metric, scalability evidence) needs strengthening for acceptance.
