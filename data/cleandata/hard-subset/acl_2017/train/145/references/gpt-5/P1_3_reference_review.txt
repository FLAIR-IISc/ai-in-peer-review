Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a probabilistic, multi-prototype word embedding model in which each word is represented as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm). Training uses a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective, where the energy between two words is the expected likelihood kernel (inner product in L2) between their distributions. The kernel admits a closed form for Gaussian mixtures, giving analytic tractability and scalability. The model aims to capture polysemy (distinct mixture components per sense), uncertainty, and entailment (via distributional inclusion). The authors provide practical stabilization details and report qualitative sense separations and quantitative gains on several word similarity datasets and a lexical entailment benchmark.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) A probabilistic multi-sense embedding with a closed-form energy: modeling words as Gaussian mixtures and using the expected likelihood kernel yields a neat, tractable training objective that extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to multi-modality without sacrificing efficiency.
2) Empirical evidence that multi-modality helps: the model more cleanly separates senses in qualitative neighbors and improves over unimodal Gaussians and skip-gram on several word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment (using min KL over component pairs).
3) Practical recipe for scalable training: diagonal/spherical covariances, numerically stable log-sum-exp computations, initialization and optimization choices that enable training on billions of tokens.

Strengths
- Methodological elegance and tractability: The expected likelihood kernel is an apt choice for mixtures—closed-form, symmetric, and compatible with max-margin ranking. The derivations are clear, and the “partial energies” intuitively align with sense-to-sense affinities.
- Clear qualitative sense separation: Nearest neighbors for ambiguous words (rock, bank, cell, star, left) convincingly show components aligning with distinct senses. The K=3 example for cell is illustrative.
- Solid improvements on several benchmarks: On WS-353 (overall and relatedness), MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287 and RW, the mixture model generally outperforms skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians; entailment AP/F1 also improves with the mixture and asymmetric KL scoring.
- Sensible discussion of uncertainty: The analysis showing reduced variance per sense compared to unimodal Gaussians addresses the “overly diffuse” criticism and connects to better entailment behavior.
- Practicality and scalability: Implementation details (subsampling, negative sampling, separate input/output embeddings, Adagrad) and stability tricks (ε, stabilized log-sum) make the approach feasible; training on multi-billion-token corpora is noteworthy.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation breadth and baseline strength: The comparisons omit several strong baselines, including high-dimensional SGNS/GloVe (e.g., 300d), multi-sense skip-gram models (e.g., Neelakantan et al., MSSG/NPMI), and more recent multi-prototype or probabilistic approaches. Using 50d across the board may understate what strong baselines can do; it is unclear whether reported wins persist when baselines are tuned (dimension, window, negatives) on the same corpora. No analogy results or downstream tasks are reported.
- Claims about uncertainty not fully borne out: For most similarity datasets, the best scoring function is maximum cosine between means, which largely ignores covariance and mixture weights. This weakens the central claim that probabilistic uncertainty drives improvements; an ablation contrasting (i) mixture means only, (ii) mixture with fixed covariances, and (iii) full learned covariances would clarify where gains come from.
- Fixed K and lack of per-word adaptivity: The model uses a global K (typically 2), with only a brief K=3 example. There is no mechanism to adapt the number of senses per word or to diagnose component collapse/sparsity. Empirical analysis of mixture weights (how often do components meaningfully specialize? for which words?) is missing.
- Limited covariance structure and identifiability: The choice of spherical/diagonal covariances helps scale but constrains uncertainty modeling; no experiments assess whether full or low-rank covariances help, or whether learned covariances encode systematic semantic regularities. Identifiability (label switching, overlapping components) and stability across runs are not analyzed.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Some references and formatting are broken; code is promised only upon publication; training time is vaguely “in days” without precise hardware/runtime stats. The interactive demo link may risk de-anonymization. Hyperparameter sensitivity (margin, negatives, window, dimension) and ablations (energy choice, number of negatives) are limited.

Questions to authors
- How does w2gm compare to strong multi-sense baselines such as MSSG (Neelakantan et al., 2015) on SCWS and WS-353 (similarity/relatedness), when all are trained on the same corpus and with matched dimensionality? Could you add these baselines?
- To what extent do gains come from multiple means versus learned covariances? Please provide ablations: (a) mixtures with fixed/small covariances, (b) single-prototype with learned covariance, (c) mixtures with shared covariance across components.
- How sensitive are results to K, dimensionality, window size, and number of negatives? Any per-word diagnostics showing which words benefit from K>1, and how mixture weights distribute?
- For entailment, you use min KL over Gaussian pairs. Did you try other asymmetric measures compatible with mixtures (e.g., expected likelihood asymmetrization, or approximate mixture KL)? How robust are the gains across thresholds/corpora?
- Can you report training time, throughput (tokens/sec), and memory footprint more precisely, and compare to SGNS and unimodal Gaussians under the same setup?
- Does the expected likelihood kernel favor high-variance components despite the log-det term? Any empirical evidence (e.g., covariance histograms over training) that the model avoids variance inflation without extra regularization?

Additional comments (minor)
- Improve reference formatting and fix typos. Clarify whether the demo preserves anonymity. Consider adding downstream extrinsic tasks to strengthen impact.
