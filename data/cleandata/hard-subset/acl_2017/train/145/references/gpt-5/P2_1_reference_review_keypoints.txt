- Proposes multimodal probabilistic word embeddings: each word is a mixture of K Gaussians, trained with a skip‑gram–style max‑margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as a closed‑form energy between mixtures.
- Provides practical training details (diagonal/spherical covariances, stability transforms, initialization, negative sampling) enabling scaling to very large corpora.
- Shows qualitative multi‑sense behavior and consistent intrinsic improvements on several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment (using min KL at evaluation).

Main contributions:
- Simple, tractable probabilistic multi‑prototype embedding using Gaussian mixtures and the expected likelihood kernel with a scalable end‑to‑end objective.
- Empirical evidence that mixtures capture polysemy and improve intrinsic benchmarks, supported by qualitative analyses and an online demo.
- A practical recipe for numerically stable, large‑scale training of probabilistic embeddings.

Strengths:
- Clean, principled formulation with an analytic energy that avoids approximations and enables efficient learning.
- Unsupervised capture of polysemy with interpretable component neighbors for ambiguous words.
- Consistent, broad (if modest) improvements across multiple intrinsic similarity datasets and entailment when using asymmetric metrics.
- Concrete implementation details and large‑corpus training that aid reproducibility.
- Insightful analysis of uncertainty effects, showing mixtures reduce over‑diffuse covariances and the regularizing role of the log‑det term.

Weaknesses:
- Incremental novelty: combines established ideas (multi‑prototype embeddings, probabilistic embeddings, ELK) with careful engineering rather than a fundamentally new principle.
- Limited evaluation breadth and baselines: no extrinsic downstream tasks and missing comparisons to stronger/recent multi‑sense baselines.
- Unclear utility of uncertainty: similarity often best with max‑cosine on means, which largely ignores covariances and weights.
- Limited ablations and statistical rigor: no variance/significance reporting and insufficient isolation of components’ contributions.
- Presentation issues: citation/formatting glitches, incomplete references, and some confusing phrasing.

Meeting ACL criteria:
- Originality: Moderate — logical extension of prior work with known tools.
- Soundness/correctness: Good — derivations and training are reasonable; mismatch between symmetric training energy and asymmetric entailment evaluation noted.
- Clarity: Generally clear but marred by reference/formatting issues and some missing details.
- Significance/impact: Moderate — scalable and interpretable, but impact would be stronger with extrinsic results and stronger baselines.
- Reproducibility/resources: Reasonable — extensive implementation details and demo, though runtime/memory specifics are lacking.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to K and to spherical vs. diagonal covariances; did you try per‑word K or model selection?
- Can you report variance over runs and perform significance testing on main benchmarks?
- Why do covariance‑leveraging metrics underperform max‑cosine; can you show datasets or metrics where uncertainty helps?
- How does w2gm compare to stronger multi‑sense baselines (e.g., MSSG, AdaGram) on SCWS and WordSim?
- Can you provide extrinsic evaluations to demonstrate downstream benefits of polysemy and uncertainty?
- Did you try asymmetric or surrogate training objectives to better align training (ELK) with entailment evaluation (KL)?
- Please clarify throughput and memory: tokens/sec, training hours on your hardware, and scaling with K and D.
