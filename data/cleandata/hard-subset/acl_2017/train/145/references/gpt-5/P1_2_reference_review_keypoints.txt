- Proposes probabilistic multi-sense word embeddings modeling each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm) trained with a max-margin ranking objective using an expected likelihood kernel.
- Main contribution: scalable mixture-of-Gaussians formulation with closed-form expected likelihood kernel enabling multi-sense probabilistic representations and uncertainty.
- Empirical contribution: w2gm improves over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and often over skip-gram baselines on similarity and entailment benchmarks, with qualitative polysemy capture.
- Practical contribution: training techniques (initialization, numerical stability, diagonal/spherical covariances) allow CPU-scale training on large corpora.
- Strength: directly addresses polysemy/homonymy and shows convincing qualitative component-wise sense distinctions (e.g., “rock”, “bank”, “cell”).
- Strength: elegant, tractable, symmetric closed-form energy (expected likelihood kernel) with clear derivations and analytical gradients.
- Strength: solid empirical gains across many similarity datasets and improved entailment performance using multi-prototype distributions with asymmetric KL at test time.
- Strength: demonstrated scalability and careful engineering details that support reproducibility and adoption.
- Strength: interpretability and modeled uncertainty via mixture components and covariances are useful advantages over point vectors.
- Weakness: limited novelty—incremental extension from unimodal Gaussians and existing multi-prototype point embeddings.
- Weakness: skip-gram/GloVe baselines may be weak or not re-tuned on same settings; training with only one negative sample might underpower comparisons.
- Weakness: performs poorly on SimLex-999, suggesting tendency to capture relatedness rather than pure similarity; analysis lacking.
- Weakness: SCWS comparisons are unclear or not clearly presented in text, limiting quantitative assessment of polysemy improvements.
- Weakness: training objective is symmetric while entailment test uses asymmetric KL, creating an objective/metric mismatch that may limit entailment learning.
- Weakness: limited ablations and sensitivity analyses for K, mixture weight usage, covariance choices, negatives, and embedding dimensionality.
- Weakness: potential mismatch between test-time max-over-components scoring and learned probabilistic semantics; limited reporting on component collapse or mixture weight distributions.
- Weakness: incomplete reproducibility/reporting—missing runtime, memory, convergence behavior, and stability across random seeds.
- Question: provide clear SCWS results (MaxSim/AveSim) and comparisons to multi-sense baselines when context/sense annotations are available.
- Question: how sensitive are results to number of negatives, window size, embedding dimension, and K; do gains persist with stronger baseline hyperparameters?
- Question: did you observe component collapse or redundancy, and how are mixture weights distributed across the vocabulary?
- Question: have you tried incorporating asymmetric training objectives (e.g., KL-based) for entailment or combining asymmetric terms with expected likelihood kernel?
- Question: report training time, memory usage, throughput for largest run, and results across multiple random seeds to assess stability.
- Additional suggestion: compare to other multi-sense methods (multi-sense skip-gram variants) and strong GloVe baselines.
- Additional suggestion: analyze why SimLex performance drops to determine whether model favors relatedness or suffers from max-over-components scoring.
- Minor: fix malformed references and tables and ensure all results and citations are clearly presented.
