Summary and main contributions
- A probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding that represents each word as a K-component Gaussian mixture, trained with a max-margin ranking loss and a symmetric “expected likelihood kernel” (probability product kernel) energy that is closed-form for mixtures.
- A set of pragmatic training/implementation choices (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-sum-exp stabilization, subsampling/negative sampling, separate input/output mixtures) claimed to scale to billions of tokens.
- Empirical evidence (mostly intrinsic) suggesting the model learns distinct senses for polysemous words and improves over (some) baselines on several word similarity datasets and on a lexical entailment benchmark.

Strengths
- Clear qualitative motivation: mixtures address the single-mode limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemy. The nearest-neighbor examples are convincing and easy to interpret (e.g., “rock” splitting into geology/music; “bank” splitting into river/finance).
- The energy choice (expected likelihood kernel) is a sound, tractable alternative to KL for mixtures; the derivation is straightforward and correct, and the numerical stabilization details (log-sum-exp, epsilon) are practical.
- Intrinsic performance: reported gains over their own unimodal Gaussian baseline on multiple word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment, and improvements of the mixture over single-component variants, are consistent with the motivation.
- Engineering details: the paper documents many choices (subsampling, Pn ∝ U3/4, initializations, separate input/output mixtures) that aid reproducibility, and states large-corpus training is feasible.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and positioning. Moving from unimodal Gaussians to Gaussian mixtures is a natural extension; expected likelihood kernels are well-known (Jebara et al., 2004); the training objective mirrors skip-gram/negative sampling and Vilnis & McCallum’s energy-based ranking. The paper does not convincingly differentiate from prior multi-sense work (e.g., Reisinger & Mooney 2010; Huang et al. 2012; Neelakantan et al. 2014 MSSG; Tian et al. 2014), nor does it compare empirically to several of these. The related work omits core multi-sense baselines (e.g., MSSG) and does not discuss why mixtures offer practical advantages over deterministic multi-sense embeddings beyond qualitative plots.
- Evaluation fairness and methodology. Several reported baselines (sg*, w2g*) are copied from Vilnis & McCallum and likely trained on different corpora and settings, while the proposed model is trained on 3.5B tokens; this undermines fairness. No comparisons to strong single-prototype baselines (GloVe) trained on the same data. The paper uses multiple scoring functions (cosine, Euclidean, expected likelihood) and reports the best per dataset, which risks metric cherry-picking; a fixed, pre-registered metric per task would be more credible. The SCWS section announces comparisons but provides no numbers/table in the main text; this feels incomplete. Entailment results are only against their own unimodal variant; no comparison to contemporary entailment-oriented embeddings.
- Objective/metric mismatch and ambiguity. Training uses a symmetric kernel; entailment evaluation uses asymmetric KL. The paper asserts KL for mixtures lacks closed form (true), yet reports “minimum KL divergence” for mixtures without defining the approximation precisely. It appears to be a min over component-pair KLs, but this is never stated mathematically; please formalize. More broadly, the paper does not analyze how the symmetric training energy induces asymmetry necessary for entailment, beyond evaluation-time heuristics.
- Technical clarity and internal inconsistencies. The paper alternates between diagonal and spherical covariances; the appendix says spherical is used in all experiments, while the main text emphasizes diagonal and even general Σ with det and inverses. If spherical covariances are used, the model’s capacity (e.g., ellipsoids in Fig. 1) is overstated. Claims of “heavy tailed uncertainty” are not substantiated—Gaussian mixtures have heavier tails than a single Gaussian but are not heavy-tailed in the usual sense, and no tail behavior is analyzed. There is no explicit regularization on covariances beyond the form of ξ; analysis of degeneracies (variance inflation/collapse) is missing. Only one negative sample per positive seems light for large vocabularies; rationale/ablation missing.
- Reproducibility, typos, and formatting. Numerous issues:
  - Mathematical error: e−1.8 ≈ 1.65 and e−2.5 ≈ 0.82 are incorrect (they are ≈0.165 and ≈0.082). This undermines the variance discussion.
  - Inconsistent model names (w2gm/w2mg/W2MG), misspellings (“constrast”, “polysemys”, “rockmostly”), hyphenation breaks (“scal- able”).
  - References are frequently malformed (duplicate authors, missing years, nonsensical trailing text, “Luong et al.” without details, TensorFlow citation incorrect, “Collobert and JasonWeston”, duplicates of authors’ names).
  - The interactive demo link with a raw IP/port is risky for double-blind review; code “upon publication” is not sufficient for reproducibility.
  - Missing experimental details: training time/epochs, convergence behavior, memory footprint, sensitivity to K, per-word sense distributions, quantitative sense disambiguation metrics, and downstream tasks.

Questions to authors
- Please precisely define the entailment scoring: is d(f,g) = min_{i,j} KL(N(μf,i,Σf,i) || N(μg,j,Σg,j))? If not, what is computed? How do you handle singularities/numerical issues in KL?
- Are covariances learned as spherical, diagonal, or full? The text shows all three at different points. If spherical in all experiments, please correct figures/text and discuss the implications for expressivity and entailment.
- How many negative samples per positive are used? Did you try >1 negatives, and what is the effect on performance and training time?
- Please provide fair baselines trained on the same corpus and vocabulary (skip-gram, GloVe, MSSG/Neelakantan et al. 2014), with a single, fixed similarity metric per task.
- SCWS: include the full quantitative table in the main paper (or at least clear summary statistics) and clarify whether context is used during scoring as in prior multi-sense evaluations.
- How do you choose K? Did you explore per-word variable K or simple pruning based on mixture weights? Provide statistics on learned mixture weights (e.g., % of words with one dominant component).
- Correct the exponential calculations in Section 4.5 and report actual learned variances; include regularization or constraints to prevent degenerate covariances.
- Please remove or properly anonymize the demo link; provide a tarball/commit hash for reproducibility, and list all hyperparameters and training budget (epochs, wall-clock hours on specified hardware).
