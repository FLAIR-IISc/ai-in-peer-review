Summary
This paper proposes representing each word type as a mixture of Gaussians (MoG) rather than a single point or a single Gaussian. The model learns mixture means, covariances, and weights using a skip-gram-style objective with negative sampling, optimized via a max-margin ranking loss. The energy (similarity) between two word distributions is defined by the expected likelihood kernel (inner product in L2 between distributions), which has a closed form for Gaussian mixtures. The paper details numerically stable training, uses large corpora, and evaluates on word similarity and entailment. Qualitative analyses claim better handling of polysemy than unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding with a mixture of Gaussians for each word type, enabling modeling of polysemy with an interpretable uncertainty structure.
2) An energy-based training approach for MoG embeddings using the expected likelihood kernel to retain analytical tractability and scalability, along with practical stabilization/initialization details.
3) Empirical evaluation showing improvements over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on multiple word similarity datasets and lexical entailment, plus qualitative demonstrations of disentangled senses.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation with closed-form energy: Modeling words as MoGs is a straightforward but meaningful generalization of Gaussian embeddings. The expected likelihood kernel yields an analytically tractable energy for mixtures, avoiding the intractability of KL for mixtures. The derivations and numerical stabilization (log-sum-exp trick, diagonal/spherical covariance, positivity constraints) are sound and clearly explained.
- Qualitative handling of polysemy: The nearest-neighbor analyses show distinct components aligning with different senses (e.g., rock: geology vs music; bank: river vs finance; cell: biology vs phone), fulfilling the core motivation of multi-sense representation.
- Quantitative gains on several benchmarks: On multiple datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW), the MoG model often outperforms both word2vec and unimodal Gaussian baselines. Using multiple similarity metrics (max cosine, min Euclidean, expected likelihood) shows robustness, and the choice of metric aligns sensibly with the training objective (Euclidean). On WordSim-353, reported performance matches or exceeds prior multi-prototype baselines.
- Entailment improvement and appropriate asymmetric scoring: Using minimum KL divergence between components provides an asymmetric measure beneficial for entailment (w1 |= w2 ≠ w2 |= w1). The MoG model consistently outperforms unimodal Gaussians across AP and F1, consistent with better uncertainty modeling and reduced variance inflation for polysemous words.
- Practicality and scalability: The paper describes training on billion-token corpora with reasonable hardware and provides implementation details (subsampling, negative sampling, Adagrad) that make replication feasible.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior Gaussian embeddings and multi-prototype vectors: The key ideas (mixtures for polysemy; skip-gram training; energy-based ranking) are natural extensions of Vilnis & McCallum (2014) and earlier multi-sense deterministic models. While combining MoGs with the expected likelihood kernel is technically neat, the conceptual advance is evolutionary rather than fundamentally new.
- Evaluation coverage and fairness gaps: The SCWS polysemy evaluation is mentioned but lacks a clear, complete results table in the text provided; comparisons to strong multi-sense baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014 MSSG; other EM/clustering-based multi-sense skip-gram variants) are missing. For entailment, comparisons focus on unimodal Gaussian and cosine; broader baselines (e.g., hypernym-specific models or taxonomic methods) are absent. It is unclear whether gains are statistically significant.
- Ambiguity in baselines and reporting: The nomenclature “w2g” is reused for both the original unimodal Gaussian embeddings and the authors’ own unimodal implementation, with “w2g*” denoting numbers from prior work; this is confusing. Some tables reference underlining/bolding that is not visible, and some datasets lack consistent baseline numbers. A clear, consolidated table with identical training data, window sizes, negative sample counts, and vocabulary coverage would increase rigor.
- Limited analysis of mixture behavior and model selection: K is mostly fixed at 2; there is little analysis of how many components are actually used (e.g., mixture weight entropy, component collapse), how K affects performance/complexity, or automated selection of K. The per-pair energy is O(K^2); computational/memory overhead and runtime are not quantified. The covariance structure is diagonal/spherical; the impact vs. full/low-rank covariances is unexplored.
- No ablations on energy/objective choices: The advantages of the expected likelihood kernel over training with KL-based energies (as in Vilnis & McCallum) are argued but not empirically demonstrated. An ablation comparing training with different energies/objectives, and with/without covariance terms, would clarify where gains arise.

Questions to authors
- SCWS and multi-sense baselines: Please provide complete SCWS results with MaxSim/AveSim and comparisons to recent multi-sense skip-gram baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014) under matched training data and hyperparameters. Are improvements statistically significant?
- Runtime and memory: What are the exact training times, memory footprint, and throughput for K=2 vs K=3 on the reported corpora and hardware? How does O(K^2) per pair affect scalability as K increases?
- Mixture utilization: For how many word types do multiple components receive substantial mass? Please report distribution of mixture entropies, fraction of words with effectively one active component, and examples of component collapse/merging.
- Sensitivity to K and covariance structure: How do results vary with K=1,2,3, and with spherical vs diagonal covariances? Any evidence that K>2 helps on polysemous datasets beyond qualitative cases?
- Energy/objective ablations: Can you compare training with expected likelihood kernel vs (approximate) KL-based energies, and margin vs logistic/negative sampling objectives, holding other factors fixed?
- Entailment evaluation: How is the threshold chosen for AP/F1? Is there a development split? Have you evaluated on additional entailment/hypernym datasets (e.g., BLESS, LEDS, EVALution) or used asymmetric scoring beyond min KL?
- Fairness of baselines: Are word2vec and unimodal Gaussian baselines trained on the same corpora, with the same window sizes, subsampling, and negatives? How many negatives per positive are used? Please clarify to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and clearly written, with practical contributions and promising empirical results. Addressing the evaluation and analysis gaps would strengthen claims of novelty and robustness.
