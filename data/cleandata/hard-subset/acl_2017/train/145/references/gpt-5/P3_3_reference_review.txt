Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), trained with a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective. To score word-word affinity, it uses the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel), which admits a closed form for Gaussian mixtures and incorporates uncertainty via covariances. The approach is intended to capture polysemy (multiple modes), entailment (distributional inclusion), and calibrated uncertainty, addressing limitations of unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014).
- Empirically, the model trained on UKWAC+Wackypedia (3.5B tokens) shows: (a) qualitative separation of senses; (b) improved correlations on several word similarity datasets over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian baselines; (c) improved lexical entailment performance using a component-wise KL-based score. The paper also describes practical stabilization tricks and training details enabling large-scale training.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) Method: a scalable, analytically tractable multimodal probabilistic word embedding using a Gaussian mixture and expected likelihood kernel within a max-margin/negative-sampling training setup.
2) Empirical evidence: qualitative sense separation and improved performance on several similarity and entailment benchmarks relative to unimodal Gaussian and word2vec baselines.
3) Practical training/stability insights for mixture embeddings (covariance parameterization, numerics, initialization), enabling training on billions of tokens.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and principled energy function choice: Using the expected likelihood kernel is well-justified—it naturally generalizes inner products to densities, keeps training tractable for mixtures (closed form), and introduces a meaningful dependence on uncertainty via the log-det and Mahalanobis terms. The analysis of how terms influence learning (means vs variances) is helpful.
- Captures polysemy in an unsupervised, scalable way: The mixture model (K=2 or 3) produces intuitive sense clusters for classic polysemous words (rock, bank, Apple, cell) without external resources, while retaining training scalability (O(K^2D) per pair; training reported to complete in days on CPU).
- Competitive to stronger than baselines on several benchmarks: Across WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW, the proposed model generally outperforms unimodal Gaussians and often word2vec, with consistent trends across multiple similarity measures. The entailment results (AP/F1) are also better than unimodal Gaussians, supporting the claim that multi-modality better models inclusion-like relations.
- Solid engineering and reproducibility details: The paper provides concrete implementation choices (diagonal/spherical covariances, Adagrad with schedule, negative sampling distribution, subsampling), numerical stabilization (log-sum-exp for energy, epsilon for covariances), and a visualization demo. This increases reproducibility and practical impact.
- Insightful treatment of uncertainty: The paper discusses the variance inflation issue in unimodal Gaussians for polysemous words and shows measured reductions in per-dimension variances under mixtures, aligning with the intuition that senses can remain sharp rather than being averaged.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparisons to other multi-sense embeddings and entailment baselines: The paper compares primarily to word2vec and unimodal Gaussians. It omits key multi-sense baselines such as Neelakantan et al. (2014, MSSG) and related methods (e.g., nonparametric sense induction), as well as stronger entailment/hypernymy baselines (e.g., directional entailment measures, SLQS/Weeds precision, or later datasets). This weakens the empirical claim of state-of-the-art performance for polysemy and entailment.
- Ambiguity around KL for mixtures in evaluation: The paper states KL between Gaussian mixtures lacks a closed form (correct), yet uses “minimum KL divergence” for entailment scoring. The intended definition appears to be min over component-wise KLs, i.e., min_{i,j} KL(N_i || N_j), but this is not explicitly stated. Without a precise definition, the entailment evaluation is unclear and potentially misleading.
- Missing or incomplete evaluations: Section 4.4 mentions SCWS and comparisons to Huang/Tian/Chen, but the corresponding results table is absent in the provided text. More importantly, the method does not perform context-sensitive sense selection during training; evaluations relying on MaxSim post hoc may not reflect true sense disambiguation ability on context-aware benchmarks (e.g., SCWS, WiC). An explicit context-scoring procedure would make the claim stronger.
- Ablation and sensitivity analyses are sparse: Key design choices (K, covariance parameterization: spherical vs diagonal, embedding dimension, margin m, number of negatives, output vs input tying) are not thoroughly ablated. The paper notes K=3 did not change much overall, but a systematic analysis (performance vs K, runtime vs K, sense interpretability) is missing. Additionally, comparisons across similarity metrics sometimes choose different best metrics per dataset; a single principled similarity measure would improve comparability.
- Fairness of baseline settings: The reported embedding dimension is D=50; many published baselines use 200–300D, which can materially affect performance on word similarity. The word2vec baselines partly come from prior papers and may not be matched in training data or hyperparameters. Without matched settings, the strength of the empirical gains is harder to assess.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the “minimum KL divergence” used for entailment: Is it min_{i,j} KL(N(μf,i, Σf,i) || N(μg,j, Σg,j))? If so, please state this explicitly and justify why this component-wise approximation is appropriate for modeling distributional inclusion between mixtures.
- How does w2gm compare to multi-sense skip-gram (Neelakantan et al., 2014) and other multi-prototype baselines on WordSim-353, SCWS, and related datasets when trained on the same corpus and dimensions? Can you add these comparisons?
- Can you report training time, hardware, and memory usage for K=2 (and K=3), and how runtime scales with K, D, and number of negatives? Did you try more negatives per positive?
- How sensitive are results to covariance parameterization (spherical vs diagonal vs tied across components)? Do diagonal covariances improve similarity or entailment vs spherical?
- For context-sensitive similarity (e.g., SCWS), do you have a token-level disambiguation scheme (selecting a component given context) rather than MaxSim over type-level mixtures? If so, please report those results.
- Are mixture weights pi interpretable and correlated with sense frequencies? Any analysis on learned pi across polysemous words?

Additional comments
- The paper is clearly written overall but the references contain many formatting issues and some entries appear corrupted; please fix for camera-ready. Also ensure the visualization link adheres to anonymity policy.
- It would be useful to report a single primary similarity measure for all datasets (e.g., expected likelihood kernel or MaxSim cosine) to avoid per-dataset metric cherry-picking, while leaving others in the appendix.
- Consider evaluating on modern hypernymy/entailment benchmarks and reporting directionality (asymmetric scoring) explicitly.

Overall evaluation
A solid, well-motivated extension of probabilistic word embeddings to mixtures with an analytically tractable training objective and promising empirical gains. The main concerns are breadth/fairness of comparisons, clarity of the KL-based entailment scoring, and missing ablations/context-sensitive evaluations. With these addressed, this work would make a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance after revisions.
